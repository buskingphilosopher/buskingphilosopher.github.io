A Debate on the Iraq War
"This House Believes the 2003 Invasion of Iraq Was Justified"
Christopher Hitchens · A Long Short War: The Postponed Liberation of Iraq (Plume, 2003) & subsequent essays and debates
Hans Blix · Disarming Iraq (Pantheon, 2004) — Former Executive Chairman, UNMOVIC; Chief UN Weapons Inspector 2000–2003
For the Motion
Christopher Hitchens
A Long Short War (2003) · "A War to Be Proud Of" (2005) · debates and essays 2002–2011
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr Chairman,

I want to begin by insisting on what this debate is actually about — and clearing away the fog of slogans, bumper stickers, and bad faith that has obscured it since the beginning. This is not a debate about whether George W. Bush is a good man. I have my own views about that and they are not uniformly flattering. It is not a debate about whether the American right has always had noble motives in the Middle East — manifestly it has not. It is a debate about whether, given the totality of what Saddam Hussein's regime was and had done, its violent removal was justified. I say yes, and I have said so consistently, and I intend to show why — not from the vantage point of hindsight, but from the evidence and principles that were available to any person willing to look at them honestly in 2002 and 2003.

I want to make one preliminary point about my own position. I did not come to this view out of neoconservative conversion or sudden patriotism. I came to it out of solidarity with the Iraqi and Kurdish opposition to Saddam Hussein — people I knew personally, people whose courage shamed me, people who had been asking the democratic world for decades to do what it was unwilling to do: take their oppressor seriously.

I. The war had already begun: the ceasefire was not peace

The war as continuation: the 1991 ceasefire set conditions Saddam systematically violatedThe first thing I need to establish is that the invasion of March 2003 was not a new war. It was the resumption of an existing one. The ceasefire of 1991 was not a peace treaty. It was a conditional armistice — and the conditions were Saddam Hussein's obligation to disarm, to account for his weapons programmes, to cease persecution of his own population, and to comply with seventeen binding UN Security Council resolutions. He violated every one of them, continuously and deliberately, for twelve years. The no-fly zones over Kurdistan in the north and the Shia south were not peacetime measures. They were active military operations, conducted under fire, to prevent the completion of genocides that had already been partially carried out. When I hear people say that the United States started a war in Iraq in 2003, I want to ask them: what were those pilots doing over Mosul every day for twelve years? There was no neutralist position available. The choice was between enforcing the ceasefire and abandoning it. Those who opposed the invasion were, whether they acknowledged it or not, voting for the abandonment of millions of people to a regime they had already seen commit genocide.

II. Saddam's Iraq was a fascist state — and antifascism is not optional

The Ba'athist regime as fascism: the antifascist case for regime change as a matter of principleI have spent my life arguing, following Orwell, that certain forms of political evil are not negotiable — that the appropriate response to fascism is not engagement, containment, or studied neutrality, but defeat. The Ba'athist state was, by any serious definition, a fascist state: it had a single-party dictatorship, a cult of the leader personality enforced with terror, systematic racial and ethnic persecution culminating in genocide, a history of aggressive war against its neighbours, and a command economy organised around the appetites of a ruling clique. If you do not understand what Ba'athism was — if you have not read Kanan Makiya's Republic of Fear, if you have not spoken to a Kurdish survivor of Halabja, if you have not tried to imagine what it meant to live in a country where you could be executed for possessing a satellite dish — then I submit that your opinion about what should have been done about it carries very limited moral weight. I know what fascism looks like because I have read about it and talked to those who lived under it. The analogy is not rhetorical. It is literal.

III. The Kurdish genocide: the case that required no WMD

The Kurdish genocide and the Anfal campaign: the humanitarian case for intervention standing aloneLet me state, with all the precision I can manage, what Saddam Hussein did to the Kurdish people of northern Iraq. In the Anfal campaign of 1986 to 1989, conducted by the Iraqi state against its own citizens, between 100,000 and 180,000 Kurdish men, women, and children were murdered. Many were gassed — at Halabja in March 1988, five thousand people died in a single chemical attack, the largest use of chemical weapons against a civilian population in history. The survivors described their children turning blue and dying in their arms. After the Gulf War of 1991, when the Shia and Kurdish populations rose in the expectation of American support that never came, Saddam's forces massacred an estimated 300,000 people while American troops stood within earshot and were ordered not to intervene. I was in a Jeep in Kurdistan in 1991 with people who asked me why the West had done this to them. They were still asking in 2003. This case — the humanitarian case — did not depend on WMD. It did not depend on September 11. It required only a willingness to look at what had already happened and to ask whether a civilised world could continue to guarantee the survival of the regime that had done it.

IV. The WMD question: inspections could not verify what the regime was designed to conceal

On WMD: the concealment programme itself was the evidence; inspections could not substitute for regime changeI want to address the WMD question directly, because I am going to be told — and I hear it already in the objections being prepared across from me — that the absence of stockpiles after the invasion discredits the entire enterprise. It does not, for the following reasons. First: the intelligence services of every major Western country, including those most opposed to the invasion — France, Germany — assessed that Iraq had active WMD programmes. These services were wrong about the stockpiles. They were not wrong that the regime was conducting systematic concealment operations. The Kay Report documented an enormous infrastructure of concealment — scientists threatened with death for cooperating with inspectors, documents destroyed, equipment moved. A concealment programme is not evidence of nothing. It is evidence of something being concealed. Second: in the absence of inspectors who were actually the government of the country they were inspecting — which Mr Blix conspicuously was not — there was no mechanism to verify compliance in a state designed from the ground up to frustrate verification. The inspections from 1991 to 1998, conducted by Rolf Ekeus, had found and destroyed more weapons than the Gulf War destroyed — which is to say, the weapons Saddam had sworn he did not have. The question was what he had acquired in the five years since UNSCOM was expelled.

V. The connection between Saddam and international terrorism

Saddam as state sponsor of international terrorism: Abu Nidal, Abu Abbas, Zarqawi, Ansar al-IslamThe claim that there was no connection between Saddam Hussein's Iraq and international terrorism deserves more scepticism than the anti-war movement applied to it. Abu Nidal, who murdered passengers at Vienna and Rome airports, lived in Baghdad under Iraqi state protection. Abu Abbas, who ordered the murder of Leon Klinghoffer by throwing him off the Achille Lauro in his wheelchair, was captured in Baghdad travelling on an Iraqi diplomatic passport — which raises the question of who issued it to him. Abu Musab al-Zarqawi, who would later lead al-Qaeda in Mesopotamia, moved from Afghanistan to Iraq before the invasion and was receiving medical treatment in Baghdad. Ansar al-Islam, an Islamist group with documented ties to al-Qaeda, was operating in the Kurdish zone of northern Iraq — the zone under nominal American protection. I am not claiming that Saddam planned September 11 or that the Ba'athist regime and al-Qaeda were ideological allies — they were not. I am claiming that Baghdad had become a city of refuge for every species of international gangster, that this was known, and that the prospect of Saddam's WMD ambitions intersecting with terrorist networks was not a fantasy conjured by neoconservative ideologues but a serious strategic assessment.

VI. The non-proliferation dividend: what the invasion actually achieved

The AQ Khan network, Libyan disarmament, and non-proliferation gains: the strategic dividend of invasionI want to list, soberly and without triumphalism, the strategic achievements that actually resulted from the removal of Saddam Hussein's regime. First: within months of the invasion, Colonel Gaddafi of Libya announced the voluntary surrender of his entire WMD programme — a programme whose existence had not been previously known and whose discovery exposed the AQ Khan network, the most dangerous nuclear proliferation operation in history. Khan's network had transferred nuclear technology to Libya, Iran, and North Korea. The invasion of Iraq scared Gaddafi into compliance and thereby exposed and partially dismantled the most serious proliferation threat of the early twenty-first century. This is not a small thing. Second: Iraq could now be certified as actually disarmed — not on the word of a psychopathic autocrat who had lied to every inspection regime that had ever visited him, but by direct verification. Third: the Kurdish autonomous region in the north, which had already demonstrated under the no-fly zone what post-Saddam Iraq could look like, was secured and expanded. These are not rhetorical victories. They are verifiable facts.

VII. The anti-war movement and its strange bedfellows

The anti-war coalition: objective support for a fascist regime dressed as pacifismI want to say something uncomfortable but necessary about the coalition that opposed this war. It was not a coalition of pacifists. It was a coalition that included people who had never opposed any military action taken by the Soviet Union, by Saddam Hussein, by the IRA, or by any anti-American armed force of any description. The slogan "No Blood for Oil" was never applied to the hundreds of thousands of Kurdish civilians killed with chemical weapons over Iraqi oilfields. The "inspections are working" lobby was never heard when UNSCOM was thrown out of Iraq in 1998. People who marched in London in February 2003 claiming to speak for the Iraqi people had never once spoken to an Iraqi exile, never read Kanan Makiya, never asked the four million Iraqis in diaspora what they wanted. The anti-war movement was not anti-war. It was pro-status-quo — and the status quo was a concentration camp above ground and a mass grave below it. I do not apologise for refusing to take instruction from that movement on moral questions.

VIII. The 1991 betrayal: the debt owed to those who rose and were abandoned

The 1991 betrayal: the Shia and Kurdish uprisings were encouraged and then abandoned — the moral debt of that failureIn February 1991, President George H. W. Bush broadcast an appeal to the Iraqi people to rise against their oppressor. The Shia in the south and the Kurds in the north did so — in their hundreds of thousands, at enormous personal risk, on the basis of an American promise that had been made and was now being made again. They rose. American forces stood within earshot. The order came down not to intervene. Saddam Hussein's helicopter gunships massacred between 100,000 and 300,000 people in the following weeks, while American soldiers listened to the screaming. The decision was made, by Henry Kissinger's student Brent Scowcroft, that regional stability mattered more than the lives of the people who had answered America's call. I opposed that decision then and I oppose it now. I did not support the 1991 war for that reason. But the moral logic of 1991 — the logic that said: these people are under a genocidal dictatorship and we have the power to remove it — did not disappear because Scowcroft chose to ignore it. It accumulated interest. The debt came due in 2003. I am not embarrassed to have supported its repayment.

IX. The case that survives all objections: no WMD, no apology

The moral case that survives WMD absence: sovereignty forfeited by genocide, aggression, and terror hostingLet me close with what I regard as the decisive philosophical point. Under what conditions does a state forfeit its right to sovereignty — its right to demand that its borders be respected by the international community? I argue that sovereignty is not unconditional. A state that uses chemical weapons against its own civilian population has violated the Genocide Convention, to which it was a signatory. A state that invades and annexes a neighbouring sovereign country — Kuwait — and has declared its intention to complete a nuclear weapon has violated the Non-Proliferation Treaty. A state that harbours international terrorists and assassins is in breach of the elementary obligations of membership in the international community. Iraq, under Saddam Hussein, had met all of these criteria — not once, but repeatedly, and not in the distant past but in the years immediately preceding 2003. The WMD question was one part of this case. It was never the whole of it. When people say that the absence of stockpiles invalidates the entire enterprise, they are conceding that Halabja did not matter, that Kuwait did not matter, that Abu Nidal's passport did not matter. I decline that concession. The moral case was always wider and deeper than the intelligence case. It remains intact. I shall lose sleep for the rest of my life for not having argued it more forcefully. I shall not lose sleep for having argued it at all.

Thank you.

Against the Motion
Hans Blix
Disarming Iraq (2004) — Chief UN Weapons Inspector, UNMOVIC 2000–2003
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr Chairman,

I approach this debate from an unusual vantage point. I was not a pacifist. I was not a defender of Saddam Hussein. I had spent my professional life trying to ensure that dangerous states gave up dangerous weapons — and I considered Iraq a dangerous state. I was the person actually charged, in the final months before the invasion, with finding out whether Iraq had weapons of mass destruction. I was on the ground. I had access to the sites. My teams were conducting inspections in real time. And what I found, and what I reported to the Security Council, and what the invasion itself then confirmed, was that Iraq's most dangerous capabilities had already been substantially dismantled — and that the process through which I was working was producing results that the rush to war foreclosed.

I say this not to vindicate myself — though events have done so — but because it is directly relevant to the central factual premise of Mr Hitchens's case, and because the lessons of what went wrong in 2003 are urgent enough that they should be stated precisely and without bombast.

I. The inspections were working — this is not opinion but documented fact

UNMOVIC's findings: Iraq was substantially disarmed by 2003 — the inspections were producing results the invasion interruptedMr Hitchens says that inspections could not work — that you would need to be the government of Iraq to inspect it. I can speak to this directly, because I was the inspector. In my report to the Security Council on 27 January 2003, I noted that Iraq was not providing the proactive cooperation required by Resolution 1441, but I was equally clear about what we were finding: no evidence of renewed production of weapons of mass destruction. In the months that followed, my teams conducted hundreds of inspections at over three hundred sites, including sites designated by American and British intelligence. We found no active WMD programmes. The Iraqi declarations, while incomplete on some historical points, were consistent with what we were finding on the ground. Our mobile Mirada radar-tracking teams found no evidence of the mobile biological weapons laboratories that Colin Powell had described at the Security Council. When the invasion came and the full resources of the United States military were devoted to finding what we had not found, they confirmed our findings. Iraq had been substantially disarmed — not by the invasion but by UNSCOM in the 1990s and by the pressures that the inspection process itself had maintained. (Disarming Iraq, Chs. 8–10)

II. The intelligence was not a reasonable basis for war — it was presented deceptively

The intelligence failure: political leaders put exclamation marks where there should have been question marksMr Hitchens says that all Western intelligence agencies assessed Iraq as having active WMD programmes, and that this assessment, while wrong about stockpiles, was reasonable. I want to challenge both claims. On the first: the intelligence assessments I was shown were hedged, conditional, and full of caveats — caveats that disappeared entirely when they were translated into the public presentations made by Colin Powell at the Security Council, by Tony Blair in the Commons, and by the Bush administration generally. Powell's presentation, which I watched carefully from my seat in that chamber, contained claims that my teams had already been unable to substantiate — specific sites, specific capabilities — presented as established facts. The question marks that honest intelligence assessment required had been replaced, as I wrote at the time, with exclamation marks. The Bush administration did not merely receive wrong intelligence. It demanded intelligence that confirmed a decision already taken, and received it from an intelligence community under intense political pressure to produce. (Disarming Iraq, Ch. 6)

III. The ceasefire argument and its legal problems

The legal basis for war: Resolution 1441 required Security Council authorisation before military action — which was never obtainedMr Hitchens argues that the 2003 invasion was simply the enforcement of the 1991 ceasefire conditions, and that those who opposed it were abandoning their obligations. This argument has a legal dimension that he does not fully address. Resolution 1441, passed unanimously in November 2002, gave Iraq a final opportunity to comply with disarmament obligations and provided for "serious consequences" in the event of continued violation — but it explicitly did not authorise military action automatically. The resolution required a return to the Security Council before force could be used. The United States and Britain did not obtain that authorisation. They tried to — and when it became clear that a second resolution would not pass, because the evidence did not warrant it, they invaded anyway. That is not the enforcement of international law. It is its unilateral circumvention. Whatever one thinks of Saddam Hussein's violations of Security Council resolutions, the remedy for violations of international law is not more violations of international law by the more powerful party. (Disarming Iraq, Chs. 11–12)

IV. The humanitarian case collapses without proportionality

The humanitarian argument fails the proportionality test: the costs of the intervention exceeded those of the alternativesMr Hitchens makes a powerful humanitarian case. I do not dismiss it. Saddam Hussein was a brutal dictator who had committed genuine atrocities. Those atrocities were real and documented and required a response. But a humanitarian justification for war requires not only that the existing situation be terrible — which it was — but that the intervention be likely to improve it, at a cost proportionate to the benefit. How many Iraqi civilians died as a consequence of the invasion? Conservative estimates put the figure at over 100,000 in the first three years. The Lancet study, using epidemiological methods, estimated 655,000 excess deaths by 2006. The Iraq Body Count database, using the most conservative media-verified methodology, documents at minimum 180,000 civilian deaths by 2014. The sectarian civil war unleashed by the invasion — Sunni against Shia, Arab against Kurd, militias against everything — was not an accident. It was the predictable consequence of destroying a state, disbanding its army, and providing no plan for what would replace it. A humanitarian intervention that kills more civilians than the regime it removes fails its own test. (Disarming Iraq, epilogue)

V. The terrorism connection was fabricated or exaggerated

The Iraq–al-Qaeda link was manufactured: the CIA and FBI found no credible connectionThe claim that Saddam Hussein's Iraq was a significant sponsor of the terrorism that attacked the United States on September 11 was, as the 9/11 Commission later established, without foundation. The CIA and FBI both reported finding no credible evidence of a collaborative relationship between Iraq and al-Qaeda. The individuals Mr Hitchens cites — Zarqawi, Ansar al-Islam — were operating in the Kurdish zone, the area of Iraq not under Saddam's control, under the protection of the very no-fly zone the United States was maintaining. Zarqawi's presence in Baghdad for medical treatment is documented, but there is no evidence that this constituted a state-sponsored relationship. Abu Nidal and Abu Abbas were ageing terrorists whose operations were largely historical by 2003 and who were not acting under Iraqi direction in the years before the invasion. The administration's sustained public effort to create an association between Saddam and September 11 was not honest intelligence assessment. It was, as Richard Clarke and others who were present have documented, a determined political campaign to link events that the available evidence did not connect. (Disarming Iraq, Ch. 7)

VI. The non-proliferation dividend came despite, not because of, the invasion's methods

Libya's disarmament: Gaddafi had been in negotiation for years before the invasion — the credit is overstatedMr Hitchens credits the invasion with producing Libya's disarmament and the exposure of the AQ Khan network. The connection is more complicated than he allows. Gaddafi had been in quiet diplomatic negotiation with British and American officials about surrendering his WMD programme since at least 2001 — two years before the Iraq invasion. The process that resulted in his December 2003 announcement was a sustained diplomatic effort, and Gaddafi himself stated that his decision was based on the guarantee of security and regime survival that the negotiations provided, not on fear produced by the Iraqi invasion. The lesson of Libya — which was then undermined when NATO overthrew Gaddafi in 2011 despite his compliance — is that diplomacy and security guarantees work, not that invasion inspires voluntary disarmament. Moreover, the AQ Khan network was exposed primarily through intelligence gathering that had been ongoing for years. Attributing it primarily to the Iraq invasion is a post-hoc narrative. (Disarming Iraq, Ch. 13)

VII. The aftermath: what the invasion actually produced

The aftermath: ISIS, sectarian war, regional destabilisation — the consequences Hitchens never adequately reckoned withMr Hitchens, with characteristic honesty, acknowledged after 2003 that the post-invasion management was catastrophically mishandled. But I want to push further than he does. The disbanding of the Iraqi army — the decision to put 400,000 trained, armed, humiliated men on the street with no income and no institution — was not an accident of poor planning. It was a predictable consequence of a war launched without adequate preparation, without a political plan, without sufficient troops to provide security, and without any genuine understanding of what post-Saddam Iraq would look like. The sectarian violence that resulted killed hundreds of thousands of people. The power vacuum created the conditions in which the Islamic State — a movement that had not existed in Iraq before the invasion — could recruit, organise, and eventually seize a territory larger than Britain. The Kurdish gains Mr Hitchens celebrates are real. They came at the cost of a regional catastrophe that has produced, by any honest accounting, more civilian death and suffering than Saddam's regime was producing in 2003 under the containment that the anti-war side was defending.

VIII. Containment was working and could have been strengthened

Containment: Saddam's military was half its 1991 strength; his nuclear programme was non-existent; targeted alternatives existedMr Hitchens dismisses containment as a strategy of passive acceptance of the status quo. The evidence does not support this characterisation. By 2003, Saddam's military capacity had been reduced to approximately half of its 1991 strength. His nuclear programme — the most dangerous long-term threat — had been dismantled by UNSCOM and independently verified by my teams as non-existent. His air force was negligible. His ballistic missile programme was constrained. The no-fly zones were functioning. The question was not whether to do nothing but whether the marginal benefit of regime change was worth the enormous and predictable costs of military occupation of a complex multi-ethnic state. The inspections I was conducting were producing a functioning verification regime that could have been sustained — and that the United States chose to end in order to commence an invasion whose legal basis was disputed, whose intelligence foundation was flawed, and whose aftermath was catastrophically underprepared. The decision was taken by a Bush administration that, as its own officials have since documented, had decided to invade Iraq before September 11 and used the attacks as a catalyst for a decision already made. (Disarming Iraq, intro)

IX. The principle Hitchens defends, generalised, is more dangerous than he acknowledges

The doctrine of pre-emptive regime change: if generalised, it threatens the international order more than the states it targetsI want to close with the principle that Mr Hitchens is actually defending, because it goes well beyond Iraq and he should be asked to justify its general form. He argues that a state forfeits its sovereignty by committing genocide, harboring terrorists, or pursuing WMD — and that any state with the power to remove such a regime is justified in doing so. Stated abstractly, this is not an unreasonable principle. Applied to the realities of international politics, it is extremely dangerous. The United States is not the only state that can invoke this principle. Russia invoked it in Georgia in 2008. China could invoke it regarding Taiwan. Every great power can find in every neighbouring state's internal conduct some grounds for the claim that sovereignty has been forfeited. The value of the international legal order — for all its imperfections, for all its compromises with powerful states — is precisely that it provides a framework for adjudicating these claims that is not determined solely by the military capacity and political will of the most powerful actor. Mr Hitchens's argument, applied consistently, dissolves that framework. The cure for Saddam Hussein is worse than the disease if it licenses every sufficiently powerful state to decide unilaterally which of its neighbours has forfeited its sovereignty. The disasters that followed the 2003 invasion are not anomalies. They are what unilateral regime change without international legitimacy and without a plan for the aftermath reliably produces. (Disarming Iraq, conclusion)

Thank you.

3.The political leadership put "exclamation marks where there should have been question marks" — demanding certainty from intelligence that warranted only probability, in service of a policy decision already effectively made.
∴The intelligence failure was not merely an honest error but a political product; the case for war was built on the suppression of uncertainty that the evidence required, not on a dispassionate assessment of available facts.
Argument III — Resolution 1441 required Security Council authorisation before forceclick for image
1.Resolution 1441 (November 2002) provided for "serious consequences" but explicitly required a return to the Security Council before those consequences could include military action — a requirement the US and UK did not fulfil.
2.When the US and UK sought a second resolution authorising force, they could not obtain it because the evidence presented — including Powell's UNSC presentation — did not convince the required majority. (Disarming Iraq, Chs. 11–12)
3.The remedy for violations of international law is not more violations by the stronger party; unilateral military action in the face of Security Council objection is not enforcement of international law but its circumvention.
∴The legal basis for the invasion was disputed and, under the most defensible reading of Resolution 1441, absent; the invasion was not the enforcement of international law but its violation.
Argument IV — The humanitarian case fails the proportionality testclick for image
1.Conservative estimates place Iraqi civilian deaths attributable to the invasion and subsequent violence at over 100,000 in the first three years; the Iraq Body Count documents at minimum 180,000 by 2014.
2.The sectarian civil war unleashed by the destruction of the Iraqi state and the disbanding of its army — producing the conditions for the Islamic State — was not an unforeseen accident but a predictable consequence of insufficient planning for the aftermath.
3.A humanitarian intervention that kills more civilians than the regime it removes, and that produces regional chaos killing multiples of those the status quo was harming, fails the proportionality test that just war doctrine requires. (Disarming Iraq, epilogue)
∴The humanitarian justification requires not only that existing conditions be bad but that the intervention improve them at proportionate cost; the Iraqi invasion failed this test on the evidence of its own consequences.
Argument V — The Iraq–al-Qaeda link was fabricated or grossly exaggeratedclick for image
1.The 9/11 Commission found no credible evidence of a collaborative relationship between Iraq and al-Qaeda; the CIA and FBI both reported finding no connection between Iraq and the September 11 attacks.
2.Zarqawi and Ansar al-Islam were operating in the Kurdish zone — outside Saddam's control, under US-enforced no-fly protection; their presence did not constitute Iraqi state sponsorship of al-Qaeda. (Disarming Iraq, Ch. 7)
3.The sustained administration campaign to create public association between Iraq and September 11 — documented by Richard Clarke, Paul O'Neill, and others present in the decision-making — was a deliberate political operation, not a considered intelligence assessment.
∴The terrorism connection that partly justified the invasion was not established by credible evidence; the strongest such evidence was actively manufactured for political purposes.
Argument VI — Libya's disarmament was a diplomatic achievement, not an invasion dividendclick for image
1.Gaddafi had been in active diplomatic negotiation about surrendering his WMD programme since at least 2001, two years before the Iraq invasion; the outcome was the product of sustained quiet diplomacy and security guarantees. (Disarming Iraq, Ch. 13)
2.Gaddafi's stated rationale was the security guarantee he had received, not fear generated by the Iraqi invasion; the subsequent NATO overthrow of his regime after his disarmament compliance demonstrated the fragility of this guarantee and its lessons for other would-be disarmers.
3.The AQ Khan network exposure was the product of years of intelligence gathering; attributing it primarily to the Iraq invasion is a post-hoc narrative that overstates the causal connection.
∴The Libya-Khan dividend belongs primarily to diplomacy, not to invasion; and the subsequent overthrow of a compliant Gaddafi taught the lesson that disarmament compliance does not guarantee security.
Argument VII — The aftermath produced more suffering than the status quoclick for image
1.The disbanding of the Iraqi army (400,000 armed, trained, unemployed men) and de-Ba'athification were predictably destabilising decisions that created the conditions for civil war and the Islamic State.
2.ISIS — which did not exist in Iraq before the invasion — recruited directly from the pool of unemployed, humiliated former military personnel and grew to control territory larger than Britain, producing hundreds of thousands of additional deaths and millions of refugees.
3.The Iraqi civilian death toll from the invasion and its aftermath, by any credible estimate, substantially exceeds the death toll Saddam's regime was producing annually under the containment regime in place in 2003.
∴The intervention's consequences, measured by its own humanitarian standard, were worse than the status quo it replaced; the catastrophe was not an unforeseen accident but the foreseeable result of invasion without adequate planning.
Argument VIII — Containment was working and could have been sustainedclick for image
1.By 2003, Saddam's military was at half its 1991 strength, his nuclear programme had been confirmed non-existent by UNMOVIC, his air force was negligible, and the no-fly zones were functioning — containment was actively constraining his capacity. (Disarming Iraq, intro)
2.The inspection process was producing verifiable results; a strengthened long-term inspection regime could have provided the certainty about Iraqi disarmament that the invasion was claimed to require — at a fraction of the human and financial cost.
3.The decision to end the inspections and invade was driven by a timetable set by the Bush administration's prior political commitment to invasion, not by evidence of imminent threat or failure of the inspection process.
∴Containment was not the passive acceptance of evil but an active and successful constraint on Iraqi capacity; its abandonment was premature and driven by political will, not military or intelligence necessity.
Argument IX — The doctrine of unilateral regime change threatens the international orderclick for image
1.Hitchens's principle — that a state forfeits sovereignty by committing genocide, hosting terrorists, or pursuing WMD — is not unreasonable in the abstract, but its unilateral application by the most powerful state dissolves the international legal framework.
2.Russia applied an analogous doctrine in Georgia (2008) and Ukraine (2022); every great power can identify in neighbouring states conduct that, on Hitchens's logic, forfeits their sovereignty. (Disarming Iraq, conclusion)
3.The value of international law is precisely that it adjudicates these claims through collective deliberation rather than through the unilateral will of the most powerful actor; a world where great powers decide unilaterally which states have forfeited sovereignty is not a safer world than one where Saddam Hussein governed Iraq.
∴The doctrine Hitchens defends, consistently applied, is more dangerous to global order than the regime it was used to justify removing; the legacy of Iraq is not liberation but a template for great-power unilateralism with catastrophic consequences.
