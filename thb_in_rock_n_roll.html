Logical Arguments

Mr President,

Let me begin with what is, for me, the most obscene fact in the entire history of popular music. Post-punk did not kill the commodity form of rock and roll — it perfected it. The genius of post-punk was to sell us alienation as the antidote to alienation. Ian Curtis does not weep because he has escaped the system; he weeps within the system, and the system sells his weeping back to us at a premium. This is what Lacan would call the superego imperative at its most vicious: enjoy your suffering. You are commanded not merely to buy the record but to feel authentically miserable while doing so. The debunking argument, if I may borrow from the philosophy our hosts have staged previously, is not that post-punk is insufficiently radical — it is that post-punk is radical in precisely the way the market requires.

On jouissance and the rock and roll body

Jouissance thesis: early rock and roll provided an excess of enjoyment that escaped the pleasure principle; it was a genuine disruption of the symbolic order; post-punk replaced this excess with managed, marketable melancholyWhat did Little Richard, Chuck Berry, and Jerry Lee Lewis give us that Joy Division could not? The answer is jouissance — that excess enjoyment which overflows the symbolic order, which cannot be fully domesticated by the superego, which escapes the neat narrative of commodity exchange. When Little Richard screamed, it was not a screaming about anything. It was a pure discharge of libidinal energy that the social machine had not yet found a container for. This is why the white establishment of the 1950s reacted with such genuine terror to early rock and roll: not because it was subversive in a political sense, but because it presented a body that was not yet fully subjected to the disciplinary regime. Post-punk, by contrast, gave us suffering that was always already narrativised, contextualised, intellectualised. The Joy Division fan does not lose herself in the music; she reflects on her losing of herself, which means she has not lost herself at all. The jouissance has been converted into a managed, sellable commodity of elegant despair.

On the capitalist trap of ironic melancholy

The capitalist trap: post-punk’s ironic self-awareness operates as ideology in the Marxist sense — it knows very well what it is doing, and it does it anyway; this knowing distance is the trap, not the escapeHere is the ideological trick at the heart of post-punk. The post-punk fan is not naive. She knows perfectly well that her leather jacket is a commodity, that the record label profits from her anomie, that the pose of alienation is itself a marketable pose. She knows all this — and she continues. This is what I have called, following Sloterdijk, cynical reason: the ideological operation does not require belief; it requires only participation. The rock and roll fan of 1956, by contrast, was in a sense more radical precisely because she was less knowing. She did not purchase Elvis as a symbol of rebellion; she experienced his pelvis as a direct assault on her nervous system. There was no ironic distance between the music and the body. Post-punk installed that distance and called it sophistication. I call it the most successful ideological operation in the history of popular culture.

On the political necessity of un-ironic excess

Political necessity: a return to visceral rock is not nostalgia but a political demand; genuine disruption requires an excess that cannot be immediately recuperated by the ironic market; raw rock provides this excess; post-punk does notThe opposition will accuse me of nostalgia. I want to be precise: I am not nostalgic for the 1950s. I am not proposing that we return to segregated radio or sock hops. I am making a structural argument. The question is: what form of popular music is capable of providing a genuine disruption to the current ideological order? And my answer is: not post-punk, which has already been fully incorporated as the sound of the knowing, hip consumer; but raw, visceral rock, which retains the capacity to produce jouissance that is not immediately converted into a pose. The Stooges in 1969, the New York Dolls, the MC5 — these bands produced music that the market could not quite assimilate, because it did not know what to do with itself, because it had not yet acquired the self-awareness that post-punk turned into a product. We need music that does not know what to do with itself. We need music that embarrasses itself. We need rock and roll.

On post-punk as the sound of capitalist realism

Capitalist realism in sound: post-punk is not the critique of capitalist realism but its sonic embodiment; its grey, affectless textures sonically enact the impossibility of imagining alternatives; early rock imagined otherwiseMark Fisher — and here I speak with genuine respect for the opposition — gave us the concept of capitalist realism: the sense that it is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism. I want to argue that post-punk is not the critique of this condition but its most perfect sonic expression. Listen to the grey textures of early Factory Records, the affectless vocals, the mechanical rhythms. This is not music that imagines alternatives to the existing order. This is music that has accepted the impossibility of alternatives and made an aesthetic programme out of that acceptance. Early rock and roll, by contrast, was music that had not yet received the memo that alternatives were impossible. It was music made by people who believed, naively and incorrectly but with enormous productive energy, that pleasure was a political act. That naive belief is what I want to recover — not the specific cultural forms of the 1950s, but the structural position of un-knowing, un-ironic excess.

On the commodity form and musical authenticity

Commodity and authenticity: authenticity in music is not a pre-commercial purity but a specific relationship to the body; rock and roll’s authenticity was bodily and immediate; post-punk’s authenticity was cerebral and mediatedI anticipate the objection that rock and roll was also a commodity, also produced and distributed within the capitalist system, also recuperated almost immediately by the entertainment industry. All of this is true. But there is a crucial difference between a music that is commodified despite itself — that retains, even within the commodity form, a residue of excess that the form cannot fully contain — and a music that makes its own commodification into its aesthetic content. Rock and roll was the former. Post-punk was the latter. The Clash knew they were selling rebellion; the Sex Pistols knew they were manufactured by Malcolm McLaren. This knowledge did not liberate them; it simply made their commodification more complete, because it left no remainder, no excess, no jouissance that the market had not already priced in. A commodity that knows it is a commodity has perfected the commodity form. Rock and roll, at its best, was always slightly more than the market bargained for.

On the return as structural demand

The return: returning to rock is not a cultural regression but a structural move to recover the possibility of un-incorporated excess; the form matters less than the structural position of the music relative to the marketSo what precisely am I calling for when I call for a return to good old rock and roll? Not a cultural museum. Not a tribute band economy. I am calling for music that occupies the structural position that early rock and roll occupied: music that produces genuine bodily excess, that has not yet converted its own energy into an object of aesthetic contemplation, that remains embarrassingly, productively alive. This may take forms we cannot anticipate. The demand is not for Chuck Berry specifically but for the function that Chuck Berry performed in the cultural system: the function of the un-assimilated remainder. Post-punk, by making assimilation into its very content, eliminated that function. The motion before this House is therefore not a conservative motion but a genuinely radical one: bury the knowing, ironic, self-commodifying melancholy and recover the possibility of music that does not know what it is doing to the listener, because that not-knowing is the condition of genuine surprise, genuine disruption, genuine jouissance.

On what the proposition demands

The proposition’s demand: the motion is not for nostalgia but for the recovery of a structural position — the position of un-ironic excess — that post-punk vacated and that the current market cannot supplyLet me be perfectly clear about what the proposition demands, so that the opposition cannot misrepresent us as cultural conservatives pining for the Eisenhower era. We demand the recovery of a structural possibility that post-punk closed off: the possibility of popular music that overwhelms the listener before she can adopt a knowing attitude toward it, that operates on the body before the mind can intervene, that creates the conditions for genuine collective enjoyment rather than the privatised, headphone-mediated consumption of carefully curated sadness. Post-punk gave us the headphones. Rock and roll gave us the dance floor. And — I insist on this — the dance floor is the more radical political space, because it is the space where the body of the other is inescapable, where private irony dissolves in collective rhythm, where the symbolic order briefly loses its grip. We want the dance floor back. We want the jouissance back. Bury post-punk.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to talk about what actually happened in the clubs and the bedrooms and the rehearsal spaces. Not the theory. The music. Because I have been there — metaphorically if not literally in every room — and I can tell you that something died when post-punk arrived and it was not something trivial. It was the thing that made rock and roll matter in the first place: the absolute refusal to be cool about it. The willingness to make a complete fool of yourself in the service of a three-chord howl that meant something entirely inexpressible and was therefore entirely necessary.

On the death of passion in popular music

The death of passion: post-punk replaced the visceral emotional commitment of rock and roll with a studied emotional distance that it called sophistication; this distance is intellectual cowardice masquerading as artistic maturityHere is what I cannot forgive post-punk. It made detachment a virtue. It took the one thing rock and roll had absolutely right — the total, embarrassing, unguarded commitment to feeling — and replaced it with a pose of cool, clinical remove that it called artistic seriousness. The Stooges were not serious in this sense. Iggy Pop did not stand behind a synthesizer managing his emotional temperature. He cut himself with broken glass and fell into the audience and made sounds that should not have been permitted in a civilised society. And this was not stupid: this was the only honest response to the world he was living in. The post-punk musician managed her feeling. She observed her alienation from a careful distance and reproduced it in technically precise, emotionally inert sound. She called this maturity. I call it the most expensive cowardice in music history.

On the elitism of experimental noise

Elitism: experimental noise required cultural capital to appreciate and was therefore structurally exclusionary; rock and roll required only a body and a willingness to move; the democracy of rock is not a cliché but a structural factThe opposition will tell us that post-punk was democratic, that it opened up music to people who had been excluded from the guitar-hero tradition. I have heard this argument and I reject it absolutely. Democratic for whom? For the art-school graduate who had read the right theory and could appreciate the correct references? For the listener who understood that the forty-five minutes of feedback was a Lacanian gesture toward the unspeakable? Rock and roll was democratic in a way that required no cultural capital whatsoever. You did not need to know anything to understand that “Johnny B. Goode” was about you, that it was about your body, that it was demanding something from you that was not a considered aesthetic response but a physical one. The democratisation of music through post-punk is the democratisation of the university seminar: you are welcome to attend provided you have done the reading. The democratisation of rock and roll was the democratisation of the body: you are welcome provided you have one.

On synthesizers and the displacement of the human

Synthesizers and the human: post-punk’s embrace of cold, clinical synthesizers was not a neutral technological choice but a deliberate displacement of the human body from the music; the guitar, by contrast, is an extension of the body and cannot be fully separated from itI want to make what may sound like a reactionary argument but is not. The synthesizer displaced the guitar not because it was a better instrument but because it was a more controllable one. You can manage a synthesizer. You can programme it. You can ensure that it does not embarrass you by exceeding what you intended. A guitar in the hands of a player who cares is an unruly object: it feeds back, it sustains, it produces harmonics that were not requested, it rewards the player who allows herself to be surprised by what the instrument produces. The synthesizer rewards control. And post-punk was above all a music of control — the management of emotional response, the technical administration of alienation, the clinical production of calculated affects. The human body was not eliminated from post-punk; it was managed. And managed humanity is a diminished humanity, in music as in everything else.

On alienation as a lifestyle brand

Alienation as brand: post-punk converted the sociological condition of alienation into an aesthetic lifestyle brand that could be purchased, displayed, and consumed; this conversion was the final triumph of the commodity form over musical rebellionPost-punk's deepest crime was making alienation chic. Before post-punk, alienation was a condition to be overcome, a problem to be solved, a wound that rock and roll temporarily healed by providing the wounded person with a community of equally wounded people who made a tremendous noise together. Post-punk made alienation into a permanent aesthetic programme. It was no longer something you suffered; it was something you curated. You bought the correct records, wore the correct clothes, adopted the correct facial expression, and thereby signalled your membership in the community of those who understood that community was impossible. This is a spectacular achievement in the history of commodification: the product being sold is the very condition that the product is supposed to relieve. The music that was meant to address alienation became the most effective vehicle for its perpetuation, because it convinced its consumers that the correct response to alienation was not to overcome it but to aestheticise it.

On the Stooges as the model

The Stooges as model: the Stooges represent the structural opposite of post-punk’s managed alienation; their music was too committed, too physical, too honest to be immediately recuperated; this is what rock and roll looks like at its most politically effectiveI want to propose the Stooges’ Fun House as the model for what we are asking for. Not because it sounds like 1956. Not because it is nostalgic for anything. But because it is a record that does not know how to be cool about itself. Every track is an emergency. Iggy’s vocals are not managed; they are expelled. The saxophone lines are not decorative; they are panicked. The whole record sounds like it is happening for the last time, like the musicians are aware that something is about to collapse and are trying to outrun the collapse by making more noise than the thing collapsing. This is what rock and roll does. It converts genuine human emergency into music that communicates that emergency directly to the listener’s body. Post-punk converted genuine human emergency into a pose of having processed the emergency artistically. The processing is the lie. The emergency was real.

On noise music and the alienation of the audience

Noise music: the noise music tradition extended post-punk’s elitism to its logical conclusion, producing music that was structurally designed to repel the uninitiated; this is not democracy but the most refined form of cultural gate-keepingAnd then came the noise musicians, who took post-punk’s elitism to its terminal conclusion. At least Joy Division had songs. At least the Fall had a hook buried somewhere in the angular racket. But the noise music tradition — the academic feedback sculptures, the institutional drone performances, the concerts at which the correct response was to look thoughtfully at the ceiling rather than to move any part of your body — this was music that had abandoned the pretence of communicating with the uninitiated. It was music for people who had earned the right to appreciate it by having correctly suffered through sufficient quantities of preliminary difficulty. I am told this is democratic. I am told that rejecting conventional musical pleasure is a political act. I say: it is the most efficient possible method for ensuring that music remains the property of those who already have the cultural capital to decode it, and that everyone else goes home feeling inadequate. Rock and roll never made anyone feel inadequate. It made them feel possible.

On what we are really fighting for

The real argument: burying post-punk is not a conservative act but a demand for music that communicates without preconditions, that converts emergency into energy rather than emergency into pose, that makes the listener feel possible rather than educatedSo here is what this debate is really about. It is not about whether Joy Division was better than Chuck Berry. It is about what we want music to do. Do we want music that provides us with an opportunity to demonstrate our sophisticated relationship to our own suffering? Or do we want music that takes the suffering and converts it into the kind of physical energy that makes collective life feel possible? Post-punk chose the former and called it art. Rock and roll chose the latter and called it fun. I side with fun, not because I am a philistine, but because I believe that the conversion of suffering into communal physical energy is a more radical political act than the conversion of suffering into an aesthetically managed object of contemplation. The opposition will give you theory. I am giving you the Stooges. The Stooges win.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I am going to say something that will scandalise the opposition and perhaps embarrass my colleagues on this side, though I think they will recognise the truth of it once I have stated it. Rock and roll is a pagan force. It is older than Christianity, older than Western civilisation as we know it, older perhaps than language. It is the return of Dionysus, who was suppressed but never destroyed by the Apollonian order that gave us cathedrals, symphony orchestras, and eventually the Factory Records aesthetic. When Elvis moved his hips, he was not performing; he was channelling something that the culture had worked very hard to suppress. And when post-punk arrived and insisted on suppressing it again — on managing it, aestheticising it, intellectualising it — it was Apollonian reaction dressed as avant-garde progress. I intend to prosecute that reaction tonight.

On the Dionysian in rock and roll

Dionysian thesis: rock and roll was a Dionysian irruption into Apollonian Western culture; its power derived from its connection to pre-rational, bodily, earth-rooted energies that Western civilisation has always sought to suppressIn Sexual Personae I argued that the great periods of Western art have always involved a dialectic between Apollonian form-giving and Dionysian dissolution. The Greeks knew this: tragedy exists precisely at the intersection of order and chaos, of the beautiful measured god and the vine-intoxicated, animal-masked reveller. Rock and roll was the twentieth century’s most powerful Dionysian eruption. It emerged from African-American musical traditions that had never fully accepted the Apollonian suppression of the body that European concert music enforced. It was rhythm before melody, body before mind, release before containment. The guitar was its instrument not because it was technically sophisticated but because in the right hands it could approximate the human voice at its most unguarded — crying, howling, moaning, laughing. The opposition will give us theory about democratic spaces. I am giving you nature. Nature does not need a theory of democratic spaces.

On post-punk as Apollonian reaction

Post-punk as Apollonian: post-punk was not a progression beyond rock but a regression to the Apollonian suppression of Dionysian energy; it made of music a formal, conceptual exercise and stripped it of its connection to the body and to pagan antiquityWhat did post-punk actually do? It took the Dionysian energy of rock and roll and submitted it to Apollonian discipline. It made the music angular, measured, conceptual. It replaced the rounded, blues-derived curves of rock guitar with the hard-edged, rectilinear rhythms of the drum machine. It replaced the howl of Dionysus with the considered, ironic statement of the conceptual artist. And it called this progress. But progress away from what toward what? Away from the body toward the mind. Away from nature toward culture. Away from Dionysus toward Apollo — and toward the worst kind of Apollo, the bloodless academic Apollo who writes grant applications about the political semiotics of dissonance. Rock and roll was a fire. Post-punk was a seminar about fire. Mark Fisher is a brilliant seminar leader. But I want the fire.

On the commercial disaster of post-punk’s legacy

Commercial disaster: the intellectual suppression of rock’s primal energy produced a music scene in which genuine mass connection became impossible; the fragmentation of post-punk into increasingly specialised microgenres is the commercial expression of its Apollonian sterilityLet me address the question of commercial viability, not because commerce is the measure of artistic value, but because commercial collapse is the measure of cultural disconnection. The post-punk aesthetic, extended over four decades, produced a music scene of increasing fragmentation, specialisation, and mutual incomprehension. The audience splintered into tiny communities of the initiated, each with its own correct references, its own theory of why the other communities were doing it wrong. Meanwhile, the mass audience that rock and roll had created and held — the audience that knew, across class and race and geography, that “Johnny B. Goode” was about them — disappeared into the streaming economy where every individual curates her personalised melancholy. This is not an accident. It is the cultural logic of Apollonian specialisation taken to its conclusion: infinite refinement, infinite subdivision, zero collective energy. The fire went out when we stopped feeding it.

On the sexual politics of rock and roll

Sexual politics: rock and roll was one of the twentieth century’s most powerful vehicles for sexual liberation, connecting listeners to their bodies and desires; post-punk’s cerebral aesthetics suppressed this liberatory dimension in favour of ironic distance from the bodyThe opposition will tell me that post-punk was sexually liberatory — that it created space for queer and female artists that the guitar-hero tradition excluded. I accept that the guitar-hero tradition was often suffocatingly masculinist. But I reject the conclusion. The answer to the masculinism of the guitar hero is not the desexualisation of music; it is the feminisation of the guitar-hero. Patti Smith understood this. She took the Dionysian energy of rock — its rawness, its physicality, its refusal to be tasteful — and deployed it without surrendering to the Apollonian chill of post-punk. She howled. She was not ironic about the howl. The liberation of music from masculinist rock heroics does not require the suppression of Dionysian energy; it requires its democratisation. Post-punk suppressed the energy; the right answer is to unleash it for everyone.

On the sterility of the avant-garde

Avant-garde sterility: the avant-garde tradition in music, which post-punk drew on, substitutes formal innovation for genuine vitality; its claim to radicalism is based on difficulty rather than power; difficulty without power is aesthetically sterileFisher will tell us that post-punk was popular modernism — that it brought the radicalism of the avant-garde to a mass audience. I want to argue that the avant-garde was the problem, not the solution. The avant-garde tradition — from Schoenberg to Stockhausen to Throbbing Gristle — made a religion of difficulty. It confused the scandalising of middle-class aesthetic expectations with genuine artistic power. But scandalising the bourgeoisie is a parlour game for the bourgeoisie. Real artistic power does not scandalise; it overwhelms. Beethoven did not scandalise his audience; he assaulted them. The Rolling Stones did not scandalise their audience; they possessed them. The avant-garde, and post-punk in its train, confused the production of difficulty for the production of power. These are not the same thing. Difficulty can be managed, discussed, intellectualised. Power cannot.

On rebuttting Fisher’s hauntology

Against hauntology: Fisher’s claim that nostalgia for rock is hauntological romanticisation misses the structural argument; the proposition is not haunted by the past but demanding the return of a structural possibility the present has foreclosedFisher will accuse us of hauntology: of being haunted by a past that never existed, of romanticising rock and roll into an imaginary golden age that the actual historical record does not support. This is a powerful charge and I want to meet it directly. The proposition is not making a historical claim. We are not saying that 1956 was a golden age or that Chuck Berry was politically uncomplicated. We are making a structural claim: that music needs the Dionysian function, that this function was performed by early rock in ways post-punk deliberately suppressed, and that the suppression has consequences. The hauntology charge misunderstands our argument as nostalgia when it is in fact diagnosis. We are not haunted by the past. We are alarmed by the present. The present lacks fire. We want the fire back. That is not hauntology; that is horticulture.

On the synthesis: rock as political necessity

Synthesis: the proposition’s three speeches converge on a single claim — music needs Dionysian energy, un-ironic excess, and the capacity to overwhelm the listener; post-punk suppressed this; the return to rock recovers it; this is political necessity, not nostalgiaLet me synthesise what my colleagues have argued tonight. Žižek has shown that post-punk converted jouissance into a marketable commodity, closing off the possibility of genuine excess. Bangs has shown that it displaced the body with the synthesizer and alienation with its retail version. And I am arguing that beneath both these analyses lies a single structural fact: post-punk was Apollonian reaction against the Dionysian eruption that rock and roll had made possible. It substituted form for energy, concept for force, irony for power. And the result — the music scene we have inherited — is a scene of enormous formal sophistication and almost no collective vitality. We have every kind of music except music that makes you feel, collectively and without embarrassment, that being alive is an emergency worth responding to. That is what rock and roll gave us. We want it back. Bury post-punk.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to begin by congratulating the proposition on an argument that is, in its way, a perfect specimen of what I have called capitalist hauntology. To be haunted, in the sense I use the term, is not simply to be nostalgic. It is to be held in place by a past that was never fully present — by an image of what music was supposed to have been that the actual historical record never quite delivered. The proposition is haunted by rock and roll. The rock and roll they are defending never existed in the way they require it to have existed: as a pure, un-commodified, Dionysian excess that the market had not already incorporated before the first record was pressed. What did exist — and this is the historical claim I want to press — was a popular music that by 1976 had become the most formulaic, corporate, ideologically predictable genre in the history of recorded sound. And it was precisely against this calcification that post-punk rose.

On rock as commodity by 1976

Rock as commodity: by the mid-1970s rock and roll had become a predictable corporate formula; stadium rock, soft rock, and AOR represented the complete assimilation of the rebellious form into the entertainment industry; post-punk was a response to this calcification, not its causeLet me be precise about the history. By 1975 and 1976, what did “good old rock and roll” look like? It looked like Led Zeppelin playing to stadiums of sixty thousand people. It looked like Fleetwood Mac’s Rumours, an album of impeccable production values and zero political content. It looked like radio formats that had reduced the entire vocabulary of rock to a handful of marketable gestures. The Dionysian irruption that Paglia invokes had been, by this point, completely and successfully contained: it had been professionalized, stadium-ised, formatted, and sold back to the audience as an experience of controlled risk. The guitar solo was still there, but it now signalled not raw emotion but technical mastery; it was not an emergency but a spectacle. Post-punk did not kill this rock. This rock had killed itself. Post-punk was the attempt — urgent, political, necessary — to think beyond what rock had become.

On popular modernism and its importance

Popular modernism: post-punk brought the radicalism of modernist art to a mass, working-class audience; this was a unique cultural moment that has not been repeated; its loss, not its existence, is the cultural disasterWhat post-punk actually achieved, at its best, was what I have called popular modernism: the conjunction of genuine aesthetic radicalism with mass accessibility that characterised the culture of the 1970s British working class. The Gang of Four, Wire, the Raincoats, Joy Division — these were bands playing to teenagers who worked in factories and left school at sixteen, and they were engaging those teenagers with formal experiments as radical as anything in the art gallery. This conjunction — avant-garde seriousness and genuine popular reach — is extraordinarily rare in cultural history. It is not rock and roll; it is not the academy; it is something that existed briefly and brilliantly in the late 1970s and has not been recovered since. The proposition wants to bury it. I want to mourn its loss and understand what conditions made it possible, because those conditions are what we need to recover — not the three-chord formula that post-punk was responding to.

On hauntology and the proposition’s nostalgia

Hauntology: the proposition is haunted by an image of rock and roll that never fully existed; their rock was always already commodified, always already incorporated; the clean origin they invoke is a retroactive fantasyHauntology, as I use the term — borrowing from Derrida but developing it in a cultural-critical direction — describes the condition of being haunted by a past that never was. The proposition invokes an early rock and roll that was pure, un-commodified, genuinely excessive, not yet recuperated by the market. But this is a fantasy. Chuck Berry was signed to Chess Records in 1955; the company was founded by Leonard and Phil Chess specifically to commercialise African-American music for white consumption. Elvis was signed to RCA Victor in 1956 for the express purpose of making his sound safe for mainstream audiences. The Dionysian excess that the proposition romanticises was, from the moment of its first recording, already mediated, packaged, and sold by an industry that knew exactly what it was doing. The “good old rock and roll” that Žižek and Paglia are defending was never “good old rock and roll” in the sense they require. It was always already the commodity form in which they claim post-punk trapped it.

On post-punk exposing capitalist realism

Exposing capitalist realism: post-punk did not embody capitalist realism but exposed it; by making the contradictions of working-class life audible in formal terms, it produced a music of genuine political intelligence that rock’s affirmative energy could notŽižek has argued that post-punk was the sonic embodiment of capitalist realism — the acceptance of the impossibility of alternatives. I want to reverse this. The grey textures of post-punk were not an acceptance of impossibility; they were an honest representation of the conditions under which working-class musicians in 1978 Salford or Leeds or Manchester were actually living. Representing those conditions honestly — without the false affirmation of a blues-derived rhetoric of transcendence that had no purchase in a deindustrialising economy — was a political act. The choice was not between genuine Dionysian excess and managed capitalist melancholy. The choice was between honest representation of specific historical conditions and the false universalism of a rock idiom that had been developed in a different economic and social context. Post-punk chose honesty. That is not capitalist realism; that is what it looks like when working-class musicians refuse to pretend that conditions are better than they are.

On what the proposition gets wrong about jouissance

Against jouissance nostalgia: the proposition’s Lacanian framework misidentifies the political valence of jouissance; unmanaged excess is as easily captured by the market as managed melancholy; the raw rock the proposition defends was incorporated faster than post-punkI want to challenge Žižek on his own theoretical ground. He argues that jouissance — unmanaged, un-ironic bodily excess — is politically disruptive because it cannot be incorporated by the market. But the history of rock strongly suggests the opposite. The raw, physical, un-ironic rock that the proposition defends was incorporated by the market with extraordinary speed: Little Richard’s wildness became Pat Boone’s cover versions within months; Elvis’s pelvis became a television performance restriction within a year and a Las Vegas residency within a decade. The market is very good at incorporating un-ironic excess: it simply contains it, packages it, and sells it to an audience that wants a controlled experience of apparent wildness. The “jouissance” that remains in early rock is not dangerous to the market; it is the market’s most saleable commodity. Post-punk’s ironic distance — its refusal to deliver the expected emotional hit — was in many ways a more effective defensive strategy against incorporation than raw excess, which the market had already learned to package.

On Joy Division and genuine suffering

Joy Division’s honesty: Ian Curtis’s suffering was not commodified managed melancholy but genuine, devastating experience expressed with formal rigour; to call it “pleasure in despair” is to misread the music and disrespect the manI want to defend Joy Division against Žižek’s characterisation of their music as “pleasure in despair.” Ian Curtis was genuinely suffering. He had epilepsy, a failing marriage, depression. The formal rigour of his music — its bleakness, its mechanical textures, its resistance to the comforting resolution that rock rhetoric typically provides — was not the adoption of a marketable pose. It was the honest formal expression of a specific, devastating experience that the vocabulary of rock could not contain. To say that Curtis’s suffering was commodified is to say something true about what happened to the recordings after his death; it is not to say anything true about the music itself. The music was an act of formal honesty. The fact that honesty can be sold is the condition of all cultural production; it does not retroactively convert the honesty into a pose.

On what the opposition demands

The opposition’s demand: recover popular modernism, not rock nostalgia; the conditions for the conjunction of avant-garde seriousness and genuine mass accessibility are what we need, and those are forward-looking conditions, not backward-looking onesWhat we are arguing for tonight is not the defence of post-punk as a finished historical achievement. We are arguing that the conditions that made post-punk possible — a working-class audience with access to art-school ideas, a music press that treated popular music as politically serious, a cultural infrastructure that allowed formal experiment to reach a mass audience — are exactly the conditions that need to be recovered and extended. The proposition wants to return to rock. We want to go forward to the next popular modernism. That next popular modernism will not sound like Joy Division; it will not sound like the Stooges. It will sound like whatever form of musical intelligence is adequate to the specific historical conditions we are currently living in — conditions that require honesty about complexity, refusal of false consolation, and the kind of formal seriousness that takes the listener seriously enough to challenge her. Do not bury post-punk. Learn from it.

Thank you.

I want to bring some material evidence into this debate, because the proposition has been making structural and theoretical arguments without, in my view, adequately engaging with what actually happened to real people in real rooms when post-punk appeared. I want to talk about what post-punk did for people who had previously had no access to music-making at all — women who were told that guitars were not for them, queer people for whom rock’s masculinist stage presence was a daily reminder of their exclusion, working-class musicians without the technical proficiency that the guitar-hero tradition demanded. These people made post-punk. And what they made was not a commodified pose of alienation; it was a transformation of who was permitted to make music and what music was permitted to say.

On the art school and its democratic function

Art school democracy: the British art school system of the 1970s provided working-class musicians with access to ideas and institutional support that the mainstream music industry denied them; post-punk was the sound of this access being exercisedThe art school — specifically, the British art school of the late 1970s, with its generous grants and its culture of experiment — was one of the few institutions in that society that permitted working-class young people to spend time thinking about ideas rather than producing labour. Siouxsie Sioux, Mark E. Smith, Howard Devoto, Gang of Four — these musicians passed through or near the art school world, and what they acquired was not the cultural capital that Bangs derides but something more specific: the permission to think that what they were doing mattered, that formal choices had political consequences, that music could be made as rigorously and intentionally as any other form of cultural production. The guitar-hero tradition did not provide this permission. It provided the permission to be technically impressive. Post-punk provided the permission to be intellectually serious. For the people who needed it most — people for whom the mainstream music industry was categorically closed — intellectual seriousness was democratic, not elitist.

On women and post-punk’s structural openings

Women in post-punk: the technical and physical demands of the guitar-hero tradition structurally excluded women; post-punk’s rejection of virtuosity as a criterion opened music-making to those the previous tradition had excludedThe proposition’s vision of good old rock and roll has, if we are honest about it, a very specific gender. The guitar hero is male. The technical virtuosity, the physical aggression, the stage domination that the tradition valorises are coded masculine in ways that were not accidental: they were structural exclusions that kept women in the audience. Post-punk’s rejection of virtuosity as a criterion — its insistence that a three-note bass line or an off-kilter drum pattern or a voice that refused to perform conventional femininity were as valid as any technically proficient guitar solo — opened music-making to people the previous tradition had systematically excluded. The Raincoats, the Slits, Siouxsie and the Banshees, Delta 5 — these were not bands of technically proficient musicians performing gender-neutral rock. They were bands that could only have existed in the specific conditions that post-punk created: the conditions in which technical proficiency was no longer the price of admission. That is not elitism. That is its structural opposite.

On queer space and post-punk collectivism

Queer space: post-punk’s rejection of the masculinist rock body created spaces in which queer performance and queer audiences could exist; the guitar-hero tradition had no such spaces; this is a concrete democratic achievement, not a theoretical oneBangs has invoked the democracy of the body. I want to take that seriously. Whose body? The rock and roll body that Paglia celebrates — the Dionysian, sexually uninhibited body of Elvis or Iggy Pop — is a specific body: it is a heterosexual male body performing for a heterosexual female (or subordinately male) audience. The queer body had no place in that performance. Post-punk’s disruption of the conventional rock-body — its androgyny, its refusal of the swaggering masculine stage presence, its embrace of performers whose bodies were awkward, angular, or deliberately de-aestheticised — created the conditions in which queer performance became possible in popular music. Siouxsie, Morrissey, Marc Almond, Pete Burns — none of these would have been possible within the performance codes of “good old rock and roll.” The democracy that post-punk produced was a democracy of bodies: not the single, universalised rock body but the multiplicity of bodies that had previously been excluded from the stage.

On structural collectivism vs the guitar hero

Collectivism: post-punk replaced the individualist guitar-hero model with collective, egalitarian band structures; this structural collectivism was a political practice, not merely an aesthetic choiceThe guitar-hero model is structurally individualist. There is one hero; everyone else is support. The audience comes to see the hero; the band provides context. This is not a political accident but a political structure: it valorises individual virtuosity over collective practice, technical mastery over collaborative invention, the extraordinary individual over the competent ensemble. Post-punk replaced this structure with a different one. The Fall, the Gang of Four, Wire, the Raincoats — these were bands in which no single member was the virtuoso hero, in which the collective sound was the point rather than the vehicle for individual expression. This structural collectivism was not merely an aesthetic choice; it was a political practice, a rehearsal in the space of music-making of a different way of organising collective endeavour. The proposition wants to return to the hero. We are defending the ensemble. And in a political moment defined by the reassertion of individualist ideology, the ensemble matters more than the hero.

On the noise tradition as political expansion

Noise as expansion: the noise tradition expanded what music could communicate by demonstrating that conventional musical pleasure was not the only register in which music could operate; this expansion was politically significant, not culturally elitistBangs has attacked the noise tradition as elitist. I want to argue the opposite. The noise tradition demonstrated — by actually demonstrating it, not merely arguing for it — that the conventions of musical pleasure were conventions, not necessities. A Throbbing Gristle performance did not ask the audience to enjoy it in the conventional sense; it asked the audience to reconsider what enjoyment was and what it was for. This is not elitist: it is the most fundamental democratic act in aesthetics, the act of questioning the standards by which aesthetic judgment operates. The guitar-hero tradition, by contrast, operated entirely within the conventional standards of musical pleasure without ever questioning them. It gave audiences what they expected. Noise gave audiences what they had not expected and could not categorise — and in doing so, it expanded the category of what music could be. The expansion of a category is never elitist; it is the structural condition of future democratic access to that category.

I want to identify the deepest fantasy at the heart of the proposition’s case, because until we name it clearly, the debate cannot proceed honestly. When the proposition calls for a return to “good old rock and roll,” they are calling for a return to a specific sonic and cultural vocabulary: one that is predominantly white, predominantly male, predominantly North Atlantic, predominantly rooted in a specific historical moment of American popular music that has been treated — by a culture industry centred in the same geography — as the universal origin of popular music as such. The Dionysian irruption that Paglia celebrates is a specifically European Dionysus, derived from a Classical tradition that required very significant violence to install as universal. I want to argue that sonic experimentation, dissonance, and noise — the traditions the proposition wants to bury — are the true engines of musical futurism, and that this futurism is not an academic luxury but a political necessity.

On rock and roll’s Eurocentric appropriation

Eurocentric appropriation: “good old rock and roll” is a Eurocentric narrative that appropriated African-American musical innovation and repackaged it as a universal popular form; returning to it naturalises this appropriation and erases its actual originsThe proposition invokes early rock and roll as a source of un-commodified, Dionysian excess. But whose Dionysus? Little Richard, Chuck Berry, Fats Domino — these musicians did not experience rock and roll as a liberatory Dionysian irruption. They experienced it as their innovation being appropriated by a culture industry that paid them less, promoted them less, and replaced them with white performers who delivered the same product in a form safer for mainstream consumption. The narrative that frames rock and roll as the universal origin of popular music — as the source to which we must return — is not a neutral historical claim. It is a narrative that has naturalised the erasure of African-American musical labour, that has made the appropriation look like invention, that has centred one musical tradition among many as the default origin point of authentic popular music. When the proposition calls for a return to good old rock and roll, they are calling for a return to the narrative that made this erasure possible.

On Afrofuturism and the sonic future

Afrofuturism: the traditions of sonic experimentation that the proposition wants to bury have their deepest roots in African-American futurist music-making; Sun Ra, George Clinton, Afrika Bambaataa; this tradition is the actual future of popular musicThe tradition of sonic experimentation that I am defending does not begin with European post-punk. It begins with Sun Ra, who in the 1950s and 1960s was making music that imagined futures so radically different from the present that they required new instruments, new performance strategies, new theories of what sound was for. It continues through George Clinton and Parliament-Funkadelic, through Afrika Bambaataa’s electro, through Detroit techno, through the entire lineage of African-American music that has used sonic experimentation to imagine futures that the present denied. This is Afrofuturism: the use of radical sonic experimentation not as an academic exercise but as a survival strategy, a way of imagining worlds in which the conditions of the present — the conditions of racial capitalism — no longer obtain. The proposition wants to return to rock. I am proposing that we follow the futures that African-American musicians have been imagining since at least the 1950s, and that these futures require precisely the sonic experimentation that the proposition wants to bury.

On the corporate capture of rock

Corporate capture: traditional rock forms are far more easily absorbed by corporate capitalism than abrasive noise; the music industry has perfected the containment of raw rock energy; noise and experimentation remain harder to packageŽižek has argued that post-punk is more thoroughly commodified than rock. I want to challenge this directly with the material evidence of the music industry’s behaviour. What does the corporate music industry do most reliably? It packages raw energy. It takes the dangerous and makes it safe, the wild and makes it predictable, the excessive and gives it a format. The history of rock and roll is the history of this operation: from Elvis’s television restrictions to the stadium rock formula to the heritage rock industry that now packages the Rolling Stones as a heritage experience. The industry has had seventy years to learn how to contain raw rock energy, and it has learned. What the industry finds genuinely difficult to package is abrasive noise. Not because the industry hasn’t tried — it has — but because abrasive noise resists the operations of containment in ways that raw energy does not. You cannot make Whitehouse into a background soundtrack. You cannot put Merzbow in a phone advertisement. You can put Chuck Berry in a car commercial without altering a single note.

On dissonance as political resistance

Dissonance as resistance: dissonance and noise are politically significant precisely because they refuse the listener the comfort of conventional musical pleasure; this refusal is a demand that cannot be assimilated into the commodity form as easily as the delivery of pleasureThe proposition has argued that music should provide collective enjoyment — should deliver the listener to a state of shared bodily excess. But I want to propose the opposite: that music which refuses to deliver conventional enjoyment, that makes demands on the listener rather than gratifying her, is performing a more urgent political function in the current moment. We live in a culture of total gratification — a culture of streaming platforms that deliver precisely the sonic experience the algorithm determines you want, at the moment you want it, without friction. In this culture, music that provides friction — that is difficult to categorise, that does not deliver what was expected, that refuses to complete the transaction of pleasure — is performing an act of political resistance by its mere existence. Dissonance is not difficulty for its own sake; it is the insistence that the listener has not yet been given everything, that something remains outside the system of gratification. The demand for rock is the demand for a gratification the system can already supply. The insistence on noise is the insistence on what the system cannot.

On rebuttting Žižek: post-punk’s irony is not the trap

Against Žižek: the ironic distance of post-punk is not the capitalist trap but the refusal of the false immediacy that the capitalist entertainment industry requires; the commodity wants your unreflective participation; post-punk demands reflectionŽižek has argued that post-punk’s knowing irony is the most perfect form of capitalist ideology, because it permits knowing participation without requiring naive belief. I want to reverse this. The capitalist entertainment industry does not want ironic distance; it wants unreflective participation. It wants you to purchase the emotional experience, not to reflect on it. It wants the music to work on you before you have time to think about what it is doing. This is exactly what rock and roll does — and exactly why the industry has always been so much better at packaging it than at packaging post-punk’s ironic difficulty. Ironic distance is not a capitalist trap: it is a refusal of the false immediacy that capitalism requires. The knowing consumer who buys the Joy Division record and reflects on her own melancholy is more resistant to the commodity form than the consumer who simply surrenders to the Dionysian energy without reflection, because reflection is the condition of resistance and surrender is the condition of capture.

On the future and what it requires

The future: the musical future requires the traditions of sonic experimentation, not a return to a commodified origin; the next forms of musical intelligence adequate to current conditions will emerge from experimental traditions, not from rock nostalgiaWhat does the musical future actually require? Not a return to forms that the industry has had seventy years to contain. Not the re-installation of the guitar hero as the default model of popular music-making. Not the re-erection of the exclusions that post-punk dismantled, as my colleague Butt has shown. It requires exactly what the proposition wants to bury: sonic experimentation, dissonance, the willingness to make demands on the listener, the imagination of futures genuinely different from the present. The traditions of Sun Ra, of Detroit techno, of noise music, of electronic experimentation — these are the traditions that have continued to imagine what music could be beyond what the market has already packaged. They are the traditions of musical futurism. The proposition is asking us to choose the past. We are offering the future.

On what the debate is really about

The real debate: the proposition’s motion is conservative, Eurocentric, and in the service of the music industry that the proposition claims to be criticising; the opposition’s defence of experimentation is the genuinely radical positionLet me name what this debate is really about. The proposition has presented itself as radical: as defending genuine jouissance, Dionysian energy, bodily liberation, against the managed melancholy of post-punk. But the effect of their motion — if enacted, if the culture actually did bury post-punk and return to good old rock and roll — would be the restoration of a white, male, North Atlantic, technically demanding, guitar-heroic tradition at the centre of popular music, with all the exclusions that tradition entailed. It would be the re-containment of African-American musical innovation within a European narrative of origin. It would be the re-exclusion of the women, queer musicians, and politically serious artists that post-punk admitted. And it would be the most convenient possible outcome for a music industry that has always known exactly how to package raw rock energy. The proposition is not radical. It is nostalgic, it is conservative, and it is, in the most precise sense, in the service of the capitalist music industry it claims to be resisting. Do not bury post-punk. It is the last thing standing between popular music and total corporate capture.
