Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I want to begin with a fact that should embarrass every participant in the conventional debate about refugee policy. Approximately 110 million people are currently forcibly displaced — the highest number since records began. The 1951 Refugee Convention, ratified by 149 states, obliges those states to provide protection to qualifying refugees. In practice, the system operates as a tragic lottery: refugees end up wherever geography determines, which correlates almost perfectly with factors that have no moral relevance to who should receive protection. Turkey hosts more refugees than the United Kingdom — not because Turkey has the most generous policy but because Syria is its neighbour. Uganda hosts more than most European states — not because Uganda has greater capacity but because its neighbours are unstable.

On the burden-sharing problem

Collective action failure — refugee protection as a global public goodThe central failure of the current system is a collective action problem. Every state would prefer that other states bear the costs of refugee protection. Every state has an incentive to under-provide protection — to make its territory inhospitable, to process claims slowly, to interpret refugee status narrowly — because the costs of protection fall on the hosting state while the benefits are shared globally. Refugee protection is a global public good — it benefits the entire international community — and like all public goods, it is subject to free-riding. The refugee burden falls on states that happen to be geographically proximate to instability and that typically have the fewest resources to devote to protection. Tradable quotas address this problem directly. Each state is assigned an obligation — based on capacity (GDP, population, territory) — to host a certain number of refugees. States that choose not to meet their obligation directly can pay other states to meet it on their behalf.

On why this produces better outcomes

Tradable quotas expand total protection and generate resources for qualityUnder the status quo: states proximate to refugee crises bear the burden; wealthier states bear little; the total number of refugees protected is determined by the willingness of proximate states. Under tradable quotas: every state bears a notional obligation determined by capacity rather than geography; states that prefer to pay contribute financially; that financial contribution enables states with high hosting capacity to provide better resources. The key insight is that tradable quotas do not merely redistribute the refugee burden — they expand it. By creating a financial flow from states that buy quota exemptions to states that sell quota fulfilment, the system generates resources that can be used to improve the quality and quantity of refugee protection. A Uganda that hosts refugees because it is paid to do so is a Uganda with resources to actually provide protection — housing, education, employment access — rather than merely warehousing people in camps.

On the commodification objection

Commodification objection — protection vs personsThe most powerful objection is that refugees are not goods; asylum is a right, not a commodity; trading obligations degrades the moral meaning of protection. But this confuses two different things: the moral status of the individual refugee and the institutional arrangements through which states discharge their obligations. Consider carbon emissions trading: it allows states and companies to trade the right to emit carbon dioxide. This does not mean the atmosphere belongs to whoever buys permits. It means a shared obligation to reduce emissions can be more efficiently discharged through trading than through uniform mandatory targets. Nobody argues carbon trading degrades the moral significance of climate change. The same logic applies here. The quota system does not say refugees are commodities. It says states' obligations to protect refugees can be discharged through a combination of direct hosting and financial contribution — one that produces more total protection than direct hosting alone.

Mr President,

My colleague has made the theoretical case. I want to make the political economy case — that tradable quotas are not merely theoretically superior to the current system but are the only reform with any realistic prospect of producing the political coalitions necessary to actually work.

On the politics of refugee protection

Harmonisation has failed — domestic politics systematically defeats burden-sharing agreementsThe dominant approach to international refugee burden-sharing has been harmonisation — attempting to agree common standards, processing procedures, and distribution mechanisms through multilateral negotiation. This approach has failed. Not because harmonisation is a bad idea but because the political conditions for it do not and will not exist. In virtually every advanced economy, domestic political pressure is consistently against accepting more refugees — not because voters are heartless but because they have legitimate concerns about the pace of integration, the fiscal costs of protection, and the management of cultural change. Governments that sign harmonised burden-sharing agreements face strong domestic pressure to defect — and they do defect, through slow processing, narrow interpretation of refugee status, and physical border enforcement. The EU relocation scheme during the 2015 crisis agreed to relocate 160,000 asylum seekers. It actually relocated fewer than 35,000. The gap between commitment and performance is the systematic product of domestic political incentives that make defection more attractive than compliance.

On what tradable quotas change

Tradable quotas change political incentives — compliance at lower political cost, positive constituencyTradable quotas change the political economy of burden-sharing in two important ways. First, they allow states to comply with their obligations at lower political cost. A state whose domestic politics resists accepting refugees can meet its obligations by paying another state to do so. The obligation remains. The state meets it. The political cost of meeting it is reduced. This makes compliance more politically sustainable. Second, they create a positive constituency for refugee protection in states that currently have no stake in the system. Under the current system, states that host few refugees have no financial interest in maintaining or expanding protection. Under a quota system with trading, states that sell quota fulfilment — Uganda, Ethiopia, Jordan — have a direct financial interest in a well-functioning quota system. They become advocates for the system rather than bystanders. This creates a political coalition — between humanitarian-leaning states in the global North and high-capacity states in the global South — that currently does not exist.

On the evidence from analogous systems

EU-Turkey analogy — financial incentives improve protection outcomesThe concern that trading mechanisms produce perverse outcomes in human rights contexts is not without foundation. But the evidence from analogous systems is more encouraging than critics suggest. The Temporary Protection initiative in Poland following the Russian invasion of Ukraine shows that states with financial incentives to host displaced persons — through EU financial support — provided dramatically better protection than states without such incentives. The EU's support for Turkey under the 2016 statement produced, for a period, significantly improved management of refugee flows and better conditions for refugees in Turkey than had existed previously. These demonstrate the core mechanism: financial incentives change behaviour in the direction of better refugee protection, and states that are paid to host refugees provide better protection than states that are merely obligated to do so.

Mr President,

I want to make what might seem a surprising argument for a philosopher known for his commitment to open borders. I want to argue that even someone who shares my starting point — even someone who believes that states have much more extensive obligations to refugees and migrants than they currently acknowledge — should support tradable refugee quotas.

On ideal theory and non-ideal theory

Non-ideal theory — second-best improvements over the status quo are morally requiredThere is a distinction in political philosophy between ideal theory and non-ideal theory. Ideal theory asks: what would a just world look like? Non-ideal theory asks: given the unjust world we actually inhabit, what reforms should we pursue? My ideal theory answer to the refugee question is something close to open borders — a world in which the accident of birthplace does not determine whether you have access to safety and prosperity. But I live in the world, not in ideal theory. And in the world, the question is not whether to implement open borders — that is not politically available — but whether tradable refugee quotas represent an improvement over the status quo. I believe they do.

On the direction of change

Tradable quotas expand total protection and generate resources for qualityThe relevant question for non-ideal theory is not whether a reform is ideally just but whether it is directionally correct — whether it moves the world toward justice rather than away from it. Tradable refugee quotas are directionally correct in two respects. First, they increase total refugee protection — financial flows from states unwilling to host to states willing and able to do so expand the total number of refugees who receive meaningful protection. A world in which 10 million refugees receive protection through a quota trading system is better than a world in which 5 million receive protection through a dysfunctional status quo. Second, they shift the basis of refugee protection from geographic accident to principled obligation. Under the current system, the refugee burden is determined entirely by proximity to crisis — a morally arbitrary factor. Under a quota system, it is determined by a formula reflecting capacity — a morally relevant factor. This is progress.

On the objection from perfectionism

Commodification objection — protection vs personsThe most important objection from my own theoretical perspective is this: by accepting tradable quotas as a second-best, am I not legitimating a system that falls far short of what justice requires, and thereby making the ideal solution harder to achieve? I want to reject this objection. The perfect is not the enemy of the good. A system that protects more refugees, distributes the burden more fairly, and creates political incentives for states to maintain rather than erode protection obligations is better than the alternative — even if it is not ideal. The history of international refugee law is a history of incremental improvements achieved through pragmatic compromise. The 1951 Refugee Convention was itself a compromise — it excluded most of those who needed protection and gave states enormous discretion in implementation. And yet it was better than what preceded it. Tradable quotas are a compromise. They are better than what we have. In a non-ideal world, that is sufficient reason to support them.

Mr President,

There are things that money can buy, and things that money should not buy. This distinction is not sentimental. It reflects a serious philosophical claim about the relationship between the nature of a good and the norms that should govern its distribution. Different social goods have different social meanings, and the appropriate distributive principles for a good must be derived from its social meaning rather than from a universal market calculus.

On what asylum means

Commodification objection — protection vs personsAsylum is not a service. It is not a product. It is recognition — the recognition that a human being, confronted with persecution, has a claim on the protection of the human community that no state can legitimately deny. The 1951 Refugee Convention is not a burden-sharing agreement. It is a statement of obligation — an acknowledgment that the Holocaust had demonstrated that states cannot be permitted to expel, persecute, or abandon people to violence without the international community having an obligation to respond. The Convention's statement that no state shall return a refugee to a place where their life or freedom would be threatened is not a quota. It is a right. It attaches to the individual. It cannot be transferred, traded, or discharged by financial payment to another state.

On what tradability does to the meaning of asylum

Commodification objection — protection vs personsWhen we introduce tradability into the refugee protection system, we change what protection means. Protection is no longer recognition of an individual's humanity and their claim to safety. It becomes a fiscal obligation that can be discharged by payment — more like a carbon credit than like a welcome. This transformation is not merely symbolic. A state that hosts refugees because it has been paid to do so has a different relationship with those refugees than a state that hosts them because it recognises their humanity and its obligations. The paid state has commercial incentives: to minimise the cost of each refugee, to maximise throughput, to provide the minimum required by the contract rather than the maximum required by decency. The host-state's relationship to the refugee becomes that of a contractor to a client — with all the incentives for cost-cutting and quality minimisation that commercial relationships generate.

On the carbon trading analogy and why it fails

Tradable quotas expand total protection and generate resources for qualityMy opponents will deploy the carbon trading analogy. A carbon credit represents a share of the global atmosphere's absorptive capacity. It is fungible because one tonne of CO₂ absorbed is equivalent to any other tonne, wherever it is absorbed. A refugee is not fungible. A Syrian refugee fleeing political persecution is not equivalent — from the perspective of her rights and humanity — to any other unit of protection obligation. Her claim to asylum is a claim to recognition of her particular situation, her particular vulnerability, and her particular need for protection. The trading system treats her as interchangeable with any other unit of protection demand. She is not. Moreover: my proposition colleague argued that domestic politics in wealthy states is an acceptable constraint on the design of refugee protection systems. I reject this concession. The task of political leadership is not to design systems optimally compatible with the worst impulses of domestic politics. It is to build the political conditions under which better obligations can be met.

Mr President,

My colleague Walzer has made the moral case against tradable refugee quotas. I want to make the empirical case — the argument that tradable quotas will not produce the outcomes the proposition claims, and that the mechanisms through which they are supposed to improve protection quality will in practice produce the opposite.

On what the proposition's model assumes

Collective action failure — refugee protection as a global public goodThe proposition's argument rests on a sequence of assumptions that need to be made explicit. First, states that buy quota exemptions will actually pay, and payments will be calibrated to the actual cost of providing protection. Second, states that sell quota fulfilment will use revenues to provide genuine, high-quality protection — rather than pocketing revenues while warehousing refugees in camps. Third, the quota formula will be set at a level that generates adequate total protection. Fourth, the trading market will function efficiently — prices set competitively rather than through bilateral monopolies, not gamed through creative accounting of who counts as a refugee. Each of these assumptions is questionable. Together, they constitute a theoretical architecture that the political and institutional realities of international refugee protection will likely not sustain.

On the race to the bottom

Tradable quotas change political incentives — compliance at lower political cost, positive constituencyThe most serious empirical concern about tradable quotas is that they will produce a systematic reduction in protection quality through competitive under-provision. States that sell quota fulfilment — states in the global South with high hosting capacity — will compete with one another for revenues that buying states generate. This competition creates downward pressure on the price of protection and, therefore, downward pressure on the quality of protection. A state that offers to house refugees at minimum cost will attract quota purchases from states that prefer cheap exemptions to expensive ones. States attempting to provide high-quality protection will find themselves priced out of the market by states offering cheaper, lower-quality alternatives. We have directly relevant evidence: Australia's offshore processing arrangement with Papua New Guinea and Nauru produced conditions of such deplorable quality — systematic mental health deterioration, sexual abuse, indefinite detention — that they were condemned by international human rights organisations. These conditions were the product of a system in which the purchaser was not the entity experiencing the conditions, and therefore had no direct incentive to ensure quality. Tradable refugee quotas institutionalise this structure across the entire international refugee system.

On the EU–Turkey evidence reconsidered

EU-Turkey analogy — financial incentives improve protection outcomesThe proposition cited the EU–Turkey statement of 2016 as evidence that financial incentives improve refugee protection. I want to examine that evidence more carefully. The statement involved the EU paying Turkey six billion euros to manage Syrian refugee flows. What did this produce? Turkey did host large numbers of Syrian refugees — approximately 3.5 million at its peak. But the vast majority had no legal status, no right to work, and no access to formal education. They lived in legal limbo, dependent on Turkish political goodwill that proved increasingly unreliable. Turkey used the refugees as a political bargaining chip, twice threatening to "open the gates" to leverage EU concessions on other issues. This is what financial incentives for refugee hosting actually look like in practice: compliance that is contingent on continued payment and political favour, that can be withdrawn as political conditions change, and that provides minimum quality protection rather than genuine asylum. This is not an advertisement for tradable refugee quotas. It is a warning against them.

Mr President,

I want to make the deepest philosophical argument against tradable refugee quotas — an argument that challenges not merely the mechanism but the conceptual framework within which the proposition operates. The proposition treats the refugee problem as a burden-sharing problem. I want to argue that this framing is wrong, and that the wrong framing produces the wrong solution.

On Hannah Arendt and the right to have rights

Non-ideal theory — second-best improvements over the status quo are morally requiredHannah Arendt, writing in the aftermath of the Second World War, identified the fundamental political problem of her time: the existence of millions of stateless people who had been stripped of citizenship and therefore had no political community within which to exercise their rights. Arendt argued that human rights — as typically conceived — depend for their realisation on membership in a political community. A person without a state, without citizenship, without membership in any political community, has no institutional framework within which to exercise their rights. Rights require a political home. And the denial of that home — statelessness — is not merely the deprivation of a benefit but the deprivation of the capacity to have rights at all. The lesson for refugee protection is this: what refugees need is not a service delivery arrangement. What they need is membership — genuine membership in a political community that recognises their humanity, grants them rights, and makes them accountable to and protected by the institutions of that community. The tradable quota system does not provide this. It provides service delivery: an obligation to house, feed, and process a certain number of persons, discharged by whoever wins the tender.

On the right to asylum as a right

Commodification objection — protection vs personsSeyla Benhabib has argued that the right to asylum is not merely a humanitarian desideratum created by treaty. It is a fundamental human right — a right that attaches to the person by virtue of their humanity. If the right to asylum is a fundamental human right, then states' obligations with respect to asylum are not quota obligations — obligations to accept a certain number of persons. They are genuine rights obligations — triggered by the presence of a person in need of protection and that cannot be discharged by financial payment to another state. The tradable quota system, by its structure, implies that states' obligations are quota obligations. This is not a rights framework. It is a welfare framework — treating protection as a good to be produced and distributed, rather than as a right to be vindicated.

On the wrong question and the right one

Non-ideal theory — second-best improvements over the status quo are morally requiredThe tradable quota proposal is a symptom of a failure of political imagination — the failure to recognise that the refugee crisis is not primarily a burden-sharing problem but a political problem, rooted in the structure of the international state system itself. The refugee crisis is produced by states — states that persecute their citizens, wage war on their populations, and generate the conditions in which millions have no viable option but flight. The response should be political: the strengthening of international accountability mechanisms for states that generate refugees, the development of institutions capable of providing genuine political membership to stateless persons, and the transformation of international refugee law from a burden-sharing system into a genuine rights system. The tradable quota proposal addresses none of this. Moreover: by providing a market-based solution, the tradable quota system reduces the pressure on wealthy states to contribute to the political conditions that generate refugees. A state that can buy its way out of its refugee obligations has no incentive to address the causes of refugee crises. The market solution is not merely inadequate. It is actively counterproductive.
