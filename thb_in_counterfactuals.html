Similarity Semantics & Modal RealismDavid Lewis — Counterfactuals (1973) & “Counterfactual Dependence and Time’s Arrow” (1979)
A counterfactual “If A were the case, C would be the case” (A ◻ C) is non-vacuously true iff in all the closest possible worlds where A holds, C holds. Closeness is determined by overall similarity — a system of weights in which large-scale agreement in matters of particular fact takes priority, small miracles to diverge from actual history take priority over large differences in future history, and perfect match in laws takes high priority. The framework is grounded in modal realism: possible worlds are genuine concrete entities, and the similarity relation ranges over real similarities among them.

Selection Functions & the Uniqueness ThesisRobert Stalnaker — “A Theory of Conditionals” (1968) & Inquiry (1984)
A counterfactual is evaluated at the unique closest world selected by a selection function f(A, w) that picks the world most similar to w in which A holds. Against Lewis, I defend the Uniqueness Thesis: there is always a single closest A-world, so counterfactuals are always either true or false rather than indeterminate. I also defend a more conservative metaphysics: possible worlds are abstract rather than concrete, selected by the Ramsey Test procedure applied to a rational belief state. The framework handles conditionals uniformly as strict conditionals relative to a contextually supplied selection function.

Systematic Defence & the Case Against RivalsJonathan Bennett — A Philosophical Guide to Conditionals (2003)
The Stalnaker-Lewis framework is the most systematically successful account of counterfactuals we have. Its rivals — the material conditional analysis, Goodman’s cotenability approach, and inferentialist accounts — all fail to handle the full range of counterfactual reasoning. Epistemic Role, Causal Structure & the Right Similarity MetricFrank Jackson — “On the Semantics and Logic of Obligation” & work on conditionals (1977–)
The power of the Stalnaker-Lewis framework lies in its capacity to connect counterfactual truth conditions to causal reasoning. The similarity metric, properly specified, tracks the causal structure of the actual world: what counts as a nearby world is a world whose causal history up to the time of the antecedent matches the actual world and then diverges by the minimal change required to make the antecedent true. This causally grounded similarity metric handles the cases that Fine and others take to be counterexamples, and it connects the semantics to the epistemology of counterfactual reasoning in an illuminating way.

Chair,

I want to begin with the phenomenon we are trying to explain, because the opposition will spend considerable energy attacking the details of the similarity metric and the metaphysics of possible worlds, and these attacks will only be compelling if we forget what problem the theory was designed to solve. The problem is this: counterfactuals are not material conditionals, they are not strict conditionals in any standard modal logic, and yet they have a systematic logical behaviour that connects them to causation, explanation, and practical reasoning in ways that any adequate theory must capture. The possible worlds similarity semantics does this. No rival theory comes close.

“We may say that A ◻ C is non-vacuously true iff some world where both A and C are true is closer to our actual world than any world where A is true but C is not.” — Lewis, Counterfactuals
i. the failures of rival analyses

Rivals fail — the material conditional analysis makes all counterfactuals with false antecedents vacuously true; strict modal analysis makes valid the fallacies of strengthening and contraposition; Goodman's cotenability is circular; only the similarity semantics handles all casesThe material conditional analysis — “If A were the case, C would be the case” is true iff it is not the case that A is true and C is false — makes all counterfactuals with false antecedents vacuously true. This is obviously wrong: “If kangaroos had no tails, they would topple over” and “If kangaroos had no tails, they would not topple over” cannot both be true. The strict conditional analysis — it is necessary that if A then C — validates strengthening the antecedent (if A ◻ C, then (A ∧ B) ◻ C) and modus tollens for counterfactuals, both of which fail: “If I had struck the match, it would have lit” does not entail “If I had struck the match in a vacuum, it would have lit.” Goodman’s cotenability approach requires specifying which background conditions “cotenable” with the antecedent to include in the derivation, but cotenability is defined in terms of counterfactuals, making the analysis circular. The similarity semantics avoids all these failures by providing a truth condition that is neither vacuous, too strict, nor circular.

ii. the logical virtues of the framework

Logic — the similarity semantics validates the correct logic of counterfactuals: it validates Limited Transitivity and Strengthening of the Consequent while invalidating Strengthening of the Antecedent and unrestricted Hypothetical SyllogismThe similarity semantics delivers the right conditional logic — a system that validates the principles that ordinary counterfactual reasoning endorses and invalidates the fallacies. It validates: the Identity principle (A ◻ A), Modus Ponens for the material conditional embedded in the antecedent-consequent relation, and Limited Strengthening of the Consequent (if A ◻ C and C entails D, then A ◻ D). It invalidates: Strengthening the Antecedent (from A ◻ C we cannot infer (A ∧ B) ◻ C), unrestricted Hypothetical Syllogism (from A ◻ B and B ◻ C we cannot infer A ◻ C), and Contraposition (from A ◻ C we cannot infer ¬C ◻ ¬A). Each of these invalidations corresponds to a genuine pattern of ordinary counterfactual reasoning: we recognise that adding antecedent conditions can defeat counterfactuals, that counterfactual chains do not compose, and that counterfactual dependencies are not symmetric. The framework’s logic is not an artefact of the possible worlds machinery; it is a principled reflection of how counterfactuals actually work.

iii. the similarity metric and fine’s objection

Fine's Nixon — Fine argues that on Lewis's similarity metric, a world where Nixon presses the button but a miracle prevents war is closer than one with war; Lewis responds with a priority system: first avoid big miracles, second match matters of particular fact, third avoid small miraclesFine presses what has become the canonical objection to the similarity metric: the Nixon/bomb case. Suppose Nixon had pressed the nuclear button in 1974. Fine argues that on a natural reading of overall similarity, a world where Nixon presses the button but some miracle prevents the launch is more similar to actuality than a world where Nixon presses the button and nuclear war ensues — because the former world agrees with actuality in the vast number of particular facts that survive a miracle, while the latter involves enormous divergence. Therefore, Fine argues, the similarity semantics predicts that “If Nixon had pressed the button, nuclear war would have ensued” is false, contrary to our intuition. My response is that Fine is using the wrong similarity metric. The correct metric — which I develop in the 1979 paper — gives priority to avoiding large miracles, then to matching matters of particular fact in a large region, then to avoiding small miracles. On this metric, the world with war is closer than the miracle world, because the miracle world requires a law violation at the moment of launch that the war world does not.

iv. modal realism and the ontology of possible worlds

Modal realism — possible worlds are genuine concrete entities; this is not a defect of the theory but its deepest virtue; it provides a uniform analysis of all modal discourse and grounds the similarity relation in real propertiesThe opposition will press the metaphysics of modal realism as a cost of the theory. I embrace the cost as a theoretical virtue. Possible worlds, on my view, are genuine concrete entities — maximal spatio-temporally connected regions of a multiverse of worlds. This view provides a uniform analysis of all modal discourse: possibility, necessity, and counterfactual dependence are all analysed in terms of quantification over possible worlds and the relations among them. It grounds the similarity relation in real properties of real worlds rather than in our attitudes toward worlds, avoiding the subjectivism that threatens competitor accounts. The cost in ontological extravagance is real. But it is not clearly greater than the cost of treating modal facts as primitive — as those who reject modal realism must do. Every adequate semantic theory must quantify over possible worlds. I say we should believe what our best theories quantify over.

I yield the floor.

I share with Lewis the commitment to a possible worlds semantics for counterfactuals, but I want to defend a variant that differs from his in two important respects: the Uniqueness Thesis and the metaphysics of possible worlds. These differences matter both philosophically and for how the framework handles the objections the opposition will press.

“A conditional is true at a world w if and only if the consequent is true at the world most similar to w in which the antecedent is true, or false if the consequent is false at that world, or true if the antecedent is impossible.” — Stalnaker
i. the selection function and the uniqueness thesis

Uniqueness Thesis — Stalnaker defends a single-world selection function against Lewis's willingness to allow ties and indeterminacy; counterfactuals are always true or false, never indeterminate; the Limit Assumption holdsLewis allows that there may be no single closest A-world — that there may be a tie between several equally close A-worlds, or that there may be an infinite descending sequence of closer and closer A-worlds with no minimum. In these cases, Lewis says the counterfactual is indeterminate. I defend the Uniqueness Thesis against this: the selection function always picks a unique world, and counterfactuals are always determinately true or false. The argument for uniqueness is that we have no difficulty evaluating counterfactuals in the cases Lewis takes to generate ties or infinite sequences. “If I had been a little taller, I would still have been under six feet” is determinately true or false even if there is no unique closest world in which I am a little taller. The context always supplies enough information to determine a unique selection, and it is context sensitivity that explains apparent indeterminacy, not genuine semantic vagueness in the truth conditions.

ii. the ramsey test and belief revision

Ramsey Test — conditionals are evaluated by hypothetically adding the antecedent to a belief state and asking whether the consequent follows; this connects the semantics to the epistemology of conditionals; selection functions represent rational belief revision operationsOne of the important connections the possible worlds framework enables is the connection to the Ramsey Test for conditionals: to evaluate a conditional, hypothetically add the antecedent to your beliefs and ask whether the consequent follows. Selection functions represent exactly this epistemic operation: f(A, w) picks the world most similar to w in which A holds, and this corresponds to the minimal revision of the belief state appropriate for the addition of A. The connection between the semantics and the epistemology of conditional reasoning is not accidental; it reflects the fact that possible worlds semantics is at its core a formalisation of the structure of rational hypothetical reasoning. Edgington will press an alternative account of this connection via the Adams’ Thesis. But the Ramsey Test connection I am drawing does not commit me to Adams’ Thesis; it is compatible with the full truth-conditional account of conditionals that the proposition is defending.

iii. the metaphysics: worlds as abstract vs. concrete

Abstract worlds — Stalnaker treats possible worlds as abstract objects (ways the world could be) rather than Lewis's concrete entities; this avoids the ontological extravagance of modal realism while retaining the semantic frameworkAgainst Lewis, I treat possible worlds as abstract objects — as ways the actual world could have been, or as maximally consistent sets of propositions — rather than as genuine concrete entities spatiotemporally disconnected from the actual world. This more conservative metaphysics avoids the extravagance of modal realism while retaining the full power of the semantic framework. The similarity ordering ranges over abstract representations of how things could be, not over a literally infinite multiverse of concrete universes. The objection that the similarity relation is then ungrounded — that without concrete worlds there is no real similarity to track — can be answered by noting that the similarity ordering is grounded in the actual world’s properties: what counts as a similar world is determined by what would be a minimal departure from actual facts and laws, and this is a question about actuality, not about other concrete entities.

iv. uniformity: handling both indicative and subjunctive conditionals

Uniformity — Stalnaker's framework treats indicative and subjunctive conditionals as instances of the same semantic form; the difference lies in the context that determines the selection function, not in different semantic formsOne advantage of my framework over Lewis’s is uniformity: it treats indicative and subjunctive conditionals as semantically of the same form — both are evaluated by a selection function relative to context. The difference between “If Oswald didn’t kill Kennedy, someone else did” (indicative) and “If Oswald hadn’t killed Kennedy, someone else would have” (subjunctive) is not a difference in semantic form but in which possible worlds are contextually relevant: the indicative is evaluated relative to worlds compatible with the speaker’s epistemic state, the subjunctive relative to worlds that diverge from actuality as specified by the antecedent. This uniformity is a theoretical virtue: it avoids the multiplication of semantic categories and explains the connection between the two conditional forms without ad hoc stipulation.

I yield the floor.

2.The Uniqueness Thesis: context always supplies enough information to determine a unique selection. Apparent indeterminacy reflects context sensitivity, not genuine semantic vagueness.
3.A semantic theory that respects the determinacy of our counterfactual judgements is preferable to one that requires indeterminacy where we judge determinately.
∴The Uniqueness Thesis is defensible: context resolves apparent ties; counterfactuals are always determinately true or false; Stalnaker’s single-world selection function is more faithful to our evaluative practice than Lewis’s multi-world framework.

1.The Ramsey Test: evaluate a conditional by hypothetically adding the antecedent to your beliefs and asking if the consequent follows. This is our epistemic procedure for assessing counterfactuals.

∴The Stalnaker framework treats indicative and subjunctive conditionals uniformly; the difference is contextual, not semantic; this uniformity is a theoretical virtue absent from frameworks that assign different semantic forms to the two types.

Chair,

I want to offer a systematic defence of the Stalnaker-Lewis framework against the full range of objections the opposition will press, drawing on my work in A Philosophical Guide to Conditionals. My strategy is to show that the objections either target a version of the framework that neither Lewis nor Stalnaker actually endorses, or can be handled by clarification and refinement rather than rejection of the framework.

i. the systematic success of the framework across conditional types

Systematic success — the framework handles simple counterfactuals, backtracking counterfactuals, future conditionals, and conditionals in causal reasoning uniformly; no rival framework approaches this generalityThe first thing to notice about the Stalnaker-Lewis framework is its systematic generality. It handles simple counterfactuals (“If I had struck the match, it would have lit”), backtracking counterfactuals (“If the match had lit, it must have been struck” — handled by contextually shifting the relevant similarity relation), future conditionals, and counterfactuals embedded in causal reasoning, all within a single uniform semantic framework. The key to handling backtracking is not ad hoc; it is the recognition that the context of utterance partly determines which similarity relation is relevant, and that in some contexts we are interested in worlds that share our actual future rather than our actual past. No rival framework achieves this generality. Goodman’s cotenability approach, inferentialist accounts, and the probabilistic accounts Edgington favours all handle subsets of the counterfactual data while failing on others. The generality of the possible worlds framework is its greatest recommendation.

ii. fine’s objection: the right response

Fine answered — Fine's Nixon case requires a similarity metric that fails to track causal-nomological relations; the causally-informed metric Lewis develops in 1979 correctly handles the case; Fine's objection is to a naive metric, not the frameworkFine’s objection has been enormously influential, but I want to press on what exactly it shows. Fine argues that on a naive reading of overall similarity, a world where a miracle prevents nuclear war is more similar to actuality than a world with war. Lewis’s response in 1979 is to specify a priority system that gives weight to nomological continuity: the war world is closer because it involves no miracle at the moment of launch, while the miracle-prevention world involves a law-violation precisely at the critical moment. Fine’s objection targets a naive metric; it does not touch the priority system Lewis actually defends. The opposition will say that Lewis’s priority system is ad hoc — constructed to get the right results in the Nixon case rather than derived from independent principles. This charge fails: the priority system is independently motivated by the connection between counterfactual dependence and causal dependence. The similarity metric that delivers the right verdicts on counterfactuals is the same metric that captures the structure of causal relationships, and this is not an accident but a deep feature of the framework.

iii. edgington’s probabilistic challenge

Against Edgington — Adams's Thesis (the probability of a conditional = the conditional probability) is incompatible with the conditional having a truth value; but this incompatibility shows that the Thesis must be restricted, not that conditionals lack truth valuesEdgington presses the Adams’ Thesis: the probability of a conditional is the conditional probability of its consequent given its antecedent. Lewis showed — in what is sometimes called the triviality result — that if conditionals have truth values and the probability of a conditional is always the conditional probability, then there can be only two truth values, which is absurd. Edgington takes this to show that conditionals lack truth values. I take it to show that Adams’ Thesis must be restricted. The Thesis holds as an approximation for a specific kind of assertibility condition: we are warranted in asserting a conditional to the extent that the conditional probability is high. But assertibility conditions are not the same as truth conditions, and the high assertibility of a conditional does not entail that its truth value is simply its conditional probability. The triviality result is a reductio of the conjunction of (a) conditionals have truth values, (b) the probability of a conditional always equals the conditional probability, and (c) there can be more than two truth values. Since (c) is secure, one of (a) or (b) must go. I say (b) should be restricted; Edgington says (a) should be abandoned. Lewis’s triviality result does not determine which.

I yield the floor.

I want to address the causal dimension of the similarity metric, because I think it is the key to answering both Fine’s objection and the opposition’s broader charge that the similarity relation is insufficiently constrained to deliver determinate verdicts on the cases we care about.

i. causal structure and the similarity metric

Causal grounding — the correct similarity metric tracks causal structure: the closest A-world shares the actual world's causal history up to the time of the antecedent and then diverges by the minimal causal change needed to make A trueThe similarity metric that Lewis needs — and that the 1979 paper gestures toward — is one that tracks the causal structure of the actual world. The correct specification is this: the closest A-world shares the actual world’s complete causal history up to the time of the antecedent event, and then diverges by the minimal causal change needed to make the antecedent true, with the causal consequences of that change propagating forward in accordance with actual laws. This metric is not merely a list of priority weights; it is a principled account of what it means for a counterfactual world to be a minimal departure from actuality. The Nixon case is then handled straightforwardly: the closest world in which Nixon presses the button is one in which the causal history of the world up to the moment of button-pressing is exactly as it actually was, the button is pressed, and the causal consequences of pressing it — including the launch sequence — propagate in accordance with actual laws. There is no world closer to actuality in which Nixon presses the button and the consequences are different, because any such world either has a different causal history up to the pressing, or it involves a miracle at the moment of launch.

ii. the epistemology of counterfactual reasoning

Epistemology — we evaluate counterfactuals by running actual causal mechanisms forward from the point of the antecedent; this matches the causal similarity metric; the epistemology and semantics are alignedThe causal similarity metric is not only independently principled; it is confirmed by the epistemology of counterfactual reasoning. When we evaluate a counterfactual, we ask: what would have happened if A had obtained? And the way we answer this question is by running the actual causal mechanisms of the world forward from the point at which A diverges from actuality, asking what the causal consequences would have been. This is precisely what the causal similarity metric tells us to do. The epistemology and the semantics are aligned: the worlds we find ourselves considering when we evaluate counterfactuals are the worlds that share our actual causal history up to the divergence point. Edgington’s probabilistic account cannot explain this alignment without invoking something very like the causal similarity relation under a different name.

iii. kvart’s counterexamples and their resolution

My response: the Bizet-Verdi case is a case of genuine contextual indecision about which similarity relation is operative. In a context that treats Italian nationality as more deeply embedded in Verdi’s essence, Bizet would have been Italian. In a context that treats French nationality as more deeply embedded in Bizet’s essence, Verdi would have been French. This is not a failure of the framework; it is a demonstration that the framework correctly captures the context-sensitivity of counterfactual evaluation. The indeterminacy reflects our contextual indecision, not a semantic defect.

Against Truth Conditions & the Probabilistic AlternativeDorothy Edgington — “On Conditionals” (1995) & subsequent work
The Stalnaker-Lewis framework makes a fundamental mistake in assigning truth conditions to conditionals. The correct account of conditionals — following Adams and Ramsey — is probabilistic: the assertibility of a conditional is measured by the conditional probability of its consequent given its antecedent, and conditionals do not have truth values in the classical sense. Lewis’s own triviality results show that any truth-conditional account of conditionals that satisfies the probability-of-conditionals thesis generates absurdity. The correct response is to abandon truth conditions for conditionals, not to restrict the probability thesis.

Objective Chance, Indeterminism & the Probability ProblemHannes Leitgeb — “A Probabilistic Semantics for Counterfactuals” (2012) & related work
The Stalnaker-Lewis framework is built for deterministic worlds and fails systematically in indeterministic ones. When the actual world contains genuine objective chances — quantum mechanical or otherwise — the notion of a closest possible world becomes ill-defined, because two worlds can be nomologically identical up to a branch point and then diverge by chance rather than by miracle. The similarity metric has no principled way to rank worlds that diverge by chance. A probabilistic semantics that replaces possible-world selection with probability distributions over worlds is better equipped to handle the actual modal structure of the world we inhabit.

Chair,

I want to press my objection to Lewis’s similarity metric as precisely as I can, because Bennett and Jackson will say that Lewis’s 1979 response adequately handles the Nixon case. I want to show that it does not — not because the 1979 response fails in the Nixon case specifically, but because the response reveals a deeper problem: the similarity metric has no principled content independent of the verdicts it is designed to deliver.

The Nixon case (Fine, 1975). Nixon actually did not press the nuclear button in 1974. Consider two counterfactual worlds: W1 — Nixon presses the button and nuclear war ensues; W2 — Nixon presses the button but a small miracle (a broken circuit, a communications failure) prevents nuclear war. Intuitively: “If Nixon had pressed the button, nuclear war would have ensued.” Naive overall similarity: W2 is vastly more similar to actuality than W1, because W1 diverges from actuality in billions of particular facts (deaths, destructions, social changes) while W2 diverges only in the additional miracle. The naive similarity semantics predicts the counterfactual is false. But we judge it true.
i. the 1979 response and its problem

Priority system is ad hoc — Lewis's 1979 priority weights are constructed to deliver verdicts matching our intuitions; they have no independent justification; the framework becomes a way of encoding our judgements rather than explaining themLewis’s 1979 response imposes a priority ordering: first avoid big miracles, second maximise match in particular facts over large regions, third avoid small miracles. On this ordering, W2 requires a small miracle (the broken circuit) while W1 requires none: it is just the causal continuation of the actual world under the assumption that Nixon presses. So W1 is closer on the priority ordering, and the counterfactual comes out true. Bennett and Jackson say this response is independently motivated by the connection to causal structure. I deny this. The priority ordering is specified in precisely the terms needed to deliver the verdict that our intuitions require on the Nixon case. It says: “avoid small miracles” — but why? Not because of any independent theory of what makes worlds similar. Because small miracles in W2 happen to put W2 on the wrong side of the intuition divide. The priority ordering is a list of weights engineered to match our counterfactual judgements. It does not explain those judgements; it encodes them.

ii. the asymmetry of time and the direction of counterfactual dependence

Temporal asymmetry — Lewis's similarity metric must explain why counterfactuals typically run forward in time, not backward; his account of this via the entropy asymmetry faces serious objections; the asymmetry is built in by hand rather than derivedLewis also owes an explanation of why counterfactual dependence is typically forward-directed: we say “If the match had been struck, it would have lit” but not “If the match had lit, it must have been struck.” This temporal asymmetry must be explained by the similarity metric. Lewis’s account appeals to the thermodynamic arrow of time: the actual world has a de facto asymmetry in the distribution of miracles — there are many small miracles that could have produced a convergent history (erasing the traces of the antecedent) but virtually none that could have produced a divergent future (generating the antecedent from nothing). This account is complex, relies on contingent physical facts about entropy, and has been extensively criticised by Elga, Price, and others who argue that it confuses the nomological and entropic arrows of time. Lewis is buying the temporal asymmetry of counterfactuals at a very high metaphysical price. That price deserves explicit recognition as a cost of the framework.

iii. the need for a principled similarity metric

No principled metric — the framework requires a principled similarity metric to generate verdicts; Lewis provides a list of priorities rather than a principled account; without the list, the framework has no content; with it, the framework merely encodes our judgementsMy deepest objection is this: the similarity framework requires, for its execution, a determinate similarity ordering over worlds. Lewis must provide this ordering. He does so by specifying priority weights. But these weights are not derived from any independent account of what makes worlds similar; they are specified as whatever weights deliver the right verdicts on the benchmark cases. The framework’s content is therefore: the truth condition of a counterfactual A ◻ C is that C holds in the closest A-world, where “closest” is determined by whatever similarity relation matches our counterfactual intuitions. This is a circular account: the truth conditions of counterfactuals are specified in terms of a similarity relation that is itself specified in terms of counterfactual intuitions. The framework provides the form of an explanation without its substance.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Lewis’s 1979 priority system is ad hoc: constructed to match intuitions rather than independently motivated
1.The 1979 priority system specifies weights that deliver the correct verdict on the Nixon case: avoid big miracles > maximise particular-fact match > avoid small miracles.
2.These weights have no independent justification from a theory of similarity. They are specified as whatever weights are needed to match our counterfactual intuitions in benchmark cases.
3.A priority ordering constructed to match intuitions does not explain those intuitions; it encodes them. The framework becomes a way of representing our judgements rather than explaining them.
∴Lewis’s 1979 priority system is ad hoc; its weights are constructed to match intuitions rather than derived from an independent theory of world-similarity; the framework encodes rather than explains our counterfactual judgements.
Argument II — The temporal asymmetry of counterfactuals is built into the similarity metric at high metaphysical cost
1.Counterfactual dependence is typically forward-directed. The similarity metric must explain this. Lewis appeals to thermodynamic entropy asymmetry: there are many convergence miracles but few divergence miracles.
2.This account relies on contingent physical facts about entropy and has been criticised for confusing nomological and entropic arrows of time. It is a high-cost explanation of a fundamental feature of counterfactuals.
3.The temporal asymmetry should be explained by a principled account of counterfactual dependence, not derived from contingent thermodynamic facts about miracle distributions.
∴Lewis’s account of the temporal asymmetry of counterfactuals via thermodynamic entropy is high-cost and contested; this is a fundamental feature of counterfactuals that the framework handles poorly.
Argument III — The framework’s content reduces to encoding our intuitions via whatever similarity relation matches them
1.The framework’s truth condition: A ◻ C is true iff C holds in the closest A-world. Closeness requires a similarity ordering.
2.The similarity ordering is specified as whatever weights deliver correct verdicts on benchmark cases. It is not derived from an independent account of world-similarity.
3.The content of the framework is therefore circular: truth conditions specified in terms of a similarity relation defined in terms of counterfactual intuitions. The framework has the form of an explanation without the substance.
∴The similarity framework is circular: the similarity relation is defined in terms of counterfactual intuitions; the framework encodes rather than explains our judgements; it provides the form of an analysis without genuine explanatory content.

Chair,

I want to press a family of counterexamples that are distinct from Fine’s Nixon case and that expose a different structural problem with the Stalnaker-Lewis framework: the problem of genuine semantic indeterminacy in cases where no similarity metric, however refined, can deliver a determinate verdict because the indeterminacy is not contextual but metaphysical.

The Bizet-Verdi case. Bizet was French; Verdi was Italian. Consider: “If Bizet and Verdi had been compatriots, Bizet would have been Italian.” And: “If Bizet and Verdi had been compatriots, Verdi would have been French.” Both antecedents are the same proposition. In the closest world where they are compatriots, which nationality do they share? There seems to be no principled answer. The Lewis framework must either say one of the counterfactuals is true (which seems arbitrary) or say both are indeterminate (which seems like a failure to evaluate counterfactuals that have a determinate content).
i. the Bizet-Verdi indeterminacy

Bizet-Verdi — no similarity metric can determine whether the closest compatriot-world has both French or both Italian; the indeterminacy is not contextual but reflects genuine underdetermination of the antecedent; the framework cannot handle symmetric casesJackson says the Bizet-Verdi case reflects contextual indecision about which similarity relation is operative — that in contexts treating Italian nationality as more essential to Verdi, the French counterfactual is true, and in contexts treating French nationality as more essential to Bizet, the Italian counterfactual is true. I deny this. The indeterminacy is not contextual. In a fixed context, with a fully specified similarity ordering, there is still no fact of the matter about whether the closest compatriot-world has both French or both Italian, because the ordering is symmetric with respect to the two candidates. The antecedent “Bizet and Verdi are compatriots” underdetermines which world is selected, not because of context-sensitivity, but because the closest-world relation is defined over pairs of worlds and the two candidate worlds are equidistant from actuality. Lewis’s framework has no principled way to select between them.

ii. overdetermination and preemption

Overdetermination — cases of symmetric overdetermination and early/late preemption generate indeterminate or wrong verdicts for the similarity semantics; the causal account Jackson offers fails to handle these cases correctlyConsider a case of symmetric overdetermination: a glass is struck simultaneously by two rocks, and breaks. “If rock A had not been thrown, the glass would still have broken.” This seems true. “If rock B had not been thrown, the glass would still have broken.” This also seems true. But the Lewis framework struggles here. The closest world in which A is not thrown is one where B is thrown and the glass breaks. The closest world in which B is not thrown is one where A is thrown and the glass breaks. Both counterfactuals come out true, which matches our intuition. But now consider: “If neither rock had been thrown, the glass would still have broken.” On the Lewis framework, this seems to require the glass to break by some miracle in the closest world where neither is thrown — which conflicts with intuition. The framework’s handling of overdetermination is correct in the simple cases but breaks down in more complex configurations, and the similarity metric has no principled resources to handle the breakdown.

iii. the comparative probability alternative

Probability alternative — Kvart's own account evaluates counterfactuals by comparative probability of antecedent-worlds; this handles symmetric cases and overdetermination by distributing weight across candidate worlds rather than selecting oneThe cases I am pressing suggest that what is needed is not a refinement of the similarity metric but a different evaluative procedure. My own account evaluates counterfactuals by comparative probability: A ◻ C is true to the extent that worlds where A and C are both true are more probable than worlds where A is true and C is false, relative to a probability distribution that takes the antecedent as given. This distributes evaluative weight across candidate worlds rather than selecting a single closest world, handling the symmetric cases the Lewis framework cannot: in the Bizet-Verdi case, the probability is equal across the candidate worlds, so neither counterfactual has a determinate truth value, which is the correct result. In the overdetermination case, the probability of the glass breaking given that neither rock is thrown is low, so the counterfactual “If neither had been thrown, the glass would still have broken” is false, which is also correct.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Bizet-Verdi case generates genuine metaphysical indeterminacy that context-sensitivity cannot resolve
1.The Bizet-Verdi antecedent underdetermines the closest world because two candidate worlds (both-French, both-Italian) are equidistant from actuality on any plausible similarity metric.
2.Jackson’s context-sensitivity response fails: in a fixed context with a fully specified similarity ordering, the two worlds are still equidistant. The indeterminacy is not contextual but metaphysical.
3.The framework has no principled way to select between equidistant candidate worlds. This is a structural failure that cannot be resolved by refining the similarity metric.
∴The Bizet-Verdi case generates genuine metaphysical indeterminacy that the Lewis framework cannot resolve; the indeterminacy is structural, not contextual; the framework fails in symmetric cases.
Argument II — Overdetermination generates incorrect or indeterminate verdicts that the similarity metric cannot resolve
1.Symmetric overdetermination: a glass hit simultaneously by two rocks breaks. The framework correctly handles single-rock counterfactuals but fails on “If neither rock had been thrown, the glass would still have broken” — which requires the glass to break miraculously in the closest rock-free world.
2.The similarity metric has no principled resources to handle the transition from two-rock to zero-rock counterfactuals without generating counterintuitive verdicts about miracles.
3.The causal account Jackson proposes faces the same problem: the causal history up to the divergence point does not determine a unique outcome in overdetermination cases.
∴Overdetermination cases generate incorrect verdicts for the similarity framework; the causal grounding Jackson proposes does not resolve these cases; a different evaluative procedure is required.
Argument III — Comparative probability semantics handles symmetric and overdetermination cases that the similarity framework cannot
1.Comparative probability semantics: A ◻ C is evaluated by comparing the probability of worlds where A&C with worlds where A&¬C, given the antecedent. This distributes weight across worlds rather than selecting one.
2.Bizet-Verdi: probability is equal across candidate worlds, so neither counterfactual is true — the correct result. Overdetermination: probability of breaking given no rocks is low — the counterfactual is false, also correct.
3.Comparative probability semantics handles the cases the Lewis framework cannot; this is a reason to prefer it over the similarity framework as the basis for counterfactual semantics.
∴Comparative probability semantics correctly handles the symmetric and overdetermination cases that defeat the similarity framework; it is the better evaluative procedure for counterfactual conditionals.

Chair,

I want to challenge something more fundamental than the similarity metric: the assumption that counterfactual conditionals have truth values at all, and that the right approach to their semantics is to provide truth conditions. I think this assumption is mistaken, and I think Lewis’s own theoretical work provides the resources to see why.

“The right account of the probability of conditionals is Adams’s: the probability of ‘If A, C’ is the conditional probability of C given A. This requires that conditionals do not have truth values in the standard sense.” — Edgington
i. lewis’s triviality results

Triviality — Lewis proved that if conditionals have truth values and Pr(A→C) = Pr(C|A) for all probability functions, then there are at most two propositions; the result shows truth-conditional approaches to conditionals are incompatible with Adams's ThesisLewis proved a series of triviality results showing that no truth-conditional account of the conditional is compatible with the equation Pr(A ◻ C) = Pr(C|A). The proof is elegant: if “A ◻ C” names a genuine proposition and its probability always equals the conditional probability, then by conditionalization, we can derive that the probability space has at most two atoms — which means there are at most two propositions. This is absurd. Bennett’s response is to say that Adams’ Thesis must be restricted: it holds as an approximation for assertibility but not as a strict equation. I think this response gets the moral of the triviality results backwards. Adams’ Thesis is not an approximation; it is the correct and fundamental principle governing our epistemic relationship to conditionals. What the triviality results show is that conditionals do not have truth values in the standard sense. The semantics of conditionals is the epistemology of conditionals.

ii. the assertibility account of conditionals

Assertibility — the correct account of conditional semantics is assertibility-conditional, not truth-conditional; a conditional is assertible to the degree that the conditional probability of its consequent given its antecedent is high; this is not a reduction of semantics to epistemology but the correct account of semantic contentI want to distinguish the assertibility account from a simple pragmatic account of conditional assertibility. I am not saying that conditionals have truth conditions but that we assert them only when the conditional probability is high. I am saying something more radical: the semantic content of a conditional just is its assertibility condition, and the assertibility condition of a conditional is given by the conditional probability. A conditional “If A, C” does not say anything about any possible world; it expresses a probabilistic relation between two propositions. It is true that this means conditionals cannot be embedded in standard Boolean operations: we cannot negate a conditional in the standard way, and conditionals cannot appear as the antecedent of another conditional without creating problems. But these are features of conditionals that the truth-conditional framework struggles to explain naturally, and the assertibility account explains them straightforwardly as consequences of the probabilistic character of conditional content.

iii. the possible worlds framework and epistemic probability

Epistemic probability — the possible worlds framework smuggles in a notion of similarity that is epistemic rather than metaphysical; the framework is best understood as a representation of epistemic probability rather than as a genuinely truth-conditional semanticsI want to press a deeper diagnosis of why the possible worlds framework has the appeal it does despite its problems. The Stalnaker-Lewis framework is attractive, I think, because the selection function is implicitly tracking epistemic probability. The “closest world in which A holds” is intuitively the world in which A holds that is most likely given our background knowledge. This is why the framework delivers plausible verdicts in the cases it handles well: it is implicitly using the conditional probability, not a metaphysical similarity relation. But this means the framework is best understood not as a truth-conditional semantics but as a representation of the epistemic probability relation that Adams’ Thesis directly captures. Stalnaker’s selection function represents a rational belief revision operation — he says so himself. The truth-conditional trappings are a misleading surface form over an essentially probabilistic semantic content.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Lewis’s triviality results show truth-conditional accounts are incompatible with Adams’ Thesis; the Thesis should be retained
1.Lewis’s triviality results: if conditionals have truth values and Pr(A ◻ C) = Pr(C|A) for all probability functions, then there are at most two propositions — absurd.
2.Adams’ Thesis is not an approximation but the correct and fundamental principle governing our epistemic relationship to conditionals. It captures what conditionals mean to us as reasoners.
3.Given the triviality results, the correct response is to abandon truth values for conditionals, not to restrict Adams’ Thesis. The Thesis is more secure than the assumption that conditionals have truth values.
∴Lewis’s triviality results show that truth-conditional accounts of conditionals are incompatible with Adams’ Thesis; since the Thesis is more securely grounded than the truth-value assumption, conditionals should be understood without truth conditions.
Argument II — The assertibility account: conditional content just is the conditional probability; this is not pragmatics but semantics
1.The assertibility account: the semantic content of “If A, C” is its assertibility condition, given by the conditional probability Pr(C|A). This is not a pragmatic account of when we assert; it is a semantic account of what conditionals express.
2.Conditionals express probabilistic relations between propositions, not propositions about possible worlds. They cannot be embedded in standard Boolean operations, and this is a feature, not a bug: it reflects their probabilistic character.
3.Problems with embedability that the assertibility account generates are not more severe than the problems the truth-conditional account faces with the similarity metric and triviality.
∴The assertibility account captures the semantic content of conditionals as conditional probability; it is a semantic, not merely pragmatic, account; its embedability difficulties are not more severe than the truth-conditional account’s problems.
Argument III — The selection function implicitly tracks epistemic probability; the framework is best understood as a representation of Adams’ Thesis
1.The “closest world where A holds” is intuitively the world where A holds that is most likely given our background knowledge. The selection function tracks epistemic probability.
2.This explains why the framework delivers plausible verdicts: it implicitly uses conditional probability, not a metaphysical similarity relation. Stalnaker himself describes the selection function as representing rational belief revision.
3.The truth-conditional trappings are a misleading surface form over an essentially probabilistic semantic content. The framework is best understood as a representation of Adams’ Thesis, not as a genuinely truth-conditional semantics.
∴The Stalnaker-Lewis framework implicitly tracks epistemic probability; its truth-conditional trappings are misleading; it is best understood as a representation of Adams’ probabilistic account rather than a genuinely truth-conditional semantics.

Chair,

I want to press the objection that is perhaps least discussed in the literature but that I regard as the most fundamental: the Stalnaker-Lewis framework is built for deterministic worlds. In an indeterministic world — and physics strongly suggests the actual world is indeterministic — the framework’s central concept of a “closest possible world” becomes deeply problematic.

i. the problem of chance and the closest world

Indeterminism problem — in a world with genuine objective chances, two worlds can be nomologically identical up to a branch point and then diverge by chance; no similarity metric can rank these worlds because they are nomologically equidistant; the selection function is undefinedIn a deterministic world, if you specify the complete history of the world up to some time t and fix the laws, you fix the complete future of the world. There is a unique closest world for any antecedent: the one that shares the actual history up to the minimal divergence point and then evolves deterministically from there. But in an indeterministic world — one with genuine quantum-mechanical objective chances — two worlds can be nomologically identical up to a branch point and then diverge by chance, not by miracle. There is no law violation in either world at the divergence point; they both evolve in accordance with the actual laws, which allow multiple outcomes with different probabilities. The Stalnaker-Lewis framework has no principled way to rank these worlds. The closest-world selection function is undefined in the face of genuine chance: if two worlds share the actual history, share the actual laws, and diverge only by the chance outcomes of indeterministic processes, there is no similarity-based reason to prefer one over the other.

ii. the Lewisian response and its failures

Lewisian response fails — Lewis acknowledges indeterminism but says we can still rank worlds by which differences are law-violations and which are chance outcomes; but this requires that chance outcomes be ranked by their probability, which is not what the similarity metric doesLewis acknowledges the problem of indeterminism and suggests that the framework can be extended: what matters is not whether a world evolves deterministically from the antecedent, but whether it evolves in accordance with the actual laws. In an indeterministic world, the closest A-world is the one where A obtains and where the subsequent evolution is in accordance with the actual laws of chance. But this response conceals a difficulty: in an indeterministic world, multiple futures are compatible with the actual laws. The framework must rank these futures, and the natural ranking is by their objective probability. But the similarity metric does not rank worlds by their objective probability; it ranks them by their overall similarity to the actual world. In cases where a low-probability outcome produces a world that is overall more similar to the actual world than a high-probability outcome would produce, the similarity framework will deliver verdicts that conflict with what the probability-based ranking would deliver.

iii. probabilistic semantics as the correct response

Probabilistic semantics — a probabilistic semantics that evaluates counterfactuals by probability distributions over worlds is better equipped for indeterministic worlds; it replaces the undefined closest-world selection with a well-defined probability measureIn my work on probabilistic semantics for counterfactuals, I propose replacing the selection of a single closest world with a probability distribution over antecedent-worlds, weighted by their objective chance given the actual history and laws up to the time of the antecedent. A counterfactual A ◻ C is then true (or true to a degree) iff the probability of C in the distribution over A-worlds is sufficiently high. This handles indeterminism directly: in an indeterministic world, the distribution over closest A-worlds just is the objective chance distribution over futures compatible with the antecedent. The framework does not require selecting a unique closest world; it requires specifying a probability measure over candidate worlds, which physics can in principle provide. This probabilistic semantics is a genuine improvement on the Stalnaker-Lewis framework: it handles everything the Lewis framework handles in deterministic cases and extends naturally to indeterministic ones where the Lewis framework requires artificial stipulation or breaks down entirely.

Thank you.

Argument I — In indeterministic worlds, nomologically identical worlds can diverge by chance; the similarity metric cannot rank them; the selection function is undefined
1.In a world with objective chances (as physics suggests the actual world has), two worlds can share the actual history up to a branch point and diverge without law violation, purely by chance.
2.Such worlds are nomologically equidistant from actuality: neither involves a miracle, both are compatible with the actual laws. The similarity metric has no principled basis for ranking them.
3.The selection function is therefore undefined in indeterministic cases: there is no unique closest A-world when multiple A-worlds are nomologically equidistant from actuality.
∴The Stalnaker-Lewis framework breaks down in indeterministic worlds: nomologically equidistant chance-worlds cannot be ranked by the similarity metric; the selection function is undefined for the most important cases in the actual world.
Argument II — Lewis’s response to indeterminism requires ranking by objective probability, which is not what the similarity metric provides
1.Lewis’s response: in indeterministic worlds, the closest A-world is the one where subsequent evolution is in accordance with actual laws of chance. But multiple futures are compatible with the laws.
2.Ranking these futures requires using their objective probabilities. But the similarity metric ranks by overall world-similarity, not by objective probability. In cases where a low-probability outcome produces a more similar world, the two rankings diverge.
3.When similarity and probability diverge, the framework delivers verdicts that conflict with what a probability-based ranking would deliver — and probability-based verdicts are more plausible in indeterministic contexts.
∴Lewis’s extension to indeterminism implicitly requires probability-ranking, which the similarity metric does not provide; when similarity and probability diverge, the framework delivers wrong verdicts.
Argument III — Probabilistic semantics handles indeterminism directly by replacing world-selection with probability distributions over antecedent-worlds
1.Probabilistic semantics: A ◻ C is true to the degree that C is probable in the distribution over A-worlds weighted by objective chance given the actual history and laws.
2.This handles indeterminism directly: in a world with objective chances, the distribution over A-worlds just is the chance distribution over futures compatible with the antecedent. No unique-world selection is required.
3.The framework handles deterministic cases as a limiting case (where the chance distribution is degenerate) and extends naturally to indeterministic ones. It is a strict improvement on the Stalnaker-Lewis framework.
∴Probabilistic semantics is strictly better than the Stalnaker-Lewis framework: it handles indeterministic worlds directly by replacing undefined world-selection with well-defined probability distributions over antecedent-worlds.
