Mr President,

I want to begin by describing the phenomenon I observed, because I believe it is both more peculiar and more philosophically significant than subsequent discussion has always recognised. Consider the following sentence:

It is raining, but I don't believe it is.

This sentence is not self-contradictory. It does not assert a logical impossibility. There are perfectly ordinary circumstances in which it would be true — it happens all the time that it is raining while someone fails to believe it is. And yet, if I were to assert this sentence sincerely, I would be saying something profoundly absurd. Not merely false — absurd. Something has gone wrong, but what?

On the two forms of the paradox

Two forms: omissive and commissiveThere are actually two distinct forms of the paradox, and any adequate solution must address both. The omissive form — "It is raining, but I don't believe it is" (p ∧ ¬Bp) — asserts the fact while denying belief in it. The commissive form — "It is raining, but I believe it isn't" (p ∧ B¬p) — asserts the fact while asserting a contrary belief. Many proposed solutions address only the omissive form. I insist that a genuine solution must account for both, because the absurdity is present in both. The commissive form is in some ways more striking: it is possible to believe a falsehood, and so there is nothing incoherent about believing it isn't raining while it is. And yet, asserting this openly is just as absurd as the omissive form.

On the asymmetry between first and third person

First-person asymmetry: no absurdity in the third person or past tenseThe paradox is confined to the first person, present tense. There is no absurdity in asserting "It is raining, but Jones doesn't believe it is" — that is a perfectly sensible observation about someone's failure of knowledge. There is no absurdity in asserting "It was raining, but I didn't believe it was" — that is a perfectly natural report of a past state of ignorance. The absurdity arises specifically and exclusively when I, now, assert both that p is the case and that I, now, don't believe it. This restriction to the first-person present tense is a clue to the solution. It tells us that the problem is not in the content of what is said — not in any logical or metaphysical impossibility — but in the performance of asserting it.

On what assertion pragmatically implies

Moore's solution: assertion implies belief; the Moorean sentence is self-underminingMy own diagnosis is this. When a person makes an assertion — sincerely, in normal circumstances — she implies that she believes what she is asserting. This is not a logical implication; it is what I called a pragmatic implication. It is part of what it means to make a sincere assertion, rather than to suppose, hypothesise, or quote. When I assert "It is raining," I imply that I believe it is raining. When I then add "but I don't believe it is raining," I explicitly deny the very thing my act of assertion has implied. The result is self-undermining: the act of assertion and the content of what is asserted are in conflict. This is the source of the absurdity — not a contradiction between two propositions but a contradiction between what the speech act implies and what the content asserts.

On the knowledge version

The knowledge version: "It is raining but I don't know it is"There is a related but distinct version of the paradox involving knowledge rather than belief: "It is raining but I don't know it is." This too is unassertable, though for subtly different reasons. Asserting p implies not merely believing p but — on some views — claiming to know p. If that is right, the knowledge version is even more directly self-undermining: I am asserting something while explicitly denying that I know it. This connects to the broader epistemology of assertion — the debate about whether the norm of assertion is knowledge rather than mere justified belief. But for the basic paradox, the belief-implication is sufficient to explain the absurdity, and I am not committed to the stronger knowledge norm.

On why the paradox matters

Why the paradox matters: it reveals something about the logic of assertionWittgenstein, in a generous letter, told me I had "said something about the logic of assertion." I think that is right. The paradox reveals that assertion is not merely the communication of propositional content — it is a speech act with constitutive norms, one of which is that the asserter implies belief in what she asserts. These norms are not reducible to the logical content of what is said; they belong to the pragmatic dimension of assertion. This is the first insight. The second is that the paradox is not primarily a paradox about belief or self-knowledge — it is a paradox about what you can sincerely say, and its location in the pragmatics of speech is where the solution belongs.

Mr President,

When I attended Moore's talk at the Moral Sciences Club in October 1944 and heard him describe the absurdity of "It is raining but I don't believe it is," I wrote to him immediately. I told him that he had said something about the logic of assertion — that the absurdity "is in fact something similar to a contradiction, though it isn't one" — and that "this just shows that logic isn't as simple as logicians think it is." I want to explain what I meant by this, because I think it goes considerably deeper than Moore's own account, and considerably deeper than the Gricean pragmatic story that will be offered by the next speaker.

On the asymmetry of "I believe" in the first person present

First-person asymmetry: "I believe p" is not a psychological reportThe key to the paradox lies in understanding what "I believe p" does in the first-person present tense. When I say "I believe it is raining," I am not making a report about my psychological state in the way that I would be if I said "Jones believes it is raining." When someone asks "Is it raining?" and I answer "I believe it is," they cannot complain: "Don't be irrelevant — I asked about the weather, not about your mind." The answer is perfectly relevant. This is because "I believe p" in the first-person present functions as a form of assertion of p — a hedged, less committed assertion, but an assertion nonetheless. It presents p as how things are, from my standpoint. It is not a detached psychological observation about my inner state.

On what this shows about Moore's sentence

Moore's sentence combines assertion with a denial of the asserter's standpointOnce we see that "I believe p" in the first person is a mode of asserting p — of presenting p as my view of how things are — we can see why "p but I don't believe p" is peculiar. The first conjunct asserts p as how things are. The second conjunct "I don't believe p" expresses something like a standing-back from any assertoric commitment — a denial that p represents my view of the world. But one cannot simultaneously present p as how things are and deny that it represents one's view. The self-undermining is not merely pragmatic, in Grice's sense of a violated maxim — it is a deeper incoherence in the logical grammar of assertion and the first-person use of belief-language.

On why this is about grammar, not psychology

Grammar not psychology: the rule is a grammatical rule about how these words workMoore described the absurdity as existing "for psychological reasons," and I told him this was wrong — or at least highly misleading. The problem is not psychological. It is grammatical — it concerns the logical grammar of assertion and the use of "I believe" in the first-person present. The rule that you cannot sincerely assert p while denying belief in p is not a contingent psychological generalisation about what minds can do. It is a grammatical rule about what these words mean — about how assertion functions in our language. To violate it is not to make a psychological mistake; it is to produce something that, like a contradiction, is ruled out by the grammar of the language itself.

On why the opposition's self-knowledge solution misses the point

Against the self-knowledge solution: the paradox is not about failure of self-knowledgeThe opposition will argue that the paradox ultimately reveals something about belief and self-knowledge — that a rational agent's beliefs must be transparent to her, and that Moorean belief is absurd because it involves first-order belief without appropriate higher-order awareness. But this explanation puts the cart before the horse. The logical grammar of assertion and first-person belief-language is prior to any theory of self-knowledge. We do not first understand self-knowledge and then derive the grammar of "I believe." Rather, the grammar of "I believe" — what it does and does not allow — is what frames the problem of self-knowledge. The paradox is a grammatical phenomenon that certain theories of self-knowledge are then called upon to explain; it is not itself explained by those theories.

On the connection to the distinction between assertion and supposition

Assertion vs supposition: "Suppose p and I don't believe p" is perfectly coherentOne of the most revealing features of the paradox is this: while I cannot assert "It is raining but I don't believe it," I can perfectly well suppose or hypothesise this. "Let us suppose that it is raining but I don't believe it" makes perfect sense — it invites us to consider a possible situation. The absurdity is confined to assertion; it does not attach to the propositional content considered in abstraction from any speech act. This shows that the paradox is indeed about the logic of assertion — specifically, about the contrast between the force of assertion and the force of supposition. Assertion commits the speaker to the content in a way that supposition does not; and it is that commitment, and its relation to the self-ascription of belief, that the paradox illuminates.

Mr President,

I want to offer the most parsimonious solution to Moore's paradox — a solution that explains the absurdity entirely within the framework of the pragmatics of assertion, without any appeal to the special grammar of first-person belief reports (which Wittgenstein invokes) and without any appeal to the metaphysics of belief or the epistemology of self-knowledge (which the opposition invokes). The solution follows directly from the Cooperative Principle and its maxims of conversational implicature.

On the Cooperative Principle and the maxim of Quality

The Cooperative Principle: the maxim of Quality grounds the absurdityThe Cooperative Principle holds that conversational contributions should be such as is required, at the stage at which they occur, by the accepted purpose or direction of the conversation. Among the maxims that specify this principle is the maxim of Quality: do not say what you believe to be false; do not say that for which you lack adequate evidence. When I assert p in normal conversational circumstances, I conversationally implicate that I believe p — because asserting p without believing it would be a violation of the Quality maxim that any rational cooperative communicator would recognise and avoid. This implicature is not a logical entailment — it is a Gricean conversational implicature, defeasible by context.

On how implicature explains the paradox

Implicature explains the paradox: asserting p implicates Bp; denying Bp cancels the implicature and undermines the assertionWhen I assert "It is raining but I don't believe it is," the first conjunct "It is raining" implicates that I believe it is raining — by the maxim of Quality. The second conjunct "I don't believe it is raining" explicitly cancels this implicature. The result is not a contradiction — nothing logically impossible has been asserted. Rather, the sentence is pragmatically self-defeating: I have asserted something and simultaneously withdrawn the conversational ground that makes my assertion an assertion. I have, in effect, told my audience: here is my assertion, but I am not cooperating with the norms that make assertions informative. The absurdity is the absurdity of an utterance that is pragmatically incoherent rather than logically impossible.

On the advantages of the pragmatic solution

Advantages of the pragmatic solution: explains the paradox without revising logic or theory of beliefThe pragmatic solution has several significant advantages over its rivals. First, it does not require any revision of the logic of belief — we need not say anything unusual about the structure of rational belief or about the conditions for rational self-knowledge. Second, it explains why the absurdity can be cancelled in certain contexts: in fiction, in philosophical illustration, in suppositions, in reported speech — precisely those contexts where the Cooperative Principle is suspended or modified. Third, it explains the connection to similar absurdities in other speech acts: "I promise to do X but I have no intention of doing X" is similarly self-defeating, for analogous implicature reasons. The paradox is one instance of a general pattern of pragmatic self-defeat.

On the objection from iterated belief

Against the objection from iterated belief: the paradox of Moorean belief is derivativeThe opposition will argue that there is a paradox of Moorean belief — not just Moorean assertion — and that any adequate solution must explain why it would be irrational to believe "p and I don't believe p," not just irrational to assert it. I want to resist this move. The paradox of Moorean belief, to the extent it exists, is derivative on the paradox of Moorean assertion. It is because we understand that to sincerely hold the belief that p is to be disposed to assert p — to take p as representing how things are — that we see incoherence in holding p while also holding that one does not believe p. The belief-incoherence is explained by the assertoric norms that govern belief, not the other way around. Shoemaker's priority thesis gets the direction of explanation backwards.

On why the pragmatic solution is complete

The pragmatic solution is complete: no deeper theory is neededThe paradox is fully explained once we recognise that assertion conversationally implicates belief in what is asserted, and that Moorean sentences cancel this implicature while making the assertion. No deeper theory of belief, self-knowledge, or consciousness is needed to explain the absurdity. The opposition's theories — higher-order thought, doxastic logic, self-transparency — are solutions to a problem that does not exist at the level they are targeting. They are trying to explain why Moorean belief is irrational; but Moorean assertion is the primary phenomenon, and its irrationality is fully explained by the maxim of Quality. The paradox is a pragmatic phenomenon through and through.

Mr President,

The proposition has told us that Moore's paradox is fundamentally about assertion — about the pragmatics of speech — and that its solution requires nothing more than recognising that assertion implicates belief. I want to argue that this account, while it captures something real, stops at a surface symptom rather than reaching the underlying condition. The phenomenon of Moorean absurdity is present not only in assertion but in belief itself — and any account that explains only the assertoric absurdity without explaining why it would be equally absurd to genuinely believe a Moorean proposition has not explained the phenomenon at its root.

On Moorean belief — not just Moorean assertion

Moorean belief: holding "p and I don't believe p" is itself absurdConsider a person who sincerely, genuinely holds the belief that it is raining and also sincerely, genuinely holds the belief that she does not believe it is raining. Setting aside the question of how she came to express this — setting aside assertion entirely — the belief state itself is incoherent. This is Moorean belief, as opposed to Moorean assertion. The agent has a first-order belief that p and a second-order belief that she lacks the first-order belief that p. These cannot both be true of the same rational agent at the same time. The incoherence is in the belief state, not in the act of asserting it. And the Gricean account has nothing to say about this — it concerns assertibility conditions, not the rationality of belief states.

On the priority thesis

Priority thesis: explain belief-absurdity first; assertion-absurdity followsI call this the priority thesis: if you have an adequate explanation of why it is irrational to hold a Moorean belief — why the belief state "p and I don't believe p" is incoherent — then you have thereby an explanation of why it is incoherent to sincerely assert a Moorean sentence. This is because sincere assertion expresses belief: to sincerely assert p is to express the belief that p. If the belief state expressed by a Moorean assertion were coherent, the assertion would be coherent too. It is precisely because the belief state is incoherent that the assertion inheriting that belief state is incoherent. The assertion-absurdity is a downstream consequence of the belief-absurdity, not its source.

On what grounds the belief-absurdity

Grounding the belief-absurdity: rational self-knowledge and the transparency of beliefWhat explains why Moorean belief is irrational? The explanation I propose is this: for a rational agent, belief is transparent to itself. A rational agent who believes p is thereby in a position to know that she believes p — the first-order belief and the second-order belief are necessarily aligned in a rational agent. This transparency of belief is not a contingent psychological fact; it is a constitutive feature of what it means to have a rational, conscious belief. A belief that could persist entirely without any second-order awareness — a belief that the agent could simultaneously correctly represent herself as lacking — is not a genuinely rational belief in the full sense. Moorean belief is absurd because it violates this constitutive feature of rational self-knowledge.

On the objection from the Gricean account

Against Grice: conversational implicature cannot explain belief-absurdityGrice's account explains why it would be pragmatically self-defeating to assert a Moorean sentence in normal conversational contexts. But it cannot explain why it would be irrational to hold a Moorean belief — why a rational agent cannot simultaneously have the belief that p and the higher-order belief that she lacks this belief. Conversational implicature is a feature of assertoric speech acts; it has no application to the private states of belief. The Gricean account leaves unexplained a phenomenon that clearly exists — the irrationality of Moorean belief — and this unexplained phenomenon is more fundamental than the assertoric absurdity the Gricean correctly explains.

On self-knowledge and what the paradox ultimately reveals

What the paradox reveals: the mind's transparency to itself and the constitutive link between belief and self-knowledgeMoore's paradox, properly understood, reveals something deep about the relationship between first-order mental states and second-order awareness of them. For genuinely rational, conscious beings, there is a constitutive link between having a belief and being in a position to know one has it. This link is what makes self-knowledge possible — and what makes Moorean belief impossible. The paradox is not merely a curiosity about what can be said. It is a window into the structure of rational consciousness — into what it means for a mind to be transparent to itself. The proposition's account, which locates the solution entirely in the pragmatics of assertion, sees the window but misses what lies beyond it.

Mr President,

Shoemaker has given the philosophical case from the theory of self-knowledge. I want to press deeper still, into the theory of consciousness. My claim is that Moore's paradox is intimately connected to the conditions under which a mental state is a conscious mental state — and that my Higher-Order Thought theory of consciousness provides the most illuminating account of why Moorean sentences are not merely pragmatically awkward but reveal something fundamental about the relationship between believing and knowing that one believes.

On Higher-Order Thought theory

HOT theory: a mental state is conscious iff accompanied by an appropriate higher-order thoughtMy Higher-Order Thought theory holds that a mental state is a conscious mental state if and only if it is accompanied by an appropriate higher-order thought — a thought to the effect that one is in that mental state. When I am consciously believing that it is raining, I am in a first-order belief state and I also have a higher-order thought: roughly, the thought that I am believing it is raining. When I am in a first-order belief state without any accompanying higher-order thought, that state is not a conscious state — it is a mental state that I am not aware of having. Beliefs without higher-order thoughts are not accessible to sincere assertion; they cannot be expressed or reported.

On how HOT theory explains Moore's paradox

HOT explains the paradox: asserting p requires conscious belief in p; a HOT to the effect that one lacks this belief is incoherentMoore's paradox arises from a conflict between the conditions for sincere assertion and the structure of conscious belief as HOT theory describes it. To sincerely assert p, the agent must have a conscious belief that p — a first-order belief accompanied by a higher-order thought that she has this belief. But when she adds "I don't believe p," she is expressing a higher-order thought to the effect that she lacks the first-order belief in p. She simultaneously has a higher-order thought that she believes p (required for the sincere assertion of p) and a higher-order thought that she does not believe p (expressed by the second conjunct). The contradiction is at the level of higher-order thoughts — it is a contradiction in the agent's own self-conception, not in the propositional content of what she asserts.

On why this is deeper than Grice's implicature

HOT is deeper than Grice: the contradiction is in the HOTs, not in the pragmaticsGrice explains the absurdity as a violation of conversational maxims — a pragmatic self-defeat that depends on the communicative context. But HOT theory locates the problem at a more fundamental level: in the structure of the agent's own higher-order thoughts about her mental states. The contradiction in the HOTs is present regardless of whether the agent is communicating with anyone. Even a solitary agent who merely entertains the Moorean thought — not as a supposition but as a sincere judgment about her own current state — is in the grip of a conflict between higher-order thoughts. The absurdity is in the mind, not in the communicative act, and HOT theory reveals its location precisely.

On the relationship between HOT theory and Shoemaker's account

HOT and Shoemaker: converging on the same point from different directionsShoemaker's account and my HOT account converge on the same fundamental insight: the paradox is grounded in the conditions for rational, conscious self-knowledge, not in the pragmatics of assertion. Where we differ is in the theoretical framework. Shoemaker grounds the transparency of belief in constitutive features of rational self-knowledge — in what it means for a mind to have genuine access to its own states. I ground it in the structural requirement of higher-order thoughts for conscious mental states. Both accounts agree that the proposition's assertoric solution is incomplete, and both agree that what is missing is an account of the relationship between first-order believing and second-order awareness of believing.

On the Descartes connection — Moore's paradox and the cogito

Moore and Descartes: Moorean absurdity mirrors the cogito's self-evidenceThere is a deep connection between Moore's paradox and Descartes's cogito that has not received sufficient attention. The sentence "I don't exist" has unproblematic truth conditions — each of us was once non-existent — and yet it has no coherent assertibility conditions, because the act of asserting it presupposes the existence of the asserter. This is the Cartesian version of the paradox. The Moorean sentence "p but I don't believe p" has a similar structure: the act of asserting it presupposes — through the requirement of conscious belief — the very state it denies. Both paradoxes reveal that assertion is not merely the communication of propositional content but the expression of a conscious mental state, and that the conditions for that mental state can conflict with the content expressed. HOT theory is the theoretical framework that makes this connection explicit.

Mr President,

My colleagues Shoemaker and Rosenthal have argued from self-knowledge and the theory of consciousness. I want to make a different and more formal argument: that Moore's paradox reveals a violation of the axioms of doxastic logic — the formal logic of belief — and that any adequate solution must be grounded in a logical account of the norms governing rational belief systems, not merely in the pragmatics of speech or in informal claims about self-transparency.

On doxastic logic and its axioms

Doxastic logic: the formal logic of rational belief systemsIn my work on epistemic and doxastic logic, I developed formal systems for reasoning about knowledge and belief. The key axiom for our purposes is what is sometimes called the positive introspection axiom or the KK-principle applied to belief: if an agent believes p, then the agent believes that she believes p. In formal notation: Bp → BBp. This axiom captures the transparency of belief — the idea that a rational agent's beliefs are accessible to her second-order awareness. If this axiom holds, then a rational agent who believes p also believes that she believes p. And this means that "Bp ∧ B¬Bp" — believing p while believing that one does not believe p — is formally inconsistent in a system satisfying this axiom.

On how doxastic logic explains Moore's paradox

Doxastic logic explains the omissive form: Bp → BBp rules out BpB¬BpThe omissive Moorean sentence in its belief form — "p but I don't believe p" — translates into the doxastic structure Bp ∧ B¬Bp. A rational agent who believes p is, by the introspection axiom, also in the state BBp — she believes she believes p. She cannot simultaneously be in the state B¬Bp — believing she does not believe p — without formal inconsistency, since BBp and B¬Bp are contradictory (they are both beliefs about the same proposition, with opposite contents). The omissive Moorean belief state is formally inconsistent in any doxastic system that includes the positive introspection axiom. This is not a pragmatic fact about what can be said — it is a logical fact about what can be consistently believed.

On the commissive form

The commissive form: Bp ∧ B¬p requires the agent to believe p and believe ¬pThe commissive Moorean sentence — "p but I believe it isn't" — translates to the doxastic structure Bp ∧ B¬p. A rational agent cannot have both Bp and B¬p without violating the consistency condition on belief — the axiom that a rational agent does not believe both p and ¬p simultaneously. The commissive form is thus directly a formal inconsistency in any minimal doxastic logic that includes the consistency axiom. This is more straightforwardly a formal contradiction than the omissive form, which requires the introspection axiom to generate its inconsistency. The formal approach explains both forms within a unified doxastic logic framework.

On the objection that the introspection axiom is too strong

The introspection axiom objection: Bp → BBp may be too strong for actual agentsThe proposition will object that the positive introspection axiom — Bp → BBp — is an idealisation that does not hold for actual agents, who routinely fail to have accurate second-order beliefs about their first-order beliefs. This is a genuine objection. Human agents are not perfectly introspective. But the objection misunderstands the status of doxastic logic. The axioms of doxastic logic describe conditions of ideal rationality — norms that govern what a fully rational agent's belief system would look like. The paradox arises for ideally rational agents; for them the axiom holds, and the Moorean belief state is formally inconsistent. The pragmatic approach similarly involves idealisations — the Cooperative Principle applies to ideally cooperative communicators. Both approaches involve normative idealisations; doxastic logic's idealisation is at least as defensible as Grice's.

On what the formal approach shows that the pragmatic approach misses

What formal logic shows: the paradox is a logical not merely pragmatic phenomenonThe deepest advantage of the formal approach is that it reveals the paradox to be a logical phenomenon — a violation of formal axioms governing rational belief — rather than merely a pragmatic one. This matters because logical violations are more fundamental than pragmatic ones. A pragmatic violation can be cancelled by context — Grice himself acknowledges that the Cooperative Principle can be suspended in fictional, hypothetical, and philosophical contexts. But a logical inconsistency cannot be cancelled by context. If the Moorean belief state is formally inconsistent, no context renders it coherent. The formal approach predicts this; the pragmatic approach does not. And the data bears it out: even in explicitly hypothetical and philosophical contexts, the Moorean sentence retains a residual absurdity that mere implicature-cancellation cannot dissolve.
