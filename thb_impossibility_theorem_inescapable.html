Mr Speaker,

I want to open this debate by placing on the table the observation that began it — the observation that led me to devote the final part of Reasons and Persons to what I then called Theory X, the as-yet-undiscovered population axiology that would avoid all the paradoxes. I have since come to doubt that Theory X exists. The work of Arrhenius and others has given that doubt a rigorous mathematical form.

The starting point is total utilitarianism: the theory that makes the goodness of a population a function of the sum of the welfare of all its members. It is the most natural theory of population ethics, and it is the theory that most policy-relevant reasoning implicitly adopts. And it entails something I call the Repugnant Conclusion.

The Repugnant Conclusion, stated precisely.

For any possible population of at least ten billion people, all with a very high quality of life, there must be some much larger imaginable population whose existence, if other things are equal, would be better, even though its members have lives that are barely worth living.

The conclusion is entailed by total utilitarianism because: a sufficiently large number of lives each containing even a tiny positive welfare surplus will have a greater total than any fixed number of lives at high welfare. The sum can always be increased by adding enough marginally positive lives. The conclusion is “repugnant” because the vast world of lives barely worth living seems clearly worse than the small flourishing world, even if its total welfare is higher.
i. the repugnant conclusion as a genuine constraint

Repugnant Conclusion — total utilitarianism forces it; avoidance requires abandoning either additivity or unrestricted domain; trade-off is structuralThe Repugnant Conclusion is not a quirk of an implausible theory; it is forced by two features that almost every plausible population axiology shares: additivity (the value of a population is in some sense a function of the values of individual lives) and the principle that adding a happy life to a population makes things better, other things equal. These two features, combined with the possibility of populations of any size, generate the Repugnant Conclusion. Any theory that avoids it must either deny that adding a happy life always makes things better — which generates the Asymmetry Paradox — or deny the additive structure — which generates other paradoxes of its own. The avoidance of the Repugnant Conclusion is not free; it has a price, and that price is always another impossibility result.

ii. average utilitarianism as no escape

Average utilitarianism — avoids Repugnant Conclusion but entails Mere Addition Paradox and anti-natalist implications; merely displaces the impossibilityThe most obvious escape from the Repugnant Conclusion is to switch from total to average utilitarianism: evaluate populations by average welfare rather than total welfare. But average utilitarianism generates its own repugnant consequences. It implies that adding a person with welfare above the current average but below the welfare of existing people is bad — even though that person has a good life and contributes positively to total welfare. More strikingly, it implies that we could improve the world by eliminating everyone with below-average welfare, since this would raise the average. These conclusions are at least as counterintuitive as the Repugnant Conclusion. The switch to average utilitarianism merely displaces the impossibility; it does not escape it.

iii. the mere addition paradox and the non-identity problem

Mere Addition Paradox — adding happy people seems good; but leads to Repugnant Conclusion by transitivity; any blocking generates its own paradoxThe Mere Addition Paradox demonstrates the tightness of the impossibility. Consider three populations. Population A: a billion people with very high welfare. Population A+: A plus an additional billion people with moderately high welfare and no effect on the first billion. Population B: two billion people at welfare intermediate between A and A+’s additional people. Most people judge: A+ is at least as good as A (Mere Addition seems permissible); B is better than A+ (it is more equal and has higher average welfare); therefore B is better than A. But B is one step towards the Repugnant Conclusion world, and by iterating this argument we reach it. Any theory that blocks the Repugnant Conclusion must deny one of these three judgements, but each denial generates its own counterintuitive implication. The impossibility is in the structure of the judgements themselves, not in the theories.

iv. conjecture: no theory escapes all paradoxes

Theory X conjecture — Parfit's original conjecture formalised by Arrhenius; structural impossibility, not mere theoretical incompletenessI ended Part IV of Reasons and Persons with a conjecture that I have not been able to refute: that there is no Theory X — no population axiology that avoids the Repugnant Conclusion, the Mere Addition Paradox, the Average Principle’s counterexamples, and all the other paradoxes simultaneously. Arrhenius has since given this conjecture a rigorous proof, and the subsequent literature has extended the impossibility results to cover prioritarian, egalitarian, and person-affecting frameworks as well. The impossibility is not a symptom of theoretical incompleteness; it is a structural feature of the domain. Our intuitions about population ethics are mutually inconsistent, and no consistent axiology can satisfy all of them.

My contribution to this debate is primarily formal. I have proved a series of impossibility theorems showing that no population axiology can simultaneously satisfy a small number of adequacy conditions, each of which is independently compelling. I want to present the structure of these results and explain why they collectively establish the motion beyond reasonable doubt.

The key insight is that the impossibility is not about any particular paradox — the Repugnant Conclusion, the Asymmetry, the Non-Identity Problem — but about the formal structure of the domain. Any theory that satisfies conditions drawn from different intuitive traditions will generate a contradiction. The choice is not between a good theory and a bad one; it is between different distributions of the theoretical cost.

The formal structure of the impossibility theorems.

Each theorem identifies a set of adequacy conditions C1, …, Cn and proves that no population ordering satisfies all of them simultaneously. The conditions are drawn from different intuitive sources: welfare addition (totalist intuitions), anti-repugnance (egalitarian intuitions), person-affecting restrictions (anti-impersonalist intuitions), and transitivity/completeness (formal rationality conditions).

The most general theorem (Arrhenius 2011) shows that no population axiology satisfies all of: the Egalitarian Dominance Condition, the General Non-Extreme Priority Condition, the Non-Sadism Condition, the Weak Non-Repugnance Condition, the Weak Quality Addition Condition, and the Weaker Non-Elitism Condition. This is a theorem about the structure of the domain, not about the inadequacy of specific theories.
i. the negative repugnant conclusion

Negative Repugnant Conclusion — any theory avoiding the positive RC faces the negative analogue; the impossibility is symmetricLess familiar than the positive Repugnant Conclusion is its negative mirror image: the Negative Repugnant Conclusion. For any population of miserable people, a sufficiently large population of people whose lives are barely not worth living is worse. Every theory that satisfies the most natural negative-domain conditions faces this conclusion by a parallel argument to the positive case. Theories that avoid the Repugnant Conclusion by deploying asymmetric treatments of positive and negative welfare — treating additions of barely-positive lives differently from the positive of barely-negative lives — face independent objections about the principled basis for this asymmetry. The impossibility is symmetric across the positive and negative domains.

ii. the monstrous conclusion

Monstrous Conclusion — any theory avoiding RC faces it or its negative twin; forced by Non-Sadism and welfare addition conditionsThe Monstrous Conclusion, identified in my 2000 paper, is the claim that a population of very many people with very good lives is worse than a population of very many people with lives barely worth living, given that the latter population is sufficiently large. This conclusion is entailed by any theory that: (i) avoids the Repugnant Conclusion by introducing a sufficiently steep diminishing value for additional lives as welfare approaches the minimum positive level; and (ii) satisfies the Non-Sadism Condition (adding lives with positive welfare is never worse than adding lives with negative welfare). The Monstrous Conclusion is not an isolated peculiarity; it is a consequence of the logical structure of any theory that tries to escape the Repugnant Conclusion while maintaining that positive-welfare additions are never sadistically bad.

iii. person-affecting views do not escape the theorem

Person-affecting views — Non-Identity Problem and the asymmetry together generate contradictions; person-affecting is not a clean escapePerson-affecting views hold that an outcome can only be worse than another if it is worse for some specific person. They appear to avoid the Repugnant Conclusion by refusing to count the welfare of possible people who do not exist in the baseline. But person-affecting views face the Non-Identity Problem: future generations may owe their existence to the very policies whose effects we are evaluating, so there is no specific person for whom the policy is worse. More formally, person-affecting views that incorporate the asymmetry — that it is bad to bring miserable people into existence but not obligatory to bring happy people into existence — generate contradictions when combined with the transitivity of “worse for” and the requirement that all welfare comparisons be person-relative. The asymmetry cannot be consistently embedded in a person-affecting framework without generating a violation of transitivity.

iv. the theorems cover all major frameworks

Framework coverage — totalism, averagism, critical-level, prioritarianism, egalitarianism all fall; no existing framework escapes the formal resultsThe range of the impossibility theorems has expanded to cover every major population-ethical framework. Total and average utilitarianism fall to the classical paradoxes. Critical-level utilitarianism (Broome, Blackorby and Donaldson) falls to the Sadistic Conclusion: setting the critical level above zero implies that it can be better to add miserable lives than happy ones, in populations of the right structure. Prioritarian views fall to the Welfare Diffusion Objection and the Monstrous Conclusion. Person-affecting views fall to the Non-Identity Problem. Pluralist-egalitarian views fall to the Ex-Ante Pareto vs. Dominance Dilemma and the Prospective Equality Paradox. No existing framework has been shown to escape all the proven conditions simultaneously. The impossibility is comprehensive.

My contribution to this debate is to show that the impossibility goes deeper than any particular theory. It is not that totalism, or prioritarianism, or egalitarianism fails on its own terms. It is that the very relation of being “better than” — the fundamental comparative relation in ethics — is not transitive when applied across populations of variable size and composition.

Transitivity of the betterness relation is one of the most basic requirements of rational choice theory. If A is better than B, and B is better than C, then A must be better than C. I shall argue, through a series of spectrum arguments, that this requirement fails when welfare is evaluated across populations. This is not merely a paradox for specific theories; it is a paradox for population axiology as such.

“The Repugnant Conclusion is not an embarrassment for total utilitarianism alone. It is, in different guises, an embarrassment for the very project of population axiology. The problem lies not in the theories but in our attempt to compare welfare states across populations by any consistent metric.” — Temkin, paraphrased from Rethinking the Good, 2012
i. the spectrum argument and intransitivity

Spectrum argument — plausible pairwise comparisons generate intransitivity; “better than” is not transitive across welfare spectraConsider a spectrum of populations: A1, A2, …, An, where each successive population has slightly lower average welfare but is very much larger. Under almost any plausible principle of welfare aggregation, Ai+1 is better than Ai: the welfare loss per person is small, and the addition of many new lives at the marginally lower welfare level more than compensates. But at the end of the spectrum, An is the vast population of barely-worth-living lives that is the Repugnant Conclusion. And A1 — the small flourishing population — is clearly better than An. We have: A2>A1, A3>A2, …, An>An−1, but A1>An. The transitivity of “better than” is violated.

ii. cyclical betterness across population comparisons

Cyclical betterness — violating transitivity generates cycles; rational choice is undermined; no stable ordering of populations existsThe intransitivity of “better than” is not merely a theoretical curiosity; it has devastating practical implications. If the betterness relation is cyclic across populations, there is no stable best option. Any population that is chosen can be shown to be worse than some other population, which is worse than yet another, which is worse than the first. Rational population policy is impossible without a transitive betterness ordering. But the spectrum arguments show that any consistent, additive valuation of welfare across populations will generate cyclical betterness. The problem is not that our theories are wrong; it is that the domain of population ethics is resistant to consistent ordering.

iii. the violation of transitivity in Voorhoeve’s pluralism

Voorhoeve pluralism — cyclic judgments arise from combining ex-ante and ex-post concerns; transitivity violated in probabilistic casesThe intransitivity is not confined to additive frameworks. Voorhoeve’s pluralist egalitarianism, which combines ex-ante fairness with ex-post prioritarianism, generates cyclical judgements when applied to probabilistic outcomes across variable populations. In a three-way choice between distributions of welfare prospects, the pluralist framework may rank X better than Y (because X provides better ex-ante prospects), Y better than Z (because Y provides better ex-post prioritarian outcomes), and Z better than X (because Z satisfies some additional egalitarian condition). The cycle is not an artefact of inconsistency in the framework; it is a consequence of the genuine tensions among the values the pluralist framework is designed to honour. Pluralism does not escape intransitivity; it institutionalises it.

iv. incommensurability as a partial but insufficient response

Incommensurability — replacing intransitivity with incommensurability helps but doesn’t solve the core problem; practical guidance still lostOne response to the spectrum argument is to deny that the pairwise betterness judgements generate a violation of transitivity: instead, some of the adjacent populations in the spectrum are incommensurable rather than ranked. This response is available but insufficient. First, incommensurability does not restore practical guidance: if A and C are incommensurable, we cannot decide between policies that generate A and C respectively, and this is practically as devastating as a cycle. Second, the incommensurability response generates its own paradox: if A1 and An are incommensurable, and each adjacent pair is also incommensurable, the egalitarian dominance conditions imply that A1 is better than An (since it has higher average welfare and is more equal). The attempt to replace intransitivity with incommensurability restores transitivity only by sacrificing egalitarian dominance, generating yet another impossibility.

I want to focus on the fate of prioritarianism — the framework that gives extra weight to welfare gains for the worse-off — under conditions of variable population. Prioritarianism is the most influential contemporary alternative to both totalism and egalitarianism in population ethics. It is designed precisely to avoid the Repugnant Conclusion while honouring both welfare addition and the intuition that those at the bottom of the welfare distribution matter more. I shall show that it fails on both counts under population expansion, through what I call the Welfare Diffusion Objection.

The Welfare Diffusion Objection, stated precisely.

Prioritarianism assigns to each person’s welfare a priority weight that is a decreasing function of their welfare level: gains for the worse-off count for more than equal-sized gains for the better-off. The total prioritarian value of a population is the sum of these weighted welfare values.

The Welfare Diffusion Objection: as population size increases, the prioritarian weights for each member shrink towards zero (since each additional person at any welfare level makes everyone at that level relatively less badly off compared to the distribution as a whole). For a sufficiently large population, the priority weights become negligibly different across welfare levels. Prioritarianism therefore collapses back into effective totalism for large populations — and entails the Repugnant Conclusion it was designed to avoid.
i. prioritarianism’s structure under population expansion

Priority weights collapse — as population grows, priority weights converge to uniformity; prioritarianism reduces to totalism for large NThe prioritarian priority weight assigned to a person depends on their welfare relative to the rest of the population or relative to some absolute threshold. In either case, as population size increases, the marginal contribution of any individual to the population’s overall welfare distribution becomes negligible. The priority weight of a person with welfare w is a function of how badly off they are relative to the population as a whole or relative to a threshold; as N grows without bound, the contribution of any single person’s welfare level to the ranking becomes arbitrarily small. The weights flatten out: for large enough populations, near-zero welfare and moderate welfare receive effectively the same priority weighting. Prioritarianism reduces to totalism. And totalism entails the Repugnant Conclusion.

ii. the monstrous conclusion as the alternative horn

Monstrous Conclusion — avoiding welfare diffusion collapse requires priority weights that produce the Monstrous Conclusion; both horns are unacceptableThe prioritarian might try to avoid the welfare diffusion collapse by making the priority weight function steeper — by increasing the rate at which priority weight increases as welfare approaches zero. But making the priority weight function steep enough to maintain genuine priority for the worst-off in large populations generates the Monstrous Conclusion: a vast population with lives barely worth living, each receiving a very high priority weight, accumulates a prioritarian value that exceeds the value of any fixed-size flourishing population. The prioritarian is on the horns of a genuine dilemma: shallow priority weights collapse into totalism and the Repugnant Conclusion; steep priority weights generate the Monstrous Conclusion. There is no weight function that avoids both.

iii. the ex-ante pareto vs. dominance dilemma for prioritarianism under uncertainty

Ex-ante Pareto vs. dominance — prioritarianism under uncertainty violates either ex-ante Pareto or outcome dominance; further dilemma under riskThe impossibility for prioritarianism deepens under uncertainty. Consider a case in which two policies have the same expected welfare for each person, but Policy X stochastically dominates Policy Y: in every possible state of the world, X produces a weakly better outcome for every person, and in some states a strictly better one. Prioritarianism under risk demands that we give extra weight to the welfare of the worse-off in each outcome; but which outcome is the worse-off world depends on which state obtains. This generates a conflict between: (i) the ex-ante Pareto principle (if X is at least as good as Y in expected welfare for everyone, and better for some, X is better overall); and (ii) the dominance principle (X, which dominates Y in every state, should be ranked better). Under certain constructions, prioritarianism must violate one of these principles. Neither violation is acceptable.

iv. the transitivity violation in prioritarian population ranking

Transitivity violation — prioritarian ranking of triples generates cycles under variable population; structural not incidentalFollowing Temkin’s spectrum argument, prioritarian rankings of populations generate transitivity violations for the same structural reasons. Consider three populations: P (large, high priority weights, modest welfare), Q (medium, intermediate), R (small, flourishing, low priority weights). The prioritarian may rank P better than Q (high aggregate priority-weighted value from many badly-off people), Q better than R (intermediate), but R better than P (the small flourishing population has far higher welfare per person and satisfies some egalitarian dominance condition). The cycle is generated not by an error in the prioritarian calculus but by the genuine tensions between the priority weighting of the badly-off and the aggregate welfare advantage of the flourishing population. Prioritarianism generates exactly the kind of intransitivity that Temkin identified as structural to population ethics.

In our 2012 paper with Marc Fleurbaey, I argued that the familiar tension between ex-ante fairness and ex-post prioritarianism generates its own impossibility result — one that operates within pluralist egalitarian frameworks rather than against them from outside. I now present the results not as a defence of pluralism but as a demonstration that pluralism, like every other approach, cannot escape the structural impossibility that afflicts population axiology.

The pluralist egalitarian holds that both prospects and outcomes matter for the evaluation of distributions: ex-ante considerations of equal prospects are independently morally significant, and ex-post considerations of priority for the worse-off are also independently morally significant. When these two considerations pull in different directions — as they do in many risky choice situations — the framework must adjudicate between them. And when adjudication is attempted under variable population, the adjudication procedure generates its own impossibility results.

The Ex-Ante Pareto vs. Dominance Dilemma, stated precisely.

Consider two policies, X and Y, applied to a fixed population under uncertainty. Policy X stochastically dominates Policy Y: in every possible state of the world, X produces a weakly better outcome for every person, and in some states a strictly better outcome for some. Ex-ante Pareto requires that X be ranked better than Y if X is at least as good in expectation for everyone and strictly better in expectation for some.

Pluralist egalitarianism introduces a complication: the ex-post priority weighting of outcomes depends on who is worse off in each outcome. This can generate cases in which the ex-ante Pareto condition is satisfied (X is better in expectation for everyone) but the dominance condition seems to demand that we evaluate X’s stochastic advantage as outweighed by its ex-post egalitarian properties. The two conditions conflict, and neither can be abandoned without sacrificing a core commitment of the pluralist framework.
i. the ex-ante pareto vs. dominance dilemma

Ex-post prioritarianism then favours Y over X in those states on egalitarian grounds. The pluralist framework must either: accept X (violating ex-post egalitarianism in the relevant states) or accept Y (violating ex-ante Pareto and Dominance). Neither horn is acceptable: violating ex-ante Pareto means that everyone can be made worse off in expectation for the sake of ex-post equality, and violating ex-post egalitarianism abandons the core pluralist commitment to priority for the worse-off in outcomes.

ii. the prospective equality paradox

Prospective Equality Paradox — equalising prospects worsens outcomes for everyone; ex-ante fairness conflicts with ex-post welfareThe Prospective Equality Paradox arises when equalising the ex-ante prospects of two people — giving each an equal probability of the better and worse outcomes — is worse in expectation for everyone than an unequal distribution of prospects. This occurs when the correlation between the better outcome and the person for whom it would contribute more welfare (due to diminishing marginal utility or priority weighting) is non-random. Equalising prospects then destroys the welfare-maximising correlation. The pluralist egalitarian must either: accept that equal prospects sometimes make everyone worse off in expectation (violating ex-ante Pareto); or accept that we should sometimes maintain unequal prospects for ex-ante Pareto reasons (violating the egalitarian commitment to equal prospects). In a two-person baseline, Policy X may be ranked better than Y for ex-ante egalitarian reasons. But when a third person is added at an intermediate welfare level, the ex-post priority weighting of the existing two people changes, because the third person changes who is “worse off” relative to the distribution. The addition of a person with no direct stake in the X vs. Y comparison for the existing two people reverses the ranking of those policies for those two people. This violates the separateness of persons: the welfare comparison of existing people should not be reversed by the addition of others whose welfare is not directly implicated in the comparison.

The Population Expansion Anomaly shows that the ranking of X and Y can be reversed by adding a third option. Together, these generate violations of transitivity that are structurally analogous to the violations Temkin identified in the totalist and prioritarian frameworks. Pluralist egalitarianism does not escape the impossibility; it generates it internally, from the tensions among its own constitutive values.

The proposition has presented the impossibility results as if they established that every population axiology is unacceptably paradoxical. I want to challenge this framing. The impossibility results show that no axiology satisfies every adequacy condition simultaneously. They do not show that every axiology is equally paradoxical, or that the paradoxes are equally serious, or that no adequate axiology exists. The question is which adequacy conditions are genuinely compelling and which rest on intuitions that should be revised.

My answer is that the Repugnant Conclusion is not as repugnant as it seems, and that the intuitions driving resistance to it are unreliable at the scale at which the conclusion operates. Totalism — the view that the value of a population is the sum of the welfare of all its members — is formally consistent, has a clear and principled structure, and generates counterintuitive verdicts only when applied to populations that are so large that our intuitions have no reliable guide. This is not an impossibility; it is an invitation to revise an unreliable intuition.

Why the Repugnant Conclusion is tolerable.

The Repugnant Conclusion concerns populations of ten billion people or more, each with welfare “barely above zero.” The difficulty in assessing this conclusion is that we have no reliable intuitive grip on what a world of ten billion barely-positive lives would actually be like. Our intuitions about such populations are extrapolations from our experience with much smaller populations; they are not calibrated to the scale at which the conclusion operates.

When the proposition says the conclusion is “repugnant,” they are relying on an intuition formed at a scale (small, familiar populations) that may not transfer to the scale at which the conclusion operates. The intuition that the vast barely-living world is worse than the small flourishing world may be a cognitive artefact of our inability to represent the full welfare of ten billion lives, not a reliable moral perception.

Each of these costs is borne by alternative theories that claim to improve on totalism. But these costs are borne in the familiar small-population cases where our intuitions are most reliable. The Repugnant Conclusion is borne only at vast scale where our intuitions are most unreliable. Critical level utilitarianism holds that only welfare above a critical level c contributes positively to the value of a population; welfare below c contributes negatively, even if the welfare is positive. A life with welfare above zero but below c has a negative impact on the value of the population, because it falls short of the threshold at which life is considered “good enough” to merit bringing into existence. By setting c above zero, we can avoid the Repugnant Conclusion: populations of marginally positive lives do not accumulate positive value.

My response to Temkin’s spectrum arguments and the intransitivity challenge is to challenge the assumption that betterness comparisons are always determinate. Temkin’s arguments establish intransitivity only if each adjacent comparison in the welfare spectrum yields a determinate “better than” verdict. I deny this. Many adjacent-population comparisons in the spectrum involve cases where neither population is better, nor are they equally good, nor are they incommensurable in the strong sense of being incomparable. They are on a par — a fourth evaluative relation that is different from all three.

The “on a par” relation was first characterised by Ruth Chang, and I have developed its implications for population ethics. The key point is that “on a par” is not transitive. If A is on a par with B, and B is on a par with C, it does not follow that A is on a par with C. This breaks the chain of comparisons that generates Temkin’s intransitivity, and it does so without sacrificing any genuinely compelling normative condition.

The “on a par” relation and population ethics.

In a welfare spectrum A1…An, many adjacent pairs are genuinely on a par: neither is better, nor are they equally good (since small improvements to either would tip the verdict), but they are not incomparable (since they are clearly in the same evaluative neighbourhood). The “on a par” relation holds between values that are in the same league but not precisely ranked.

The spectrum argument fails if any adjacent comparison is “on a par” rather than ranked. Since “on a par” is not transitive, the chain of comparisons cannot generate a cyclical ordering through transitivity. And since A1 and An are not on a par (A1’s superiority over An is clear), the argument does not establish that the betterness relation is cyclic.
i. the “on a par” relation dissolves the spectrum argument

“On a par” — fourth evaluative relation; not transitive; breaks the spectrum chain; intransitivity not establishedTemkin’s spectrum argument requires each adjacent comparison to be a ranked “better than” verdict. But when we compare two populations that differ by a small welfare decrease and a moderate population increase, the comparison may be genuinely indeterminate: not “better,” not “worse,” not “equally good,” but “on a par.” The “on a par” relation is characterised by the property that small improvements to either option tip the verdict in its favour — a property that distinguishes it from genuine equality (where neither small improvement tips the verdict) and genuine incomparability (where no comparisons are possible). If adjacent comparisons are “on a par,” the spectrum argument fails: the chain required to generate intransitivity is broken, and the conclusion of cyclical betterness is not established.

ii. imprecise comparisons and the failure of the transitivity argument

Imprecise comparisons — betterness has imprecise borderlines; adjacent comparisons fail to determine a verdict; transitivity not violatedMany value comparisons in population ethics involve imprecise or vague betterness relations: the domain of value does not provide precise verdicts for every pair of options. When the comparison is imprecise, the “is better than” predicate does not apply determinately to adjacent spectrum pairs. The transitivity of “better than” requires determinate verdicts at each step; if some steps yield only imprecise verdicts, transitivity cannot be deployed to generate a cycle. Temkin’s argument requires that each adjacent comparison be not merely plausibly better but determinately better. The imprecision of betterness in the population domain provides exactly the reason to deny this.

iii. the “on a par” relation and the Voorhoeve-Fleurbaey dilemma

I appear in this debate in an unusual position: I presented the paradoxes for the Proposition, and now I must defend the pluralist egalitarian framework against the charge that those paradoxes constitute an impossibility result that destroys it. I can do this consistently because I believe the paradoxes are genuine tensions — genuine difficulties that the framework must manage — but not contradictions that establish the framework’s impossibility. The framework is incomplete in edge cases, not inconsistent in general.

The distinction matters. An impossibility result shows that a framework cannot consistently satisfy a set of conditions. A tension shows that a framework faces difficult cases in which its constitutive values pull in different directions. Every plausible ethical framework faces tensions; that is what makes ethics hard. The question is whether the tensions can be managed within the framework, and I shall argue that the tensions I identified with Fleurbaey can be managed by a careful priority structure for the constitutive values of the pluralist framework.

The pluralist egalitarian framework and its priority structure.

The pluralist egalitarian framework has three constitutive commitments: (i) ex-ante fairness — equal prospects matter independently for their own sake; (ii) ex-post prioritarianism — priority to the worse-off in outcomes; (iii) the separateness of persons — the welfare of each person is evaluated independently and not subsumed in aggregate comparisons.

These three commitments sometimes pull in different directions. The priority structure I defend gives lexical priority to the separateness of persons, then to ex-post prioritarianism in clear cases, then to ex-ante fairness as a tie-breaker in cases where ex-post comparisons are genuinely indeterminate. This structure does not eliminate the tensions but manages them in a way that generates consistent verdicts in the cases that matter most for practical decision-making.
i. the ex-ante pareto vs. dominance dilemma is manageable

Dilemma manageable — priority structure favours dominance in clear cases; ex-ante fairness as tie-breaker; consistent verdict availableThe Ex-Ante Pareto vs. Dominance Dilemma, as Fleurbaey and I presented it, requires that both ex-ante and ex-post criteria generate determinate, conflicting verdicts. The cases that generate genuine dilemmas are precisely those where neither ex-ante nor ex-post criteria yield clear verdicts — and in those cases, the correct verdict is Rabinowicz’s “on a par” rather than a forced choice. ii. the prospective equality paradox is a genuine tension, not a contradiction

Prospective Equality Paradox — genuine tension between values; framework accommodates it by prioritising separateness; not a contradictionThe Prospective Equality Paradox shows that equalising prospects can make everyone worse off in expectation when welfare gains are positively correlated with the beneficiary’s welfare level. This is a genuine tension: ex-ante fairness (equal prospects) conflicts with ex-ante Pareto (everyone better off in expectation). The pluralist framework resolves this tension by appeal to the separateness of persons: when equalising prospects makes everyone worse off in expectation, there is no person whose claim to equal prospects outweighs the cost in their own expected welfare. The separateness of persons — the principle that welfare comparisons should be made person by person — yields the verdict that the non-equalising distribution is better, since it is better for each person in expectation. This is a consistent verdict, not a contradiction.

iii. the population expansion anomaly is an artefact of aggregation, not a contradiction

Population Expansion Anomaly — artefact of relative prioritarianism; absolute prioritarianism avoids it; anomaly is a calibration issue not an impossibilityThe Population Expansion Anomaly arises from relative prioritarianism: the priority weight assigned to a person depends on their welfare relative to the rest of the population. When a new person is added, the reference class for relative comparisons changes, potentially reversing prior rankings. But this is an artefact of the relative formulation of prioritarianism, not a feature of all prioritarian frameworks. Absolute prioritarianism — which assigns priority weights as a function of absolute welfare levels, not relative standing — does not generate the anomaly: adding a new person changes neither the welfare of existing people nor their absolute priority weights. The population expansion anomaly is a calibration issue for relative prioritarianism, not an impossibility for the pluralist egalitarian framework as such.
