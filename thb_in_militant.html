Militant is not a tendency within the broad church of democratic socialism. It is a separate, entrist organisation with its own membership, its own finances, its own newspaper, its own full-time staff, and its own Trotskyist programme that is utterly alien to Labour’s democratic traditions. Tolerating it destroys Labour’s electoral credibility and its basic integrity as a democratic party. It must be removed.

Traditionalist LabourismPeter Shore, Shadow Leader of the House, 1983–84
The question is not whether Labour should be broad or narrow, tolerant or intolerant of dissent. The question is whether a separate organisation, operating under its own discipline and pursuing goals incompatible with Labour’s own, can be permitted to use Labour’s name and Labour’s machinery for its own ends. Conference,

I will start with some plain speaking, because this party has had too little of it for too long. What we are debating today is not, as our opponents on the left would have you believe, a question of tolerance, or of the breadth of the broad church, or of whether working-class militants have the right to be heard in their party. Of course they do. That question is not in dispute. The question is something entirely different: whether a separate, self-disciplined, externally funded Trotskyist organisation should be permitted to use the Labour Party as its vehicle while owing its primary allegiance to its own leadership and its own programme.

“I’ll tell you what happens with impossible promises. You start with far-fetched resolutions. They are then pickled into a rigid dogma, a code, and you go through the years sticking to that, out-dated, mis-placed, irrelevant to the real needs, and you end in the grotesque chaos of a Labour council — a Labour council — hiring taxis to scuttle round a city handing out redundancy notices to its own workers.”
i. the organisational reality of militant

Organised entryism — Militant is a separate party inside the party: own press, funds, full-timers, and binding discipline that overrides Labour loyaltyLet me set out the organisational facts, because some people prefer to speak about this abstractly. Militant Tendency has its own newspaper, the Militant, published since 1964, with its own editorial board and circulation network. It has its own full-time staff — at its peak over two hundred people — paid from its own funds, raised independently of the Labour Party. It has its own internal discipline: members are expected to give a proportion of their income to the tendency, to attend its own educational meetings, to accept its own leadership’s guidance on how to vote and what to argue inside Labour structures. This is not a current of opinion within a party. This is a party within a party, and it uses Labour’s name and Labour’s democratic structures as a cover for its own quite different purposes.

ii. the liverpool catastrophe

Liverpool — Militant-controlled council set illegal budget, sent redundancy notices to 30,000 workers; exposed as gesture politics at workers' expenseThe proof is in the record of what Militant actually does when it holds power. In Liverpool, a Labour council under Militant’s effective direction set a budget it knew to be illegal, refused to set a legal rate, and then — when the crisis they had manufactured came to a head — issued redundancy notices to thirty thousand council workers in order to dramatise their confrontation with the government. Thirty thousand workers. Labour workers. People who had joined trade unions and voted Labour all their lives, and who received their P45s delivered by taxi as a piece of political theatre staged by people claiming to act in their interests. That is not socialism. That is the politics of gesture dressed up as the politics of principle, and the thirty thousand people who received those notices know the difference.

iii. entryism and the corruption of democratic structures

Democratic subversion — entryism systematically corrupts selection meetings, GMCs, and local parties; ordinary members driven out by organised factionThe method by which Militant operates is not open debate and persuasion — the methods of democratic politics. It is entryism: the systematic infiltration of General Management Committees, constituency parties, and trade union branches by organised blocks of members who vote as a unit and whose first loyalty is to the tendency’s own leadership. Members who disagree with Militant — ordinary trade unionists, community activists, long-serving ward members — find themselves isolated, outmanoeuvred at meetings stacked with faces they do not recognise, and eventually driven out of parties they have served for decades. This is not the broad church. It is the occupation of the church by a sect that does not share its faith and does not respect its congregation.

iv. the electoral cost of tolerance

Electoral cost — Labour lost 1983 catastrophically; Militant provided Tories with their most damaging ammunition; expulsion is precondition for recoveryI am accused of being motivated by electoral opportunism. Very well, let us talk about electoral reality, because the people who most need a Labour government are the people who are being destroyed by this Conservative government: the unemployed, the council tenants, the workers in the collapsing industries. And they will not get a Labour government while Labour is successfully characterised — not unfairly characterised — as a party in the grip of extremists who want to nationalise everything, abolish the second chamber by decree, and repudiate international alliances. In 1983 we received our worst share of the vote since 1918. Militant did not cause that alone. But Militant, and what Militant represented in the public mind, was central to it. The people who need Labour most are the people paying the price of our tolerance.

v. constitutional legitimacy of expulsion

Constitutional case — Labour's constitution prohibits membership of organisations with their own programme; Militant openly meets this criterion; expulsion is legally properThere is a constitutional argument, and it is straightforward. Clause II of the Labour Party constitution provides that the party shall not affiliate with or give support to organisations that have their own programme, principles, and policy distinct from those of the Labour Party, and that seek to operate as a separate party or tendency within the Labour Party. Militant has openly and publicly operated under its own programme — the British Perspectives of the Militant tendency, a document of explicit Trotskyist analysis — since the 1960s. Its supporters have repeatedly insisted that they are a distinct political tendency with a coherent theoretical identity. They cannot in the same breath claim that this makes them immune from a provision in Labour’s own rules designed precisely for their situation. The constitution provides the mechanism. We are using it.

I will say one thing more. I do not take pleasure in this. These are Labour people, however mistaken. But the duty of this party’s leadership is to the party as a whole, and to the millions of people in this country who need a Labour government. That duty requires us to act.

Argument I — Organisational identity: Militant is a party within a party, not a tendency within a broad church
1.Militant has its own newspaper, full-time paid staff (200+), independent fundraising, internal educational structures, and binding internal discipline obliging members to tithe income and accept its leadership’s political direction.
2.A political organisation with these features — its own programme, its own finances, its own discipline — is definitionally a separate political party, not a tendency or current of opinion.
3.The Labour Party’s constitution prohibits membership of organisations that operate as separate parties or tendencies within Labour. Militant demonstrably meets this criterion.
∴Militant is not a legitimate internal tendency deserving protection as part of Labour’s broad church; it is a separate organisation using Labour’s structures for its own purposes, and its members are in breach of Labour’s constitution.
Argument II — The Liverpool evidence: Militant in power damages the working-class communities it claims to champion
2.In Liverpool 1985, Militant-influenced Labour councillors set an illegal budget and, when challenged, served redundancy notices on 30,000 council employees to manufacture a political confrontation with central government.
2.Those 30,000 workers — the constituency Militant claims to represent — suffered real material harm (loss of employment security, pensions disruption) as a consequence of Militant’s political theatre.
3.An organisation that harms the workers it claims to champion, in order to pursue a confrontational political strategy, has forfeited the moral claim that its radicalism is in working people’s interests.
∴The Liverpool record demonstrates that Militant’s politics, when implemented, damages the very communities it professes to serve; its radicalism is a form of gesture politics conducted at workers’ expense.
Argument III — Entryism is the systematic subversion of democratic party structures
1.Militant’s method is not open persuasion but organised infiltration: packing GMC meetings, manipulating selection conferences, and operating as a disciplined bloc to install its own candidates in council seats and parliamentary selections.
2.This method systematically excludes ordinary members from effective participation in their own party, replacing open democratic contest with the capture of formal structures by an organised outside force.
3.A party that permits the systematic subversion of its own democratic mechanisms by an entryist organisation has effectively ceased to be a democratic party in any meaningful sense.
∴Militant’s entryist method is incompatible with internal party democracy; tolerating it does not reflect the broad church tradition but its negation; expulsion is a defence of democracy, not an attack on it.
Argument IV — Electoral reality: association with Militant makes a Labour government impossible, depriving the poor of representation
1.Labour’s 1983 general election result — 27.6% of the vote, its lowest since 1918 — reflected in part a public perception that the party had been captured by extremists. Militant’s programme and profile contributed materially to this perception.
2.The people most harmed by Conservative government — the unemployed, low-wage workers, council tenants — are the people who most need a Labour government and who are denied it by Labour’s unelectability.
3.An unelectable party of principle provides no more material benefit to the working class than no party at all; electability is not opportunism but a precondition of being useful.
∴Expelling Militant is not electoral opportunism but a moral obligation to the communities that need Labour in government; tolerating Militant condemns those communities to permanent Tory rule in the name of an internal purity they do not share.
Argument V — Constitutional legitimacy: Labour’s rules provide clear and proper authority for expulsion
1.Labour’s constitution prohibits membership of organisations with their own programme, principles, and policies distinct from Labour’s, or that seek to operate as a separate party or tendency within it.
2.Militant openly acknowledges its own programme (the British Perspectives document), its own organisational identity, and its own theoretical distinctiveness. Its supporters have claimed these as sources of pride.
3.Having publicly satisfied the constitutional criterion for exclusion, Militant cannot simultaneously claim immunity from the constitutional mechanism its members’ conduct has triggered.
∴Expulsion is constitutionally proper, democratically legitimate, and entirely consistent with Labour’s own rules; it is not purge or witch-hunt but the lawful application of the party’s own constitutional provisions.

Chair,

I want to come at this question from a different angle from Neil Kinnock, though I reach the same conclusion. I am not primarily interested in the organisational question — in the question of whether Militant has its own membership cards or its own bank account. Those are real and important facts, but they are not the deepest reason why Militant cannot be part of the Labour Party. The deepest reason is ideological: Militant’s programme is not a more thorough version of Labour’s own aims. It is a fundamentally different political project, rooted in a different theory of history, a different theory of the state, and a different theory of how political change is achieved.

i. the marxist state theory and its implications

State theory — Militant's Marxist theory of the capitalist state makes parliamentary democracy a transitional instrument, not a permanent commitment; incompatible with Labour's democratic constitutionMilitant operates on the Trotskyist theory that the capitalist state — including its parliamentary institutions — cannot be genuinely reformed but must ultimately be replaced by working-class state power. Parliament, on this analysis, is useful as a platform, as a tribune from which to address the masses, but it is not the vehicle through which fundamental socialist transformation is achieved. The transformation will come through extra-parliamentary action: through the general strike, through the building of workers’ soviets, through the revolutionary overthrow of the existing state apparatus. This is not a secret; it is the explicit content of the British Perspectives document and of Ted Grant’s writings, available in any Militant bookshop. A party that holds parliamentary democracy to be a transitional instrument cannot be a parliamentary party in any meaningful sense, regardless of whether it temporarily uses parliamentary methods.

ii. the programme is undeliverable and the pretence is dishonest

Programme — nationalisation of top 200 monopolies, abolition of Lords by decree, repudiation of NATO: not radical Labourism but a Trotskyist transitional programme designed to expose rather than reform the systemConsider what Militant actually proposes: the nationalisation of the 250 monopolies that they say dominate the British economy, with compensation paid only on the basis of proven need; the abolition of the House of Lords by resolution of a Labour Cabinet, not by legislation; the repudiation of existing international treaty obligations; and the construction of a socialist planned economy in Britain within the lifetime of a single Parliament. This is not a set of policies. It is a transitional programme in the technical Trotskyist sense — a set of demands designed not to be delivered but to expose the contradictions of the capitalist system. The purpose of the programme is to demonstrate that reform is impossible, thereby pushing the working class towards revolutionary consciousness. It is dishonest to present this as a form of more vigorous Labourism. It is a different political project wearing Labourist clothes.

iii. the broad church has limits

Broad church limits — Labour has always had internal boundaries; democratic socialism is the common ground; Trotskyism falls outside it; breadth is not the same as boundlessnessI want to address directly the argument that Labour is and always has been a broad church, and that Militant is simply at the left end of that broad church. It is true that Labour has always been a coalition — of trade unionists and Fabians, of pacifists and imperialists, of guild socialists and co-operators. That breadth has been one of Labour’s great strengths. But the broad church has always had a foundation — a set of values and methods without which the coalition ceases to be Labour at all. That foundation is a commitment to achieving socialist goals through democratic means, with respect for the democratic will of the electorate even when that will is inconvenient. Militant does not share that foundation. Its commitment to parliamentary democracy is provisional and instrumental. A church that has no doctrinal core is not a broad church; it is simply an empty building available for hire to whoever turns up.

iv. the social democratic tradition demands organisational integrity

Social democratic tradition — Revisionist Labour from Crosland to Gaitskell understood that without organisational self-discipline, the party cannot function as a governing partyHugh Gaitskell, a leader of this party I greatly admired, fought at Scarborough in 1960 to preserve what he called the “fight, fight, and fight again” character of the party he loved. He understood that Labour as a governing party required something more than enthusiasm and commitment. It required the capacity to make decisions, to implement them, and to present a coherent and unified face to the electorate. That capacity is destroyed when separate organisations operate within the party under their own discipline, with their own agenda, subordinating party decisions to their own purposes. The revisionist tradition in Labour — from Crosland’s The Future of Socialism to the revisionism of the 1980s — has always understood that means matter: that how you pursue socialism determines what kind of socialism you achieve. Militant’s means are incompatible with Labour’s ends.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Trotskyist state theory makes Militant’s commitment to parliamentary democracy provisional and instrumental
1.Militant’s theoretical framework (derived from Ted Grant and the Fourth International) holds that the capitalist state cannot be fundamentally reformed through parliamentary means; parliament is useful as a tribune but not as the vehicle of socialist transformation.
2.This analysis implies that parliamentary democracy is a transitional instrument to be used until extra-parliamentary action becomes possible or necessary, not a permanent political commitment.
3.Labour’s constitution and tradition commit it irrevocably to achieving its aims through democratic parliamentary means. A party that regards this commitment as provisional cannot be a genuine member of the Labour family.
∴Militant’s Trotskyist state theory makes its use of Labour’s parliamentary structures purely tactical; its underlying commitment to extra-parliamentary transformation is fundamentally incompatible with Labour’s democratic constitution.
Argument II — Militant’s programme is a Trotskyist transitional programme, not a version of Labour radicalism
1.A transitional programme (in the Trotskyist sense) is a set of demands designed not to be implemented under capitalism but to expose its contradictions and accelerate the development of revolutionary consciousness in the working class.
2.Militant’s programme — nationalisation of the 250 monopolies, abolition of the Lords by Cabinet resolution, transformation of the economy in a single Parliament — is undeliverable under any foreseeable conditions and functions as a set of transitional demands in precisely this sense.
3.Presenting a transitional programme as if it were achievable radical Labourism is a form of political dishonesty that Labour should not endorse by including its advocates within its membership.
∴Militant’s programme is not a more radical version of Labour policy; it is a different kind of political project — a Trotskyist transitional programme — that uses the language of Labourism while pursuing fundamentally incompatible aims.
Argument III — The broad church has a democratic socialist foundation; Trotskyism falls outside its boundaries
1.Labour has always been a coalition of diverse traditions, but the coalition has always shared a foundational commitment to achieving socialist aims through democratic means, respecting the will of the electorate.
2.Militant’s commitment to parliamentary democracy is provisional and instrumental; it does not share the foundational democratic commitment that defines the boundaries of the Labour coalition.
3.A church without a doctrinal core is not a broad church but a building available for any purpose; the broad church tradition requires that there be something at its foundation, and that foundation is democratic socialism.
∴The broad church argument does not protect Militant, because Militant does not share the democratic socialist foundation that defines the boundaries of Labour’s broad church; its inclusion would dissolve the church rather than broaden it.
Argument IV — The revisionist tradition: means and ends are inseparable; Militant’s methods corrupt Labour’s goals
1.The revisionist social democratic tradition (Crosland, Gaitskell) holds that the methods by which socialism is pursued are not separable from the kind of socialism achieved; democratic means are constitutive of democratic socialist ends.
2.Militant’s methods — entryism, internal discipline overriding party loyalty, the use of party structures as a cover for a separate organisation — are undemocratic in character and corrupt the democratic socialist values Labour is trying to embody.
3.A party that permits its structures to be used by organisations employing undemocratic methods is already, to that extent, compromising the democratic socialist values it claims to hold.
∴From the revisionist tradition’s standpoint, the question of Militant’s means is inseparable from the question of Labour’s ends; Militant’s undemocratic methods are incompatible with the democratic socialist goals Labour exists to pursue.

Chair,

I speak from a particular vantage point in this debate: I am someone who chose to leave the Labour Party rather than remain in it while organisations like Militant corroded its internal democracy. Some people regard that decision as a betrayal. I regard it as an act of political honesty. But that debate is not for today. What I want to offer today is an account of what the internal life of Labour constituency parties actually looked like in the late 1970s and early 1980s, before the Bennite and Militant mobilisation had run its course.

i. the experience of ordinary members

Intimidation — ordinary Labour members in infiltrated CLPs experienced organised intimidation, deselection threats, and the systematic exclusion of moderate voices from party lifeI am not speaking of the grand ideological debates, though they matter. I am speaking of what happened in ordinary constituency Labour parties up and down the country when Militant and the hard left organised effectively in them. I am speaking of members who had served their parties for twenty and thirty years — councillors, trade unionists, community workers — who found themselves facing orchestrated votes of no confidence, deselection threats, and the kind of personal hostility that is designed not to win an argument but to drive a person out. I am speaking of women members who found the culture of infiltrated parties aggressive and intimidating in ways that party meetings had never been before. I am speaking of Jewish members of the Labour Party who found some of the rhetoric that came with the hard-left package deeply uncomfortable. These are not abstract constitutional questions. They are the lived experience of Labour’s own members.

ii. the lesson of the SDP split

SDP split — the Gang of Four left because internal reform was impossible; Militant's presence accelerated the fracture that cost Labour a generation of powerThose who blame the SDP split for Labour’s electoral difficulties are not entirely wrong. I accept my share of responsibility for the damage the split caused. But I would ask people to consider what drove us to make that decision. It was not ambition. It was not disagreement over policy at the margins. It was the calculation — which I still believe was correct — that the Labour Party had, in the period of the Bennite ascendancy and Militant infiltration, ceased to be a vehicle through which democratic socialist politics could effectively be pursued. We were wrong about some things. We were wrong, I now think, to believe that the SDP could replace Labour as the vehicle for centre-left politics in Britain. But we were not wrong about what was happening to Labour. The fact that Kinnock is now having to take these measures confirms what we said then.

iii. democratic accountability and the local state

Local accountability — Militant-controlled councils pursued ideological programmes not endorsed by local electorates; this violates democratic accountability at its most basic levelThere is a democratic argument that goes beyond the internal life of the Labour Party. Militant’s grip on several local councils meant that councils were pursuing ideological programmes — illegal budgets, confrontational industrial relations, symbolic politics — that local electorates had never been asked to endorse and would not have endorsed if they had been asked. The voters of Liverpool elected a Labour council to run their city’s services. They did not elect a Trotskyist council to use their city as a laboratory for revolutionary politics. The accountability of elected representatives to their electors is not a bourgeois formality that Marxists can set aside when it is inconvenient. It is the basic contract of democratic politics.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Entryism caused real harm to ordinary Labour members through intimidation and exclusion
1.In infiltrated CLPs, long-serving moderate members faced organised votes of no confidence, deselection threats, and personal intimidation designed to drive them from the party, not to win ideological arguments.
2.This behaviour caused real harm to real individuals — the destruction of political careers built on decades of service — and fundamentally changed the culture of local party life in ways that made many members leave.
3.An organisation whose methods cause this kind of harm to the party it has infiltrated cannot claim the protection of the party’s broad church tradition, which exists to protect genuine diversity, not organised aggression.
∴The harm Militant caused to ordinary Labour members through intimidation and exclusion is a concrete indictment of its methods and a ground for expulsion independent of any ideological argument.
Argument II — The SDP split was a symptom of Militant’s damage; its costs confirm the severity of the problem
1.The decision of the Gang of Four to leave Labour and found the SDP was driven in significant part by the assessment that the Bennite ascendancy and Militant infiltration had made Labour unworkable as a vehicle for democratic socialist politics.
2.The SDP split cost Labour experienced politicians, organisational capacity, and a significant share of its electoral coalition, contributing to the 1983 catastrophe.
3.If the presence of Militant and the hard left was a proximate cause of the SDP split, and the split materially damaged Labour’s prospects, then the costs of tolerating Militant are far greater than its defenders acknowledge.
∴The SDP split was a measurable consequence of the conditions Militant helped create within Labour; its costs in terms of electoral damage confirm that the toleration of entryism has consequences extending far beyond internal party culture.
Argument III — Militant-controlled councils violated democratic accountability of elected representatives to local electorates
1.Elected councillors are democratically accountable to their local electorates for the exercise of their powers. This accountability is the basic contract of democratic local government.
2.Militant-influenced councils in Liverpool and elsewhere pursued ideological programmes — illegal budgets, confrontational strategies — that local voters had never endorsed and were not given the opportunity to endorse or reject.
3.Using elected office as a vehicle for an ideological programme not endorsed by the electorate is a violation of democratic accountability that cannot be justified by appeals to a higher political purpose.
∴Militant’s use of Labour-controlled councils to pursue programmes not endorsed by local electorates violated the basic democratic accountability that justifies the holding of elected office; this is a democratic, not merely a party-political, objection.

Chair,

I want to make a case that is different from my colleagues’ in tone if not in conclusion. I am not, primarily, an ideological moderate. I am a democratic socialist of a fairly traditional kind. I believe in public ownership, in economic planning, in the redistribution of wealth, in a genuinely egalitarian society. I say this because I want it understood that when I argue for action against Militant, I am not doing so from the right of this party, and I am not doing so because I find their politics inconvenient. I am doing so because I believe their method of operation is incompatible with the kind of party the Labour movement needs.

i. the question of organisational self-respect

Organisational self-respect — a party that cannot enforce its own rules and boundaries has ceased to function as an organisation; expulsion is an act of institutional self-preservationThere is a prior question, before we reach the ideological arguments: can any organisation function effectively if it cannot define and enforce its own boundaries? This is not a question unique to political parties. A trade union that cannot distinguish between its own members and members of a rival organisation has a practical problem. A co-operative society that cannot require its members to abide by its rules will cease to function. The Labour Party has rules. Those rules have been violated, openly and continuously, by an organisation that has made no secret of its determination to use Labour’s structures for its own ends. The question of whether to enforce those rules is partly ideological and partly simply a question of whether the Labour Party intends to continue functioning as an organisation.

ii. socialist pluralism requires honest pluralism

Honest pluralism — genuine pluralism requires participants to be open about their programme; Militant's concealment is incompatible with genuine democratic debateI believe in a Labour Party that is genuinely pluralist: that accommodates vigorous disagreement about strategy, tactics, and policy within the framework of shared democratic socialist values. That kind of pluralism requires honesty: participants in the debate must be open about what they believe and what they are trying to achieve. Militant has not been honest with the Labour Party. It has presented itself as a group of committed socialists, offering its newspaper as a service to the movement, when in fact it is a party with its own programme, its own leadership, and its own agenda that it is pursuing through Labour’s structures. Democratic debate requires democratic honesty. You cannot build a genuinely pluralist party if some of the participants are concealing their real objectives. True pluralism requires that participants acknowledge what they are.

iii. the long-term interest of the labour movement

Labour movement interest — Militant damages not just the Labour Party but the broader movement: unions, co-ops, local government; the whole movement pays the price of the party's internal disorderThe damage caused by this situation is not confined to the Labour Party. It extends to the whole of the labour movement. Trade unions whose officials are Militant members face accusations of political entryism that damage their standing with their own membership. Local councils run by Militant sympathisers face the disruption of services that their working-class constituents depend on. The co-operative movement, the housing associations, the community organisations that look to Labour for political support all suffer when Labour is perceived as a party in the grip of extremists. The question is not just whether Militant should be allowed to pursue its agenda within Labour. It is whether the whole labour movement should be made to pay for the indulgence of one tendency within one party.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Organisational self-respect: any functioning organisation must be able to define and enforce its own membership boundaries
1.All functioning organisations — parties, unions, co-operatives — require the capacity to define their own membership, enforce their own rules, and maintain their own organisational integrity.
2.Militant has openly and continuously violated Labour’s constitutional rules by operating as a separate organisation within it. The rules provide a clear mechanism for addressing this.
3.A party that cannot or will not enforce its own rules in the face of open violation has effectively surrendered its capacity to function as a purposive organisation.
∴Expulsion is a matter of organisational self-preservation as much as ideological principle; a party that cannot enforce its own boundaries cannot function, and the failure to act against Militant undermines the Labour Party’s basic institutional integrity.
Argument II — Genuine pluralism requires honest pluralism; Militant’s concealment is incompatible with democratic internal debate
1.Democratic pluralism within a party requires that participants be open about their programme, their organisational allegiances, and their ultimate objectives. Hidden agendas corrupt democratic debate.
2.Militant has concealed from ordinary Labour members the full nature of its programme, its organisational structure, and its theoretical commitments, presenting itself as a group of committed socialists rather than a separate party.
3.This concealment is a form of political dishonesty that makes genuine pluralist debate impossible; you cannot debate honestly with people who are concealing what they actually believe and who they actually answer to.
∴Militant’s concealment of its full programme and organisational identity is incompatible with genuine democratic pluralism; the broad church argument assumes honest pluralism, which Militant’s entryist method precludes.
Argument III — The whole labour movement bears the costs of tolerating Militant within the Labour Party
1.The damage of Militant’s presence extends beyond the Labour Party to the whole labour movement: trade unions, Militant-influenced councils disrupting services, and community organisations whose credibility is damaged by association.
2.The labour movement’s standing with the public — its capacity to represent working people credibly — is damaged by the perception that its political wing is in the grip of extremists.
3.The question of tolerating Militant is therefore not a question about the Labour Party’s internal affairs alone; it is a question about the broader labour movement’s capacity to serve the interests of working people.
∴The costs of tolerating Militant are borne by the whole labour movement; the decision to expel is therefore an act of responsibility to the movement as a whole, not merely a piece of internal party management.

Chair,

I want to begin by saying something that I think has been largely absent from this debate on the other side: a statement of principle. I am going to argue against expulsion not because I agree with all of Militant’s politics — I do not — but because I believe the principle at stake is more important than any particular dispute about a particular tendency. The principle is this: that the Labour Party is, and must remain, a democratic socialist party that resolves its internal disagreements by debate and persuasion, not by exclusion and proscription.

“The right to discuss, to criticise, to persuade — this is not a concession by the leadership to the membership. It is the foundation of everything the Labour movement stands for.”
i. the historical record of proscription

iii. the argument from electability is a permanent alibi for accommodation

Electability alibi — the argument from electability has been used against every left advance in Labour history; it is an elastic justification for abandoning socialist policies under establishment pressureThe argument from electability deserves serious scrutiny because it is the argument that, in various forms, has been used against every significant advance by the left within the Labour Party. In the 1950s, the argument from electability was used against unilateral nuclear disarmament. In the 1960s, it was used against industrial democracy. In the 1970s, it was used against the Alternative Economic Strategy. In each case, the claim was that the left’s policies were unpopular and that Labour must move to the centre to win. In each case, moving to the centre did not produce the promised electoral breakthrough. The argument from electability is infinitely elastic: it can always be used to justify the next step of accommodation to establishment opinion. If the majority of Labour members and affiliated trade unionists believe that Militant’s programme is wrong, let them say so and vote accordingly. Let Militant’s arguments be put to conference and defeated. That is how a democratic party is supposed to work. The resort to expulsion is an admission that the leadership does not trust the party’s democratic processes to produce the right result. It is saying: we cannot win this argument in open debate, so we will remove the people making the arguments. That is not democracy. That is the suppression of democracy by administrative power.

Once it is established that a Labour leadership can expel a group of members whose politics it finds inconvenient, there is no principled stopping point. The mechanism exists; the template is set. The next leadership will use it against a different group. The right wing of this party has already shown that it regards the trade union link as an obstacle to electability, that it regards public ownership as an embarrassment, that it regards the commitments of successive Labour manifestos as extremist. If expulsion is a legitimate tool today, it is a legitimate tool tomorrow. The left will not always be the leadership, but the precedent will always be available to whoever is.
