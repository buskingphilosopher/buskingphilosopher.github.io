Mr President,

The sorites paradox begins with a simple observation that is philosophically explosive. Consider a heap of sand from which we remove one grain at a time. The argument runs:

P1: 10,000 grains of sand make a heap.
P2: If n grains make a heap, then n−1 grains make a heap.
C: Therefore, 1 grain of sand makes a heap — and even 0 grains.

P2 is the tolerance premise. It seems obviously true: surely one grain cannot make the difference between a heap and a non-heap. Yet P1 is clearly true, C is clearly false, and the argument is classically valid. Something has gone wrong — and I want to argue that what has gone wrong is not in P2's spirit but in the classical logic that takes a premise which is approximately true and treats it as categorically true, producing absurd consequences by iterated application.

"The word 'heap' does not pick out a precise property that things either have or do not have. It picks out a graded property — a matter of degree. Bivalence — the requirement that every proposition is either true or false — is appropriate for mathematics and for discourse about sharp properties. It is inappropriate for the vast territory of human language concerned with properties that come in degrees: tall, bald, red, old, rich, hot. In this territory, the correct logic is one that admits of degrees of truth."
i. the tolerance premise is true to a degree

Degree truth — tolerance premise is 0.999... true not 1; each application reduces truth by small amount; sorites valid but soundness failsIn fuzzy logic — the multi-valued logic developed by Lotfi Zadeh and applied to vagueness by Kenton Machina and others — propositions can take any truth value in the interval [0, 1], not merely 0 or 1. The tolerance premise "if n grains make a heap, then n−1 grains make a heap" is assigned a truth value very close to 1 — say, 0.9999 — for each step of the sorites series. It is not completely false: removing one grain from a large heap does not destroy the heap-ness of the pile; it merely diminishes it slightly. But it is not completely true either: heaps admit of degrees, and each step along the sorites series diminishes the degree of heap-ness by a small but real amount. The sorites argument, under this analysis, does not derive a falsehood from truths by valid reasoning. It derives a falsehood from near-truths by valid reasoning — and 10,000 applications of a premise with truth value 0.9999 produces a conclusion with truth value approximately 0.9999^10000 ≈ 0.0001. The conclusion is nearly false — which it is. The logic is preserved; only bivalence is given up.

ii. the penumbra — borderline cases are genuinely intermediate

Penumbra — borderline cases have intermediate truth value; not a matter of ignorance but of genuine semantic indeterminacyVague predicates have a clear positive extension (certainly a heap), a clear negative extension (certainly not a heap), and a penumbra of borderline cases where no determinate truth value applies. For degree theory, the penumbra is not a region of ignorance — as the epistemicists will claim — but a region of genuine semantic indeterminacy: the predicate simply does not have a sharp boundary, and no amount of investigation will reveal one, because there is none to be found. The borderline cases genuinely have intermediate truth values. A pile of 5,000 grains in a typical heap configuration is a heap to degree 0.5 — not "definitely a heap but we don't know it" or "definitely not a heap but we don't know it" but genuinely intermediate. This analysis respects both our intuition that the heap gradually diminishes and our intuition that borderline cases are not merely cases of ignorance.

iii. the Sorites argument — where it goes wrong

Sorites failure point — tolerance premise partially false at each step; repeated application compounds partial falsity; the inductive premise must be deniedThe sorites argument fails because it treats the tolerance premise as fully true and applies it iteratively. In classical logic, if P is true and Q follows from P, then Q is true — full stop. If we accept P2 as fully true, then the 10,000-step derivation from P1 to C is classically valid and C must be accepted as true. The degree-theoretic response is to deny that P2 is fully true: it has a high degree of truth but not degree 1. Under standard fuzzy logic's modus ponens for conditional premises that are less than fully true, the conclusion's truth value degrades with each application. The argument is formally valid — but the premises are not all true, and the conclusion's degree of truth is genuinely very low. Denying P2 is less counterintuitive on degree theory than it appears: we are not denying that the premise is almost true for each step. We are denying that it is exactly true — and it is not exactly true, because removing one grain does (to a tiny degree) make a difference.

iv. the higher-order vagueness problem

Higher-order vagueness — the boundary between truth-value regions is itself vague; degrees of truth face their own sorites; acknowledged difficultyI want to be honest about the principal objection to degree theory: the problem of higher-order vagueness. If we fix a degree threshold — say, degree 0.5 — and say that "heap" is true iff the pile has heap-degree ≥ 0.5, we have reintroduced a sharp boundary, only at the level of degrees rather than at the level of the predicate itself. And this sharp boundary seems just as arbitrary as the original sharp heap/non-heap boundary. The boundary between "degree of heap-ness ≥ 0.5" and "degree of heap-ness < 0.5" is itself vague: there will be cases where it is unclear whether the degree of heap-ness is above or below 0.5. The degree theory, strictly applied, seems to require degrees of degrees — and this regress may be vicious. Proponents like Machina have responded by allowing infinitely many truth values and avoiding sharp thresholds at any level. The objection is real; it is not decisive; and every theory of vagueness faces some version of it.

v. against classical solutions — they all posit unintuitive sharp boundaries

Against epistemicism and contextualism — sharp boundaries are unintuitive; degree theory respects phenomenology of vaguenessThe opposition will argue that there are sharp boundaries — we just don't know them (epistemicism) or they shift with context (contextualism). Both positions accept that at any given moment there is a precise number of grains n such that n grains make a heap and n−1 grains do not. This seems to me deeply wrong. The heap predicate is not like "the tallest person in this room" — a property that has a determinate referent even though we may not know who it is. It is a property that genuinely has no determinate referent, because it was never the purpose of the heap predicate to track precise numerical quantities. The predicate encodes a graded concept, and any analysis that forces it to have a sharp boundary is misrepresenting what the predicate means. Degree theory is the only analysis that takes the vagueness of vague predicates seriously as a semantic phenomenon rather than an epistemic one.

My colleague has proposed degree theory as the response to the sorites. I want to offer a different and, I believe, more philosophically defensible response that preserves the virtues of classical logic much more thoroughly: supervaluationism. The key insight of supervaluationism, developed systematically by Kit Fine in his 1975 paper "Vagueness, Truth and Logic," is that a proposition about a borderline case is neither true nor false — not because it has a third value but because the semantics of vague predicates underdetermines a unique truth value in the borderline region, and truth should be understood as truth under all admissible ways of making the semantics precise.

"Vague predicates admit of multiple sharpenings — multiple ways of making them fully precise that are consistent with their established use. 'Heap' might be sharpened to require exactly 5,000 grains, or exactly 4,500, or exactly 5,200. Each sharpening is admissible; none is privileged. A borderline-case proposition is true if it comes out true under every admissible sharpening — supertrue — false if false under every sharpening — superfalse — and neither otherwise. This is supervaluationism."
i. precisifications and supertruth

Precisifications — admissible sharpenings; supertruth = true in all precisifications; neither true nor false in borderline casesA precisification of a vague predicate is an assignment of a sharp boundary consistent with the predicate's established use: for "heap," it might be any number above some minimum; for "tall," it might be any number within a reasonable range. Multiple precisifications are admissible — our linguistic practices do not determine a unique one. Supervaluationism defines truth as supertruth: a proposition is true (full stop) iff it is true in every admissible precisification. A proposition about a clear case is supertrue: "10,000 grains make a heap" is true in every precisification that recognises a threshold anywhere near that many grains. A borderline proposition — "5,000 grains make a heap" — is true in some precisifications and false in others: it is neither supertrue nor superfalse. It is indeterminate: lacking a truth value, but not having a third value as degree theory holds.

ii. preserving classical logic — the key advantage

Classical logic preserved — tautologies are supertrue; modus ponens valid; only bivalence and excluded middle for borderline cases failSupervaluationism preserves classical logic to a remarkable extent — far more than degree theory does. Every classical tautology is supertrue: "either this is a heap or it isn't" is true under every precisification, because each precisification makes the predicate precise, and under any precise predicate a given pile either is or is not a heap. The law of excluded middle is supertrue even though no particular member of the disjunction need be supertrue: "10 grains is either a heap or not a heap" is supertrue, even though "10 grains is a heap" is superfalse and "10 grains is not a heap" is... actually supertrue. Every classical valid inference from true premises leads to a true conclusion, because the inference is valid under every precisification. What fails is not classical logic but bivalence: not every proposition is supertrue or superfalse. For borderline cases, there is a third status — indeterminate — that classical logic does not accommodate at the level of individual propositions.

iii. penumbral connections — a decisive advantage over degree theory

Penumbral connections — predicate constraints within a precisification; "if red then not orange" preserved; degree theory cannot handle thisOne of the most important advantages of supervaluationism over degree theory is its elegant handling of penumbral connections. A penumbral connection is a constraint that holds between vague predicates: if something is red, it is not orange; if someone is tall, they are not short; if something is a heap, it consists of many things. These connections must hold in any adequate theory of vagueness, because they reflect the logical relationships between predicates. Degree theory handles them badly: if "red" and "not-orange" are both assigned intermediate truth values, their conjunction can have a truth value lower than either conjunct, allowing cases where something is red to degree 0.6 and not-orange to degree 0.6 — but it is possible that the same patch is orange to degree 0.6, generating a clash. Supervaluationism handles penumbral connections perfectly: admissible precisifications are required to respect them — no precisification can count a patch as both red and orange.

iv. the sorites under supervaluationism

Sorites resolved — tolerance premise is superfalse; every precisification has a sharp boundary; the premise fails in every precisificationThe supervaluationist diagnosis of the sorites is elegant. The tolerance premise — "if n grains make a heap, then n−1 grains make a heap" — is superfalse. Under every admissible precisification, there is some specific threshold n such that n grains make a heap and n−1 do not. For each precisification, the tolerance premise has at least one instance that is false — namely, the pair (threshold, threshold−1). The premise is therefore false in every precisification — superfalse. The sorites argument has a false premise. Classical logic is used correctly: the argument is valid but unsound. Bivalence does not hold for borderline cases, but the sorites argument itself is diagnosed without invoking borderline cases in the premise: the tolerance premise is determinately false, and the argument fails at the first premise.

v. the objection — truth without a truthmaker

Truthmaker objection — "p or not-p" supertrue without either disjunct being true; strange disjunctions; Williamson's objection to supervaluationismThe most serious objection to supervaluationism comes from Williamson: it generates what seem to be strange disjunctions. "There is a precise number n such that n grains make a heap and n−1 do not" is supertrue — it follows from the superfalsehood of the tolerance premise. But for no specific number n is it supertrue that n grains are the cutoff. We have a true existential claim without any true instance — a truth without a truthmaker. Many philosophers find this deeply problematic. I acknowledge it is a real cost. But I believe it is the correct description of the situation: there must be a cutoff — classical logic demands it — but nothing in the semantics of "heap" determines which one it is. The indeterminacy is in the semantics, not in the world, and the supervaluationist framework is the most precise way available to represent this semantic indeterminacy.

Mr President,

The motion before this house asserts that vague predicates have no sharp boundaries and that classical logic must be revised. I want to defend the opposite on both counts. Vague predicates do have sharp boundaries. We simply do not know where those boundaries are — and a principled explanation of that ignorance is available. Classical logic is entirely preserved. The sorites argument is unsound because the tolerance premise is false. This position — epistemicism — has been developed most fully by Timothy Williamson in Vagueness (1994) and Roy Sorensen in Blindspots (1988), and I want to add to their case the argument I find most compelling from a broader perspective: that the alternatives to sharp boundaries are philosophically intolerable, and that a sharp boundary — even an unknowable one — is far preferable to revising classical logic or positing genuine semantic indeterminacy.

"Consider the predicate 'bald.' There is some precise number n of hairs such that a man with n hairs is not bald but a man with n−1 hairs is bald. We do not know what n is. No investigation of our linguistic practices will reveal it. But it exists. This is not a comfortable view — but the alternatives are more uncomfortable. A world in which the word 'bald' does not have a determinate extension is a world in which logic does not apply to ordinary language — and that is a world in which far too much is sacrificed."
i. the epistemicist argument — classical logic demands sharp boundaries

Classical logic demands sharp boundary — LEM applied to heap: every pile is either a heap or not; classical semantics for predicatesThe starting point of epistemicism is deceptively simple: classical logic's law of excluded middle applies to every proposition, including those involving vague predicates. For any pile of sand with a specific number of grains, either it is a heap or it is not a heap. This is a tautology — it is necessarily true. There is no third status, no "neither heap nor non-heap," no intermediate truth value. If this tautology holds, then for any predicate P and any object x, either P(x) is true or P(x) is false. The predicate divides objects into those to which it applies and those to which it does not — and this division is sharp, because for any object, one side of the division is where it belongs. There is, therefore, a last man in the sorites series who is bald, a man with some specific number of hairs n such that he is bald and a man with n+1 hairs is not bald. We do not know what n is. But it exists.

ii. Kagan's argument — threshold arguments in ethics and elsewhere

Kagan's threshold argument — practical reasoning requires sharp cutoffs; acting on vague predicates commits one to a threshold; consistency demands itShelly Kagan's contribution to the epistemicist position comes from practical philosophy rather than formal semantics, and it is worth dwelling on. In collective action cases — should I recycle? — agents face the problem that their individual act seems to make no difference. The sorites structure appears: if n people recycling is enough to make a difference, surely n−1 people is also enough. Kagan argues that rational practical reasoning requires that there be a threshold: some number n of participants such that n makes the relevant difference and n−1 does not. We cannot act at all — cannot make judgements about when to act — if we deny that such thresholds exist. The same argument applies to heap-predication and to all vague predicates that play a role in practical reasoning. A world without sharp boundaries is a world in which practical reason collapses into paralysis. The threshold must exist; our ignorance of it is the epistemically appropriate condition for a predicate whose extension is determined by complex facts about our linguistic use.

iii. Williamson's explanation of ignorance — margin-for-error and semantic plasticity

Semantic plasticity — boundary shifts across nearby usage worlds; we cannot know which boundary is actual; margin-for-error explains ignoranceThe natural objection to epistemicism is: how can we be ignorant of a sharp boundary that our own linguistic practices determine? Williamson's answer invokes semantic plasticity: the boundary of a vague predicate is sensitive to patterns of use across nearby possible worlds. In nearby possible worlds where we use "bald" slightly differently, the boundary is slightly different. The actual boundary is determined by the actual pattern of use — but knowing where the boundary is would require knowing which of the nearby worlds is actual, and our knowledge of our own usage patterns is not fine-grained enough to determine this. This is the margin-for-error argument applied to semantic knowledge: just as perceptual knowledge has margins of error (we cannot know we are seeing a number exactly at the limits of our visual acuity), semantic knowledge has margins of error (we cannot know the boundary of "bald" because the boundary could have been slightly different with a slightly different usage pattern).

iv. against degree theory — the higher-order vagueness regress

Against degree theory — higher-order vagueness is vicious for degree theory; every assignment of degree is arbitrary; classical logic sacrificed for no gainDegree theory responds to the sorites by assigning intermediate truth values to borderline cases. But this immediately generates higher-order vagueness: what is the truth value of "this pile of sand has heap-degree 0.5"? Presumably there is a borderline region for this claim too. And what is the truth value of "this pile of sand has heap-degree 0.5 to degree 0.7"? The regress is vicious: each assignment of degree generates a new borderline region, and no finite number of degrees of degree can resolve it. The epistemicist avoids this regress entirely: there is a sharp boundary, and there is a sharp fact about whether each object falls above or below it. We are ignorant of which fact, but the facts are all determinate. Classical logic is preserved at every level, and the regress that plagues degree theory is avoided.

v. against supervaluationism — truth without a truthmaker is unacceptable

Against supervaluationism — true existential with no true instance; truth-value gaps lose inference rules; distinctive operators problematicSupervaluationism tells us that "there is a precise number n such that n grains make a heap and n−1 do not" is supertrue, but for no specific n is it supertrue that n is the cutoff. This is truth without a truthmaker, and it generates practical paradoxes. It means we can truly say "there is a last bald man" without being able to say of any specific person whether they are the last bald man — not because we are ignorant of who it is, but because there is no fact of the matter. This is deeply counterintuitive. Furthermore, supervaluationism loses some classical inference rules at the subvaluation level — certain inferences that are classically valid are not valid at the level of individual precisifications — and the introduction of the "Definitely" operator generates further complications that multiply the philosophical difficulties. Epistemicism is simpler: there is a fact, we do not know it, classical logic applies throughout.

I want to offer a third path between the two positions we have heard from the proposition side and the epistemicist position of my colleague. Degree theory says truth comes in degrees and classical logic must be revised. Supervaluationism says borderline propositions have no truth value. Epistemicism says there are sharp boundaries and we are ignorant of them. I want to argue for contextualism: the view that vague predicates have sharp context-relative boundaries — boundaries that shift as the context of utterance changes — and that this explains both the tolerance intuition and its ultimate falsity without mysterious ignorance or non-classical logic.

"The tolerance premise seems true when we consider a sorites series one step at a time, because each individual step genuinely looks the same as the previous one. But this appearance depends on the context in which the judgment is made. Shift the context — consider a larger range — and sharp differences emerge. The tolerance premise is context-sensitive: it is true in some contexts and false in others. In every context in which the sorites argument is run, the tolerance premise is false. The illusion of its truth arises from conflating different contexts."
i. Raffman's psychological account — variable application

Raffman — variable application; same number of grains judged differently in different comparison sets; psychological evidence for context-sensitivityDiana Raffman's approach begins from the psychological evidence about how people actually apply vague predicates. In a classic series of experiments, subjects are asked to apply "green" to a sequence of colour patches that range from clearly green at one end to clearly blue at the other. When proceeding from green to blue, subjects shift their judgment from "green" to "not green" at a certain point — but when proceeding from blue to green, they shift at a slightly different point. The application of the predicate is not constant: it varies with the comparison set, the direction of presentation, and the framing of the judgment. This suggests that vague predicates are not assigned a fixed extension — they are applied in context-sensitive ways. "Is x a heap?" does not have a context-independent answer for borderline cases: the answer depends on what comparison cases are salient, what count as "similar" cases, and what purpose the judgment serves.

ii. Graff Fara's interest-relative account

Graff Fara — interest-relative boundaries; tall relative to current practical interests; sharp boundary exists but is interest-relative; no epistemicist mysteryDelia Graff Fara's development of the contextualist approach adds a crucial dimension: the extension of a vague predicate at any context is determined by the practical interests that are operative in that context. "Tall" does not mean the same thing at a basketball scouting session as at a kindergarten teacher evaluation — the relevant standard of height shifts with the context and what it is being used for. Crucially, this is not the same as saying there is no sharp boundary in any context. In any given context, with fixed practical interests, "tall" has a sharp boundary — some precise height is the threshold, determined by the interests operative in that context. The boundary is sharp at any moment; it merely shifts across contexts. The epistemicist's mysterious unknowable boundary is replaced by a context-relative boundary that is perfectly knowable in principle: you know what the operative interests are, so you know (in principle) where the boundary falls.

iii. why the tolerance premise is false in every context

Tolerance premise false in context — at the sharp boundary within a context, one step makes a difference; the sorites exploits context shiftThe decisive move in contextualism is explaining why the tolerance premise seems true while being false in every context. The tolerance premise "if n grains make a heap, then n−1 grains make a heap" seems true because we evaluate it locally: when n is in the middle of the series, the difference between n and n−1 grains seems negligible. But in any context with a fixed practical interest, there is some precise threshold n* such that n* grains make a heap and n*−1 do not, relative to those interests. At the threshold, the tolerance premise has a false instance: n* grains make a heap but n*−1 do not. The premise is false in every context — even though it seems true when we evaluate it locally. The sorites paradox exploits our tendency to evaluate the tolerance premise locally (where it seems true) while running the whole argument over the full series (where the context would have shifted). This is the illicit context shift that the sorites exploits.

iv. against epistemicism — semantic plasticity is unmotivated mystery

Against epistemicism — semantic plasticity postulates an unknowable fact; our use determines meaning but cannot determine a single boundary; contextualism avoids the mysteryWilliamson's semantic plasticity explanation is ingenious but it replaces one mystery with another. We are supposed to be ignorant of the boundary of "bald" because the boundary is sensitive to fine-grained facts about our usage patterns across nearby possible worlds — facts we cannot access. But this raises the question: what justifies the assumption that our usage patterns do determine a unique sharp boundary, as opposed to a vague boundary or a context-relative one? Contextualism provides a more parsimonious explanation: our usage does determine boundaries, but they are context-relative. The appearance of a single unknowable boundary arises from conflating the many context-relative sharp boundaries into a single predicate extension. There is no mysterious unknowable fact; there are many knowable context-relative facts.

v. classical logic preserved — without epistemic mystery or semantic gaps

Contextualism virtues — classical logic, no gaps, no degrees, no mystery; tolerance premise false in every context; sharp boundaries knowable in principleContextualism preserves classical logic completely: at any fixed context, the predicate has a sharp boundary, the law of excluded middle holds, and every proposition has a determinate truth value. There are no truth-value gaps (as supervaluationism requires), no intermediate truth values (as degree theory requires), and no unknowable sharp boundaries (as epistemicism requires). The tolerance premise is false in every context — there is always a threshold — but its apparent truth in local evaluations is explained by the context-sensitivity of the predicate. The sorites argument is unsound because the tolerance premise is false; classical logic is sound; and the explanation of the tolerance intuition is naturalistically respectable: it reflects the context-sensitivity of predicate application rather than genuine semantic indeterminacy or epistemic limitation.
