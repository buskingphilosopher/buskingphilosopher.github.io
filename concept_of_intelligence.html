The Concept of Intelligence

Published May 09, 2026

It is very natural to suppose that the word “intelligence” names a single, general capacity: something like an all-purpose ability to succeed across tasks, to learn quickly, to solve problems efficiently, whatever their domain. On this picture, intelligence is rather like a kind of mental horsepower, equally applicable whether one is doing mathematics, playing chess, or running a government.

But if we turn from this picture to the way the word is actually used, things begin to look less uniform.

Let us start with a simple observation. We say, without strain, “She’s very intelligent,” when speaking of someone who excels at mathematics, abstract reasoning, or theoretical work. We say it, too, of someone who writes perceptively, argues clearly, or grasps complex ideas quickly.

Now consider a different case. Someone is exceptionally skilled at bodybuilding. They are disciplined, precise in their training, knowledgeable about nutrition, able to refine technique over time. Do we say, in the same tone, “He’s very intelligent,” on that basis?

Not ordinarily. More naturally, we would say, “He’s very disciplined,” or “He really understands his training,” or perhaps “He’s smart about how he trains.” The word “intelligent,” used without qualification, does not sit quite as comfortably here.

So already we can see that “intelligence,” in ordinary use, is not simply applied to any domain of excellence.

Of course one might respond that perhaps intelligence is present in both cases, but we only notice it in the academic one. Yet this does not quite capture the linguistic fact. For if someone were to insist, “Bodybuilding at the highest level is just as much a display of intelligence as solving differential equations,” the claim would not sound false so much as redefinitional. It invites us to extend the word beyond its usual range.

This suggests that the definition we began with, namely intelligence as a general capacity, is not simply making explicit what we already mean, but is, in part, revising the concept.

Let us look more closely at how the ordinary word behaves.

We are happy to say:

(1) “She’s intelligent, but not very practical.”
(2) “He’s intelligent, but lacks common sense.”

These are not contradictions. Indeed, they are familiar kinds of criticism. But if intelligence were simply a general capacity to succeed at tasks, such statements would be puzzling. A genuinely general capacity should, one might think, show up across domains.

Yet our use of the word allows – indeed, expects – this kind of unevenness.

It is also worth noticing that we often contrast “intelligence” with other kinds of ability rather than grouping them under it. We distinguish intelligence from:

(a) physical strength,
(b) manual skill,
(c) emotional sensitivity,
(d) social adeptness.

We may, of course, speak of “emotional intelligence” or “social intelligence,” but here the adjective does some work. It signals that we are extending or adapting the term. Without it, “intelligent” on its own tends to gravitate toward a narrower range.

What is that range?

Roughly speaking – and we should not expect sharp boundaries – it centres on capacities involving:

(i) abstraction,
(ii) reasoning,
(iii) understanding,
(iv) learning in symbolically or conceptually structured domains.

This is why mathematics so often serves as a paradigm case. It is not that mathematics defines intelligence, but that it lies near the centre of the word’s ordinary use.

Now, how does this relate to the more expansive, theoretical notion of intelligence as a general capacity?

One possibility is to treat the theoretical notion as identifying something underlying the various abilities we observe – a latent trait that manifests differently across domains. On this view, ordinary language is simply picking out one prominent manifestation and mistaking it for the whole.

But this move is not as innocent as it may appear. For it involves shifting from a concept embedded in everyday practices – of praise, evaluation, and comparison – to a technical construct designed for explanation and measurement.

And the two do not line up neatly.

For example, in ordinary contexts, calling someone “intelligent” often carries a certain kind of commendation. It is not merely descriptive, it is also evaluative. It suggests a kind of respect for their way of thinking. By contrast, a technical notion of general cognitive ability may include capacities that do not carry the same evaluative weight in everyday life.

This helps explain why we hesitate to apply “intelligent” to certain forms of excellence. It is not that they lack complexity or skill, but that they do not fall within the evaluative frame that the word ordinarily invokes.

We can also see, now, how confusion may arise.

If we learn to think of intelligence as a general capacity, we may look at ordinary usage and conclude that people are being inconsistent or narrow-minded when they reserve “intelligent” for academic domains. But from the perspective of the ordinary language philosophers, what has happened is rather different.

Two different concepts are in play:

1. An ordinary concept, with its own patterns of use and evaluation,
2. A theoretical construct, introduced for explanatory purposes.

The mistake is to assume that the latter is simply the “true meaning” of the former.

This does not mean that the theoretical notion is illegitimate. It may be extremely useful in psychology or education. But it does mean that we should not expect it to govern ordinary usage without friction.

Finally, we might note that attempts to broaden the ordinary use of “intelligent” to include, say, bodily or practical excellence, are not merely descriptive corrections, but normative proposals. They invite us to reshape our evaluative landscape and to recognise as “intelligence” forms of ability that have traditionally been described in other terms.

Such proposals may be worthwhile. But they should not be mistaken for straightforward clarifications of what we already mean.

So, as before, the lesson is not that one usage is correct and the other mistaken, but that they serve different purposes. The ordinary word “intelligent” operates within a particular set of distinctions and evaluations. The theoretical notion of intelligence as general capacity operates within a different framework.

Confusion arises when we slide between them without noticing the shift.

And clarity, in this case, consists not in choosing one over the other, but in keeping track of which one we are using, and why.
