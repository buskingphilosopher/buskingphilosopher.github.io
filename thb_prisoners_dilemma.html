Mr President,

I want to begin with something that should disturb everyone in this chamber. The Prisoner's Dilemma is not a curiosity of game theory. It is a demonstration that the dominant theory of practical reason — the Self-Interest Theory, which holds that each person has most reason to do whatever will best serve her own long-run interest — is collectively self-defeating. When each agent acts as that theory recommends, all agents end up worse off than they would have been had each acted otherwise. A theory of rationality that systematically produces outcomes worse for everyone cannot be the correct theory of rationality. That is the argument. It is simple. Its implications are not.

On the structure of the dilemma

The self-defeat argumentConsider two prisoners, each facing the choice to confess or remain silent. Each knows that if both stay silent, both receive one year. If both confess, both receive eight years. If one confesses while the other stays silent, the confessor goes free and the silent prisoner receives ten years. Defection — confessing — strictly dominates cooperation for each prisoner individually: whatever the other does, you are better off confessing. And yet, when both reason this way, both receive eight years. Had both reasoned differently — had both cooperated — both would have received one year. The theory that told each to defect has made both worse off. This is not a paradox to be dissolved. It is a reductio of self-interest theory as a complete account of practical reason.

On what collective self-defeat means

Collective self-defeat as a criterion of theoretical failureIn Reasons and Persons I distinguish between theories that are individually self-defeating and those that are collectively self-defeating. A theory is collectively self-defeating when the universal adoption of what it recommends produces outcomes worse for everyone than the outcomes produced by the universal adoption of some alternative theory. Self-interest theory is collectively self-defeating in exactly this sense. This is not a peripheral defect. It is a defect at the very centre of the theory — a proof that the theory fails by its own standard, since the standard of self-interest theory is that each agent does well for herself, and collective adoption of the theory ensures that each agent does worse. A theory that fails by its own standard has failed comprehensively.

On the range of cases the Dilemma describes

The dilemma's ubiquityThe opposition will say that the Prisoner's Dilemma describes an artificial situation — two prisoners in separate cells, unable to communicate or commit. But the structure of the Dilemma is reproduced in an enormous range of real situations: arms races between nuclear powers, overfishing of shared waters, climate change — where each nation's dominant strategy is to defect from emissions reduction while others bear the cost — tax evasion, antibiotic resistance, and the tragedy of the commons in almost every form it takes. The question whether individual rationality is collectively self-defeating is not a question about prison cells. It is a question about the structure of practical reason wherever individually rational agents interact over shared resources or shared risks.

On what the Dilemma demands of practical reason

The demand for revision of practical reasonWhat should we conclude? Not — as Gauthier will argue — that rational agents should simply adopt a disposition to cooperate as a more effective means to self-interest. That response concedes the self-interest framework and attempts to rescue it through a more sophisticated account of its implementation. My conclusion is deeper: the Dilemma shows that self-interest theory is the wrong framework. Morality — other-regarding concern — is not a constraint on rationality, nor a sophisticated strategy for self-interest. It is part of what practical reason requires. An agent who cares only about herself is not a perfectly rational agent who happens to be amoral. She is an agent with a defective theory of what she has reason to do. The Dilemma is the proof.

On the separateness of persons and the impossibility of purely personal reasons

Against purely personal reasonsThe self-interest theorist believes that each person has reasons that are essentially indexed to herself — reasons that disappear when she is not among those affected. But consider: if it is bad that someone suffers, why should it matter whether that someone is me? The badness of suffering is not a property that varies with the identity of the sufferer. If anything matters at all, then what matters matters in a way that is not essentially personal. The Prisoner's Dilemma reveals this by showing that purely personal reasons, universally acted upon, produce outcomes bad for every person. The argument from collective self-defeat is thus not merely a practical argument about how to do better. It is an argument about the fundamental structure of practical reason — that reasons must be agent-neutral if they are to be adequate reasons at all.

Mr President,

Professor Parfit has given us the philosophical case from without: the Prisoner's Dilemma exposes the self-defeat of self-interest theory and demands that we revise our foundational account of practical reason. I want to press the case from within the self-interest framework itself — to show that even an agent committed to maximising her own outcomes has decisive reason to revise her theory of rational choice, and that this revision, when properly understood, vindicates the motion.

On straightforward and constrained maximisation

Constrained vs straightforward maximisationIn Morals by Agreement I distinguish two dispositions a rational agent might have. A straightforward maximiser asks, at every decision point, which available action maximises her expected utility — and performs that action. A constrained maximiser disposes herself to cooperate in mutually beneficial interactions with others who are similarly disposed, provided the expected gain from cooperation exceeds what she could achieve alone. The crucial claim is this: the constrained maximiser does better, in expectation, than the straightforward maximiser — not as a lucky accident, but as a structural feature of the Prisoner's Dilemma and all situations with its payoff structure. The disposition to constrained maximisation is itself the more rational disposition.

On why the disposition matters more than the act

Dispositions and translucencyThe opposition will say that at the moment of choice in a one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma, defection is always individually rational, regardless of one's dispositions. This objection misconceives where rationality enters. If agents are even partially translucent — if their dispositions are detectable by others to some degree — then an agent known to be a constrained maximiser will be selected as a cooperative partner, while an agent known to be a straightforward maximiser will not. In a world where cooperative opportunities arise repeatedly, the agent with the disposition to constrained maximisation will be included in more mutually beneficial interactions and will accumulate more welfare than the straightforward maximiser. The disposition is the rational object of choice, even if the act of cooperation in any single interaction appears sub-optimal from a myopic perspective.

On the objection from act-evaluation

Against act-by-act evaluationThe standard objection — pressed most forcefully by Binmore — is that rational choice must be evaluated act by act at the moment of decision, and at that moment defection dominates. But this is to insist on a framework of evaluation that is itself part of what the Prisoner's Dilemma puts in question. If act-by-act maximisation systematically produces outcomes worse for every agent, then act-by-act maximisation is not the right framework for evaluating rationality. A theory of rationality that evaluates dispositions rather than acts — asking which dispositions produce the best outcomes for agents who adopt them — is a revision of our theory of practical reason of precisely the kind the motion demands. The Prisoner's Dilemma shows not only that self-interest theory is self-defeating but that act-evaluation is the wrong unit of assessment.

On the connection between rationality and morality

Morality as rational constraintThe conclusion I draw from constrained maximisation is more conservative than Parfit's. I do not claim that morality is a constituent of rationality in the sense that agent-neutral concern is built into what it means to have reasons. I claim something more modest and, I think, more defensible: morality — construed as the set of mutually beneficial constraints on self-interested behaviour — is rationally justified from within the self-interest framework itself, because agents who adopt moral constraints do better by their own lights than agents who do not. The Prisoner's Dilemma is the demonstration. What it demands of practical reason is not that we abandon self-interest but that we understand self-interest correctly — as best pursued through principled constraints rather than act-by-act maximisation.

On the baseline and fair cooperation

Fair division and the cooperative surplusThere is a further dimension to the revision the Dilemma demands: not only must rational agents dispose themselves to cooperate, they must cooperate on terms that are fair. An agent who cooperates only when the division of the cooperative surplus strongly favours her is not a constrained maximiser — she is a strategic cooperator whose disposition to cooperate is conditional on terms she could not reasonably expect others to accept. Genuine constrained maximisation requires that the division of the cooperative surplus be proportionate to each agent's contribution and opportunity cost — what I call the minimax relative concession principle. The Dilemma thus demands not only that we revise our theory of practical reason away from act-by-act maximisation, but that we build into the revised theory a principle of fair division that is itself a rational constraint.

Mr President,

Professor Parfit has told us that the Prisoner's Dilemma exposes the self-defeat of individual rationality and demands a revision of practical reason. Professor Gauthier has told us that rational agents should adopt a disposition to constrained maximisation. Both are wrong, and wrong in ways that reveal a shared confusion about what the Prisoner's Dilemma actually shows and what rationality actually requires.

On the one-shot dilemma and what it actually proves

Defection is uniquely rational in the one-shot dilemmaIn a genuine one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma — a single interaction between two agents who will never meet again, whose choices are simultaneous and unobservable — defection is uniquely rational. This is not a paradox requiring philosophical resolution. It is a mathematical theorem. For any set of payoffs satisfying the Dilemma's structure, the only Nash equilibrium is mutual defection. The proposition presents mutual defection as a regrettable outcome that reveals a defect in self-interest theory. But mutual defection is simply what two rational agents produce when they correctly apply the theory of games to their situation. The regrettable outcome is not produced by a defective theory of reason. It is produced by a genuinely difficult situation — one in which the constraints of the game make cooperation impossible to establish through individual rationality alone.

On the confusion of the one-shot and the iterated game

One-shot vs iterated DilemmaThe proposition's case depends, almost entirely, on sliding between the one-shot and the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma without acknowledging the difference. In the iterated game — where players interact repeatedly over time — cooperation is indeed a Nash equilibrium under the folk theorem, achievable through strategies like tit-for-tat. Axelrod's celebrated tournaments showed that cooperative strategies can thrive when the shadow of the future is sufficiently long. But this result does not show that self-interest theory is self-defeating or that practical reason must be revised. It shows that when the game is iterated and agents can credibly threaten future punishment for defection, cooperation is the self-interested strategy. Self-interest theory handles the iterated case perfectly well. The proposition has confused the two cases and presented the iterated case as a refutation of self-interest theory in the one-shot case.

On Gauthier's constrained maximisation

Against constrained maximisationGauthier's constrained maximisation faces a decisive objection: at the moment of decision in a one-shot interaction, a constrained maximiser who has disposed herself to cooperate faces a choice between honouring her disposition and doing what actually maximises her utility. If she is rational at the moment of choice, she will defect — since defection dominates. But if she will rationally defect at the moment of choice, a rational opponent who anticipates this will not cooperate either. The disposition to constrained maximisation is therefore not a stable disposition for a rational agent. Gauthier's proposal requires that a rational agent be committed to acting irrationally at the moment of choice — an incoherent demand on any plausible theory of rationality.

On evolution and the emergence of cooperation

Evolution explains cooperation without revising rationalityThe real explanation of cooperation in Prisoner's Dilemma situations is evolutionary, not philosophical. Through repeated interactions in stable populations, cooperative strategies outcompete purely defecting strategies — not because cooperators are more rational, but because the ecological structure of repeated interaction makes cooperation more fit. Evolution and cultural learning produce the norms of reciprocity, fairness, and mutual obligation that sustain cooperation in human societies. These norms are not the deliverances of revised rational choice theory. They are the products of a selection process operating on behaviours and dispositions over time. Parfit and Gauthier have misidentified the phenomenon: they have found a problem that evolution solved, and proposed that philosophy should solve it instead. Philosophy is the wrong tool.

On what the motion's demand for revision would actually require

The cost of revising practical reasonThe motion asks us to revise our theories of practical reason. Let us be clear about what this means. To revise practical reason so that cooperation is rationally required in the one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma is to make it rationally required even when the other agent is certain to defect — since the structure of the one-shot game gives each agent no information about the other's choice. An agent who cooperates when she knows the other will defect is not more rational than an agent who defects. She is simply worse off. The proposition's revision of practical reason, if consistently applied, would require agents to accept exploitation as the price of philosophical consistency. This is not a revision of practical reason. It is its abandonment.

Mr President,

My colleague Binmore has made the game-theoretic case: defection is uniquely rational in the one-shot game, cooperation emerges through evolution, and no revision of practical reason is required. I want to press a different and in some ways more fundamental objection — one that takes the proposition's own concerns about rationality seriously and shows that those concerns, properly understood, do not support the motion.

On the Toxin Puzzle and the limits of intention

The Toxin PuzzleConsider what I call the Toxin Puzzle. A billionaire offers you one million dollars if, at midnight tonight, you intend to drink a mildly unpleasant but harmless toxin tomorrow afternoon. You do not need to drink it — only to intend to. But here is the puzzle: can you rationally intend to drink the toxin? At the moment tomorrow afternoon when you would drink it, there is no reason to do so — the money has already been paid for the intention, not the drinking. A rational agent who knows she will have no reason to drink the toxin tomorrow cannot now form a sincere intention to drink it, because intentions are sensitive to the agent's expectations of her future reasons. And if she cannot sincerely intend it, she cannot earn the million. The puzzle shows that there are situations where straightforward rationality prevents agents from reaping available benefits — not because the theory is self-defeating in Parfit's sense, but because rational intention has limits that no revision of the theory removes.

On the Paradox of Deterrence

The Paradox of DeterrenceThe Paradox of Deterrence has the same structure. A nation threatens to launch nuclear weapons if attacked — a threat that deters the attack. But if the attack occurs anyway, launching the weapons would be irrational: it would cause massive death for no benefit, since the deterrence has already failed. A rational nation that knows it would not launch if attacked cannot make a credible deterrent threat — and so cannot deter. The paradox reveals a genuine tension between the rationality of threats and the rationality of carrying them out. But the right response to this paradox is not to revise rationality so that following through on irrational threats becomes rational. The right response is to examine whether commitment devices — treaties, automated systems, credible precommitments — can make the threat credible without requiring irrational action.

On why these puzzles do not support the motion

Puzzles show limits, not defects requiring revisionBoth the Toxin Puzzle and the Deterrence Paradox show that there are situations where an agent's inability to commit to future irrational actions prevents her from reaping available benefits. Parfit and Gauthier interpret this as showing that individual rationality is self-defeating and must be revised. But I draw a different conclusion: these cases show that rationality has limits — situations where being rational is costly — but they do not show that rationality is defective. The cost of rationality in these situations is the same as the cost of honesty or integrity in situations where dishonesty would pay. We do not conclude from the existence of such situations that we should revise our theory of honesty. We conclude that being honest is sometimes costly. Rationality is sometimes costly too. This is not a defect. It is a feature of a demanding standard.

On the proposal to revise practical reason

Against revising practical reasonThe proposition asks us to revise our theories of practical reason so that cooperation in the Prisoner's Dilemma becomes rationally required. But what would such a revision look like? One option — Gauthier's — is to evaluate dispositions rather than acts. Another — Parfit's — is to incorporate agent-neutral reasons into the basic structure of rationality. Both revisions purchase cooperation at a significant cost. Gauthier's revision requires that rational agents act, at the moment of choice, on dispositions they have adopted — even when those dispositions recommend against what would maximise utility at that moment. This is a revision of rationality that makes rationality backward-looking in a way that conflicts with the basic intuition that rational action responds to current reasons. Parfit's revision requires abandoning the idea that reasons are essentially related to the agent's own perspective — a revision so deep that it dissolves the concept of rational agency rather than revising it.

On what the Dilemma actually demands

Institutions not revised rationalityThe correct conclusion from the Prisoner's Dilemma is not that we must revise our theory of practical reason. It is that we must design institutions that change the payoff structure of the game so that individual rationality and collective benefit align. When defection is punished by law, by reputation systems, by social sanction, or by treaty — the Dilemma's structure is altered. Cooperation becomes the dominant strategy because the payoffs have changed, not because rationality has changed. Hobbes understood this: the solution to the Prisoner's Dilemma is the Leviathan — a third party whose enforcement power transforms the game. The proposition's demand for a philosophical revision of rationality is unnecessary if the right institutional response is available. And it is always available, at least in principle. What the Dilemma demands is not better philosophy. It is better institutions.
