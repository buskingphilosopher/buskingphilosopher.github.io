Logical Arguments

Mr President,

The sceptic about other minds imagines that knowledge is built from the inside out. We begin with the solitary Cartesian ego — certain only of its own sensations, its own thoughts, its own inner theatre — and then, by some inferential ladder, attempt to climb outward to knowledge of an external world and, finally, to the most tenuous knowledge of all: that there are other minds.

I want to argue that this picture is not merely philosophically difficult. It is incoherent. It gets the order of explanation exactly backwards. Once we see why, the sceptical problem about other minds does not become harder to solve. It dissolves.

On the conditions for any thought at all

The argument from triangulation and contentAny creature capable of thought must possess the concept of objective truth. To think that something is the case is to think that it is the case independently of whether one believes it is the case. Thought is not mere sensation — sensation does not have a truth condition. Pain is not true or false. But the thought that there is a table in the room, or that it will rain tomorrow, has a truth condition. It purports to represent how things are, independently of how they seem. Now: the concept of objective truth — the concept of things being thus and so independently of one's own perspective — cannot be acquired in isolation. It cannot be conjured from within the solitary mind. For the concept of objectivity requires the contrast between what seems to be the case and what is the case. And that contrast only becomes available to a creature that has been in communication with others — creatures who can correct its mistakes, whose reactions reveal that things are not always as they seem.

On triangulation

The argument from triangulation and contentConsider learning the meaning of a word. A child learns to use "table" in the presence of tables. But how does she know what the word refers to? Tables are the most salient common feature of her environment — but so are the light reflections off the tables, the spatial regions occupied by them, the temporal moment of encounter. Without another mind to triangulate with — to confirm that it is the table and not the light reflection that the word picks out — the child cannot fix the reference of her terms. And without fixed reference, there is no determinate content to thought. A thought that could equally be about tables or about light reflections off tables is not a thought about either. Triangulation requires two minds and a shared world — two creatures responding to a common stimulus, each responding to the other's responses.

On inverting the Cartesian order

The argument from inverting the Cartesian orderThe Cartesian picture says: first the individual mind with its private certainties; then, by inference, the external world; then, most tenuous of all, other minds. Knowledge of other minds is the last and least secure step. I say: knowledge of other minds is not the last step. It is a precondition of the first. There cannot be an individual mind with determinate thoughts unless there is already a social setting within which content is fixed, in which the concept of objective truth is acquired, in which the distinction between appearance and reality becomes available. The existence of other minds is not a conclusion to be argued to from a solitary starting point. It is a precondition of there being any starting point at all.

On the interpersonal character of knowledge

The argument from holistic and interpersonal knowledgeThe acquisition of knowledge is not a progression from the subjective to the objective. It does not begin inside the head and reach, with difficulty, an external world and other minds. It emerges holistically and is interpersonal from the start. What we know, we know together. The concept of objective truth is a shared achievement. The fixing of content is a social achievement. The very idea of a mind — of a creature with thoughts that purport to represent a world — is an achievement that requires other minds. The sceptic cannot get the problem started without already presupposing what she claims to doubt.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleague Davidson has shown that the Cartesian order is inverted — that knowledge of other minds is a precondition of thought itself. I want to approach the same conclusion from a different direction: through the impossibility of a private language, and through what this impossibility reveals about the grammar of mental concepts.

On the private language argument

The private language argumentSuppose, as the sceptic assumes, that mental states are essentially private and that the words we use to describe them get their meaning by being privately attached to private objects — that "pain" means what it does because I have ostensively defined it in my own mind by attending to the sensation and saying "this is what I will call pain." This supposition is incoherent. For a word to mean something, there must be a distinction between using it correctly and using it incorrectly. But in the case of a purely private ostensive definition — where the only check on correct application is my own memory of the original sensation — there is no genuine distinction between getting it right and getting it wrong. My memory of what I originally called pain is not an independent check. It is simply another mental state that itself requires checking. The private ostensive definition provides no genuine standard of correctness. Where there is no standard, there is no rule. Where there is no rule, there is no meaning. The private language is not a language at all.

On the grammar of mental concepts

The private language argumentMental concepts do not function as names for private inner objects of which behaviour is merely a symptom. They are embedded in practices, in forms of life, in patterns of response that are essentially social. To learn to use the word "pain" is not to associate it with a private sensation and then cautiously infer that others have similar private sensations. It is to learn a form of life in which pain behaviour, pain avoidance, pain expression, and pain response are part of a seamlessly interconnected social practice. The sceptical problem about other minds rests on a grammatical confusion: it assumes the mental is essentially inner and behaviour merely evidence for it. But this is a misleading picture of how mental concepts work.

On the asymmetry between first and third person

The argument from first/third person asymmetryI do not want to deny the asymmetry between the first-person and third-person cases. "I am in pain" does not function as a description of an inner state I have observed and identified. It is an expression — a linguistic act in a social practice — grammatically analogous to a natural expression of pain rather than to a report of an observation. But this asymmetry does not support scepticism. It supports a revised picture of what mental concepts are. The mistake is to think that first-person authority — the special status of "I am in pain" — shows that the mental is essentially private. It shows only that first-person and third-person uses of mental concepts play different grammatical roles. Both roles are embedded in social practices. Both require other people and shared forms of life. In our ordinary lives, we do not treat others as puzzles whose inner lives we must infer. We see their joy, their anger, their distress directly. This is not naïvety. It is the correct grammar of our mental concepts, visible once we stop distorting our language through the Cartesian picture.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleagues have attacked the Cartesian picture from the philosophy of language and from the theory of content. I want to attack it from the metaphysics of persons — from the question of what kind of entity a person is, and what this reveals about the grammar of other minds scepticism.

On the concept of a person as primitive

The argument from the primitive concept of a personThe other minds sceptic operates with a picture in which there are two separate kinds of thing: physical bodies, which are publicly observable, and minds, which are private. The question then becomes: how do we know that certain bodies are accompanied by minds? But this picture makes an error at the level of basic ontology. It treats "person" as a composite concept — a body plus a mind — rather than as a fundamental category in its own right. I want to argue that "person" is a primitive concept. Persons are entities to which we ascribe both physical predicates and personal predicates — pain, belief, intention, anger — and these predicates are ascribed to the same entity. They are not ascribed to two different things — a body and a mind — that somehow go together. The composite picture is the mistake that generates the sceptical problem.

On the logic of personal predicates

The argument from the primitive concept of a personPersonal predicates have a logical feature entirely missed by the Cartesian picture. They are predicates that we ascribe both to ourselves — on the basis of no observation — and to others — on the basis of their behaviour and circumstances. The self-ascription and the other-ascription are applications of the same predicate to the same kind of entity. The Cartesian is mesmerised by the asymmetry in the basis for ascription and concludes that one application is direct and the other indirect. But if personal predicates are primitively applicable to persons as a fundamental ontological category, then both applications are direct applications of the same concept. The asymmetry shows only that persons can be known from two perspectives with different evidential structures — not that one is a case of genuine knowledge and the other mere inference.

On the incoherence of the sceptical position

The argument from the incoherence of denying other mindsConsider what the sceptic is committed to. She has thoughts, beliefs, intentions. She regards these as genuinely her own — as states of an entity she identifies as herself. But she cannot identify herself as a person — as a subject of experience — without employing the concept of a person. And the concept of a person is the concept of an entity to which both physical predicates and personal predicates are ascribed. It is already a concept that applies to a type — not to a single individual. Furthermore: concepts are general. A concept of pain that could only be applied to a single case — one's own — is not a concept of pain. For pain to be a concept I apply to myself, it must be in principle applicable to other cases. The logical grammar of personal predicates presupposes that they can be applied to entities other than oneself. The sceptic, in doubting other minds, is sawing off the branch she sits on.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to begin with an admission that will not be expected from someone on this side. The argument from analogy — which I have myself defended as the best available basis for belief in other minds — is much weaker than it appears. And I want to argue that this weakness is not a deficiency in my presentation, but a structural feature of the problem that cannot be overcome.

On the argument from analogy and its structural weakness

The argument from triangulation and contentThe argument from analogy runs: I know in my own case that certain bodily states and behaviours are accompanied by mental states. I observe that other humans have similar bodies and behave similarly in similar circumstances. By analogy, I infer they too have mental states. This is the best argument available. It has good inductive structure. But it has a decisive weakness. In standard inductive reasoning, we generalise from many observed cases to unobserved ones. Here, the observed basis for generalisation is a single instance — my own case. When I infer from one piece of copper that all copper conducts electricity, I have at least observed the one case. When I infer from my own case to others, I have confirmed the correlation between physical state, behaviour, and mental state in exactly one instance. And every other case to which I extend the inference is unobservable not merely in the sense that I have not yet checked it, but in the sense that it is unobservable in principle.

On the possibility of philosophical zombies

The argument from philosophical zombiesThe structural weakness in the argument from analogy opens the door to a possibility that cannot be closed without begging the question. The possibility is this: that there exist entities behaviourally and physically indistinguishable from minded creatures but lacking any inner experience whatsoever. I do not claim to know that other humans are philosophical zombies. I claim only that the argument from analogy does not give me sufficient reason to be confident they are not. The zombie hypothesis is consistent with every observation I can make. An hypothesis consistent with every possible observation is not one that inductive argument can rule out.

On what we are left with

The argument from epistemic honesty about other mindsWe are left with a belief in other minds that is practically indispensable and rationally undergrounded. I do not propose that we abandon it. I propose that we be honest about its epistemic status. It is a belief held on the basis of an argument from analogy so weak that it barely deserves the name of knowledge. My opponents will produce transcendental arguments, arguments from the conditions of language, arguments from the concept of a person. I will argue that each either smuggles in what it purports to establish, or establishes only that we cannot coherently doubt other minds — which is not the same as showing that they exist.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleague Russell has been admirably honest about the weakness of the argument from analogy. I want to press the problem further, and in particular to challenge the transcendental arguments that my opponents deploy. Davidson, Wittgenstein, Strawson — all argue that the very possibility of thought, language, and conceptual knowledge presupposes the existence of other minds, and that therefore scepticism is self-refuting. This is elegant. It is also, I will argue, a strategy that confuses conceptual necessity with existence.

On transcendental arguments and their limits

The argument from inverting the Cartesian orderDavidson argues: for propositional thought to be possible, communication with others must have occurred; I have propositional thought; therefore other minds exist. But this argument establishes, at most, that if I have propositional thought, then I have been in communication with other minded creatures. It is a conditional. It tells me what would have to be true if I have propositional thought. It does not tell me that I do have propositional thought, or that the creatures I have been in communication with have minds in the full sense. Transcendental arguments establish only what must be presupposed for experience to be possible — not what is actually the case in the external world. Kant himself recognised this limitation. Even if I must presuppose other minds to engage in propositional thought, this establishes only that I must presuppose them — not that they exist. A sceptical hypothesis — that my "communication" has been with sophisticated automata, or that I am a brain in a vat with simulated social interactions — may be consistent with the transcendental argument's conclusion while still leaving open the question of whether those presupposed minds exist.

On the private language argument's limits

The private language argumentWittgenstein's private language argument succeeds against a particular form of Cartesianism — the view that mental words get their meaning by being privately attached to private objects. I accept this. But I do not accept that it establishes the existence of other minds. What the private language argument establishes is that our mental language is socially embedded — that criteria for correct application of mental terms are in shared practices. This is a claim about language. The question remains: do the creatures whose practices underwrite the correct use of mental language have genuine inner experiences? The social embeddedness of mental language tells us what the criteria for applying mental terms are. It does not tell us whether, behind the behaviour satisfying those criteria, there is anything it is like to be the creature in question. The sceptical question can be reformulated: I grant that my mental concepts are socially embedded. I ask: do the creatures whose behaviour satisfies those criteria have phenomenal consciousness?

On the hard problem and what remains

The argument from the hard problem of consciousnessThe deepest reason for scepticism about other minds is not the weakness of the argument from analogy. It is the hard problem of consciousness — the explanatory gap between physical processes and phenomenal experience. We do not have a satisfactory account of why any physical process is accompanied by experience. We do not know why there is something it is like to be me rather than nothing. Given this explanatory gap, the inference from the physical and behavioural similarity of others to the existence of their inner experience remains an inference that cannot be closed. My opponents argue that the sceptical question cannot coherently be asked. I argue that it can, that it has not been answered, and that the most honest position acknowledges that belief in other minds — however practically indispensable — rests on an epistemic foundation that has not been secured.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to make a different kind of argument — one that goes deeper than the epistemological question about what we can know, and raises a more fundamental question about the nature of consciousness itself and the challenge of achieving genuine understanding of another mind.

On the subjective character of experience

The argument from the subjective character of experienceThe fundamental fact about consciousness is that it has a subjective character. There is something it is like to be a bat — something it is like to navigate by sonar, to hang upside down in darkness, to perceive the world through sound. This subjective character — the what-it-is-like-ness of experience — is not captured by any objective description of the bat's neurophysiology, its behaviour, or its ecological niche. Now the problem about other minds is not merely the epistemological question of how I can know that other beings have experiences. It is the deeper question of whether I can even form an adequate concept of what another's experience is like from the inside. When I try to imagine what it is like to be a bat, I do not achieve understanding of the bat's experience by imagining myself navigating by sonar. I merely imagine what it would be like for me — with my kind of consciousness — to navigate by sonar. I imagine a modification of my own experience, not a genuinely different form of experience.

On why Davidson's triangulation does not close the gap

The argument from triangulation and contentDavidson argues that content is fixed by triangulation — two minds responding to a shared world. I want to accept the triangulation account of content while denying that it closes the gap about other minds. Even if the content of my thoughts is fixed by social interaction — even if I could not have any thoughts without engaging with other apparent minds — this does not establish that those apparent minds have subjective experience. It establishes that I have been in causal interaction with entities whose behaviour triangulates with mine to fix the content of my representations. Whether those entities have anything it is like to be them is a further question. The triangulation account tells us about the causal conditions for the fixation of content. The hard problem of consciousness tells us that the subjective character of experience is not captured by any causal account. Therefore the triangulation account does not establish the existence of phenomenal consciousness in other beings.

On the irreducibility of the first-person perspective

The argument from the subjective character of experienceThe deepest point is this. The first-person perspective — the fact that experience is always experience from a point of view — is not merely a practical limitation on our knowledge of others. It is a metaphysical feature of consciousness itself. Consciousness is essentially perspectival. There is no view from nowhere with respect to consciousness. The attempt to arrive at knowledge of another's consciousness from the third-person perspective is the attempt to arrive at a non-perspectival view of something that is essentially perspectival. This attempt cannot succeed in principle. This does not mean that others definitely lack consciousness. It means that the gap between my perspective and theirs cannot be closed by argument, by triangulation, or by any interpersonal procedure. The existence of other minds is not something that can be established. It is something that must be taken on faith — or accepted as a brute assumption without epistemic justification.

Thank you.
