Poetic Analysis · Victorian Imperial Poetry

Recessional

Rudyard Kipling — The Times, 17 July 1897 (Diamond Jubilee)
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
"Recessional" (1897) is in the public domain. First published in The Times on 17 July 1897; collected in The Five Nations (Methuen, 1903).
Structure & Context
Form: Five stanzas of six lines each, rhyming ABABCC; the CC couplet in every stanza is either the refrain or a variant closing it Occasion: Written for Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee celebrations, 1897, at the height of British imperial power — the poem counsels humility at the moment of maximum pride The refrain: "Lest we forget" recurs five times; the phrase is now most strongly associated with Remembrance Day, a meaning Kipling did not intend but could not prevent "Recessional": The title names the hymn played as clergy and choir process out at the end of a service — the poem positions Empire itself as a church service now ending Nineveh and Tyre: Both were the greatest cities of their ages; both are ruins. The biblical reference is the poem's most compressed argument: all empires end Reception: The poem was controversial on publication — many Jubilee readers expected celebration, not warning; Kipling's editor initially rejected it as too downbeat
Stanza I
God of our fathers, known of old, Lord of our far-flung battle-line, Beneath whose awful Hand we hold Dominion over palm and pine— Lord God of Hosts, be with us yet, Lest we forget—lest we forget!
Stanza II
The tumult and the shouting dies; The Captains and the Kings depart: Still stands Thine ancient sacrifice, An humble and a contrite heart. Lord God of Hosts, be with us yet, Lest we forget—lest we forget!
Stanza III
Far-called, our navies melt away; On dune and headland sinks the fire: Lo, all our pomp of yesterday Is one with Nineveh and Tyre! Judge of the Nations, spare us yet, Lest we forget—lest we forget!
Stanza IV
If, drunk with sight of power, we loose Wild tongues that have not Thee in awe, Such boastings as the Gentiles use, Or lesser breeds without the Law— Lord God of Hosts, be with us yet, Lest we forget—lest we forget!
Stanza V
For heathen heart that puts her trust In reeking tube and iron shard, All valiant dust that builds on dust, And guarding, calls not Thee to guard, For frantic boast and foolish word— Thy mercy on Thy People, Lord!
