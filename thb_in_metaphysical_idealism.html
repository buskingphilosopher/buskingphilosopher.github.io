Absolute Idealism & The Regress of RelationsF.H. Bradley • Appearance and Reality (1893)
Reality is a single, unified, sentient Absolute Experience. The common-sense world of distinct things standing in relations is self-contradictory and therefore mere appearance. Bradley’s central argument: relations are incoherent. Neither treating them as independent nor as internal to their terms escapes a vicious infinite regress. The dissolution of relations collapses pluralism and drives us to the Absolute. Space, time, causation, the self, and matter are all demonstrated to be contradictory — hence phenomenal, not real.

Idealist Ethics & Positive FreedomT.H. Green • Prolegomena to Ethics (1883)
All knowledge presupposes a unifying spiritual principle that cannot itself be the product of experience. The self that knows is not a product of natural processes but a condition of their intelligibility. Morality consists not in the satisfaction of natural desires but in self-realisation through participation in a common good. Green drew the practical consequences of idealism for politics: genuine freedom is positive — the power of the fully realised self — not merely the negative absence of constraint.

The Unreality of TimeJ.M.E. McTaggart • “The Unreality of Time” (1908)
Time is unreal. The A-series (events as past, present, future) is essential to time but generates a vicious contradiction: every event must be past, present, and future, yet these are mutually exclusive properties. The B-series (earlier-later relations) cannot rescue time because it presupposes the A-series for change. The apparent flow of time is a feature of our limited perspective on a timeless, spiritual reality. McTaggart’s argument is among the most technically precise and most scandalous products of the idealist programme.

Chair,

I want to make it clear from the outset that the position I am defending is not a pious or comfortable one. It is a position driven by argument — by the most rigorous application of logic to the categories we employ in our ordinary and scientific thinking. And the conclusion of that argument is surprising, scandalous even: that the world as we ordinarily conceive it — a world of many things standing in relations, spread out in space and time, causally connected — is fundamentally self-contradictory. It is appearance, not reality.

The idealist starting point: all reality is experience.

I begin from a point that may seem too obvious to dispute: we cannot step outside experience to compare what we know with how things are independently of all knowing. Every attempt to describe “reality as it is in itself” is itself an act of thought, an exercise of experience. The very concept of something “independent of mind” is itself a concept — a product of the mental activity it claims to transcend.

This does not mean reality is the private experience of any one mind. It means that the distinction between “inner” and “outer,” “mental” and “physical,” is itself a distinction drawn within experience and is not the ultimate distinction between experience and something wholly alien to it. Reality — the Absolute — is experience; but it is experience vast, complete, and unified beyond anything that finite minds can directly grasp.
i. the contradiction of relations

Bradley's regress — neither external nor internal relations are coherent; both generate infinite vicious regresses; relations dissolve the pluralist worldThe heart of my case against the common-sense world is the argument that relations are incoherent. Consider a simple relational fact: A is to the left of B. We have two terms (A and B) and a relation (to-the-left-of). Now: in what does the relating consist? The relation must somehow connect A and B. But if the relation is a third entity connecting A and B, it must itself be related to A and B by further relations — and those relations by further relations still. We have an infinite regress that never delivers the original unity we were trying to explain. On the other hand, if we say the relation is not a third entity but is internal to A and B — part of what A and B are — then the “terms” A and B are not independent things that happen to stand in the relation; they are constituted by it. But then A cannot be what it is without B, and B cannot be what it is without A, and neither can be what it is without all the other things they stand in relations to. The conclusion is unavoidable: the relational structure of the world is self-contradictory. A world of many things in relations is a world whose description generates vicious infinite regresses and constitutive dependencies that collapse the distinction between terms and relations entirely.

ii. space and time as appearance

Space and time contradictory — spatial parts require relations that generate the regress; time requires instantaneous present that is infinitely divisible and hence extended; both are appearanceApplying this argument to space and time yields their unreality as genuine features of reality. Space is a system of spatial relations — of things having positions relative to one another. But these positions are themselves relationally constituted: to be “here” is to be in a specific relationship to everything else. The infinite regress of relations shows that spatial position cannot be absolutely determinate. Similarly with time: any moment, to be genuinely present, must be a punctual instant without duration; but any duration, however small, is divisible into earlier and later parts. The present is either a nothing (no duration) or an extension (with parts), and neither is coherent. The contradiction in our concept of the present mirrors the contradiction McTaggart will elaborate more fully: our ordinary temporal experience is self-undermining.

iii. the self as appearance

Self contradictory — finite self requires distinction between self and not-self; this distinction is relational and hence incoherent; the finite self is a partial, contradictory aspect of the AbsoluteThe same argument demolishes the ordinary conception of the self. To be a self — a finite, individual mind — is to stand in distinction from what is not-self: from the external world, from other selves, from one’s own past and future states. But these distinctions are relational. The self’s identity is constituted through its differences from everything else. This means the finite self, like space and time, is not an ultimate, self-subsistent reality but a partial, internally contradictory perspective on the Absolute. The finite self is real as an appearance — it is genuinely how the Absolute shows itself at this particular point of view — but it is not reality as it is in itself. When thought is pressed to its conclusions, the distinction between self and world, between inner and outer, dissolves. What remains is the Absolute: a single, all-encompassing experience in which all finite distinctions are at once preserved and overcome.

iv. the Absolute: positive characterisation

The Absolute — unified sentient experience; all finite appearances are absorbed into and harmonised within it; thought falls short of the Absolute but points toward itMy opponents will say I have described the Absolute only negatively — as what remains when contradictions are removed. What positive content can be given to it? I answer: the Absolute is experience. Not the fragmentary, perspectival experience of finite minds, but a complete, sentient experience that includes and harmonises all the fragmentary experiences of all finite perspectives. We can have some positive idea of this because feeling — immediate, non-relational experience — gives us a hint of what experience without the contradictions of the relational would be like. A moment of pure feeling, before it is conceptualised and cut up into subject and object, into self and world, is closer to the Absolute than any theoretical system. The Absolute is the completion of what finite experience always already reaches toward but never grasps. Thought is essentially self-transcending: every judgment, every inference, every attempt to think truly about things, points beyond itself toward a unity it cannot itself express.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Bradley’s regress: relations are incoherent whether treated as external or internal to their terms
1.If a relation R between terms A and B is external — an independent third entity — it must itself be related to A and B by further relations R′ and R′′. These require further relations still: a vicious infinite regress results that never delivers relational unity.
2.If R is internal to A and B — constitutive of what they are — then A and B cannot be what they are without R, and A cannot be what it is without B (and without everything else A is related to). Terms collapse into their relations; the distinction between term and relation is dissolved.
3.Neither option is coherent: the relational structure of the world is self-contradictory. A genuinely pluralist world — one of many independent things in genuine relations — cannot be articulated without contradiction.
∴Relations are incoherent whether treated as external or internal; the pluralist world of common sense and science, which is a world of things in relations, is self-contradictory and hence mere appearance; reality must be a unified, non-relational whole.
Argument II — Space and time are contradictory: their relational structure generates the same regress; both are appearance
1.Space is constituted by spatial relations: positions are determinate only relative to other positions. The infinite regress of relations means no spatial position is absolutely determinate. Space cannot be a feature of ultimate reality.
2.Time generates its own contradiction: the present moment must be punctual (no duration) to be genuinely present, but a purely punctual instant is an infinitely divisible nothing. Any duration is divisible into earlier and later; any attempt to locate the present yields a regress.
3.Space and time are therefore not features of ultimate reality but of our partial, finite perspective on the Absolute. They are “appearance” in Bradley’s sense: self-contradictory but not mere illusion, since appearances are how the Absolute shows itself to finite minds.
∴Space and time are contradictory and therefore appearance: the spatial world of physics and the temporal world of history are real as perspectives on the Absolute but are not features of ultimate, unconditional reality.
Argument III — The finite self is appearance: its identity is constituted through relational distinctions that the regress dissolves
1.The finite self is constituted by its distinction from what is not-self: from the external world, other selves, and its own past and future. These distinctions are relational.
2.The regress of relations shows that these relational distinctions cannot yield an ultimately self-subsistent, absolutely determinate self. The finite self is not an atomic individual but a partial perspective constituted through its relations to everything else.
3.When the finite self is followed to its logical conclusion, the distinction between self and world, inner and outer, dissolves into the Absolute. The self is real as appearance but not as an ultimate, unconditional reality.
∴The finite self is appearance: its identity is constituted through relations that dissolve under the regress argument; the self is not an ultimate, self-subsistent atom but a partial, self-transcending perspective on the Absolute.
Argument IV — The Absolute as unified sentient experience: all finite appearances are harmonised within it
1.The Absolute must be experience because every attempt to characterise reality as independent of all experience is itself an exercise of experience. We cannot step outside experience to find something wholly alien to it.
2.The Absolute is not the fragmentary experience of finite minds but a complete, sentient experience that harmonises all finite perspectives into a non-contradictory whole. Feeling — immediate, pre-relational experience — provides a positive hint of what non-relational unity is like.
3.Thought is essentially self-transcending: every judgment and inference points beyond the finite to a unity it cannot itself express but which it presupposes. The Absolute is the completion of what thought always already aims at.
∴The Absolute is unified sentient experience that harmonises all finite appearances; it is positively characterised through the self-transcendence of thought and the non-relational immediacy of feeling; it is not nothing but the completion of what finite minds always already reach toward.

Chair,

Bradley has laid the metaphysical foundations. My contribution is to extend the idealist argument into the domain of logic, knowledge, and the practical life — to show that not only does the common-sense metaphysics of things-in-relations contradict itself, but that the formal logic of the opposition embodies the same contradiction at the level of the concept.

“The abstract universal, taken by itself, is a mere emptiness; it achieves generality by omitting precisely what makes the particulars in question what they are. Reality operates with concrete universals: wholes in which each part is what it is by virtue of its place in the whole.” — Bosanquet, adapted
i. the abstract universal and its failure

Abstract universal — the bare class-concept shared by many particulars is a philosophical fiction; it achieves generality by throwing away the very content that makes things knowable; only concrete universals are realRussell and Moore will defend a view of logic in which concepts are abstract universals: properties or classes that are common to many particulars precisely because they abstract away from the differences between those particulars. The concept “red” applies to many things by leaving aside everything that makes each red thing distinctively what it is. I deny that this is how real knowledge operates. The abstract universal is an intellectual convenience, not a genuine object of knowledge. When we genuinely understand something — when we know what oxygen is, what a sonata is, what courage is — we do not know it through a bare common property extracted from many instances. We know it through a concrete universal: an organised whole whose parts are intelligible only in relation to one another and to the whole. The periodic table is not a list of abstract properties shared by elements; it is a systematic whole in which each element’s nature is constituted by its relations to all the others.

ii. the logic of judgment and inference

Judgment self-transcending — every judgment predicates something of reality as a whole; the subject of judgment is always ultimately the Absolute; finite judgments are always partial, always seeking completionEvery judgment has the same deep structure: a subject (something about reality) and a predicate (something true of it). But what is the subject of judgment? When I say “the rose is red,” I do not merely predicate redness of a particular object identified in isolation. I make a claim about how the world is: I am implicitly claiming that reality, at the place where the rose is, is such as to support the predication of redness. Every finite judgment is therefore a partial claim about reality as a whole. And since any partial claim about a systematic whole is only fully true when seen in its connections with all other truths, every finite judgment falls short of complete truth. Truth admits of degrees: the more comprehensive and coherent a system of judgments, the closer it approaches the complete truth that only the Absolute can fully instantiate. This is the coherence theory of truth that Bradley and I share — and it is the theory that best describes how scientific knowledge actually progresses: by integrating particular findings into an ever-more-comprehensive system.

iii. the state as moral organism

Moral organism — the state is not a contract between atomic individuals but a concrete universal in which individuals find their full realisation; individuality requires social constitutionThe practical consequences of the idealist logic are equally radical. Liberalism — the political philosophy of the opposition — conceives of individuals as atomistic, self-contained units who enter into social arrangements instrumentally. The state is a contract: a device for securing the pre-social interests of pre-social individuals. On the idealist view, this is self-contradictory in exactly the way the abstract universal is. An individual who existed in isolation, prior to and independently of social relations, would be not a rich individual but a bare abstraction: the individual minus everything that makes her specifically who she is. Language, moral concepts, self-consciousness itself — all are constituted through social relations. The individual is not prior to society but is constituted through her place within it. The state is not an instrument serving pre-given individual interests but a concrete universal in which individuals realise their nature. Attacking the state as an external constraint on pre-political individuals misunderstands the logic of individuality itself.

iv. the coherence theory of truth against correspondence

Coherence truth — the correspondence theory presupposes a comparison between thought and an independent reality, which is incoherent; truth is coherence; the completest possible coherent system just is the AbsoluteRussell and Moore will defend a correspondence theory of truth: a proposition is true if it corresponds to a fact. But this theory presupposes what it cannot deliver: a comparison between thought and a thought-independent reality. The very act of comparing thought with reality is itself an act of thought; we cannot step outside thought to verify the correspondence from a neutral vantage point. The coherence theory says instead: truth is what fits into the most comprehensive and consistent system of beliefs. This is not a merely epistemic criterion — it is a metaphysical one: the most coherent possible system just is reality. The perfectly coherent system is the Absolute. Every finite judgment that achieves coherence with other judgments achieves a degree of truth proportional to its comprehensiveness. This explains the progressive character of science and mathematics without appealing to a correspondence with mind-independent facts that we can never directly access.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The abstract universal is a fiction: genuine knowledge operates with concrete universals that are intelligible only as systematic wholes
1.The abstract universal achieves generality by omitting the specific relations that make each particular what it is. It is therefore not a genuine object of knowledge but an intellectual shorthand that discards the very content through which particulars are understood.
2.Genuine knowledge — of elements, sonatas, virtues, species — operates with concrete universals: organised wholes in which each part’s nature is constituted by its relations to the other parts and to the whole. The periodic table is a paradigm: each element is what it is in virtue of its systematic relations to all others.
3.The abstract-universal logic of Russell and Moore therefore misrepresents the structure of real knowledge by treating as fundamental what is merely a derivative convenience stripped of the systematic relations that make things intelligible.
∴The abstract universal is a philosophical fiction that misrepresents knowledge; real knowledge operates with concrete universals whose terms are intelligible only through their systematic relations; this supports the holistic, relational structure of idealist metaphysics.
Argument II — Every finite judgment implicitly predicates something of reality as a whole; truth is coherence with the Absolute
1.Every judgment is a claim about how reality is. Any partial claim about a systematic whole is only fully true when seen in its connections with all other truths. Finite judgments are therefore partial and fall short of complete truth.
2.Truth admits of degrees: the more comprehensive and coherent a system of judgments, the closer to complete truth it approaches. The perfectly coherent system is the Absolute.
3.This explains the progressive character of scientific knowledge without appeal to a correspondence with mind-independent facts: science progresses by integrating particular findings into a more comprehensive and coherent whole.
∴Every finite judgment aims at the Absolute and falls short; truth is coherence within a comprehensive system; the coherence theory best describes the progressive, integrative character of genuine knowledge.
Argument III — The individual is constituted through social relations; the state is a concrete universal, not an external constraint on pre-social atoms
1.Liberal individualism conceives of individuals as pre-social atoms who enter social arrangements instrumentally. But language, self-consciousness, and moral concepts are all constituted through social relations. An individual minus all social constitution would be an empty abstraction, not a rich individual.
2.The state is a concrete universal: a systematic whole in which individuals realise their nature rather than an external constraint on a pre-political self. To attack the state as an imposition on atomic individuals is to mistake abstraction for reality.
3.The idealist analysis of individuality-through-social-constitution mirrors the analysis of terms-through-relations: both show that the apparently self-subsistent atom is in fact constituted through and dependent upon its systematic relations.
∴Individuals are constituted through social relations; the state is a concrete universal in which individual nature is realised, not an external constraint; liberal atomism commits the same fallacy as the abstract universal in treating the derivative as foundational.
Argument IV — The correspondence theory of truth is incoherent; the coherence theory correctly identifies truth with systematic coherence within the Absolute
1.The correspondence theory requires a comparison between thought and a thought-independent reality. But the act of comparing is itself an act of thought. We cannot step outside thought to verify correspondence from a neutral vantage point; the comparison is always conducted within thought.
2.The coherence theory avoids this problem: truth is fit within the most comprehensive and consistent system. This is a criterion we can apply from within thought, and it is the criterion by which scientific and mathematical knowledge actually progresses.
3.The completest possible coherent system just is the Absolute: reality and the perfect system of truth coincide. The coherence theory is therefore not merely an epistemic criterion but a metaphysical identification of truth with being.
∴The correspondence theory is incoherent because it requires an impossible neutral vantage point outside thought; the coherence theory correctly identifies truth with systematic coherence; and the perfect system of coherent truth is the Absolute.

Chair,

I want to press the idealist case at the point where empiricism is most vulnerable: the question of what makes knowledge possible at all. Hume showed that experience, taken as a mere sequence of sensory impressions, cannot yield the categories — causation, substance, identity — that science requires. Kant showed that these categories must come from the mind. I go further: I argue that the knowing subject itself requires a spiritual principle that cannot be explained in naturalistic terms.

The transcendental argument for idealism.

Hume’s sceptical empiricism established that a mere sequence of sensory impressions cannot generate the synthetic connections — causal necessity, numerical identity, substance persistence — that our knowledge of nature requires. Kant agreed: these connections come from the spontaneity of the mind, not from what is passively received in sensation. But Kant left the unifying self — the transcendental unity of apperception — as a mere logical presupposition, not as something we can know.

Green’s extension: the unifying principle of knowledge must itself be a form of consciousness, and consciousness cannot be derived from or reduced to natural processes. Natural processes are themselves constituted by the categories of knowing consciousness; consciousness cannot simultaneously be the condition of the intelligibility of nature and a product of that nature.
i. the naturalist cannot account for the knower

Knower not natural — consciousness is a condition of nature's intelligibility; it cannot itself be a product of natural processes it conditions; naturalism is self-underminingThe natural scientist explains phenomena by reference to natural causes: physical processes, chemical reactions, biological mechanisms. But what is the knowing subject who performs this explanation? If the knower is herself a natural product — a brain state, a physiological process — then the same categories the knower uses to constitute the natural world must apply to her. She too is a thing among things, an event in the causal order. But here is the problem: the categories of the natural world — causation, substance, identity over time — are not found in the raw given of sensation; they are contributions of the knowing mind. A mind that is itself constituted by those same categories cannot be their source. Naturalism is self-undermining: it employs a knowing subject to explain nature but then tries to explain the knowing subject as a part of nature, thereby destroying the foundation of the very explanation it is trying to give.

ii. the spiritual principle and its implications

Eternal consciousness — the unifying principle of knowledge is eternal, not temporal; it presupposes and makes intelligible the world of nature; it is what idealists call the AbsoluteThe unifying principle of knowledge must be, as I argued in the Prolegomena to Ethics, an eternal consciousness — not a consciousness that came into being at a time and will pass away, but a principle that is the condition of the intelligibility of time itself. This eternal consciousness is not the individual finite minds of particular persons; it is the Absolute Mind that Green’s contemporaries, Bradley especially, develop in metaphysical detail. What is important for ethics and politics, as well as for pure metaphysics, is this: the individual human mind is not a natural product but a partial expression of this eternal consciousness. Human beings participate in the divine intelligence; their capacity for self-conscious rational thought is not a product of animal evolution but an expression of what lies at the ground of reality.

iii. positive freedom and the common good

Positive freedom — genuine freedom is the full actualisation of rational self; negative freedom (mere absence of constraint) is freedom to be an animal; the common good is the condition of true self-realisationThe practical implications for ethics and politics follow directly from the metaphysics. If the individual is not a bare atom of desire but a partial expression of eternal consciousness, then her true good is not the satisfaction of immediate desires but the full realisation of her rational and social nature — the development of all her capacities in harmony with the capacities of others. This is what I call positive freedom: not merely the absence of external constraint (which the liberal tradition calls freedom) but the power of the fully realised self. A person who is formally free from external constraint but is ignorant, enslaved to appetite, and unable to develop her rational capacities is not genuinely free. Genuine freedom requires the social conditions — education, health, economic security — that make full self-realisation possible. This is the idealist foundation of what would later become the welfare state.

iv. against Hume’s scepticism: the unity of experience requires a unifier

Unity argument — experience is unified; its unity cannot come from the impressions themselves (Hume: each impression is atomic); unity must come from a unifying principle that is not itself one of the impressionsHume showed that impressions are atomic and discrete: each impression is what it is and has no necessary connection with any other. But our experience is not a mere sequence of unconnected atoms; it is a unified, connected whole. I can compare my current experience with past experiences, recognise patterns across time, perceive causal connections. This unity cannot come from the impressions themselves, since they are discrete and without necessary connection. It must come from a principle of unity that is not itself one of the impressions — a spontaneous, unifying activity of consciousness. Hume could not account for this because he restricted his materials to impressions alone. Once we acknowledge the unifying activity of consciousness as a philosophical datum, we are on the path to idealism: the world of objects is not given independently of consciousness but is constituted by consciousness’s unifying activity.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Naturalism is self-undermining: consciousness conditions nature’s intelligibility and cannot itself be a natural product
1.The categories of natural explanation — causation, substance, identity — are not found in raw sensation but contributed by the knowing mind. The knowing subject is the condition of the intelligibility of nature.
2.Naturalism tries to explain the knowing subject as a natural product. But a natural product is itself constituted by the categories the knowing subject contributes. The knower cannot be simultaneously the condition of nature’s intelligibility and a product of that nature.
3.Naturalism destroys the foundation of its own explanation: it cannot both use the knowing subject to explain nature and explain the knowing subject as part of nature. The self-undermining structure of naturalism forces us to a non-naturalistic account of consciousness.
∴Naturalism is self-undermining: it employs a knowing subject whose nature it cannot explain on its own terms; the knowing subject must be understood as a spiritual principle that is the condition of nature’s intelligibility, not a product of it.
Argument II — The unifying principle of knowledge is an eternal consciousness that participates in the Absolute
1.The unifying principle of knowledge must be eternal: it cannot be a temporal thing that came into being at a time and will cease, since it is the condition of the intelligibility of time itself.
2.Individual finite minds participate in this eternal consciousness: their capacity for self-conscious rational thought is not a product of animal evolution but an expression of the Absolute. Individual human minds are partial realisations of the divine intelligence.
3.This has direct implications for ethics: human beings are not mere animals to be governed by desire but partial expressions of eternal consciousness with a vocation for full rational self-realisation.
∴The unifying principle of knowledge is an eternal consciousness; finite minds participate in it; this gives human beings a dignity and vocation that naturalism cannot account for and that grounds idealist ethics and politics.
Argument III — Positive freedom is the full actualisation of rational nature; negative freedom is merely animal freedom
1.If the true self is rational and social, then the good for each individual is the full realisation of her rational and social capacities in harmony with others. The mere absence of external constraint does not guarantee this realisation.
2.Positive freedom — the power of the fully realised self — requires the social conditions (education, health, economic security) that make full development possible. These are legitimate objects of political organisation.
3.Negative freedom — mere absence of external constraint — is freedom to be an animal: to follow appetite without rational self-direction. It is the appropriate freedom for a being with no social or rational vocation; it is insufficient for beings who participate in eternal consciousness.
∴Genuine freedom is positive: the power of the fully realised rational self; the state has a legitimate role in providing the conditions for this realisation; the liberal identification of freedom with the absence of constraint misunderstands what human beings are.
Argument IV — The unity of experience requires a unifying principle not found among the impressions: Hume’s atomism self-destructs
1.Hume: each impression is atomic and discrete; there is no necessary connection between impressions. But experience is unified: we compare, recognise patterns, and perceive connections across time. This unity cannot come from the discrete impressions themselves.
2.The unity must come from a spontaneous, unifying activity of consciousness that is not itself an impression. Acknowledging this is the first step toward idealism: consciousness is not a passive receiver but an active constituter of the experienced world.
3.Hume’s own philosophy presupposes this unity when it compares impressions, identifies relations between them, and draws conclusions — activities that require the very unifying principle his atomism denies.
∴Hume’s impressionist atomism is self-refuting: it presupposes a unifying consciousness it denies; acknowledging this active, unifying consciousness is the first step toward idealism, as Kant recognised and Green extended into a full idealist metaphysics.

Chair,

My argument is the most technically precise in the idealist armoury, and the one that has most exercised the analytic philosophers who oppose us. I argue that time is unreal — that the temporal structure of the world as we ordinarily experience it cannot be a feature of ultimate reality. This conclusion, which sounds paradoxical, follows from a careful examination of what our concept of time actually requires.

The A-series and the B-series.

We can order events in time in two distinct ways. First, we can order them by the relations of being earlier than, simultaneous with, and later than. This gives us what I call the B-series: a fixed, permanent ordering of events. The Battle of Hastings is always earlier than the French Revolution, regardless of when we make the claim.

Second, we can characterise events as past, present, or future. This gives us what I call the A-series. This second ordering is essential to our experience of time as flowing, as passing, as having a direction. The B-series alone gives us a static map; the A-series gives us the dynamic character of time that makes the difference between a living past and a dead one.

My argument: time requires the A-series (without it, there is no genuine change); but the A-series is self-contradictory. Therefore time is unreal.
i. why the B-series alone cannot constitute time

B-series insufficient — the B-series is a permanent, static ordering; it cannot by itself yield genuine change or the temporal flow that distinguishes real time from mere successionThe B-series ordering of earlier-than, simultaneous-with, and later-than is a permanent relational structure. The Battle of Hastings is always earlier than Waterloo: this is an unchanging fact about the world. But time, as we ordinarily experience it, is not a static map. It flows: the Battle of Hastings recedes ever further into the past; what is now future becomes present and then past. This passage, this flow, is essential to what we mean by time. The B-series, precisely because it is a permanent, unchanging relational structure, cannot by itself account for this passage. A world with only B-series facts is a “block universe” in which all events are equally real at their respective positions; nothing is past or present or future; nothing changes. This is not time as we experience and as we mean it when we use temporal language. The A-series is indispensable.

ii. the A-series generates a vicious contradiction

A-series contradiction — every event must be past, present, and future; these are mutually exclusive; the attempt to resolve the contradiction by appeal to times generates a regressBut the A-series is self-contradictory. Every event must be past, present, and future — for it will have all three properties at different times. Yet past, present, and future are mutually exclusive: no event can be both past and present, or present and future, or past and future simultaneously. The natural response is to say: the event is present at one time, past at another, future at yet another. But this response reintroduces the A-series at a higher level: the event is “present” at a moment which is itself present, past, or future. We need a second A-series to characterise the moments in the first A-series. But the second A-series generates the same contradiction, requiring a third — and so on without end. The regress is vicious: at every level we need the A-series to make sense of time, and at every level it contradicts itself. We can never get a coherent account of time.

iii. the unreality of time and what follows

Unreality of time — time is appearance; reality is timeless; the Absolute is not temporal; our sense of temporal flow is an expression of our limited, finite perspectiveThe conclusion is that time is unreal. This does not mean that there is no ordering of events, no earlier-later structure, no change in any sense whatsoever. The B-series relationships may represent something real. What is unreal is time as it is normally conceived: as flowing, as having a present moment, as making a genuine distinction between the no-longer and the not-yet. This temporal flow is a feature of how finite minds experience the world from their limited perspective — it is not a feature of ultimate reality. Ultimate reality is the Absolute, which is not in time and does not flow. I want to begin by saying that it is very difficult to know what the idealists are asserting. They speak of the Absolute, of appearances that are not mere illusions but are “real as appearances,” of reality that is both One and yet somehow contains all the finite diversity we experience. I have spent a great deal of time with these texts, and I confess that I am not certain I always understand them. But I am quite certain that their central premise — that all of reality is in some sense mind-dependent — rests on a confusion that I can identify and correct.

The idealist’s central claim and its logical source.

Every form of idealism, from Berkeley’s subjective idealism to Bradley’s absolute idealism, relies on some version of the principle esse est percipi: to be is to be perceived (or more broadly: to be is to be experienced). This principle, if true, would mean that nothing exists independently of consciousness. Tables, trees, planets — all are mind-dependent entities.

The principle is asserted as necessary: not merely as a contingent fact about how things happen to be, but as a truth that must hold given what existence and experience are. The idealist says: you cannot even conceive of a thing existing unperceived, because the very act of conceiving it is itself a perception, an exercise of consciousness. The thing in your conception is always a thing-as-conceived.

This argument, which goes back to Berkeley, is the engine of all idealism. My claim: it rests on a confusion.
i. the act-object distinction

Act-object distinction — consciousness is transparent; its objects are distinct from it; to be aware of blue is not for blue to be part of the awareness; idealism conflates act and objectWhen I am aware of blue, there are two things: my awareness (the act) and the blue I am aware of (the object). The idealist conflates these two things. She says: you cannot separate the blue from the awareness of blue, because whenever you try to examine the blue, you find it always as an object of awareness. But this confuses the transparency of consciousness with the absence of its object. Consciousness is transparent: when I look at blue, I seem to see straight through the awareness to the blue itself. The awareness does not intrude as a third thing between me and the blue; it is simply the relation by which I am in contact with the blue. But this transparency is precisely what shows that the blue is distinct from the awareness: the awareness “vanishes” into directness at the blue, and what remains is the blue itself, which is not part of the awareness but its independent object. The idealist mistakes the inseparability of act and object-in-awareness for the identity of act and object.

ii. the yellow sensation argument

Yellow sensation — the sensation of yellow and the sensation of blue share the same act of consciousness; what differs is the object; therefore the object is distinct from the actConsider the sensation of yellow and the sensation of blue. These are two different sensations. What is common to both is something I call “consciousness” or “awareness” — the act of being aware. What differs is the object: yellow in one case, blue in the other. If the sensation “just were” the yellow — if there were no distinction between the act of awareness and the yellow object — then we could not distinguish between the sensation of yellow and yellow itself. But we clearly can: yellow is a colour that exists in the world, while the sensation of yellow is my experiencing of it. This distinction, which common sense takes for granted, shows that the act of consciousness and its object are not identical. The idealist cannot collapse them without making it impossible to distinguish between a quality and the experience of a quality.

But I want to press a deeper point that I developed in “A Defence of Common Sense.” Even if I cannot identify the precise flaw in McTaggart’s argument for the unreality of time, I know more certainly that time is real than I know any premise of his argument to be true. The certainty with which I know that there are material objects, that the earth has existed for a long time, that other people have minds, that I have had experiences in the past — this certainty is greater than the certainty of any abstract philosophical argument. When a philosopher argues to the conclusion that space is unreal or that matter does not exist, the correct response is not to revise one’s belief in space and matter. It is to look for the flaw in the argument. Common sense is not infallible; but it provides the criterion against which abstract philosophical arguments are tested, not the other way round.

iv. the naturalistic fallacy and the independence of goodness

Open question argument — goodness is a simple, non-natural property; it cannot be defined in terms of natural properties (including idealist ones like self-realisation); the idealist conflation of good with self-realisation commits the naturalistic fallacyIn ethics, the idealists commit what I call the naturalistic fallacy. Whatever natural or metaphysical definition we offer — self-realisation, rational completion, harmony with the Absolute — we can always coherently ask: “But is it good?” This open question would be closed if the definition were correct: we could not coherently ask “Is what is good, good?” But we can coherently ask “Is self-realisation good?” — and the fact that the question makes sense shows that “good” is not defined by “self-realisation.” The idealist ethics rests on a definition of goodness that is both theoretically confused and substantively wrong.

I was myself a Hegelian — briefly, in the early 1890s — and I know what it is to feel the seductive force of the idealist system. There is something marvellous in the ambition to derive the nature of all reality from a few logical principles. But I came to see that the system rests on a logical error: the confusion between the logical and the ontological, and specifically the doctrine of internal relations, which makes it impossible to do mathematics or science at all.

The doctrine of internal relations and why it destroys knowledge.

Bradley’s idealism rests on the claim that all relations are “internal” to their terms: that A cannot be what it is without standing in the relations it in fact stands in. To know any one thing, you must know all its relations; to know all its relations, you must know all the things it is related to; and since everything is related to everything else, to know any one thing completely you must know everything.

This is what I called “logical holism” — and it is a catastrophe for knowledge. If knowing A requires knowing B, C, D, and everything else in the universe, no one can know anything. No science is possible, no mathematics is possible, no inference from premiss to conclusion is possible, because every such inference would require prior knowledge of everything. The doctrine of internal relations is not a deep metaphysical insight but a paralyser of thought.
i. external relations are coherent and scientifically necessary

External relations — a relation is external if its terms can be specified independently of it; arithmetic and physics require such relations; Bradley's argument against them fails to show incoherenceI maintain that relations can be external: that A can be related to B without that relation being constitutive of what A or B is. Two is greater than one: this is a relation between two numbers. But two does not cease to be two if we consider it in isolation; its nature as the successor of one does not require the simultaneous presence of one for two to exist. The number two can be specified, understood, and reasoned about independently of all its numerical relationships — and then those relationships can be investigated separately. Bradley’s argument that this generates a vicious regress fails to show that external relations are incoherent; it shows only that there must always be a relation between a relation and its terms, which is a trivially true consequence of the logical structure of relational propositions and not a vicious regress at all.

ii. logical atomism and the structure of facts

Logical atomism — the world consists of atomic facts; atomic propositions correspond to them; complex propositions are truth-functions of atomic ones; no holism requiredThe correct metaphysics — which I developed as “logical atomism” — holds that the world consists of logically independent atomic facts: facts of the form “a has property F” or “a stands in relation R to b.” These facts are logically independent: the truth of any atomic proposition does not entail the truth or falsity of any other atomic proposition. Complex propositions are truth-functions of atomic ones. This structure makes knowledge possible: I can know individual atomic facts without knowing everything, and I can infer complex truths from atomic ones without the whole universe needing to be simultaneously apprehended. Logic and mathematics, which Bradley’s doctrine makes impossible, are the clearest evidence that the world has this atomistic, externally-relational structure.

iii. mathematical logic against the coherence theory

Mathematical logic — logic and mathematics demonstrate truths by formal derivation from axioms; this requires external, formal relations; Bosanquet's coherence theory cannot account for mathematical necessityBosanquet defends a coherence theory of truth: a proposition is true if it coheres with the most comprehensive and consistent system. But this theory cannot account for the necessity of mathematical truths. “2 + 2 = 4” is not merely coherent with a system; it is necessarily true in virtue of the formal relations between the concepts involved. Mathematical proof is a derivation from axioms by formal rules, not a progressive integration into a larger whole. If truth were coherence, it would be possible to imagine a comprehensive and consistent system in which “2 + 2 = 5” — and we cannot. Mathematical necessity is a knockout blow against the coherence theory and, with it, against the idealist metaphysics that the coherence theory supports.

iv. against the unreality of time: B-series is sufficient

Physics requires only the B-series: the earlier-later ordering of events is all that is needed to describe the causal and temporal structure of the physical world. The A-series properties — past, present, future — are indexical; they shift depending on when the description is made and who is making it. This is a feature of perspective, not of time itself. McTaggart’s argument proves only that time cannot be described from a standpoint outside all perspective, which is exactly what we should expect. It does not prove that time is unreal.

Moore has shown that esse est percipi is false. Russell has shown that the doctrine of internal relations is logically confused. I want to press a more radical point: it is not that the idealists are wrong about the metaphysical facts, but that their propositions are not the kind of thing that can be wrong — or right — at all. The statements of metaphysics are not false propositions but pseudo-propositions: strings of words that have the grammatical form of factual claims but lack the logical character of any claim at all.

The verification principle.

A proposition is factually meaningful if and only if it is either: (a) analytically true or false — true or false in virtue of the meaning of its terms alone; or (b) empirically verifiable in principle — such that some possible observation or experiment would be relevant to its truth or falsehood.

Statements that satisfy neither condition are not false but meaningless: they make no genuine claim about the world and have no cognitive content, however impressive their grammatical construction may be.
i. the idealist’s propositions are unverifiable and non-analytic

Meaninglessness diagnosis — "The Absolute is a unified sentient experience" is neither analytically true nor empirically verifiable; no observation could count for or against it; it is a pseudo-propositionTake the idealist’s central claim: “Reality is a unified Absolute Experience that transcends all finite appearances.” Is this analytically true — true in virtue of what the terms mean? No: the denial of it (“reality is not a unified Absolute Experience”) is not a logical contradiction. Is it empirically verifiable? What observation, what experiment, what empirical inquiry could confirm or disconfirm it? The idealist says the Absolute transcends all finite experience; it is precisely beyond anything we could observe or measure. No possible observation is relevant to its truth. The proposition therefore fails both tests. It is not false; it is meaningless. This is not to say it lacks emotional or psychological significance; it may be the expression of a certain profound feeling about the unity of things. But as a claim about what there is — as a metaphysical proposition — it is without content.

ii. the history of metaphysics as eloquent nonsense

Historical diagnosis — metaphysics is the systematic attempt to transcend the limits of possible experience; its propositions have always been unverifiable; it is not bad science but not science at allThe verification principle does not merely condemn Bradley and Bosanquet. It condemns the entire history of metaphysics: Plato’s Forms, Aristotle’s substance, Kant’s thing-in-itself, Hegel’s Absolute Spirit, and Bradley’s Absolute Experience are all pseudo-propositions. This is not an accident but a structural feature of the metaphysical enterprise. Metaphysics defines itself as the inquiry into what lies beyond possible experience — beyond what science and ordinary observation can reach. But a proposition about what lies beyond all possible experience is a proposition no possible observation can bear on. Metaphysics is not bad science; it is not science at all. It is the attempt to reach beyond the limits of meaningful discourse while maintaining the grammatical appearance of factual assertion.

iii. the verification principle and its application to theology and ethics

Ethics and theology equally affected — moral statements like "torture is wrong" are not factual descriptions but expressions of emotion plus prescriptions; theological statements are equally meaningless; the principle is uniformly appliedThe verification principle applies beyond metaphysics. Theological claims — “God exists,” “the soul is immortal” — are neither analytic nor empirically verifiable and are therefore meaningless. Ethical claims — “torture is wrong,” “kindness is good” — are not descriptions of non-natural properties (as Moore thought) but expressions of emotion combined with prescriptions: they say “I disapprove of torture; do not torture” rather than asserting a fact. Green’s idealist ethics, which derives moral imperatives from the metaphysics of self-realisation, is doubly confused: the metaphysical base is meaningless, and even if it were not, moral claims are not descriptions of facts but expressions of attitudes. The entire idealist programme — from the Absolute in metaphysics to positive freedom in politics to self-realisation in ethics — is built on propositions that have no cognitive content.

iv. what legitimate philosophy looks like

Legitimate philosophy — philosophy’s task is logical analysis of scientific and ordinary language; it clarifies meaning; it does not construct systems of transcendent propositions; it is continuous with science, not its rivalThe verification principle does not eliminate philosophy; it redefines it. Philosophy’s legitimate task is the logical analysis of the propositions of science and ordinary language. It clarifies what these propositions mean, how they relate to each other, and what their implications are. It does not construct systems of propositions that transcend all possible experience. It is not a rival to science, a superior source of knowledge about what the world contains; it is a tool for clarifying the claims that science and common sense make. This is a humbler but more honest role than the one the idealists claimed for philosophy. And it is enormously productive: the logical analyses of Russell, Frege, and Wittgenstein have clarified the foundations of mathematics, the structure of scientific reasoning, and the nature of meaning in ways that no amount of system-building about the Absolute ever could.

1.Philosophy’s legitimate task is the logical analysis of the propositions of science and ordinary language: clarifying their meaning, their relations, and their implications. This is productive and honest work.
2.Philosophy is not a rival to science: it does not construct propositions about what there is that go beyond science. It is continuous with scientific inquiry and serves it by clarifying its foundations.
3.The logical analyses of Russell, Frege, and Wittgenstein — of mathematics, language, and inference — have been more productive than centuries of idealist system-building precisely because they remain within the limits of meaningful discourse.
∴Legitimate philosophy is logical analysis continuous with science; metaphysical system-building is pseudo-propositional; the analytic programme has been more productive than idealism precisely because it respects the limits of meaningful discourse.

Chair,

I find myself in the odd position of agreeing with both sides and with neither. Ayer thinks I have condemned metaphysics as meaningless. The idealists think I have condemned empiricism as superficial. In the Tractatus I said something more peculiar than either: that the things which are most important — ethics, aesthetics, the logical form of the world, the mystical — cannot be said at all. They can only be shown. And what both the idealists and the analysts try to say — try to put into words — is precisely what cannot be said but only shown.

The picture theory of language and its limits.

Language pictures the world. A proposition is a picture of a fact: it shares logical form with the fact it depicts, in the way a map shares geometrical form with the territory it represents. For a proposition to picture a fact, it must have the same logical multiplicity: the same number of distinguishable elements arranged in the same logical structure.

From this follows the limit of language: language can only picture facts — states of affairs in the world. It cannot picture its own logical form, because to picture the logical form of language, a further language would be needed that shared that form, and so on. The logical form of language — what makes it possible for language to picture the world at all — shows itself in the structure of propositions but cannot be described in any proposition.
i. what can be said and what can only be shown

Said vs. shown — factual propositions say how things are; logical form shows itself in the structure of propositions; the idealists try to say what can only be shown and thereby produce nonsenseThe idealists are trying to say something about the structure of reality — that it is unified, relational, experiential in a cosmic sense. But the structure of reality is not a fact that can be pictured by a proposition. It is what makes picturing possible at all. When Bradley says “Reality is a unified Absolute Experience,” he is trying to describe what it would take for any description to be possible. But the conditions of description cannot themselves be described — they can only be shown in the structure of every description. The idealists’ propositions are not false; they are nonsense, as Ayer says. But they are a peculiar kind of nonsense: they try to say what genuine understanding of the world recognises as unsayable, and in this they show something true even as they fail to say it.

ii. the world as the totality of facts

World as facts — "The world is the totality of facts, not of things" is itself at the limit of sense; it tries to say something about logical form; it shows rather than states; Russell's atomism and Bradley's monism both try to do what cannot be doneI wrote in the Tractatus that “The world is the totality of facts, not of things.” This looks like a substantive metaphysical claim — like a rejection of Russell’s thing-based ontology, or a partial concession to Bradley’s relational view. But I have come to think it is itself at the limit of sense: it tries to say something about the logical form of the world, which cannot be said but only shown. Both Russell’s logical atomism — which asserts the logical independence of atomic facts — and Bradley’s monism — which asserts the logical interdependence of everything — are attempts to describe the logical form of reality. But the logical form of reality cannot be described: it shows itself in the structure of every proposition. Both sides of this debate are engaged in the same impossible enterprise, and their mutual refutations each have the form of the very nonsense they are trying to refute.

iii. whereof one cannot speak

Silence — the right conclusion of both sides' arguments is silence about the things they most care about; ethics, aesthetics, the mystical are real but unsayable; the Tractatus ends by pointing beyond languageThe most honest response to both the idealist system and the analytic reaction against it is the conclusion of the Tractatus: whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent. This is not a nihilistic conclusion. I did not say that what cannot be said is unimportant or unreal. I said it cannot be said. The things the idealists most care about — the unity of the world, the Absolute, the good life, the mystical sense of the world as a limited whole — are not nothing. They are, perhaps, the most important things. But they cannot be captured in propositions. They show themselves in the texture of a life well-lived, in the experience of ethics and aesthetics, in the sense — which cannot be expressed but which we all sometimes have — that the world is not just the sum of facts but is somehow, as a whole, meaningful. The idealist reaches for this with elaborate metaphysical machinery; I can only point to it in silence.

iv. the limits of Ayer’s positivism

Against Ayer — Ayer claims the unsayable is meaningless; Wittgenstein claims it shows itself; the mystical and ethical are not nothing; the positivist elimination of metaphysics throws out something real with the nonsenseAyer reads the Tractatus as support for logical positivism: metaphysical propositions are meaningless and should be eliminated. But this reading misses something. I did not say the unsayable is worthless or that nothing is lost when we fall silent about it. I said it cannot be said — not that it is nothing. The mystical, the ethical, the beautiful: these are not less than nothing; they are more than can be said. Ayer’s positivism throws out something real along with the nonsense. The idealists were right that there is something beyond the reach of empirical science and logical analysis — something that any account of the world adequate to human experience must somehow acknowledge. They were wrong to think they could say it. The correct response is not to say it better but to recognise, finally, the limit of language: to understand that the most important things can only be shown, and to stand in appropriate silence before what cannot be said.

Argument I — Said vs. shown: the idealists try to say what can only be shown; logical form cannot be described, only displayed

2.The idealists try to describe the structure of reality — its unity, its experiential character, its relational constitution. But the structure of reality is logical form: what makes any description possible. It cannot itself be described; it shows itself in every description.
3.The idealists’ propositions are therefore nonsense — they try to say what can only be shown. But they are a peculiar nonsense: in trying to say the unsayable, they gesture toward something genuinely real that cannot be captured in any proposition.
∴The idealists produce nonsense because they try to say what can only be shown; logical form cannot be described; but their nonsense is not empty — it gestures toward a real limit of language that both idealists and analysts need to acknowledge.

2.Russell’s claim that atomic facts are logically independent, and Bradley’s claim that all things are internally related, are both attempts to describe the logical form of reality. Both are therefore at the same limit.
3.Their mutual refutations are therefore each in the same impossible position: both sides are engaged in the attempt to say what cannot be said, and their objections to each other have the form of the same nonsense they are trying to refute.
∴Both logical atomism and absolute idealism try to describe logical form; both are at the limit of sense; their debate is not a genuine disagreement about facts but a mutual attempt to say what can only be shown.
