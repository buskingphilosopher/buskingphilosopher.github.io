Mr Speaker,

The question before this House is whether the mind and its contents — sensations, thoughts, desires, the felt quality of experience — are entirely physical. I shall argue that they are, and that the correct account is the simplest one available: mental states are identical to brain states. They are the very same thing described from two different angles, just as lightning is identical to an electrical discharge and heat is identical to mean molecular kinetic energy.

This is the Type Identity Theory: for each mental state type, there is a corresponding physical brain state type with which it is identical. When I feel a pain, what is occurring is a neural event of some specific kind — C-fibre firing is the traditional placeholder, though the real identity awaits empirical neuroscience. The mental and the physical are not correlated, not causally related as distinct things; they are literally one and the same process.

Identity Theory: the core claim.

Mental state M = Brain state B. Not “M is correlated with B,” not “M supervenes on B,” not “M is realised by B.” Identity, plain and simple.

This identity is contingent, not necessary — it is discovered empirically, as all identities between natural kinds are. We did not know a priori that water = H₂O or that genes = DNA sequences. We discovered these identities by scientific investigation. Similarly, the identity of mental states with brain states is an empirical discovery, not a conceptual truth. This makes the identity robust and interesting, not suspicious.
i. the argument from parsimony

Parsimony — mental states piggyback on brain states; Occam's Razor; dualism doubles entities without explanatory gainWhy should we not accept identity theory? The dualist says: because sensations have properties that no brain state could have — they are intrinsically qualitative, subjective, phenomenal. But notice what the dualist is doing. They are accepting all of the physical causal story about the brain, accepting that the brain does everything causally relevant to behaviour, and then adding on top of this a further layer of non-physical sensation that rides piggyback on the physical without doing any additional causal work. This is ontological extravagance of the worst kind. Occam’s Razor requires us to identify the sensation with the brain state rather than postulate it as a mysterious non-physical rider. Topic-neutral analysis — sensation reports are topic-neutral; compatible with physicalism; no commitment to non-physical qualiaWhen I report that I am having a red sensation, the report is “topic-neutral”: it commits me only to there being something going on in me that is caused by red objects and causes certain behaviours. It does not commit me to any specific claim about the intrinsic nature of that something. The physicalist says that something is a brain state, and the topic-neutral analysis of sensation reports is perfectly compatible with this identification.

ii. science as the guide to ontology

Science guides ontology — just as heat = molecular motion, pain = neural state; science has always vindicated physicalism about previously mysterious propertiesThe history of science is a history of identifying apparently non-physical phenomena with physical ones. Vital force was once thought necessary to explain life; it was replaced by biochemistry. Heat was once thought to be a subtle fluid called caloric; it was identified with molecular motion. Lightning was once thought to be supernatural; it is now identified with electrical discharge. In each case, what seemed mysterious and irreducible to physics turned out to be physical after all. Why should consciousness be different? The dualist who insists on the irreducibility of consciousness is making the same bet as the vitalist who insisted on the irreducibility of life — and the vitalist lost.

iii. against functionalism: types, not tokens

Against functionalism — type identity is stronger; functional roles are themselves implemented physically; Putnam's multiple realisability overstatedPutnam will argue against me that pain is defined by its functional role, not its physical realisation, and that this allows for multiple realisability. I accept that token identity is uncontroversial: each particular pain event is a particular brain event. My claim is stronger: the mental types, the kinds of mental state, are identical to physical types. Multiple realisability is empirically overstated: in practice, the same cognitive functions tend to be realised in the same physical substrates across members of the same species. And even where multiple realisability holds, the functional role is itself physically implemented; there is no level at which we escape the physical.

Mr Speaker,

I am a physicalist, but I am a more liberal one than Smart. The identity theory he defends is a kind of chauvinism: it ties mental states to specific physical substrates in a way that makes it impossible for any creature with a different physical make-up to share our mental states. This is not just philosophically parochial; it is almost certainly empirically false. And more importantly, it mislocates what matters about mental states.

My thesis is functionalism: mental states are defined by their functional roles — their causal relations to sensory inputs, behavioural outputs, and other mental states — not by the physical stuff that realises those roles. Pain is whatever state plays the pain-role: being caused by tissue damage, causing avoidance behaviour, causing attention to the site of damage, interacting with beliefs and desires in the appropriate ways. If neurons play that role in us, neurons implement pain for us. If silicon plays it in a Martian, silicon implements pain for the Martian. The functional organisation is what matters; the substrate is contingent.

Multiple Realisability: the decisive argument against type identity.

Consider pain. Identity theory says pain = C-fibre firing (or some specific neural type). But consider an octopus: its nervous system is radically different from a human’s. If an octopus can be in pain — and there is every reason to think it can — then pain cannot be identical to C-fibre firing, because octopuses have no C-fibres.

More radically: consider a silicon-based robot with a functional organisation identical to ours. If functionalism is correct, such a robot would be in pain when its internal state plays the pain-role. Identity theory would deny this. Functionalism is the more principled and more inclusive account.
i. multiple realisability demolishes type identity

Multiple realisability — pain can be realised in different physical substrates; identity theory is too narrow; functionalism is substrate-independentThe multiple realisability argument is decisive against Smart’s type identity theory. If pain = C-fibre firing (as identity theory requires), then any creature without C-fibres cannot be in pain. But this is absurd: octopuses, Martians, and silicon robots may have experiences that are functionally identical to pain without sharing our neural substrate. The mental is multiply realisable: the same mental state type can be realised by different physical state types. This means the mental type cannot be identical to any specific physical type. What mental types have in common across their different physical realisations is their functional role, not their physical constitution. Functionalism captures exactly this.

ii. the vindication of special sciences

Special sciences — psychology is autonomous; mental kinds cut nature at its joints; reduction to physics loses the relevant generalisationsFunctionalism vindicates the autonomy of psychology and the special sciences. The generalisation “pain causes avoidance behaviour” is a genuine scientific law; it holds across all physical realisers of pain, regardless of their physical constitution. If we reduce psychology to physics — replacing “pain” with “C-fibre firing” — we lose this generalisation, because it no longer holds for octopuses and Martians. Mental kinds cut nature at its joints in a way that physical kinds do not capture. The special sciences of psychology, economics, and ecology are not merely approximate summaries of physics; they are genuine descriptions of real patterns in the world that supervene on but are not reducible to physics.

iii. against eliminativism: functional organisation is real

Against eliminativism — functional organisation is genuinely real; beliefs and desires are real functional states; Churchland eliminates too muchChurchland will tell us that beliefs and desires are fictional entities of a discredited folk theory, to be replaced by neural descriptions. But this goes too far. Functional states are real: the pattern of causal organisation that constitutes a belief is a genuine feature of the world, not a fiction. The fact that we do not yet have a complete neuroscientific reduction of belief does not mean belief is unreal; it means our science is incomplete. Eliminativism conflates the inadequacy of folk psychological explanation with the non-existence of the psychological entities. We should reduce psychology to neuroscience, not eliminate it.

iv. against epiphenomenalism and property dualism

Against property dualism — qualia are functional states; no explanatory gap if we properly understand functional role; Chalmers's zombie thought experiment is confusedChalmers will argue that functional descriptions leave out the “what it is like” of experience — the qualitative feel, the qualia. But qualia just are functional states: to have a quale is to be in a state with a certain functional role in perception, attention, and report. There is no additional phenomenal property over and above the functional organisation. The zombie thought experiment — imagining a being functionally identical to us but lacking experience — is not coherent, because experience just is a certain kind of functional organisation. A perfect functional duplicate would, by that very duplication, have the same experiences.

Both Smart and Putnam assume that folk psychology — the common-sense framework of beliefs, desires, intentions, and felt experiences — carves the mind at its real joints. Smart wants to reduce it to brain states; Putnam wants to vindicate it as a functional-level description. I want to argue that both are wrong, because folk psychology is a radically false theory that will be replaced, not reduced, by mature neuroscience.

This is eliminative materialism. The elimination is not scepticism; it is the optimistic prediction that a better science will replace a worse one. When we eliminated phlogiston from chemistry, we did not become sceptical about combustion; we got a better account of it. When we eliminate beliefs and desires from psychology, we will not become sceptical about cognition; we will get a vastly superior neuroscientific account of it.

“The folk psychology of the Greeks is essentially the folk psychology we use today. And it has been equally stagnant. Our own [scientific] progress has been nil.” — Churchland, 1981
i. folk psychology is a stagnant research programme

Folk psychology stagnant — no progress in 2500 years; bad scientific theories get eliminated; folk psychology should tooConsider the track record of folk psychology. We have been using the same framework of beliefs, desires, and intentions to explain human behaviour for at least two and a half millennia, since Aristotle. In that time, our scientific understanding of the physical world has advanced immeasurably; our folk psychological understanding of the mind has advanced not at all. We still explain behaviour by attributing beliefs and desires in exactly the way Greeks did. This stagnation is a sign of a bad theory. Good theories generate progress; they yield novel predictions, they integrate with adjacent sciences, they open new avenues of investigation. Folk psychology has done none of these things. Its stagnation is evidence that it is not tracking the real structure of cognition.

ii. the failure of folk psychology to integrate with science

No integration with neuroscience — folk psychology cannot be mapped onto neural architecture; propositional attitudes have no neural correlatesA successful theory should integrate with its neighbours. Chemistry integrates with atomic physics; genetics integrates with molecular biology; evolutionary theory integrates with population genetics. Folk psychology has conspicuously failed to integrate with neuroscience. We have no idea how to map beliefs and desires onto neural states. There is no neural analogue of a sentence-like propositional attitude stored in a discrete location. The brain does not seem to store representations in anything like the format that folk psychology implies. This failure of integration is strong evidence that the folk psychological framework does not correspond to the actual architecture of the mind.

iii. against functionalism: functional roles presuppose folk psychology

Against functionalism — functional roles defined in folk-psychological terms; if folk psychology is false, so are its functional characterisationsPutnam’s functionalism tries to save folk psychology by identifying mental states with functional roles rather than physical substrates. But the functional roles are defined in folk psychological terms: pain is defined as what is caused by tissue damage and causes avoidance behaviour — where “avoidance behaviour” is itself a folk category. If folk psychology is radically false, then the functional roles it defines are artefacts of a false theory, not carve-outs at nature’s joints. Functionalism inherits folk psychology’s failures; it does not escape them by moving to a more abstract level of description.

iv. the promise of mature neuroscience

Neuroscience will replace folk psychology — better explanatory vocabulary; vector activation spaces; not scepticism but scientific progressThe eliminativist prediction is optimistic, not pessimistic. Mature neuroscience will give us concepts that are vastly more powerful than beliefs and desires: concepts that describe the actual computational and representational architecture of the brain — activation patterns across neural networks, vector representations in high-dimensional spaces, predictive coding hierarchies. These concepts will explain everything that folk psychology explained, and much that it could not — the cognitive distortions of mental illness, the architecture of memory, the mechanisms of attention — in far greater depth and with far greater predictive accuracy. The hard problem of consciousness — the dualist’s prize exhibit — is itself a folk psychological pseudo-problem that will dissolve once we have the right neuroscientific vocabulary.

I want to begin at the root. The entire dispute between my colleagues on the physicalist side and our opponents on the dualist side has a common presupposition that I wish to challenge: both sides assume that mental concepts refer to inner states or processes — the question is only whether those inner states are physical or non-physical. I deny the presupposition. Mental concepts are not about inner states at all; they are about behavioural dispositions and capacities.

Descartes erected what I call the dogma of the Ghost in the Machine: the view that the mind is a spiritual substance housed in a physical body, an immaterial inner theatre in which thoughts and sensations occur as private episodes, accessible only to the possessor of that theatre. I argue that this is not a false theory but a category mistake — a mistake about what logical type mental concepts belong to. It is like asking how much the University of Oxford weighs after being shown all its colleges, libraries, and offices and asking where the University is.

The category mistake explained.

Descartes assumed that because we can ask “what kind of thing is this college?” and get an answer, we can also ask “what kind of thing is the university?” and expect a parallel answer. But “the university” is not an additional entity alongside the colleges; it is a way of describing the institutional arrangement of the colleges.

Similarly, mental concepts do not refer to additional inner entities alongside bodily processes. “Intelligence,” “belief,” “anger” describe the way in which a person conducts their bodily behaviour — the dispositions and capacities they exercise — not hidden inner episodes in an immaterial substance. The error is not empirical but grammatical.
i. the ghost in the machine is a category mistake

Category mistake — dualism mistakes dispositional concepts for episode concepts; mental terms describe how not what; the ghost doesn't existThe Cartesian picture arises from a systematic category mistake. When we say that a person is intelligent, we do not mean that there is an inner mental act called “intelligence” occurring alongside their behaviour; we mean that they are disposed to behave in intelligent ways — to solve problems efficiently, to learn quickly, to adapt to novelty. Intelligence is a dispositional concept; it describes a range of counterfactual conditionals about how a person would behave in various circumstances. To look inside the skull for a separate thing called “intelligence” is to make the same mistake as looking for the university after visiting all the colleges.

ii. knowing-how vs knowing-that: the irreducibility of dispositions

Knowing-how — practical competences are not inner propositional states; intelligent performance is not preceded by ghost-acts; infinite regress refutes inner theatreThe Cartesian view implies that intelligent behaviour is caused by inner mental acts of willing, intending, and deliberating that occur in the immaterial mind. But this generates a vicious regress. If every intelligent action requires a prior inner mental act to guide it, what guides the inner mental act? It must be guided by a prior prior act, and so on infinitely. The regress is stopped only by admitting that some intelligent behaviour is not preceded by inner directing acts — that is, by admitting dispositional accounts. Knowing-how — riding a bicycle, speaking a language — is not a matter of knowing a set of propositions and executing them; it is a matter of having practical capacities that are exercised directly, without inner intermediaries.

iii. behaviourism against all inner-state theories

Against inner states — identity theorists and functionalists still posit inner states; Ryle's behaviourism avoids the regress and the inner theatre altogetherSmart and Putnam, for all their physicalist credentials, still posit inner states — brain states or functional states — to explain behaviour. They have not escaped the grammar of the Cartesian picture; they have merely materialised the ghost. But the problem was never the immateriality of the ghost; it was the ghost itself — the idea that mental terms refer to inner episodes that cause behaviour. The behaviourist account eliminates the ghost entirely: mental terms describe behavioural dispositions, not the inner states that allegedly produce them. This is a more thoroughgoing escape from Cartesianism than any form of inner-state physicalism.

iv. the privacy of sensation reconsidered

Privacy of sensation — even sensation reports are partly dispositional; Wittgenstein's private language argument; sensation concepts are not purely innerThe hardest case for behaviourism is sensation: surely my pain is a genuinely private inner episode, not a mere disposition? But even here the behaviourist analysis has more purchase than the dualist admits. What would it mean to have a pain that made absolutely no difference to behaviour — that caused no wincing, no avoidance, no reports, no attention? The concept of pain is partly constituted by its connections to behaviour and circumstances; a purely inner episode with no behavioural connections would not be recognisable as pain at all. Wittgenstein’s private language argument shows that a sensation concept that is entirely private — entirely disconnected from public criteria — would not be a concept at all.

Let me tell you what consciousness is not. It is not a special place in the brain where experience happens. It is not a magic fluid that some physical processes have and others lack. It is not a single stream of unified experience flowing through a Cartesian theatre. And most importantly for this debate: qualia — those intrinsic, non-relational, ineffable phenomenal properties that Chalmers and Jackson make so much of — do not exist. Or more precisely: they are not what philosophers think they are.

I am a heterophenomenologist and a functionalist. I take first-person reports very seriously — they are crucial data about the mind. But I do not treat them as incorrigible reports of a distinct phenomenal realm. When you report a vivid red experience, you are reporting that something is going on in your cognitive system that is playing a certain functional role. There is no additional fact about what that redness “is really like.”

“Qualia are simply not as 'intrinsic' and 'ineffable' and 'private' and 'directly apprehensible' as philosophers have claimed. Indeed the philosophical tradition surrounding qualia has rather inflated these claims.” — Dennett, 1991
i. the multiple drafts model and the cartesian theatre

Multiple drafts — no Cartesian theatre; no single place consciousness happens; parallel processing, no master narrativeThe Cartesian theatre is the assumption that somewhere in the brain there is a place where it all comes together — where the sensory streams are unified into a single conscious experience. This is a myth, and an empirically refuted one. The brain runs many processes in parallel; there is no single master stream of consciousness, no Cartesian theatre. The “Multiple Drafts” model captures what actually happens: content is being continually edited and revised by multiple parallel processes, with no single locus of conscious integration. What we call consciousness is the output of this massively parallel editorial process, not a stream of experience occurring in a special inner space.

ii. quining qualia: the alleged properties of qualia do not cohere

There is a way out of the impasse between physicalism and dualism that neither side has properly reckoned with: panpsychism. I hold that consciousness is not a mysterious latecomer that somehow emerges from non-conscious physical processes; it is a fundamental and ubiquitous feature of reality, present all the way down, woven into the fabric of the physical world itself.

Panpsychism is not the fairy-tale view that rocks and tables are conscious in the way humans are. It is the more modest claim that the fundamental constituents of physical reality — electrons, quarks, the fields of quantum mechanics — have some form of proto-experiential or experiential properties, and that human consciousness is what these micro-level properties look like when they are organised in the specific way that brains organise them.

Galileo’s Error and the origin of the hard problem.

Galileo made a crucial methodological decision at the beginning of modern science: to mathematise nature by stripping it of qualitative properties. Colour, sound, smell were “secondary qualities” — projections of the mind onto a colourless, soundless, scentless mathematical world. This was an enormous success for physics. But it baked in a problem: if the physical world is defined as lacking qualitative properties, then qualitative properties — consciousness — can never be part of the physical world. The hard problem was not discovered; it was manufactured by Galileo’s methodological exclusion of quality from physics.

The solution is to revise the Galilean picture: to recognise that the qualitative is not excluded from physics but is, rather, the intrinsic nature of physical reality that physics describes from the outside in purely structural terms.
i. the hard problem points to panpsychism

Hard problem points to panpsychism — emergence from non-consciousness is harder than the combination problem; panpsychism is the better betChalmers is right about the hard problem: no physical account explains why there is something it is like to be a brain. But the dualist response — postulating phenomenal properties as a further ontological layer over the physical — is not the only response. The panpsychist response is simpler: if consciousness cannot emerge from wholly non-conscious physical stuff, then physical stuff is not wholly non-conscious. The emergence of consciousness from nothing-like-consciousness is the deepest version of the hard problem. Panpsychism dissolves this version by denying the non-consciousness of the physical, not by adding a separate phenomenal layer.

ii. the intrinsic nature argument

Intrinsic nature — physics describes structure not intrinsic nature; experience is the only intrinsic nature we know; it is reasonable to generalisePhysics describes the physical world in purely structural terms: electrons have mass, charge, spin — all relational, dispositional properties. Physics tells us what electrons do, not what they are. Every physical description is dispositional: a description of how things behave relative to other things. This leaves the intrinsic nature of physical reality — what it is in itself, independently of its relational properties — entirely unspecified by physics. But we know one thing that has an intrinsic, non-relational nature: experience. The redness of red experience, the painfulness of pain, are intrinsic properties, not merely dispositional ones. The natural hypothesis is that the intrinsic nature of physical entities in general is experiential or proto-experiential in character. iii. against eliminativism and deflationary functionalism

Eliminativism proposes to eliminate or deflate the most certain datum in all of science. iv. the combination problem and why it beats the hard problem

Combination problem — how micro-experiences combine; hard but less severe than the hard problem; panpsychism is still the better optionI acknowledge panpsychism’s main difficulty: the combination problem. How do the micro-experiential properties of electrons and neurons combine to produce the unified, rich phenomenal experience of a human being? This is genuinely hard. But it is not as hard as the hard problem it replaces. The hard problem asks how consciousness emerges from nothing resembling consciousness; the combination problem asks how consciousness of one kind gives rise to consciousness of another kind. The latter is a problem of transformation; the former is a problem of creation from nothing. Transformation from experience to experience is less mysterious than the emergence of experience from sheer non-experience. Panpsychism is therefore a better bet than any physicalist account that faces the full hard problem.
