Mr President,

When you open Facebook, Instagram, or TikTok, you are presented with a terms-of-service agreement. You click "I agree." The law — and much of the philosophical literature — treats this as the moment of moral transformation: the moment at which what would otherwise be an intrusion on your privacy becomes permissible. We argue that this view is wrong. Not merely practically inadequate. Philosophically wrong. The click is doing no moral work. And once we understand why, we can see what actually makes data processing permissible or not.

On the failure of the consent model

Consent fails: structural features of SNS terms ensure consent is neither informed nor genuinely autonomousThe consent model holds that data processing is permissible when users have given autonomous, informed consent to the terms of service. We do not deny that autonomous consent would be morally transformative — that a genuine, informed, voluntary agreement would change the moral landscape. We deny that this is what actually occurs. Consider the structural features of social networking service consent. Terms of service are typically dozens of pages long, written in legal language designed for liability protection rather than comprehension. Empirical research consistently shows that fewer than one percent of users read these terms. More fundamentally: the data practices consented to involve complex inferences, predictive modelling, and data sales to third parties whose uses users cannot anticipate. Genuine informed consent requires that the person understands what they are agreeing to. The academic and legal ideal of consent requires this understanding. The "consent" obtained by SNS operators does not approximate it. And once we recognise that the formal requirement of consent is not met in substance, the consent model cannot do the moral work it is assigned.

On the value of choice view — what actually grounds permissibility

VoC view: permissibility is grounded in the value of the opportunity set offered, not in the user's response to itWe propose instead that what makes data processing permissible is not the user's response — the click of agreement — but the quality of the opportunity the SNS offers. Drawing on Scanlon's contractualism and Voorhoeve's work on the value of opportunity, we argue that a person's options are morally adequate when they are such that she could not reasonably reject facing them. This means: when the opportunity to exchange personal data for valuable services is genuinely good enough — when the services are sufficiently valuable, when the data use is appropriately limited, when reasonable alternatives exist — then the processing is permissible regardless of whether the user actually deliberated carefully over the terms. Conversely, when the opportunity set is exploitative — when the value exchanged is meagre relative to the data surrendered, when default settings systematically extract more than users would endorse on reflection, when there are no meaningful alternatives — the processing is impermissible regardless of formal consent. What matters is the structure of the options, not the signature on the form.

On the generic and specific user — how to assess opportunity value

Generic and specific user: opportunity assessment must consider both the range of users and their actual decision-making dispositionsA crucial question for the VoC view is: how do we assess whether an opportunity is sufficiently valuable? We argue that this assessment must be made across a range of users — not merely a hypothetical rational deliberator, and not merely the actually clicking individual. There are two dimensions. First, we must consider the generic user: the range of persons who will encounter this opportunity, with their different information levels, decision-making capacities, and vulnerability profiles. An opportunity that is genuinely valuable for the information-rich and psychologically robust user may be exploitative for the cognitively vulnerable, the time-pressured, or the informationally impoverished. Second, we must consider what actual users — with their actual heuristics, biases, and dispositions — will actually choose when facing the options as presented. An SNS that uses dark patterns to steer users toward maximal data surrender may formally offer a choice while practically ensuring that choice systematically disserves users' interests. Opportunity quality must be assessed in light of actual, not idealised, decision-making.

On third-party interests — the social dimension of data processing

Third parties: individual user consent cannot authorise harms to those who never consented — social effects require a different standardThe consent model focuses on the bilateral relationship between user and platform. But data processing has profound effects on third parties who are not party to any consent transaction. Behavioural data aggregated from millions of users enables targeted political advertising that manipulates democratic processes. Predictive models built from personal data discriminate in housing, insurance, and employment. The data of one user informs inferences about people who never shared that data at all. None of these third-party effects are addressed by the consent of the individual user who clicked "agree." The VoC framework requires us to assess the value of the opportunity not merely for the consenting user but for the range of persons affected by the data processing. This includes — as a matter of explicit moral requirement — the interests of third parties who are affected but never consulted. A data practice that generates real value for users while creating systematic harms to democratic institutions or to the rights of non-users cannot be rendered permissible by the user's consent alone.

On the regulatory implications — from consent to opportunity improvement

Regulatory shift: move from consent enhancement to opportunity quality regulation — the standard becomes what users would endorse, not what they did acceptThe practical implications of the VoC view differ substantially from the consent model. Under the consent model, the regulatory goal is improved consent: better notices, clearer terms, more granular choices. Under the VoC view, the goal is improved opportunities: ensuring that the actual options users face are ones that advance their genuine interests. This means that default settings should be set to the level of data use that users would endorse on reflection — not to the maximum extraction the platform can defensibly present as consented to. It means that SNS operators cannot justify harmful data practices by pointing to terms users formally agreed to, if those terms did not constitute a genuinely valuable opportunity. It means that data practices should be evaluated against a standard of what a reasonable user with accurate information about the practice's consequences would choose — not against the bare fact of a click. The shift is from "did you agree?" to "were your options good enough?"

On Scanlon's contractualism as the philosophical foundation

Contractualist foundation: reasonable rejection is the correct standard — not consent, not aggregate welfare, but what no one could reasonably rejectOur account draws on Scanlon's contractualism, according to which an action is permissible if and only if it is governed by principles that no one could reasonably reject. This contractualist framework is well suited to the data context for several reasons. First, it directs attention to the quality of the opportunity set — are these terms ones that any affected person, properly informed and reasoning correctly about their interests, could reasonably accept? Second, it incorporates third-party interests through the requirement that no one — including those not party to the data transaction — could reasonably reject the governing principle. Third, it grounds the standard in what is reasonable rather than in what was actually chosen, which is precisely the move we need when actual choices are systematically distorted by information asymmetry, cognitive bias, and structural power. The contractualist framework explains why consent is insufficient — it can be given under conditions that no one could reasonably endorse — while providing a positive alternative standard for assessing permissibility.

On why this view is more demanding than it first appears

The view is demanding: most current SNS data practices fail the VoC standard, not merely the consent standardWe want to be clear that the VoC view is not a relaxation of the moral standard governing data processing. It is not a licence for SNS operators to bypass consent because they judge their offerings to be valuable. The standard of reasonable rejection is stringent. Most current SNS data practices, we submit, fail it. Default settings that extract maximal data unless users actively opt out cannot be justified as offering users opportunities they could not reasonably reject — the asymmetry of information and default power is too great. The sale of sensitive personal data to third-party advertisers who build predictive models affecting users' lives in ways they did not anticipate cannot be rendered permissible by pointing to a service that is genuinely useful. The harvesting of data to train AI systems whose downstream effects are currently unknowable cannot be authorised by the abstract possibility that users could have read the terms. Our view is more demanding than consent because it evaluates the actual structure of the options — not just whether the form was signed.

Mr President,

I join my colleagues on the proposition in their rejection of consent as the grounding norm for data processing permissibility. But I want to press further — to argue that the problem with consent in the data context is not merely that users fail to read terms of service. The problem is structural and systemic. Surveillance capitalism has constructed an economic logic in which consent is not just practically imperfect but philosophically incoherent as a legitimating norm. And I want to suggest that while the Value of Opportunity framework represents a genuine advance, it may still be too permissive — it risks licensing practices that should be condemned at a deeper level.

On surveillance capitalism and the expropriation of behavioural surplus

Behavioural surplus: SNS operators unilaterally claim data as a raw material — this is expropriation, not exchange, and consent cannot transform itThe economic logic of surveillance capitalism, as I have documented, begins with a fundamental act of unilateral appropriation. Google discovered, early in its development, that the behavioural data generated by users' searches — their spelling errors, click patterns, revision sequences, location data — was extraordinarily useful for predicting and influencing behaviour. This data was a byproduct of the search service; it was not the intended exchange. It was surplus — behavioural surplus — that Google claimed unilaterally and deployed for its own purposes. The same logic extended to Facebook, to Instagram, to TikTok, to every platform that monetises through behavioural prediction. The exchange is presented as bilateral: service for data. But it is in fact an expropriation of human experience, reclassified as a raw material for economic processes that users never agreed to and do not benefit from. When we ask whether consent can legitimise this, we are asking whether a unilateral claim to someone's experiential residue can be transformed into a legitimate exchange by the ritual of a click. I submit that it cannot — not because the click is uninformed, but because the thing being "consented to" was never the user's to agree to in the first place.

On instrumentarian power — why consent is not merely impractical but structurally corrupted

Instrumentarian power: the power asymmetry between platform and user structurally corrupts the possibility of genuine consentConsent, to be morally transformative, must be given in conditions of relative equality, genuine choice, and freedom from manipulation. Surveillance capitalism systematically destroys each of these conditions. The power I call "instrumentarian" — the ability to know individual behaviour, to predict it, and to modify it through precisely targeted interventions — is held by the platform and denied to the user. The user does not know what the platform knows about them. The user does not know how that knowledge is being deployed to shape their decisions. The user cannot meaningfully evaluate the consequences of sharing data, because those consequences are produced by processes that are proprietary, opaque, and subject to constant revision. In this context, the formal possibility of clicking "I agree" or "I disagree" is not a genuine choice — it is the simulation of choice, offered as a political and legal shield for practices that would otherwise be recognisable as surveillance and control. Consent cannot be rescued by making terms more readable, because the fundamental asymmetry of knowledge and power is the problem, not the typography of the terms.

On where I agree with Wolmarans and Voorhoeve — and where I push further

Agreement and divergence: VoC is right to move beyond consent but may still be too permissive about the exchange itselfI agree with my colleagues that consent cannot ground permissibility in this context, and that the relevant standard must assess the quality of opportunities offered. But I want to press on whether the opportunity exchange model — service for data — is itself the right frame for assessing permissibility. When Wolmarans and Voorhoeve ask whether the opportunity to exchange data for services is sufficiently valuable, they are accepting the framing of a bilateral exchange. But the exchange model presupposes that the data has legitimately become the platform's to use. I am questioning that presupposition. If the data is an expropriation of human experience — a unilateral claim on the residue of people's lives — then making the exchange more valuable does not resolve the underlying wrong. It is analogous to asking whether a toll on a highway was fairly set, when the deeper question is whether someone had the right to erect a toll at all. The VoC framework improves the terms of an exchange whose legitimacy remains, on my account, fundamentally contested.

On the aggregation problem and the social construction of surveillance

Aggregation: individually harmless data points aggregate into surveillance infrastructure that could not have been consented to individuallyA further dimension of the problem concerns aggregation. Individual data points — a search query, a location, a click — may seem trivial. But the aggregation of billions of such data points across millions of users and years of interaction produces a surveillance infrastructure whose capabilities vastly exceed anything a user could have anticipated or consented to when they first signed up. Solove has documented the aggregation problem in detail: combining publicly available information can produce profiles that violate privacy in ways that none of the individual disclosures did. This aggregation is not something users consented to — it is an emergent property of the system that did not exist at the moment of any individual's consent. The VoC framework must grapple with this: the opportunity being assessed is not merely the present exchange but the future uses of aggregated data, including uses that have not yet been developed. Assessing opportunity value in the present cannot fully account for a system that continuously discovers new ways to exploit existing data.

On the democratic stakes — why data processing permissibility is not merely a personal matter

Democratic stakes: surveillance capitalism threatens the epistemic infrastructure of democracy — personal data processing is a collective political problem, not a bilateral exchangeMy colleagues have noted that data processing affects third parties. I want to strengthen this point by specifying the nature of the collective harm. Surveillance capitalism's behavioural modification techniques — micro-targeted advertising, emotional contagion experiments, algorithmic manipulation of information feeds — are not merely commercial tools. They are instruments capable of shaping the beliefs, preferences, and political behaviour of entire populations. The Cambridge Analytica scandal demonstrated that personal data, harvested through a social network, could be weaponised to target individual voters with personalised political messages designed to exploit psychological vulnerabilities. This is not a marginal use case. It is the logical extension of the business model. When we ask whether data processing is permissible, we are not merely asking whether individual users get a fair deal. We are asking whether a private power structure that can modify the beliefs of millions is compatible with democratic self-governance. This question cannot be answered at the level of individual opportunity quality.

On what genuine permissibility requires — beyond VoC

Beyond VoC: permissibility may require systemic constraints on data use that no individual opportunity assessment can provideIf the critique I have developed is correct, then genuine permissibility of data processing requires more than opportunity improvement. It requires systemic constraints on the kinds of data use that are permissible at all — constraints that cannot be licensed by individual user agreement, however well-informed and genuinely autonomous. Specifically: behavioural prediction and modification techniques that operate below the threshold of conscious awareness cannot be rendered permissible by any individual agreement, because they subvert the very deliberative capacity that makes agreement meaningful. Data uses whose downstream effects include democratic manipulation cannot be licensed at the individual level because their harms are collective and cannot be consented to individually. The category of impermissible data uses must be defined at the systemic level — through democratic deliberation about the kinds of data practices a free society should permit — rather than through the bilateral exchange model that both the consent framework and, I argue, the VoC framework presuppose.

On what I concede — and what remains of the VoC framework

Concession: VoC is a genuine improvement over consent for the domain of legitimate data exchanges; it just does not define that domainI want to close by acknowledging what my critique does not establish. I have argued that the VoC framework accepts the framing of a bilateral exchange whose legitimacy is itself in question. But I do not claim that all data processing is impermissible, or that the exchange framework is never appropriate. Within the domain of data processing practices that are not impermissible at the systemic level — practices that do not involve covert behavioural modification, democratic manipulation, or unilateral expropriation of behavioural surplus — the VoC framework provides the correct standard for assessing individual permissibility. My argument is that this domain is narrower than Wolmarans and Voorhoeve assume. They take the exchange model as given and ask what quality of opportunities it must offer; I argue that prior to that question, we must determine which practices are permissible candidates for the exchange model at all. These are complementary, not competing, projects — and together they provide a more complete account of data processing permissibility than either alone.

Mr President,

I find myself in an unusual position. The proposition draws on my own contractualist framework — on the account of the value of choice I have developed across several decades of work — to argue for a conclusion that I think goes further than my framework supports, and in a direction I am not fully comfortable with. I want to engage with the proposition on its own terms, to acknowledge what is correct in its critique of consent, and then to explain where I think the Value of Opportunity view, as they develop it, faces serious problems — some of which reflect limitations in my own original account that I have since tried to address.

On what I accept — the limits of the forfeiture view

Forfeiture view rejected: choice does not licence burdens merely because they were formally chosen — the value of choice grounds both the right to choose and the conditions under which choice can bear moral weightMy account of the value of choice was developed partly as a critique of what I call the Forfeiture View — the idea that a person may permissibly be required to bear a burden if and only if they have actively chosen to accept it, because the act of choosing forfeits their right to complain about the outcome. I reject the Forfeiture View because it focuses exclusively on the bare fact of choice, divorced from the quality of the options available and the conditions under which choice was made. A choice made under severe duress, or from a severely constrained option set, or without adequate information about its implications, does not have the legitimating force that the Forfeiture View assigns to choice as such. In this much, I agree with Wolmarans and Voorhoeve. The SNS click-through is not a choice that bears the moral weight the consent model assigns to it. The options offered are not adequate; the information available is not adequate; the power differential is severe. Insofar as the proposition critiques the Forfeiture View as applied to data consent, it is correct.

On the generic user — a problem for the VoC view that I identified

Generic user problem: Scanlon's original framework uses a generic user standard that Voorhoeve modifies — but this modification creates new difficultiesWolmarans and Voorhoeve note that my original contractualist account evaluates option sets by reference to what a generic user has reason to value and how a generic user can be expected to fare given the opportunities. They then modify this to require assessment for the full range of users with different decision-making abilities. This modification is motivated by real concerns — the generic user standard may underweight harms to more vulnerable users. But I want to press on a difficulty this modification creates. If we assess opportunity quality by reference to the range of actual users, we must decide how to aggregate across that range. Do we assess by the worst-off user? By some weighted average? By whether most users benefit? Each aggregation method will produce different verdicts. The proposition does not specify how to aggregate across the range of users, and I am not sure a principled answer is available. My original generic user standard avoided this aggregation problem by idealising; the modified standard inherits it but does not resolve it.

On the limits of the VoC account — Olsaretti's objection

Olsaretti's objection: VoC can only generate responsibility for a restricted class of burdens — the framework may be incomplete in ways that affect the data contextSerena Olsaretti has pressed a powerful objection to my Value of Choice account — one that I think bears on Wolmarans and Voorhoeve's application of it. Olsaretti argues that VoC, in its most distinctive version, can only justify responsibility attributions for a restricted class of burdens: those that are the opportunity-accompanying costs of choices that are genuinely valuable in themselves. For burdens that are simply the consequences of choices that were available and made, VoC has less to say — it collapses, in these cases, toward the Forfeiture View it was designed to replace. The data context raises precisely this question: are the privacy burdens of SNS use opportunity-accompanying costs of genuine choices that are valuable in themselves? Or are they simply the consequences of entering a space that offers no real alternative? If the latter — and the structural dominance of major platforms makes this question live — then VoC cannot generate the clean verdict the proposition requires.

On reasonable rejection and who counts as reasonably rejecting

Reasonable rejection: who is the appropriate rejector in the data context? The diversity of interests among users makes the contractualist standard indeterminateThe proposition says that data processing is permissible when no affected party could reasonably reject the governing principles. But in the data context, this standard faces a serious indeterminacy problem. Some users value the services highly and consider the data exchange genuinely beneficial — they could not reasonably reject it. Others are privacy-sensitive, find the data collection intrusive, and would prefer to pay directly rather than with their data — they might reasonably reject the current arrangement. A third group — the cognitively vulnerable, the informationally impoverished — may be harmed in ways they do not fully recognise. How do we determine whether "no one could reasonably reject" the relevant principle, given this diversity? My contractualist framework was designed for relatively homogeneous moral situations; its application to a context of profound heterogeneity in preferences, information levels, and vulnerability requires refinement that neither I nor Wolmarans and Voorhoeve have fully provided.

On the value of consent as more than a procedure

Consent has intrinsic value: the VoC framework risks treating consent as merely instrumental, losing sight of what consent actually does for personsThe proposition argues that consent is not the grounding norm for data processing permissibility. I want to resist a version of this argument that goes too far. Consent — genuine, informed, voluntary consent — is not merely a procedure for transferring a permission. It is the expression of a person's autonomous standing as the author of their own life, the recognition that this person's decision about this matter belongs to them. When we bypass consent because we have determined that the opportunity offered was sufficiently valuable, we risk treating the person as an object of our beneficent assessment rather than as an agent whose own decision is what matters. This is not a merely procedural concern. It is the recognition that persons have a right to make decisions about their own information that cannot be overridden simply because we — or the platform, or the regulator — judge the outcome to be good for them. The VoC framework, in making the permissibility of data processing depend on opportunity quality rather than on the person's own choice, risks a form of paternalism that the proposition has not adequately grappled with.

On the right to refuse — and what it means for the VoC framework

Right to refuse: the VoC framework does not adequately account for the person's right to refuse a genuinely good opportunityThe most direct challenge to the VoC view is this: can a person permissibly refuse an opportunity that, by the proposition's standard, was sufficiently valuable? If data processing is permissible when the opportunity is good enough — and it remains permissible regardless of whether the user actually valued it or chose it — then the user's refusal appears to carry no moral weight. But this seems wrong. A person's right to control their own personal information is not merely a right to a good outcome — it is a right to decide, even suboptimally, about matters that fall within their personal domain. The proposition might respond that VoC determines permissibility at the level of the platform's policy, not the individual user's choice — the platform is permitted to offer the opportunity, but the user retains the right to decline. But this response does not fully address the paternalism worry: the platform's data practices are governed by the standard of what good opportunities look like, not by whether users actually endorse them. Individual refusal, on this account, seems to have no regulatory force.

On what I conclude — a more modest use of the VoC framework

Modest VoC: the framework should supplement, not replace, consent — it sets minimum conditions for the option set, but genuine consent within those conditions remains the grounding normI want to propose a more modest use of my framework than Wolmarans and Voorhoeve defend. Rather than replacing consent with opportunity quality as the grounding norm for permissibility, I suggest that opportunity quality sets a necessary but not sufficient condition. Platforms are obligated to ensure that the options they offer are such that no one could reasonably reject facing them — this is a threshold condition that sets the floor below which no consent can legitimise data processing. But above that floor, genuine consent — actual, informed, voluntary agreement by the person whose data is at stake — remains the grounding norm for the specific data uses that occur. This preserves both the proposition's insight that the option set must be adequate, and the value of consent as the expression of personal autonomy within adequate options. It avoids the paternalism worry and respects the person's right to decide about their own information, while acknowledging that the decision must occur within an option set that passes a minimum quality standard.

Mr President,

The debate so far has been conducted entirely within a framework that I think is fundamentally misdirected. Both the proposition and the opposition have assumed that the question of data processing permissibility is essentially a question about the bilateral relationship between a user and a platform — about the quality of the options offered, or the validity of the consent given, or the value of the exchange. I want to argue that this bilateral framing misses the nature of privacy as a moral and social phenomenon. Privacy is not primarily about what individuals choose to share with platforms. It is about the appropriate flow of information in social contexts defined by distinct norms. Once we understand this, both the consent framework and the Value of Opportunity framework appear as variants of the same fundamental error.

On contextual integrity — privacy as appropriate information flow

Contextual integrity: privacy is appropriate information flow according to context-specific norms — not individual control or transactional qualityThe theory of contextual integrity, which I have developed over several years, defines privacy not as individual control over information, nor as the outcome of a good transaction, but as the appropriate flow of information according to the norms of the social context in which that information was originally disclosed. When you share your health information with your doctor, you do so in a context with specific norms: the information flows appropriately to other treating physicians, to the relevant hospital records, and perhaps to certain insurance functions — but not to your employer, your family, or advertisers. The privacy violation occurs not when information is shared — sharing is often the point — but when information flows outside the norms appropriate to the context in which it was originally shared. Applied to SNS, the theory generates this verdict: information disclosed on a social network — in the context of social connection, self-expression, community building — violates contextual integrity when it flows to advertisers, political campaigns, predictive modelling firms, or employers, because these flows violate the norms of the social context in which the information was originally shared.

On why the consent model fails from a contextual integrity perspective

Consent is the wrong frame: what matters is context-appropriate flow, not individual agreement — users cannot consent to violations of contextual norms on behalf of the contextThe consent model fails, on my account, not because users fail to read terms of service, but because the consent model asks the wrong question. When a user clicks "I agree" to terms allowing their data to flow to advertisers, the question is not whether they agreed but whether this flow is appropriate to the social context in which the information was originally shared. And the answer, typically, is that it is not. The context of social networking — the norms under which people share photos, opinions, relationship statuses, political views — does not include the norm that this information flows to commercial third parties for targeted advertising. Users sharing information in this context have a reasonable expectation, grounded in the contextual norms of social networking, that it remains within that context. The fact that they clicked a legal agreement does not change the contextual norms — just as your doctor's ability to get you to sign a waiver does not make it contextually appropriate for them to share your health information with advertisers. Consent cannot override contextual norms, because those norms are not the individual's to waive.

On why the VoC framework also fails from a contextual integrity perspective

VoC also fails: it assesses the value of the transaction rather than the appropriateness of the information flow — it imports the consent framework's bilateral error into a different registerThe Value of Opportunity framework avoids some of the consent model's failures, but it inherits its fundamental error. Both frameworks focus on the bilateral relationship between user and platform — on what is offered and what is received, on whether the deal is fair, on whether the options are good enough. But this bilateral framing treats data as something that moves between two parties to a transaction, and asks whether the terms of the transaction are adequate. The contextual integrity framework asks a different question: is the information flow appropriate to the social context in which the information originated? These are fundamentally different questions. Information that originated in a social context — shared among friends, expressed in a community, disclosed in a context of trust and reciprocity — does not become appropriate for commercial flow simply because the transaction through which it moves is a fair one. The fairness of the transaction does not determine the appropriateness of the flow. The VoC framework, in assessing the quality of the opportunity offered by the platform, is still treating the data as a commodity whose movement is governed by transactional norms rather than contextual ones.

On the social construction of privacy norms — why they cannot be waived individually

Social norms: contextual privacy norms are collective, not individual — individuals cannot waive them unilaterally, just as they cannot waive norms of professional confidentialityA crucial feature of the contextual integrity framework is that the relevant norms are social and collective, not individual and personal. When I share a medical diagnosis with my doctor, the confidentiality norm that governs that disclosure is not my personal preference for privacy — it is a norm embedded in the institution of medicine, in the fiduciary relationship between doctor and patient, in the social practice of healthcare. I cannot waive that norm by signing a form, because the norm is not mine alone to waive — it belongs to the context and to all who inhabit it. The same is true of social networking contexts. The norms under which people share personal information on social networks — the implicit expectations of the context — are collective. They are not the property of any individual user, to be waived by their agreement or satisfied by an adequate opportunity. When platforms violate these contextual norms, they do wrong to all participants in the context, not merely to those who happen to have read the terms carefully enough to have "agreed" to the violation.

On the SNS as a broken contextual space — why the problem is architectural

Broken context: SNS architecture systematically violates the contextual norms under which information is shared — the context is corrupted at its foundationThe deepest problem with social networking services, from a contextual integrity perspective, is that they are designed to systematically violate the contextual norms under which information is shared. The architecture of a platform like Facebook or Instagram creates a space that presents itself as a social context — a place for sharing among friends, family, and communities — while simultaneously operating as a commercial surveillance infrastructure that channels information shared in this social context to commercial third parties. This is not a marginal or incidental feature of SNS architecture. It is the business model. The platform creates a contextual space with social norms, harvests information disclosed under those social norms, and redirects it to a commercial context governed by entirely different norms. No improvement in consent procedures and no improvement in the quality of the opportunity offered addresses this architectural problem. The contextual space itself is corrupted at its foundation.

On what a contextual integrity approach to regulation requires

Contextual regulation: regulation should enforce context-appropriate information flows, not improve consent or opportunity — the standard is contextual norms, not transactional qualityThe regulatory implications of contextual integrity differ substantially from both the consent model and the VoC framework. Under the consent model, regulation aims to improve the consent procedure. Under VoC, regulation aims to improve the quality of the opportunity offered. Under contextual integrity, regulation aims to enforce context-appropriate information flows — to ensure that information disclosed in one context does not flow to another in ways that violate the norms of the originating context. Practically, this means that data shared in the context of social networking cannot be redirected to commercial advertising without violating contextual norms, regardless of what users agreed to or what opportunity they were offered. It means that the appropriate regulatory target is the information flow itself — the technical and contractual mechanisms by which data crosses contextual boundaries — rather than the consent procedure or the option set presented to users. The GDPR's principle of purpose limitation — data collected for one purpose should not be used for another — is a partial expression of contextual integrity, though it is implemented through consent machinery that contextual integrity shows is insufficient.

On what I concede — and where I join the proposition's critique

Concession: contextual integrity and VoC agree that current SNS data practices are impermissible — they disagree about why and about what would make them permissibleLet me be clear about where contextual integrity agrees with the proposition, despite disagreeing about the framework. Both the VoC view and contextual integrity condemn most current SNS data practices as impermissible. Both reject the consent model as an adequate grounding for permissibility. Both identify that the current regulatory framework — which treats consent as the key to permissibility — is fundamentally misdirected. Where we differ is in our account of what would make data processing permissible, and what regulatory approach follows from that account. Wolmarans and Voorhoeve say: improve the quality of the opportunity offered, and data processing can be permissible within the exchange framework. I say: data processing is permissible only when the information flows are appropriate to the context in which the data was originally shared — which is a standard that can rarely be met by SNS data practices, regardless of the quality of the opportunity offered. These are not merely terminological differences. They generate different verdicts about what is permissible and different programmes for regulation.
