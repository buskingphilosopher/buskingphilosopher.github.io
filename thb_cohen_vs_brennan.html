A Live Philosophical Debate
"Why Not Socialism?" versus "Why Not Capitalism?"
G. A. Cohen  ·  Princeton University Press, 2009   |   Jason Brennan  ·  Princeton University Press, 2014
Socialist
G. A. Cohen
Why Not Socialism? (2009)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Professor Brennan,

I want to begin with what I have always found to be the most clarifying device in political philosophy: a thought experiment simple enough to strip away the ideological static we have all accumulated, and clear enough to reveal what, underneath it all, we actually believe.

Imagine a camping trip. A group of friends travel together into the countryside. There is fishing to be done, firewood to gather, meals to cook. Nobody among us stakes a private claim to the lake. Nobody charges rent for the patch of ground where the tent is pitched. Nobody who catches three fish hoards them in exchange for a fee while others go hungry. We share what there is. We contribute what we can. When someone turns an ankle, we do not weigh the expected return before helping them back to camp. We help them because they need help. The camping trip is, as I put it in my book, a microcosm of a socialist community.

Now: what is it that makes the camping trip feel like the natural, decent, obvious way to organise things — and what prevents us from extending that logic to the society we actually live in?

The two principles socialism requires

The camping-trip argument: socialist principles feel naturalThe camping trip reveals that human beings are, in the right conditions, entirely capable of organising themselves around two principles: the socialist principle of equality — "to each according to her needs" — and the socialist principle of community — "from each according to her abilities." These are not exotic impositions on human nature. They are the principles we embrace, without coercion and without argument, whenever we actually choose to live together in a small community of trust. My question is not whether socialism is theoretically conceivable. My question is: if we accept these principles for the camping trip, what exactly is the argument for rejecting them at larger scale? What changes, and why should that change be morally decisive?

Against market inequality: the distributive objection

Market outcomes reflect morally arbitrary factorsI want to press a point about what market outcomes actually reflect. Markets distribute rewards in accordance with a combination of: the talents you were born with, the family you were born into, the society whose infrastructure you happened to inherit, and the luck of market demand happening to want what you can supply at the moment you supply it. None of these factors — not one — is something for which you can take genuine moral credit. The talented surgeon did not earn her talents. The entrepreneur did not build the roads, the legal system, or the educated workforce that made his enterprise possible. The financier who happened to be positioned correctly in 2009 was not thereby demonstrating virtue. Markets reward people for things that are not, in any morally deep sense, their own. They are a machine for laundering luck into the appearance of desert.

The socialist ethos: the motivational heart of the matter

The socialist ethos: community, not self-interest, as the motivating springProfessor Brennan will tell you — I have read his book carefully — that the "courageous, true, and admirable" case for capitalism is not that it satisfies greed but that it harnesses self-interest in the service of coordination. Fine. But I want to examine what that concession actually costs him. To say that capitalism must rely on self-interest as its motivating spring is to say that capitalism can only function if most people, most of the time, are asking "what is in it for me?" rather than "what does my community need?" This is not a minor observation. This is an admission that capitalism requires, and in turn reproduces, a motivational structure that is at odds with the most fundamental human decencies. The camping trip does not run on self-interest. The friendship does not run on self-interest. The family does not run on self-interest. I am not claiming self-interest is unreal. I am claiming that a society organised around it — that makes it the engine of its coordination — pays a price in solidarity, in mutual recognition, and in the texture of daily social life that economists are singularly ill-equipped to measure.

On the Wilt Chamberlain argument and its limits

Against Nozick-style desert: talents are not self-owned in the relevant senseMy colleague Professor Brennan's position draws, as all sophisticated defences of capitalism do, on something like the Nozickean intuition: that people are entitled to the fruits of their talents because their talents are, in a meaningful sense, their own. But this is the step I resist. What does it mean for a talent to be your own? You did not choose your genetic endowment. You did not choose the parents whose encouragement cultivated your abilities. You did not choose the school, the teacher, the book that arrived at the moment you were ready to be changed by it. The self that now possesses these talents is itself the product of a thousand unchosen encounters. Now: I do not conclude from this that you are merely a vehicle for forces beyond your control. You are a person. You have a life. But the inference from "this talent is mine" to "therefore I may keep all of whatever the market will pay me for it, and others have no claim on those proceeds" is a very long jump. It passes through a conception of self-ownership that, examined carefully, is far thinner than it initially appears.

Does human nature defeat the socialist project?

The human nature objection: people are not, irremediably, self-interestedI anticipate the central objection from Professor Brennan's side of the table: human nature. People are self-interested. They will free-ride. They will defect. You cannot run a modern economy on the motivational assumptions of a camping trip. The camping trip only works because it is small, face-to-face, and temporary. I want to be honest about this objection. I do not dismiss it. I think it identifies a real and serious problem for socialism. But I want to make two observations. First: the objection assumes that the motivational profile we observe in capitalist societies is a fact about human nature rather than a fact about what capitalism does to human beings. Human beings raised in institutions that reward cooperative behaviour — that teach, from early childhood, that community matters and that contributing to the common good is admirable — may behave very differently from human beings raised in capitalist institutions. The cause-and-effect here runs in both directions, and those who appeal to human nature often forget this. Second: the motivational problem is, as I explicitly acknowledge in my book, a genuine difficulty for socialism. But it is a technological problem, not a moral problem. The desirability of socialism does not stand or fall with its current feasibility. That is like saying we should not want to cure cancer because we do not yet know how. What I establish is that socialism is what we should want; the question of how to get there without the engine stalling is, as I put it, a further question.

On Brennan's "Mickey Mouse Club" and the Plekhanov objection

Against Brennan's "camping trip fallacy": scale does not change the moral logicProfessor Brennan's central counter to my camping trip is what I expect he will call the "scale problem." A camping trip of eight is not a society of eighty million. The mutual visibility, personal accountability, and shared affective bonds of the camping trip are not reproducible at national scale, and without them, the socialist principles do not generate the behaviour we observe on the trip. I take this seriously. But I want to distinguish the moral argument from the practical argument. The camping trip is not offered as a blueprint. It is offered as a moral lens. It shows us what we believe about distributive justice when we strip away the accumulated rationalisations of a market society and ask ourselves directly. The conclusion — that we believe, at bottom, in distribution according to need and contribution according to ability — is a moral conclusion. Whether that conclusion can be institutionalised at scale is a further, practical question. Brennan's argument does not show that the moral conclusion is wrong; it shows that its implementation faces obstacles. These are not the same thing.

On community as an irreducible value

Community as a distinct socialist value, beyond equalityI want to end by saying something about community that I think does not receive sufficient attention in debates about distributive justice — which tend to focus on shares. Socialism is not only about how much everyone gets. It is about the nature of the relationships through which people interact. A society in which every significant transaction is mediated by the price mechanism — in which my relationship to you is, at bottom, one of mutual advantage — is missing something important. It is missing what I call the socialist community: a mode of relating to others in which I am genuinely motivated by their wellbeing, not merely by my own interest in their goodwill. This is the spirit of the camping trip. We can debate whether it can be institutionalised. But we should not let the difficulty of institutionalising it lead us to pretend that it is not what we actually want. The camping trip is not an anomaly or a fantasy. It is a glimpse of something we are already capable of being.

The "technological" limit: socialism is desirable even if not yet achievableI close with this. If Professor Brennan's argument is that socialism is impossible, then we are not really disagreeing about values — we are disagreeing about facts, facts about human motivation and institutional design. That is a productive disagreement. But if his argument is that capitalism is not merely more feasible but actually more just — that the inequalities it generates are deserved, that its motivational reliance on self-interest is a feature rather than a bug, that community is a nice-to-have rather than a moral necessity — then we have a deeper dispute. I believe community is not optional. I believe that a society in which people's fates are as unequal as they are under capitalism, for reasons as morally arbitrary as those capitalism tracks, falls short of what justice requires. I believe the camping trip describes not a fantasy but an aspiration. And I believe we are capable of more than capitalism asks of us.

Thank you.

Argument I — The camping trip: socialism feels naturalclick for image
1.In the camping trip thought experiment, participants naturally organise around need-satisfaction and ability-based contribution without coercion.
2.The principles governing the camping trip — "to each according to need, from each according to ability" — are accepted as obviously correct by virtually everyone who considers the scenario.
3.If we endorse these principles when we think clearly about small-scale cooperative life, we require a positive argument for why larger scale should generate different principles.
∴The camping trip reveals that socialist distributive principles are those we actually endorse under conditions of clear moral thinking; rejecting them at larger scale requires special justification.
Argument II — Market outcomes track morally arbitrary factorsclick for image
1.Market rewards are distributed in proportion to talent, inherited social position, infrastructure one did not create, and luck of market timing — none of which is the recipient's doing.
2.A just distribution cannot be grounded in morally arbitrary factors — factors for which the recipient deserves no credit.
3.The talented, the well-born, and the lucky did not earn the advantages that make their market success possible.
∴Market distributions are not just distributions; they are a mechanism for translating moral luck into material inequality.
Argument III — Capitalism requires a morally inferior motivational structureclick for image
1.Capitalism functions by coordinating activity through the price mechanism, which requires participants to act primarily on self-interest.
2.A society whose coordination mechanism requires self-interest as its engine reproduces and reinforces self-interested motivation across the whole of social life.
3.Community — the genuine orientation toward others' wellbeing as an end, not merely a means — is a morally superior motivational structure to self-interest, as the camping trip and the family both demonstrate.
∴Capitalism pays a moral price in solidarity and community that cannot be offset by its productive efficiency.
Argument IV — Self-ownership is thinner than it appearsclick for image
1.The entitlement view of market rewards rests on the claim that people own their talents and are therefore entitled to market returns on those talents.
2.Talents are the product of genetics, upbringing, social infrastructure, and circumstance — none of which is chosen by the person who has them.
3.The self that "possesses" talents is itself constituted by unchosen factors; the move from "this talent is part of me" to "I am entitled to all the market will yield for it" does not follow without further argument.
∴The entitlement foundation of capitalist distribution is philosophically unstable; self-ownership, examined carefully, does not generate the strong proprietary claims capitalism requires.
Argument V — Human nature is plastic, not fixedclick for image
1.The objection that socialist principles cannot work because people are irredeemably self-interested treats observed motivational profiles as fixed facts about human nature.
2.Human motivation is substantially shaped by the institutions people are raised within; capitalist institutions cultivate self-interested behaviour by rewarding it.
3.Socialist institutions, if established, might cultivate cooperative and community-oriented motivation over generations.
∴Appeals to human nature against socialism conflate the motivational profile capitalism produces with a permanent fact about human beings.
Argument VI — Scale changes feasibility, not moral truthclick for image
1.The camping trip is a moral argument, not a policy blueprint; it establishes what principles of distribution we endorse, not how to implement them.
2.Showing that socialist principles are difficult to implement at scale shows that socialism faces a practical obstacle, not that socialism is wrong.
3.The desirability of a goal is logically independent of its current technological or institutional feasibility.
∴The scale objection defeats a naive socialist blueprint but does not defeat the moral case for socialism as an ideal to be pursued.
Argument VII — Community is a distinct value, not reducible to equalityclick for image
1.Socialism requires not just equal shares but a form of social relationship in which people are genuinely motivated by each other's wellbeing.
2.This communal motivation — present in friendships, families, and the camping trip — is lost when all significant social coordination is mediated by market exchange.
3.The loss of community is a moral cost that does not appear in standard economic welfare calculations.
∴Even if capitalism matched socialism on distributive equality, its corrosion of community would remain a distinct moral objection to it.
Argument VIII — The "technological" limit does not diminish the idealclick for image
1.Cohen explicitly acknowledges that the motivational problem — getting people to act on socialist principles at scale — is a genuine and as-yet-unsolved problem.
2.This problem is a problem of social technology, not a problem of moral truth; we may not know how to achieve the ideal without it being less of an ideal.
3.Analogously, the difficulty of curing cancer does not make health less of a value worth pursuing.
∴Socialism remains what we should want even if we do not yet know how to achieve it without institutional failure; feasibility and desirability are distinct questions.
Capitalist
Jason Brennan
Why Not Capitalism? (2014)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Professor Cohen,

It is a genuine pleasure to engage with your argument, and I want to make clear at the outset that my disagreement with you is not the disagreement of someone who finds socialism morally repugnant. I find much in your vision admirable. The camping trip is a beautiful image. I simply think it proves less than you believe it does — and that capitalism, properly understood, is not merely the best available system but is actually the morally correct system for beings like us.

Let me begin by pressing on the thought experiment itself, because I think a thought experiment is only as good as what it actually tests.

The camping trip is not a model of socialism — it is a model of voluntary community

The camping trip proves voluntary community, not imposed socialismYou ask: if we endorse socialist principles on the camping trip, why not in society at large? But I want to examine what is actually doing the work in your thought experiment. The camping trip is voluntary. Everyone chose to come. Everyone can leave. The group is small enough that free-riding is immediately visible and socially costly. The participants are bound by prior personal relationships of trust and affection. And crucially — this is my central point — if some members of the camping party found the socialist arrangements objectionable and wanted instead to operate on market principles, they would be free to leave and form their own camp. Your thought experiment proves that voluntary communities can and do organise themselves around socialist norms. It does not prove that these norms should be imposed, by state coercion, on people who do not consent to them. A world of voluntary socialist communities — what I call a world of Mickey Mouse Clubs, or rather, what you call socialism at small scale — is entirely compatible with capitalism at the level of the political system. Capitalism does not forbid the camping trip. It permits it.

What capitalism actually is: the voluntary-exchange argument

Capitalism is the system of voluntary community at maximum scaleI want to reframe what capitalism is, because I think you are arguing against a caricature. Capitalism is not the system in which greed is rewarded and community destroyed. Capitalism is the system in which people are free to form whatever cooperative, communal, or exchange-based arrangements they choose, and in which the results of voluntary exchange are respected. A world of universal capitalism is a world in which people can form your camping trips, your kibbutzim, your worker cooperatives, and your communes — and also, for those who choose it, engage in market exchange. Socialism, by contrast, requires that the socialist distribution be imposed on everyone, including those who would prefer different arrangements. The asymmetry matters enormously. Capitalism permits socialism. Socialism forbids capitalism. If community is genuinely valuable — as I agree it is — then the system that permits community while also permitting other forms of association is more respectful of human diversity than the system that mandates one form for everyone.

On the motivational structure: self-interest is not shameful

Self-interest is not morally inferior; the courageous capitalist case accepts it honestlyYou say that capitalism requires self-interest as its engine and that this is a moral cost. I want to challenge both the description and the evaluation. On the description: capitalism does not require that everyone act selfishly all the time. It requires only that the price mechanism be free to coordinate activity. Many people acting within capitalist institutions are motivated by community, friendship, craft, and social mission — the price system simply aggregates and transmits information that allows their diverse motivations to be coordinated. On the evaluation: you seem to assume that acting from self-interest is morally inferior to acting from altruism or community feeling. But self-interest, honestly declared and consistently pursued within a framework of rights and voluntary exchange, is not shameful. It is honest. The person who says "I will help you if you help me, and we will both be better off" is not a moral deficient. She is a participant in the great cooperative achievement that is the market order. What is genuinely shameful is free-riding — claiming the benefits of cooperation while refusing to contribute — and it is capitalism, not socialism, that builds in the strongest structural incentives against it.

On the moral-arbitrary objection: market rewards track contribution

Markets reward contribution, not merely luck; the arbitrary-factor argument proves too muchYou argue that market rewards reflect morally arbitrary factors — talent, family, infrastructure — rather than genuine desert. I find this argument significantly overstated. First, markets do reward something morally non-arbitrary: contribution. The surgeon who spends twenty years training and then operates with skill for sixty hours a week contributes enormously to others' wellbeing. The market reward she receives is not arbitrary in the morally pejorative sense; it reflects that she has done something genuinely valuable for others. You may say her talent was unchosen. True. But her effort, her dedication, her choices about how to use her talents — these are hers in a meaningful sense. Second, your argument proves too much. If the fact that talents are unchosen undermines entitlement to market returns, it equally undermines entitlement to the love, respect, and social standing that talented and virtuous people attract. We do not conclude, on learning that a person's warm personality was shaped by fortunate upbringing, that we should forcibly redistribute social relationships. Third: the relevant alternative to market distribution is not some desert-tracking ideal distribution but the actual outputs of political and bureaucratic decision-making. The state redistributing market outcomes has its own arbitrary features — it tracks political power, organised interests, and bureaucratic incentives rather than need or desert. The comparison must be honest.

On the human nature objection: Cohen's honesty and its implications

Cohen's own admission: the motivational problem is not merely "technological"I want to pay you the compliment of taking your concession about human nature entirely seriously. You acknowledge that the motivational problem for socialism — that people will free-ride, defect, and pursue self-interest even in socialist institutions — is a genuine and as-yet-unsolved problem. You call it "technological." You say it does not diminish the ideal. But I want to push back on this framing. The motivational problem is not like the technical challenge of curing cancer, where the obstacle is our ignorance of a mechanism and the solution, once found, would involve no moral cost. The "solution" to the motivational problem under socialism has historically taken one of two forms: either extensive coercive enforcement — the state monitoring, punishing, and incentivising individual behaviour to prevent defection — or the transformation of human nature through education and cultural re-engineering. Both of these solutions carry significant moral costs of their own. The first corrodes the very freedom that makes community meaningful. The second is the stuff of which authoritarian projects are made. A technological problem whose solutions require these costs is not merely a technical difficulty. It is a warning about the nature of the ideal itself.

The scale argument is not merely practical — it is moral

Scale transforms moral relationships: anonymity and pluralism change the ethicsYou present the scale argument as a practical obstacle to an otherwise valid moral ideal. I want to argue that scale generates a genuinely different moral situation, not merely a harder implementation problem. At the scale of a camping trip, I know my fellow participants. I know their needs, their abilities, their contributions, and their characters. I can assess their claims on the common stock directly. Socialist distribution — distribution according to need — is feasible here precisely because needs are visible and assessable. At the scale of a modern society of millions of strangers with radically different values, conceptions of the good, and views about what counts as a need or a contribution, no central authority can possess the information required to distribute according to need. This is not merely Hayek's knowledge problem, though it is that too. It is also a moral pluralism problem: whose conception of need should govern? A society in which the state determines what counts as a legitimate need and allocates accordingly is not a more realised version of the camping trip. It is something categorically different — something that requires state power over the most intimate aspects of human life.

On community: capitalism enables more community than socialism

Capitalism enables plural community; socialism imposes one formYou argue that capitalism corrodes community by making the market the dominant mode of social relation. I want to invert this. Capitalism — understood as a system of voluntary exchange and free association — creates the conditions for an enormous diversity of communal forms. Religious communities, secular communes, worker cooperatives, voluntary mutual aid societies, professional guilds, families — all of these flourish within capitalist societies and all of them involve the precisely the community spirit you celebrate. What capitalism does not do is force everyone into the same community with the same distributive norms. And I regard this as a feature, not a failure. Human beings disagree about what community looks like, what mutual obligation entails, and what a good life requires. A system that respects this disagreement by permitting diverse forms of association is, I want to argue, morally superior to one that resolves the disagreement by imposing a single communal form on everyone. Your camping trip is a beautiful community. My claim is that capitalism makes a world of camping trips possible. Socialism makes one, mandatory, very large camping trip from which there is no exit.

The counterfactual: what actually produces socialist norms?

Socialist norms in practice have required coercion; voluntary socialism confirms capitalismI want to end with a challenge I believe is decisive. Everywhere that genuinely socialist norms have governed social life at scale — meaning not in the small voluntary communities that capitalism also permits, but in actually imposed socialist regimes — the results have involved extensive coercion, information failure, and the suppression of precisely the individual freedom and diverse communal life that makes human flourishing possible. You will say these failures were not true socialism. Perhaps. But the pattern is not coincidental. It reflects the structural difficulty of imposing need-based distribution and ability-based contribution on people who have not consented, who disagree about what each requires, and who respond to socialist incentives by defecting. The camping trip works because of consent, visibility, and exit. Remove those conditions — as any large-scale socialist state must — and you remove the conditions that made the socialist norms work. What remains is coercion dressed as community.

The positive case: capitalism is not merely feasible but justThe positive case for capitalism is this: it is the system that takes human beings as they actually are — diverse in their values, their conceptions of the good, their preferred forms of association, and yes, their self-interest — and creates conditions in which they can cooperate, flourish, and build communities of their choosing without requiring agreement on a single vision of the good life. Socialism, for all its beauty as a vision, requires that vision to be shared. Capitalism requires only that rights be respected. And I believe that in a world of genuine moral and cultural diversity, the latter requirement is the more just one.

Thank you.

3.A system that permits more diverse forms of association is, other things being equal, more respectful of human plurality than one that imposes a single associative form.
∴On the criterion of respecting human diversity in communal life, capitalism is superior to socialism; it enables Cohen's camping trip rather than foreclosing it.

2.Self-interest, honestly declared and exercised within a framework of voluntary exchange and respect for rights, is not a vice — it is a form of honest, accountable social participation.
3.The market's coordination of self-interested behaviour produces outcomes — the reduction of poverty, the generation of wealth, the transmission of dispersed information — that serve others' interests just as effectively as altruistic motivation might.
∴The motivational objection to capitalism misidentifies self-interest as shameful; a more nuanced account recognises it as a legitimate and productive form of social participation.

2.Socialism, by contrast, requires the socialist distributive form to govern all significant economic relationships, preventing the preference of those who would choose different arrangements.
3.In conditions of genuine moral and cultural diversity, a system that permits many forms of community is more respectful of persons than one that imposes a single form.
∴On the value of community itself, capitalism is superior: it generates a world of many communities; socialism generates a single, mandatory one.

1.Where socialist norms have governed social life at scale through state imposition, the results have involved coercion, information failure, and the suppression of diverse communal life.
2.This pattern reflects the structural necessity of coercing defectors and imposing a contested conception of need — not merely contingent historical failures.
3.Voluntary socialist communities that function well (cooperatives, kibbutzim, communes) do so precisely because they operate within a capitalist framework that guarantees consent, exit, and property rights.
∴The historical record of imposed socialism confirms the theoretical argument: the conditions that make socialist norms work are conditions that capitalism provides and socialism undermines.

1.Capitalism is designed to work with human beings as they actually are — diverse in their values, motivations, conceptions of the good, and preferences for association.
2.Socialism, as Cohen acknowledges, requires either that people be transformed or that they be coerced; it is not designed for actual human beings but for an ideal type.
3.A political system that works for actual human beings, respects their diversity, and requires only that rights be respected is more just than one that requires a shared vision of the good that most people do not hold.
∴Capitalism is not merely more feasible but more just than socialism: it is the system adequate to morally serious, pluralist, imperfect human beings.
