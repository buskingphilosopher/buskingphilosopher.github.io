Leda and the Swan

W. B. Yeats — The Tower (1928)
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
Structure & Context
Form: A deformed Petrarchan sonnet — fourteen lines of loose iambic pentameter with an approximate ABAB CDCD EFG EFG rhyme scheme; but the volta is buried mid-line 11 rather than signalled at the octave break, ambushing the reader as the assault ambushes Leda Rhyme: Conspicuously impure throughout — push/rush and tower/power are half rhymes at structurally significant positions; the sonic irresolution of the poem's sound mirrors the irresolution of its central question, which is never answered Mythology: Zeus, king of the gods, takes the form of a swan to rape Leda; from the union are born Helen of Troy and Clytemnestra, and from Helen the Trojan War, and from Clytemnestra the murder of Agamemnon — an entire civilisation's catastrophe originating in this one moment Yeats's system: In A Vision (1925), Yeats presents the rape of Leda as the 'annunciation' initiating classical civilisation — as the Annunciation to Mary initiated Christian civilisation. Each historical cycle begins with a divine violence, a god entering human history through a woman's body The central question: 'Did she put on his knowledge with his power?' — whether the violated gains the vision of the violator — has generated more critical commentary than almost any other line in twentieth-century poetry; Yeats's own answer in A Vision is deliberately inconclusive The sonnet form: The love sonnet tradition is violated by its subject matter in the same way Leda is violated by Zeus; the Petrarchan form — invented for the idealisation of a distant, untouchable woman — is here applied to the opposite: a woman physically seized. The form's own conventions become part of the poem's argument

A sudden blow: the great wings beating still Above the staggering girl, her thighs caressed By the dark webs, her nape caught in his bill, He holds her helpless breast upon his breast. How can those terrified vague fingers push The feathered glory from her loosening thighs? And how can body, laid in that white rush, Feel the strange heart beating where it lies? A shudder in the loins engenders there The broken wall, the burning roof and tower And Agamemnon dead. Being so caught up, So mastered by the brute blood of the air, Did she put on his knowledge with his power Before the indifferent fingers let her drop?
