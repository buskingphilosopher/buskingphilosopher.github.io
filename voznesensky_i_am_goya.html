Poetic Analysis · Soviet Lyric · War Poetry in Translation

I am Goya

Andrei Voznesensky — "Goya" (1959), translated by Stanley Kunitz
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
Structure & Context
Form: Four short stanzas, each opening or closing on the identity-claim "I am" — Goya, grief, the tongue of war, hunger, the gullet, Goya again, and finally Goya once more. The Russian original is built on a dense web of alliteration on the consonant Г (g/G) — Гойя (Goya), Горе (grief), Голос (voice), Голод (hunger), Горло (gullet) — a phonetic chain the Kunitz translation cannot fully reproduce but which underlies the poem's incantatory, ritual repetition of "I am." Polyptoton: The Russian original ("Ya Goya... Ya gore... Ya golod... Ya gorlo") repeats words derived from the same explosive, guttural root sounds — Goya, grief, hunger, gullet all share a hard /g/ onset. This sonic device, largely lost in translation, mimics both the choking sounds of strangulation and the concussive thud of falling artillery, binding the poem's separate images into a single physical sensation of impact. Biographical context: Voznesensky was born in 1933 and was a child during the Second World War, evacuated with his mother to the Urals in 1941 — the year the poem repeatedly invokes. The poem was first published in 1959 and brought him early fame; Khrushchev would denounce him as a "bourgeois formalist" only a few years later for the kind of associative, image-driven technique this poem exemplifies. Goya as figure: Francisco Goya (1746–1828) produced "The Disasters of War," a series of etchings depicting the atrocities of the Spanish War of Independence with unflinching, nightmarish directness — mutilated bodies, executions, famine. Voznesensky does not write about Goya; he becomes Goya, claiming the artist's perspective as his own voice for documenting twentieth-century atrocity, collapsing two centuries and two wars into a single speaking "I." Anthropomorphism: The enemy is given "a beak" — war as a predatory bird stripped of human morality, a primal tearing beast — while the sky at the poem's close is made "unforgetting," granted a memory it must carry forever. Living, sensing qualities are pushed onto the abstract and the inanimate throughout, so that war and the cosmos itself become actors in the poem rather than backdrops to it. 1941: The year of Operation Barbarossa, the Nazi invasion of the Soviet Union — the beginning of what Russia calls the Great Patriotic War, in which an estimated 27 million Soviet citizens died. "The snows of the year 1941" situates the poem's atrocity specifically within this catastrophe, the deadliest front of the Second World War, fought substantially on Soviet soil and in Soviet winters. Translation note: Stanley Kunitz's English version is itself a celebrated act of poetic recreation rather than literal transcription — Kunitz, a major American poet in his own right, worked from versions and consultations rather than fluent Russian, and the English text is closer to a collaborative re-composition than a crib. "Grapes of wrath" is understood to be Kunitz's own addition, importing an American biblical-apocalyptic register into a Russian poem about a Spanish painter and a German invasion. The closing image: "Hammered stars into the unforgetting sky – like nails" fuses three registers in one image: the stars as literal night sky over the 1941 front; the stars of Soviet iconography (the red star); and the crucifixion (nails), making the act of memorialisation itself an act of violence — the sky is wounded by the very stars that are meant to commemorate the dead, and the sky's memory is permanent ("unforgetting") because the wound is permanent.

I am Goya of the bare field, by the enemy's beak gouged till the craters of my eyes gape I am grief I am the tongue of war, the embers of cities on the snows of the year 1941 I am hunger I am the gullet of a woman hanged whose body like a bell tolled over a blank square I am Goya O grapes of wrath! I have hurled westward the ashes of the uninvited guest and hammered stars into the unforgetting sky – like nails I am Goya
