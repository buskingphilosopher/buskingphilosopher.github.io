Preference Utilitarianism & the Person CriterionPeter Singer — Princeton • Practical Ethics, 1979 / 1993 / 2011
The sanctity of human life, simply as human life, has no defensible secular foundation. What matters morally is personhood — the capacity for self-consciousness, future-directed preferences, and rational agency. A being with these capacities has a strong preference to continue existing; killing them without consent thwarts that preference and is deeply wrong. But where that preference is absent or is itself a preference to die, the case against euthanasia collapses. Voluntary euthanasia for a competent person who prefers death over continued suffering is not only permissible but may be morally required by beneficence.

Logical Arguments

The debate about euthanasia is, at its core, a debate about what makes a human life valuable and what makes its ending wrong. The opposition rests on the doctrine of the sanctity of human life — the claim that human life, simply as such, has inviolable worth that places an absolute prohibition on intentionally ending it. I want to show that this doctrine, outside a religious framework that most participants in this debate do not share, has no defensible philosophical foundation. And once it is abandoned, the case against voluntary euthanasia collapses.

What makes killing wrong? Not the bare biological fact of death. We do not think it is wrong to kill a human being who is permanently and irreversibly unconscious, or indeed a human organism in a persistent vegetative state, in the way that we think it wrong to kill a person who is fully conscious, self-aware, and has a rich future ahead of them. The reason killing is typically wrong is that it deprives a being of a future that it has strong reason to value, and specifically of the continuation of a life that contains experiences, activities, and projects that that being desires to continue. It frustrates a preference — the most fundamental preference a self-conscious being has.

Singer’s taxonomy: persons, conscious beings, and the right to life
Person (in the morally relevant sense): a self-conscious being, aware of itself as existing over time, with future-directed preferences including the preference to continue existing.
Merely conscious being: sentient, capable of pleasure and pain, but without self-consciousness or future-directed preferences. Interests in current experiences, but no interest in continued existence as such.
Right to life: grounded in the preference to continue living. Only persons, in the above sense, have a right to life in the full sense. Killing a person against their will is seriously wrong because it thwarts this preference. Killing a person at their informed, competent request — when they prefer death to continued suffering — does not thwart but fulfils their preferences.
Implication: voluntary euthanasia for a competent person who genuinely prefers death does not wrong them. It respects their preference-based right over their own life. Withholding it, when death is what they want, wrongs them by forcing on them a mode of existence they have determined is worse than death.
i. the speciesist foundation of the sanctity doctrine

Speciesism — sanctity of human life privileges species membership; this is morally analogous to racism; what matters is personhood not speciesThe sanctity of life doctrine, as ordinarily stated, holds that every human life has inviolable value simply in virtue of being human. But this is a form of what I have called speciesism — the arbitrary privileging of the interests of one species over those of other beings with equivalent or greater morally relevant capacities. We do not extend the right to life to all members of the species Homo sapiens regardless of their capacities: we do not think it seriously wrong to disconnect a permanently vegetative human being from life support. Nor do we deny any protection to self-conscious non-human animals. The morally relevant distinction is not species membership but the presence of capacities — self-consciousness, future-directed preferences, rational agency — that give an individual a stake in its own continued existence. The sanctity doctrine draws the wrong line, and it does so for reasons that are, at bottom, no more defensible than the reasons for drawing the line at race or sex.

ii. voluntary euthanasia fulfils rather than violates the preference-based right to life

Preference fulfilment — voluntary euthanasia fulfils the dying person’s most fundamental preference; the right to life is not a duty to live; competent choice to die must be respectedOpponents of euthanasia often invoke the right to life as if it were a duty to live. But a right is something one can waive. The right to life protects an individual’s preference to continue living against those who would end it; it is not a prohibition on choosing to end one’s own life when that is one’s considered, competent preference. A terminally ill person in uncontrollable pain who has decided, after careful reflection, that they wish to die is not having their right to life violated when a physician assists them. They are having their most fundamental preference about their own existence respected. To refuse them this assistance — to force them to continue living against their will — is the violation of autonomy, not the assistance.

iii. the argument from consistency with animal ethics

Animal consistency — we regard euthanising suffering animals as compassionate; if the relevant criterion is suffering and not species membership, this should apply to humans tooThere is a revealing asymmetry in the way our society treats the suffering of animals and the suffering of humans at the end of life. When a beloved pet is suffering from terminal illness — in pain, with no prospect of improvement — we regard euthanasia as the compassionate, indeed the morally required, response. To allow the animal to continue suffering when death could be quick and painless would strike us as cruel. But when a human being is in equivalent circumstances — suffering, terminal, requesting death — we deny them the same compassion. This inconsistency can only be explained if we think there is something about being human, over and above suffering and preference, that requires continued life. But I have shown that no such principle is defensible outside a religious framework. The consistency argument strongly supports permissibility.

iv. quality of life and the utilitarian case for permissibility

Quality of life — classical utilitarianism also supports permissibility where life contains more suffering than wellbeing; both preference and classical utilitarianism converge on permissibilityEven those who resist preference utilitarianism and work within a classical utilitarian framework that focuses on the balance of pleasure and pain should reach the same conclusion in paradigm euthanasia cases. A life consisting of uncontrollable pain, loss of dignity, and no prospect of recovery is a life with a negative balance of welfare. Continuing such a life, all else being equal, increases the total amount of suffering in the world. Ending it, quickly and painlessly, reduces it. The utilitarian case — on either the preference or the classical version — converges strongly on the permissibility of voluntary euthanasia in such cases. The opposition has no utilitarian answer to this; their case rests almost entirely on the deontological claim that human life is intrinsically inviolable, and I have shown that claim to be without secular foundation.

The sanctity doctrine fails. Personhood and preference are what matter. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The sanctity of human life doctrine is speciesist; what grounds the right to life is personhood, not species membership
1.Speciesism: privileging beings on the basis of species membership alone, without regard to morally relevant capacities, is analogous to racism and sexism — it draws the moral line in the wrong place.
2.The sanctity of life doctrine protects all human beings qua human, regardless of the presence of self-consciousness, future-directed preferences, or any other morally relevant capacity. This is precisely the speciesist error.
3.The morally relevant distinction is personhood — self-consciousness and future-directed preferences. Beings with these capacities have a strong interest in continued existence; beings without them do not have the same interest, whatever their species.
∴The sanctity doctrine rests on an arbitrary species preference that cannot be given secular justification; it should be replaced with a personhood criterion that both explains why killing persons is wrong and why voluntary euthanasia for a person who prefers death is permissible.
Argument II — The right to life is a waivable preference right; voluntary euthanasia fulfils rather than violates it
1.The right to life is grounded in the preference to continue living. It protects that preference from being overridden by others. It is not a duty to live against one’s own preferences.
2.A competent person who has considered their situation and prefers death to continued suffering has, in effect, waived their right to life’s protection. Assisting them fulfils their preference; refusing them violates their autonomy.
3.Forcing continued life on a person who prefers death — who has determined that the balance of their existence is negative — is itself a serious wrong, a violation of the most fundamental domain of self-determination.
∴Voluntary euthanasia does not wrong the patient; it respects their preference-based authority over their own existence; the prohibition wrongs them by imposing continued life against their competent, considered preference.
Argument III — The animal consistency argument: euthanising suffering animals is compassionate; species membership cannot justify the asymmetry
1.We regard euthanasia for suffering, terminally ill animals as the compassionate and morally required response. To deny it would be cruelty.
2.We deny equivalent relief to suffering, terminally ill humans who request it. This asymmetry can only be justified if species membership confers a special obligation to prolong life regardless of suffering.
3.Species membership, without an accompanying claim about personhood or some other morally relevant property, cannot bear this weight. The speciesist asymmetry is indefensible.
∴The animal consistency argument exposes an indefensible asymmetry in our current practice; applying the same compassion standard consistently across sentient beings strongly supports the permissibility of voluntary euthanasia for suffering humans.
Argument IV — Classical and preference utilitarianism converge on permissibility in paradigm euthanasia cases
1.Classical utilitarianism: a life consisting wholly of suffering with no prospect of recovery has a negative welfare balance. Ending it reduces overall suffering. Utility favours permissibility.
2.Preference utilitarianism: the dying person has a preference to die; fulfilling that preference increases preference satisfaction; refusing it frustrates their most fundamental preference. Both versions converge.
3.The opposition’s case rests on the deontological claim that human life is intrinsically inviolable. That claim requires either a religious foundation or a secular account of special human dignity neither of which has been successfully specified.
∴Both versions of utilitarianism support permissibility in paradigm cases; the opposition’s only recourse is a deontological sanctity-of-life claim that lacks defensible secular grounding; the utilitarian convergence strongly favours permissibility.

I want to offer the deepest philosophical grounding for the permissibility of euthanasia available in the contemporary literature: an account grounded in personal identity, in what we are, and in what the wrongness of killing actually consists of. My argument in The Ethics of Killing is that the wrongness of killing derives from the frustration of what I call time-relative interests — and that this account, properly understood, not only permits but in some cases requires that we assist in the dying of those whose time-relative interest in continued life has been extinguished by their condition.

The standard objection to euthanasia says: death is a harm because it deprives the person of all the future good they would otherwise have experienced. This is true for paradigm cases of killing. But it relies on a particular account of what we are and how we are connected to our futures. I want to examine that account carefully, because when we do, we find that the deprivation argument for the wrongness of killing actually supports rather than opposes the permissibility of euthanasia in the relevant cases.

The time-relative interest account and its application to euthanasia
Standard deprivation account: death is bad for a person because it deprives them of all future good. The wrongness of killing = the magnitude of this deprivation.
McMahan’s refinement: the wrongness of killing derives from frustrating time-relative interests — interests whose strength is modulated by the psychological connections between the person now and the person who would have had the future goods. Strong psychological connection = strong time-relative interest in continued life = killing is seriously wrong. Weak psychological connection (as in severe dementia, terminal illness, permanent vegetative state) = weaker time-relative interest = killing is less seriously wrong, or may not be wrong at all.
Application: a terminally ill patient in unremitting suffering, whose psychological connections to any valuable future are severed by their condition, has a weak or absent time-relative interest in continued life. They may, however, have a strong time-relative interest in the cessation of suffering. Euthanasia, on the time-relative interest account, serves rather than frustrates their most significant remaining interest.
i. what we are and how we are connected to our futures

Personal identity — we are embodied minds; psychological continuity determines how strongly connected we are to our futures; this modulates the wrongness of killingI defend the view that we are essentially embodied minds rather than biological organisms or immaterial souls. Our identity over time consists in psychological continuity — the overlapping chains of memory, intention, belief, desire, and character that connect our past, present, and future selves. These connections are what make the future goods that we will experience genuinely ours in the morally relevant sense. When those psychological connections are strong — when there is a richly continuous psychological life stretching into a valuable future — killing is seriously wrong because it severs a person from a future that is deeply theirs. When those connections are weak or absent — when the condition has already severed the psychological continuity that constitutes personal identity in the relevant sense — the wrongness of killing is correspondingly diminished.

ii. the time-relative interest in terminal illness

Terminal illness — psychological connections to a valuable future are severed; time-relative interest in continued life is weak; time-relative interest in ending suffering is strong; euthanasia serves the stronger interestConsider a patient with terminal cancer in the final weeks of life. Their condition has severed most of the psychological connections that ordinarily ground a strong time-relative interest in continued life. Their future self — the self that would exist in six months had they lived — is not richly psychologically continuous with who they are now. They cannot coherently project themselves into that future or identify strongly with the person they would become. What they do have is an immediate, powerful, present-tense interest in the cessation of suffering. The time-relative interest account implies that euthanasia in this case does not wrong the patient by depriving them of a future that was significantly theirs. It serves their most pressing remaining interest. This is not merely permissible; on a beneficence account, it is required.

iii. responding to the personal identity objection

Identity objection — some argue that future persons whose lives we end are sufficiently psychologically continuous that euthanasia wrongs them; McMahan’s account shows this is weaker in terminal casesThe most serious objection to my account is that even terminal patients retain sufficient psychological continuity with their future selves to ground a significant time-relative interest in continued life. I have two responses. First, even if some psychological continuity remains, the strength of that continuity in severe terminal cases is dramatically less than in normal life, and this is enough to show that the wrongness of killing in such cases is correspondingly less — less than the wrong of allowing the patient to continue suffering. The balance of interests favours euthanasia. Second, where the patient has a competent, considered preference to die, this preference itself is evidence that the patient does not strongly identify with their future continuing self in the way that grounds a strong time-relative interest in continuing to live. Their preference and my theoretical account converge.

iv. the case of dementia and the non-identity challenge

Dementia — advance directives for euthanasia raise non-identity problems; McMahan’s account provides resources for addressing them by focusing on psychological continuity at the time of deathMy account also illuminates the hardest cases: patients with advanced dementia who had previously expressed a wish to die rather than live in that condition. Kamm and others have pressed the problem of whether an advance directive from a psychologically discontinuous earlier self can bind the person who now exists. My account addresses this directly: the person who now exists in advanced dementia is not psychologically continuous, in the relevant sense, with the person who made the advance directive — and yet the patient now before us has a weak time-relative interest in continued life because they have few future-directed preferences or psychological connections to a valuable future. This independently weakens the case against euthanasia for the demented patient, even setting aside the advance directive. The account supports permissibility from multiple directions simultaneously.

The time-relative interest account supports permissibility. I yield the floor.

Argument I — We are embodied minds; psychological continuity modulates the strength of the time-relative interest in continued life
1.We are essentially embodied minds, not biological organisms. Our identity over time consists in psychological continuity — overlapping chains of memory, intention, belief, desire, and character connecting past, present, and future selves.
2.Time-relative interests: the strength of an individual’s interest in a future good is modulated by the strength of their psychological connections to the future self who would experience it. Strong connections = strong interest; weak connections = weak interest.
3.The wrongness of killing derives from frustrating time-relative interests in continued life. When those interests are strong, killing is seriously wrong. When they are weak or absent, the wrongness is correspondingly reduced.
∴The wrongness of killing is not uniform across all cases of killing persons; it varies with the strength of the time-relative interest in continued life, which is determined by the degree of psychological continuity between the person now and their future self.
Argument II — Terminal illness severs psychological connections; the time-relative interest in continued life is weak; euthanasia serves the stronger interest in ending suffering
1.Terminal illness severs the psychological connections that ground a strong time-relative interest in continued life: the patient cannot coherently project themselves into a rich future, identify with the person they would become, or form strong future-directed preferences.
2.What remains is a strong present-tense interest in the cessation of suffering. On the time-relative interest account, this interest is not outweighed by the weakened interest in continued life.
3.Euthanasia serves the patient’s most pressing remaining interest. It does not wrong them by depriving them of a future that was significantly theirs; their condition has already severed their connection to that future.
∴In paradigm terminal cases, euthanasia does not wrong the patient on the time-relative interest account; it serves their strongest remaining interest and frustrates only a weakened interest in continued life that has been severed by their condition.
Argument III — Residual psychological continuity in terminal cases does not restore the full wrongness of killing; the interest balance still favours euthanasia
1.Objection: even terminal patients retain some psychological continuity with their future selves, so some time-relative interest in continued life remains.
2.Response: the strength of that continuity in severe terminal cases is dramatically less than in normal life; even if some wrongness attaches to killing, it is less than the wrong of forcing continued suffering on the patient.
3.The patient’s competent preference to die is itself evidence that they do not strongly identify with their continuing future self — evidence that corroborates the theoretical account rather than conflicting with it.
∴Even if residual psychological continuity generates some time-relative interest in continued life, this is outweighed by the strong interest in ending suffering; the balance of interests supports permissibility independently of the patient’s expressed preference.
Argument IV — The account illuminates dementia cases: advance directives and weak time-relative interests both independently support permissibility
1.Advanced dementia patients lack strong psychological continuity with any valuable future; their time-relative interest in continued life is weak independently of any advance directive.
2.An advance directive from an earlier, psychologically continuous self provides additional grounds for permissibility, though the non-identity problem (whether the earlier self can bind the present patient) requires careful handling.
3.The account supports permissibility in dementia cases from two independent directions: the weak time-relative interest of the current patient, and the prior competent preference expressed in the advance directive. These converge rather than conflict.
∴The time-relative interest account supports permissibility in both terminal and dementia cases; its resources for handling advance directives and non-identity challenges are richer than any competing account, making it the most philosophically defensible foundation for the permissibility of euthanasia.

My contribution to this debate is methodological as well as substantive. I want to show, through a series of carefully constructed four-step arguments, that the opposition cannot maintain a consistent position that permits some forms of assistance in dying — which nearly everyone already accepts — while prohibiting voluntary active euthanasia. Each step moves from a practice the opposition already accepts to a practice that is not morally distinguishable from it. By the end of four steps, we have arrived at the permissibility of voluntary active euthanasia. If the opposition wants to resist, they must reject one of the steps. But each step is either already accepted medical practice or follows from principles the opposition cannot plausibly deny.

Kamm’s four-step argument from accepted practice to VE.

Step 1 (accepted): Physicians may withdraw life-sustaining treatment at the patient’s competent request. We accept that passive euthanasia, at patient request, is permissible.

Step 2 (accepted): Physicians may administer terminal sedation — sedation to unconsciousness — at patient request, even where this foreseeably shortens life. We accept a form of intentional action that foreseeably causes death.

Step 3 (accepted): Physicians may administer pain relief in doses that foreseeably hasten death, under the doctrine of double effect. We accept intentional beneficial action whose foreseen side-effect is death.

Step 4 (contested but entailed): None of Steps 1–3 can be consistently distinguished, on principled grounds, from direct administration of a lethal agent at patient request. The same patient, with the same preference to die, the same suffering, and the same physician intent to benefit them: the only difference is the causal mechanism. Kamm argues no morally relevant distinction between these mechanisms can be specified.
i. the failure of the acts/omissions distinction

Acts/omissions — withdrawing treatment is an act not a mere omission; the distinction does not track what the opposition needs it to track; the four-step argument crosses itThe opposition standardly relies on the acts/omissions distinction to justify permitting withdrawal of treatment while prohibiting active euthanasia. But this distinction does not hold under examination. Withdrawing a ventilator is an act — a positive physical intervention — not a mere omission. And if we follow Rachels’ bare difference argument, the same patient dying after ventilator withdrawal and the same patient dying after a lethal injection, with all other factors held constant, are morally equivalent cases. The acts/omissions distinction does not carve nature at its moral joints. If it did, terminal sedation — which is clearly an act — would already be impermissible on the opposition’s principles. They cannot rely on this distinction without inconsistency.

ii. the doctrine of double effect and its limits

DDE — permits foreseen but unintended death as side-effect; Kamm argues the intention/foresight distinction is insufficient to bear the moral weight the opposition places on it; triple effect doctrineThe opposition invokes the doctrine of double effect to permit pain relief that hastens death, arguing that death is foreseen but not intended. Kamm has done more than any other philosopher to map the precise conditions under which the doctrine holds and to expose its limits. Her doctrine of triple effect argues that in some cases we may permissibly intend a lesser harm in order to avoid a greater one, even when the resulting death was neither a mere foreseen side-effect nor a case where death was the means. More importantly, the DDE, properly understood, already permits a great deal that approaches active euthanasia in its practical effect. The opposition cannot invoke DDE to licence terminal sedation and simultaneous withdrawal of nutrition — which is functionally equivalent to causing death — while prohibiting direct lethal injection. The distinction is one of mechanism, not of moral substance.

iii. the problem of physician assistance versus patient self-administration

PAS vs VE — many opponents accept PAS but reject VE; Kamm argues the distinction between self-administration and physician administration carries no independent moral weight given the same intent and outcomeMany opponents of voluntary active euthanasia accept physician-assisted suicide — where the physician provides the means and the patient administers it themselves. But this distinction carries no independent moral weight. The same patient, the same preference, the same physician intention to benefit, the same lethal outcome: the only difference is whether the patient or the physician performs the final physical act. If the physician’s assistance is already permissible — because what matters is the patient’s competent preference and the physician’s beneficent intent — then the final physical act of administration cannot be the moral watershed. The opposition who accepts PAS but rejects VE is drawing a line at precisely the point that has no moral weight, and they owe an account of why the last causal link in an otherwise identical chain changes the moral character of the whole action.

iv. consistency and the demand for principled distinctions

Consistency — the opposition must identify a principled distinction between what it permits and what it prohibits; no such distinction survives scrutiny; the four-step argument succeedsMy overall argument is a demand for consistency. The opposition accepts withdrawal of treatment, terminal sedation, DDE-licenced hastening of death, and in some cases physician-assisted suicide. I ask them to specify, at each transition, what principled moral distinction separates these accepted practices from voluntary active euthanasia. No such distinction has survived philosophical scrutiny. The killing/letting-die distinction fails for the reasons Rachels establishes. The acts/omissions distinction fails because withdrawal is an act. The intention/foresight distinction fails because terminal sedation is intentionally lethal in effect. The self-administration distinction fails for the reasons I have just given. If no principled distinction can be drawn, the only consistent positions are: prohibit everything including treatment withdrawal, or permit everything including VE. The opposition has already committed to permitting treatment withdrawal. Consistency requires permitting VE.

No principled distinction survives. The four-step argument succeeds. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Withdrawing treatment is an act; the acts/omissions distinction does not justify permitting it while prohibiting VE
1.Withdrawing a ventilator or feeding tube is a positive physical intervention — an act — not a mere omission. The acts/omissions distinction does not map onto the permitted/prohibited line the opposition needs.
2.Rachels’ bare difference argument: a case differing from another only in that one is a killing and the other a letting-die, with all morally relevant factors held constant, is morally equivalent. This applies to withdrawal vs. lethal injection.
3.The opposition cannot consistently invoke the acts/omissions distinction to permit treatment withdrawal while prohibiting VE without specifying what moral difference the distinction tracks in these cases. No such specification has been given.
∴The acts/omissions distinction fails to justify permitting treatment withdrawal while prohibiting VE; the opposition is committed to a principled distinction it cannot specify; the four-step argument begins by undermining this foundational asymmetry.
Argument II — The doctrine of double effect already licences actions functionally equivalent to VE; it cannot draw the line the opposition needs
1.DDE permits pain relief that foreseeably hastens death, and terminal sedation combined with withdrawal of nutrition — both of which are functionally equivalent to causing death, differing only in mechanism and speed.
2.If DDE licences these practices, the intention/foresight distinction it relies on cannot simultaneously prohibit direct lethal injection with the same intent (to benefit the patient) and the same outcome (death).
3.Kamm’s doctrine of triple effect shows that even the DDE’s framework has wider scope than its defenders admit; the intention/foresight distinction is insufficient to bear the moral weight the prohibition requires.
∴The DDE, applied consistently, already licences actions whose moral structure is identical to VE; the intention/foresight distinction cannot sustain a prohibition on VE that it does not also impose on terminal sedation and DDE-licenced hastening of death.
Argument III — The PAS/VE distinction carries no independent moral weight; physician administration vs. self-administration is not a moral watershed
1.Many opponents of VE accept PAS. But in PAS and VE: same patient, same preference, same physician intent to benefit, same lethal outcome. The only difference is whether the patient or the physician performs the final physical act.
2.If what grounds permissibility is the patient’s competent preference and the physician’s beneficent intent — as the opposition concedes in accepting PAS — then the final physical act of administration cannot change the moral character of the whole action.
3.The opposition who draws the line at self-administration owes an account of why this last causal link is a moral watershed when everything else is identical. No such account has been successfully given.
∴The PAS/VE distinction carries no independent moral weight; opponents who accept PAS are committed by consistency to accepting VE; the last causal link in an otherwise identical moral chain is not a moral watershed.
Argument IV — Consistency requires either prohibiting everything or permitting VE; the opposition has already committed to permitting treatment withdrawal
1.The four-step argument: treatment withdrawal → terminal sedation → DDE-licenced hastening of death → PAS → VE. At each transition the moral distinction required by the opposition has been shown to fail.
2.The only consistent positions are: prohibit everything (including treatment withdrawal, which nearly no one accepts) or permit everything including VE. The opposition has already committed to step 1.
3.No principled distinction has survived scrutiny at any of the intermediate steps. The four-step argument from accepted practice to VE succeeds.
∴The four-step argument succeeds: the opposition is committed by their own accepted practices to permitting voluntary active euthanasia; the distinctions they need to resist the conclusion — acts/omissions, killing/letting-die, intention/foresight, PAS/VE — all fail under scrutiny.

My 1975 paper in the New England Journal of Medicine advanced a single, focused argument: there is no intrinsic moral difference between killing and letting die. I want to restate that argument with full precision, because it has been misunderstood in ways that have allowed the opposition to evade its force. The argument is not that killing is always as permissible as letting die. It is that the bare difference between killing and letting die — considered in isolation from all other morally relevant factors — carries no moral weight of its own. Whether a particular killing or letting-die is permissible depends on those other factors. And once we see this, the standard defence of passive euthanasia while prohibiting active euthanasia collapses.

The Bare Difference Argument: Smith and Jones.

Smith drowns his young cousin in the bath in order to inherit the family estate. He waits until the child slips, then pushes the child’s head under water. Smith has killed the child.

Jones intends to do exactly the same thing. He goes to the bathroom ready to drown the child. But the child slips and falls face-first into the water. Jones stands by and watches the child drown, ready to push the child’s head under if necessary. Jones has merely let the child die.

Rachels’ claim: both men acted from exactly the same motive (greed), both intended exactly the same end (the child’s death), both would have produced exactly the same result. The only difference is that one killed and the other let die. If we think Jones is less culpable than Smith simply because he let rather than killed, we are giving the bare difference moral weight it does not deserve. Smith and Jones are equally culpable.
i. the argument stated precisely

Bare difference argument — Smith kills / Jones lets die / same motive same outcome same intent; if Jones is less culpable the bare difference is doing the work; but it is notThe Smith and Jones case is a controlled thought experiment. We hold constant: motive (greed), intent (the child’s death), outcome (the child’s death), relationship to the child (cousin), and every other potentially morally relevant factor. The only variable is whether the person killed or let die. If there is a moral difference between Smith and Jones, it must be attributed to the bare killing/letting-die difference. But I contend there is no moral difference — both are equally culpable. Therefore the bare difference carries no independent moral weight. The patient is the same; the prognosis is the same; the outcome is the same; the intent is the same. We add only that the active case is typically faster and therefore less distressing. I want to approach the permissibility of euthanasia from a direction that has been underemphasised in this debate: not from abstract principles of autonomy or the logic of the killing/letting-die distinction, but from the concrete reality of dying. What actually happens to people at the end of life? What deaths are available to them? And which of those deaths is least bad? Once we ask these questions — once we look at the phenomenology of dying rather than the theoretical structure of rights and duties — the case for permissibility becomes not just philosophically sound but morally urgent.

I have argued throughout my career that the right framework for end-of-life ethics is what I call the “least worst death” framework. Everyone must die. The question is not whether death will occur but which available death is least bad for the particular person in the particular circumstances. Medicine already operates within this framework when it chooses palliative over curative treatment, or when it selects among modes of terminal care. Euthanasia is simply the extension of this existing medical logic to its most direct form.

i. the least worst death framework

Least worst death — everyone must die; the question is which available death is least bad; euthanasia is the natural extension of existing medical logic; the framework is already implicit in palliative careThe least worst death framework asks: given that this patient will die, and given the range of deaths that are medically available to them, which is the least harmful? This question is not unusual in medicine. When a physician switches from curative to palliative care, they are making exactly this judgment. When they choose terminal sedation over continued aggressive treatment, they are choosing a better death over a worse one. When they administer opioids in doses they know may hasten death, they are trading some life-extension for death-quality. What I am arguing is that voluntary active euthanasia is the most direct and most effective instrument for achieving the least worst death in cases where other means cannot. Its prohibition forces patients toward worse deaths — slower, more painful, more prolonged — in the name of a principle that the rest of medicine already implicitly rejects.

ii. the duty argument: physicians may be obligated to assist

Duty to assist — in some cases physician has not just permission but positive duty to assist in dying; continued life is a harm; beneficence requires ending it; the Hippocratic tradition misconstrues the physician’s roleI want to go further than most participants in this debate and argue that in some cases, physicians have not merely a permission but a positive duty to assist in dying. When continued life consists only of suffering — when there is no meaningful palliative alternative, when the patient’s preferences are clear, and when death would be a benefit — a physician who refuses to assist is not maintaining neutrality. They are choosing to impose a harm on their patient. The Hippocratic injunction “do no harm” is not an argument against euthanasia; it is an argument for it, in the cases where continued life is the harm. The physician who stands by while a patient suffers an avoidable death has violated their most fundamental professional obligation as surely as the physician who over-treats.

Global comparison — deaths in countries with legal euthanasia show no slippery slope; suffering at end of life is a policy choice not inevitable; the empirical evidence dismantles the opposition’s fearsI have spent decades examining how end-of-life decisions are made across different healthcare systems and legal regimes. The empirical evidence is consistent: in jurisdictions that have legalised physician assistance in dying — the Netherlands, Belgium, Oregon — the slippery-slope fears of the opposition have not been realised. Rates of involuntary euthanasia have not increased. Vulnerable populations have not been systematically targeted. What has happened is that patients with terminal diagnoses have a form of control and assurance that those in prohibiting jurisdictions lack. The suffering at the end of life that the opposition presents as the inevitable cost of humanity’s finiteness is in significant part a policy choice — the choice to prohibit the most effective means of preventing it.

I argue that the least worst death framework extends to these cases. If death is not a deprivation of a future that the patient values — if the patient genuinely regards continued life as not adding to their wellbeing — then the case for permissibility is not defeated by the absence of terminal illness. This is the frontier of the debate. The philosophical resources to address it are already present in the arguments we have made; they need only to be applied consistently.

I want to make the case for what I regard as the most defensible foundation for a right to assisted death: not bare autonomy, not preference satisfaction in the abstract, but the concrete well-being of the individual patient. My argument in Assisted Death is that a right to assisted dying follows from a welfare-based account of the wrongness of killing, combined with a rich account of what well-being actually consists in. If what makes killing wrong is that it deprives a person of future well-being, then when continued living makes a person worse off — all things considered, from their own subjective standpoint — the strongest argument against killing no longer applies.

The welfare argument is stronger than both the autonomy argument and the preference argument for one important reason: it is not vulnerable to the objection that the patient’s preferences have been distorted by depression, fear, or inadequate palliative care. A welfare-based right to die requires not just a preference to die but a grounded judgment that continued life is genuinely worse than death for this particular individual. This is a more demanding standard — but it is also a more convincing foundation.

i. the welfare account of the wrongness of killing

Welfare account — killing is wrong primarily because it deprives the person of future well-being; where continued life does not add to well-being, the primary argument against killing loses forceThe wrongness of killing derives primarily from its deprivation effect: killing a person takes away from them the future well-being they would otherwise have had. This is why killing persons is typically one of the most serious wrongs: persons typically have rich futures full of experiences, relationships, and projects that contribute substantially to their well-being. But the wrongness of killing, on this account, is not uniform. It varies with the quality and quantity of the future well-being that killing forecloses. When a person’s remaining future consists only of suffering — when continued living, all things considered, makes them worse off rather than better — the deprivation argument against killing loses its force. We cannot be depriving a person of future well-being if their future holds only suffering.

ii. well-being, not mere preference, as the foundation

Well-being vs preference — a welfare-based right is more robust than a preference-based one; it is not vulnerable to the distorted-preference objection; it requires a grounded welfare judgmentThe autonomy and preference arguments for assisted dying are vulnerable to an objection from the opposition: the dying patient’s preference to die may be distorted by depression, inadequate pain management, fear, or social pressure. If preferences can be so distorted, a preference-based right to die is only as reliable as our ability to assess whether the preference is genuinely autonomous. My welfare-based account is more robust. It requires not merely that the patient prefers death but that their preference reflects a grounded and accurate assessment of their own well-being — that continued life is genuinely, and not merely apparently, worse than death for this person. This is a more demanding standard, but it is also one that more securely grounds the right to die in something that matters morally rather than in the mere psychological fact of preference.

iii. the safeguards question

Safeguards — the welfare account naturally generates the safeguards the opposition demands; it requires assessment of actual welfare not mere preference; it is compatible with robust procedural protectionA welfare-based account of assisted dying naturally generates the safeguards that the opposition rightly demands. If the right to die is grounded in a welfare judgment, then the assessment of that judgment — whether continued life genuinely diminishes well-being for this patient — is a proper medical and philosophical inquiry, not just an exercise in preference satisfaction. This implies: independent medical assessment of the prognosis; evaluation of whether pain management and palliative care could improve the welfare balance; psychological assessment to ensure the judgment is not distorted by depression; and confirmation that the request is persistent and informed. These safeguards are not ad hoc concessions to the opposition; they are internal requirements of the welfare account itself. A right to die grounded in welfare is a more responsible and more carefully specified right than one grounded in bare autonomy.

This is the Epicurean claim — that death is not bad for the person who dies because there is no longer anyone there to be harmed. My response is that the welfare argument for assisted dying is not primarily about the state of being dead but about the process of dying. The patient does not request euthanasia in order to benefit from being dead; they request it to avoid the suffering of a protracted dying process. The welfare comparison is between the suffering of continued living and the relief of a quick death. This comparison involves only experiences the patient will actually have — it does not require us to attribute positive welfare to the state of death itself.

I want to begin by defending something that has been too quickly dismissed in the proposition’s speeches: the moral significance of the doing/allowing distinction. This distinction is not, as Rachels claims, a morally inert relic of confused thinking. It tracks a real and important difference in the moral structure of actions — the difference between negative duties, which we owe to everyone and which are typically strong, and positive duties, which we may owe to specific individuals and which are typically weaker. This difference is foundational to moral thought, and it survives the Bare Difference Argument.

Rachels’ Smith and Jones case is not a counterexample to the doing/allowing distinction. It is a case where both characters violate a duty — Smith the negative duty not to kill, Jones the positive duty not to let a child under his care drown. What the case shows is that both types of duty can generate strong obligations; it does not show that the distinction between them is without moral significance. A drowning stranger who I could rescue but do not rescue is a very different case from a person I deliberately drown. Both may be morally criticisable, but they are not criticisable in the same way or on the same grounds.

Foot’s framework: negative duties, positive duties, and euthanasia
Negative duty: a duty not to do something — not to kill, not to harm. Owed to everyone. Very strong. Difficult to override.
Positive duty: a duty to do something — to preserve life, to assist, to rescue. Owed in specific relationships or circumstances. Weaker; can be outweighed by competing considerations.
Application to euthanasia: The prohibition on killing is a negative duty. The duty to preserve life is a positive duty. Foot argues that even where euthanasia would benefit the patient — even where the welfare case is established — the negative duty not to kill must be weighed and can override the positive duty to benefit.
What makes active euthanasia different: it requires the physician to perform an act that kills the patient. This places it under the prohibition of the negative duty not to kill — a prohibition that cannot be suspended simply because death would benefit the patient. The structure of the duty, not just its consequences, matters.
i. the doing/allowing distinction is morally real

Doing/allowing distinction — negative duties not to kill are stronger than positive duties to save; the distinction tracks a real moral asymmetry; Rachels’ case does not refute itThe doing/allowing distinction tracks the difference between being the initiating cause of harm and failing to prevent harm that is already in train. When I push someone off a cliff, I initiate a harmful causal sequence. When I walk past a drowning person without helping, I fail to intervene in a harmful sequence already in progress. These are morally different in ways that matter: the first imposes on me a much stronger obligation to justify my action; it makes me the author of the harm in a way that failing to prevent does not. This is not a merely formal distinction. It connects to deep facts about agency, responsibility, and what we owe one another. The negative duty not to kill — not to initiate the fatal causal sequence — is near-absolute in a way that the positive duty to preserve life is not. Active euthanasia involves initiating the fatal sequence. Letting die involves allowing a sequence already in progress to continue. They are not morally equivalent.

The welfare case is a necessary condition for permissibility. But it is not sufficient. The negative duty not to kill also applies, and this duty is not automatically suspended because killing would produce good consequences. If it were, the prohibition on killing would collapse into a consequentialist calculation, and we would be obligated to kill anyone whose death would produce a welfare benefit — an implication no one accepts. The negative duty not to kill imposes a constraint on what we may do even in pursuit of good outcomes, and that constraint is not dissolved by the patient’s consent or by the welfare benefit to the patient. Consent does not transform killing from prohibited to permitted simply by altering the victim’s preference.

My account is therefore more nuanced than Singer presents it. I am not a simple sanctity-of-life absolutist. A patient’s right over their own life does not automatically generate a duty on anyone else to kill them. Argument I — The doing/allowing distinction tracks a real moral asymmetry between negative and positive duties; Rachels’ case does not refute it
1.Negative duty (not to kill): near-absolute, owed to everyone, does not require specific relationships or circumstances. Positive duty (to preserve life): owed in specific relationships, weaker, can be outweighed by competing considerations.
2.Rachels’ Smith and Jones case: both men violate duties (Smith: negative duty not to kill; Jones: positive duty of care not to let a child drown). The case shows both types can generate strong obligations; it does not show the distinction between them is morally inert.
3.Active euthanasia initiates a fatal causal sequence (negative duty violation). Passive euthanasia allows a sequence already in progress to continue (positive duty question). These are structurally different in ways that matter for moral evaluation.
∴The doing/allowing distinction is morally real and tracks the difference between negative and positive duties; Rachels’ argument does not refute it; active euthanasia falls under the prohibition of the near-absolute negative duty not to kill in a way that passive euthanasia does not.
Argument II — Welfare is a necessary but not sufficient condition for permissible euthanasia; the negative duty not to kill cannot be overridden by welfare alone
1.Euthanasia that does not benefit the patient is impermissible on any account. Welfare is a necessary condition. But if welfare were also sufficient, we would be obligated to kill anyone whose death would produce a welfare benefit — an implication no one accepts.
2.The negative duty not to kill imposes a constraint on action that is not automatically dissolved by good consequences. Killing for welfare benefit is constrained by the structural features of the negative duty, not just by the welfare calculation.
3.Consent does not transform killing from prohibited to permitted simply by altering the victim’s preference; consent can waive some protections but cannot suspend the negative duty entirely.
∴The welfare case is necessary but not sufficient for permissible euthanasia; the negative duty not to kill applies even when killing would benefit the patient; its structural force is not dissolved by welfare or consent alone.
Argument III — Foot’s position is not an absolute prohibition; but the conditions for permissible euthanasia are narrower than the proposition claims
1.Euthanasia is permissible under Foot’s account only when: (a) the patient genuinely benefits from death; (b) the patient consents and waives the protection of the negative duty; and (c) no alternative prevention of harm is available.
2.These conditions are more demanding than Singer’s preference satisfaction or Sumner’s welfare balance. The structural features of the negative duty continue to generate reasons against active euthanasia even in cases satisfying welfare and consent conditions.
3.The proposition has not adequately addressed the structural features of the negative duty; their arguments establish welfare and consent but not the additional conditions the duty requires.
∴Foot’s account is nuanced, not absolutist; but the conditions for permissible euthanasia are considerably narrower than the proposition claims; the structural force of the negative duty generates reasons against active euthanasia that welfare and consent arguments do not defeat.
Argument IV — A right to die does not entail a right to physician assistance; the physician’s independent moral status matters
1.The right to refuse treatment is a right over one’s own body: to decline interventions. The right to demand assistance in dying is a claim on another person — the physician — to perform a killing. These are structurally different claims.
2.A patient’s right over their own life does not automatically generate a duty on anyone else to kill them. The physician has independent moral obligations, including the obligation not to kill, which cannot simply be discharged by the patient’s consent.
3.The physician is a moral agent, not an instrument of patient preferences. Treating them as obligated to kill on request reduces their moral agency to a tool for preference satisfaction.
∴The right to die does not entail a right to physician assistance; the physician’s independent moral obligations, including the negative duty not to kill, cannot be suspended by the patient’s preferences; the step from patient autonomy to physician obligation requires independent argument the proposition has not provided.

I want to offer the most unusual argument in this debate: an argument against a right to die that does not appeal to the sanctity of life, does not deny that euthanasia can benefit patients, and does not rest on slippery-slope fears. My argument is internal to the case for autonomy. I argue that institutionalising the right to die can actually undermine rather than enhance the autonomy of the dying patients whose interests it is supposed to serve.

The argument runs as follows. Having an option is not always beneficial. Sometimes having an option makes you worse off by changing the context in which you make other choices. If you are offered a piece of poisoned cake, the option makes you worse off — not because you will necessarily take it, but because now you must choose whether to accept a gift that would kill you, and refusing is more socially complicated than if the option had never been offered. The right to die has a similar structure. It is an offer that many terminally ill patients would be better off not having — because having it transforms the moral context of their decision to continue living.

i. the paradox of options

Paradox of options — more options do not always increase autonomy; euthanasia as option places patients in a position of justifying their continued existence; the option can coerceThe standard argument for a right to die assumes that more options always expand freedom. But this is false, as the economics of options shows clearly. Having the option to sell your house makes you worse off when your creditors use it to justify seizing your property. Having the option to confess makes you worse off when your captors use it to intensify the pressure to do so. In the same way, having the option of euthanasia places terminally ill patients in a position where they may be expected to justify their choice to continue living — to explain to families, to healthcare providers, to themselves, why they are not taking the “rational” option of ending their suffering. The option does not merely add a possibility to the patient’s situation; it restructures the entire moral context of their dying. It can transform the decision to continue living from a default into a choice that requires active justification.

ii. the unfair burden argument

Unfair burden — terminal patients may feel obligated to die rather than impose a burden; legalising euthanasia creates pressure on vulnerable patients who do not want to be a burdenThis leads directly to what I regard as the most serious practical problem with a right to die: the unfair burden problem. Terminally ill patients are already in a position of dependence — on their families emotionally, on the healthcare system financially, on caregivers practically. In a society that values people in accordance with their economic and social contributions, the terminally ill may (often rightly) perceive their continued existence as a burden on those they love. If euthanasia is available as an option, the patient who chooses to continue living must now actively refuse the option. They may perceive their refusal as imposing on their family a burden that euthanasia would relieve. Their decision to continue living is no longer free; it is constrained by the knowledge that the alternative is available and that choosing against it may harm those around them. The option has made them less free, not more.

iii. dignified personhood and the Kantian objection

Kantian personhood — human dignity and personhood have unconditional value; euthanasia policies that make personhood conditional on economic contribution violate this; Velleman’s Kantian alternativeMy deeper objection is Kantian. Human personhood — the capacity for rational agency and self-determination — has value that is not conditional on welfare outcomes or preference satisfaction. The dying person who is suffering, whose preferences may be death, whose welfare balance may tip toward death, is nonetheless a person with unconditional dignity. A policy that makes their continued existence a matter of economic calculation — that presents death as the rational option when the welfare balance tips negative — treats their personhood as something to be weighed against costs rather than something with unconditional worth. Singer and Sumner offer welfare-based accounts of the right to die that implicitly frame the value of a life as a function of its welfare balance. I argue that this is incompatible with the recognition of genuine human dignity.

iv. the alternative: euthanasia without a right to die

Alternative proposal — euthanasia may be justifiable in specific cases without being institutionalised as a right; the institutionalisation is what causes the harm; Velleman’s nuanced positionMy position is not that euthanasia is never justified. I believe it can be, and on Kantian grounds: when a person’s dignity is being irremediably compromised by their condition, and when their capacity for rational decision-making is itself sufficiently intact to make the decision genuine, there may be grounds for assisting in their death. But I oppose institutionalising this as a protected option — a right that patients may invoke against physicians and insurers. The harm I am identifying is not the harm of euthanasia in individual cases but the harm of making euthanasia an option that is routinely available, advertised, and presented to dying patients as a rational choice. It is the offer, not the act in individual cases, that is most dangerous to the autonomy of the dying.

The offer harms those it purports to help. I yield the floor.

1.Having an option is not always beneficial: some options make you worse off by restructuring the context in which you make other decisions. The poisoned cake case, the creditor case, and others show this.
2.The right to die places patients in a position where they may be expected to justify their choice to continue living. The decision to continue living is transformed from a default into an active choice that requires justification.
3.This restructuring can reduce autonomy rather than enhance it: the patient who cannot easily refuse the option (for social, emotional, or financial reasons) faces a constrained rather than a free choice about whether to continue living.
∴The paradox of options shows that the right to die may undermine rather than enhance the autonomy of the very patients it is supposed to benefit; the standard autonomy argument for euthanasia misses this by assuming options always expand rather than sometimes constrain freedom.

3.Their decision to continue living is no longer free: it is constrained by knowledge of the available alternative and the costs their refusal imposes. The option has made them less free, not more.

∴Welfare-based accounts of the right to die are incompatible with the Kantian recognition of unconditional human dignity; they treat personhood as conditional on welfare outcomes in ways that undermine the very dignity they purport to respect.
Argument IV — Euthanasia can be justified in individual cases without institutionalising a right; the right, not the act, is what causes the harm
1.Velleman accepts that euthanasia can be justified in individual cases on Kantian grounds: when a person’s dignity is irremediably compromised and their rational agency intact. This is not an absolute prohibition.
2.The harm Velleman identifies is not individual acts of euthanasia but the institutionalisation of euthanasia as a routinely available option that restructures the moral context of dying for all terminally ill patients.
3.The proposition conflates the permissibility of euthanasia in individual cases with the institutional right to euthanasia as a protected option. These are different claims with different moral structures and different consequences.
∴Velleman’s argument is not a general prohibition but a specific objection to institutionalising the right to die; the harm flows from the offer, not from the individual act; the proposition has not addressed this distinction and cannot claim Velleman’s acceptance of individual cases as support for a general right.

I want to press two distinct slippery-slope arguments against the legalisation of voluntary euthanasia, and I want to insist at the outset that these are not the same argument. They are independent and they succeed independently. The first is empirical: the safeguards proposed for voluntary euthanasia cannot in practice be made effective. The second is logical: the principles that justify voluntary euthanasia already commit us, by their own internal logic, to non-voluntary euthanasia. Battin says the empirical evidence from the Netherlands and Belgium does not support the slippery slope. I will show it does. Singer says the logical argument is a fear-mongering extrapolation. I will show it is a deductive entailment.

i. the empirical slope: safeguards are not effective

Empirical slope — Dutch and Belgian evidence shows safeguards have not been maintained; the threshold for eligibility has expanded; involuntary cases are documented; the slope is realThe Dutch experience demonstrates the empirical slope in operation. Voluntary euthanasia was accepted in the Netherlands first through judicial tolerance from 1984, and subsequently through legislation. The original safeguards were clear: competent patients, unbearable suffering, no reasonable alternative. What has happened since? The category of “unbearable suffering” has expanded to include psychiatric illness and existential suffering. Eligibility has been extended to minors. Reported cases have increased sharply each year. And documented cases of euthanasia without clear patient request have occurred. Battin says this does not amount to a slippery slope. I say it is precisely the slope we predicted: once the principle is established that death can be a medical benefit for some patients, the natural pressures of medicine, economics, and consistency push the category outward. The Dutch Groningen Protocol — permitting euthanasia for severely disabled newborns — is exactly what the logical slope argument predicted would follow.

ii. the logical slope: the principles of VE entail NVE

Logical slope — the principles that justify VE — beneficence and relief of suffering — apply with equal force to NVE; voluntary euthanasia commits us to non-voluntary euthanasiaThe logical argument proceeds as follows. The justification for voluntary euthanasia rests on two principles: the patient’s autonomy (they have requested death) and the duty of beneficence (death benefits them by ending suffering). Now consider a patient who is suffering equally but who cannot consent — perhaps an infant born with a severe painful condition, or a patient in the late stages of dementia. The autonomy principle no longer applies. But the beneficence principle applies with full force: if death benefits a competent patient who requests it, why does it not equally benefit an incompetent patient in identical circumstances? The answer cannot be: because the incompetent patient did not consent. That answer makes beneficence conditional on consent in a way that has no precedent in medical ethics generally. We do not refuse to provide other medical benefits to incompetent patients on the ground that they cannot consent.

iii. battin’s global evidence and its limits

Battin’s evidence — the Dutch and Belgian experience does not refute the slope; it illustrates it; the scope of eligibility has expanded precisely as the logical argument predictsBattin argues that the empirical evidence from the Netherlands, Belgium, and Oregon does not show a slippery slope. She points to the rarity of involuntary euthanasia and the careful procedural safeguards. But this is not the right benchmark. The question is not whether involuntary euthanasia is common but whether the scope of eligible cases has expanded beyond what the original safeguards specified — and it has, in exactly the ways the logical argument predicts. Belgium now permits euthanasia for minors, for psychiatric illness, and for existential suffering. The Netherlands have the Groningen Protocol. Canada’s MAID regime has expanded from terminal illness to chronic conditions, with proposals for psychiatric MAID and even advance directives. The empirical trajectory vindicates the logical argument.

iv. the inviolability argument and why it matters for law

Inviolability in law — the prohibition on intentional killing is a bright-line rule that guides healthcare professionals; abandoning it introduces impossible judgment calls; the rule has value beyond the cases it directly governsBeyond the slope arguments, there is a distinct argument from the value of legal bright lines. The current prohibition on intentional killing in medicine is not just a rule about individual cases; it is a structural principle that guides the entire practice of medicine by establishing a clear boundary. Healthcare professionals can rely on it as a constraint that removes certain choices from the table entirely. Legalising voluntary euthanasia removes this bright-line constraint and replaces it with a judgment call: is this patient’s suffering unbearable enough? Is their request sufficiently informed? Is there a reasonable alternative? These judgments are extraordinarily difficult to make consistently, are subject to physician bias and economic pressure, and cannot be effectively supervised. The value of the bright-line rule lies precisely in its unconditional character; once it is made conditional, it provides much weaker guidance and much weaker protection.

The empirical slope is real. The logical slope is a deduction. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The empirical slope: the Dutch and Belgian experience shows that safeguards have expanded and the original criteria have not been maintained
1.Dutch experience: original safeguards (competent patients, unbearable suffering, no reasonable alternative) have been progressively weakened. Eligibility now extends to psychiatric illness, existential suffering, and minors (the Groningen Protocol for severely disabled newborns).
2.Belgium: extended to minors, to psychiatric illness, to cases where suffering is existential rather than physical. Canada: expanded from terminal illness to chronic conditions; proposals for psychiatric MAID and advance directives.
3.The trajectory across jurisdictions shows consistent expansion of eligibility beyond original safeguards, in exactly the directions the logical slope argument predicts. This is the empirical slope in operation.
∴The empirical slope argument succeeds: the experience of permissive jurisdictions shows consistent expansion of eligibility beyond original safeguards; Battin’s refutation based on absence of involuntary euthanasia uses the wrong benchmark; the slope is the expansion of criteria, not the appearance of explicit involuntary killing.
Argument II — The logical slope: the principles that justify VE logically commit us to NVE
1.VE is justified by two principles: (a) autonomy — the patient has requested death; (b) beneficence — death benefits the patient by ending suffering. Both are required for VE.
2.For NVE (incompetent patients): principle (a) does not apply. But principle (b) applies with full force: if death benefits a competent patient in circumstance C, it equally benefits an incompetent patient in identical circumstance C.
3.The response “the incompetent patient did not consent” makes beneficence conditional on consent in a way that has no precedent in medical ethics: we provide other medical benefits to incompetent patients without requiring consent.
∴The logical slope is a deductive entailment: the principles that justify VE (beneficence + autonomy) already commit proponents to NVE under the beneficence principle alone; the response from consent generates an inconsistency with the rest of medical ethics; VE and NVE must be accepted together or rejected together.

1.The bright-line rule against intentional killing guides medical practice by placing certain decisions off the table entirely. Its unconditional character is its value: healthcare professionals need not make the judgment call because it is never an option.
2.Legalising VE replaces the bright line with a series of judgment calls: sufficiently unbearable suffering? Sufficiently informed request? No reasonable alternative? These judgments are extraordinarily difficult, subject to bias and economic pressure, and cannot be consistently supervised.
3.The protection the bright line provides is systematically weaker once it is made conditional; the value of an absolute rule is precisely that it does not admit exceptions that must be individually adjudicated.
∴The bright-line rule against intentional killing has value beyond the cases it directly governs; legalising VE destroys this structural value and replaces it with judgment calls that are practically impossible to make consistently; the protection afforded to vulnerable patients is systematically weakened.

Singer treats the sanctity of life doctrine as a piece of religious superstition that collapses without a theological foundation. I want to show that this is wrong. A secular account of the special value of human life — one that grounds the prohibition on intentional killing without appeal to divine ownership of life or to biological humanity as such — is available, and it supports the prohibition more robustly than Singer’s speciesism charge allows.

The value of human life, on my account, does not derive from species membership. It derives from the distinctive capacities that typical adult humans possess and that constitute a form of being that matters in a special way: the capacity for moral agency, for rational self-determination, for the formation of meaningful relationships, and for what philosophers call narrative self-constitution — the organisation of a life into a coherent story with its own meaning and direction. These capacities are not exclusive to biological humans, but they are, in the vast majority of cases, realised in human beings. And their realisation creates a form of being that merits respect in ways that are not captured by a simple welfare calculus.

i. the secular sanctity of life argument

Secular sanctity — the special value of human life is grounded in capacities for moral agency and narrative self-constitution; this is a secular not a religious claim; Singer mischaracterises the argumentThe secular version of the sanctity argument does not claim that human life has value simply in virtue of being biologically human — which is the speciesist claim Singer attacks. It claims that human life typically embodies capacities — moral agency, rational self-determination, narrative self-constitution, the capacity for deep relationships — that constitute a form of being with distinctive worth. This worth is not merely the worth of welfare states (pleasures, satisfied preferences) but a form of second-order worth: the worth of being the kind of entity that can take responsibility for its own life, form meaningful commitments, and be held accountable as an author of its own actions. Intentionally killing such a being is different in kind from killing a being that lacks these capacities, not because of its species but because of what it is.

ii. what intentional killing expresses

Expression — intentional killing expresses an attitude toward human life that is incompatible with recognising its special worth; this is true even with good intentions; the attitude matters not just the consequencesThe most important feature of my argument is that it is concerned with what intentional killing expresses, not just with its consequences. When a physician intentionally kills a patient — even with that patient’s consent, even with beneficent intent, even in circumstances where death benefits the patient — they perform an action that has a specific meaning in the context of the physician-patient relationship and in the broader social context. They enact the judgment that this person’s life is no longer worth living. They treat the ending of a human life as a medical treatment. This expresses an attitude toward the worth of that life that is, I argue, incompatible with the full recognition of that life’s special value. It is not that bad consequences follow from this expression; it is that the expression itself is a form of disrespect, even if the patient requests and benefits from it.

iii. the intention/foresight distinction

Intention/foresight — the distinction between intending death and foreseeing it is morally relevant; it is not merely causal but expressive; the same outcome produced by different intentions has different moral characterKamm argues that the intention/foresight distinction, as embodied in the doctrine of double effect, is insufficient to carry the moral weight the opposition places on it. I disagree. The distinction is not merely causal — it is not just about whether you cause the death or merely foresee it as a side-effect. It is expressive: it tracks whether you treat the patient’s death as a means or as a side-effect of something else you are doing. The physician who administers pain relief knowing it may hasten death treats the death as an unfortunate side-effect of achieving a different goal (pain relief). The physician who administers a lethal injection treats the death as the goal — as the intended outcome. These actions have different meanings, express different attitudes toward the patient’s life, and stand in different relationships to the norm that human life has special worth. The distinction is morally real and the proposition has not adequately addressed it.

iv. solidarity and the social meaning of permissive euthanasia policy

Social solidarity — a permissive euthanasia policy changes what illness and dying mean in society; it signals that lives below a welfare threshold are not worth sustaining; this erodes solidarity with the vulnerableThe final dimension of my argument concerns the social meaning of a permissive euthanasia policy. Individual acts of euthanasia occur within a social context that gives them meaning beyond the individual case. A policy that institutionalises voluntary euthanasia sends a signal to society — and in particular to the ill, the elderly, the disabled, and the dying — about which lives are worth sustaining. When death becomes a medical option for lives deemed not worth living, it changes what illness means: from a situation in which society commits to caring for the suffering, to a situation in which death is one of the services available for managing the suffering. This erodes the forms of social solidarity — the commitment to care for one another through suffering rather than to eliminate suffering by eliminating the sufferer — that a humane society requires.

Intentional killing expresses disrespect for human worth. The prohibition stands. I yield the floor.

The proposition’s case rests on a foundational assumption that I want to interrogate with some care: that dying patients who request euthanasia are doing so as rational autonomous agents whose preferences accurately reflect their genuine interests. This assumption is philosophically crucial. Without it, the autonomy argument collapses into permission for distorted preferences, the welfare argument loses its grounding in genuine well-being, and Singer’s preference utilitarianism counts the preferences of the depressed and the panicking as straightforwardly morally decisive. I want to show that this assumption is far more fragile than the proposition acknowledges, and that the fragility has profound implications for the permissibility case.

My argument deploys what I call the adaptive preference problem. Adaptive preferences are preferences that have been formed in response to circumstances of deprivation, suffering, or constrained options, and that would not have been formed — or would have been formed differently — under better circumstances. They are preferences that accommodate to circumstances rather than expressing the agent’s genuine values. The terminally ill patient in pain, depression, and social isolation is in precisely the circumstances most likely to generate adaptive preferences — and the preference to die is exactly the kind of preference that can be adaptively formed.

i. the conditions for rational suicide are rarely met

Rational suicide — the conditions for a genuinely rational autonomous preference to die are stringent; depression, pain, and social isolation systematically undermine the rationality the autonomy argument requiresWhat would a fully rational, genuinely autonomous preference for death look like? It would require: accurate information about one’s prognosis and the realistic alternatives; a stable and unimpaired deliberative capacity; freedom from depression that distorts assessments of future welfare; adequate pain management that prevents pain itself from driving the preference; and an absence of social pressure. These are very demanding conditions. The circumstances under which euthanasia is typically requested — terminal diagnosis, severe pain, loss of function, anxiety about being a burden, often clinical depression — are precisely the circumstances that systematically undermine deliberative rationality. Studies consistently show that most requests for assisted dying are withdrawn when pain is adequately managed. This is not the profile of a stable rational preference; it is the profile of an adaptive response to circumstances.

ii. the psychiatric euthanasia frontier

Psychiatric euthanasia — if autonomy grounds permissibility, we cannot restrict it to physical illness; psychiatric suffering is real; but psychiatric conditions most severely impair the rationality that autonomy arguments requireThe adaptive preference problem is most acute at what is now the frontier of the debate: psychiatric euthanasia. Belgium and the Netherlands now permit euthanasia for psychiatric suffering that is treatment-resistant. The philosophical logic is compelling: if euthanasia is justified by unbearable suffering and competent autonomous preference, why should the suffering of severe depression, personality disorder, or treatment-resistant PTSD be treated as less real than physical suffering? The proposition cannot easily resist this extension without abandoning the principles that ground their case. But this extension brings the adaptive preference problem into acute focus: the psychiatric conditions that most warrant euthanasia on autonomy grounds are precisely those that most severely impair the rationality and autonomous agency that the autonomy argument requires. We are being asked to accept that the most authentically autonomous expression of a severely depressed person is the preference to die.

iii. the medicalization critique

Medicalization — transforming euthanasia into a medical procedure changes the meaning of the preference to die; it may create social legitimacy for preferences that social and psychological support could addressThere is a further problem with embedding euthanasia within medicine: the medicalization of dying transforms the social meaning of the preference to die in ways that may generate rather than merely respond to that preference. When euthanasia is a medical procedure — available, discussed openly by physicians, sanctioned by healthcare systems — the preference to die acquires a social legitimacy that it would not have had in its absence. Patients learn that their suffering is the kind of suffering for which the medical system offers death as a solution. This medicalisation may reinforce adaptive preferences rather than addressing their underlying causes. The patient who hears from their physician that euthanasia is an available option may form a preference to die partly as a result of the offer itself — exactly the mechanism Velleman identifies.

iv. the implications for the permissibility case

Implications — if the rationality assumption fails, the autonomy argument fails; the welfare argument also requires a non-adaptive preference judgment; the burden of proof on the proposition is much higher than they acknowledgeWhat are the implications of the adaptive preference problem for the permissibility case? If we cannot reliably distinguish genuine autonomous preferences for death from adaptive responses to inadequately managed pain, depression, and social pressure, then the autonomy argument cannot do the work the proposition requires. Sumner’s welfare account requires not merely that the patient prefers death but that their preference reflects a grounded accurate assessment of their well-being. But if the preference is adaptive, it does not reflect a grounded accurate assessment; it reflects an accommodation to avoidable circumstances. The welfare argument therefore requires not just patient consent but a determination that the preference is non-adaptive — that it would survive better pain management, treatment of depression, and relief of social pressure. This determination is practically extremely difficult to make and currently no euthanasia regime makes it reliably.

The rationality assumption fails. The burden has not been met. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The conditions for rational autonomous preference for death are stringent and are rarely met in the circumstances of terminal illness
1.Rational autonomous preference for death requires: accurate prognosis information, unimpaired deliberative capacity, freedom from depression distorting welfare assessments, adequate pain management, and freedom from social pressure. These are demanding conditions.
2.The circumstances of terminal illness — pain, loss of function, anxiety, depression, social isolation — are precisely those that systematically undermine deliberative rationality. Most requests for assisted dying are withdrawn when pain is adequately managed.
3.This withdrawal pattern is not the profile of a stable rational preference; it is the profile of adaptive accommodation to avoidable circumstances.
∴The conditions for genuine rational autonomous preference for death are stringent and are systematically undermined by the circumstances of terminal illness; the autonomy argument cannot simply assume that preferences expressed in these circumstances are genuinely autonomous without defeating its own foundation.
Argument II — Psychiatric euthanasia brings the adaptive preference problem into acute focus; psychiatric conditions impair the very rationality the autonomy argument requires
1.The proposition’s autonomy argument cannot coherently restrict euthanasia to physical illness: psychiatric suffering is real, and the autonomy logic applies equally. This is the frontier of the debate.
2.But psychiatric conditions (severe depression, treatment-resistant PTSD) that most warrant euthanasia on autonomy grounds are precisely those that most severely impair the rational autonomous agency the argument requires.
3.The proposition is caught in a dilemma: either restrict euthanasia to physical illness (which requires abandoning their own principles) or extend it to psychiatric conditions (where the adaptive preference problem is most severe).
∴The psychiatric euthanasia frontier reveals the deepest flaw in the proposition’s case: the autonomy argument, taken to its logical conclusion, licences euthanasia precisely in the cases where the rationality it requires is most compromised; the proposition cannot resolve this dilemma without abandoning core principles.
Argument III — The medicalization of euthanasia may generate the preferences it claims only to respond to
1.When euthanasia becomes a medical procedure — available, discussed, sanctioned — the preference to die acquires social legitimacy it would not otherwise have. Patients learn that their suffering is the kind for which medicine offers death.
2.This medicalisation may reinforce adaptive preferences rather than addressing their underlying causes: the patient who hears the option discussed may form a preference for death partly in response to the offer itself.
3.The proposition assumes medicine responds to a pre-existing preference for death; but medicalising euthanasia may partly generate the preference it claims to serve.
∴Medicalisation may create rather than merely respond to adaptive preferences for death; the proposition’s assumption that euthanasia requests are autonomous responses to independent suffering is undermined by the possibility that the offer of euthanasia itself shapes the preference.
Argument IV — The welfare argument also requires non-adaptive preference judgment; current euthanasia regimes cannot make this determination reliably
1.Sumner’s welfare argument requires that the preference for death reflect a grounded accurate assessment of well-being. An adaptive preference does not meet this standard: it reflects accommodation to avoidable circumstances, not a grounded judgment.
2.The determination that a preference is non-adaptive — that it would survive adequate pain management, treatment of depression, and relief of social pressure — is practically extremely difficult to make.
3.No current euthanasia regime reliably makes this determination; the safeguards in place assess consent and competence but not the adaptive/non-adaptive distinction that the welfare argument requires.
∴The adaptive preference problem defeats both the autonomy and the welfare arguments as currently specified: neither can be established without determining that the preference for death is non-adaptive; current regimes do not make this determination; the burden of proof on the proposition is much higher than their arguments acknowledge.

I want to offer the opposition a different kind of argument: one that proceeds not from the sanctity of life doctrine but from a general theory of what makes killing wrong — a theory that I believe is the most defensible available, and that applies, with important qualifications, to most euthanasia cases. My future-like-ours account, developed to explain why killing adult humans is typically seriously wrong, turns out to have significant bearing on the euthanasia debate in ways that have not been fully appreciated.

The central question is: what makes killing a person wrong when it is wrong? Not merely that it causes suffering to others. Not merely that it violates a social norm. The most plausible answer is: killing deprives the victim of their future — of all the experiences, activities, projects, and enjoyments that would otherwise have constituted their life going forward. This deprivation is one of the greatest harms one can suffer, because it forecloses all the goods that continued existence would have contained. Call this the Future Like Ours account (FLO).

The FLO account and its application to euthanasia
Why is killing wrong? Killing deprives the victim of a “future like ours” — a future that includes valuable experiences, activities, projects, and enjoyments (EAPEs) that the person would otherwise have had.
The wrongness of killing scales with the value of the future being foreclosed. Killing a young healthy person forecloses a rich future of EAPEs — maximally wrong. Killing someone with no valuable future forecloses nothing of value — less or not wrong on this account.
Application to euthanasia: Marquis argues that even terminal patients typically retain some future of value — remaining relationships, moments of peace or connection, the completion of projects, the experience of care and love. Their future is shortened, but it may still contain valuable EAPEs. Killing them forecloses those remaining goods. However: Marquis also accepts that when a future contains no EAPEs — only suffering with no prospect of relief — the FLO account does not generate a strong case against killing. The conditions for permissibility are narrow but real.
i. the FLO account applied to terminal cases

FLO in terminal cases — even terminal patients typically have some future of valuable experiences; euthanasia forecloses these; the deprivation remains a harm even if smaller than in healthy patientsThe FLO account does not require that the foreclosed future be rich and lengthy to generate a strong case against killing. It requires only that the future contain experiences, activities, projects, and enjoyments that the person would have valued. Terminal patients — even those in significant pain — often have remaining futures that contain such goods: moments of connection with family, experiences of care and support, the completion of personal projects, the saying of goodbyes, the final chapters of a life story. These are goods of real value. Euthanasia forecloses them. The deprivation account therefore generates a prima facie case against euthanasia even for terminal patients, though a case that is weaker than in the paradigm case of killing a healthy young person. The proposition needs to show not merely that the patient is suffering but that their remaining future contains no EAPEs at all — a much more demanding standard.

ii. the account permits but does not require euthanasia in extreme cases

Permissibility conditions — FLO permits euthanasia only when the remaining future contains no valuable experiences; this is a narrow condition that most euthanasia requests do not satisfyI do not deny that the FLO account can, in extreme cases, support the permissibility of euthanasia. When a person’s remaining life truly contains only suffering — when there are no remaining EAPEs, no valuable future experiences of any kind, when even consciousness itself has become pure anguish — then death deprives them of no future goods, and the prima facie case against killing is absent. Euthanasia in these narrow cases is not impermissible on the FLO account. But this is a very narrow condition. It requires that the remaining future be genuinely free of any valuable experiences, and it places a substantial burden of proof on anyone claiming that this extreme condition is met. Most terminal patients, even those in significant pain, have futures that contain some EAPEs. The proposition has not established that their patients meet the narrow condition my account specifies for permissibility.

iii. mcmahan’s time-relative interests and the flo account

McMahan vs Marquis — McMahan’s time-relative interests modulate the wrongness of killing by psychological connection; Marquis argues this underestimates the wrongness in cases where some connection remainsMcMahan’s time-relative interest account modulates the wrongness of killing by the strength of psychological connections between the present person and their future self. Where connections are weak, the wrongness is reduced. I want to press a challenge to this account: psychological connection is not the only thing that makes a future valuable. Even a terminal patient whose psychological connections to their future are weakened by illness may have a future that contains valuable experiences — not because they have rich psychological connections to those experiences but because those experiences are intrinsically valuable to the person who will have them. The FLO account does not require strong psychological connection; it requires only that the future contain EAPEs that are valuable to the individual who will experience them. McMahan’s account, on my view, underestimates the wrongness of killing in cases where some valuable future remains even without strong psychological connection.

iv. the scope of the debate: the flo account sets the right terms

Right terms — the FLO account sets the correct terms for the debate; permissibility turns on whether the remaining future contains valuable experiences; this is a more accurate framing than welfare balance or preference satisfactionI want to suggest that the FLO account sets the debate on the right terms — terms that are more accurate than either Singer’s preference account or Sumner’s welfare balance. The question is not whether the patient has a preference to die (which may be adaptive), or whether the balance of welfare is negative (which is difficult to assess). The question is whether the patient’s remaining future contains valuable EAPEs that killing would foreclose. This is a concrete and assessable question; it focuses attention on what is actually lost by euthanasia rather than on what the patient currently prefers. And it generates a more demanding standard for permissibility than either Singer or Sumner acknowledge: permissibility requires not just present suffering but a genuinely future-free of value — a standard that most euthanasia requests, even those from apparently sincere and suffering patients, do not meet.

The future like ours is typically not empty. The deprivation remains. I yield the floor.
