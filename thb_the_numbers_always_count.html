Mr President,

The question before this House is one of the most practically urgent in normative ethics. It is not a puzzle about trolleys. It is a puzzle about every triage decision in every hospital, every allocation of scarce medical resources, every cost-benefit analysis in public policy. When we must choose between saving one person from death and saving five people from death, the view that we should save the five is so intuitively overwhelming that one might wonder why we are debating it at all. We are debating it because John Taurek, and after him T.M. Scanlon and Alex Voorhoeve, have constructed philosophical frameworks that appear to challenge it. I want to argue that those challenges fail — and that the numbers always count, in a sense I shall specify carefully.

On the aggregation question and what it really asks

The aggregation question precisely statedThe question of aggregation is not: can we trade any harm to one person off against any benefit to many? That question has an obvious answer — no. We should not break one person's arm to cure a billion hangnails. The question is: when the harms are of comparable seriousness — death against death, paralysis against paralysis — do the numbers of people affected matter? And the answer is evidently yes. A policy that saves five lives is better than a policy that saves one life, all else equal, and this is not because we have aggregated five trivial benefits into something that outweighs death. It is because five deaths are worse than one death. The principle I defend is not that any accumulation of small benefits outweighs a serious harm. It is that, for harms of relevantly similar gravity, the numbers matter.

On what Taurek actually showed — and what he did not

Taurek's actual argument and its limitsJohn Taurek's 1977 paper "Should the Numbers Count?" argues that there is no impersonal standpoint from which we can say that five deaths are worse than one — because each person only has their own death to face, and from that person's standpoint, the loss is total regardless of how many others are dying. This is a striking argument, but it establishes less than it appears to. It shows that there is a sense in which the five cannot pool their losses into a single super-loss that outweighs David's loss. It does not show that we have no reason to save five over one. We can have reason to save the greater number because — once we have saved one, we have further reason to save each additional person who can be saved at no extra cost. The incremental structure of the saving opportunity gives us reason to save more, without pooling anyone's losses into a combined sum.

On Voorhoeve's relevance threshold and why it fails

Against Voorhoeve's relevance thresholdAlex Voorhoeve's Aggregate Relevant Claims theory holds that we should only count a person's claim against the claims of others when those other claims are sufficiently weighty to be "relevant" — roughly, when they are comparable in severity to the claim at stake. This produces the right result in Taurek's case (we save the five) and also in the headache case (we should not sacrifice one life to cure a billion headaches). But Voorhoeve's relevance threshold is itself philosophically arbitrary. What determines whether a claim is relevant? If I face death and you face paralysis, is your claim relevant to mine? If a hundred thousand people each face a broken arm, are their claims collectively relevant to my death? Voorhoeve's theory requires a principled account of relevance that tracks our intuitions — and the intuitions themselves are doing all the work. The theory is an articulation of the intuitions, not a justification for them.

On the self-sacrifice case and what it reveals

Brown's self-sacrifice objection to ARCIn my paper "Aggregation and Self-Sacrifice," I raise a challenge that I think is decisive for Voorhoeve's framework. Voorhoeve derives his relevance threshold by connecting it to what a fully self-regarding agent would accept in an analogous personal case — if you would accept a small risk of death to avoid a certainty of paralysis, then in the interpersonal case, the paralysis claim is relevant to the death claim. But this connection between self-regarding prudence and other-regarding morality is spurious. Whether a rational agent would trade a small probability of death against certain paralysis is a question about her attitudes to risk, her degree of risk-aversion, her values — not a question whose answer fixes the moral weight we should attach to claims in interpersonal trade-offs. Voorhoeve has derived a moral threshold from a psychological fact about individual risk preferences. That inference does not go through.

On why the numbers always count — the residue argument

The residue / always-aggregate argumentMy positive argument is this. Suppose we are choosing between saving one person and saving five people, all from the same harm. Match one person from each group. In that matched pair, we have no reason to prefer either — their claims are equal. But the five have residual claimants — four people whose claims are simply not matched by anyone on the other side. Those four unmatched claims give us decisive reason to save the five. This reasoning does not require pooling anyone's losses. It requires only recognising that unmatched claims — claims that face no competing claim on the other side — are decisive. The numbers always count because there will always be more unmatched claims on the side of the greater number. And there is nothing philosophically arbitrary about responding to unmatched claims.

I urge the House to vote for the motion. The numbers always count — not because we can pool losses into a super-sum, but because unmatched claims are decisive, and a greater number always generates more of them.

Mr President,

I want to approach the aggregation question from a direction that I believe is more fundamental than the formal machinery of claims and matching. I want to ask: what is the deep philosophical error in the view that the numbers do not count? And I want to argue that the error is a commitment to a purely personal conception of reasons — a conception that fails on independent grounds and that, once abandoned, makes the case for aggregation compelling.

On agent-neutral reasons and the badness of suffering

Agent-neutral reasons: suffering is bad regardless of whose it isConsider what grounds our concern for any person who is suffering. It is not merely that her suffering is bad for her — it is that suffering is bad, full stop, and she is suffering it. If it is bad that someone suffers — if suffering has a kind of impersonal badness — then the question of whose suffering it is cannot be the fundamental moral question. The moral question is: how much suffering is there, and can we reduce it? This is an agent-neutral reason: a reason that does not essentially index itself to the perspective of a particular agent or a particular sufferer. When Taurek insists that each person only has their own loss to face, he is correct about the phenomenology of loss. But phenomenology does not determine moral weight. The badness of five people dying is not five times the badness of one person's death experienced subjectively — but morally, five deaths are worse than one, and any account of reasons that cannot accommodate this has missed something important.

On the claim that Scanlon's contractualism vindicates aggregation

Justifiability to each person supports aggregationScanlon's contractualism asks: which principles could not be reasonably rejected by anyone? I argued in "Justifiability to Each Person" (2003) that this test, properly applied, vindicates the aggregative answer. Consider the situation from behind a veil of ignorance: each person might be the one or one of the five. A person who could be any of the six has reason to prefer the principle that saves the five — because under that principle, her probability of being saved is five-sixths rather than one-sixth. Now, Scanlon and Otsuka deny this reasoning is valid. But I think their denial requires a conception of "reasonable rejection" so strong that it makes contractualism collapse into a veto: any individual in any minority can reasonably reject any principle that does not save her specifically. That is not a plausible account of reasonable rejection — it is the Taurekian view restated in contractualist language.

On the separateness of persons — the opposition's most powerful card

The separateness of persons: taking it seriously without abandoning aggregationThe opposition will invoke the separateness of persons — the Rawlsian observation that persons are distinct, that gains for one do not compensate losses for another, and that any framework that treats society as a single subject maximising welfare violates this truth. I accept the separateness of persons. I deny that it entails anti-aggregationism. The separateness of persons is a constraint on how we aggregate, not a prohibition on aggregating. It prohibits us from treating five people as a composite person whose combined welfare simply exceeds one person's welfare. But it does not prohibit us from recognising that five individual persons each have claims, and that five individual claims to be saved from the same harm collectively provide stronger reason than one individual claim. Aggregating individual claims — not pooling them into a super-claim — respects persons as separate.

On Voorhoeve's ARC and the puzzle of partial aggregation

ARC and the problem of partial aggregationAlex Voorhoeve's Aggregate Relevant Claims is a sophisticated and carefully constructed theory, and I want to engage with its most serious challenge rather than dismissing it. ARC holds that we should aggregate only relevant claims — claims that meet a threshold of severity relative to the claim they are aggregated against. This produces results that match our intuitions across a remarkable range of cases: it saves the five from death over the one from death, and refuses to sacrifice the one to cure a billion headaches. My objection is not that ARC gets the cases wrong. It is that ARC gets them right for the wrong reasons. The relevance threshold is doing philosophical work that has not been paid for. We need an account of why claims below the threshold do not aggregate, and Voorhoeve's account — linking the threshold to what rational agents would accept in prudential cases — does not provide one that stands up to scrutiny.

On the headache case — the real test of aggregationism

The headache case and the limits of full aggregationI want to be honest about the most difficult case for my position: the headache case, posed by Scanlon. Should we allow one person to die in order to prevent a sufficiently large number of people from suffering headaches? Aggregationism appears to imply yes — at some number of headaches, their combined badness exceeds the badness of one death. I think this implication can be resisted without abandoning aggregationism. The resistance lies not in a relevance threshold but in the distinction between aggregating comparable goods and aggregating incommensurable ones. Death and headache relief are not on the same scale of value — there is no fact about how many headache-minutes of relief add up to a death-equivalent. Where there is genuine incommensurability, aggregation is undefined — not trumped, but inapplicable. Within the domain of comparable harms, aggregation applies, and the numbers always count.

Mr President,

My position is more carefully stated than the motion allows. I am not a numbers sceptic. I agree that we should save five from death over one from death. I agree that the numbers matter. What I deny is the motion as stated — that the numbers always count, and that morality requires full aggregation across persons. The theory I defend — Aggregate Relevant Claims — makes the numbers count in exactly the cases where they should, and refuses to make them count in the cases where they should not. This is not a compromise between aggregationism and anti-aggregationism. It is the right theory.

On the two intuitions ARC must accommodate

Two intuitions: Life vs Lives and the headache caseThere are two intuitions about aggregation that any adequate theory must accommodate simultaneously. The first: we ought to save five people from permanent paralysis rather than save one person from death, if we must choose — and the reason is at least partly that there are five of them, not just one. The numbers count. The second: we ought to save one person from death rather than cure a billion people of mild headaches, however large that number grows. The numbers do not always count. The proposition — full aggregationism — can accommodate the first intuition but must deny or explain away the second. The numbers sceptic — Taurek — can accommodate the second but must deny or explain away the first. Only partial aggregationism, in the form of ARC, accommodates both simultaneously. That is its primary virtue, and it is a substantial one.

On what ARC says and how relevance works

ARC: the relevance criterion explainedARC holds that when we face competing claims, we should first identify which of the competing claims are relevant to each other — where relevance is understood as meeting a threshold of severity. Only relevant claims may be aggregated. A claim is relevant to another when it concerns a harm of sufficient gravity to be "in the same conversation" as the claim against which it is weighed — when the claimant could not reasonably insist that the other person's loss is simply too small to be weighed against her own. The relevance criterion is not arbitrary: it is grounded in what persons could reasonably demand of each other in a contractualist framework. A person facing death cannot reasonably insist that the headache sufferer's claim is simply beneath consideration — but she can reasonably insist that the headache is not in the same moral conversation as her death. Relevance tracks what persons owe each other to take seriously.

On Brown's self-sacrifice objection

Reply to Brown's self-sacrifice objectionCampbell Brown argues that my relevance threshold illicitly derives a moral conclusion from an agent's personal risk preferences — that it makes the moral weight of claims depend on individual psychology. This objection mischaracterises what the self-sacrifice link is doing. I am not saying that the relevance threshold is whatever a particular risk-averse or risk-tolerant individual would accept in a prudential case. I am using the structure of prudential reasoning — the kinds of trade-offs a fully rational agent could endorse across possible outcomes affecting herself — to give an agent-neutral account of what it means for harms to be "in the same moral conversation." The move from prudential to interpersonal comparison is not from psychology to morality. It is from one instance of rational weighing to a structurally analogous instance of interpersonal weighing. Both are governed by the same underlying concept of what counts as a proportionate trade-off.

On the residue argument — Brown's positive case

Reply to the residue argumentBrown's residue argument holds that after matching one claimant from each side, the unmatched residual claimants on the side of the greater number provide decisive reason to save the many. ARC agrees with this — in the case where all claims are relevant to each other. Five people each face death; one person faces death. Match one from each side. Four unmatched death-claims remain. Save the five. But notice what happens in the headache case under the residue argument. One person faces death; one billion people face headaches. Match the one death-claim against one headache claim. The death claim wins. There are 999,999,999 unmatched headache claims. Brown's argument, applied here, implies that the billion headache-claims provide decisive reason to let the one person die — because the unmatched residue is vast. The residue argument, unqualified by relevance, is full aggregationism in disguise and inherits its most troubling implication.

On the broader significance of partial aggregation

Partial aggregation in practice: priority-setting and health policyThe stakes of this debate are not merely academic. In health care priority-setting — in bodies like NICE in the UK — a commitment to full aggregation implies that we should always allocate resources to maximise the number of quality-adjusted life years saved, regardless of the severity of the conditions being treated. This means that, at some point, devoting resources to treating conditions of moderate severity will take priority over treating conditions that are severe but affect fewer people. Partial aggregation, of the kind ARC provides, captures the intuition that there is a floor of severity below which claims simply should not be traded off against larger numbers of milder claims — and this has direct policy implications for how we structure health care allocation. The difference between full and partial aggregation is not merely philosophical. It is the difference between two approaches to distributive justice with very different implications for who gets care.

Mr President,

I want to approach the numbers problem from the contractualist foundation I developed in What We Owe to Each Other, and to show that the contractualist framework — properly understood — produces a nuanced position that vindicates saving the greater number in most cases while resisting the claim that the numbers always count. My colleague Michael Otsuka has pressed the most serious challenge against my own approach, and I want to engage with that challenge honestly, because it sharpens what is at stake in this debate.

On the contractualist approach to the numbers problem

Scanlon's tie-breaking argumentThe contractualist approach asks: which principles for resolving conflicts between persons could not be reasonably rejected by anyone affected? Applied to the case of one person who would die versus five who would die, I argue that a principle of saving the greater number can be justified as follows. Consider any member of the five. She faces death. She knows that one other person also faces death. If we save the one, she dies needlessly — needlessly, because there is another person who faces the same fate, and who could be saved instead. A principle of saving the greater number allows the individual within the larger group to say: my claim was considered alongside each of the others; I was not set aside in favour of a lesser claim, but was part of the group that provided the stronger aggregate of individually considered claims. No one's claim is ignored; every claim is considered; the tie is broken by the existence of additional claimants.

On Otsuka's objection — the problem of pairwise comparison

Otsuka's scepticism: pairwise comparison does not yield aggregationMichael Otsuka has pressed a powerful objection to my tie-breaking argument. He argues that when we consider each member of the five individually — asking whether her claim is stronger than the one person's claim — we must answer no. Death is death. One person facing death has exactly the same strength of claim to be saved as another person facing death. There is no pairwise comparison between any member of the five and the one that gives the member of the five a stronger claim. Yet my argument appears to rely on the idea that, taken together, the five have a stronger claim than the one. But taken together is precisely the move Taurek prohibits. Otsuka's objection is that my tie-breaking argument either relies on aggregation — in which case I am a crypto-aggregationist — or it fails to justify saving the many — in which case I have not solved the numbers problem.

On how contractualism responds to Otsuka

Scanlon's response: reasonable rejection and the individualist restrictionI want to defend my position against Otsuka's objection without collapsing into full aggregationism. The key is to distinguish what individual A can reasonably reject from what individual A can claim as her right. A principle of saving the one over the five can be reasonably rejected by each member of the five individually — not because her claim is stronger than the one's claim, but because a principle that systematically saves the one over the many is not justifiable to each of the many taken individually. Each member of the five can say: this principle, applied to our situation, gives me no reason to be saved over the one, even though we are identically situated. The asymmetry in the principle's application — always saving the one — cannot be justified to any of the five as the outcome of a procedure that took her claim seriously. This is the contractualist core of saving the greater number, and it does not require pooling claims.

On the headache case and the limits of aggregation

The headache case: where aggregation must stopThe hardest case for any position in this debate is the headache case. A billion people face headaches; one person faces death. Should we let the person die to cure the headaches? My contractualism gives a clear answer: no. A principle that allows one person to die to cure a billion headaches could be reasonably rejected by that person — not because the headaches do not matter, but because the principle does not take her claim with sufficient seriousness. What counts as "sufficient seriousness" is determined by the comparative gravity of the competing claims: death and headache relief are simply not in the same moral category, and a principle that treats them as commensurable — that allows sufficiently many headaches to outweigh a death — cannot be justified to the person who faces death. Parfit's proposal to handle this through incommensurability rather than a relevance threshold is interesting but under-specified. What makes two harms incommensurable? I think the answer returns us, inevitably, to something like Voorhoeve's relevance criterion.

On what the Scanlonian framework ultimately delivers

Contractualism and partial aggregation: convergence with ARCThe Scanlonian contractualist framework, I believe, converges with Voorhoeve's ARC in its practical deliverances, though it arrives there by a different route. Both frameworks save the five from death over the one from death. Both refuse to sacrifice the one to cure billions of headaches. Both allow aggregation of comparable claims while blocking aggregation across claims of radically different gravity. Where they diverge is in the theoretical grounding: Voorhoeve grounds the relevance criterion in a structural concept of proportionate trade-offs; I ground it in what principles can be reasonably rejected by those they most severely affect. In practice, these two approaches are likely to converge. The difference between them is theoretical — a question about the foundations of the relevance constraint rather than its application. And on the foundational question, my view is that contractualism provides the more fundamental and more principled account.

1.A principle allowing one death to cure a billion headaches can be reasonably rejected by the person who faces death — because the principle does not take her claim with sufficient seriousness relative to the gravity of her loss.
2.The comparative gravity of competing claims — death vs. headache relief — is itself a consideration that enters the contractualist adjudication.
3.Parfit's incommensurability proposal under-specifies what makes harms incommensurable — the answer returns us to something like the relevance criterion.
∴Contractualist reasonable rejection blocks trivial aggregation in the headache case without invoking a separate relevance threshold — the reasonable rejection criterion itself incorporates comparative gravity.
V — Contractualism and ARC converge in practice; contractualism provides the deeper foundationclick for image
1.Contractualism and ARC converge in their practical deliverances — both save five from death over one, both block aggregation of headaches against a death, both allow partial aggregation of comparable harms.
2.They diverge in theoretical grounding: ARC grounds relevance in structural prudential weighing; contractualism grounds it in what can be reasonably rejected by those most severely affected.
3.Contractualism provides a more fundamental and principled account because it grounds the relevance constraint in the moral relationship between persons, not in the structure of prudential trade-offs.
∴Contractualism and ARC are convergent theories of partial aggregation — contractualism provides the deeper philosophical foundation by grounding the relevance constraint in reasonable rejection rather than structural prudential comparison.
