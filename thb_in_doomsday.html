Logical Arguments

I want to begin with what is, I believe, one of the most important and most misunderstood arguments in contemporary philosophy: the Doomsday Argument. I have been developing and defending this argument since 1989, and I have spent thirty years watching it be misunderstood, dismissed, and refuted — and watching those refutations fail under pressure. Let me state it with full precision before the opposition attacks it.

The argument is this. You exist. You are a human being. There have been, as best we can estimate, approximately 60 to 100 billion humans born before you or roughly contemporaneous with you. There will be some total number N of humans who ever live. The question the Doomsday Argument asks is: what does your birth rank — approximately 60 billion — tell you about N? And the answer, delivered by a straightforward application of Bayesian probability theory, is: it is evidence that N is not orders of magnitude larger than 60 billion. If N were, say, 6 trillion, we would expect the vast majority of humans who ever lived to have birth ranks greater than 60 billion — yet here you are, among the first one percent. This is surprising if N is enormous; it is not surprising if N is relatively modest. Therefore our birth rank is evidence that doom — the cessation of the human species — is closer than our naive prior estimates suggest.

The Doomsday Argument: formal structure
Let n = your birth rank (∼60 billion). Let N = total number of humans ever.
Self-Sampling Assumption (SSA): You should reason as if you are a random observer drawn uniformly from the set of all observers in your reference class.
Under SSA: P(n | N) = 1/N for each n ≤ N (your birth rank is drawn uniformly from {1,...,N}).
By Bayes: P(N | n) ∝ P(n | N) × P(N)
For two hypotheses Hfew (N = 200 billion) and Hmany (N = 200 trillion):
P(n=60bn | Hfew) = 1/200bn     P(n=60bn | Hmany) = 1/200tn
Likelihood ratio: P(n | Hfew) / P(n | Hmany) = 1000
Conclusion: Your birth rank shifts your probability estimates toward smaller N by a factor of 1000 relative to the prior ratio. If your prior gave Hfew and Hmany equal probability, your posterior gives Hfew a 1000:1 advantage. This is the Doomsday update — and it is valid Bayesian reasoning.
i. the urn analogy and why it works

Urn analogy — drawing ball 7 from one of two urns (10 vs 1,000,000 balls) is strong evidence for the small urn; the DA has identical structure; the inference is validThe argument is most easily understood through the urn analogy. You are presented with two urns. One contains ten balls numbered 1 to 10. The other contains one million balls numbered 1 to 1,000,000. You draw a ball at random and it shows the number 7. What does this tell you about which urn you drew from? Clearly it is strong evidence that you drew from the small urn: if you were drawing from the million-ball urn, getting a number as low as 7 would have probability one in a million; if you were drawing from the ten-ball urn, it has probability one in ten. The likelihood ratio is 100,000 in favour of the small urn. The Doomsday Argument has exactly this structure. Humanity is one of two urns: one with 200 billion total humans, one with 200 trillion. You have drawn birth rank 60 billion. This is strong evidence for the small urn — for a humanity that ends relatively soon. The inference is valid Bayesian reasoning, and refusing to accept it requires rejecting standard probability theory.

ii. the self-sampling assumption is the correct epistemic principle

SSA — reasoning as random observer from your reference class is the correct Bayesian principle for self-locating belief; it is used in other contexts we accept; DA is just its applicationThe Self-Sampling Assumption — that you should reason as if you are a random sample from the set of observers in your reference class — is not invented for the purpose of generating the Doomsday Argument. It is the correct general principle for reasoning under self-locating uncertainty. We use it in every context where we need to infer something about the world from our own position in it. If I wake up with no memory and find myself in a hospital, I reason: most people who find themselves in hospitals are patients, not surgeons — so I am probably a patient. This is SSA in action. The DA is simply SSA applied to the question of our birth rank. Those who reject the DA must explain what is wrong with SSA as a general epistemic principle — and all attempts to do so have produced worse problems than the ones they tried to solve.

iii. the DA survives the SIA objection

SIA response — SIA cancels the DA but introduces the Presumptuous Philosopher: SIA implies a tiny probability of a small world even before any sampling; this is not a solution but a trade of problemsThe most popular response to the DA is the Self-Indication Assumption (SIA), originating with Dieks: given that you exist, you should favour hypotheses under which more observers exist, because your existence is more likely if there are more observers. SIA does mathematically cancel the DA — but at a severe cost. Consider what SIA implies before any sampling. A cosmological theory that predicts 10100 observers gets vastly higher prior probability than one predicting merely 1010 observers — regardless of any physical evidence. As Bostrom’s Presumptuous Philosopher thought experiment shows: SIA implies that a philosopher sitting in an armchair can determine that the universe contains enormously many observers simply from the fact of their own existence, without doing any physics. This is not a solution to the DA — it is a flight from one problem into a worse one. The DA, applied carefully, is a genuine Bayesian update and it stands.

iv. what the DA implies practically

Practical implications — DA does not predict specific catastrophe; it raises baseline risk probability; combined with empirical x-risk evidence the case for urgency is strengthenedI want to be clear about what the DA implies and does not imply. It does not predict the mechanism of doom. It does not tell us whether humanity will end by nuclear war, pandemic, asteroid, or AI. It does not tell us exactly when doom will occur. What it does is deliver a Bayesian update on our prior probability that humanity has a very large future. Before the DA, we might have given 90% probability to humanity flourishing for many millions of years. After the DA, that probability should be substantially lower — the update is real and is not negligible. Combined with the direct empirical evidence about mechanisms — the evidence that Ord and Bostrom have assembled — the probability that we face extinction in the near-term future is alarmingly high. The moral response to this is not equanimity but urgency.

The argument is valid. The update is real. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The DA has the same structure as the urn inference: it is valid Bayesian reasoning from birth rank to N
1.Drawing ball 7 from a 10-ball urn vs. a million-ball urn: likelihood ratio 100,000:1 in favour of the small urn. Standard valid probabilistic inference.
2.The DA has identical structure: birth rank ~60 billion from a humanity of N. Hfew (N=200bn) vs. Hmany (N=200tn): likelihood ratio 1000:1 in favour of Hfew.
3.Under SSA, birth rank is uniformly distributed in {1,...,N}. The inference is mechanically identical to the urn case. Any objection to the DA must show what is different in structure — and no such difference has been successfully identified.
∴The DA is valid Bayesian reasoning: birth rank constitutes genuine evidence about N; the update toward smaller N is mechanically equivalent to the urn inference; rejecting it requires rejecting standard Bayesian probability theory.
Argument II — The SSA is the correct general principle for self-locating belief; the DA is its application
1.SSA: reason as if you are a random observer drawn from your reference class. This is used in every case of inference from self-location: waking up in a hospital, finding a message in a bottle, the Sleeping Beauty problem.
2.SSA is not a special assumption invented for the DA; it is the standard Bayesian treatment of indexical uncertainty about one’s own position.
3.Those who reject the DA by rejecting SSA must specify an alternative principle for self-locating reasoning that (a) handles the hospital case correctly and (b) does not generate worse paradoxes. No such alternative has been successfully specified.
∴The SSA is the correct epistemic principle; the DA is its straightforward application; rejecting the DA requires rejecting SSA as a general principle, which generates worse problems than it solves.
Argument III — SIA cancels the DA but introduces the Presumptuous Philosopher paradox; it trades one problem for a worse one
1.SIA: given your existence, favour hypotheses with more observers. SIA cancels the DA: multiplying the SSA-based posterior by P(existence | N) = k/N restores the prior. Mathematically exact cancellation.
2.But SIA implies: a universe with 10100 observers gets prior probability 1090 times higher than a universe with 1010 observers, from armchair reasoning alone. This is the Presumptuous Philosopher: pure a priori inference determines cosmological population sizes.
3.A principle that implies an armchair philosopher can determine whether the universe is large or small without doing any physics is not an acceptable epistemic principle. SIA is too permissive: it generates information from nothing.
∴SIA is not a satisfactory response to the DA: it trades the DA’s uncomfortable conclusion for a worse paradox (Presumptuous Philosopher); the DA remains standing as the lesser of two evils.
Argument IV — The DA delivers a genuine Bayesian update that raises baseline extinction probability; combined with empirical x-risk evidence it demands urgency
1.The DA does not predict the mechanism of doom or its timing. It delivers a Bayesian update on the prior probability that humanity has a very large future: this probability should be substantially lower than naive intuition suggests.
2.Combined with Ord’s direct empirical estimates (1-in-6 over this century from x-risk mechanisms), the update is not theoretical but practically significant: the aggregate probability of near-term extinction is alarmingly high.
3.The correct moral response to a genuine probability update of this magnitude is urgency and reorientation of priorities, not equanimity grounded in refusals to engage with the probability calculus.
∴The DA delivers a real probability update; combined with empirical evidence about x-risk mechanisms it creates a compelling case for treating existential risk as a priority; equanimity in the face of this evidence is not rationality but denial.

I want to shift the ground of this debate from the Doomsday Argument — which is genuinely inconclusive and should be treated with appropriate caution — to the question of existential risk more broadly, where I believe the case for urgency is overwhelming independently of the DA. My two contributions to this debate are: first, a formal resolution of the DA that shows it is neither simply valid nor simply refuted but genuinely inconclusive pending the reference class problem; and second, a direct argument that existential risks, properly assessed by their mechanisms rather than by probabilistic self-location arguments, warrant treating x-risk mitigation as a global policy priority.

Bostrom’s observation selection theory: the DA is inconclusive, not refuted
The DA requires a fully specified reference class R: the set of observers from which you are randomly sampled.
If R = all humans (narrow): DA goes through roughly as Leslie describes. Birth rank 60 billion is evidence against very large N.
If R = all possible observers in any world-state (wide): SIA enters; the cancellation occurs; the DA is blocked — but Presumptuous Philosopher follows.
If R = something in between: the verdict depends on precisely where the boundary is drawn — and no principled non-arbitrary rule for specifying R has been identified.
Bostrom’s verdict: The DA is neither straightforwardly valid nor refuted. Its conclusion depends on the reference class, which remains unsettled. The appropriate epistemic response is uncertainty about the size of the Bayesian update. But the direct x-risk arguments — from mechanisms, not from self-location — do not depend on resolving the reference class problem.
i. the reference class problem and its implications for the DA

Reference class problem — SSA requires specifying R; no principled specification of R is available; DA verdict varies radically with R; this makes DA genuinely inconclusiveThe DA requires the Self-Sampling Assumption, which requires a reference class — the set of observers from which I am supposedly a random sample. Leslie acknowledges this but treats it as a minor technical issue: just use a reasonable reference class and the argument goes through. I deny this. The reference class problem is not a minor technical issue but the central unresolved problem in anthropic reasoning. The DA’s conclusion — the size of the Bayesian update — is highly sensitive to the choice of R. With a narrow reference class (just humans of your general cognitive type), you get a large doomsday update. With a broader reference class (all possible observers), SIA enters and blocks the update. With intermediate choices, you get intermediate results. And there is no principled, non-arbitrary rule for picking R. The DA is therefore genuinely inconclusive: its conclusion is uncertain not just in magnitude but in direction.

ii. the astronomical value of existential risk reduction

Astronomical value — the expected value of preventing extinction includes the astronomical number of potential future humans; even tiny probability reductions on extinction have enormous expected valueSetting aside the DA entirely, the direct argument for prioritising existential risk reduction is simple and powerful. If humanity survives the current century and subsequent centuries, the number of humans who may eventually live is vast — potentially trillions or quadrillions of individuals over millions of years, or more. The total amount of welfare and value that could be created is correspondingly enormous. The expected value of reducing the probability of extinction by even a tiny fraction — 0.000001% — is therefore enormous: it is the expected value of preserving all those potential lives. This is the astronomical value thesis: x-risk reduction has expected value vastly greater than any intervention focused on currently living people, because it safeguards the potential welfare of everyone who might ever live. No DA required.

iii. direct mechanism-based x-risk assessment

Direct mechanisms — engineered pandemics, misaligned AI, nuclear conflict each have independent mechanistic arguments for catastrophic risk; these do not depend on SSAThe mechanistic case for existential risk is independent of any anthropic argument. Engineered pandemics: the combination of increasingly accessible biotechnology with the theoretical possibility of engineering pathogens of extreme lethality and transmissibility creates a mechanism that did not exist a century ago and whose risk profile has no historical precedent. Misaligned artificial general intelligence: if AGI systems develop goals that are not aligned with human values, the result could be the rapid transformation or elimination of the conditions for human civilisation — a mechanism that follows from the logic of optimisation processes under misaligned objectives. Nuclear war: even limited nuclear exchanges could trigger nuclear winter scenarios with global civilisational consequences. Each of these mechanisms has a structural case for catastrophic risk that is entirely independent of any probabilistic self-location argument.

iv. responding to the fanaticism objection

Fanaticism objection — Thorstad and others argue astronomical value makes any tiny probability reduction dominate; Bostrom argues this is not fanaticism but correct expected value reasoningThe main contemporary objection to the astronomical value thesis — pressed by Thorstad and others — is that it generates fanaticism: any tiny probability of preventing extinction has such enormous expected value that it dominates all other considerations. This is said to be a reductio. I disagree. Expected value reasoning is correct, and the conclusion that x-risk reduction dominates is not absurd but follows from correct moral mathematics applied to realistic estimates of the potential future. The fanaticism objection proves too much: it would also condemn any intervention whose expected benefit is very large relative to its cost — including many interventions we already endorse. The correct response to the scale of potential future value is not to discount it out of discomfort with its implications but to take it seriously and act accordingly.

The reference class problem leaves the DA inconclusive, not refuted. The direct x-risk case stands. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The reference class problem makes the DA inconclusive; its conclusion is highly sensitive to R; this is not a refutation but a suspension of verdict
1.The DA requires SSA, which requires R. Narrow R (your cognitive type) gives large doomsday update. Wide R (all possible observers) introduces SIA, blocking the update. No principled rule for picking R exists.

3.This is not a refutation of the DA but a demonstration that it requires more foundational work in anthropic reasoning before its conclusion can be established. The reference class problem is unsolved.
∴The DA is genuinely inconclusive pending resolution of the reference class problem; neither Leslie’s enthusiastic acceptance nor Dieks’s confident refutation is warranted; the correct response is calibrated uncertainty about the magnitude and direction of the update.

3.This reasoning is entirely independent of the DA and of SSA. It requires only: (a) that future people’s welfare matters; (b) that there could be many future people; (c) that our actions can affect extinction probability. All three are defensible.

I want to make a point that cuts through the entire technical debate about the Doomsday Argument: the case for treating existential risk as the paramount moral challenge of our time does not require the Doomsday Argument at all. In The Precipice I approached existential risk not through anthropic probability theory but through direct empirical and structural assessment of the actual mechanisms that could end or permanently curtail humanity’s future. The conclusion — that humanity currently faces existential risk of roughly 1-in-6 over this century, higher than at any previous period in our history — is based on base rates, expert elicitation, mechanism analysis, and comparative historical evidence. It is as independent of the self-sampling assumption as any other empirical assessment.

Let me be specific about what we face. This century has seen, for the first time in human history, the development of technologies with genuine extinction-level potential. Nuclear weapons provide the first: even current arsenals could, through a combination of direct destruction and nuclear winter, kill the vast majority of the human population. Engineered pandemics provide the second: the convergence of increasingly accessible genetic engineering technology with the theoretical possibility of engineering pathogens more lethal and transmissible than anything naturally occurring creates a risk mechanism that did not exist before the twentieth century. And misaligned artificial general intelligence — if and when it arrives — provides a third: an optimisation process operating at superhuman speed and intelligence with objectives not aligned to human welfare could rapidly transform the conditions for human civilisation in ways from which recovery might be impossible.

1-in-6 estimate — direct empirical assessment of x-risk mechanisms, not DA-based; nuclear, pandemic, AI, and other risks aggregate to roughly 1-in-6 by 2100; this is historically unprecedentedMy estimate of approximately 1-in-6 aggregate existential risk over this century is derived not from the DA but from summing independent mechanism-specific estimates: roughly 1-in-1000 from natural risks (asteroid, supervolcano), roughly 1-in-1000 from nuclear war, higher estimates for engineered pandemics and misaligned AI given the rapid development of these capabilities. The death of everyone alive today would be a catastrophe of inconceivable magnitude. Existential catastrophe is at least that bad and potentially orders of magnitude worse — because it forecloses not just current lives but all future lives. Even under a conservative estimate of the potential human future, the expected moral cost of a 1% probability of existential catastrophe is greater than the certain death of every human currently alive. This is not fanaticism; it is basic multiplication of probability by value.

We are in a period of unprecedented technological power combined with inadequate institutional safeguards. This structural mismatch is the precipice. Will MacAskill and I want to make the normative case that the opposition has most directly challenged: the case for strong longtermism, and with it, the astronomical value thesis. The claim of strong longtermism is that, in many decision situations, the best thing we can do is what is best for the long-run future. This is a normative thesis, not an empirical one, though it has empirical components. And I want to show that it is far more robust — across moral frameworks, across empirical assumptions, and across decision-theoretic views — than the opposition has acknowledged.

The opposition’s normative challenge, pressed most carefully by Thorstad, focuses on what he calls the astronomical value thesis: that existential risk mitigation has expected value vastly greater than other interventions. Thorstad argues that even granting the importance of the future, the specific claim that x-risk reduction has astronomical value is defeated by three mathematical mistakes that longtermists typically make. I want to address each of his mistakes directly and show that they do not defeat the astronomical value thesis.

i. the case for strong longtermism

Strong longtermism — expected value of long-run effects swamps short-run effects; this follows from: the vastness of the potential future, that future people matter, and that our actions affect the future; robust across moral frameworksThe core argument for strong longtermism is simple. The potential future of humanity, if we survive and flourish, contains an enormous amount of value — trillions of lives well-lived over millions of years. This future is not guaranteed: there are existential risks that could prevent it. Our actions affect the probability of reaching that future. Expected value reasoning then implies that actions which reduce existential risk — even very slightly — have enormous expected value, because they preserve a tiny probability of an enormous future. This argument does not depend on extreme assumptions. It follows from: future people’s welfare matters equally to present people’s; there may be enormously many future people; we have some influence over whether they exist. These three claims are all defensible across a wide range of moral views. Strong longtermism does not require total utilitarianism, or perfect impartiality, or fanatical risk-neutrality. It is a robust conclusion across moral uncertainty.

ii. responding to thorstad’s three mathematical mistakes

Thorstad’s mistakes — period risk vs cumulative risk; background risk; population dynamics; Greaves argues these do not defeat astronomical value thesis; they reduce magnitude but not the dominance conclusionThorstad identifies three alleged mistakes in x-risk mathematics. First, focusing on cumulative risk rather than period risk: using a high per-century risk to calculate expected long-run value double-counts risk across periods. Second, ignoring background risk: high existential risk means the expected future is shorter, reducing the value of marginal risk reduction. Third, neglecting population dynamics: the value of the future is not simply a function of population size. I published the first technical refutation of the Doomsday Argument in 1992, in the Philosophical Quarterly. My paper was titled “Doomsday — Or: the Dangers of Statistics.” The title was deliberately chosen: the DA is not a deep insight about our temporal position but a case of misapplied statistics — specifically, of failing to account for a prior probability that the DA’s Bayesian framework silently suppresses. Once that prior is reinstated, the DA vanishes.

Let me explain the mistake precisely. The DA applies Bayes’s theorem as follows: I observe my birth rank n. I update my prior probability over hypotheses about N (total humans). The update is: P(N | n) ∝ P(n | N) × P(N). Leslie sets P(n | N) = 1/N, reasoning that if N is the total, my birth rank is uniformly drawn from {1,...,N}. This produces the doomsday update. But Leslie suppresses a crucial question: what is P(N)? What is my prior probability over different values of N? And crucially: what determines whether I exist at all?

But Leslie suppresses: P(I exist | N)

I want to press a different and in some ways deeper objection to the DA than Dieks’s. Dieks accepts the Bayesian framework of the DA and argues that the correct prior cancels the update. My objection is more fundamental: the DA’s Self-Sampling Assumption is not just incomplete — it is incoherent when applied to a reference class that includes future individuals. You cannot be randomly selected from a set of individuals the majority of whom do not yet exist and may never exist. The notion of random sampling requires a mechanism, and no mechanism draws you from the set of all future humans.

Let me make this precise. Random sampling requires a sampling process: some physical or procedural mechanism by which the sample is drawn from the population. When I randomly select a ball from an urn, there is a physical mechanism — the ball is drawn by a hand from a container of balls. When I randomly sample from a population of humans who have ever lived, what is the mechanism? The SSA requires that I be drawn from the set {all humans who will ever live}, including humans yet unborn and humans who may never be born if doom occurs early. But there is no mechanism that draws an individual from this set. My birth occurred because of specific causal events — the meeting of my parents, their biological capacity to conceive, and a vast causal history stretching back millennia. None of this constitutes a sampling mechanism from the set of all possible future humans.

The Shooting Room Paradox — Eckhardt’s decisive challenge to the DA.

Consider a series of rounds of a game. In each round, a group of people enters a room. Two dice are rolled. If the result is double-six, everyone in the room is shot. If not, everyone leaves, the group size is multiplied by ten, and a new round begins.

Suppose you find yourself in the shooting room. What is the probability that you will be shot? The DA-style reasoning says: most people who ever enter the room are in the last round (since the last round is ten times larger than all previous rounds combined). If you are a “random sample” from all people who enter, you are almost certainly in the last round — so your probability of being shot is nearly 1 (since the last round ends with a double-six).

But this is wrong. The probability of double-six on any given roll is 1/36. Your probability of being shot, conditional on being in the room, is 1/36 — not nearly 1. The DA-style reasoning gives the wrong answer because it treats a future-contingent reference class as if it were already fixed. The correct probability tracks the actual random process (the dice), not the anthropic inference from your position in a reference class that does not yet exist.

I want to approach the Doomsday Argument from a different direction from both Dieks and Eckhardt. My 2003 paper in Synthese argued that both major versions of the Doomsday Argument — Leslie’s wedge and Gott’s line — rest on empirical assumptions about the sampling process that underlies our observations. These assumptions are not a priori valid probabilistic principles; they are substantive empirical claims that have testable consequences. And when those consequences are tested, the assumptions are empirically disconfirmed. The DA is not defeated by internal probabilistic objections alone; it is defeated by evidence.

This is an important methodological point. Leslie presents the DA as if it were a piece of pure Bayesian reasoning that is valid a priori, given only the SSA. But no Bayesian inference is free of empirical assumptions. Every application of Bayes’s theorem requires a likelihood function — a model of how the observation was generated — and that model is an empirical claim about the world. The DA’s likelihood function is P(birth rank = n | N) = 1/N — the claim that my birth rank is uniformly distributed given N. This is an empirical claim about the mechanism that determines birth order, and it is false.

i. the sampling assumption is an empirical claim

Empirical claim — the DA’s SSA is not a priori valid; it is an empirical claim about how observers are temporally distributed; this claim has testable consequences; when tested it failsThe DA’s SSA claims that each observer is equally likely to be located at any temporal position in the duration of their species. This is equivalent to claiming that observers are uniformly distributed across time within their species’ duration. But whether observers are uniformly distributed is an empirical question, not a logical necessity. In fact, the temporal distribution of human observers across history is not uniform: the human population has grown dramatically over time, meaning that more observers are alive at later times. A random draw from the set of all humans who ever live is much more likely to be born in the twentieth or twenty-first century than in 10,000 BCE — not because of any sampling procedure, but because of population dynamics. The SSA’s uniform distribution assumption is already empirically false, before we even consider whether sampling from the unborn is coherent.

ii. gott’s line and its testable consequences

Gott’s line tested — Gott’s Copernican principle predicts longevity distributions for any phenomenon; these predictions can be tested against historical data; when tested, they perform poorlyGott’s version of the DA uses the Copernican Principle — that we should not assume we occupy a special temporal position — to predict that the remaining duration of any phenomenon is of the same order of magnitude as its past duration. This generates the prediction: if humanity has existed for 200,000 years, we should expect it to last between 5,100 and 7.8 million more years (with 95% confidence). This prediction is testable. Gott himself tested it on Broadway shows and political leaders and found decent performance. But Sober shows that the underlying sampling assumption — that our observation is drawn uniformly from the phenomenon’s duration — is empirically false for biological species, which do not produce constant numbers of observations throughout their duration. Species produce far more observations (members) late in their duration than early. This disconfirms the uniform sampling assumption that drives Gott’s prediction.

iii. leslie’s wedge and the population growth problem

Leslie’s wedge tested — the uniform birth rank distribution assumed by Leslie is disconfirmed by actual population dynamics; non-uniform distributions change the posterior substantiallyLeslie’s version of the DA requires that birth rank be uniformly distributed in {1,...,N} conditional on N. But human population dynamics have been dramatically non-uniform: the population has grown exponentially, meaning that far more humans have been born in the twentieth century than in any previous century. If we adjust the DA to account for this non-uniformity — if we use the actual distribution of birth ranks given population dynamics rather than the uniform distribution — the doomsday update changes substantially or disappears. The observed birth rank of approximately 60 billion is not surprising given population dynamics that have produced explosive recent growth; it is roughly what we should expect regardless of when doom occurs. The DA’s inference from birth rank to N is therefore not robust to realistic modelling of how birth ranks are actually distributed.

iv. empirical disconfirmation as the decisive refutation

Empirical refutation — sampling assumptions are testable; when tested, they fail; this is a decisive refutation that does not depend on resolving the SIA/SSA debate; it defeats the DA on purely empirical groundsThe great advantage of my approach over both Dieks’s and Eckhardt’s is that it does not require resolving contested questions in the theory of probability or the metaphysics of reference classes. It requires only empirical testing of a testable claim. The DA assumes a particular model of how observers are temporally distributed. That model generates predictions about the distribution of birth ranks conditional on N. Those predictions are testable and when tested they are disconfirmed by the actual pattern of human population dynamics. This empirical disconfirmation defeats the DA regardless of whether SIA is correct, regardless of whether the SSA is coherent, and regardless of how the reference class problem is resolved. The DA is simply not an adequate model of how our temporal position is generated, and this adequacy failure defeats it on purely empirical grounds that are prior to all the philosophical debates about anthropic reasoning.

The sampling assumption is an empirical claim. It fails empirical testing. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The DA’s SSA is an empirical claim about temporal distribution of observers; it is already disconfirmed by population dynamics
1.SSA claims observers are uniformly distributed across the duration of their species. This is an empirical claim, not a logical necessity — it requires that the number of observers born per unit time is constant.
2.Human population has grown dramatically over time: exponentially more humans are born in recent centuries than in earlier ones. The distribution of observers is not uniform; it is heavily weighted toward recent times.
3.The SSA’s uniform distribution assumption is therefore empirically disconfirmed before we even consider whether sampling from future individuals is coherent.
∴The SSA’s uniform temporal distribution assumption is an empirical claim that is already disconfirmed by human population dynamics; the DA does not model how observers are actually temporally distributed, so its likelihood function P(n | N) = 1/N is false.
Argument II — Gott’s line is testable and performs poorly for biological species whose observation rates change over time
1.Gott’s line: if you observe a phenomenon at a random point in its duration, predict remaining duration of similar magnitude to past duration. Generates 95% CI for human longevity of 5,100 to 7.8 million more years.
2.The underlying assumption: observation occurs at a uniformly random point in the phenomenon’s duration. For Broadway shows and presidential terms this may be approximately true. For biological species it is not: species produce far more observers late in their duration than early.
3.The non-uniform observation rate for biological species disconfirms the sampling assumption that drives Gott’s predictions; the predictions are therefore unreliable for the human case.
∴Gott’s line rests on a uniform-sampling assumption that is empirically false for biological species; the predictions it generates for human longevity are therefore unreliable; the empirical disconfirmation of the sampling assumption defeats Gott’s version of the DA.
Argument III — Leslie’s wedge uses a uniform birth rank distribution that is disconfirmed by actual population dynamics
1.Leslie’s DA: P(n | N) = 1/N (birth rank uniformly distributed in {1,...,N}). This assumes equal probability of being born at any rank — which requires a roughly constant birth rate over human history.
2.Human birth rates have been dramatically non-constant: exponential growth in the twentieth century means the vast majority of births are concentrated in recent centuries. The actual distribution of birth ranks is non-uniform.
3.Adjusting the DA for actual population dynamics substantially alters or eliminates the doomsday update: a birth rank of 60 billion is roughly expected under realistic population models regardless of when doom occurs.
∴Leslie’s wedge uses a uniform birth rank distribution that is empirically false; when the actual non-uniform distribution is used, the doomsday update changes substantially or vanishes; the DA is not robust to realistic population modelling.
Argument IV — Empirical disconfirmation defeats the DA independently of philosophical debates about SIA, SSA, and reference classes
1.The DA’s sampling assumption generates testable predictions about birth rank distributions. These predictions are empirically disconfirmed by actual population dynamics.
2.This empirical refutation does not require resolving the SIA/SSA debate, the reference class problem, or any other contested question in anthropic reasoning theory. It requires only empirical testing of a testable claim.
3.The DA is an inadequate model of how our temporal position is generated. This adequacy failure is prior to all philosophical debates and defeats the DA on purely empirical grounds.
∴Empirical disconfirmation of the sampling assumption is a decisive refutation of the DA that is independent of all philosophical debates about anthropic reasoning; the DA fails because it mismodels the actual process that determines birth ranks.

I want to address the proposition’s strongest position — not Leslie’s Doomsday Argument, which my colleagues have refuted, but the moral mathematics of existential risk that Bostrom, Ord, and Greaves and MacAskill defend. Specifically, I want to challenge the astronomical value thesis: the claim that existential risk mitigation is orders of magnitude more valuable than any other use of philanthropic or policy resources. This claim, I will show, contains three mathematical mistakes that, when corrected, substantially reduce its force. And more strikingly, the proposition faces an internal paradox: the more pessimistic you are about existential risk — the higher you estimate it to be — the lower the expected value of any marginal intervention to reduce it.

Let me first acknowledge what I accept. Future people matter morally. Existential catastrophe would be an enormous tragedy. There is something to the thought that reducing extinction risk is important. What I deny is the specific quantitative claim that existential risk mitigation has “astronomical” value — value so vastly greater than other interventions that it should dominate all moral priority-setting. This claim requires several steps of mathematical reasoning that I will show are flawed.

Thorstad’s three mistakes in the moral mathematics of x-risk
Mistake 1: Cumulative vs. period risk
Longtermists often cite high cumulative x-risk (Ord: 1-in-6 over this century) to calculate the value of x-risk mitigation. But the relevant quantity for calculating the value of a marginal intervention is period risk — the risk in the period during which the intervention is effective. Cumulative risk over a century is not the right denominator for calculating the value of an intervention that reduces risk in the next decade.
Mistake 2: Background risk
High x-risk pessimism implies high background probability of extinction. If the per-century risk is 1/6, then even if we reduce it slightly, the probability of eventually going extinct remains very high. The astronomical value of the far future only materialises if we actually survive to reach it. High background risk dramatically reduces the expected value of marginal risk reduction.
Mistake 3: Population dynamics
The astronomical value argument assumes that the expected future population is enormous. But realistic population projections show declining fertility rates; the expected future population may be much smaller than the trillions assumed. Smaller expected population substantially reduces the astronomical value thesis.
i. the high-risk low-reward paradox

High-risk low-reward — x-risk pessimism and astronomical value are in tension; higher background risk means lower expected value of marginal risk reduction; the proposition cannot hold both simultaneouslyThe most striking result in my 2023 Philosophy and Public Affairs paper is what I call the high-risk low-reward paradox. Longtermists like Ord hold two positions simultaneously: (1) existential risk pessimism — humanity faces high existential risk this century, perhaps 1-in-6; and (2) the astronomical value thesis — reducing existential risk has astronomical expected value because of the vast potential future. But these two positions are in tension. The expected value of a marginal reduction in existential risk depends on the probability that humanity survives to realise the future value. If existential risk is very high — say, 16% per century — then the expected remaining duration of humanity is relatively short and the expected value of the far future is correspondingly reduced. Across a range of reasonable assumptions, existential risk pessimism significantly reduces the expected value of marginal risk mitigation, potentially by orders of magnitude. The proposition cannot coherently hold both high pessimism and astronomical value.

ii. the period risk mistake

Period risk mistake — using cumulative risk to justify marginal intervention is a category error; the relevant risk is in the period of intervention; this substantially reduces the expected value of specific interventionsThe first mistake is using cumulative existential risk to motivate the value of specific period-limited interventions. Ord’s 1-in-6 estimate is a cumulative estimate over the twenty-first century. But when we calculate the expected value of, say, a policy intervention that reduces biosecurity risk over the next decade, the relevant quantity is the period risk — the risk of extinction in the next decade from bioweapons specifically. This is orders of magnitude lower than the cumulative century risk. Using the cumulative figure to justify the astronomical value of a decade-limited bioweapons-specific intervention is a category error. It conflates the risk landscape over the whole century with the marginal risk reduction achievable by a specific intervention in a specific period targeting a specific risk category. The expected value of any real-world intervention, correctly calculated, is substantially lower than the astronomical figures longtermists typically cite.

iii. the expected future and realistic population projections

Population projections — the astronomical value thesis assumes enormous future populations; realistic projections show declining fertility; expected future population may be much smaller; this substantially reduces the astronomical valueThe astronomical value thesis gets much of its force from the assumption that if humanity survives, we will eventually number in the trillions or more over millions of years. But realistic population projections paint a more modest picture. Global fertility rates are declining; UN projections suggest world population may peak this century and then decline. If realistic trajectories are used rather than the maximal speculative future assumed by Bostrom and MacAskill, the expected value of the future is substantially smaller — still large, but not necessarily so astronomically large that it dominates all other considerations. The astronomical value thesis requires not just that some future value is possible but that the expected future value, averaged across all possible outcomes weighted by probability, is enormous. This is a much stronger claim and requires much stronger support than the mere possibility of a large future.

iv. what survives the corrections

What survives — x-risk mitigation remains important after corrections; Thorstad does not claim it is worthless; he claims it is not orders of magnitude more valuable than other interventions; the dominance claim failsI want to be clear about what my critique does and does not establish. I do not claim that existential risk mitigation is unimportant or that it should be deprioritised. I accept that future people matter, that extinction would be bad, and that there are good reasons to invest resources in x-risk reduction. What I deny is the specific quantitative claim that x-risk mitigation is orders of magnitude more valuable than other interventions — the astronomical value thesis as a dominance claim. After correcting the three mistakes I have identified, the expected value of x-risk mitigation is substantially lower than leading estimates suggest, potentially low enough that near-term interventions — global health, poverty reduction, climate adaptation — are competitive with it. The moral priority ordering that the proposition claims is not robustly established by correct expected value reasoning.

Pessimism undermines astronomical value. The corrections matter. The dominance claim fails. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The high-risk low-reward paradox: x-risk pessimism and astronomical value are in tension; higher background risk reduces the expected value of marginal reduction
1.Existential risk pessimism (Ord: 1-in-6 per century) + astronomical value thesis (vast expected future) are both held by leading longtermists. But high existential risk implies shorter expected remaining duration of humanity.
2.The expected value of marginal risk reduction depends on the expected remaining duration: higher background risk means shorter expected duration means lower expected future value that the intervention preserves.
3.Across a range of assumptions, existential risk pessimism significantly reduces the expected value of marginal risk mitigation — potentially to the point where the astronomical value thesis fails. The two positions are in internal tension.
∴The high-risk low-reward paradox shows that x-risk pessimism and astronomical value cannot both be held coherently: higher background risk reduces the expected value of marginal interventions, potentially defeating the astronomical value thesis the proposition needs to justify its priority claims.
Argument II — The period risk mistake: using cumulative century risk to justify decade-limited interventions is a category error
1.Ord’s 1-in-6 is a cumulative century risk. But the value of a specific period-limited intervention is determined by the period risk — the risk in the period during which the intervention is effective — not the cumulative risk.
2.Period risk for a specific category (bioweapons, AI misalignment) over a specific period (next decade) is orders of magnitude lower than the cumulative century risk across all categories. Using the cumulative figure conflates these.
3.The expected value of any real-world intervention, calculated using period risk, is substantially lower than the figures longtermists cite. The period risk mistake systematically inflates expected value estimates.
∴The period risk mistake is a category error that systematically inflates expected value estimates for x-risk interventions; correcting it substantially reduces those estimates and weakens the astronomical value thesis.
Argument III — Realistic population projections reduce the expected future substantially below the astronomical figures assumed
1.The astronomical value thesis assumes that if humanity survives, we will eventually number in the trillions over millions of years. This assumption drives the astronomical value claim.
2.Realistic population projections show declining fertility; UN projections suggest population may peak this century and decline. Expected future population on realistic trajectories is much smaller than the speculative maximum.
3.The astronomical value thesis requires that expected future value, averaged across all possible outcomes weighted by probability, is enormous. This requires not just that the maximum future is large but that the expected future is large. Realistic projections cast doubt on this.
∴Realistic population projections substantially reduce the expected value of the future below the astronomical figures assumed by the proposition; the astronomical value thesis requires stronger empirical support than the mere possibility of a large future.
Argument IV — What survives the corrections: x-risk mitigation is important but not orders of magnitude more so than near-term interventions
1.Thorstad accepts: future people matter, extinction is bad, x-risk mitigation is worth pursuing. What he denies is the dominance claim — that x-risk mitigation is orders of magnitude more valuable than competing interventions.
2.After correcting the three mistakes (period risk, background risk, population dynamics), the expected value of x-risk mitigation is substantially lower than leading estimates, potentially making near-term interventions (global health, poverty reduction) competitive.
3.The moral priority ordering that the proposition claims — that x-risk mitigation should dominate all other moral priorities — is not robustly established by correct expected value reasoning after these corrections are applied.
∴The three corrections defeat the dominance version of the astronomical value thesis; x-risk mitigation remains important but is not robustly orders of magnitude more valuable than near-term interventions; the proposition’s claim to absolute moral priority fails once the mathematics is done correctly.
