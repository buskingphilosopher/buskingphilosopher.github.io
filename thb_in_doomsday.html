Logical Arguments

I want to begin with what is, I believe, one of the most important and most misunderstood arguments in contemporary philosophy: the Doomsday Argument. I have been developing and defending this argument since 1989, and I have spent thirty years watching it be misunderstood, dismissed, and refuted — and watching those refutations fail under pressure. Let me state it with full precision before the opposition attacks it.

The argument is this. You exist. You are a human being. There have been, as best we can estimate, approximately 60 to 100 billion humans born before you or roughly contemporaneous with you. There will be some total number N of humans who ever live. The question the Doomsday Argument asks is: what does your birth rank — approximately 60 billion — tell you about N? And the answer, delivered by a straightforward application of Bayesian probability theory, is: it is evidence that N is not orders of magnitude larger than 60 billion. If N were, say, 6 trillion, we would expect the vast majority of humans who ever lived to have birth ranks greater than 60 billion — yet here you are, among the first one percent. This is surprising if N is enormous; it is not surprising if N is relatively modest. Therefore our birth rank is evidence that doom — the cessation of the human species — is closer than our naive prior estimates suggest.

The Doomsday Argument: formal structure
Let n = your birth rank (∼60 billion). Let N = total number of humans ever.
Self-Sampling Assumption (SSA): You should reason as if you are a random observer drawn uniformly from the set of all observers in your reference class.
Under SSA: P(n | N) = 1/N for each n ≤ N (your birth rank is drawn uniformly from {1,...,N}).
By Bayes: P(N | n) ∝ P(n | N) × P(N)
For two hypotheses Hfew (N = 200 billion) and Hmany (N = 200 trillion):
P(n=60bn | Hfew) = 1/200bn     P(n=60bn | Hmany) = 1/200tn
Likelihood ratio: P(n | Hfew) / P(n | Hmany) = 1000
Conclusion: Your birth rank shifts your probability estimates toward smaller N by a factor of 1000 relative to the prior ratio. If your prior gave Hfew and Hmany equal probability, your posterior gives Hfew a 1000:1 advantage. This is the Doomsday update — and it is valid Bayesian reasoning.
i. the urn analogy and why it works

Urn analogy — drawing ball 7 from one of two urns (10 vs 1,000,000 balls) is strong evidence for the small urn; the DA has identical structure; the inference is validThe argument is most easily understood through the urn analogy. You are presented with two urns. One contains ten balls numbered 1 to 10. The other contains one million balls numbered 1 to 1,000,000. You draw a ball at random and it shows the number 7. What does this tell you about which urn you drew from? Clearly it is strong evidence that you drew from the small urn: if you were drawing from the million-ball urn, getting a number as low as 7 would have probability one in a million; if you were drawing from the ten-ball urn, it has probability one in ten. The likelihood ratio is 100,000 in favour of the small urn. The Doomsday Argument has exactly this structure. Humanity is one of two urns: one with 200 billion total humans, one with 200 trillion. You have drawn birth rank 60 billion. This is strong evidence for the small urn — for a humanity that ends relatively soon. The inference is valid Bayesian reasoning, and refusing to accept it requires rejecting standard probability theory.

ii. the self-sampling assumption is the correct epistemic principle

SSA — reasoning as random observer from your reference class is the correct Bayesian principle for self-locating belief; it is used in other contexts we accept; DA is just its applicationThe Self-Sampling Assumption — that you should reason as if you are a random sample from the set of observers in your reference class — is not invented for the purpose of generating the Doomsday Argument. It is the correct general principle for reasoning under self-locating uncertainty. We use it in every context where we need to infer something about the world from our own position in it. If I wake up with no memory and find myself in a hospital, I reason: most people who find themselves in hospitals are patients, not surgeons — so I am probably a patient. This is SSA in action. The DA is simply SSA applied to the question of our birth rank. Those who reject the DA must explain what is wrong with SSA as a general epistemic principle — and all attempts to do so have produced worse problems than the ones they tried to solve.

iii. the DA survives the SIA objection

SIA response — SIA cancels the DA but introduces the Presumptuous Philosopher: SIA implies a tiny probability of a small world even before any sampling; this is not a solution but a trade of problemsThe most popular response to the DA is the Self-Indication Assumption (SIA), originating with Dieks: given that you exist, you should favour hypotheses under which more observers exist, because your existence is more likely if there are more observers. SIA does mathematically cancel the DA — but at a severe cost. Consider what SIA implies before any sampling. A cosmological theory that predicts 10100 observers gets vastly higher prior probability than one predicting merely 1010 observers — regardless of any physical evidence. As Bostrom’s Presumptuous Philosopher thought experiment shows: SIA implies that a philosopher sitting in an armchair can determine that the universe contains enormously many observers simply from the fact of their own existence, without doing any physics. This is not a solution to the DA — it is a flight from one problem into a worse one. The DA, applied carefully, is a genuine Bayesian update and it stands.

iv. what the DA implies practically

Practical implications — DA does not predict specific catastrophe; it raises baseline risk probability; combined with empirical x-risk evidence the case for urgency is strengthenedI want to be clear about what the DA implies and does not imply. It does not predict the mechanism of doom. It does not tell us whether humanity will end by nuclear war, pandemic, asteroid, or AI. It does not tell us exactly when doom will occur. What it does is deliver a Bayesian update on our prior probability that humanity has a very large future. Before the DA, we might have given 90% probability to humanity flourishing for many millions of years. After the DA, that probability should be substantially lower — the update is real and is not negligible. Combined with the direct empirical evidence about mechanisms — the evidence that Ord and Bostrom have assembled — the probability that we face extinction in the near-term future is alarmingly high. The moral response to this is not equanimity but urgency.

The argument is valid. The update is real. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The DA has the same structure as the urn inference: it is valid Bayesian reasoning from birth rank to N
1.Drawing ball 7 from a 10-ball urn vs. a million-ball urn: likelihood ratio 100,000:1 in favour of the small urn. Standard valid probabilistic inference.
2.The DA has identical structure: birth rank ~60 billion from a humanity of N. Hfew (N=200bn) vs. Hmany (N=200tn): likelihood ratio 1000:1 in favour of Hfew.
3.Under SSA, birth rank is uniformly distributed in {1,...,N}. The inference is mechanically identical to the urn case. Any objection to the DA must show what is different in structure — and no such difference has been successfully identified.
∴The DA is valid Bayesian reasoning: birth rank constitutes genuine evidence about N; the update toward smaller N is mechanically equivalent to the urn inference; rejecting it requires rejecting standard Bayesian probability theory.
Argument II — The SSA is the correct general principle for self-locating belief; the DA is its application
1.SSA: reason as if you are a random observer drawn from your reference class. This is used in every case of inference from self-location: waking up in a hospital, finding a message in a bottle, the Sleeping Beauty problem.
2.SSA is not a special assumption invented for the DA; it is the standard Bayesian treatment of indexical uncertainty about one’s own position.
3.Those who reject the DA by rejecting SSA must specify an alternative principle for self-locating reasoning that (a) handles the hospital case correctly and (b) does not generate worse paradoxes. No such alternative has been successfully specified.
∴The SSA is the correct epistemic principle; the DA is its straightforward application; rejecting the DA requires rejecting SSA as a general principle, which generates worse problems than it solves.
Argument III — SIA cancels the DA but introduces the Presumptuous Philosopher paradox; it trades one problem for a worse one
1.SIA: given your existence, favour hypotheses with more observers. SIA cancels the DA: multiplying the SSA-based posterior by P(existence | N) = k/N restores the prior. Mathematically exact cancellation.
2.But SIA implies: a universe with 10100 observers gets prior probability 1090 times higher than a universe with 1010 observers, from armchair reasoning alone. This is the Presumptuous Philosopher: pure a priori inference determines cosmological population sizes.
3.A principle that implies an armchair philosopher can determine whether the universe is large or small without doing any physics is not an acceptable epistemic principle. SIA is too permissive: it generates information from nothing.
∴SIA is not a satisfactory response to the DA: it trades the DA’s uncomfortable conclusion for a worse paradox (Presumptuous Philosopher); the DA remains standing as the lesser of two evils.
Argument IV — The DA delivers a genuine Bayesian update that raises baseline extinction probability; combined with empirical x-risk evidence it demands urgency
1.The DA does not predict the mechanism of doom or its timing. It delivers a Bayesian update on the prior probability that humanity has a very large future: this probability should be substantially lower than naive intuition suggests.
2.Combined with Ord’s direct empirical estimates (1-in-6 over this century from x-risk mechanisms), the update is not theoretical but practically significant: the aggregate probability of near-term extinction is alarmingly high.
3.The correct moral response to a genuine probability update of this magnitude is urgency and reorientation of priorities, not equanimity grounded in refusals to engage with the probability calculus.
∴The DA delivers a real probability update; combined with empirical evidence about x-risk mechanisms it creates a compelling case for treating existential risk as a priority; equanimity in the face of this evidence is not rationality but denial.

I want to shift the ground of this debate from the Doomsday Argument — which is genuinely inconclusive and should be treated with appropriate caution — to the question of existential risk more broadly, where I believe the case for urgency is overwhelming independently of the DA. My two contributions to this debate are: first, a formal resolution of the DA that shows it is neither simply valid nor simply refuted but genuinely inconclusive pending the reference class problem; and second, a direct argument that existential risks, properly assessed by their mechanisms rather than by probabilistic self-location arguments, warrant treating x-risk mitigation as a global policy priority.

Bostrom’s observation selection theory: the DA is inconclusive, not refuted
The DA requires a fully specified reference class R: the set of observers from which you are randomly sampled.
If R = all humans (narrow): DA goes through roughly as Leslie describes. Birth rank 60 billion is evidence against very large N.
If R = all possible observers in any world-state (wide): SIA enters; the cancellation occurs; the DA is blocked — but Presumptuous Philosopher follows.
If R = something in between: the verdict depends on precisely where the boundary is drawn — and no principled non-arbitrary rule for specifying R has been identified.
Bostrom’s verdict: The DA is neither straightforwardly valid nor refuted. Its conclusion depends on the reference class, which remains unsettled. The appropriate epistemic response is uncertainty about the size of the Bayesian update. But the direct x-risk arguments — from mechanisms, not from self-location — do not depend on resolving the reference class problem.
i. the reference class problem and its implications for the DA

Reference class problem — SSA requires specifying R; no principled specification of R is available; DA verdict varies radically with R; this makes DA genuinely inconclusiveThe DA requires the Self-Sampling Assumption, which requires a reference class — the set of observers from which I am supposedly a random sample. Leslie acknowledges this but treats it as a minor technical issue: just use a reasonable reference class and the argument goes through. I deny this. The reference class problem is not a minor technical issue but the central unresolved problem in anthropic reasoning. The DA’s conclusion — the size of the Bayesian update — is highly sensitive to the choice of R. With a narrow reference class (just humans of your general cognitive type), you get a large doomsday update. With a broader reference class (all possible observers), SIA enters and blocks the update. With intermediate choices, you get intermediate results. And there is no principled, non-arbitrary rule for picking R. The DA is therefore genuinely inconclusive: its conclusion is uncertain not just in magnitude but in direction.

ii. the astronomical value of existential risk reduction

Astronomical value — the expected value of preventing extinction includes the astronomical number of potential future humans; even tiny probability reductions on extinction have enormous expected valueSetting aside the DA entirely, the direct argument for prioritising existential risk reduction is simple and powerful. If humanity survives the current century and subsequent centuries, the number of humans who may eventually live is vast — potentially trillions or quadrillions of individuals over millions of years, or more. The total amount of welfare and value that could be created is correspondingly enormous. The expected value of reducing the probability of extinction by even a tiny fraction — 0.000001% — is therefore enormous: it is the expected value of preserving all those potential lives. This is the astronomical value thesis: x-risk reduction has expected value vastly greater than any intervention focused on currently living people, because it safeguards the potential welfare of everyone who might ever live. No DA required.

iii. direct mechanism-based x-risk assessment

Direct mechanisms — engineered pandemics, misaligned AI, nuclear conflict each have independent mechanistic arguments for catastrophic risk; these do not depend on SSAThe mechanistic case for existential risk is independent of any anthropic argument. Engineered pandemics: the combination of increasingly accessible biotechnology with the theoretical possibility of engineering pathogens of extreme lethality and transmissibility creates a mechanism that did not exist a century ago and whose risk profile has no historical precedent. Misaligned artificial general intelligence: if AGI systems develop goals that are not aligned with human values, the result could be the rapid transformation or elimination of the conditions for human civilisation — a mechanism that follows from the logic of optimisation processes under misaligned objectives. Nuclear war: even limited nuclear exchanges could trigger nuclear winter scenarios with global civilisational consequences. Each of these mechanisms has a structural case for catastrophic risk that is entirely independent of any probabilistic self-location argument.

iv. responding to the fanaticism objection

Fanaticism objection — Thorstad and others argue astronomical value makes any tiny probability reduction dominate; Bostrom argues this is not fanaticism but correct expected value reasoningThe main contemporary objection to the astronomical value thesis — pressed by Thorstad and others — is that it generates fanaticism: any tiny probability of preventing extinction has such enormous expected value that it dominates all other considerations. This is said to be a reductio. I disagree. Expected value reasoning is correct, and the conclusion that x-risk reduction dominates is not absurd but follows from correct moral mathematics applied to realistic estimates of the potential future. The fanaticism objection proves too much: it would also condemn any intervention whose expected benefit is very large relative to its cost — including many interventions we already endorse. The correct response to the scale of potential future value is not to discount it out of discomfort with its implications but to take it seriously and act accordingly.

The reference class problem leaves the DA inconclusive, not refuted. The direct x-risk case stands. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The reference class problem makes the DA inconclusive; its conclusion is highly sensitive to R; this is not a refutation but a suspension of verdict
1.The DA requires SSA, which requires R. Narrow R (your cognitive type) gives large doomsday update. Wide R (all possible observers) introduces SIA, blocking the update. No principled rule for picking R exists.

3.This is not a refutation of the DA but a demonstration that it requires more foundational work in anthropic reasoning before its conclusion can be established. The reference class problem is unsolved.
∴The DA is genuinely inconclusive pending resolution of the reference class problem; neither Leslie’s enthusiastic acceptance nor Dieks’s confident refutation is warranted; the correct response is calibrated uncertainty about the magnitude and direction of the update.

3.This reasoning is entirely independent of the DA and of SSA. It requires only: (a) that future people’s welfare matters; (b) that there could be many future people; (c) that our actions can affect extinction probability. All three are defensible.

I want to make a point that cuts through the entire technical debate about the Doomsday Argument: the case for treating existential risk as the paramount moral challenge of our time does not require the Doomsday Argument at all. In The Precipice I approached existential risk not through anthropic probability theory but through direct empirical and structural assessment of the actual mechanisms that could end or permanently curtail humanity’s future. The conclusion — that humanity currently faces existential risk of roughly 1-in-6 over this century, higher than at any previous period in our history — is based on base rates, expert elicitation, mechanism analysis, and comparative historical evidence. It is as independent of the self-sampling assumption as any other empirical assessment.

Let me be specific about what we face. This century has seen, for the first time in human history, the development of technologies with genuine extinction-level potential. Nuclear weapons provide the first: even current arsenals could, through a combination of direct destruction and nuclear winter, kill the vast majority of the human population. Engineered pandemics provide the second: the convergence of increasingly accessible genetic engineering technology with the theoretical possibility of engineering pathogens more lethal and transmissible than anything naturally occurring creates a risk mechanism that did not exist before the twentieth century. And misaligned artificial general intelligence — if and when it arrives — provides a third: an optimisation process operating at superhuman speed and intelligence with objectives not aligned to human welfare could rapidly transform the conditions for human civilisation in ways from which recovery might be impossible.

1-in-6 estimate — direct empirical assessment of x-risk mechanisms, not DA-based; nuclear, pandemic, AI, and other risks aggregate to roughly 1-in-6 by 2100; this is historically unprecedentedMy estimate of approximately 1-in-6 aggregate existential risk over this century is derived not from the DA but from summing independent mechanism-specific estimates: roughly 1-in-1000 from natural risks (asteroid, supervolcano), roughly 1-in-1000 from nuclear war, higher estimates for engineered pandemics and misaligned AI given the rapid development of these capabilities. The death of everyone alive today would be a catastrophe of inconceivable magnitude. Existential catastrophe is at least that bad and potentially orders of magnitude worse — because it forecloses not just current lives but all future lives. Even under a conservative estimate of the potential human future, the expected moral cost of a 1% probability of existential catastrophe is greater than the certain death of every human currently alive. This is not fanaticism; it is basic multiplication of probability by value.

We are in a period of unprecedented technological power combined with inadequate institutional safeguards. This structural mismatch is the precipice. Will MacAskill and I want to make the normative case that the opposition has most directly challenged: the case for strong longtermism, and with it, the astronomical value thesis. The claim of strong longtermism is that, in many decision situations, the best thing we can do is what is best for the long-run future. This is a normative thesis, not an empirical one, though it has empirical components. And I want to show that it is far more robust — across moral frameworks, across empirical assumptions, and across decision-theoretic views — than the opposition has acknowledged.

The opposition’s normative challenge, pressed most carefully by Thorstad, focuses on what he calls the astronomical value thesis: that existential risk mitigation has expected value vastly greater than other interventions. Thorstad argues that even granting the importance of the future, the specific claim that x-risk reduction has astronomical value is defeated by three mathematical mistakes that longtermists typically make. I want to address each of his mistakes directly and show that they do not defeat the astronomical value thesis.

i. the case for strong longtermism

Strong longtermism — expected value of long-run effects swamps short-run effects; this follows from: the vastness of the potential future, that future people matter, and that our actions affect the future; robust across moral frameworksThe core argument for strong longtermism is simple. The potential future of humanity, if we survive and flourish, contains an enormous amount of value — trillions of lives well-lived over millions of years. This future is not guaranteed: there are existential risks that could prevent it. Our actions affect the probability of reaching that future. Expected value reasoning then implies that actions which reduce existential risk — even very slightly — have enormous expected value, because they preserve a tiny probability of an enormous future. This argument does not depend on extreme assumptions. It follows from: future people’s welfare matters equally to present people’s; there may be enormously many future people; we have some influence over whether they exist. These three claims are all defensible across a wide range of moral views. Strong longtermism does not require total utilitarianism, or perfect impartiality, or fanatical risk-neutrality. It is a robust conclusion across moral uncertainty.

ii. responding to thorstad’s three mathematical mistakes

Thorstad’s mistakes — period risk vs cumulative risk; background risk; population dynamics; Greaves argues these do not defeat astronomical value thesis; they reduce magnitude but not the dominance conclusionThorstad identifies three alleged mistakes in x-risk mathematics. First, focusing on cumulative risk rather than period risk: using a high per-century risk to calculate expected long-run value double-counts risk across periods. Second, ignoring background risk: high existential risk means the expected future is shorter, reducing the value of marginal risk reduction. Third, neglecting population dynamics: the value of the future is not simply a function of population size. I accept that these corrections are relevant and reduce leading estimates of the expected value of x-risk reduction. But I deny that they defeat the astronomical value thesis. Even with Thorstad’s corrections applied, the expected value of reducing existential risk by a small amount — if it can be done — remains vast relative to the expected value of near-term interventions. The corrections reduce the magnitude of the advantage; they do not eliminate it.

iii. the neglect of future people

Future people neglect — ordinary moral thought neglects future people systematically; we focus on identifiable present individuals; but future people’s welfare is just as real; this is the central normative claim of longtermismThe deepest normative claim of longtermism is this: the welfare of future people matters morally, and it matters just as much as the welfare of present people. This is contested. Most people, in their practical moral reasoning, give far less weight to distant future people than to present people. MacAskill’s analogy is instructive: distance in time is morally no more relevant than distance in space. Just as it is now widely accepted that the welfare of distant people — in other countries, on other continents — matters as much as the welfare of our neighbours, so the welfare of distant future people should matter as much as the welfare of people alive today. If this is right, and if the potential future of humanity is large, then the case for prioritising the long-run future is not merely defensible but compelling. The opposition has not challenged this normative premise; they have challenged the mathematics. But the mathematics, even corrected, supports the conclusion.

iv. the epistemic challenge and why it does not defeat longtermism

Epistemic challenge — we cannot predict the long-run effects of our actions; Tarsney’s epistemic challenge; but this affects both long-run and short-run reasoning; under uncertainty, x-risk reduction is a robust strategyThe most serious challenge to longtermism that is not addressed by the opposition in this debate is Tarsney’s epistemic challenge: given radical uncertainty about the long-run effects of our actions, perhaps we cannot reliably identify which actions are best for the long-run future. If the long-run effects of our actions are effectively unpredictable, the expected value of any action as assessed from the long-run perspective is essentially uniform — and longtermism’s dominance claim collapses. MacAskill and I have responded to this challenge. Certain classes of action — x-risk reduction — have long-run effects that are not uniformly uncertain. The causal pathway from “prevent extinction” to “long-run human flourishing” is clearer and more robust than for other interventions. X-risk reduction is a particularly tractable target for longtermist reasoning precisely because its long-run effects, while uncertain, are more predictably positive than those of most other interventions.

Future welfare matters. The future is vast. Extinction forecloses it. Urgency follows. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Strong longtermism is robust across moral frameworks: future welfare matters, the future may be vast, and our actions affect it
1.Future people’s welfare matters morally, and it matters equally to present people’s welfare. This is a defensible position across a wide range of moral frameworks — not just total utilitarianism.
2.The potential future of humanity, if we survive and flourish, contains an enormous amount of potential value: trillions of lives well-lived over millions of years.
3.Our actions affect existential risk, and therefore the probability of that future being realised. Expected value reasoning then implies that x-risk reduction has vast expected value, across a wide range of moral and empirical assumptions.
∴Strong longtermism is a robust conclusion that does not depend on extreme moral or empirical assumptions; it follows from the defensible combination of: future welfare matters equally; the future may be enormous; we can influence it; therefore the expected value of long-run actions swamps short-run ones.
Argument II — Thorstad’s three corrections reduce the magnitude of the advantage but do not defeat the astronomical value thesis
1.Thorstad identifies three mistakes: cumulative vs. period risk, background risk, population dynamics. These are real corrections that reduce leading estimates of the expected value of x-risk reduction.
2.But even with these corrections applied, the expected value of a marginal reduction in existential risk — if it can be achieved — remains vastly larger than the expected value of near-term welfare interventions, because the potential future is so enormous.
3.The corrections reduce the magnitude of the advantage; they do not eliminate it. Thorstad himself does not claim they show x-risk reduction is dominated by other interventions; he claims they show it is less dominant than leading estimates suggest. This concedes the point.
∴Thorstad’s three corrections are relevant but do not defeat the astronomical value thesis; they reduce the magnitude of the dominance claim while leaving the qualitative conclusion — that x-risk reduction has vastly greater expected value than near-term interventions — intact.
Argument III — The normative premise that future people matter equally is not challenged by the opposition; their critiques are mathematical, not moral
1.The deepest longtermist claim is that future people’s welfare matters morally just as much as present people’s. MacAskill: distance in time is no more morally relevant than distance in space.
2.The opposition has not challenged this normative premise. Dieks, Eckhardt, Sober, and Thorstad all attack the technical reasoning — the DA, the probability calculations, the expected value estimates. None of them argues that future people simply do not matter.
3.But if future people matter equally, and if the potential future is large, then even corrected versions of the mathematics support longtermism’s dominance claim. The normative foundation is secure even if the mathematical estimates are imprecise.
∴The normative foundation of longtermism — that future people matter equally — is not challenged by the opposition; their technical critiques leave this foundation intact; and this foundation, combined with realistic estimates of future potential, supports the astronomical value thesis even under Thorstad’s corrections.
Argument IV — X-risk reduction is a tractable longtermist target: its long-run effects are more robustly positive than for other interventions
1.Tarsney’s epistemic challenge: if long-run effects are unpredictable, longtermism’s dominance claim may collapse because expected values become uniform.
2.Response: x-risk reduction is a particularly tractable longtermist target. The causal pathway from “prevent extinction” to “long-run human flourishing” is clearer and more robust than for most other interventions.
3.Extinction is an absorbing state: there is no recovery from it. Preventing it preserves all possibilities; not preventing it forecloses them. This makes x-risk reduction’s long-run effects more predictably positive than interventions whose long-run effects are more complex and reversible.
∴X-risk reduction is epistemically tractable as a longtermist target: its long-run effects are more robustly positive than for most interventions because extinction is an absorbing state; the epistemic challenge to longtermism does not apply with equal force to x-risk reduction.
