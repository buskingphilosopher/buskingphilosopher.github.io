Motion before the House
"This House Would Lower the Voting Age to Six"
Proposition Speaking for the motion
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I want to begin with a thought experiment. Imagine you are designing a democracy from scratch. You have a population. You must decide who gets a vote. You reach, almost instinctively, for the concept of competence. Voters, you think, should be capable of understanding what they are voting for. They should be rational. They should be informed.

And then I want to ask you: by that standard, why do we currently include so many people who demonstrably fail it — and exclude an entire class of people on the basis of an assumption we have never seriously tested?

The voting age of eighteen is not the product of careful democratic theory. It is an accident of history, a relic of the age of conscription, carried forward by inertia and defended by condescension. It is time to think again.

On the child as citizen

The argument from citizenship and stakeChildren are not pre-citizens. They are citizens. They live in the societies whose governments make decisions that affect them. They breathe the air whose quality is determined by environmental policy. They attend the schools whose funding is determined by education policy. They will inherit the debt, the climate, and the institutional arrangements that today's voters are now choosing. They have, in the most fundamental sense, a stake.

And yet they are excluded from the mechanism by which those decisions are made. We call this democracy. It is, for children, nothing of the kind. It is a form of benevolent — or not so benevolent — guardianship, in which adults make decisions on behalf of a population that had no say in selecting them.

Why six?

The argument for six as thresholdSix is roughly the age at which children enter formal education — the moment the state begins to make substantial, consequential, formalised decisions about their lives. It seems reasonable that when the state begins to treat you as a subject of its educational apparatus, you should have some voice in who runs it. More importantly, six is the age at which most children have developed a perspective. A six-year-old has preferences, values, attachments, fears. She knows what she cares about. She may not articulate a fully coherent political philosophy — but neither, if we are being honest, can most adult voters.

On competence and its double standard

The argument from the competence double standardWe do not apply a competence test to adults. We do not disqualify voters who cannot name members of the cabinet, who have never read a party manifesto, who choose candidates on the basis of name recognition alone. The adult franchise is universal precisely because we believe competence is not the right test for enfranchisement — there is something demeaning and dangerous about having the state decide who is smart enough to participate in its own governance. The moment you accept that principle for adults, you have conceded that competence is not the relevant criterion. And if it is not relevant for adults, it cannot coherently be decisive for children.

On the tyranny of the present

The argument from intergenerational equityDemocracies have a structural bias toward the present. They aggregate the preferences of living voters and translate them into policy. Climate change is the paradigm case. The people making decisions about carbon emissions today are disproportionately old. The people who will live with the consequences are disproportionately young — or not yet alive. Enfranchising children would introduce into the electorate a cohort whose time horizons are necessarily longer — who will be alive in 2070 and 2080 when today's climate decisions come to fruition — and whose instincts are therefore more likely to favour policies whose costs are borne now and whose benefits arrive later.

On parental influence

The argument from parental influenceAdults also vote as their social environment tells them. The idea that adult voting is the product of pure rational deliberation, insulated from the influence of family, culture, class, and peer group, is not supported by any serious study of voting behaviour. Even if children do initially vote as their parents direct, this is not obviously bad — it means more votes for views held by someone in the household. And the franchise has never required that voters be free of external influence. It has required that voters have the formal right to choose. Enfranchising children gives them that right.

On democratic health

The argument from civic socialisationVoting is an education in itself. The act of choosing, of considering options, of placing a mark on a ballot, is a socialising experience that attaches people to democratic institutions and gives them a stake in their outcomes. We currently exclude children from this experience for eighteen years, then expect them to participate fully and enthusiastically the moment they turn eighteen. This is not a recipe for healthy civic culture. It is a recipe for the disengagement and alienation from politics we see in abundance among young adults. Enfranchising children from six would begin their civic education earlier and produce adults more invested in the system.

I propose not a revolution but a correction. A correction to a franchise that has, for too long, treated the future as an externality and children as subjects rather than citizens.

Thank you.

Opposition Speaking against the motion
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr President,

My opponent is a genuinely interesting thinker. He is also, on this question, genuinely wrong. And I want to be precise about where and why.

The case for enfranchising six-year-olds is not a monstrous proposition. It is a serious one that deserves serious refutation — which means engaging with the actual arguments and following them to the places where they go wrong.

On children as citizens — a distinction that matters

The argument from citizenship and stakeWe are told that children are citizens with a stake. All of this is true. But having a stake in an outcome is not, by itself, sufficient grounds for participating in the mechanism that produces it. Toddlers have a stake in road safety legislation. Dogs have a stake in animal welfare law. Future generations have a stake in climate policy. We do not, on the basis of stake alone, conclude that all these parties should vote. The relevant question is not whether you have interests affected by political decisions. It is whether you have the capacity to form, revise, and express political judgements in a way the democratic process can meaningfully incorporate. Stake is necessary but not sufficient.

On six as a threshold — an arbitrary number

The argument for six as thresholdThe proposition correctly notes that eighteen is arbitrary. True. But the response to an arbitrary line is not to draw another arbitrary line at six and declare it principled. Why six? Because children start school? But the state makes decisions about children before they are born — through maternity policy, tax credits, healthcare. Should we enfranchise foetuses? The proposition's logic, pushed even slightly, becomes absurd. Six is not a principled threshold. It is a provocative number chosen to make the argument feel radical enough to be interesting. That is fine as intellectual strategy. It is not democratic theory.

On competence — a deliberately misconstrued objection

The argument from the competence double standardThe proposition dismisses the competence objection by pointing out that we do not test adult competence. Clever — but it misreads what the objection actually is. The objection to child suffrage is not that children fail a test adults pass. It is that children are in a process of cognitive and moral development that makes political judgement not yet fully available to them. A six-year-old does not yet have a stable sense of personal identity — a settled understanding of who she is, what she values, how those values relate to the social world. Political judgement requires not just preferences but a self stable enough to form preferences that are genuinely one's own. Moreover: if the competence double standard argument proves anything, it proves too much. We should also enfranchise three-year-olds, two-year-olds, and infants. The proposition does not want that conclusion — but its argument gives it no principled place to stop.

On short-termism — a real problem, a wrong solution

The argument from intergenerational equityThe short-termism argument is the strongest in the proposition's arsenal. Democracies are structurally biased toward the present. This is a real democratic pathology. But enfranchising six-year-olds is not the remedy. First, it is not clear that children think longer-term than adults in politically relevant ways. A six-year-old is oriented to the immediate — to the concerns of her household, classroom, street. The capacity for extended temporal reasoning is itself a developmental achievement that arrives in adolescence. Second, the problem of intergenerational equity has well-developed institutional solutions that do not require abandoning developmental prerequisites of the franchise — parliamentary commissioners for future generations, constitutional long-term impact assessments, lowering the voting age to sixteen. The proposition has identified a real problem and reached for a solution that does not obviously address it.

On parental influence — a problem gravely understated

The argument from parental influenceThe proposition concedes that children may vote as their parents direct, then dismisses this as no more troubling than social influences on adult voters. This is a significant understatement. The influence of a peer group or media on an adult is categorically different from the influence of a parent on a six-year-old. The adult has, at least in principle, the psychological and economic independence to form political judgements that diverge from her environment. The six-year-old does not. She is dependent on her parents for everything — shelter, food, emotional security, interpretation of the world. What child enfranchisement would produce in practice is the multiplication of parental votes. Parents with three children would have five votes; parents with five children, seven. This is not extending democracy to children. It is a gerrymander disguised as progressivism.

On democratic health — an argument that cuts both ways

The argument from civic socialisationThe proposition argues that early enfranchisement would attach children to democratic institutions and produce more engaged adults. But the argument cuts both ways. If children vote as their parents direct — as the proposition concedes is likely — then their early civic experience is not one of independent democratic participation. It is one of performing their parents' political identity. The civic education argument assumes the vote is experienced as genuinely one's own. Under conditions of near-total parental influence, that assumption does not hold. There are better ways to attach young people to democratic institutions: civic education in schools, youth parliaments, lowering the voting age to sixteen, where evidence from Scotland suggests young voters engage seriously and thoughtfully.

The real question

The question is not whether children deserve political voice — they do. The question is whether the specific mechanism of the individual franchise, cast by a six-year-old in a polling booth, is the right way to give them that voice.

The argument from democratic selfhoodThe franchise is a particular kind of political act — one that requires a particular kind of selfhood, a particular degree of independence, and a particular capacity for political judgement that develops over time and is not yet present at six. Democracy is not simply the aggregation of all preferences. It is the aggregation of preferences by persons capable of the kind of autonomous, revisable, politically aware judgement that democratic theory requires. That capacity arrives. It just has not arrived at six.

Thank you.
