Scanning Shakespeare's Second Sonnet

Published April 26, 2026

I aim to provide a careful metrical analysis of Shakespeare's second sonnet, following the same approach as for the first: examining how meter, sound, and sense are interwoven, and how an understanding of these relationships can deepen our appreciation of the poem. As with Sonnet 1, I will proceed line by line, attending to the metrical figures at work — iambs, trochees, spondees, epitrites, paeons, choriambs — and to the phonetic texture of alliteration, assonance, and consonant clusters. I will also track the broader sonic patterns that accumulate across the whole sonnet.

Sonnet 2 continues the argument of Sonnet 1 — that the beautiful young man must reproduce before age destroys his beauty — but where Sonnet 1 framed that appeal in terms of the world's need, Sonnet 2 makes it devastatingly personal. It confronts the young man with the arithmetic of time: forty winters, a besieged brow, a sunken cheek. And then it offers him an escape — not from time, but from the shame of having wasted his youth.

Scansion

Here is my scansion of the sonnet, with foot divisions:

When for | ty win | ters shall | besiege | thy brow,
And dig | deep tren | ches in | thy beau | ty's field,
Thy youth's proud li | very, so | gazed on | now,
Will be | a tat | ter'd weed, | of small | worth held:
Then be | ing ask'd| where all | thy beau | ty lies,
Where all | the trea | sure of | thy lus | ty days,
To say, | within | thine own | deep sun | ken eyes,
Were an | all-ea | ting shame, | and thrift | less praise.
How much | more praise | deserv'd | thy beau | ty's use,
If thou | couldst an | swer: 'This | fair child | of mine
Shall sum | my count, | and make | my old | excuse,'

This were | to be | new made | when thou | art old,
And see | thy blood | warm when | thou feel'st | it cold.

First Quatrain

Line 1: When forty winters shall besiege thy brow,
When for | ty win | ters shall | be-siege | thy brow,

What a line with which to open. Where Sonnet 1 began with the broad, outward-looking flourish of 'From fairest creatures we desire increase', Sonnet 2 opens with a specific, arithmetically precise threat: forty winters. The number lands with the weight of a verdict. Forty winters — not 'many winters', not 'years to come' — but forty: enough to bury a man's youth entirely.

Metrically, the line is almost entirely regular iambic pentameter, and that regularity is itself expressive. The measured, steady beat — when FOR | ty WIN | ters SHALL | be-SIEGE | thy BROW — enacts the relentless tick of time. There is no escape in metrical variation; the beat just keeps coming, as the winters will.

But note the word 'besiege'. It introduces a military metaphor — winter as an army laying siege — and in doing so it sets up the extended conceit of lines 1–2: the forehead as a battlefield. The 'b' of 'besiege' is the first of a powerful alliterative chain of B's that will recur across the sonnet (brow, beauty's, by, blood, blood). Compare the sequence of B's in Sonnet 1: 'beauty's Rose, bear, But thou, bright eyes, buriest, bud.' Shakespeare reaches for this sound when the stakes are highest.

Note also the assonance of 'forty' and 'brow' — the open 'o' and 'ow' sounds — which lend the line a low, ominous resonance. The short, sharp 'i' of 'winters' and 'besiege' contrast with these rounder vowels, creating a tonal push and pull that mirrors the drama of the image itself.

Line 2: And dig deep trenches in thy beauty's field,
And dig | deep tren | ches in | thy beau | ty's field,

The military metaphor deepens. 'Dig deep trenches' — the wrinkles on the face are now the earthworks of a siege. The line is again metrically regular, but look at how the first three stresses fall: dig, deep, tren— all heavy, single-syllable words, and all with sharp consonant attacks. The line feels like digging. It is a wonderful example of what Alexander Pope called 'an echo to the sense'.

The alliteration of 'dig deep' has a blunt, percussive force — the double 'd' is almost physically effortful, as if the spade is striking hard earth. Then the word 'deep' adds assonance with 'besiege' from the previous line, continuing the chain of long 'ee' sounds that will echo throughout the poem.

'Beauty's field' is a strikingly pastoral image to set against the military vocabulary of 'besiege' and 'trenches'. The contrast is deliberate and poignant: what was once a flowering meadow of youth is now a battlefield. Note that 'beauty's' here echoes 'beauty's Rose' in Sonnet 1 — the same word, but where there it bloomed, here it is under siege. The possessive form ('beauty's field') emphasises ownership: this is the young man's own territory, and it is being invaded by time.

Line 3: Thy youth's proud livery, so gazed on now,
Thy youth's proud li | very, so | gazed on | now,

Now the rhythm shifts. The opening of the line — Thy youth's proud li— forms a 2nd epitrite (di–dum–dum–dum), a figure that opens with a single light syllable before three consecutive heavy stresses. The triple weight of 'youth's proud li–' (with the stress of 'livery' falling on its first syllable) gives the phrase a proud, swaggering quality entirely appropriate to its meaning: youth showing off its finest clothes.

'Livery' was the uniform or costume denoting a servant's allegiance to a great household — so 'youth's proud livery' is at once the costume of youth, and a reminder that youth itself is in service to time, a master who will eventually demand it back. Shakespeare's puns and double meanings are always compact and exact.

The phrase 'so gazed on now' is crucial. The word 'now' is placed at the end of the line — at the point of maximum rhetorical emphasis — and it is the hinge on which the whole first quatrain turns. 'Now' is the only thing standing between the beauty described and the ruin that is coming. The light syllables 'so gazed on' lead into 'now' almost as an afterthought, a parenthetical — and yet 'now' carries all the weight.

Line 4: Will be a tatter'd weed, of small worth held:
Will be | a tat | ter'd weed, | of small | worth held:

And here is the pay-off: the livery of youth will become a tattered weed. The word 'weed' in Shakespeare's time meant a garment (as well as the plant), making the contrast with 'livery' exact: the proud uniform decays into rags. The image is devastating in its simplicity.

The line returns to regular iambic pentameter — metrically unadorned, blunt, inevitable. The caesura after 'weed' (a strong pause created by the comma) gives us a moment to register the image before the line resumes with the dismissive verdict: 'of small worth held'. Note that this phrase is passive — 'held' by whom? By the world, by posterity. It is not the speaker judging; it is everyone.

The consonant cluster at the heart of the line — 'tatter'd weed' — is clogged and difficult to say smoothly, and this sonic friction seems to enact the shabbiness of the image: the tattering of the cloth. Compare the similarly congested consonant clusters in Sonnet 1's 'Feed'st thy light's flame with self substantial fuel', which served a related purpose of conveying blockage and waste.

Note too the assonance linking this line to its predecessor: 'now' and 'brow' rhyme (the sonnet's first rhyme), and 'held' ends on a short 'e' that will echo in the second quatrain.

Second Quatrain

Line 5: Then being ask'd where all thy beauty lies,
Then be | ing ask'd | where all | thy beau | ty lies,

The second quatrain opens with a new, more intimate scenario: the young man, old now, being questioned by someone — perhaps a child, perhaps the world — about where his beauty went. The line is metrically regular, and that regularity again serves the sense: this is a simple, unanswerable question.

The word 'Then' is the temporal hinge. All of the first quatrain has been setup; now we are projected forward into that future where the siege has succeeded, the field is ruined, the livery tattered. 'Then' ushers in the scene of reckoning.

Note the reappearance of 'beauty' — this is the third time the word has appeared in three different forms ('beauty's field', 'beauty lies'), and each time it is more threatened. The repeated use of 'all' — 'where all thy beauty lies' — heightens the sense of totality: it is not some beauty that will be lost, but all of it.

And 'lies' — a richly ambiguous word. The beauty 'lies' somewhere: it is stored, it rests. But 'lies' also means deceives. The young man's beauty, if not perpetuated through a child, becomes a kind of lie — a promise unfulfilled.

Line 6: Where all the treasure of thy lusty days,
Where all | the trea | sure of | thy lus | ty days,

Line 6 echoes line 5 with a kind of antiphonal repetition: 'Where all thy beauty lies' is answered by 'Where all the treasure of thy lusty days'. The phrase 'where all' begins both lines (an anaphora), and this parallelism enacts the relentlessness of the questioner — asking again, pressing harder.

'Lusty days' is an extraordinary phrase — lusty meaning vigorous and full of life (not merely sexual, though that sense is not entirely absent). It conjures the physical reality of youth at its peak, and in doing so makes the contrast with the aged, ruined figure of the first quatrain all the more acute.

The word 'treasure' picks up an economic metaphor that will recur throughout the sonnet — youth's beauty is a form of capital, and like all capital it can be invested (through procreation) or squandered. This is a favourite Shakespearean theme, and the language of usury, thrift, and waste runs through the Sonnets as a persistent undercurrent.

Line 7: To say, within thine own deep sunken eyes,
To say, | within | thine own | deep sunk | en eyes,

Now the imagined answer comes: this is what the old man would say if he had no child to point to. The phrase 'within thine own deep sunken eyes' is a brilliant, horrible image — the eyes that are sunk deep in their sockets, the body turning back in on itself, all the beauty retreated inward and inaccessible. It is the visual embodiment of the self-absorption attacked throughout Sonnet 1.

Compare line 5 of Sonnet 1: 'But thou, contracted to thine own bright eyes' — where 'bright eyes' was the emblem of narcissistic self-regard. Here, in a future the young man has brought upon himself, those 'bright eyes' have become 'deep sunken eyes'. The transformation is exact and merciless.

Metrically, the spondee 'deep sunk–' is worth noting. Two consecutive stressed syllables slow the line to a halt just at the word 'sunken' — as if the metric pressure of those two stresses physically pushes the eyes down into their sockets. This is a striking example of meter serving meaning at the level of the individual syllable.

And note the long 'ee' sounds: 'deep', 'sunken' — which continue the chain of 'ee' assonance begun with 'besiege' and 'deep trenches' in the first two lines.

Line 8: Were an all-eating shame, and thriftless praise.
Were an | all-eat | ing shame, | and thrift | less praise.

And here is the second quatrain's devastating pay-off. To say that all the beauty is locked inside your own aged eyes — that answer 'were an all-eating shame'. The compound 'all-eating' is Shakespearean coinage at its most compressed: shame that devours everything, the way beauty that is not shared devours itself. It echoes 'all-eating' from the vocabulary of the first quatrain — making a famine where abundance lies — and looks forward to the couplet's 'blood warm'.

'Thriftless praise' completes the thought. Praise of one's own beauty — the kind of self-admiring praise that has no issue, no return — is thriftless: it wastes what it has. The economic metaphor crystallises: beauty hoarded is beauty spent to no purpose.

The line's meter is largely regular, with the possible light stressing of 'an' and the interesting juncture at the comma between 'shame' and 'thriftless'. That pause lets 'shame' land before the final verdict is delivered. The 'th' sounds of 'thriftless' and the sibilant 'ss' of 'praise' produce a soft but slightly hissing end to the line — almost contemptuous in its quietness.

Third Quatrain

Line 9: How much more praise deserv'd thy beauty's use,
How much | more praise | deserv'd | thy beau | ty's use,

The third quatrain offers the escape route — and it opens with a rhetorical question that pivots on the word 'praise'. The second quatrain ended with the shame of 'thriftless praise'; now the speaker offers a vision of praise that would be deserved — praise that has been earned by using beauty well.

The phrase 'beauty's use' is precise and pointed. 'Use' means both usage and usury — putting beauty to work, generating interest on the capital of youth. This is not a cold commercial metaphor; it is a warm one. To use beauty is to share it, to propagate it, to let it live beyond one's own lifespan.

Metrically, the line opens with a spondee: 'How much' — two equally weighted syllables that slow the line slightly, giving 'How much' the force of genuine astonishment. The speaker is not merely asking; he is marvelling at how much better things could be. The spondee's weight throws emphasis forward onto 'more praise', and the whole line builds toward the monosyllable 'use' — short, economical, exact.

Line 10: If thou couldst answer: 'This fair child of mine
If thou | couldst ans | wer: 'This | fair child | of mine

And now, in the most dramatically startling moment of the sonnet, we hear the young man's voice — or rather, we are given the words he could say if only he had a child to point to. The caesura after 'answer:' followed by the direct speech marker creates a sudden, theatrical intimacy. It is as if the speaker steps aside and puts words in the young man's mouth.

'This fair child of mine' — the word 'fair' is the third recurrence of beauty-language in the sonnet (after 'beauty' and the implied beauty of 'proud livery'), and it is crucially different: the beauty is no longer the young man's own, no longer locked in those deep sunken eyes, but outward, visible, shared — in a child. 'Fair' also picks up the opening word of Sonnet 1: 'From fairest creatures we desire increase'. The circle closes.

The meter here is again regular, but note the way 'fair child' forms a spondee — two stresses together — so that those two words sit side by side with equal weight, as if to say: these two things, together, are the answer to everything.

Line 11: Shall sum my count, and make my old excuse,'
Shall sum | my count, | and make | my old | excuse,'

The child 'shall sum my count': a brilliant accounting metaphor. Life's ledger, its count of years and beauty, will be tallied up — and the child is the final sum, the proof that the account has been managed wisely. 'Make my old excuse' is equally precise: the child is the old man's excuse, his justification, his defence against the charge of having wasted his beauty.

The meter is regular and the syntax clean, which is itself meaningful: the answer is simple, the arithmetic is clear. Contrast this with the tangled syntax of the second quatrain, where the shame was couched in subordinate clauses and delayed predications ('To say, within thine own deep sunken eyes, / Were an all-eating shame'). The good path is clear; the shameful one is confused.

Note the double 'm' of 'sum my' — a small assonance that ties the words together as a unit, as if the summing and the possessing were inseparable acts.

Line 12: Proving his beauty by succession thine.
Prov-ing | his beau | ty by | succes | sion thine.

The final line of the third quatrain is metrically the most interesting in the poem. It opens with a trochee — 'Proving' — a displaced beat that gives the word a forward, demonstrative energy, as if thrusting the proof before us. The trochee after a spondee or following a clause-ending often has this clarifying, demonstrative effect in Shakespeare.

'Proving his beauty by succession thine' — the child proves his beauty is the father's by the fact of hereditary succession. The legal language of 'succession' sits comfortably alongside the earlier accounting language of 'sum my count' and 'excuse': this is a world where beauty has legal and financial standing, where it can be inherited and proven in court.

And 'thine' — the final word — is metrically stressed and end-stopped, placed with great care. The beauty is the young man's own: 'thine'. The very possessive that was a mark of narcissism throughout Sonnet 1 ('thine own bright eyes', 'Within thine own bud') is here redeemed. 'Thine' beauty, projected outward into a child, is no longer selfish; it is generous, and it endures.

The Couplet

Line 13: This were to be new made when thou art old,
This were | to be | new made | when thou | art old,

The couplet arrives with a simplicity that is almost breathtaking. 'This were to be new made when thou art old' — to have a child is to be born again. The phrase 'new made' carries the force of renewal, of creation; it echoes the religious language of rebirth without quite becoming it. 'New made' is also the language of the craftsman — as if the young man, in fathering a child, becomes both the raw material and the finished article.

Metrically, the line is regular — iambic pentameter with no major variation — and this regularity feels like resolution. The turbulence of the first two quatrains, and the demonstrative energy of the third, have given way to a calm, steady statement of truth.

The word 'old' at the line's end is crucial. It is the first time the word has appeared explicitly (though it has been implicit throughout), and by placing it here — in the couplet's opening line, at the rhyme position — Shakespeare makes it simultaneously a threat and a promise. Old age is coming; but this is how you face it.

Line 14: And see thy blood warm when thou feel'st it cold.
And see | thy blood | warm when | thou feel'st | it cold.

And here is the closing line, and it is magnificent. The final couplet has set up 'old / cold' as its rhyme — two of the plainest, bleakest monosyllables in the language — and the second line deploys them with stunning economy. 'And see thy blood warm when thou feel'st it cold.'

'Thy blood' — the blood of the young man — will run warm in the veins of a child even as the young man himself feels it growing cold with age. The image is at once physiological and emotional, scientific and tender. It is the most concrete image in the sonnet, and the most consoling.

Metrically, look at the central spondee: 'blood warm'. Two equally stressed syllables, sitting side by side — the warm-blooded vitality of the child suspended in the middle of the line between the father's 'see' and the father's 'cold'. It is a metrical enactment of the sonnet's central idea: the child's warmth held within the frame of the father's life.

The assonance of 'warm' and 'cold' — short 'o' sounds in each — gives the line a phonetic balance that mirrors its thematic balance. And the line ends on 'cold' — the poem's last word, the poem's last sound, and yet not a despairing one. Because the whole last line has been about warmth inside coldness: the blood warm, the child alive. The ending is 'cold', but the feeling the line leaves us with is warmth.

Compare the final line of Sonnet 1: 'To eat the world's due, by the grave and thee.' Both closing lines end at the extreme of time — the grave, the cold — but both lines find something in that extreme. In Sonnet 1 it was the grim arithmetic of waste; here it is the consolation of succession. The second sonnet is, for all its confrontational arithmetic about forty winters and tattered weeds, ultimately the more hopeful of the two.

Concluding Remarks: The Sonnet as a Whole

Sonnet 2 is shaped by three interlocking movements: the first quatrain's military assault on the young man's future; the second quatrain's vision of shameful inadequacy; and the third quatrain's offer of escape, confirmed by the couplet's consoling image of warm blood in cold age.

Phonetically, the sonnet is unified by a network of long 'ee' sounds — 'besiege', 'deep', 'beauty', 'sunken eyes', 'deserved', 'succession' — that give the poem a sustained, slightly mournful tone, as of something both precious and elusive. Against this, the short, hard vowels of 'dig', 'weed', 'small', 'held', and 'sum' provide blunt, economic counterweights.

The B-alliteration — 'brow, beauty's, by, blood' — runs through the poem like a thread, connecting the besieged brow of the first line to the warm blood of the last. It is as if the poem moves from 'brow' (the outward face of beauty, vulnerable to time) to 'blood' (the inner vitality of beauty, capable of renewal). That journey from surface to substance is the sonnet's argument in miniature.

And the economic metaphor — beauty as treasure, as capital to be used wisely or squandered thriftlessly — gives the poem a precision and a practicality that makes its emotional appeal all the more powerful. This is not merely a plea to fall in love or to enjoy youth; it is an argument, laid out with the rigour of an accountant and the warmth of a friend, for why the young man's beauty must not be allowed to die with him.

What a sonnet, and what a progression from the first. Where Sonnet 1 ended with the grave as a devourer — 'To eat the world's due, by the grave and thee' — Sonnet 2 ends with life persisting within death's cold: 'And see thy blood warm when thou feel'st it cold.' The argument is advancing, and so is the hope.
