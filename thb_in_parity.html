Parity — The Fourth Value RelationRuth Chang — Oxford / Rutgers • Ethics, 2002
The three classical value relations — better than, worse than, equally good — do not exhaust the logical space of positive evaluative comparability. There is a fourth relation, parity, holding between items that are comparable but where none of the three trichotomous relations applies; it is established by the Small Improvement Argument and the Chaining Argument, and it is irreducible to vagueness or indeterminacy because it survives any permissible precisification of the relevant evaluative predicates.

Parity can be given a rigorous analysis via fitting attitudes: two items are on a par if and only if it is both rationally permissible to prefer x to y and rationally permissible to prefer y to x — with neither preference being rationally required or prohibited. Rough Equality & Imprecise ComparabilityDerek Parfit — Oxford • Reasons and Persons, 1984
In Reasons and Persons I introduced the notion of rough equality and imprecise comparability to handle cases where the standard trichotomy breaks down — cases that Chang now calls parity. I believe she is right that vagueness alone cannot account for these cases; rough equality is a genuine evaluative phenomenon, and the practical and population-ethical implications of recognising it are profound, connecting to the very problems in axiology that have occupied us for four decades.

Logical Arguments

I want to begin by being precise about the thesis I am defending, because it has often been misunderstood. I am not claiming that incomparability never occurs. I am not claiming that all evaluative comparisons can be resolved. I am claiming something more specific: that there is a fourth basic positive value relation — being on a par — that holds between certain items, and that this relation is distinct from better than, worse than, equally good, and also from incomparability. The claim is that some items are comparable but in a way that resists trichotomous classification.

Let me begin with a canonical example. Mozart was a great musician and composer. Michelangelo was a great visual artist. How do they compare in terms of creative achievement? Most people have the clear intuition that neither is better than the other overall. But they also have the clear intuition that they are not equally good — they are so different in kind that precise equality seems wrong. And they are not incomparable — we can rank them against obviously lesser artists in ways that are determinate and sensitive to their respective excellences. They are, I say, on a par.

The logical space of value relations — trichotomy vs. tetrachotomy
Trichotomy thesis (T): For any comparable x, y, either x > y, y > x, or x = y.
Standard view: incomparability = failure of all three. Comparable = one of the three holds.
Tetrachotomy thesis (Chang): For any comparable x, y, either x > y, y > x, x = y, or x ≈ y (x is on a par with y).
Parity: a fourth positive value relation; x and y are comparable but none of the three trichotomous relations holds. x ≈ y is a genuine relation, not a placeholder for ignorance or vagueness.
The crucial structural feature of parity: if x ≈ y, then (x + ε) ≈ y for small improvements ε. Parity is not a threshold phenomenon; it is stable under small perturbations. This is what distinguishes it from equality (which tips over immediately to > under small improvement) and incomparability (which may give way to comparability).
i. the small improvement argument

Small Improvement Argument — if x were slightly better, x would be better than y, but x is not better than y; so x is not worse, not equal, but comparable; parityThe Small Improvement Argument (SIA) is the central argument for parity. It proceeds as follows. Consider a case where a career as a lawyer and a career as a clarinetist seem evaluatively tied — not precisely equal, but neither clearly better. Now make a small improvement to one of them: give the lawyer career a marginally better salary, or give the clarinetist career a marginally better concert hall. The intuition is: after this small improvement, the improved option is better than the other. But here is the key: if before the improvement the options were equally good, making one marginally better should tip it to being better than the other — and it does. But if before the improvement one option was worse than the other, a small improvement to the worse option could not make it better than the other. So the improvement tells us something about the original relation. The SIA establishes that the original options were not worse than, not better than, and not equal to each other — yet comparable. This is parity.

The Small Improvement Argument formally stated.

Let L = lawyer career, C = clarinetist career.
Let C′ = C with a small improvement (marginally better concert hall).

Premise 1: C′ is better than L. (Small improvement tips the balance.)
Premise 2: C is not better than L. (The original case is one of apparent parity.)
Premise 3: The improvement is genuinely small — it cannot turn a worse option into a better one, only a tied option into a better one.

From P1 and P2: C and L are not related by “C better than L.”
From P3: C is not worse than L. (If C were worse, a small improvement could not make C′ better than L.)
From P1 and P3: C and L are not equally good. (If they were equal, making C slightly better would produce C′ > L, which is consistent — but then any small improvement to L should also tip it. Symmetric small improvements to each do not restore the original balance if they were equal.)

Conclusion: None of “better than,” “worse than,” or “equally good” applies. Since L and C are comparable (they are not incomparable), a fourth relation — parity — must hold between them.
ii. the chaining argument for comparability

Chaining argument — Mozart and a bad sculptor are comparable; chain of small differences cannot trigger incomparability; so Mozart and Michelangelo are comparableThe SIA establishes parity in paradigm cases. The Chaining Argument establishes that the cases are ones of genuine comparability rather than incomparability. Imagine a sequence of sculptors: starting from a poor sculptor who is clearly worse than Mozart in terms of creative achievement, and ascending step by step to Michelangelo. At each step, the sculptor is slightly better than the previous. Now: Mozart is clearly comparable with (and better than) the poor sculptor at the bottom of the chain. And a small unidimensional difference — a single step up the chain — cannot trigger incomparability where before there was comparability. If Mozart is comparable with sculptor n, and sculptor n+1 is only marginally better than sculptor n in a single evaluative dimension, Mozart is comparable with sculptor n+1. By induction up the chain, Mozart is comparable with Michelangelo. The chaining argument shows that the Mozart-Michelangelo comparison is one of genuine comparability, and since none of the trichotomous relations applies, it must be parity.

iii. why parity is not vagueness

Parity is not vagueness — vagueness resolved by precisification; parity survives all precisifications; phenomenology of hard choices is distinctiveThe most seductive rival to parity is Broome’s vagueness view: what appears to be parity is actually indeterminacy in the “better than” relation, caused by vagueness in our evaluative predicates. I have two arguments against this identification. First: the argument from phenomenology. Genuine evaluative uncertainty — cases where we do not know which option is better — has a distinctive phenomenological character. We feel uncertainty, a sense that we might be wrong, a pull toward gathering more information. Hard cases of the parity kind feel different: we do not feel uncertain about which is better; we feel that the question of which is better is the wrong question. This is the phenomenology not of ignorance but of a genuine fourth relation. Second: the argument from precisification. If apparent parity were vagueness, it should be resolvable by stipulating a precise meaning for the vague predicate. But no stipulation resolves the Mozart-Michelangelo case. There is no precisification of “better than in creative achievement” on which either composer clearly wins. The apparent parity survives all precisifications, which shows it is not a function of vagueness in the predicate.

iv. parity and practical reason

Parity and practical reason — parity cases are hard choices; Chang’s voluntarist response; will-based reasons; parity reveals structure of rational agencyThe philosophical payoff of recognising parity is profound. When two options are on a par, neither instrumental nor evidential reasoning can identify which to choose — because the options are genuinely tied in the relevant sense. Does this mean choice is irrational? No. I argue in subsequent work that parity cases reveal the structure of rational agency: when values are on a par, you can create a reason for choosing one option over the other through an act of will — a commitment, a decision about who to be. The hard choices that parity cases constitute are not failures of reason but exercises of a distinctive kind of rational agency in which the will constitutes reasons rather than following them. This is not arbitrary choice but the deepest form of rational self-determination. Recognising parity is thus not just a contribution to value theory; it is a reconception of what rational agency requires and permits.

The fourth relation exists. The arguments establish it. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Small Improvement Argument: parity is established by the asymmetry between small improvements and equal options
1.Premise 1 (SIA): L = lawyer career, C = clarinetist career. C′ = C with a small improvement. C′ is better than L. (The improvement tips the balance.)
2.Premise 2 (SIA): C is not better than L. (The original is a case of apparent evaluative tie — not one of clear superiority.)
3.Premise 3 (SIA): The improvement is genuinely small. A small improvement cannot turn a worse option into a better one; it can only tip a tied option to better. If C were worse than L, C′ could not be better than L after only a small improvement.
∴From P2 and P3: C is not worse than L. From P1 and symmetry: C is not equal to L. From P2: C is not better than L. Since L and C are comparable (shown by the Chaining Argument), a fourth relation — parity — must hold.
Argument II — The Chaining Argument: comparability is established for all pairs in the chain, so Mozart and Michelangelo are comparable
1.Mozart is comparable with (and better than) a clearly poor sculptor s₀. Comparability: {Mozart, s₀}. ✓
2.Small Unidimensional Difference Principle: between two comparable items, a small improvement in a single evaluative dimension to one of them cannot trigger incomparability. If {Mozart, sₙ} are comparable, and sₙ₊₁ is only marginally better than sₙ in one dimension, {Mozart, sₙ₊₁} are also comparable.
3.By induction up the chain from s₀ to Michelangelo (= sₙ for some large n), Mozart and Michelangelo are comparable.
∴Mozart and Michelangelo are comparable; since none of the trichotomous relations holds between them, they must stand in the fourth relation — parity.
Argument III — Parity is not vagueness: it survives all precisifications and has a distinctive phenomenology
1.Argument from precisification: if apparent parity were vagueness in "better than," it should be resolvable by specifying a precise meaning for the predicate. But no precisification of "better than in creative achievement" makes Mozart clearly better than Michelangelo or vice versa. Apparent parity survives all precisifications.
2.Argument from phenomenology: genuine evaluative uncertainty feels like ignorance — we feel we might be wrong, we seek more information. Parity cases feel different — we feel that "which is better?" is the wrong question, not that we lack the answer to the right question.
3.Vagueness resolves under precisification; parity does not. The phenomenological difference is real and tracks the theoretical distinction.
∴Parity is not a species of vagueness; it survives all precisifications of the evaluative predicates and has a phenomenological character distinct from genuine evaluative uncertainty — both arguments establish that parity is a genuine fourth relation, not an artefact of semantic indeterminacy.
Argument IV — Parity grounds a reconception of rational agency: will-based reasons in hard choices
1.When two options are on a par, neither instrumental nor evidential reasoning determines which to choose: no reason-tracking process identifies a winner, because neither option is better.
2.But choice in parity cases can be rational: through a voluntary commitment — a decision about who to be — the agent creates a reason for choosing one option over the other. The will constitutes reasons rather than following them.
3.This is the deepest form of rational self-determination: not arbitrary choice but the exercise of a rational agency that creates rather than tracks value. Hard choices, properly understood, reveal the will as a source of reasons, not merely a responder to them.
∴Recognising parity transforms our conception of rational agency: hard choices are not failures of reason but exercises of a distinctive will-based rationality; parity thus has profound implications not just for value theory but for the philosophy of action.

Chang has given us the intuitive case for parity. I want to provide the formal underpinning. My 2008 paper “Value Relations” constructs a general account of value relations via fitting attitudes, and shows that parity emerges naturally from this framework — not as a theoretical add-on but as one of a family of value relations that the fitting-attitude analysis automatically generates.

The basic idea of the fitting-attitude analysis is this: value relations between items are constituted by patterns of rationally permissible and rationally required attitudes — primarily preferences — that a rational agent can have toward those items. X is better than Y if and only if preferring X to Y is the only rationally permissible attitude. X and Y are equally good if and only if both preferring X to Y and preferring Y to X are rationally impermissible, but indifference is required. And crucially: X and Y are on a par if and only if both preferring X to Y and preferring Y to X are rationally permissible — neither preference is required, neither is prohibited.

A preference ordering can rank x above y (x ≻ y), y above x (y ≻ x), or express indifference (x ~ y).

Parity is the unique relation where all three attitudes (x ≻ y, y ≻ x, x ~ y) are permissible but none is required. This is the formal expression of being "in the same league" while differing in kind.
i. why the fitting-attitude analysis vindicates parity

FA analysis vindicates parity — parity falls out naturally from the formal model; not ad hoc; genuinely distinct from incomparabilityThe crucial feature of the fitting-attitude analysis is that parity emerges from it automatically. Once we have the machinery of permissible preference orderings, we can ask: which patterns of permissibility correspond to which value relations? The pattern for parity — both strict preferences permissible, none required — is a genuine cell in the logical space of patterns. It is not introduced to rescue Chang’s intuitions; it falls out of the formal model as one of the possibilities the model recognises. Moreover, parity is logically distinct from incomparability on this analysis: incomparability is the relation where no preference of any kind is permissible (neither strict preference nor indifference), while in parity all three preference attitudes are permissible. These are structurally different patterns, and the difference matters for rational choice.

ii. parity and the structure of rational choice

Parity in rational choice — parity permits either strict preference; rational agent with par items may permissibly prefer either; this is different from incomparability’s requirement of indifferenceWhat does the fitting-attitude analysis imply for rational choice between par items? If x and y are on a par, then preferring x to y is rationally permissible, and preferring y to x is also rationally permissible. This means a rational agent who prefers x to y in a parity case is not making a rational error: she is exercising one of the permissible attitudes. But nor is she rationally required to prefer x; she could equally rationally prefer y. This is not the same as indifference: indifference between x and y is also permissible when they are on a par, but preferring either one is equally permissible. The structure is one of rational latitude: you can go either way, and going either way is defensible. This is importantly different from incomparability, where the permissible attitude is indifference only, and strictly preferring either option would be irrational.

iii. the taxonomy of value relations: parity is not alone

Rich taxonomy — fitting-attitude analysis generates 14 logically possible value relations; parity is one of the well-motivated ones; others include superiority, inferior preference-permissibility patternsThe fitting-attitude analysis, taken to its full generality, generates a rich taxonomy of value relations. There are fourteen logically possible patterns of permissibility over the three preference attitudes, and each corresponds to a distinct candidate value relation. Of these, five are the most natural and theoretically well-motivated: better than, worse than, equally good, parity, and incomparability. The existence of the other nine possibilities shows that value theory has far more structure than the standard trichotomy acknowledges, and that parity is just one of several potential additions to our evaluative vocabulary. This does not mean all fourteen relations actually obtain in our value practices; but it shows that the logical space is much richer than the trichotomy thesis assumes, and that there is no a priori reason to think the trichotomy exhausts it.

iv. objections to the fitting-attitude analysis of parity

Objections handled — preferences vs value judgements objection; relativisation response; monadic favouring solves the preference ordering problemThe most serious objection to the fitting-attitude analysis is the preferences-versus-value-judgements objection: if strict preferences involve betterness judgements — if to prefer x to y is to judge that x is better than y — then saying both preferences are permissible in parity cases implies that it is permissible to judge both that x is better than y and that y is better than x. But these cannot both be true if “better than” is a standard asymmetric relation. My response in my revised 2012 paper is to reinterpret the underlying notion of preference: rather than treating preference as a dyadic attitude directed toward a pair, we treat it as a difference of degree between monadic attitudes of favouring — each directed toward a single item. The permissibility then concerns the degree of favouring, not a judgement about betterness. This reinterpretation dissolves the objection while preserving the formal structure of the analysis.

The formal model vindicates parity. It is a coherent and well-defined value relation. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The fitting-attitude analysis generates parity automatically; it is not introduced ad hoc
1.The FA analysis: value relations are constituted by patterns of rationally permissible attitudes. Better than: strict preference required. Equal: indifference required. Parity: all three attitudes (x≻y, y≻x, x~y) permissible, none required.
2.Parity falls out of the formal model as one of the genuinely possible patterns in the logical space of permissibility. It is not introduced to rescue pre-theoretic intuitions but emerges from the analysis as a legitimate cell in the taxonomy.
3.Parity is logically distinct from incomparability (where no attitude is permissible, and indifference is required by default) — the formal difference is structural and not merely a matter of labelling.
∴The fitting-attitude analysis vindicates parity as a coherent, logically well-defined value relation that emerges automatically from the formal model rather than being introduced ad hoc to accommodate pre-theoretic intuitions.
Argument II — Parity implies rational latitude in choice: both strict preferences are permissible, neither required
1.When x and y are on a par, preferring x to y is rationally permissible and preferring y to x is rationally permissible. Neither preference is rationally required or prohibited.
2.This means a rational agent who chooses x over y in a parity case is not making a rational error: she exercises a permissible preference. But she could equally permissibly have chosen y.
3.This structure — rational latitude rather than rational indifference — is different from both equality (indifference required) and incomparability (indifference required by default, strict preference impermissible).
∴Parity implies a distinctive rational latitude: both strict preferences are permissible, neither required; this makes parity structurally different from equality and incomparability in ways that matter for the theory of rational choice.
Argument III — The FA analysis reveals a rich taxonomy of value relations; parity is one of the most theoretically motivated
1.There are 14 logically possible patterns of permissibility over the three preference attitudes. Each is a candidate value relation. Five of these — better, worse, equal, parity, incomparable — are the most theoretically well-motivated.
2.This rich taxonomy shows the trichotomy thesis is not a priori necessary: the logical space of value relations is far richer than better/worse/equal, and parity is a natural and well-motivated extension.
3.The existence of multiple possible value relations does not mean all obtain; but it defeats the trichotomy thesis as a logical necessity and makes room for parity as a real and important relation.
∴The FA taxonomy reveals a logically rich evaluative space; parity is one of the most theoretically well-motivated of the non-standard relations, and its logical coherence is demonstrated by its place in the formal model.
Argument IV — The preference-vs-value-judgements objection is dissolved by reinterpreting preference as monadic favouring
1.Objection: if preferences involve betterness judgements, then permissible preference for both x over y and y over x implies it is permissible to judge both that x is better and that y is better — contradiction.
2.Response: reinterpret preference as a difference of degree between monadic attitudes of favouring — each directed at a single item, not at the pair. Permissibility concerns the degree of favouring, not a betterness judgement.
3.Under this reinterpretation, it can be permissible to favour x more than y and to favour y more than x — on different occasions or in different frames — without contradictory betterness judgements arising.
∴The preference-vs-value-judgements objection is dissolved by the monadic favouring reinterpretation; the FA analysis of parity is vindicated without the contradiction, and parity remains a coherent value relation.

Let me speak as someone who identified the phenomenon that Chang now calls parity — without yet having the right name for it. In Reasons and Persons I introduced the notion of rough equality and imprecise comparability to handle cases like the one at the heart of this debate. I did not develop these notions sufficiently. Chang has supplied what I lacked: a precise characterisation of the relation and rigorous arguments for its existence. I want to endorse her project and explain why I believe vagueness cannot do the work Broome wants it to do.

In Reasons and Persons I faced a problem in population ethics that required exactly the kind of relation Chang is defending. The Repugnant Conclusion can be avoided — so I thought — if we say that the extremely good lives of world A and the barely-worth-living lives of world Z are not commensurable in a way that permits simple additive comparison. This requires neither that A is better than Z, nor that Z is better than A, nor that they are precisely equally good — it requires a fourth relation. Chang’s parity is, I believe, the right analysis of what I was reaching for.

Rough equality in Reasons and Persons: the phenomenon Parfit identified.

Parfit (1984, pp. 430–432): “When we are choosing between two courses of action, one of which would produce a great benefit but only for some people, while the other would benefit more people but by less, the comparison seems not to be trichotomous. There is no precise point at which one option becomes better or worse than the other, and they may not be equally good. This is what I call rough equality.”

Note: Parfit explicitly distinguished rough equality from ignorance (we are not uncertain about the comparison) and from vagueness (it is not that our predicates are vague but that the items themselves stand in a non-standard evaluative relation). This is exactly the phenomenon Chang formalises as parity.
i. rough equality and the repugnant conclusion

Rough equality and RC — imprecise comparability between A-lives and Z-lives avoids simple additive comparison; this is where NWLT and Chang’s parity connectThe Repugnant Conclusion arises because standard total utilitarianism assumes that all welfare levels are commensurable on a single scale: any amount of welfare in Z can, in principle, add up to more than the total in A. If we say instead that the welfare of A-lives and the welfare of Z-lives stand in a relation of rough equality or parity — comparable but not trichotomously rankable — then the additive mechanism that drives the Repugnant Conclusion breaks down. We cannot simply sum up the Z-welfare and compare it with the A-total, because the comparison between Z-lives and A-lives is itself parity-structured, not quantitative. Chang’s parity, applied to the population-level comparison, is one of the most promising analytical tools for diagnosing why the Repugnant Conclusion seems wrong without requiring a fully lexical structure of the kind Thomas and Carlson defend.

ii. why vagueness is the wrong diagnosis

Vagueness wrong diagnosis — Parfit’s own phenomenology of rough equality was not ignorance; it was a positive recognition of a non-standard relation; Broome misidentifies the sourceBroome wants to say that apparent rough equality — what I called imprecise comparability — is really vagueness in our evaluative predicates. But this misidentifies the source of the phenomenon. When I reflected on the comparison between A-lives and Z-lives, I was not experiencing uncertainty about which was better in the way that I experience uncertainty about a factual matter. I was experiencing something different: a recognition that the question “which is better?” is not well-formed in the way that standard comparative questions are. The comparison does not bottom out in a precise evaluation because the items themselves, not our concepts of them, stand in a non-standard relation. Vagueness is a property of language; parity is a property of value. The phenomenological asymmetry between genuine evaluative uncertainty and the experience of hard cases of comparison strongly supports Chang over Broome.

iii. on the collapsing principle and broome’s argument

Collapsing principle — Broome uses it to derive that vagueness implies indifference; but the principle is dubious; Gustafsson’s counterexample is tellingBroome’s argument that apparent parity is vagueness rests on a key premise he calls the Collapsing Principle: if it is indeterminate whether x is better than y, then x and y are equally good. This principle is meant to show that vagueness about “better than” collapses into equality, not into an intermediate fourth relation. I believe this principle is dubious, and Gustafsson has produced a compelling counterexample to it. The principle says that indeterminacy about “better” collapses to equality; but Gustafsson shows there are cases where it is determinately not the case that x is better than y, determinately not the case that y is better than x, and determinately not the case that x equals y — yet none of this is explained by vagueness in “better than.” The phenomenon resists Broome’s analysis, and the collapsing principle is the defective hinge on which his entire argument turns.

iv. parity and the structure of moral mathematics

Moral mathematics — parity shows our value concepts have more structure than the trichotomy assumes; this matters for population ethics, personal identity, and the aggregation of welfareI want to close by emphasising what is at stake for moral mathematics in the parity debate. If parity is real, then the standard assumption of total commensurability in welfare comparisons is false: not all welfare comparisons resolve into a simple ordering. This has direct implications for the paradoxes of population ethics that have occupied me for forty years. The Repugnant Conclusion, the Mere Addition Paradox, the Non-Identity Problem — all of these involve comparative judgements between populations or lives that the standard trichotomous model handles inadequately. Parity offers a more subtle and more faithful account of the evaluative structure of these comparisons. Chang has done philosophy a great service by naming, characterising, and arguing for the reality of the phenomenon I only gestured at.

The phenomenon is real. Chang has named it correctly. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Rough equality in Reasons and Persons is the phenomenon Chang formalises as parity; the identification is correct
1.Parfit (1984): rough equality is a non-standard evaluative relation holding between items where neither is better than the other, they are not precisely equally good, and the comparison is not one of genuine ignorance or vagueness.
2.Chang's parity satisfies all three conditions: parity holds when neither x is better than y nor y is better than x, when x and y are not equally good, and when the relation is not traceable to vagueness in the evaluative predicates.
3.The identification rough equality = parity is therefore correct; Chang has provided the formal analysis that Parfit's rough equality required and lacked.
∴Parfit's rough equality is the same phenomenon as Chang's parity; the 2002 paper provides the formal analysis that completes what Reasons and Persons identified but could not characterise precisely.
Argument II — Parity, applied to population-level comparisons, helps explain why the Repugnant Conclusion seems wrong
1.The Repugnant Conclusion requires that the welfare of Z-lives and A-lives be commensurable in a way that permits simple additive comparison. If A-lives and Z-lives stand in a parity relation, this commensurability fails.
2.When the comparison between Z-lives and A-lives is parity-structured rather than quantitative, the additive mechanism that drives the RC breaks down: we cannot simply sum Z-welfare and compare it with A-total.
3.Parity at the population level is therefore one of the most promising tools for diagnosing the RC's wrongness without requiring fully lexical structure.
∴Parity, applied to population-level comparisons, provides a principled diagnosis of the RC's apparent wrongness; it explains why additive comparison breaks down without requiring the strong lexical structure of NWLT.
Argument III — Broome's Collapsing Principle is dubious; Gustafsson's counterexample shows it can derive outright contradictions
1.Broome's Collapsing Principle: if it is indeterminate whether x is better than y, then x and y are equally good. This is meant to show vagueness collapses to equality rather than generating a fourth relation.
2.Gustafsson's counterexample: the Collapsing Principle, combined with the claim that there are borderline cases of some predicate, derives an outright contradiction. If the principle is applied to borderline cases, it implies that it is both true and false that x = y in such cases.
3.A principle that generates outright contradictions from innocuous premises is false; the Collapsing Principle should be rejected; Broome's argument from vagueness therefore fails at its key premise.
∴The Collapsing Principle is false or empty; Broome's argument that apparent parity reduces to vagueness therefore fails at its key premise; the phenomenon of apparent parity cannot be explained by vagueness in the betterness predicate.
Argument IV — Parity has profound implications for moral mathematics: it shows welfare comparisons have more structure than the trichotomy assumes
1.If parity is real, total commensurability of welfare on a single scale is false: some welfare comparisons do not resolve into a simple ordering, even in principle.
2.This affects the Repugnant Conclusion, Mere Addition Paradox, Non-Identity Problem, and all paradoxes that depend on the assumption that all welfare values are straightforwardly additive and comparable.
3.Parity offers a more faithful account of the evaluative structure of these comparisons than the trichotomous model; it is an indispensable tool for moral mathematics.
∴Parity is not merely a curiosity in value theory but an indispensable tool for moral mathematics; its recognition transforms how we approach population ethics, personal identity, and welfare aggregation.

Incomparabilism / Rational EligibilityJoseph Raz — Oxford • The Morality of Freedom, 1986
Chang does not need parity to solve the problem she identifies. Genuine incomparability — the determinate failure of all three trichotomous relations — already exists, already accounts for hard cases of comparison, and already has a satisfactory theory of rational choice under it: the non-comparativist view that a choice is rationally justified if it is not worse than any alternative, without any alternative being better. The Small Improvement Argument fails because it is not established that the comparison between the lawyer career and the clarinetist career before the small improvement is determinate. If it is indeterminate whether C is better than L, then it may also be indeterminate whether C′ is better than L — and the argument requires the latter to be determinate. Chang’s arguments against the indeterminacy response — from phenomenology and from perplexity — do not succeed, and the indeterminacy explanation is more parsimonious than parity.

Sorites / Chaining Argument FailsLuke Elson — Reading • Ethics, 2014
Chang’s Chaining Argument is a sorites argument in disguise. The Small Unidimensional Difference Principle — that a small improvement in one dimension cannot trigger incomparability where before there was comparability — is a premise in a sorites series. If it is accepted, it proves too much: it shows that any two items at all are comparable with each other, which is false. The chaining argument is therefore fallacious, and with it falls the strongest argument for the comparability required by parity.

Logical Arguments

I want to advance the view that I first defended in Chang’s 1997 edited volume and have developed in subsequent work: that apparent incomparability — including what Chang calls parity — is best explained as vagueness in our comparative predicates. The evaluative predicate “better than” is vague, in exactly the way that “bald” or “heap” are vague. When it is indeterminate whether x is better than y, this is because “better than” does not determinately apply or determinately fail to apply, not because a new kind of relation holds.

This is a familiar kind of phenomenon in philosophy of language. Vague predicates have borderline cases: cases where the predicate neither determinately applies nor determinately fails to apply. “Bald” has borderline cases where it is neither determinately true that someone is bald nor determinately true that they are not bald. “Better than” has borderline cases where it is neither determinately true that x is better than y nor determinately true that y is better than x nor determinately true that they are equally good. These are the cases that Chang calls parity. But they are more simply and elegantly explained by vagueness.

The vagueness explanation of apparent parity
Supervaluationism about “better than”:
A sentence is true = true on all permissible precisifications of the vague terms.
A sentence is false = false on all permissible precisifications.
A sentence is indeterminate = true on some precisifications, false on others.
For the Mozart-Michelangelo case:
On some precisifications of "better than in creative achievement": Mozart is better.
On other precisifications: Michelangelo is better.
On yet others: they are equally good.
Supervaluationist verdict: It is indeterminate who is better. No false claim is made. No fourth relation is needed. The appearance of parity is explained by the vagueness of the evaluative predicate — not by a sui generis relation holding between the items.
i. the collapsing principle and why vagueness implies equality

Collapsing principle — indeterminacy in betterness relation collapses to equality on supervaluationist account; Chang needs to show this principle failsMy key argument is the Collapsing Principle. Consider a case where it is indeterminate whether x is better than y (and where, by symmetry, it is also indeterminate whether y is better than x). On a supervaluationist account of vagueness, this means: on some precisifications of “better than,” x is better than y; on others, y is better than x; and on yet others, neither is better (they are equal). But “x is equally good as y” is then determinately true on all the precisifications where “x is better than y” is determinately false and “y is better than x” is determinately false. The result: when “better” and “worse” are indeterminate, “equal” is determinately true in the remaining space. Vagueness about comparative superiority collapses to equality — and equality is one of the standard three relations. No fourth relation is required.

ii. responding to the argument from phenomenology

Phenomenology response — apparent difference in feel of hard cases may track vagueness not parity; perplexity is epistemic not metaphysicalChang argues that hard cases of comparison feel phenomenologically different from cases of genuine evaluative uncertainty: we don’t feel ignorant, we feel that the question is wrong. I accept that the phenomenology is real. But I deny that it tracks what Chang claims. The feeling that “which is better?” is the wrong question may simply be the characteristic feeling of confronting a vague predicate in a borderline case. When someone asks “is this person bald?” and they are a borderline case, we feel perplexity that goes beyond simple ignorance — we feel that the question may be poorly posed. This is the standard phenomenology of vagueness. Chang’s argument from phenomenology does not distinguish vagueness-perplexity from parity-perplexity; it assumes they are different without establishing it.

iii. the argument from precisification: a response

Precisification response — Chang says parity survives precisification; but this just shows the predicate’s vagueness is ineliminable by single stipulation; it doesn’t establish a fourth relationChang argues that if apparent parity were vagueness, it should be resolvable by precisification — by stipulating a precise meaning for “better than.” But no such precisification resolves the Mozart-Michelangelo case. I have two responses. First: ineliminable vagueness is not an argument against vagueness as the explanation. Many vague predicates resist simple stipulatory resolution because the vagueness has multiple dimensions; this is higher-order vagueness, not a new evaluative relation. Second: even if we accepted that no precisification resolves the case, this would establish only that the vagueness is ineliminable — that there is genuine semantic indeterminacy that no linguistic stipulation can remove. This is consistent with the vagueness explanation; it does not establish a fourth relation.

iv. parsimony and the theoretical cost of parity

Parsimony — vagueness is a well-understood phenomenon with an established framework; parity requires a new primitive; parsimony favours vaguenessThe vagueness explanation of apparent parity is more parsimonious than Chang’s. Vagueness is a well-understood phenomenon: we have supervaluationism, epistemicism, degree theories, and other frameworks for handling it. Explaining apparent parity as vagueness deploys existing theoretical machinery. Chang’s explanation requires positing a new primitive — a sui generis fourth relation irreducible to the standard three — and an entirely new structural claim about the evaluative domain. This is a very significant theoretical cost. For parsimony to favour Chang, she would need to show that vagueness definitively cannot explain the cases she identifies. She has not shown this. Until she does, parsimony dictates the simpler explanation.

Vagueness explains everything that parity is supposed to explain. The fourth relation is not needed. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Collapsing Principle: vagueness about betterness collapses to equality, not to a fourth relation
1.Supervaluationism: a sentence is true if true on all permissible precisifications; indeterminate if true on some and false on others.
2.Collapsing Principle: when "x is better than y" and "y is better than x" are both indeterminate, "x is equally good as y" is determinately true on all precisifications where neither betterness claim holds. The space left by vagueness about betterness is filled by equality.
3.This means apparent indeterminacy in betterness collapses to equality — one of the standard three relations — not to a fourth relation. No fourth relation is required to explain the phenomenon.
∴The Collapsing Principle shows that the vagueness explanation of apparent parity is internally coherent: what appears to be a fourth relation is actually indeterminate equality, which is explained without any theoretical innovation.
Argument II — The phenomenological difference between hard cases and genuine uncertainty tracks vagueness, not parity
1.Chang argues that hard cases feel phenomenologically different from genuine uncertainty: we feel the question is wrong, not that we lack the answer. But confronting a vague predicate in a borderline case also produces this phenomenology.
2.When asked "is this person bald?" about a borderline case, we feel perplexity that goes beyond simple ignorance — we feel the question may be poorly posed. This is the standard phenomenology of vagueness, not a distinctive feature of parity.
3.Chang's phenomenological argument assumes that vagueness-perplexity and parity-perplexity are phenomenologically distinct, but this is precisely what needs to be argued, not assumed.
∴The phenomenology of hard cases does not establish parity over vagueness; the characteristic feeling of confronting a borderline vague predicate is indistinguishable from the feeling Chang attributes to parity.
Argument III — Ineliminable vagueness explains why precisification does not resolve parity cases; no fourth relation is needed
1.Chang: if apparent parity were vagueness, stipulating a precise meaning for "better than" should resolve the case. But no stipulation resolves the Mozart-Michelangelo comparison.
2.Response: multi-dimensional vagueness with higher-order components resists simple stipulatory resolution. This is consistent with vagueness; it does not establish a fourth relation.
3.The ineliminability of the apparent parity establishes only that the semantic indeterminacy is deep — not that a new relation holds. Deep vagueness is more parsimonious than a sui generis fourth relation.
∴Chang's argument from precisification shows only that the vagueness is deep and multi-dimensional; it does not establish that a fourth relation holds; deep vagueness is a parsimonious explanation consistent with the evidence.
Argument IV — Parsimony favours vagueness: it explains parity cases using established theoretical machinery without new primitives
1.The vagueness explanation deploys existing, well-understood theoretical machinery (supervaluationism, epistemicism, degree theories) to explain apparent parity.
2.Chang's explanation requires a new primitive — a sui generis fourth value relation irreducible to the standard three — and a new structural claim about the evaluative domain.
3.For parsimony to favour Chang, she must show vagueness definitively cannot explain the cases. She has not. Until she does, parsimony dictates the simpler explanation.
∴Parsimony decisively favours the vagueness explanation: it accounts for apparent parity using established machinery without new primitives; Chang's parity incurs significant theoretical costs that are justified only if vagueness is demonstrably insufficient.

I want to address Chang’s project from a perspective that neither Broome nor the other opponents on this side quite occupy. I am not sceptical that there are genuinely hard cases of comparison. I am not sceptical that these cases cannot always be resolved by vagueness or ignorance. I am sceptical that they require a fourth positive value relation. My view is that genuine incomparability — the determinate failure of all three trichotomous relations — already exists, already accounts for the cases Chang identifies, and already has a satisfactory account of rational choice under it. Parity is a solution to a problem that incomparabilism has already solved.

My account of incomparability, developed in The Morality of Freedom, holds that some items are genuinely, determinately incomparable — not vaguely comparable, not roughly equal, but positively incomparable. And that incomparability is compatible with rational choice: a choice is rationally justified if the option chosen is not worse than any alternative. This is what I call the non-comparativist view.

Raz’s non-comparativist theory of rational choice under incomparability.

Comparativist thesis: A choice is rationally justified if and only if the chosen option is at least as good as every alternative. This requires complete comparability — every pair of options must be ranged against each other.

Non-comparativist thesis (Raz): A choice is rationally justified if the chosen option is not worse than any alternative. This does not require complete comparability — it requires only that no available option is better than the chosen one.

Application to hard cases: If a lawyer career and a clarinetist career are genuinely incomparable (neither is worse than the other, since neither is better), then choosing either is rationally justified under the non-comparativist thesis. No fourth relation is needed; incomparability itself explains the rational permissibility of either choice.
i. incomparability is already genuine and determinate

Genuine incomparability exists — Raz’s argument from value pluralism; incommensurable values produce determinately incomparable items; this is real not vagueThe existence of genuine value pluralism — the fact that there are multiple, irreducibly different kinds of value — already establishes that there will be cases of genuine incomparability. When a career in law and a career in music differ not merely quantitatively but qualitatively in the kinds of value they embody — the distinctive satisfactions of intellectual rigour and public service on one hand, and of aesthetic creation and expression on the other — there may simply be no fact of the matter as to which is better. Not because “better than” is vague, and not because they stand in some exotic fourth relation, but because the values they embody are genuinely plural and irreducible, and no common scale exists on which to compare them. This is incomparability, and it is a real and positive fact about the evaluative structure of the world.

ii. the non-comparativist solution to rational choice

Non-comparativist solution — rational eligibility without comparability; choice justified if not worse; Chang’s parity does not add to this pictureChang argues that incomparability faces a problem for practical reason: if neither option is better, and neither is worse, and neither is equal, how can rational choice be justified? Chang’s answer is parity: the options are comparable in the fourth relation, and this comparability grounds rational choice. My answer is different and, I believe, simpler. The non-comparativist thesis does not require a comparison to justify choice: if option A is not worse than any available alternative, then choosing A is rationally justified, full stop. The incomparable option is rationally eligible not because it stands in some positive comparative relation to the alternatives but because it does not stand in the negative relation of being worse than them. Incomparability is not an obstacle to rational choice; it is a condition under which any of the incomparable options is rationally eligible.

iii. against chang’s chaining argument: it begs the question against incomparability

Chaining begs the question — SUDP assumes incomparability cannot arise between comparable items by small improvement; this assumes what needs to be shown; incomparabilist rejects SUDPChang’s Chaining Argument relies on the Small Unidimensional Difference Principle (SUDP): between two comparable items, a small improvement in one dimension cannot trigger incomparability. This principle is the load-bearing premise of the chaining argument. But the incomparabilist should reject it. Consider: Mozart is comparable with a clearly worse sculptor at the bottom of the chain. But at some point in the chain, as the sculptor becomes more and more accomplished in the visual arts, the comparison with Mozart — who excels in music — may reach a genuine point of incomparability. Not because of vagueness, and not because of some fifth relation, but because the values at stake have become genuinely incommensurable. The SUDP rules this out by assumption, but that is precisely what needs to be established by argument, not assumed as a premise.

iv. parity adds nothing to the incomparabilist picture

Parity adds nothing — incomparability already explains hard cases; parity’s claim to be “comparability” is the only difference; but this difference carries no explanatory weightWhat does parity add to the incomparabilist picture? Chang says the crucial difference is that par items are comparable while incomparable items are not. But what work does “comparability” do in her account? She says par items can be ranked against third items in determinate ways — but incomparable items can also be ranked against third items in determinate ways. She says parity grounds rational choice through the will — but incomparabilism grounds rational choice through rational eligibility without parity. She says parity has a distinctive phenomenology — but I have denied this. At every point where parity is supposed to add something, incomparability does the same work. The fourth relation is theoretically idle.

Incomparability is real and sufficient. Parity adds nothing. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Genuine value pluralism establishes genuine incomparability without any fourth relation
1.There are multiple, irreducibly different kinds of value — the value of intellectual rigour, aesthetic creation, public service, personal intimacy, and so on — that are not reducible to a common scale.
2.When two options embody genuinely different kinds of value in irreducibly different ways, there may be no fact of the matter as to which is better. This is genuine incomparability — a determinate positive fact about the world, not a function of vagueness or ignorance.
3.This incomparability does not require a fourth relation; it requires only the denial that any of the three trichotomous relations applies — which is precisely what incomparability means.
∴Value pluralism establishes genuine incomparability without any fourth relation; the cases Chang identifies as parity are more parsimoniously explained as genuine incomparability arising from irreducibly plural values.
Argument II — The non-comparativist thesis justifies rational choice under incomparability without requiring parity
1.Non-comparativist thesis: a choice is rationally justified if the chosen option is not worse than any available alternative. This does not require that the option be better than, worse than, equal to, or on a par with every alternative.
2.Under incomparability: neither option is worse than the other (since neither is better). Therefore both options are rationally eligible under the non-comparativist thesis.
3.Parity is not needed to ground rational choice: incomparability already grounds it through rational eligibility. Chang's claim that incomparability threatens rational choice is rebutted by the non-comparativist account.
∴The non-comparativist theory justifies rational choice under incomparability without any fourth relation; Chang's primary motivation for introducing parity — to ground rational choice in hard cases — is already satisfied by incomparabilism.
Argument III — The Chaining Argument begs the question against incomparabilism by assuming SUDP without argument
1.The Chaining Argument depends on the Small Unidimensional Difference Principle: between two comparable items, a small improvement in one dimension cannot trigger incomparability. This is not established; it is assumed.
2.The incomparabilist should reject SUDP: as a sculptor ascends the chain toward Michelangelo, the comparison with Mozart (who excels in music) may reach a genuine point of incomparability where the values diverge enough to make comparison impossible.
3.SUDP rules out this possibility by assumption; but this is precisely what the chaining argument is supposed to establish, not what it can assume. The argument begs the question against the incomparabilist.
∴The Chaining Argument begs the question against incomparabilism by assuming SUDP — the principle that the incomparabilist would deny; the argument for comparability of Mozart and Michelangelo has a suppressed premise that the incomparabilist rejects.
Argument IV — Parity is theoretically idle: everything it explains is already explained by incomparabilism
1.Chang says parity explains: determinate ranking against third items (but incomparable items can also be determinately ranked against thirds); rational choice (but incomparabilism explains this through rational eligibility); distinctive phenomenology (but Broome shows vagueness produces the same phenomenology).
2.At every explanatory role that parity is supposed to fill, incomparabilism does equal or superior work without introducing a new primitive relation.
3.A theoretical posit that does no explanatory work over its rivals should be rejected by parsimony.
∴Parity is theoretically idle: everything it explains is explained equally well by incomparabilism; the fourth relation does no additional explanatory work and should be rejected by parsimony in favour of genuine incomparability.

I want to challenge the Small Improvement Argument directly — not by defending vagueness or incomparability, but by showing that the argument itself is invalid. The SIA fails because it does not establish what it needs to establish: that the comparison between C and L before the small improvement is determinate in the relevant way. Once we allow that the comparison might be indeterminate, the argument collapses.

The SIA proceeds as follows. We have C (the clarinetist career) and L (the lawyer career). We say: C′ (with a small improvement) is better than L. But C is not better than L. And since the improvement is small, C cannot be worse than L (a small improvement to a worse option cannot make it better). And C and L are not equally good (because if they were, any small improvement to one would tip it to better; but a symmetric improvement to L does not tip it back).

I accept each of these claims as intuitively compelling. But the argument has a hidden premise that is doing all the real work, and that premise is false.

i. the hidden premise: that the comparison before the improvement is determinate

Hidden premise — SIA assumes comparison between C and L is determinate before improvement; but if indeterminate, the argument fails; C′ may also be indeterminately better than LThe SIA requires that the comparison between C and L before the small improvement is determinately a case of evaluative tie — not merely that it appears tied, but that it is a specific kind of tied. Specifically, the argument requires that after the improvement, C′ is determinately better than L. But consider the possibility that the comparison between C and L is evaluatively indeterminate. If it is indeterminate whether C is better than L, then it may also be indeterminate whether C′ is better than L. The small improvement does not automatically produce a determinate verdict; it might merely shift the location of the indeterminacy slightly. Under indeterminacy, the premise “C′ is better than L” may itself be indeterminate, in which case the argument does not get started. Chang needs to establish that C′ is determinately better than L — not merely apparently or intuitively better — and the SIA does not establish this.

ii. the argument from phenomenology: chang’s defence fails

Phenomenology fails — the feeling of perplexity in hard cases is compatible with indeterminacy; phenomenology cannot distinguish the casesChang has a response: the phenomenological argument. She says that in genuine cases of indeterminacy, we experience a feeling of uncertainty or ignorance — a sense that we do not know which option is better. But in parity cases, we feel something different: we feel that the question “which is better?” is inappropriate. This difference in phenomenological character, she argues, shows that parity cases are not cases of indeterminacy. I deny that the phenomenological distinction is stable. Consider a vague predicate like “tall” applied to a borderline case. If we are asked “is this person tall?” and they are of borderline height, we do not primarily feel ignorant — we feel that the question is inappropriate. The phenomenology of vagueness-perplexity closely resembles what Chang describes as parity-perplexity. The phenomenological argument does not successfully distinguish the two phenomena.

iii. the argument from perplexity: also unsuccessful

Perplexity argument fails — asymmetry between C and L after improvement doesn’t establish parity; indeterminacy can produce this patternChang also appeals to what I call the argument from perplexity: if C and L are indeterminate, then both C′ (small improvement to C) and L′ (small improvement to L) should be determinately better than the other. But this is perplexing: it seems like C′ should be better than L but not necessarily better than L′. Chang argues this perplexity arises because C and L are on a par: the small improvement tips C into being better than L, but does not tip L′ into being better than C (or vice versa). The perplexity is therefore evidence of parity rather than indeterminacy. My response: under indeterminacy, the pattern Chang describes is also explicable. If the comparison is indeterminate in a specific direction — C is on the high side of the indeterminate range and L on the low side — a small improvement to C can move it above the indeterminate range (making it determinately better) while L′ remains in the range. This explains the asymmetry without parity.

iv. the collapsing principle reconsidered: gustafsson’s objection

Collapsing principle counterexample — Broome’s principle derivatively contradictory; Gustafsson’s counterexample: but this shows principle false or empty, not that indeterminacy failsI want to be precise about my relationship to Broome on the Collapsing Principle. I offered a counterexample to Broome’s Collapsing Principle: the principle allows us to derive an outright contradiction from the claim that some predicate has a borderline case. This is correct, and the counterexample stands. But this does not mean that the indeterminacy explanation fails. The indeterminacy explanation does not require the Collapsing Principle. I can hold both that (a) the SIA fails because the comparison between C and L may be indeterminate, and (b) the Collapsing Principle is false or empty. The indeterminacy explanation survives the counterexample to the Collapsing Principle; it simply cannot be advanced through Broome’s specific argument. The two conclusions are independent.

The SIA has a hidden indeterminacy premise. Without it the argument fails. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The SIA has a hidden premise: the pre-improvement comparison must be determinately a specific kind of tie
1.The SIA requires: C′ is determinately better than L (after small improvement). But if the comparison between C and L is indeterminate, the small improvement may shift the indeterminacy rather than producing a determinate verdict.
2.If it is indeterminate whether C is better than L, it may also be indeterminate whether C′ is better than L. A small improvement does not automatically produce determinacy.
3.The premise "C′ is better than L" is a hidden assumption in the SIA that needs to be established independently; the SIA does not establish it.
∴The SIA fails because it does not establish that C′ is determinately better than L after the small improvement; under evaluative indeterminacy, this premise may itself be indeterminate, and the argument collapses.
Argument II — The argument from phenomenology does not distinguish vagueness-perplexity from parity-perplexity
1.Chang: in parity cases we feel the question "which is better?" is inappropriate — not that we lack an answer but that the question is wrong. In genuine uncertainty we feel ignorant.
2.Counterexample: in borderline vagueness cases (a person of borderline height asked "are you tall?"), the characteristic feeling is that the question is inappropriate — not primarily that we are ignorant. This is the phenomenology of vagueness, not parity.
3.The phenomenological distinction Chang appeals to is not stable; vagueness produces the same phenomenological signature she attributes to parity.
∴The argument from phenomenology does not successfully distinguish parity from vagueness; the characteristic "question is wrong" feeling accompanies borderline vagueness cases just as it accompanies Chang's paradigm parity cases.
Argument III — The argument from perplexity is also unsuccessful: directional indeterminacy explains the asymmetry
1.Chang's perplexity argument: under parity, C′ (small improvement to C) is better than L, but L′ (small improvement to L) is not better than C (or vice versa). This asymmetry is supposed to show the items stand in a positive fourth relation, not mere indeterminacy.
2.Response: directional indeterminacy can explain this pattern. If C is at the high end of the indeterminate range and L at the low end, a small improvement to C moves it above the range (determinately better) while a small improvement to L keeps it within the range.
3.Directional indeterminacy is compatible with the asymmetric small-improvement pattern Chang describes; the perplexity argument does not establish parity over indeterminacy.
∴The argument from perplexity is unsuccessful: directional indeterminacy explains the asymmetric small-improvement pattern that Chang cites as evidence of parity; the argument does not distinguish the two explanations.
Argument IV — The counterexample to Broome's Collapsing Principle does not defeat the indeterminacy explanation of apparent parity
1.Gustafsson's counterexample to the Collapsing Principle: the principle derives an outright contradiction from the existence of borderline cases, so it is false or empty. Broome cannot use it to argue that vagueness collapses to equality.
2.But the indeterminacy explanation of apparent parity does not require the Collapsing Principle. I can hold that (a) the SIA fails due to possible indeterminacy of the C-L comparison, and (b) the Collapsing Principle is false, simultaneously and consistently.
3.The counterexample to the Collapsing Principle defeats Broome's specific argument for the vagueness view but not the indeterminacy view itself; the two are independent.
∴The counterexample to the Collapsing Principle refutes Broome's argument but not the indeterminacy explanation; the SIA's hidden indeterminacy premise remains problematic, and the indeterminacy explanation survives independently of the Collapsing Principle.

I want to press an objection to Chang’s Chaining Argument that I believe is the most serious structural challenge to her position. My claim, defended in my 2014 Ethics paper, is that the Chaining Argument is a sorites argument in disguise. And if this is right, accepting its conclusion — that Mozart and Michelangelo are comparable — commits us to a much stronger conclusion that almost no one would accept: that any two items at all, however different, are comparable. The chaining argument proves too much, and proving too much is a reductio.

Let me remind you of the argument’s structure. We have a chain of sculptors ascending from a clearly poor sculptor (s₀) to Michelangelo (sₙ). Mozart is clearly comparable with s₀. The Small Unidimensional Difference Principle (SUDP) says that a small improvement in one dimension cannot trigger incomparability where before there was comparability. By induction: if Mozart is comparable with sₖ, then (since sₖ₊₁ is only marginally better in one dimension) Mozart is comparable with sₖ₊₁. Therefore Mozart is comparable with every sculptor in the chain, including Michelangelo.

The Chaining Argument as a sorites — the formal structure
Sorites premise: If x is a heap with n grains, then x is a heap with n-1 grains.
Chaining premise (SUDP): If {Mozart, sₙ} are comparable, and sₙ₊₁ is only marginally better than sₙ in one dimension, then {Mozart, sₙ₊₁} are comparable.
Both have the same logical form: if property P holds for some level, it holds for the next incremental level. Both lead, by induction, from a clearly true base case to a clearly false conclusion when run to extremes.
The sorites problem for the chaining argument: if we run the chain long enough — from the poor sculptor, through Michelangelo, through Beethoven, through a great footballer, through a great general, through a great mathematician — SUDP implies that Mozart is comparable with everyone in the chain, including the great general and the mathematician. But clearly Mozart and Napoleon are not comparable in terms of creative achievement in any meaningful sense. The chaining argument proves too much.
i. the argument proves too much: sorites extended beyond the original chain

Proves too much — SUDP extends the chain beyond any plausible comparability boundary; Mozart comparable to Napoleon proves argument is flawedThe SUDP says that a small unidimensional improvement cannot trigger incomparability where before there was comparability. If this principle is true, then start any chain from a clearly comparable base case and you will reach, by small steps, any item at all. Starting from the poor sculptor (comparable with Mozart), ascending through great sculptors, arriving at Michelangelo, then extending through other visual artists, through great architects, through great engineers, through great generals — SUDP keeps the comparability relation open at every step. Eventually we arrive at Napoleon, who achieved greatness in the military domain. Is Napoleon comparable with Mozart in terms of creative achievement? Surely not — not because Napoleon is clearly better or worse, but because the dimensions of their achievements are now so disparate that the question has no purchase. But SUDP says they are comparable. This is a reductio of the argument.

ii. what goes wrong: SUDP is a sorites premise

SUDP is a sorites premise — tolerance principle generates sorites; SUDP is a tolerance principle about comparability; accepting it leads to absurdityThe SUDP has exactly the structure of a sorites premise. A sorites premise is a tolerance principle: if an item has property P at level n, it has property P at level n+1 (for small increments). Sorites premises are individually plausible but collectively disastrous — they lead from true base cases to false conclusions by accumulation of small steps. The SUDP is a tolerance principle about comparability: if comparability holds at one level of the chain, it holds at the next. This generates a sorites over the dimension of comparison. The argument is that we should not accept the SUDP without restriction, for exactly the same reason we should not accept any other tolerance principle without restriction: they are individually plausible but collectively false, and the chaining argument is a disguised instance of a sorites fallacy.

iii. what this shows about chang’s project

Implications for Chang — without chaining, SIA only establishes parity or incomparability or indeterminacy; comparability must be established independently; SIA is ambiguous between parity and incomparabilityWhat are the implications of the sorites objection for Chang’s project? The Chaining Argument was supposed to establish that Mozart and Michelangelo are comparable. If the argument fails, Chang is left with only the SIA, which establishes only that none of the three trichotomous relations applies between C and L — but does not establish whether this is because they are on a par (comparable, fourth relation) or genuinely incomparable (no relation). The SIA is therefore ambiguous between parity and incomparability. Chang needs the Chaining Argument to establish comparability, and the Chaining Argument fails as a sorites. Without it, parity is not established over incomparability.

iv. a diagnosis: what the chaining argument actually shows

Chaining shows vagueness — the chain’s inability to identify a sharp transition from comparability to incomparability shows that “comparable” is itself vague; this supports Broome not ChangThere is an important positive conclusion to draw from the failure of the Chaining Argument. The argument fails because there is no sharp point in the chain where comparability gives way to incomparability. This is exactly what we should expect if “comparable” is itself a vague predicate. Just as “heap” has no sharp boundary, “comparable” may have no sharp boundary: some pairs are determinately comparable (Mozart and a bad sculptor), some are determinately incomparable (Mozart and Napoleon, in terms of creative achievement), and some are borderline. The failure of the Chaining Argument is therefore evidence for vagueness in the comparability predicate, which supports Broome’s general approach and undermines Chang’s specific claim that the Mozart-Michelangelo comparison is determinately one of genuine comparability.

The chaining argument proves too much or it proves nothing. Either way, parity is not established. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Chaining Argument proves too much: SUDP implies Mozart is comparable with Napoleon, which is absurd
1.SUDP: if {Mozart, sₙ} are comparable, and sₙ₊₁ is only marginally better than sₙ in one dimension, then {Mozart, sₙ₊₁} are comparable. Applying SUDP repeatedly: Mozart is comparable with every item reachable from a clearly comparable base by small unidimensional improvements.
2.Starting from a bad sculptor (clearly comparable with Mozart), ascending through sculptors, visual artists, architects, engineers, military strategists — SUDP implies Mozart is comparable with Napoleon in terms of creative achievement.
3.But Mozart and Napoleon are clearly not comparable in terms of creative achievement — not because Napoleon is better or worse, but because the comparison has no purchase. SUDP leads to a clearly false conclusion.
∴The Chaining Argument proves too much: SUDP, applied without restriction, implies universal comparability, including clearly absurd cases; an argument that proves too much is fallacious.
Argument II — SUDP is a sorites premise (tolerance principle); it generates a sorites paradox about comparability
1.Sorites premises are tolerance principles: if P holds at level n, it holds at level n+1 (for small increments). They are individually plausible but collectively disastrous — they lead from true base cases to false conclusions.
2.SUDP has the same logical form: if comparability holds for the pair at step n in the chain, it holds for the pair at step n+1. This is a tolerance principle over the comparability dimension.
3.Accepting SUDP without restriction generates a sorites paradox about comparability; the chaining argument is a disguised sorites, and its conclusion should be rejected for the same reasons we reject sorites conclusions.
∴The Chaining Argument is a sorites argument in disguise; SUDP is a tolerance principle that should not be accepted without restriction; accepting it leads from a true base case to a false conclusion about universal comparability.
Argument III — Without the Chaining Argument, the SIA is ambiguous between parity and incomparability
1.The SIA establishes: none of "better than," "worse than," or "equally good" applies between C and L. But it does not establish whether this is because C and L are comparable (parity) or genuinely incomparable.
2.The Chaining Argument was supposed to establish comparability, allowing Chang to conclude parity rather than incomparability. If the Chaining Argument fails, this establishment is unavailable.
3.Without the Chaining Argument, the SIA is ambiguous between parity and incomparability; Chang has not established the comparability that distinguishes parity from incomparability.
∴Without the Chaining Argument, the SIA cannot establish parity over incomparability; the argument for a fourth relation rather than genuine incomparability fails at the step of establishing comparability.
Argument IV — The failure of the Chaining Argument supports vagueness in the comparability predicate
1.The Chaining Argument fails because there is no sharp point in the chain where comparability gives way to incomparability. This is expected if "comparable" is itself a vague predicate with borderline cases.
2.Some pairs are determinately comparable (Mozart and a bad sculptor), some determinately incomparable (Mozart and Napoleon), and some borderline — just as "heap" has determinately heaps, non-heaps, and borderline cases.
3.This supports Broome's general approach (vagueness in evaluative predicates) and undermines Chang's claim that Mozart and Michelangelo are determinately comparable rather than in a borderline comparability zone.
∴The failure of the Chaining Argument is evidence for vagueness in the comparability predicate itself; this supports Broome's approach and undermines Chang's claim that the Mozart-Michelangelo comparison is determinately one of genuine comparability (and hence parity).
