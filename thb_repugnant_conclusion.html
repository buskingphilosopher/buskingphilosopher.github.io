Mr President,

I want to begin with an act of intellectual honesty that I have found, over many decades, both necessary and painful. The Repugnant Conclusion states that for any possible population of at least ten billion people, all with very high quality of life, there must be some much larger imaginable population whose existence would be better, even though its members have lives that are barely worth living. I find this conclusion repugnant. The name I gave it was not ironic. And yet, after more than thirty years of searching for a satisfying escape route, I have come to believe — reluctantly — that we cannot escape it without generating conclusions that are at least as troubling.

On the argument's compelling structure

The Mere Addition PrincipleThe argument begins with a premise that seems very difficult to deny: that adding a person to the world who will have a life worth living makes the world better, other things being equal — the Mere Addition Principle. From this, together with the transitivity of "better than," the repugnant conclusion follows by a sequence of small steps. Each step seems acceptable. The conclusion seems monstrous. This is the structure of the problem. And the structure is not easily escaped.

On why the alternatives fail

Failure of alternativesAverage Utilitarianism avoids the RC but implies that a world with one supremely happy person can be better than one with a billion very happy people — manifestly absurd. Critical Level Utilitarianism generates the Very Repugnant Conclusion: adding millions of genuinely good but below-threshold lives makes the world worse. Person-affecting views cannot condemn creating lives of suffering. Variable value approaches introduce their own ad hoc thresholds. Every alternative pays a price. The question is which price is most acceptable — and I believe the RC's price is lowest, because the alternatives violate intuitions that are more robustly calibrated and more central to our moral thinking.

On revising the intuition

Unreliable population intuitionsOur moral intuitions were calibrated in the context of fixed populations — never designed to handle questions about adding large numbers of potential people to the world. When we feel that a trillion barely-happy people is worse than ten billion very happy people, we may be feeling the pull of a cognitive artefact rather than a genuine moral perception. When an argument has compelling premises and a repugnant conclusion, we face a choice: reject a premise, or revise the intuition. The premises here — the Mere Addition Principle, transitivity, welfare aggregation — are load-bearing premises for almost everything we believe about distributive justice. We are not well positioned to simply abandon them.

Mr President,

My colleague Parfit accepts the repugnant conclusion reluctantly. I accept it confidently — as the straightforward consequence of hedonistic utilitarianism, a moral view I hold robustly and that survives scrutiny. The RC, far from being repugnant, is simply what taking the equal value of all welfare seriously implies.

On why the conclusion is not actually repugnant

Unreliable population intuitionsThe conclusion is repugnant to our intuitions. But those intuitions are the product of evolutionary pressures that selected for concern with existing people — family, tribe, those already in our social world. They were not calibrated to handle large populations of potential people. Consider the people in the z-world — their lives are worth living, they are glad to exist, they experience genuine wellbeing. Who are we to say their existence does not matter? The intuition that it does not is a form of elitism about welfare — treating some conscious experiences as less morally significant because of who has them. This is incompatible with the equal moral worth of persons.

On the alternatives as failures of nerve

Failure of alternativesAverage utilitarianism is indifferent to additional happy people provided they fall below the current average — implying one supremely happy person can outweigh a billion moderately happy ones. This is far more repugnant than anything Parfit identified. Lexical threshold views arbitrarily privilege lives above a threshold — a form of welfare elitism deeply uncomfortable for anyone committed to equal moral worth. The correct response to the RC is not to find a clever escape route within population ethics. It is to accept what the equal value of welfare clearly implies.

On equal value of welfare

The Mere Addition PrincipleEvery unit of welfare has equal moral significance regardless of who experiences it or how good the rest of their life is. The Mere Addition Principle follows directly: adding genuine welfare without harming anyone makes the world better. The RC follows from this via transitivity. To deny this is to say certain conscious experiences matter less — that the quiet satisfaction of the z-world's inhabitants counts for nothing against the richer experiences of the smaller population. I cannot accept this. The equal value of welfare is not a technical premise. It is a deep moral commitment to the equal standing of all conscious experience.

Mr President,

I published a paper called "In Defence of Repugnance." I want to defend it here with the same directness. The title is not provocative. It is descriptive. The repugnant conclusion deserves a defence — not a reluctant acceptance, not an apologetic acknowledgement, but a positive argument that the conclusion is correct and that our intuitions against it are systematically mistaken.

On the source of the intuition against the RC

Unreliable population intuitionsWhy does the RC strike us as repugnant? I want to give a precise answer. Our intuition against it is driven primarily by what I call the "mere addition" bias — a systematic tendency to undervalue large increases in the number of happy people relative to increases in the welfare of existing people. This bias has several sources. We find it psychologically easier to represent the welfare of a small group vividly than to represent the aggregate welfare of a vast population. We are drawn to concrete, specific people over statistical lives. We are biased toward quality because we can imagine quality differences easily and quantity differences only abstractly. None of these are moral insights. They are psychological tendencies that produce systematic errors in our population-level moral reasoning.

On what reflection actually shows

The Mere Addition PrincipleWhen we reason carefully, consider the z-world's inhabitants one by one, and ask whether each additional life worth living adds value — the answer is obviously yes. Each person in the z-world has a life containing genuine goods. Their lives contain friendship, curiosity, pleasure, meaning — diluted, perhaps, compared to lives at higher welfare levels, but genuine. The intuition that a world containing trillions of such lives is worse than a world containing billions of much better lives fails to survive reflection on what those lives actually contain. The RC is the result of taking seriously, one by one, the value of each life worth living. The alternative is to say that at some point, adding more genuinely good lives stops mattering. That is the truly repugnant conclusion.

On the "elitism of the imagination"

Intellectual honesty about theoretical costsThere is a deeper error underlying resistance to the RC — what I call the elitism of the imagination. When we contemplate the z-world, we imagine lives that seem barely worth living from our perspective — drab, thin, joyless. But lives barely worth living are lives that their owners regard as worth living. The z-world's inhabitants do not experience their lives as barely worth living. They experience them as their lives — as valuable, as worth caring about. Our resistance to the RC is partly a failure of imagination: we project our standards onto lives that, by the lights of those who live them, are genuinely good. The RC asks us to take those lives seriously. We should.

Mr President,

I want to make a case for accepting the repugnant conclusion that differs from my colleagues' — one grounded not merely in abstract theoretical considerations but in the concrete practical stakes of what we decide. I work on existential risk and the long-run future of humanity. And I want to argue that what is at stake in population ethics — and in this specific debate — is not a philosophical puzzle but one of the most practically consequential questions we face.

On the practical stakes of population ethics

Intellectual honesty about theoretical costsIf humanity survives the next few centuries and spreads across even a small fraction of the galaxy, the number of future people could be astronomically large — perhaps 10 to the power of 23 or more over the lifespan of the universe. The question of how to count those future people — whether to sum welfare, average it, apply critical levels, or weight by priority — is not merely academic. It determines what we should sacrifice today for the future, how we should weigh existential risks against ordinary harms, how we should think about the value of human civilisation continuing at all. In that context, a population axiology that dismisses the RC because of an intuition calibrated for small-scale social life is not merely theoretically inadequate. It is dangerous.

On totalism and the value of the future

The Mere Addition PrincipleThe totalist view — that the goodness of a world is the sum of its welfare — gives us a coherent, consistent, and action-guiding framework for thinking about the long-run future. It says that preventing existential catastrophe is among the most important things we can do, because the catastrophe would eliminate a vast quantity of potential welfare. It says that creating conditions for flourishing matters — not just for the present generation but for all those who will follow. This framework is not infinitely demanding, and it is not committed to any particular policy. But it is committed to taking seriously the welfare of all future people, including the vast numbers whose lives might be brief or modest by our current standards. That commitment is expressed precisely in accepting the RC.

On the alternatives from a longtermist perspective

Impossibility and the need for practical guidanceThe alternatives to totalism — average views, critical level views, person-affecting views — all generate deeply problematic verdicts when applied to the long-run future. Average views imply that civilisational survival matters only if future people will be happier than the current average — a conclusion that would sometimes recommend civilisational extinction. Critical level views, if the critical level is set high enough to avoid the RC, imply that creating billions of people with good lives is bad — making them useless as guides to policy. Person-affecting views struggle to explain why preventing extinction is important at all, since there is no existing person who will be harmed by the failure of the species to continue. From a longtermist perspective, totalism is not merely one option among equals. It is the only framework that takes the future seriously.

Mr President,

I want to approach this debate from an unusual direction. I am not going to defend one of the standard alternatives to totalism. I am going to argue something more fundamental: that the repugnant conclusion is one symptom of a deeper impossibility — and that accepting it represents a choice of theoretical cost, not a solution.

On the impossibility theorems

Impossibility theoremsI have proven a series of impossibility theorems showing that no population axiology can simultaneously satisfy a small number of independently plausible conditions. These include: the Egalitarian Dominance Condition (a perfectly equal population is better than one where all are worse off than the worst-off person in the first); the General Non-Extreme Priority Condition (it is better to give a larger benefit to a worse-off person than a smaller benefit to a better-off person under specified conditions); the Dominance Addition Condition (adding people much better off than the worst-off improves the population); and the Weak Non-Sadism Condition (adding people with positive welfare is not worse than adding people with negative welfare). Each condition is highly plausible. Each is supported by strong intuitions. And yet — by formal proof — no population axiology satisfies all four simultaneously.

On what the impossibility implies

Intellectual honesty about theoretical costsAccepting the RC does not solve the problem. It merely represents one particular allocation of theoretical costs — one particular way of violating one of the conditions. Every possible approach violates at least one. The proposition presents the RC as the least bad option. But it has not established that the Egalitarian Dominance Condition — which totalism violates by implying trillions of barely-positive lives outweigh billions of very good lives — is less important than the conditions violated by alternatives. That comparison has not been made. It is being assumed.

On intellectual humility

Intellectual humility in population ethicsPopulation ethics is genuinely hard — not in the sense that we have not yet found the right answer, but in the sense that there may be no fully satisfactory answer. The impossibility theorems suggest the domain is inconsistent with our moral commitments, not merely that we lack an insight. This does not mean we should give up. It means we should be modest. The repugnant conclusion should not be naïvely rejected as if rejection were free — but it should not be confidently accepted as if acceptance were free either. Every position pays a price. The question of which price to pay has not been answered by this debate.

Mr President,

Both sides have accepted, almost without question, the logical structure of the argument leading to the repugnant conclusion. The argument uses transitivity: if A is better than B, and B is better than C, then A is better than C. I want to challenge the logic itself — specifically, the transitivity of "better than" as applied to populations.

On the argument's dependence on transitivity

Rejecting transitivityThe argument constructs a sequence A, A+, B, B+...Z. At each step, the next population is argued to be better. The RC is then derived by transitivity. But why should we accept transitivity for complex population comparisons? Consider: is Beethoven's Fifth Symphony better than Miles Davis's Kind of Blue? Is Kind of Blue better than Abbey Road? Does it follow that Beethoven's Fifth is better than Abbey Road? These are comparisons between things good in very different ways. Transitivity may break down when applied to comparisons between incommensurable goods — and population comparisons are precisely such comparisons.

On why transitivity fails here

Rejecting transitivityConsider the sequence from A to Z carefully. Each intermediate comparison is made along a particular dimension — total welfare or number of happy lives. But comparing A directly to Z tracks different features: quality of life, richness of experience, depth of wellbeing. The seemingly paradoxical result dissolves once we recognise that the intermediate comparisons and the direct comparison are tracking different morally relevant features. "Better than" is not a single transitive relation — it is a family of context-sensitive relations. Forcing them into a single ordering produces the RC. The RC is an artefact of that forced reduction, not a genuine moral truth.

On why this is not mere paradox-avoidance

Intellectual humility in population ethicsI anticipate the charge that rejecting transitivity is desperate — saving us from one paradox at the cost of logical incoherence. I do not accept this. Transitivity is a formal requirement for consistent preferences in individual decision theory. It is not obviously required for ethical comparisons between complex social states along multiple incommensurable dimensions. The multi-criteria decision-making literature has extensively documented conditions under which transitivity breaks down. Population comparisons are precisely such conditions. I am recommending a more nuanced picture of what rational comparison requires — one that takes seriously the genuine incommensurability of the values at stake.

Mr President,

I want to offer an escape from the repugnant conclusion that is principled, grounded in welfare theory, and honest about its own costs — the critical level approach. I do not pretend it is problem-free. I do argue that it is preferable to accepting the conclusion my opponents defend.

On what the critical level approach says

Failure of alternativesThe critical level utilitarian view holds that an addition to a population increases the value of the world if and only if that person's welfare exceeds a critical level — a positive threshold above mere positive welfare. Below this threshold, adding a person with a good life does not improve the world. The repugnant conclusion is avoided because the z-world's inhabitants, though their lives are worth living, may not meet the critical level. Their addition does not therefore improve the world sufficiently to outweigh the lower welfare of the vastly larger population compared to the original ten billion.

On the theoretical grounding of a critical level

The Mere Addition PrincipleThe critical level approach is not arbitrary. It reflects the observation that the Mere Addition Principle, while intuitively appealing for lives well above neutral, is not intuitively compelling for lives only barely above neutral. Adding a person whose life contains marginally more pleasure than pain — but only marginally — does not obviously make the world better. The critical level captures the intuition that there is a morally relevant distinction between lives that genuinely flourish and lives that merely scrape above the threshold of bare worth. This is not elitism. It is a recognition that the value of a life for the purpose of population aggregation is not simply whether it is positive, but whether it rises to a level that meaningfully contributes to the good.

On the Very Repugnant Conclusion and what it actually shows

Intellectual honesty about theoretical costsMy opponents will invoke the Very Repugnant Conclusion — the objection that the critical level view implies adding billions of good-but-below-threshold lives makes the world worse. I acknowledge this cost. But I want to be precise about what it shows. It shows that if the critical level is set too high, we generate implausible verdicts about large numbers of good lives. This does not show that the critical level approach is wrong — it shows that the critical level must be set carefully, and that its precise calibration is a genuine theoretical challenge. It does not show that accepting the RC is preferable. The Very Repugnant Conclusion is a problem for a badly calibrated critical level. A well-calibrated critical level avoids both.

Mr President,

I want to offer a different escape from the repugnant conclusion — one that does not require abandoning the total view, does not invoke arbitrary critical levels, and does not reject the transitivity of rational preference. I call it the variable value approach: the marginal value of adding an extra person to an already large population diminishes as the population grows.

On the variable value approach

The Mere Addition PrincipleThe key insight is this: the value of adding a person to a world is not constant across different population sizes. When a population is small, adding an additional person with a good life adds substantial value — there is room for more happiness, more diversity, more of what makes human existence rich. But as population grows very large, the marginal value of an additional person declines. Not because their welfare counts for less — every unit of welfare still counts — but because the contribution of an additional person to the overall value of the world diminishes as the world already contains a very large population of people with lives worth living. The Mere Addition Principle remains roughly true for moderate populations but loses its force at extreme scales.

On how this avoids the RC

Failure of alternativesThe RC arises because the MAP is applied without diminution across the entire sequence from A to Z. At each step, adding more people improves things — and so the process continues until we reach a world of trillions at barely positive welfare. The variable value approach interrupts this process. At some point in the sequence, the marginal value of additional people at low welfare levels becomes sufficiently small that the gain from adding them does not outweigh the reduction in average welfare. We get a population that is optimal — large enough to take advantage of the benefits of many lives, but not so large that the declining marginal value of additions drives us to the z-world.

On the theoretical grounding of diminishing marginal value

Unreliable population intuitionsThe diminishing marginal value of additional people is not merely a convenient escape route. It has independent theoretical support. Consider that the goods available from human existence — friendship, love, discovery, culture — are goods that can be shared and are enhanced by the existence of others, but up to a point. An additional person in a world of ten billion contributes to and benefits from a rich network of human relationships and achievements. An additional person in a world of a trillion, where resources are stretched and welfare is barely positive, contributes and receives far less. The diminishing returns are grounded in the structure of the human goods that make life worth living — they are not arbitrary.

Mr President,

I want to offer what I believe is the most philosophically fundamental escape from the repugnant conclusion — one that does not require technical modifications to the total view, does not invoke impossibility theorems, and does not rely on rejecting transitivity. It requires only that we get clear about the structure of our obligations.

On the person-affecting view

The Mere Addition PrincipleThe Mere Addition Principle says that adding a person with a life worth living makes the world better. I deny this. More precisely: the addition of a person who will have a good life makes the world better only if it is better for someone — only if there is a person for whom the addition represents an improvement over their alternative. But the person who is added has no alternative. Before they exist, there is no subject with respect to whom existence could be better or worse. The MAP is therefore not a principle about making things better for people. It is a principle about bringing people into existence — which is a very different thing. And our obligations, I argue, are about the former, not the latter.

On Narveson's slogan

Intellectual humility in population ethics"We are in favour of making people happy, but neutral about making happy people." This is my central claim. We have strong reasons to improve the welfare of people who exist — their welfare can be better or worse for them, and they have interests in its improvement. We do not have comparable reasons to create new people, even people who will be happy — because until they exist, there is no person whose interests would be served by their creation. The RC arises from treating these two kinds of value as commensurable — treating the welfare of the billions in the z-world as counting positively in a way that can outweigh the higher welfare of the smaller population. On the person-affecting view, this comparison is misconceived from the outset.

On the Non-Identity objection and what it really shows

Failure of alternativesThe standard objection to person-affecting views is the Non-Identity Problem: if we create a different person than we would have otherwise, that person cannot claim to have been harmed — they would not have existed otherwise. I accept that the person-affecting view has difficulty with some Non-Identity cases. But this difficulty does not defeat the view in the domain that matters for the RC. The RC is about choosing between possible worlds with different populations — worlds in which we could choose to bring different numbers of people into existence. The Non-Identity Problem is most pressing for cases where we harm specific people relative to their alternatives. For the RC, where the relevant populations are largely disjoint, the person-affecting view gives clear verdicts: we have no obligation to bring the z-world into existence, and its existence does not make things better in any morally relevant sense.

Mr President,

I want to make a different kind of argument from my colleagues. While they have offered escape routes — critical levels, variable values, person-affecting views — I want to defend the intuition against the repugnant conclusion more directly. The case for accepting the RC, I argue, is considerably weaker than Parfit, Tännsjö and Huemer have suggested. And the intuition against it is considerably more reliable.

On what the intuition against the RC actually tracks

Unreliable population intuitionsMy opponents have argued that the intuition against the RC is unreliable — a cognitive bias, an artefact of our evolutionary history, a form of welfare elitism. I want to challenge this dismissal. The intuition against the RC is not merely the intuition that a smaller population of very happy people feels better than a larger population of barely happy people. It tracks something more specific and more defensible: the intuition that the quality of individual lives matters in a way that cannot be fully captured by their aggregate quantity. This is not a bias. It is a substantive moral insight about the relationship between individual wellbeing and social value. The question is whether our population axiology can do justice to it.

On the reliability of the intuition

Intellectual honesty about theoretical costsThe charge that the intuition against the RC is unreliable rests on an analogy with other domains where strong intuitions have turned out to be mistaken — racial prejudice, parochialism, status quo bias. But the intuition against the RC does not have the structure of these biases. It does not privilege one group over another. It does not reflect an irrational attachment to existing arrangements. It reflects a considered judgement, on reflection, that a world in which individual lives are impoverished — even if they are numerous — is worse than a world in which individual lives flourish, even if they are fewer. This judgement survives careful scrutiny. It is not a bias.

On what the proposition needs to establish

Impossibility theoremsThe proposition asks us to accept the RC on the grounds that the alternatives are worse. But this argument is only as strong as the claim that accepting the RC carries less theoretical cost than any alternative. That claim has not been established. Arrhenius has shown that every position pays a price. The proposition has not shown that the RC's price — violating the intuition that ten billion flourishing lives are better than trillions of barely living ones — is lower than the price of any alternative. In the absence of that showing, the intuition against the RC stands. It is the proposition that needs to make its case. It has not done so.

Mr President,

I want to approach this debate from the perspective of moral uncertainty — specifically, from the question of what we should believe about population ethics given that we are deeply uncertain about which population axiology is correct. I argue that under appropriate treatment of moral uncertainty, we should not accept the repugnant conclusion.

On moral uncertainty in population ethics

Impossibility theoremsPopulation ethics is a domain of extreme moral uncertainty. The impossibility theorems proved by Arrhenius show that no available population axiology is fully satisfactory. Every position has compelling objections. In this situation, the rational response is not to pick one theory and follow it to its conclusions — it is to reason under moral uncertainty, weighing the verdicts of different theories by our credences in them. The question is not "what does our preferred population axiology say?" but "given our uncertainty about which population axiology is correct, what should we believe about the RC?"

On what reasoning under moral uncertainty implies

Intellectual humility in population ethicsWhen we reason under moral uncertainty, we should not simply follow the verdicts of the theory we find most plausible — particularly when those verdicts are extreme and the theory is contested. The RC is the verdict of totalism — a theory that has compelling arguments in its favour but also faces devastating objections. Under moral uncertainty, we should weight the verdict of totalism by our credence in totalism. Since our credence in totalism is significantly less than one — given the genuine force of the alternatives and the impossibility theorems — the verdict that we should accept the RC is correspondingly diluted. The RC should be accepted only if totalism is correct. Since we are uncertain whether totalism is correct, the rational attitude under uncertainty is not to accept the RC but to remain uncertain about it.

On why the proposition's argument does not overcome uncertainty

Unreliable population intuitionsThe proposition argues that the alternatives to totalism are all worse, and that therefore we should accept the RC as the least bad option. But this argument assumes that we can confidently identify totalism as superior to all alternatives. The impossibility theorems show that we cannot. Every available population axiology has compelling objections. The appropriate response under uncertainty is not to accept the RC because totalism seems marginally less bad than its rivals. It is to acknowledge that our credence in any particular population axiology is low, and that the extreme verdicts of those axiologies — including the RC — should be held with corresponding tentativeness. The RC is not merely counterintuitive. It is the extreme verdict of an uncertain theory. Accepting it as if it were established is not intellectual honesty. It is overconfidence.
