Logical Arguments

Mr President,

Black Wednesday — the 16th of September 1992 — has become shorthand for humiliation in British political history. The pound crashed out of the ERM. The Treasury spent billions in a single afternoon. Interest rates were raised to 15% in a single day. And yet the lesson usually drawn from Black Wednesday is the wrong lesson. The lesson usually drawn is: fixed exchange rates are a delusion, currency pegs cannot be defended against the market. This lesson is wrong — or at least, it is the right answer to the wrong question.

On what the ERM was actually trying to do

ERM as credibility-borrowing inflation anchorThe ERM was primarily a mechanism for importing credibility — for allowing high-inflation countries like Britain and Italy to borrow the anti-inflationary reputation of the Bundesbank by anchoring their currencies to the Deutschmark. Britain's inflation problem in the 1980s was genuine and serious. The mechanism by which the Thatcher government had reduced it — monetarism, broadly defined — had been effective but extraordinarily costly. The search for an alternative inflation anchor — one that could provide monetary discipline without requiring the political cost of explicitly targeting monetary aggregates — was entirely reasonable. By pegging to a currency managed by an institution whose anti-inflationary credentials were impeccable and whose independence was constitutionally entrenched, a government could delegate its monetary credibility to Frankfurt. The theory was not wrong.

On what went wrong

Failure from wrong parity, reunification shock, capital liberalisationThree things combined to make the ERM unworkable in 1992. First, Britain entered at the wrong parity — 2.95 Deutschmarks, almost certainly too high by 1992, a political decision driven partly by Lawson's informal Deutschmark shadowing before formal entry. Second, German reunification created an asymmetric shock for which the ERM had no mechanism. Absorbing East Germany required massive fiscal expansion; the Bundesbank, confronting inflationary pressure, responded with high interest rates; those interest rates propagated across the ERM including to Britain, which was in recession and needed lower rates. The ERM assumed broadly symmetric conditions; reunification produced the most dramatically asymmetric shock imaginable. Third, capital liberalisation in 1990 transformed the speculative landscape. Before it, currency pegs could be defended because speculative capital was limited. After it, the volume of speculative capital dwarfed the reserves of any central bank.

On why the principle survives

Failure of execution not principle — coordination was lackingNone of these failures is a failure of the ERM's underlying principle. They are failures of execution, of political judgement, and of institutional design. An ERM entered at the right parity, supported by a mechanism for policy coordination between members facing asymmetric shocks, and designed with some form of capital flow management, might have worked. The earlier period of ERM participation — before German reunification, before full capital liberalisation — had delivered genuine convergence and genuine inflation reduction across member states. The principle that exchange rate discipline can serve as an inflation anchor is not refuted by the events of 1992. It is illustrated by them. What those events show is not that the principle was wrong but that achieving its objectives requires a degree of political and institutional coordination that Europe in 1992 did not have.

Mr President,

I want to make a more precise version of the case that the ERM's principle was sound and its failure contingent. My argument draws on the "impossible trinity" — the observation that a country cannot simultaneously maintain a fixed exchange rate, free capital flows, and independent monetary policy. You can have any two, but not all three.

On the impossible trinity and the ERM's design

The impossible trinity — ERM designed for capital controls worldThe ERM was designed in the 1970s and early 1980s, when capital flows were substantially controlled. In that era, the impossible trinity was not a binding constraint, because one of its corners — free capital movement — had been deliberately limited. Countries could maintain fixed exchange rates and independent monetary policy simultaneously, because speculative capital was not fully mobile. This was the world for which the ERM was designed. And in that world, it worked. The 1983–1990 evidence is clear: ERM membership was associated with lower inflation and greater exchange rate stability. The mechanism delivered what it was designed to deliver.

On how the world changed

Capital liberalisation made the halfway house unworkableThe world changed in 1990, when the European Community implemented free movement of capital required by the Single Market programme. This change — appropriate on economic grounds, since capital controls impose serious efficiency costs — fundamentally altered the ERM's dynamics. Once capital was fully mobile, the impossible trinity became a binding constraint. Countries in the ERM faced a stark choice: subordinate monetary policy entirely to the requirements of the peg, or face speculative attack. For Britain in 1992, with unemployment above 10% and the economy in recession, the interest rates required to defend the pound were politically and economically unsustainable. The peg broke not because the principle of exchange rate discipline was wrong but because the conditions required to make it work — either capital controls or full monetary union — had not been created.

On what would have been required

Failure of execution not principle — coordination was lackingThe ERM, to work under full capital mobility, required either a genuine European lender of last resort capable of providing unlimited support to currencies under speculative attack, or full monetary union. The 1992 crisis was, in a real sense, Europe being pushed by the logic of the impossible trinity toward the answer the Delors Committee had already identified: that a single market with free capital flows required a single currency. The ERM's failure was not a failure of the principle of exchange rate coordination but a failure of the halfway house — caught between the old world of capital controls for which it had been designed, and the new world of free capital flows for which it had not been redesigned. The principle was right. The institution was stranded between two worlds.

Mr President,

I want to make the monetary policy case — the argument that exchange rate anchoring as an inflation targeting mechanism was sound in principle, and that its failure in Britain was the product of a specific and identifiable policy error, not a structural flaw.

On credibility and the time-consistency problem

ERM as credibility-borrowing inflation anchorModern monetary economics has established the time-consistency problem of monetary policy. A central bank free to choose any monetary policy at any time faces an incentive to inflate — to trade off higher inflation for lower unemployment in the short run. Even if it genuinely intends to maintain price stability, private agents who know it faces this incentive will not fully believe its commitment. They will build inflation expectations into wage and price setting, making the bank's job harder and potentially generating inflation as a self-fulfilling prophecy. The solution is credible commitment — institutional arrangements that make deviation from price stability costly. Independent central banks with explicit inflation targets are one solution. Exchange rate pegs to credible low-inflation currencies are another. The ERM offered Britain the second solution. This was, in principle, exactly the right solution to Britain's problem — a Bank of England that was not independent, had no explicit inflation target, and whose credibility had been damaged by a decade of stop-go policy.

On what the Lawson boom revealed

Failure from wrong parity, reunification shock, capital liberalisationThe specific failure in Britain was the Lawson boom of 1987–1988. Chancellor Lawson, convinced that the medium-term financial strategy had eliminated inflation as a serious concern, loosened fiscal and monetary policy simultaneously. The result was an asset price boom, a current account deficit, and an inflation rate that reached nearly 10% by 1990 — precisely when Britain was entering the ERM. Britain entered with inflation significantly above the German rate. This meant that even at constant nominal interest rates, British real interest rates were lower than German ones — which meant the exchange rate was under persistent real appreciation pressure, eroding competitiveness, and the burden of adjustment fell entirely on domestic deflation. This was a recipe for the recession of 1991–92 and for the parity becoming unsustainable.

On what the lesson actually is

ERM failure as German political interest — the hegemony argumentThe lesson is not that exchange rate anchoring was wrong. The lesson is that exchange rate anchoring requires entering with appropriate fundamentals — with inflation already aligned with the anchor country's, with fiscal policy consistent with the peg, and with a realistic assessment of the sustainable exchange rate. Britain entered with none of these conditions met. The failure was a failure of preparation. A government that had maintained its fiscal discipline during the Lawson boom, that had pursued monetary tightening rather than loosening in 1987–88, and that had entered the ERM at a lower and more sustainable parity would have had a very different experience in 1992. The principle is vindicated by the counter-factual.

Mr President,

I published an essay in 1953 called "The Case for Flexible Exchange Rates." It was controversial at the time. It is no longer controversial. Everything that has happened since — including the events of 1992 — has confirmed what that essay argued. Fixed exchange rates are a form of price control. And price controls, in the long run, always fail.

On what exchange rates are

Fixed rates as price controls — structural impossibilityAn exchange rate is a price — the price of one currency in terms of another. Like all prices, it performs an informational and allocative function. It tells producers and consumers in different countries about the relative costs of goods and services across national boundaries. It adjusts to reflect differences in inflation rates, productivity growth, capital flows, and preferences. When underlying fundamentals change, the exchange rate adjusts to reflect the new equilibrium. When a government pegs the exchange rate, it prevents this adjustment. It substitutes an administered price for a market price. The consequences are predictable: the administered price will diverge from the equilibrium price whenever underlying fundamentals change, and the longer the divergence is maintained, the larger the eventual correction will be. This is not a comment about the ERM specifically. It is a general truth about price controls.

On why the failure was not contingent

Failure from wrong parity, reunification shock, capital liberalisationMy proposition colleagues argue the ERM's failure was contingent — the product of bad luck, bad timing, and bad execution. If only Britain had entered at a better parity, if only German reunification had not occurred, if only capital liberalisation had been managed — the mechanism would have worked. I want to argue this is a comforting fiction. Countries in the ERM were at different stages of their business cycles, had different inflation rates, different productivity trajectories, different labour market structures, and different fiscal positions. These differences required different monetary policies. The ERM required a single monetary policy anchored to German conditions. This is the contradiction at the heart of the mechanism. No amount of coordination, no choice of parity, and no redesign of the institutional architecture could have resolved it. The mechanism was trying to solve a problem that has only one real solution: either a single economy with a single central bank, or separate economies with separate monetary policies.

On the pre-1990 record — reinterpreted

ERM failure as German political interest — the hegemony argumentMy opponents will cite the evidence that the ERM worked between 1983 and 1990. I do not dispute the evidence. I dispute the interpretation. What the ERM delivered in that period was not the benefit of exchange rate coordination. It was deflationary pressure imposed on high-inflation countries by the requirement to match German interest rates. The mechanism worked, in the short run, by forcing painful domestic adjustment on countries that had allowed inflation to develop. Germany got the benefits of exchange rate stability — its export sector was protected from competitive devaluations. The high-inflation countries got a deflationary straitjacket. This was not a cooperative arrangement. It was a hegemonic one, dressed in the language of coordination.

Mr President,

I want to make an argument that is less fashionable than my colleague Friedman's and, I think, more important. The argument is not primarily economic. It is political. The ERM was not a badly executed attempt at a good idea. It was a well-executed attempt at a bad idea — a political project that served specific national interests, above all French and German interests, while imposing costs on other participants who were not fully informed of what they were signing up for.

On the political origins of the ERM

ERM failure as German political interest — the hegemony argumentThe ERM was not created because European economists had concluded that fixed exchange rates were optimal for the Community. It was created because France, under President Giscard d'Estaing, wanted to constrain German monetary power. Germany had the most credible central bank in Europe, the hardest currency, and the most successful export sector. The Deutschmark's strength was, from a French perspective, a problem — not because France wanted to inflate, but because German monetary supremacy translated directly into German economic dominance. The ERM was the French answer: by tying all European currencies to the Deutschmark through a fixed rate mechanism, France could make German monetary power a collective resource rather than a unilateral German advantage. Germany would be constrained — by political obligations to its partners — from raising interest rates as sharply as it might otherwise choose.

On what Britain was not told

ERM as credibility-borrowing inflation anchorBritain joined the ERM in October 1990, more than a decade after the mechanism had been established. By 1990, the ERM had evolved significantly. It had become, de facto, a zone of German monetary hegemony. The other members did not have monetary policy — they had a choice between matching German interest rates or leaving the mechanism. This was not the system presented to the British public or, arguably, to the British cabinet. The argument for British membership had been made in terms of inflation control — the credibility-borrowing argument my proposition colleagues have made. What was not made explicit was that credibility-borrowing is not free. Its price is the subordination of monetary policy to the requirements of the anchor currency. For Britain, with a different economic structure from Germany, different sectoral exposures, and different business cycle timing, this price was very high.

On the mechanism working as designed

Failure of execution not principle — coordination was lackingThe events of 1992 were not a failure of the ERM. They were the mechanism working exactly as it was designed to work — imposing discipline on countries whose fundamentals did not fit the requirements of the peg. The discipline was economically and politically intolerable for Britain. The appropriate conclusion is not that the mechanism failed but that it was inappropriate for Britain, and that Britain should not have joined. The proposition distinguishes right in principle from wrong in practice. But for Britain, it was wrong in principle — the principle of credibility-borrowing through exchange rate subordination was the wrong principle for the wrong country at the wrong time. And those responsible for the decision did not fully disclose the terms of the bargain to those who would pay its costs.

Mr President,

I want to close with an argument that is more technical than my colleagues' but ultimately the most powerful case against the ERM as a matter of principle — the argument from second-generation currency crisis theory. The ERM was inherently vulnerable to self-fulfilling speculative attack regardless of fundamentals, and this vulnerability was a structural feature of the mechanism, not a contingent failure of execution.

On first-generation and second-generation crisis models

Capital liberalisation made the halfway house unworkableThe traditional first-generation theory of currency crises holds that speculative attacks succeed only when a country's fundamentals are inconsistent with its exchange rate target. On this view, a country with sound fundamentals need not fear speculative attack. The first-generation model seems to support the proposition's position: if Britain's fundamentals had been sound, the attack would not have succeeded. But the events of 1992 do not fit the first-generation model well. The French franc — which had lower inflation than Germany, a current account surplus, and broadly sound fiscal policy — was attacked and came within a hair's breadth of breaking. What was happening?

On the second-generation model

The impossible trinity — ERM designed for capital controls worldThe second-generation model, developed in the aftermath of 1992, shows that fixed exchange rates can be subject to self-fulfilling crises — crises that occur not because fundamentals are inconsistent with the peg, but because the government's willingness to defend the peg is conditional on the cost of defence, and that cost depends on whether a crisis occurs. The logic: the government finds it optimal to maintain the peg if defence costs are low. Those costs — high interest rates' impact on output, employment, banking stability — depend on the scale of the speculative attack. The scale of attack depends on whether speculators believe the peg will be maintained. If they believe it will be maintained, no attack occurs. If they believe it will be abandoned, an attack occurs, defence costs rise, the government abandons the peg, and their belief is confirmed. Both outcomes can be equilibria simultaneously. Which one occurs depends not on fundamentals but on coordination among speculators and beliefs about others' actions.

On what this implies for the ERM as principle

Fixed rates as price controls — structural impossibilityThe second-generation model implies that the ERM, under full capital mobility, was inherently unstable regardless of fundamentals. Even with perfect fundamentals, a currency peg is vulnerable to self-fulfilling attack whenever speculators have reason to doubt the government's political will to pay the cost of defence. And in a democracy, political will to pay the cost — high interest rates, recession, unemployment — is always contestable. The proposition argues that with better fundamentals, the ERM would have survived. The second-generation model shows this is wrong. Even with perfect fundamentals, a country whose government lacks unlimited resources and unlimited political will — which is all governments, always — is vulnerable. The ERM's vulnerability in 1992 was not the result of specific errors of execution. It was the result of a fundamental structural property of fixed exchange rates under capital mobility. The ERM was wrong in principle.
