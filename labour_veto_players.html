The Party That Cannot
Remove or Keep Its Leader

Labour's leadership crisis is a live veto player deadlock. Starmer cannot be removed because no single actor holds enough power to force it. He cannot govern effectively for the same reason.

Analysis date: 28 May 2026 · Sources: Opinium, Wikipedia, William Hill, Polymarket

By mid-May 2026, over 95 Labour MPs had called on Starmer to resign — and he refused. This is not stubbornness. It is a veto player problem: removing a sitting Prime Minister requires multiple actors to coordinate simultaneously, and each potential veto player faces different incentives, different ideal points, and different thresholds of acceptable outcomes.

What each player will tolerate

Coalition winsets

Where deals are geometrically possible

The green zone marks where the parliamentary party, the membership, and the Burnham camp simultaneously prefer an outcome to Starmer staying. The Starmer-loyalist circle does not intersect this zone — hence deadlock. The right bloc (NEC + loyalists) can prevent a challenge but cannot generate a positive majority for continuity.

Borda count

The least-bad outcome

When no single outcome commands unanimous support, Borda identifies the consensus candidate — the outcome all players can tolerate even if none loves it. A managed Burnham transition scores across most factions; an immediate forced removal scores high with rebels but zero with loyalists; Starmer staying scores high with loyalists but zero with the 95+ rebels.

Three predictions from veto player theory
Prediction 1 — the NEC veto will partially collapse

Tsebelis predicts that a veto player loses credibility when its obstructionism becomes visibly counterproductive. The NEC's 8–1 block on Gorton accelerated the crisis it was meant to prevent. The Makerfield reversal — NEC approved Burnham's candidacy on 15 May — confirms this: the formal veto player yielded when the political cost of holding exceeded the cost of conceding.

Prediction 2 — Starmer's survival depends on veto player fragmentation

Over 110 backbenchers signed a letter opposing a leadership challenge. This is not support for Starmer — it reflects the parliamentary party's collective action problem: Streeting, Rayner, and Burnham each want a contest but each prefers a different winner. As long as they cannot coordinate, Starmer's indifference curve overlaps with the no-change zone, and the status quo (his remaining) persists despite the opposition bloc being numerically larger.

Prediction 3 — the Borda winner will be the next leader

In an empty all-player winset, the Borda winner — the candidate who accumulates the most second and third preferences — has a structural advantage. Burnham scores first with the membership (20%), acceptable to the parliamentary party, and is Polymarket's 47.6% favourite. Rayner (9/4 bookmakers) and Streeting (11/2) each score higher with sub-groups but lower across the full preference distribution. The Borda logic predicts Burnham.

Theoretical verdict

Labour's crisis is a textbook demonstration of Tsebelis's insight that policy stability and leadership stability are structurally equivalent problems. Just as a winset requires all veto players to prefer change to the status quo simultaneously, a leadership transition requires the parliamentary party, the NEC, the membership, and the Prime Minister to align on a successor simultaneously. They cannot — and so Starmer remains, not because he commands support, but because his opponents cannot coordinate. The Borda count predicts the resolution: the candidate who is least bad for the most factions wins — and by that measure, the Makerfield by-election is not just a parliamentary procedure. It is the winset opening.
