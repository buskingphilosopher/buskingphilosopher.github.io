Logical Arguments

Professor Waldron,

I want to begin by registering what I think is the most important feature of the framework within which we are debating — a feature which, once noted, should condition everything that follows. We are not debating whether killing is good. We are not debating whether it is ever regrettable. We are debating whether, given that a state of continuous armed conflict exists between democratic states and terrorist organisations, a particular method of prosecuting that conflict is morally and legally justifiable. The question is not whether we are at war. We are. The question is whether we may fight it this way.

And I want to insist, at the outset, that the starting point for this analysis is neither the police station nor the courtroom but the battlefield. The moment you grant — as I think you must — that there is an ongoing armed conflict between, say, the United States and al-Qaeda, or between Israel and Hamas, the entire moral and legal framework shifts. Combatant status is relevant. The laws of armed conflict apply. And the prohibition on killing that governs peacetime relations between individuals gives way, for those who have chosen to take up arms against a state, to a very different moral and legal calculus.

I. The status of the conflict: this is war, not policing

Armed conflict framework: the legal and moral default is combat, not law enforcementMy foundational claim is that the conflicts in which targeted killing is employed — the campaign against al-Qaeda after September 11, the conflict between Israel and Hamas, the campaigns against ISIS — constitute genuine armed conflicts in the legal and moral sense. This is not a metaphor or a rhetorical convenience. It is a determination with concrete legal consequences. In an armed conflict, combatants — those who have voluntarily joined an organisation engaged in hostilities against a state — may be killed. Not arrested, not tried, not sentenced: killed. This is what soldiers do. This is what the laws of war have always permitted. The challenge posed by non-state terrorist actors is that they fight without uniforms, without rank, without the visible markers that distinguish combatants from civilians on a conventional battlefield. But the absence of those markers does not change their status as parties to a conflict. It changes the epistemic difficulty of identifying them — which is a real problem, and one I take seriously — but it does not transform the moral framework from armed conflict to law enforcement. (Ch. 2, §2)

II. The status of terrorists as unlawful combatants

Unlawful combatants: targetable as combatants but forfeiting POW protections by fighting outside the laws of warWhere I want to be precise — because Professor Waldron will press me on this — is on the category I call the unlawful or unprivileged combatant. Following Michael Walzer and the established tradition of just war theory, I begin with the observation that all persons begin with immunity from lethal attack. The question is always how that immunity is lost. My answer is: by voluntarily joining an organisation engaged in armed hostilities, and by choosing to fight in ways that deliberately violate the distinction between combatants and civilians — by hiding among civilians, using civilian infrastructure as cover, targeting non-combatants as a matter of deliberate policy. Such fighters are not entitled to the protections accorded to lawful combatants — to prisoner-of-war status, to trial before execution — because they have forfeited those protections by fighting outside the laws of war. But they are targetable as combatants. They may be killed in the prosecution of the armed conflict. And targeting them specifically — naming them, finding them, killing them — is not assassination in the morally or legally prohibited sense. It is the conduct of war. (Ch. 2, §§3–5)

III. Named killing is more discriminating, not less — the moral advantage of targeting

Named killing as more morally precise than area bombardment: identifying the combatant reduces civilian harmHere is the point that I think Professor Waldron's framing of the issue as inherently troubling most obscures: targeted killing, precisely because it involves the careful identification of a named individual before any lethal force is used, is actually the most discriminating form of lethal force available in asymmetric warfare. Compare the alternatives. Area bombardment — the traditional instrument of war against an enemy operating among a civilian population — kills everyone in the target zone, combatant and non-combatant alike. The targeted killing of a known terrorist commander kills him specifically, having made at least a serious attempt to establish that he is who the intelligence says he is and that he is doing what the intelligence says he is doing. The moral architecture of targeted killing — identifying the individual, establishing his combatant status, confirming his continuing involvement in hostile operations — is precisely the architecture of discrimination that just war theory demands. The fact that the killing is named and premeditated, which disturbs Professor Waldron, is from the perspective of discrimination a virtue, not a vice. (Ch. 2, §5)

IV. The due process objection: why the criminal law model does not apply

Due process in wartime: the criminal law model cannot be transposed to armed conflict — combatants may be killed without trialProfessor Waldron will argue — and he does argue — that targeted killing violates the right to due process: that even a known terrorist is entitled to be charged, tried, and convicted before being killed. I want to be direct about why this argument, for all its appeal to liberal instincts, is mistaken in the context of armed conflict. No soldier on a conventional battlefield is required to arrest the enemy combatant before shooting him. No pilot is required to read a fighter pilot on the other side his Miranda rights before engaging him in combat. The legal framework of armed conflict does not require that combatants be convicted before they are killed. What it requires is that they be combatants — that they have lost the immunity from lethal attack that civilians retain — and that killing them is consistent with the principles of military necessity, distinction, and proportionality. Targeted killing that satisfies these criteria does not violate due process. It applies the correct legal and moral framework: armed conflict, not criminal prosecution. Due process requirements pertain to individuals who remain within the civilian framework. Combatants in an ongoing armed conflict are not in that framework. (Ch. 2, §6)

V. The location argument: sovereignty and pursuing combatants across borders

Extraterritorial targeted killing: states may pursue hostile combatants into ungoverned territory or failed statesA further objection to targeted killing concerns location: that conducting lethal operations in foreign states violates their sovereignty and constitutes an illegal use of force in international law. I want to address this carefully. The objection has real force where the host state is capable and willing to apprehend or neutralise the threat itself. Where a government can and will act, unilateral action by the targeted state is hard to justify. But much of the targeted killing that is actually conducted occurs in zones where the host government cannot control its own territory — ungoverned spaces in Yemen, Somalia, the tribal regions of Pakistan, the lawless periphery of failed states. When a state cannot or will not suppress a belligerent force operating from its territory, the targeted state has a recognised right in international law to take necessary and proportionate action to neutralise the threat. The violation of sovereignty in these cases is against the sovereignty of a state that has already been compromised by the presence of a hostile armed group. This does not make targeted killing across borders unconditionally permissible. But it does mean the sovereignty argument is not the categorical prohibition that critics claim. (Ch. 2, §3)

VI. Drones as a more humane instrument of discriminate warfare

Drones as precision instruments: higher discrimination, lower collateral damage than conventional alternativesThe technological dimension of targeted killing — the use of drone platforms for remote surveillance and precision strike — has become the focus of popular outrage that I think conflates the technology with the policy. I want to make a straightforward and evidence-based point: drone warfare, as a technology of targeted killing, is more discriminating than any previous instrument of asymmetric warfare. A drone can loiter over a target for hours, confirming identity and establishing pattern-of-life before any strike is authorised. Its operators can observe the target's surroundings and delay or abort if civilians are present. The missile it delivers can be adjusted in flight. No previous instrument of counterinsurgency has offered anything like this combination of precision and real-time human judgement. The appropriate comparison is not drones versus arrest-and-trial. It is drones versus the alternatives that were previously available in similar operational contexts: area bombardment, large ground force operations, or simply allowing the threat to persist. Against these alternatives, drone-enabled targeted killing produces lower collateral damage, lower friendly casualties, and greater discrimination between combatants and civilians. (Ch. 2, §11)

VII. The assassination of enemy scientists: a further justified extension

Targeting nuclear scientists: extreme threats to civilians may justify assassination outside formal armed conflictI want to address one of the more controversial extensions of the targeted killing argument — one I develop in Part III of my chapter — which concerns the assassination of scientists developing weapons of mass destruction for hostile regimes. The cases I have in mind are real: the killing, attributed to Israel, of Iranian and Syrian nuclear scientists and engineers working to develop nuclear or chemical weapons programmes that, if completed, would pose existential threats to civilian populations. These cases sit at the edge of the framework I have been developing: they may not occur in the context of a recognised armed conflict, and the targets are arguably civilians rather than combatants in the traditional sense. But I argue that the threshold for justified killing in the face of imminent, catastrophic, irreversible harm to civilian populations — the kind of threat a nuclear weapon in hostile hands represents — is met in such extreme cases. This is a narrow exception, carefully circumscribed by the nature of the threat and the absence of alternatives. But I do not think we can pretend the category of justified killing stops absolutely at the edge of the formal armed conflict framework when the stakes are existential. (Ch. 2, §§III.1–3)

VIII. In reply to Waldron: the death-list framing is a rhetorical distortion

Reply to Waldron's death-list framing: targeting lists are intelligence products within a lawful process, not hit listsProfessor Waldron argues that what is distinctive and troubling about targeted killing is the maintenance by governments of "death lists" — lists of named individuals to be hunted down and killed — and that this practice is characteristically associated with state terror and death squads rather than with lawful warfare. I find this framing rhetorically powerful but philosophically misleading. Every legitimate military operation involves identifying targets before engaging them. The only difference between a targeting list and the intelligence preparation that precedes any conventional military strike is the granularity and persistence of the identification. Calling the result a "death list" rather than a "target package" is a choice of vocabulary that loads the conclusion into the description. What we need to ask is not whether names are maintained on a list — of course they are, in any intelligence-driven operation — but whether the process by which names are placed on the list, assessed, and acted upon satisfies the requirements of lawful armed conflict. When it does, the list is a legitimate instrument of discriminate warfare. When it does not, the problem is with the process, not with the existence of individual targeting as such. (Ch. 4)

IX. In reply to Waldron: the slippery slope argument proves too much

Reply to Waldron's slippery slope: restricting justified targeted killing does not require prohibiting all forms of itProfessor Waldron's most powerful argument is not about any particular targeted killing but about what a world of generalised targeted killing would look like — a world in which every state maintains lists of its enemies and hunts them across borders with impunity. I take this concern entirely seriously. The potential for abuse of targeted killing authority is real, and the historical record of states abusing lethal authority against political opponents — rather than genuine security threats — is well-documented. But the appropriate response to the abuse potential of a practice is not its categorical prohibition. It is the development of the oversight mechanisms, procedural constraints, and accountability structures that distinguish legitimate from illegitimate instances. Targeted killing under appropriate legal constraints, judicial or quasi-judicial review, and meaningful oversight — such as the arrangements imperfectly attempted by the Obama administration's Presidential Policy Guidance — is a different practice from targeted killing as unconstrained executive whim. Waldron's slippery slope argument proves too much: by the same logic, we could not permit states to maintain armed forces at all, since armed forces, unrestrained, are also instruments of potential atrocity. The remedy is restraint and oversight, not prohibition. (Ch. 4)

Logical Arguments

Professor Meisels,

I want to begin, as you did, with a clarification of what I am and am not arguing. I am not a pacifist. I do not claim that lethal force is never permissible in the defence of a population against terrorist threat. I think the killing of Osama bin Laden — head of an organisation in a state of ongoing armed conflict with the United States — was probably justified, considered on its own terms. What I argue is that the practice of targeted killing, as it has developed and as it is likely to continue developing, is something we should be deeply and specifically troubled by — and that the philosophical frameworks we have been offered to legitimise it are seriously deficient in ways that Professor Meisels's careful argument, despite its many merits, has not resolved.

My concern is not primarily with any individual targeted killing. It is with targeted killing as a systematic, institutionalised practice — with the standing authority that governments claim to maintain lists of named individuals to be hunted down and killed. And I want to show why that practice is different in morally important ways from ordinary combat, why the frameworks offered to legitimise it are inadequate, and what the world looks like if we accept it without the caution it deserves.

I. The distinctiveness of named killing: individualised targeting is not ordinary combat

Named killing differs fundamentally from ordinary combat: the deliberateness of the individual identification changes the moral character of the actProfessor Meisels argues that targeted killing is simply a more discriminating form of ordinary combat. I want to resist this assimilation. There is a morally significant difference between killing that occurs in the heat of battle — where the combatant kills the enemy combatant who is present and threatening — and the deliberate, planned, persistent hunting of a named individual across time and geography. In ordinary combat, the soldier kills the soldier in front of him. In targeted killing, a government maintains a list of names, an intelligence apparatus tracks those names across the globe, and a killing is authorised at a moment chosen by the state rather than dictated by the pressure of battle. The deliberateness, the persistence, and the individualised character of this killing are not incidental. They change its nature. They make it closer, morally, to execution than to combat — to an act of personalised lethal authority of a kind that liberal legal traditions have always regarded with the greatest suspicion. (Ch. 3)

II. The death-list problem: governments maintaining lists of those to be killed

Death lists: the authority to maintain and act on a list of named persons to be killed is inherently dangerous regardless of who holds itThe specific practice I want to draw attention to is the maintenance, by the executive branch of government, of a list of named individuals who are to be hunted down and killed. Professor Meisels says I am loading the conclusion into the vocabulary by calling this a "death list." But consider what the thing actually is. It is a list maintained by a branch of government, without judicial involvement, containing the names of people who will be killed as a consequence of being on it. The addition of names to the list is an executive determination, made on the basis of intelligence that may be wrong, that may be influenced by political considerations, and that is subject to no independent review. The individual named on the list has no opportunity to contest the determination before it is acted upon. He does not know he is on the list. He cannot challenge the intelligence. He cannot present exculpatory evidence. He will simply, at a moment of the government's choosing, be killed. This is not something we should be comfortable calling "discriminating warfare." It is something we should be uncomfortable calling anything, because the discomfort is precisely the point. (Ch. 3)

III. The epistemic problem: intelligence failures kill the wrong people

The epistemic problem: targeted killing acts on intelligence that is frequently wrong, with lethal and irreversible consequencesProfessor Meisels tells us that targeted killing embeds discrimination in the targeting process — that the requirement to identify the individual before killing him represents a moral advance over less discriminating forms of warfare. But this argument presupposes that the identification is reliable. The historical record of targeted killing programmes is, to put it gently, not uniformly reassuring on this point. The drone strike programme in Pakistan and Yemen produced, in its early years, a pattern of misidentification that killed significant numbers of civilians who were not the intended targets. The "signature strikes" authorised by the Obama administration — strikes against persons whose pattern of behaviour matched that of a militant without their individual identity being confirmed — represent the collapse of the discriminatory ideal that Professor Meisels is defending into something much closer to area targeting by algorithmic profiling. The problem is not that discrimination is a bad ideal. It is that the intelligence processes on which targeted killing depends to realise that ideal are fallible in ways that produce lethal, irreversible, uncorrectable errors. (Ch. 3)

IV. The due process objection is not a confusion of frameworks

Due process in targeted killing: the individualised nature of the act generates due process requirements that ordinary combat does notProfessor Meisels dismisses the due process objection by insisting that armed conflict law, not criminal law, is the applicable framework. I want to push back. It is precisely because targeted killing is individualised — because it involves a government identifying a specific person and deciding to kill that specific person — that due process concerns arise that do not arise in ordinary combat. When a soldier kills the combatant in front of him, the circumstances make the individual identification immediate and contextually determined. When a government places a name on a list and pursues that individual across continents over months, the identification has been made deliberately, in advance, on the basis of intelligence that can be assessed, contested, and corrected — if there were any mechanism for doing so. The deliberately individualised character of targeted killing is precisely what generates due process requirements. You cannot invoke the individualised nature of the killing to claim it is more discriminating and simultaneously invoke the impersonal character of combat to avoid the accountability that individualised lethal decisions should require. (Ch. 3)

V. The unlawful combatant category is legally contested and dangerously elastic

The unlawful combatant category: legally unstable and liable to indefinite expansion by the stateProfessor Meisels relies heavily on the category of "unlawful" or "unprivileged" combatant to explain why terrorists may be killed without the protections ordinarily accorded to prisoners of war or criminal suspects. I have serious reservations about this category as she employs it. It is not a settled category in international humanitarian law. The Third Geneva Convention provides protections to persons who do not meet the criteria for lawful combatant status — protections that have been systematically undermined by the "unlawful combatant" designation as used in the war on terror. More fundamentally, the category is defined by the state conducting the targeting, on the basis of the state's own intelligence assessment, without independent determination. This means the category is as broad as the state chooses to make it. Yesterday it covered al-Qaeda leadership. Today it covers low-level ISIS affiliates. Tomorrow it may cover anyone whose pattern of association with a proscribed organisation satisfies an algorithm. The category does exactly what dangerous legal categories always do: it provides a principled-sounding justification for the exercise of unlimited state discretion. (Ch. 3)

VI. The world of generalised targeted killing: what the universalised practice produces

The universalisation problem: in a world where all states claim targeted killing authority, the practice systematically favours the powerful against the weakI want to consider what a world looks like in which the targeted killing authority claimed by the United States and Israel is accepted as a legitimate principle available to all states. We are asked to endorse, in effect, a principle under which any state may maintain a list of persons — its own citizens or foreigners — whose existence it regards as a sufficient security threat to justify lethal action, and may pursue those persons across borders with lethal force. Now imagine that principle in the hands of states with very different conceptions of "security threat" — authoritarian states that consider journalists, opposition politicians, and human rights lawyers to be security threats; states with territorial ambitions that consider the leaders of resistance movements to be legitimate targets; states with expansive definitions of terrorism that encompass all armed opposition. The principle Professor Meisels defends is not self-limiting. It does not come with a built-in restriction to cases that would satisfy her criteria. It comes with the elastic logic of the "unlawful combatant" category and the boundless appetite of governments for security authority. A world of generalised targeted killing is not a world of more discriminating warfare. It is a world of legalised assassination at scale. (Ch. 3)

VII. The character of the state: what maintaining death lists does to a government

Targeted killing and the character of the state: maintaining death squads and death lists corrupts the institutional character of governmentThere is a dimension of the targeted killing question that Professor Meisels does not adequately address: what the practice does to the state that conducts it, not just to the individuals targeted. Governments that develop the institutional infrastructure for targeted killing — intelligence units that track named individuals, special operations forces trained specifically for assassination, drone operators practised in remote lethal action — are developing capacities and institutional habits that do not confine themselves to the cases that justified their development. We have seen this clearly in the history of intelligence agencies: the CIA's assassination programmes of the 1970s, developed against foreign leaders, were not contained to foreign contexts. The institutional capacity, once developed, migrates. It is used for political purposes, for settling scores, for eliminating inconvenient people. Governments tend to lie reflexively when targeted killings come to light. This is not accidental. It is the behaviour of institutions that know the authority they are exercising is not comfortably defensible in public. The character of the state that routinely kills named individuals — even named enemies — is different from the character of the state that does not. That difference matters. (Ch. 3, "Death Squads and Death Lists")

VIII. In reply to Meisels: the oversight solution is not available in the form she requires

Reply to Meisels on oversight: the oversight mechanisms she proposes are either unavailable or inadequateProfessor Meisels argues that the response to the abuse potential of targeted killing is better oversight — judicial review, transparency, accountability structures. I want to accept the aspiration and question the substance. The targeting process, as it actually operates, involves intelligence assessments that cannot be shared publicly without compromising sources and methods, legal determinations made by executive lawyers without independent review, and killing decisions authorised by officials who are never subsequently held to account for mistaken identifications. The Obama administration's much-praised Presidential Policy Guidance — the closest the United States has come to formalising oversight — was itself a unilateral executive document, not subject to congressional approval or judicial review, and was abandoned almost immediately by the succeeding administration. The oversight she invokes exists largely as a promise rather than a reality, and there is a structural reason for this: genuinely independent oversight of covert lethal operations is almost impossible to design in a way that is both effective and consistent with the operational security requirements of the programmes. The call for oversight is right in principle. The suggestion that it resolves the problem is too optimistic about both the design of institutions and the behaviour of governments under pressure. (Ch. 5)

IX. In reply to Meisels: the boundary between war and peace is being deliberately dissolved

Reply on the armed conflict framework: targeted killing deliberately dissolves the war/peace distinction in ways that are permanent and self-expandingProfessor Meisels's argument depends throughout on the armed conflict framework. If we are in a state of armed conflict with al-Qaeda, the laws of war apply, combatants may be killed, and due process requirements do not attach. But I want to identify what this framework costs us, beyond any particular targeted killing it authorises. The "war on terror" framework — the claim that we are in a state of ongoing armed conflict with a non-state, geographically diffuse, ideologically reproducing enemy — is a framework with no inherent geographical or temporal limits. It authorises lethal action anywhere a member of a proscribed organisation can be found, for as long as the organisation exists. It has been used to authorise strikes in Yemen, Somalia, the Philippines, and Mali — countries with which the United States is not formally at war. It has been used to kill an American citizen, Anwar al-Awlaki, without charge or trial, on the basis of a classified legal memorandum. The claim that we are in a permanent, globalised armed conflict is not a neutral description of a pre-existing state of affairs. It is a political-legal construction, made by governments for governments, that converts the exception into the rule and the temporary into the permanent. The appropriate caution is not about any particular targeted killing but about the framework within which they all occur — a framework that Professor Meisels defends but whose expansion she does not, and perhaps cannot, adequately control. (Ch. 5)

∴The individualised, deliberate character of targeted killing generates due process requirements that cannot be discharged by invoking the armed conflict framework; the logic of precision implies the logic of accountability.
Argument V — The unlawful combatant category is contested and dangerously elasticclick for image
1.The "unlawful combatant" category is not settled in international humanitarian law and has been applied to undermine Geneva Convention protections in ways international law scholars have broadly criticised.
2.The category is defined by the targeting state on the basis of its own intelligence assessment, without independent determination — meaning it is as broad as the state chooses to make it. (Ch. 3)
3.Dangerous legal categories defined by those who benefit from their breadth have a documented tendency toward expansion: from al-Qaeda leaders to low-level affiliates to "signature strike" behavioural profiles.
∴The unlawful combatant category provides a principled-sounding justification for the exercise of unlimited state discretion in targeting; its elasticity is not a defect to be remedied but a structural feature of the way it operates.
Argument VI — Universalised targeted killing authority produces a world of legalised assassinationclick for image
1.The targeted killing principle, if accepted, is available to all states — including authoritarian states that define "security threat" to include journalists, opposition leaders, and human rights lawyers. (Ch. 3)
2.Meisels's criteria for legitimate targeted killing — armed conflict context, unlawful combatant status, IHL compliance — do not constrain states that simply assert their satisfied; the principle has no built-in self-limitation.
3.A world in which every state may maintain lists of named persons to be killed wherever they are found is not a world of more discriminating warfare but of institutionalised, globalised, state-sanctioned assassination.
∴The universalisation of the targeted killing principle produces consequences that dwarf the benefits of any particular application; the world it creates is more dangerous than the one it replaces.
Argument VII — Targeted killing corrupts the institutional character of the stateclick for image
1.Institutional capacities developed for targeted killing — intelligence tracking units, special operations assassination teams, remote drone operators — do not confine themselves to the cases that justified their development; they migrate to other uses. (Ch. 3, "Death Squads and Death Lists")
2.The CIA's assassination programmes, developed against foreign leaders in the 1970s, were not contained to foreign contexts; the institutional habit of state killing, once established, seeks new applications.
3.Governments that reflexively lie about their targeted killing programmes — as both US and UK governments have documented histories of doing — are not behaving like institutions confident in the legitimacy of what they are doing.
∴Targeted killing changes the character of the state that conducts it in ways that extend beyond any individual killing; the institutional corruption it produces is a cost that must be weighed against its tactical benefits.
Argument VIII — The oversight solution is structurally unavailableclick for image
1.Genuine independent oversight of covert lethal operations is almost impossible to design in a way that is both effective and consistent with operational security; classified targeting decisions cannot be subjected to the public scrutiny that gives oversight its force. (Ch. 5)
2.The Obama Presidential Policy Guidance — the most developed attempt at oversight — was a unilateral executive document, not subject to legislative or judicial approval, and was abandoned immediately by the succeeding administration.
3.Oversight that exists only at the executive's pleasure, that can be abandoned without constitutional process, and that cannot be enforced by any external body is not oversight in the relevant sense.
∴The oversight solution Meisels invokes exists largely as an aspiration rather than a reality; the structural features of covert lethal programmes make genuinely independent oversight nearly impossible to realise.
Argument IX — The armed conflict framework is self-expanding and geographically unlimitedclick for image
1.The "global war on terror" armed conflict framework has no inherent geographical or temporal limits; it authorises lethal action wherever a proscribed organisation's member can be found, for as long as the organisation exists.
2.It has been used to authorise strikes in Yemen, Somalia, the Philippines, and Mali — states with which the US is not formally at war — and to kill an American citizen without charge or trial. (Ch. 5)
3.This framework is not a neutral description of pre-existing hostilities but a political-legal construction made by governments for governments, converting the temporary exception into permanent institutional practice.
∴The armed conflict framework on which Meisels's entire argument depends is itself constructed and expansive; defending targeted killing within it accepts and legitimises an architecture of permanent, globalised, legally unconstrained lethal authority.
