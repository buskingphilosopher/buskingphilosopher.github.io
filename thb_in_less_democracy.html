Logical Arguments

Chair,

I want to make it clear at the outset what I am not arguing. I am not arguing that democracy is bad. I am not arguing for authoritarianism, dictatorship, or the abolition of elections. I am arguing something far more precise and, I believe, far more defensible: that democracy, like any powerful medicine, has an optimal dose — and that the richest nations on earth have, in their enthusiasm for the thing, slightly exceeded it.

Think of it as a Laffer curve not for taxation but for democracy itself. At zero democracy you get tyranny: corrupt, extractive, and economically ruinous. As you add democracy the outcomes improve dramatically. But the curve is not linear. It bends. Past a certain point, additional direct accountability to the voting public begins to make governance worse, not better. The question I pose is not whether we want democracy — of course we do — but whether we have correctly identified that optimal point on the curve. My argument is that we have overshot it, modestly, and that the correction is modest too.

I. Election proximity makes politicians worse — the core empirical finding

Shorter terms → worse policy: the election-proximity evidenceI spent time as a staffer in the United States Senate, and what I observed there is confirmed by the empirical literature: politicians behave differently as elections approach. The closer an election, the more a legislator's votes cluster around what is popular rather than what is prudent. This is not a speculation about human nature in the abstract. It is a documented empirical pattern. Studies of fiscal policy show that governments systematically increase spending and cut taxes in election years — the political business cycle — producing deficits, debt, and the deferral of necessary but painful adjustments. The implication is direct: if we want better policy, we should lengthen the distance between politicians and their next electoral reckoning. Longer terms — moving the United States House of Representatives from two-year to four- or six-year terms — would give legislators more room to make difficult decisions without immediate electoral punishment. The six-year Senate term already produces measurably different behaviour from the two-year House term. The evidence is there. We are simply choosing not to read it.

II. Central bank independence: the paradigm case for technocracy

Independent central banks: the gold-standard proof of technocracy's benefitsIf there is one institution that proves my case beyond reasonable doubt, it is the independent central bank. For decades, economists argued that monetary policy should be insulated from electoral pressure, and for decades, politicians resisted. The argument was simple: voters like low interest rates and they dislike unemployment more than they dislike inflation; therefore democratic politicians will always err on the side of loose money, generating the inflation-unemployment cycle that characterised the 1970s. Give the central bank independence — appoint its governors on long terms, insulate them from daily political pressure, let them make unpopular decisions — and you get lower and more stable inflation without sacrificing long-run growth. The empirical consensus on this is about as close to settled as economics gets. The countries that pursued central bank independence in the 1980s and 1990s got dramatically better monetary outcomes than those that did not. This is not a theoretical prediction. It is a historical fact. And if the principle works for monetary policy — if keeping the money supply out of democratic hands improves outcomes — then we should at least ask whether the principle generalises. I believe it does.

III. Appointed judges and regulators outperform elected ones

Appointed versus elected judiciary: the performance gapThe United States is unusual among advanced democracies in electing many of its judges. Most mature constitutional democracies appoint their judiciaries, and the evidence strongly suggests this produces better justice. Studies of elected versus appointed state court judges in America show that elected judges are more likely to decide cases in ways that track public sentiment — harsher sentencing before elections, softer after — rather than in ways that track legal principle. The same pattern holds for elected city treasurers versus appointed ones: elected treasurers produce measurably worse financial outcomes for their municipalities, apparently because the skills required to win an election and the skills required to manage a municipal treasury are different skills, and optimising for one impedes the development of the other. The deeper point is this: we do not elect surgeons, air traffic controllers, or actuaries. We recognise that some functions require specialised knowledge that the median voter cannot assess and that electoral accountability is therefore not the right mechanism for them. The question is why we draw the line where we do, and whether we have drawn it in the right place.

IV. Voter quality shapes national outcomes — the cognitive skills evidence

Voter cognitive skills and national outcomes: the IQ-policy linkMy previous book, Hive Mind, assembled the evidence that a nation's average cognitive skill level predicts an enormous range of economic and political outcomes — levels of trust, institutional quality, long-run growth, democratic stability. The point for present purposes is that voters differ enormously in the information and analytical capacity they bring to political decisions, and these differences have consequences. Bryan Caplan's work on the myth of the rational voter documents systematic biases in what the median voter believes about economics — anti-market bias, anti-foreign bias, make-work bias, pessimism bias — biases that lead, when aggregated through the electoral mechanism, to systematically bad economic policy. Well-informed voters correct for these biases. The suggestion I make — carefully and with full acknowledgment of its political difficulties — is that we might consider giving additional weight to the votes of more-educated citizens. Several existing democracies do something like this: Ireland reserves seats in its upper house for graduates of elite universities; the franchise has historically been restricted in ways that tracked, however imperfectly, judgments about civic competence. I am not proposing disenfranchisement. I am proposing that we take seriously the evidence that voter quality matters and think carefully about what follows from that.

V. Bondholders as constitutional constraint: skin in the game

Bondholders as farsighted stakeholders with real disciplinary powerOne of the more unconventional proposals in my book is to formalise the role of sovereign bondholders in the legislative process — to give them, in some form, explicit representation in the upper chamber. The intuition is this. Bondholders are creditors of the state. When the state pursues fiscally irresponsible policies — running structural deficits, building unsustainable debt, inflating away its obligations — bondholders lose money. They are, uniquely among all political actors, directly and financially penalised by fiscal short-termism. They therefore have the strongest incentive of any constituency to push for long-term fiscal prudence. Financial markets already exercise this function informally: when a government's bond yields spike, it is the market sending a disciplinary signal about the sustainability of its policies. The question is whether we should formalise this mechanism — giving these farsighted, financially-at-risk stakeholders a formal seat at the table — rather than leaving it to the informal and often-ignored pressure of yield movements. My answer is yes. It is a way of injecting long-term thinking into a process that is structurally biased toward the short term.

VI. Political machines and transactional politics: the case for insulation

Political machines and earmarks: insulating deal-making from populist backlashHere I want to defend something that my opponents will find counterintuitive: the political machine, and its instrument the earmark. We have spent decades treating political machines and vote-trading as forms of corruption to be stamped out in the name of clean, transparent, responsive democracy. The effect has been to make governance worse, not better. Political machines — the organised networks of relationships and reciprocal obligations that structured mid-twentieth century American politics — served a crucial function: they insulated legislators from immediate electoral pressure and created space for bargaining and deal-making across party lines. The earmark, for all its bad reputation, was the currency of that system. It allowed legislators to trade votes on contentious legislation, building the coalitions that complex governance requires. As earmarks have been eliminated and machines dismantled in the name of transparency and responsiveness to voters, governance has become less about policy and more about performance — more about what plays well on social media and less about what actually works. Jonathan Rauch's work on political realism makes this point compellingly, and I endorse it entirely. Sometimes the right answer is less sunshine, not more.

VII. The case studies: the EU and Singapore

EU and Singapore: technocracy's track record at scaleI close with two case studies. The European Union is routinely criticised as a democracy deficit — an unaccountable technocracy imposed on sovereign peoples. But look at what the EU's explicitly technocratic institutions have actually delivered. The European Central Bank — whose independence from elected governments is constitutional — produced monetary stability across a diverse currency union for decades. The appointed European Commission, staffed by career technocrats, produced a genuinely competitive single market. The failures of EU governance — the incoherent response to the 2015 refugee crisis, the chaotic management of the Greek debt crisis — are in significant part failures of the EU's democratic elements: the Council, where national politicians respond to domestic electoral pressures rather than European common interest. Then there is Singapore. I describe Singapore as fifty percent less democracy rather than ten, and I am not recommending Singapore as a model. But Singapore's per capita GDP places it among the wealthiest societies on earth, its governance indicators are excellent, its long-term policy orientation is exceptional, and it has lifted its population from genuine poverty in a single generation. The Singaporean case demonstrates what is possible when a government is insulated enough from short-term electoral pressure to make and sustain genuinely long-term decisions. The lesson is not "copy Singapore." The lesson is that electoral accountability is one mechanism for producing good governance among several, and it is not always the dominant one.

The modesty of the proposal

The 10% framing: incrementalism, not revolutionI want to close by emphasising the modesty of what I am proposing. I am not arguing for the abolition of elections or the installation of a technocratic junta. I am arguing for longer terms, more independent central banks, appointed rather than elected judges, some additional weight for informed voters, a formalised role for bondholders, and the rehabilitation of political deal-making. Each of these proposals is either already in place in some advanced democracy or has been seriously proposed by mainstream political scientists. The title of my book — Ten Percent Less Democracy — is deliberately calibrated. It is enough to make a difference. It is not enough to make a dictatorship. The question is not whether elites should have any insulation from popular pressure. Some insulation already exists everywhere — in central banks, in appointed judiciaries, in tenured civil services. The question is whether the current calibration is correct. I believe it is slightly off. I believe ten percent in the direction of more elite discretion and longer time horizons would make government measurably better. Not perfect. Better.

Thank you.

Opposition
The Case Against
Responding to Jones's arguments in full
Speech
Logical Arguments

Chair,

Professor Jones is a careful and intellectually honest scholar, and his book deserves to be taken seriously. I intend to take it seriously. But taking it seriously means following the arguments through to places where he does not follow them himself — noticing what the evidence actually establishes, distinguishing claims that are modest and defensible from claims that are much more radical than the title suggests, and asking who, exactly, benefits from each of the reforms he proposes and who bears their costs.

Let me begin with what I regard as the central methodological problem of the whole enterprise, and then work through his specific claims.

The Laffer curve for democracy: a seductive but inapt analogy

The Laffer-curve analogy: where it misleadsJones opens with the analogy of a Laffer curve for democracy: a relationship between democratic intensity and governance quality that peaks somewhere in the middle, implying that most rich democracies have overshot the optimum. The analogy is rhetorically elegant, but it does a great deal of work that the subsequent evidence does not actually support. The Laffer curve has a specific shape for a specific reason — the revenue implications of zero and hundred-percent tax rates are analytically derivable. The "democracy Laffer curve" has no such analytical foundation. Whether governance quality peaks at forty percent democracy or ninety percent democracy, and where any given country sits on that curve, are entirely empirical questions. Jones's book provides scattered evidence that some specific democratic features produce some specific suboptimal outcomes. It does not establish the shape of the overall curve, it does not locate actual countries on it, and it therefore does not establish that those countries have overshot rather than undershot. The master framing of the book is unverified by its own evidence.

On longer terms: the evidence is thinner than presented

Election-proximity evidence: confounders, reverse causation, and selection effectsJones argues that politicians behave worse as elections approach, and that longer terms would fix this. The observation that electoral cycles correlate with fiscal loosening is real. But the inference that longer terms are the cure is substantially less well-supported than the diagnosis. First: the pre-electoral fiscal expansion Jones documents is largely a phenomenon of presidential and majoritarian systems with underdeveloped checks. Countries with strong independent fiscal institutions — independent budget offices, constitutional debt brakes, technocratic treasuries — show much weaker electoral business cycles even with short terms. The problem may be the absence of constraints on fiscal discretion, not the frequency of elections. Second: longer terms create their own accountability vacuum. A government with a six-year mandate has six years to pursue policies its electorate opposes before facing a reckoning. The abuse potential of insulation is not symmetric with its benefit. Jones's evidence shows that proximity to elections changes behaviour; it does not show that the changed behaviour is, on balance, worse than the behaviour insulation would produce.

On central bank independence: a special case, not a general principle

Central bank independence: why it doesn't generalise the way Jones claimsThe evidence for central bank independence improving monetary outcomes is genuine and I do not contest it. But Jones uses it as the paradigm case for a general principle — that insulating governmental functions from electoral accountability tends to produce better outcomes — and this generalisation does not hold. Central bank independence works for a highly specific set of reasons: monetary policy has a single, measurable, socially agreed objective (price stability); monetary experts have genuine technical expertise that the public lacks; the time lags between monetary decisions and their effects are well-understood; and there is a clear mechanism — inflation — by which failures become visible and correctable. None of these features is present in most domains of governance. Foreign policy, welfare, immigration, industrial strategy, climate — these are value-laden, multi-dimensional, contested questions where there is no technocratic consensus to defer to and no expert corps whose authority is unambiguous. The inference from "monetary technocracy works" to "technocracy works, generally" crosses a gulf that Jones never adequately bridges.

On appointed judges: selection effects and accountability capture

Appointed judiciary: the accountability-capture problem Jones underweightsJones's evidence that appointed judges outperform elected ones is real in the limited domains he examines. But the alternative to electoral accountability for judges is not accountability-free appointment — it is appointment by some other political process, typically by executives and legislators who are themselves electorally accountable. The question is not "elections versus merit" but "which accountability mechanism best approximates merit selection while preserving responsiveness to the governed." Appointed judiciaries are vulnerable to a distinct pathology: capture by the appointing authority. The Supreme Court of the United States is appointed, not elected, and it has become one of the most intensely politicised institutions in American public life — because the appointment process is itself a site of intense electoral politics. The British judiciary is appointed and genuinely insulated, but it operates within a constitutional tradition very different from the American one and cannot straightforwardly be transplanted. Jones's comparison — elected versus appointed — conflates the appointment mechanism with the degree of political capture, which are separate variables.

On voter quality and weighted suffrage: where the argument becomes dangerous

Weighted suffrage: the insuperable problems of epistocracyThis is the part of Jones's book that matters most and that he treats most gingerly. He proposes, carefully and with many caveats, that more-educated voters should receive additional electoral weight. The history of this idea is not encouraging. Plural voting — giving educated or propertied citizens more votes — was a live proposal in Victorian political theory, associated with John Stuart Mill, and it was abandoned not because theorists changed their minds about the merits but because the practical and moral problems proved insuperable. Who decides what counts as informed? In any system where educational credentials confer additional electoral weight, those who design the credential system have enormous power over political outcomes — and those designers are not themselves neutrally technocratic. Educational attainment in all societies is heavily correlated with class, race, and inherited advantage. A weighted suffrage system would systematically amplify the political voice of the already-privileged and attenuate the voice of those whose lives are most directly affected by the policies in question. The claim that educated voters have less biased political views deserves scrutiny too: educated voters have different biases, not fewer — they are more likely to support policies that benefit the professional class, underweight the concerns of manual workers, and systematically underestimate the political salience of inequality. "Less biased" here means "shares more of my priors," and this is not a stable foundation for disenfranchisement.

On bondholders as a constitutional branch: cui bono?

Bondholder representation: the most radical proposal, dressed as prudenceOf all Jones's proposals, giving sovereign bondholders formal legislative representation is the most radical, and it is the one whose distributional consequences he most conspicuously fails to examine. Bondholders are not a randomly selected group of farseeing stakeholders. They are, overwhelmingly, wealthy. They are financial institutions, pension funds, and high-net-worth individuals whose primary interest in fiscal policy is the protection of the real value of their claims. Their preference for "fiscal prudence" is not neutral good governance — it is a preference for policies that prioritise debt service over public services, that restrain government spending even when spending is the appropriate response to a recession, and that treat the interests of creditors as lexically prior to the interests of citizens who depend on the state for healthcare, education, and social insurance. The bond markets' "disciplinary signal" that Jones celebrates has, in practice, meant austerity imposed on populations that voted against it — Greece, Italy, Spain — in the service of creditor interests. Calling this mechanism a constitutional check on democratic excess is a political choice dressed as institutional design.

On political machines: whose transactions, whose deals?

Political machines and earmarks: the corruption Jones rehabilitates is realJones's defence of political machines and earmarks as functional deal-making mechanisms has genuine insight. The decline of transactional politics has coincided with legislative dysfunction, and the connection is not purely coincidental. But the rehabilitation of the machine glosses over what political machines actually were and who they served. American political machines were engines of ethnic patronage, systematic vote-buying, and the distribution of public resources to private interests in exchange for political support. They insulated politicians from voter accountability, yes — but the insulation served the machine's internal interests, not some notion of enlightened governance. The argument that we should accept some corruption in exchange for legislative functionality is not obviously wrong, but it requires a much more careful accounting of whose functional deals are being enabled and at whose expense. Earmarks were famously vehicles for pork-barrel spending that benefited narrow constituencies at national cost. The claim that their elimination made governance worse needs to contend with the possibility that what improved was the visibility of the corruption, not the correction of its effects.

On the EU and Singapore: cherry-picked success, ignored failure

The case studies: survivorship bias and non-comparable baselinesJones's two case studies — the EU and Singapore — are subject to significant selection bias. The EU is cited for its technocratic successes (the ECB, the single market) while its democratic failures are attributed to its democratic elements. But the 2010–2015 Eurozone crisis — in which the ECB and European Commission imposed austerity on elected governments, overriding the expressed democratic preferences of Greek, Italian and Spanish populations — is precisely the kind of technocratic governance Jones celebrates. The political delegitimisation of the EU that followed, including Brexit, is in significant part a democratic backlash against precisely this technocratic overreach. Singapore is even more problematic as a model. Singapore's economic success is correlated with, but not obviously caused by, its democratic deficit. City-states with homogeneous populations, deep ports, and strategic geographic position have historically done well across a wide range of governance structures. Lee Kuan Yew was not wrong that Singapore needed discipline in 1965. The question is whether the absence of opposition parties, restrictions on press freedom, and the criminalisation of political dissent are necessary conditions of Singapore's success or incidental features of its history. Jones does not establish this, and the inference from Singapore's case to a general endorsement of technocracy is correspondingly weak.

The question Jones does not ask

The deeper objection: democracy's value is not only instrumentalThere is a question running beneath Jones's entire project that he never directly addresses: what is democracy for? Jones treats democracy instrumentally throughout — as a mechanism for aggregating preferences and producing good policy. On this framing, the case for democracy is purely a case about outcomes, and wherever another mechanism produces better outcomes, that mechanism should be preferred. But democracy has a non-instrumental dimension that this framing cannot accommodate. Democracy is a form of political self-determination — the principle that the people governed by a set of laws are the authors of those laws. This is not valuable merely because it produces good laws. It is valuable as an expression of political equality: the claim that each person's interests and judgements count equally in determining the terms of political life. When Jones proposes to weight the votes of the educated more heavily, to give bondholders a legislative chamber, or to insulate more decisions from electoral pressure, he is not merely adjusting a policy instrument. He is revising the answer to the question "whose judgement counts in governing this society?" And he is revising it in a direction that systematically favours those who are already advantaged. The strongest objection to ten percent less democracy is not that his specific proposals would produce worse outcomes — some of them, in some contexts, might not. The strongest objection is that the framework within which he evaluates them treats the most fundamental question of political philosophy as a minor engineering problem.

Thank you.
