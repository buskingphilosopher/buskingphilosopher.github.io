Mr President,

I want to introduce this debate with a case that is, I think, much simpler than the sophisticated theoretical apparatus that will follow. I want to draw attention to something that I believe most people, when they reflect carefully, actually agree with — even though they immediately try to argue themselves out of it.

The drug case

I have a supply of a life-saving drug. David needs all of it to survive. Five other people each need one-fifth. I cannot save all six. What should I do?

The standard answer is: save the five. More lives saved. But I want to press a question before we accept that answer. Suppose David is a close friend of mine. I give the drug to David. Have I done something morally wrong? To many people, the answer is clearly no — I may have a special reason to help a friend, and there is nothing obviously wrong about acting on it. But if that is right, it follows that there is no strict moral requirement to save the five. The obligation to save the greater number cannot be what compels me.

On what it would mean to say five deaths are worse than one

No impersonal standpoint for summing lossesConsider the claim that it would be worse if five people died than if one person died. Worse for whom? For each of the five, of course — but each of the five faces the same loss as David, their own death, and David's death is no less terrible from David's perspective because four others happen also to be dying. From David's perspective, he loses everything. From each of the five's perspective, each loses everything. There is no impersonal standpoint from which these losses are added up into a sum five times greater than David's loss. No one experiences five deaths. Each person experiences their own. The arithmetic of lives saved is not a moral arithmetic of welfare summed across persons.

On the claim that numbers constitute a reason

What does a number of persons add to a claim?It is said that the five have a stronger claim because there are more of them. But what exactly does the number add to the claim of any individual among the five? Each individual among the five has a claim grounded in her own life, her own interests, her own welfare. Her claim is not stronger because four other people happen to be in the same boat. Consider: if I tell person A that I will save her rather than David because there happens to be a person B standing next to her — a person who has her own separate claim — what exactly have I communicated to A? That her claim by itself was not sufficient? That she needed B to make up the numbers? This seems to treat A not as an individual with her own claim, but as a fraction of a larger claim. This is precisely what the aggregative view does, and it is precisely what I think we should reject.

On what equal concern actually requires

Equal concern: giving each person an equal chanceEqual concern for persons requires that I give each person's situation equal weight. It does not require that I multiply persons into a super-sum. If I genuinely care equally about David and each of the five, I can express this by giving each person an equal chance — by tossing a fair coin, or by using a weighted lottery that gives David a one-sixth chance and each of the five a one-sixth chance. This, I submit, is what equal concern actually looks like: attending to each person as an individual, not as a unit in a calculation. The aggregationist tells me that equal concern requires me to save the five. But equal concern, properly understood, requires me to treat each person's claim as equally weighty — which is exactly what a lottery does, and exactly what saving the five without any lottery procedure fails to do for David.

On the objection from Parfit

Reply to Parfit's agent-neutral argumentDerek Parfit will argue that suffering has impersonal badness — that it is bad regardless of whose it is — and that therefore five deaths are impersonally worse than one death. I accept that suffering is bad. I deny that this yields the conclusion that five deaths sum into something five times worse than one death. The impersonal badness of suffering is a feature of each person's situation. It gives us reason to be concerned about each person. It does not give us a principle for aggregating those concerns across persons into a total sum. The step from "each person's death is bad" to "five deaths are five times as bad as one death" is the step I am questioning, and Parfit's appeal to impersonal badness does not justify it — it merely restates what is at issue.

On what the debate is actually about

The real question: aggregation across personsI want to be clear about what I am and am not claiming. I am not claiming that the five people's lives do not matter, or that David's life matters more than theirs. I am claiming that there is no moral requirement to save the five rather than the one, because the argument for that requirement rests on treating five separate losses as if they constituted a single combined loss greater than David's. They do not constitute such a combined loss. Each person's loss is their own. The numerical difference is morally relevant only if we already accept the aggregative framework — and that framework is precisely what I am asking this House to question.

Mr President,

I want to be clear from the outset: I disagree with Taurek's conclusion. I believe we ought to save the five. But I believe this for reasons that are fundamentally different from the aggregative reasons on offer from the opposition. I do not believe we ought to save the five because five deaths constitute a larger sum of loss than one death. I believe we ought to save the five because a principle of saving the greater number can be justified to each person affected — including, crucially, to each member of the larger group — in a way that the alternative principle cannot. The contractualist framework, not the aggregative framework, is what justifies saving the greater number.

On the contractualist approach to the numbers problem

The tie-breaking argument: justified to each person individuallyMy approach begins with the question: which principles for resolving conflicts could not be reasonably rejected by anyone affected? A principle that saves the one over the five can be reasonably rejected by each member of the five considered individually. Why? Not because her claim is larger than the one's — it is not. Each faces death, and each claim is equally weighty. But a principle of systematically saving the one, when the one and any member of the five are matched and their claims are equal, gives the member of the five no reason that she could find addressed to her. After matching her claim against the one, she sees that the claims are equal — and yet the principle tells her she loses. The existence of four others in the same situation breaks this tie. This is not aggregation. It is tie-breaking.

On the individualist restriction and why it matters

The individualist restriction: no interpersonal aggregationThe most important feature of the contractualist approach is the individualist restriction. When we ask whether a principle can be reasonably rejected, we ask what any one person could reasonably object to — not what the combined objections of many people add up to. Voorhoeve's Aggregate Relevant Claims also respects this restriction in a modified form: it aggregates claims only when they are individually relevant to the claim on the other side. But the restriction rules out what standard utilitarianism requires: directly summing the welfare of many and comparing it to the welfare of one, as if the many's combined welfare constituted a single object of comparison. The individualist restriction is the contractualist commitment that ensures we are reasoning about what we owe to each person, not what produces the greatest aggregate benefit.

On Otsuka's challenge to the tie-breaking argument

Otsuka's challenge: pairwise comparison yields no aggregative conclusionMy colleague Michael Otsuka has pressed a serious objection. He argues that in any pairwise comparison — any one member of the five against the one — the claims are equal, and no pairwise comparison yields a stronger claim for the member of the five. Yet my tie-breaking argument seems to rely on the five's claims "taken together" — which is precisely the aggregative move Taurek prohibits. I accept this challenge as genuine. But I think it can be met. The tie-breaker is not that the five together have a stronger claim. The tie-breaker is that after the equal claims are matched, there remain further claimants on the side of the five whose claims face no competing claim. It is not that the five win the contest — it is that the contest for the first place is tied, and there are further contestants only on one side.

On why this is not aggregation in disguise

Why tie-breaking is not aggregationVoorhoeve and Brown may worry that the tie-breaking argument is really just aggregation under another name. I want to distinguish the two clearly. Aggregation takes the claims of the many, sums them, and compares the sum to the one's claim. The sum does additional work: it makes the many's claim stronger in proportion to their number, so that even if each individual claim is smaller than the one's, enough of them aggregate into something greater. Tie-breaking does none of this. The claims of the many are never summed. Each claim is individually compared to the one's claim and found equal. The extra claimants do not add weight to anyone's claim. They simply create additional obligations that go unaddressed by the alternative principle. This is a logically and morally distinct operation.

On what we owe to each person — and the World Cup case

The World Cup case and the limits of aggregationScanlon's World Cup case captures what I mean by the individualist restriction in practice. An engineer is trapped and suffering agonising electric shocks. Rescuing him requires interrupting a football broadcast. Millions of viewers would miss fifteen minutes. The utilitarian view — comparing aggregate viewer discomfort against one engineer's agony — implies that at some number of viewers, we should leave him to suffer. I find this deeply wrong. And it is wrong because we are comparing things that should not be compared by summing: the acute suffering of one person against the mild inconvenience of many. The individualist restriction blocks this comparison. We ask what any individual viewer could reasonably demand — and no individual viewer can reasonably demand that we leave a man to suffer agonising shocks for the sake of fifteen minutes of football.

Mr President,

I occupy an unusual position in this debate: I disagree with both the proposition and with certain versions of the opposition. I think Taurek is wrong that the numbers never count. But I also think full aggregationism — the view that any number of small benefits can outweigh a serious harm if large enough — is wrong. The view I defend, Aggregate Relevant Claims, is that numbers count when and only when the competing claims are sufficiently weighty to be morally relevant to each other. This is not a compromise between extremes. It is the correct theory.

On the two intuitions that must both be accommodated

Two intuitions: numbers count, but not alwaysThere are two intuitions about this problem that strike most people as equally compelling, and that any adequate theory must simultaneously accommodate. First: we ought to save five people from death rather than one, and part of the reason is that there are five of them. Numbers count. Second: we ought to save one person from death rather than cure any number of people of mild headaches — no matter how large that number grows. Numbers do not always count. Taurek cannot accommodate the first intuition. Full aggregationism cannot accommodate the second. Only partial aggregation, in the form of Aggregate Relevant Claims, accommodates both. This double adequacy is the primary virtue of ARC.

On what ARC says: the relevance criterion

ARC: the relevance criterion explainedAggregate Relevant Claims holds that when we face competing claims, we may aggregate only those claims that are relevant to each other — that are sufficiently weighty to be "in the same moral conversation." A claim is relevant to another when the person holding it could not reasonably insist that the competing harm is simply too insignificant to be weighed against her own. Five people each face death. One person faces death. Each of the five can point to her own death and say: your claim — one death — is in the same moral conversation as mine. So the five death-claims are all relevant to the one death-claim, and they aggregate. A billion people face headaches. One person faces death. No headache sufferer can say that their headache is in the same conversation as a death. So the headache-claims are not relevant to the death-claim, and they do not aggregate.

On Taurek's lottery proposal

Against the lottery: why equal chances are not required by equal concernTaurek argues that equal concern for persons requires giving each person an equal chance — a one-in-six lottery. I want to challenge this directly. Equal concern requires that we give each person's claim equal weight in our deliberation. It does not require that the outcome of our deliberation be random. If I deliberate carefully and determine that the five death-claims together provide stronger reason to save the five than the one death-claim provides to save the one, I have given each person's claim equal weight in my deliberation — I have not short-changed any individual. The lottery is not the only expression of equal concern. It is the expression of concern in the absence of reasons; it is not required when reasons point clearly in one direction.

On how the relevance criterion is grounded

Grounding the relevance criterion in rational prudential comparisonWhat makes one claim relevant to another? I ground the relevance criterion in a structural comparison to rational prudential choices. Suppose a rational agent must choose between a certain minor harm and a small risk of a more serious harm. If she could not reasonably decline to accept the minor harm to avoid the risk — if the trade-off is one she could endorse — then in the interpersonal case, the minor harm claim is relevant to the serious harm claim. This is not a derivation of moral facts from individual psychology. It uses the structure of rational trade-off reasoning to identify which harms are "in the same conversation" — a structural concept that applies interpersonally.

On Scanlon's tie-breaking argument — convergence and remaining disagreement

Convergence with Scanlon on the numbers case; divergence on the headache caseScanlon's tie-breaking argument and my ARC converge in their verdict on the life-versus-lives case: save the five, for reasons that are person-by-person justifiable and not aggregative in the objectionable sense. Where we diverge is in our accounts of what makes a claim relevant for tie-breaking purposes. Scanlon's approach, as Michael Otsuka has shown, risks either collapsing into aggregation — if the residue of unmatched claims does increasing work as numbers grow — or failing to justify saving the many in the original case. ARC avoids this by specifying the relevance criterion independently, before asking whether claims aggregate. The relevance criterion does the work that the tie-breaking argument struggles to do cleanly.

Mr President,

My role in this debate is somewhat different from my colleagues'. I am not primarily here to defend a particular verdict — though I share the intuition that saving the five is right, and I think the motion as stated goes too far. I am here to examine, with some rigour, whether the moderate position that most people find intuitive — that numbers count in some cases but not others — can be given a formally precise and consistent articulation. My paper "Is Close Enough Good Enough?" attempts this investigation, and I want to share what I found: that moderate aggregation faces serious structural problems that have not yet been solved, and that honest participants in this debate should acknowledge them.

On what the Close Enough View requires

The Close Enough View: what it requires formallyThe moderate position — what Parfit calls the "Close Enough View" — holds that aggregation is permissible when, and only when, conflicting harms are sufficiently similar. Five deaths are close enough to one death; headache relief is not close enough to a death. This sounds natural. But making it formally precise reveals immediate complications. We need an account of what "close enough" means, a function that measures the significance of harms, and a ratio threshold below which a harm is too insignificant to aggregate with a more serious one. These are not mere implementation details. They are substantive theoretical commitments, and different choices produce very different moral verdicts.

On the problems facing Generalised Collectivism

Generalised Collectivism: aggregates only relevant harms, but generates Transposition Pareto violationsThe most natural moderate extension of standard aggregationism — what I call Generalised Collectivism — holds that we should minimise the total significance of all relevant unprevented harms, where only claims above the relevance threshold count. This is, in essence, Voorhoeve's ARC translated into a formal choice function. The problem is that it generates violations of a plausible principle I call Transposition Pareto: if outcome A is Pareto-superior to a relabelling of outcome B, then A should be preferred. In certain three-person cases, Generalised Collectivism implies indifference between outcomes where one is straightforwardly better for two out of three people and worse for no one. This is a serious defect. Even moderate non-aggregationists should find it unacceptable.

On the problems facing Weighted Numbers

Weighted Numbers: diminishing returns on additional persons, but generates weak trumping everywhereAnother candidate — Weighted Numbers — achieves moderation by making the weight of additional persons diminish as their number grows. Rather than treating each additional person's claim as equally significant, it uses a strictly concave function of the number of claimants. This avoids some of the problems of Generalised Collectivism. But it generates a different and arguably worse problem: it implies that any harm, however slightly more significant, weakly trumps any less significant harm — for some n, preventing n instances of the slightly worse harm is better than preventing any number of the slightly better harm. The result is that ten-minute headaches weakly trump nine-minute headaches. This is not moderate aggregation. It is ubiquitous weak-trumping wearing moderate clothes.

On the obligation cycle problem facing all moderate views

Obligation cycles: all moderate views face intransitive "better than" relationsThe most fundamental structural problem facing any moderate aggregationist view is that moderation entails obligation cycles. Because the "close enough" relation is intransitive — harm A may be close enough to harm B, and harm B close enough to harm C, without A being close enough to C — there will be triples of outcomes in which each is obligatory over one of the others, cycling. Paris is close enough to London, London close enough to Geneva, but Paris not close enough to Geneva. This intransitivity infects the "obligatory" relation between outcomes, producing cycles. Cycles force the moderate view to choose: either accept prohibition dilemmas (situations where no option is permissible), or accept violations of contraction consistency (an option can be permissible in a larger menu but impermissible in a smaller one). Neither is comfortable.

On the best available candidate: Individualist-Collectivist Hybrid

ICH+TCM: the most defensible moderate candidate, with acknowledged costsMy investigation concludes that the most promising interpretation of the Close Enough View is what I call the Individualist-Collectivist Hybrid combined with Transitive Closure Maximisation. ICH first applies a lexical individualist criterion — minimising the most significant unprevented harm, with further lexical tiebreakers — and falls back on standard (Extreme Collectivist) aggregation only when the individualist criterion leaves the choice undecided. Transitive Closure Maximisation handles the resulting obligation cycles by declaring all options in a cycle permissible. This combination is compatible with Transposition Pareto, Expanded Tie-breaking, and No Prohibition Dilemmas. But I want to be honest about its costs: it lacks a unified rationale, its hybrid structure looks ad hoc, and its handling of obligation cycles by declaring all options permissible may strike some as an evasion rather than a solution. The costs of moderation are real. Whether they are worth paying is a question I deliberately leave open.

The debate is not, I think, settled. Moderate aggregation is defensible — but not easily, and not without acknowledging that our intuitions drive us toward a view whose formal articulation generates structural problems we have not fully resolved.
