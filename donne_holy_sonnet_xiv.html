Holy Sonnet XIV — "Batter my heart, three-person'd God"

John Donne — Divine Meditations (c. 1609–10; published 1633)

Structure & Context
Form: A Petrarchan sonnet in two conceits — the octave figures the soul as a town wrongly held by an occupying power; the sestet figures it as a woman wrongly betrothed. The volta arrives cleanly at line 9, in the conventional position Rhyme: Famously irregular, and line 12's 'for I' is the clearest case — it shares its end-sound with no other line in the sestet. Critics have long read this as the self standing acoustically alone, unintegrated, exactly as the poem describes a soul that cannot order itself Theology: The poem dramatises the Reformed doctrine of irresistible grace — that the will, corrupted since the Fall, cannot choose God unaided and must be overridden by violent divine intervention; this is why the poem's verbs of destruction are also verbs of salvation Biographical context: Likely written 1609–10, during a period of financial ruin and personal crisis, several years before Donne's ordination in 1615 — the poem's desperation has often been read as more than rhetorical The 'ravish' tradition: Mystical writers — St. Paul 'caught up to the third heaven,' St. Teresa of Ávila's visions — describe divine encounter in ecstatic, often erotic terms. Donne's final line draws on this tradition and pushes it into outright sexual violence A structural parallel: Like Yeats's 'Leda and the Swan,' this is a sonnet about violent supernatural 'ravishment,' and its final paradox turns on a single word holding two near-opposite senses at once — exactly as Yeats's 'still' does in 'beating still'

Batter my heart, three-person'd God; for you As yet but knock, breathe, shine, and seek to mend; That I may rise and stand, o'erthrow me, and bend Your force to break, blow, burn, and make me new. I, like an usurp'd town, to another due, Labour to admit you, but oh, to no end; Reason, your viceroy in me, me should defend, But is captived, and proves weak or untrue. Yet dearly I love you, and would be loved fain, But am betroth'd unto your enemy; Divorce me, untie, or break that knot again, Take me to you, imprison me, for I Except you enthrall me, never shall be free, Nor ever chaste, except you ravish me.
