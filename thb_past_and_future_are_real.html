Fellow enquirers,

The motion asks whether the past and future are just as real as the present. I want to argue that they are — and that the most powerful argument for this conclusion comes not from armchair metaphysics but from physics. Special relativity does not merely make the block universe plausible. It makes the denial of the block universe very nearly incoherent.

The Block Universe (Eternalism) All times — past, present, and future — exist with equal ontological status. The four-dimensional spacetime manifold is a single, unchanging block. What we call “the present” is not a metaphysically privileged moment but an indexical: saying “it is now 2025” is like saying “I am here in Paris” — true relative to a position, not an absolute fact about ontological priority.
i. the argument from special relativity

Putnam’s SR argument — all observers’ presents are equally realIn my 1967 paper “Time and Physical Geometry,” I argued that the Special Theory of Relativity, together with the plausible metaphysical principle that all and only real things are real for all observers, entails eternalism. Here is the argument: consider two spatially separated observers, A and B, in relative motion. According to the Relativity of Simultaneity, events that are simultaneous for A are not simultaneous for B. Now: if only the present exists, then what exists for A and what exists for B must coincide — because what exists is an objective matter, not a perspectival one. But there is no frame-independent notion of simultaneity in special relativity. Therefore, any event that is present for some observer must be present for all — and therefore must exist. Since, by a chain of such observers, we can make any past or future event present for some observer, all past and future events must exist. The block universe is not a metaphysical preference. It is what the physics demands.

ii. “now” as indexical: the spatial analogy

Indexical NOW — analogous to HERE; no ontological privilegeThe presentist thinks that being present is a metaphysically significant property — that there is something it is about the present moment that makes it, and it alone, real. But consider the word “here.” When I am in Paris, I say “here is Paris” — but this does not mean Paris is ontologically privileged over London or New York. “Here” is an indexical: it picks out a location relative to my standpoint. The word “now” works exactly the same way. “It is now 2025” is true relative to my temporal standpoint, just as “here is Paris” is true relative to my spatial standpoint. The presentist thinks that “now” marks a unique ontological boundary; the eternalist says this is the same mistake as thinking “here” marks a unique ontological boundary. No one thinks places that are not here are therefore unreal. No one should think times that are not now are therefore unreal.

iii. the past is real — truthmakers demand it

Truthmakers — past-tensed truths require past entities to existConsider the sentence “Caesar crossed the Rubicon.” This sentence is true. What makes it true? The truthmaker theorist — and I think any realist should be a truthmaker theorist — says a true sentence requires something in reality to correspond to it. But if only the present exists, nothing in present reality corresponds to the fact that Caesar crossed the Rubicon. The past event, the crossing itself, no longer exists. The presentist must say that presently existing things — perhaps traces, dispositions, or abstract facts — make past-tensed truths true. This is deeply implausible: the truth that Caesar crossed the Rubicon does not seem to be about present traces but about a past event. The most natural and parsimonious account of what makes past-tensed truths true is that past events exist — not here and now, but at their own temporal locations, as real as distant spatial locations.

iv. what distinguishes eternalism from the growing block

Eternalism vs. Growing Block — the future exists tooBroad’s growing block theory accepts that the past and present exist but denies that the future does. I find this ontologically unstable. If the argument from special relativity establishes that all of one observer’s present is real, and that observer’s present includes events that are in another observer’s future, then the future must be real. The growing block tries to make ontological room for the openness of the future — the sense in which the future is not yet determined — by making the future ontologically absent. But the openness of the future is a modal question (what is possible), not an ontological one (what exists). Quantum indeterminacy means that what will happen is not yet fixed; it does not mean that times at which events will happen do not exist. The growing block conflates epistemic openness with ontological absence.

Argument I — Putnam’s SR argument: relativity of simultaneity entails eternalism
1.All and only real things are real for all observers (objective realism about existence).
2.SR: any event simultaneous with the present for some observer is real-for-that-observer; by chains of observers in relative motion, any past or future event is present for some observer.
3.Therefore, any past or future event is real for some observer; by (1), it is real absolutely.
∴All past, present, and future events are equally real; the block universe follows from SR plus objective realism about existence.
Argument II — “Now” is indexical: no more ontological privilege than “here”
1.“Here” picks out a location relative to the speaker’s standpoint; distant places are not unreal merely because they are not here.
2.“Now” picks out a time relative to the speaker’s temporal standpoint; past and future times are not unreal merely because they are not now.
3.The presentist treats “now” as marking an ontological boundary where “here” does not — but there is no principled asymmetry between temporal and spatial indexicals that would justify this.
∴The presentist’s special treatment of “now” as ontologically significant is unmotivated; “now” is an indexical like “here” and marks no objective boundary of existence.
Argument III — Truthmakers: past-tensed truths require past events to exist
1.True sentences require truthmakers: something in reality must correspond to them.
2.“Caesar crossed the Rubicon” is true; if only the present exists, no present entity plausibly constitutes the truthmaker for this past event.
3.The most parsimonious account of the truthmaker is the event itself, existing at its own temporal location.
∴Past-tensed truths are grounded in the reality of past events at their temporal locations; presentism cannot account for this without invoking implausible surrogate truthmakers.
Argument IV — Growing block is unstable: openness of the future is modal, not ontological
1.The growing block accepts past and present but not future; it treats the future’s ontological absence as capturing its openness.
2.The openness of the future is a modal fact (what is possible) not an ontological one (what exists); SR shows that events in one observer’s future are in another’s present, and must therefore exist.
3.The growing block conflates epistemic uncertainty about future outcomes with ontological absence of future times.
∴The growing block’s exclusion of the future is unmotivated by the relevant modal considerations; the SR argument extends the eternalist commitment to future events as well as past ones.

Fellow enquirers,

I agree with the proposition’s motion that the past is real — as real as the present. But I find myself unable to accept that the future enjoys the same status. My view, which I introduced in Scientific Thought in 1923, is that the universe is a growing block: the past and the present exist as a continuously accumulating four-dimensional structure, and new slices of reality are added to the block as events occur. The future does not yet exist. It has not happened yet — and not-yet-happened is precisely what not-yet-existing means.

i. the asymmetry of time demands an asymmetric ontology

Growing Block — temporal asymmetry requires ontological asymmetryTime is not symmetric. The past is fixed, determined, knowable from traces and memories. The future is open, undetermined, knowable (at best) by inference and prediction. This asymmetry is not merely epistemic — it is not just that we happen to know the past and not the future. It is a deep asymmetry in the structure of reality. What has happened cannot be unhappened; what has not yet happened might not happen at all. The eternalist treats this asymmetry as merely perspectival — as arising from our position in the block, not from any real difference between past and future. I find this deeply unsatisfying. The direction of causation, the direction of entropy increase, the direction of the traceable-vs.-inferrable distinction — all of these point to a genuine ontological asymmetry that the eternal block cannot accommodate. A metaphysics of time should have room for the difference between what has occurred and what has not yet occurred; the growing block builds that asymmetry into its foundations.

ii. objective becoming without the absurdity of presentism

Growing Block over Presentism — becoming is real but past enduresThe growing block theory occupies a middle position between presentism and pure eternalism, and it is better than both. Against presentism: the past is not nothing. To say that Caesar’s crossing of the Rubicon no longer exists is to say something that strains credibility to breaking point. Caesar crossed the Rubicon. That event is fixed, immutable, a permanent addition to the structure of reality. The growing block captures this: the past exists as a completed, immutable layer of the four-dimensional block. But unlike the pure eternalist, I do not extend this ontological generosity to the future. What will happen has not happened. There is genuine becoming — the block genuinely grows — and this is what makes time different from space. Space does not grow; the block does not acquire new spatial dimensions. It acquires new temporal ones.

iii. the problem of the present — and why it matters

The problem of the present — growing block locates NOW at the edgeOne challenge for the growing block is the so-called “problem of the present”: if the block grows and the leading edge of the block is the present, what distinguishes my position at the leading edge from the position of someone who lived one hundred years ago, who was also at the leading edge when they were alive? The objector says: from inside the block, every temporal location looks like the present — so the growing block cannot explain why I experience myself as being at the present rather than one hundred years ago. I think this objection, while serious, is not fatal. The leading edge of the block is privileged not because it is subjectively experienced differently from within but because it is the only temporal location at which new existence is being added. That is an objective structural fact about the block, not a perspectival one. The present is where the growing is happening.

iv. what distinguishes me from the moving spotlight

Broad vs. Skow — growing block vs. moving spotlight metaphysicsSkow’s moving spotlight theory attempts to combine the full eternalist ontology — all times exist, including future ones — with an objective, moving present. I coined the term “moving spotlight” in this book, in fact, and I coined it critically. The moving spotlight has an ontological peculiarity that the growing block avoids: it posits a metaphysically special property of presentness that moves through an already-complete four-dimensional block. But what is this property of presentness, and what is the mechanism by which it “moves”? If all times already exist, there is nothing for the spotlight to illuminate that is not already there. The spotlight becomes a mysterious, causally inert extra property attached to the block — like a torch shining on a statue that is already fully lit. The growing block, by contrast, has no such extra moving property: the present is simply the leading edge at which the block currently terminates, and there is no mystery about why it is there.

Fellow enquirers,

Both the pure eternalist and the growing blocker agree that the past is real. I agree with both. But I want to argue for something neither of them fully accommodates: that there is genuine, objective temporal passage — that time really flows — and that this passage is compatible with, indeed requires, the full eternalist ontology that Putnam defends. My view — the Moving Spotlight Theory — holds that all times exist in the four-dimensional block, but that one time, the present, is objectively distinguished: it “glows with a special metaphysical status” that moves through the block as time passes.

i. the experience of passage demands explanation

Passage demands explanation — pure eternalism cannot account for flowThe most immediate datum of our temporal experience is that time passes. Events occur, then recede into the past; the future approaches and becomes the present. This is not merely a cognitive illusion — it is the most fundamental structure of our experience of being in the world. The pure eternalist — the B-theorist who says that tenses are merely indexical — must say that this passage is not a genuine feature of reality but an artefact of our position in the block. I find this explanatory gap troubling. If I say “thank goodness the operation is over” when I meant “thank goodness it is now after the operation,” this response makes sense only if the nowness of the post-operation moment is a genuine, objective property, not merely a perspectival index. Arthur Prior argued this point forcefully: the relief I feel is not about a tenseless B-relation but about the absolute presentness of the end of suffering. The moving spotlight theory takes this seriously.

ii. how the spotlight combines eternalism and passage

Moving Spotlight — eternalist block plus moving objective presentThe moving spotlight theory holds that spacetime is a four-dimensional block in which all times exist — so far, the pure eternalist is with me. But it adds that one time is objectively singled out as present: this is not a perspectival indexical fact but a genuine, absolute metaphysical fact about which temporal location currently has the property of presentness. And this property moves: what is present in 2025 will no longer be present in 2026; the spotlight of presentness will have moved to 2026. This is what makes time differ from space. Space has no moving spotlight: there is no objective “here” that moves through space. But time has an objective “now” that genuinely travels through the block, and this is what accounts for the passage of time.

iii. against the growing block: the future must be real

Against Growing Block — Putnam’s SR argument shows the future existsBroad’s growing block denies the reality of the future. But Putnam’s argument from special relativity applies here with full force: if A’s present includes events that are in B’s future, and A’s present is real, then B’s future is real. The growing block’s denial of the future’s reality is inconsistent with special relativity. Furthermore, the growing block has a troubling epistemological consequence: if we are always at the leading edge of the growing block, we cannot know we are at the leading edge. Someone deep within the block — in what is, objectively, the past — would have the same experiential profile as someone at the leading edge. We cannot distinguish, from the inside, whether we are at the growing tip or embedded in a completed past. The moving spotlight avoids this: we are at the objectively present time because the spotlight is here, and this is a genuine metaphysical fact even if not always accessible from within experience.

iv. answering the “what rate does it move?” objection

Rate of passage objection — answered by primitive temporal flowThe standard objection to the moving spotlight is: at what rate does the spotlight move? If time passes, does it pass at one second per second? This seems trivially true or meaninglessly circular. But I think this objection, pressed by Smart and others, fails. The question “at what rate does the present move?” is ill-formed in the same way that asking for the location of space is ill-formed. The passage of time is not itself a process occurring within time that has a rate; it is the primitive metaphysical background against which all rates are measured. We do not ask at what rate change occurs in general — we use change to define rates. Equally, we should not ask at what rate time passes — we use the passage of time to define durations. The objection presupposes that passage must be measurable from a higher-order temporal perspective; but that perspective does not exist, and passage does not require it.

Fellow enquirers,

I oppose this motion, and I want to start from a phenomenological datum that I think the proposition has not taken seriously enough. When a painful experience ends — when a dental operation is over, when a long journey concludes — I say “thank goodness that’s over.” I do not say “thank goodness it occurs at a time earlier than this utterance.” The relief I feel is not a tenseless B-series fact. It is a fact about what is now the case — that the pain is now in the past, no longer present, no longer happening. This distinction, which I argued in Past, Present and Future, is the key to understanding why presentism is not merely intuitively compelling but philosophically indispensable.

“It is not a tautology that the present moment is present. It is a genuine, non-indexical fact about the world that distinguishes the now from the then — and that fact is the ground of the only kind of relief, anticipation, and regret that we actually experience.”
i. the “thank goodness” argument: tenses are real and irreducible

Thank goodness — tense is primitive and real, not reducible to B-relationsThe B-theorist says: tensed statements can be reduced to tenseless ones. “The operation is now over” means, on the B-theory, “the operation occurs at a time earlier than this utterance.” But these two sentences are not the same. If they were the same, my relief at the end of the operation would be equivalent to relief that the operation is temporally located before a certain utterance — and I could have had that relief before the operation took place, since the B-relation of occurring-before-the-utterance is a timeless fact. The peculiarity of relief, anticipation, and regret — that they are appropriate only at certain times, not others — reveals that tenses are not reducible to B-relations. The now-ness of the present is not a relational fact. It is a primitive, intrinsic property of the world at this moment, and that property is what grounds the experience of time’s passage.

ii. tense logic: the formal case for presentism

Tense Logic — A-theory requires primitive tense operators not reducible to quantifiersMy formal contribution to this debate was the development of tense logic: a logical system in which the primitive operators P (“it was the case that”) and F (“it will be the case that”) are not reducible to quantification over times. On the B-theory, “P(p)” means something like “there exists a time t earlier than now such that p is true at t.” But this quantification over times presupposes that those past times exist to be quantified over — which is exactly what the presentist denies. If tense logic is taken seriously — if P and F are genuine primitive operators rather than disguised quantifiers — then the B-theory’s reduction fails. The logic of tense requires that tensed facts be primitive and that the present moment have a status that no merely relational B-fact can replicate.

iii. the relativity objection answered

SR objection answered — presentism requires neo-Lorentzian interpretation of relativityPutnam argues that special relativity refutes presentism because there is no frame-independent notion of simultaneity. This is a powerful objection that I take seriously. My response is that presentism requires adopting a neo-Lorentzian interpretation of relativity: one in which there is a privileged frame of reference — corresponding to the absolute present — even though that frame is not empirically accessible from within the theory. The Special Theory of Relativity, as a physical theory, is empirically adequate. But empirical adequacy does not settle metaphysical questions about what is real. The neo-Lorentzian holds that there is an absolute fact about simultaneity; we cannot detect which frame is the privileged one, but this is an epistemic limitation, not a metaphysical one. Presentism is compatible with the physics; it requires an interpretation of the physics that the physics itself cannot rule out.

iv. what distinguishes me from mctaggart

Prior vs. McTaggart — tenses are real, not contradictory; time is not unrealMcTaggart argues that the A-series, which requires tenses, is contradictory — that every event is both past, present, and future, which is impossible. He concludes that time is unreal. I accept that tenses are primitive and that the A-series is fundamental. I deny that the A-series is contradictory. McTaggart’s argument is that an event is past, present, and future all at once. But this is not what any presentist or A-theorist claims. A-properties apply to events at different times: the operation was future, is now present, and will be past. These are not simultaneous incompatible attributions. They are different facts obtaining at different times. The tense operators P and F distinguish them precisely: P(present(e)) and F(past(e)) and present(e) are not contradictory; they are governed by different tense operators. McTaggart’s argument fails because it treats tensed truths as if they were simultaneous tenseless truths — which is exactly what the A-theorist denies.

Fellow enquirers,

I occupy in this debate the most radical position available, and I want to defend it with full seriousness. I do not merely oppose the motion by saying the past and future are not real. I oppose it by arguing that time itself is unreal — that what we call “temporal” facts are either contradictory (if taken as genuine A-series facts) or incomplete (if reduced to B-series relations). Neither A nor B alone can constitute real time, and together they generate a vicious regress. The conclusion — which I know most philosophers find counterintuitive — is that time, as we ordinarily conceive it, does not exist.

The A-series and B-series B-series: Events ordered by the permanent, tenseless relations “earlier than,” “later than,” and “simultaneous with.” These relations never change. A-series: Events ordered as future, present, or past — properties that change: an event is first future, then present, then past. McTaggart argues both are required for real time, but the A-series is primary since only it allows for genuine change.
i. why the A-series is essential to time

A-series essential — B-series alone gives no changeMy first argument is that the B-series alone cannot constitute real time. The B-series orders events by the tenseless relations of earlier and later. But these relations are permanent and unchanging: the Battle of Hastings is always earlier than the French Revolution, regardless of when that sentence is uttered. If time consisted only of B-series relations, nothing would ever change — the “earlier” relation would just be another static spatial-type ordering. But time is not static. Change is essential to time. And change requires that events acquire and shed the properties of being future, present, and past. Therefore the A-series is essential: without A-properties, there is no genuine change, and without genuine change, there is no real time.

ii. why the A-series is contradictory

A-series contradictory — every event has incompatible A-propertiesBut the A-series generates a contradiction. The A-series properties — past, present, future — are mutually incompatible. Nothing can simultaneously be past, present, and future. Yet every event possesses all three properties: the Battle of Hastings was future (before it occurred), was present (while it occurred), and is now past (since it occurred). The same event has all three incompatible determinations. The obvious response is: it has them successively, not simultaneously. But this response introduces a second A-series to explain the first — a series of times at which the event is future, at which it is present, at which it is past. And each of these times in turn has all three A-properties simultaneously, requiring a third A-series to explain them. And so on infinitely. The regress is vicious: we never reach a level at which the contradiction is resolved without being regenerated.

iii. prior’s tense operators fail to escape the regress

Against Prior — tense operators regenerate the regressPrior claims that tense operators P and F resolve my paradox by distinguishing temporal attributions. “The event is now present,” “It was future,” “It will be past” are governed by different operators. But this response only pushes the problem back one level. When I say “It was future” using the operator P, I am saying: at some past time, the event had the property of being future. But that past time itself must be located in the A-series: it was itself future, is now past. We have reintroduced a second-level A-series, and the regress continues. Prior’s operators do not eliminate the need for A-series facts; they accumulate them. At every level, we require a further temporal context to interpret the previous level’s A-properties, and each temporal context reintroduces the same contradiction.

iv. what follows: the C-series and the appearance of time

C-series — real order without real time; idealist conclusionIf time is unreal, this does not mean that the apparent temporal order of events is illusory in every respect. I distinguish a C-series: a permanent, non-temporal ordering of events that is the shadow of what we mistakenly call time. This ordering is real; what is unreal is the dynamic, A-series dimension we project onto it. The experience of temporal passage — which Skow and Prior rightly identify as a fundamental datum of our cognitive life — is real as experience; but experience is not a reliable guide to the ultimate structure of reality. The idealist conclusion I am drawn to is that the C-series is grounded in something non-temporal — most probably in the structure of mind itself, as my larger metaphysical system in The Nature of Existence suggests. Time, as a feature of mind-independent reality, does not exist.

ii. why the moving spotlight is unnecessary

Against Moving Spotlight — objective NOW posits an unexplanatory extra propertySkow argues that the experience of passage requires an objective, moving present — a spotlight that singles out one time as absolutely NOW. I think this posit is both unexplanatory and unnecessary. Unexplanatory because: even if there were a moving spotlight, our experience of it would still require explanation in terms of how information-processing systems embedded in the block track the spotlight’s position. The spotlight by itself does not explain experience; only the story about embedded systems does. Unnecessary because: once we have the embedded-systems account, we have all the explanation of passage we need. iii. against presentism: the physics of memory and causation

Against Presentism — causal asymmetry and memory require past events to existThe presentist says only the present exists. But the experience that gives our temporal life its character — memory of the past, expectation of the future — is causally constituted by past events. My memory of yesterday does not merely represent yesterday as something that was; it is a causal trace of yesterday’s events in today’s neural state. If yesterday does not exist, what did the causing? The presentist must say: present traces carry the causal work. But present traces are causally inert with respect to the past events they represent; the causal work was done by those past events, which therefore must have existed. The block universe gives a natural account of memory, causation, and the asymmetry of influence — causes precede effects in the block — that presentism can only simulate through increasingly baroque present-tensed surrogates.

iv. the self-locating perspective: why “now” feels special without a special metaphysics

Just as I need to represent where I am spatially — knowing that I am “here” in London, not abstractly that London exists — I need to represent when I am temporally. The indexical “now” is the temporal analogue of “here”: it is essential to my self-locating perspective as an agent embedded in space and time.
