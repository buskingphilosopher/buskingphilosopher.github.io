Mr President,

I want to begin not with a polemic but with a legal and historical claim that I believe can be demonstrated rigorously: that the international legal order of which the United Nations is the central expression was not constructed in opposition to empire but in continuity with it. The terminology changed. The institutional architecture was modernised. The vocabulary of race and civilisation was retired. But the operative logic — the power to distinguish between those who govern and those who are governed, between those who set standards and those who are required to meet them — remained structurally intact.

"The colonial encounter was not an aberration that modern international law has overcome. It was the crucible in which international law's foundational concepts — sovereignty, self-determination, trusteeship, intervention — were forged. You cannot understand what these concepts mean today without understanding what they were designed to do then."
i. the civilising mission — reproduced, not retired

Civilising mission — dynamic of difference persistsThe League of Nations Mandate System is the direct institutional ancestor of the United Nations Trusteeship system, and both express the same foundational logic: that certain peoples are not yet ready for self-governance and require tutelage by those who are. In the Mandate System, this was expressed in the language of civilisational stages. In the UN era, the language shifted: "trusteeship," "development," "capacity building," "good governance." But as I argue in Imperialism, Sovereignty and the Making of International Law, the "dynamic of difference" between governed and governing peoples was not eliminated — it was secularised. The categories changed from "civilised" and "uncivilised" to "developed" and "developing." The power relation encoded in those categories did not change at all. To call this a coincidence of language rather than a continuity of structure is not an argument — it is an evasion.

ii. sovereignty — a gift from those who already have it

Sovereignty paradox — granted on terms set by the grantorThe decolonisation moment — 1945 to 1970 — is presented in mainstream international legal scholarship as the universalisation of sovereignty: the moment at which all peoples were finally admitted to the family of nations as equals. This description omits the decisive detail. Sovereignty, in the post-colonial UN system, was not simply granted — it was granted on terms defined entirely by those who already possessed it. The new states were admitted to a legal order whose rules — on property rights, contracts, the protection of foreign investment, the structure of international economic institutions — had been designed without them and in many cases explicitly against their interests. The sovereignty they received was formal but not substantive: they could vote in the General Assembly but could not veto in the Security Council; they could sign the IMF Articles but could not alter the conditionalities that came with IMF credit.

iii. the Security Council as constitutional empire

UNSC P5 veto — 1945 power frozen into constitutional lawThe Security Council's architecture encodes the distribution of power of 1945 into what functions as constitutional law for the international system. Five states — the victors of the Second World War — were granted permanent membership and veto power. These states were, without exception, colonial powers or powers that had engaged in colonial expansion. The Soviet Union had absorbed large territories through force; France and Britain retained vast colonial empires; the United States had its sphere of dominance across Latin America and the Pacific. Together they constituted the governing council of a world in which the vast majority of the world's population had no representation. Eighty years later, this arrangement is unchanged. India — 1.4 billion people, nuclear power, democratic state — has no permanent seat. Nigeria, Brazil, Indonesia, and South Africa are absent. The veto power that no structural reform has yet succeeded in reaching because any amendment requires the consent of those whose power it would diminish.

iv. humanitarian intervention and R2P — the civilising mission in uniform

R2P — selectivity, normative framing, post-colonial targetsThe Responsibility to Protect doctrine — endorsed by the UN General Assembly in 2005 — represents the most recent iteration of the civilising mission's logic. As Anne Orford has documented in International Authority and the Responsibility to Protect, R2P reconstructs executive authority at the international level in ways that reproduce, rather than constrain, the structure of colonial governance. Its application is selective in a pattern that is not random: R2P has been invoked in Libya, but not Palestine; in Côte d'Ivoire, but not Yemen when the intervening parties are permanent Security Council members. The states most frequently subject to its application are precisely the states whose sovereignty is weakest — former colonies whose institutional capacity was systematically undermined during the colonial period and continues to be undermined by the economic conditionalities of the Bretton Woods institutions. R2P does not mark the end of the colonial pattern. It is the colonial pattern's latest expression.

v. what "reform" cannot reach

Reform limits — structural, not procedural, problemThe standard response to this analysis is: reform the Security Council; extend the veto; make the institutions more representative. I do not dismiss these proposals. But I want to insist on a structural point that procedural reform cannot address. The problem is not only that the wrong states hold power within an otherwise sound institutional architecture. It is that the foundational concepts of the order — sovereignty, development, intervention, conditionality — were formulated in and by the colonial encounter, and carry within them the assumptions and asymmetries of that encounter. Reforming who sits at the table does not automatically reform what the table is for. To give Global South states a permanent seat in a Security Council that still authorises military intervention under R2P is to extend them a form of power that will be used against other Global South states — as the Libya vote demonstrated when the African Union members of the Council were outmanoeuvred.

1.The League Mandate System expressed a governing logic: some peoples require tutelage by those more "civilised." The UN Trusteeship and development frameworks express the same logic in secularised vocabulary.
2.The replacement of "civilised/uncivilised" with "developed/developing" changes terminology while preserving the underlying power relation: the right to define and adjudicate who qualifies as a full sovereign actor.
3.A structural continuity of this kind — the same power relation under successive terminological covers — is the defining feature of institutional imperialism.
∴The UN reproduced rather than eliminated the colonial "dynamic of difference"; its modernised vocabulary is cover for a structurally continuous imperial logic.

1.Post-colonial states were admitted to a legal order whose property, investment, and institutional rules were designed without them and served prior colonial interests.
2.Formal equality (General Assembly votes) coexisted with substantive inequality: no veto, no control over IMF conditionality, no effective capacity to revise the international economic order's foundational rules.
3.Sovereignty that is granted on the grantor's terms, and cannot be exercised against those terms, is not sovereignty — it is a managed independence that preserves the colonial relationship in contractual form.

∴The Security Council is a constitutional lock encoding colonial power distribution into international law; its structure is definitionally imperial.
Argument IV — R2P: humanitarian intervention as civilising mission
1.R2P reconstructs international executive authority in ways that reproduce colonial governance structures — the right of the "responsible" to intervene in the "failing" (Orford).
2.Its application is systematically selective: post-colonial weak states are targets; P5 members and their allies are immunised by the veto.
3.This selectivity is not incidental — it is structurally determined by the same power asymmetry that colonial governance expressed: the right to judge others from a position that is itself beyond judgment.
∴R2P is the civilising mission in contemporary legal dress — selective, P5-administered, and applied primarily to former colonial states whose institutional weakness the colonial period produced.
Argument V — Structural not procedural: reform cannot reach the root
1.The imperial problem is not only who holds power within the UN but what the system's foundational concepts — development, conditionality, intervention — mean and whose interests they encode.
2.Procedural reform (extending the P5, adding elected seats) inserts new actors into an unchanged conceptual architecture that continues to express colonial-era assumptions.
3.Institutions express, not merely reflect, their founding conditions; changing their membership without changing their conceptual vocabulary does not change what they do.
∴The imperial character of the UN is structural, embedded in its founding concepts; procedural reform is necessary but insufficient — it changes who wields the instrument without changing what the instrument is.

Mr President,

My colleague Professor Anghie has made the legal and political science case. I want to make the economic one — and to argue that the political architecture of the United Nations cannot be properly understood in isolation from the economic architecture of the Bretton Woods institutions that were constructed simultaneously and in direct institutional connection with it. Together, the UN, the World Bank, and the IMF constitute a single system of what I call "collective imperialism" — the management of peripheral economies by the core powers through multilateral rather than bilateral colonial structures.

"Imperialism did not end when the flags changed. It continued through more sophisticated mechanisms: the unequal exchange embedded in terms of trade, the conditionality attached to international credit, the intellectual property regimes that lock technological advantage in the global North, and the dollar-denominated financial system that extracts seigniorage from the global periphery to the American centre. These are not market outcomes. They are political arrangements sustained by international institutions."
i. unequal exchange — the economic structure of empire

Unequal exchange — terms of trade, value transferThe mechanism I identify as central to contemporary imperialism is unequal exchange: the systematic transfer of value from peripheral economies to the core through terms of trade that do not reflect real productivity differentials. Peripheral countries export primary commodities and labour-intensive manufactures at prices that do not capture the full value of the labour embodied in them, while importing capital-intensive manufactures and intellectual property at prices that do. The result is a persistent net transfer of value from South to North that dwarfs the transfers in the opposite direction through development aid. Emmanuel and I documented this mechanism in the dependency theory tradition; the empirical evidence from UNCTAD data consistently demonstrates that primary commodity exporters face secular terms-of-trade deterioration relative to manufactured goods — the Prebisch-Singer hypothesis confirmed across seven decades of data.

ii. conditionality as structural adjustment — the IMF's colonial function

IMF conditionality — SAPs as economic governanceThe IMF Structural Adjustment Programmes of the 1980s and 1990s — which the World Bank coordinated — required debtor governments in the Global South to implement a standard package: currency devaluation, trade liberalisation, privatisation of state enterprises, reduction of public expenditure, removal of subsidies, and financial deregulation. These conditions were not derived from an empirical assessment of each country's developmental needs. They were derived from a theoretical framework — Washington Consensus neoclassical economics — that embedded the interests of creditor nations and international capital in technical language. The consequences are empirically documented: IMF adjustment programmes were associated with reduced public health expenditure, increased infant mortality, deindustrialisation, and persistent debt — not as unfortunate side effects but as predictable structural outcomes of policies that prioritised creditor recovery over developmental investment. This is not neutral technocratic management. It is governance.

iii. the intellectual property regime — locking technological advantage

TRIPS — IP as imperial rent extractionThe TRIPS Agreement — negotiated within the WTO framework, supported by UN-adjacent institutions — extended US-style intellectual property protection globally, requiring all WTO members to enforce 20-year patent monopolies on medicines, seeds, and technologies. The effect was to make permanent the technological advantage of the global North by raising the legal cost of technology transfer and imitation — precisely the mechanisms by which every successful late industrialiser, from Germany and Japan to South Korea and Taiwan, had developed. The United States and European nations built their industrial capacity through extensive copying of foreign technologies; they then used the multilateral trade and intellectual property regime to pull up the ladder behind them. The UN system — through the WTO, WIPO, and associated institutions — enforces this arrangement globally, under the name of "rules-based international order."

iv. collective imperialism — bilateral replaced, not abolished

Collective imperialism — multilateral structure, same functionThe decisive conceptual move I want to make is this: the replacement of bilateral colonial relationships with multilateral institutional arrangements does not constitute the abolition of imperialism — it constitutes its collectivisation. Britain no longer directly governs Nigeria. But Nigeria's access to international credit markets is conditioned by IMF assessments; its trade relations are governed by WTO rules it did not design; its debt is denominated in dollars whose supply is controlled by the US Federal Reserve; and its sovereignty over natural resources is constrained by bilateral investment treaties whose dispute resolution mechanisms favour international capital over host-state regulation. These mechanisms are more efficient than colonial administration because they require no occupation force and generate their own ideological legitimisation through the language of universal standards and international law.

v. the dollar system — monetary imperialism

Dollar hegemony — seigniorage, reserve currency privilegeThe Bretton Woods system — officially ended in 1971 but effectively continuing — established the US dollar as the world's reserve currency, giving the United States what Giscard d'Estaing called the "exorbitant privilege": the ability to run persistent trade deficits financed by the world's willingness to hold dollar-denominated assets as reserves. The rest of the world must earn dollars through trade surpluses to service dollar-denominated debts. The United States simply issues them. This structural asymmetry — in which one nation's domestic monetary policy is, in effect, the world's monetary policy — is enforced through the IMF's Article VIII obligations and the dollar-denominated structure of global commodity markets. The UN did not create this system, but it provides the political framework within which it operates, and the Security Council's power protects the military capability that ultimately underwrites the dollar's reserve status.

Thank you.

2.All other states must earn foreign exchange through trade surpluses to service external debts; the US creates the means of payment by domestic monetary policy, imposing its interest rate and inflation choices on the global economy.
3.This asymmetry — one state's domestic monetary policy as the world's monetary policy — is not a market outcome; it is a political arrangement ultimately backed by military capability that the UNSC power structure protects.
∴Dollar hegemony is imperial monetary privilege collectivised through multilateral institutions; the UN's political architecture protects the military capacity that ultimately underwrites it.

Mr President,

I want to take the proposition's argument seriously — far more seriously than its proponents might expect from a liberal institutionalist. Professor Anghie's historical account contains important truths about the colonial origins of international law. Professor Amin's economic analysis identifies real mechanisms of structural inequality. I do not dismiss these. What I contest is the conceptual conclusion: that the UN is empire "in disguise" — that it is, at its functional core, an instrument of great power domination over the weak. This claim is empirically falsifiable and, I will argue, empirically false.

"The proposition assumes that because powerful states built the international institutions, those institutions must primarily serve powerful states. This conflates origins with effects. We do not evaluate democratic parliaments by asking whether they were built by men of property — we evaluate them by asking what they actually do. The same standard of evidence must apply to the United Nations."
i. institutions constrain their creators

Institutions constrain creators — Keohane's central findingThe central finding of my work in After Hegemony and subsequent research is that international institutions, once established, acquire autonomy and normative force that constrains even their most powerful creators. The United States built the post-war institutional order. The United States has been constrained by it — in ways it did not anticipate and often did not welcome. The ICJ ruled against the US in Nicaragua v. United States. The Security Council refused to authorise the 2003 Iraq invasion, forcing the US to act outside the UN framework and pay significant political costs for that choice. WTO dispute settlement panels have ruled against American trade practices repeatedly. If the UN were simply empire's instrument, these results would be impossible — an empire does not build instruments that successfully rule against it.

ii. the counterfactual — what empire without the UN looks like

Counterfactual — pure great power competition worse for the weakThe proposition's argument implicitly assumes that the alternative to the UN system is better for the Global South. The counterfactual is not a world of genuine multilateral equality — no such world has ever existed. The counterfactual is nineteenth-century international relations: gunboat diplomacy, explicit spheres of influence, unilateral military intervention without even the requirement of international legitimacy, and colonial administration openly justified as civilisational mission. The UN system has not abolished great power privilege — I concede this. But it has imposed procedural and normative costs on the exercise of that privilege that are non-trivial. The US did not simply invade Iraq without attempting to secure Security Council authorisation. Russia has exercised its veto 150 times since 1945 to block resolutions it found threatening — which means those resolutions existed and had to be vetoed. An empire does not need to veto its own orders.

iii. decolonisation — the UN's actual record

Decolonisation — UN enabled, not impededThe proposition identifies the UN with the perpetuation of empire. But the historical record of the UN's role in decolonisation substantially contradicts this. The UN General Assembly's Declaration on the Granting of Independence to Colonial Countries and Peoples (Resolution 1514, 1960) — passed 89-0 with 9 abstentions, over the opposition of colonial powers — provided political and legal legitimacy for independence movements across Asia and Africa that colonial powers would have preferred to suppress. The UN's Decolonisation Committee maintained pressure on remaining colonial territories. UN peacekeeping missions in Namibia, East Timor, and elsewhere actively facilitated transitions to independence against the resistance of occupying powers. These are not the actions of an institution designed to preserve empire — they are the actions of an institution whose universalist mandate created space for anti-colonial politics.

iv. the motion's category error — power is not empire

Category error — power asymmetry ≠ empireI want to raise a conceptual objection to the motion as framed. Empire, as a concept in political science, denotes a specific form of governance: the direct or indirect political control of one political community over another, with the subordinate community's interests systematically subordinated to the dominant community's. Power asymmetry is a necessary condition of empire, but not a sufficient one. The United States is more powerful than Botswana. This does not make the international relationship between them imperial in any analytically precise sense. If "empire" means nothing more than "a system in which power is unequally distributed," then the word loses all discriminatory force — every political system in history has been imperial by this definition, and the concept cannot do any analytical work. The proposition owes us a more precise definition, and when it provides one, I believe the UN will not satisfy it.

v. the real problem and the realistic remedy

Reform agenda — UNSC expansion, weighted IMF votesThe serious critique of the UN is not that it is empire but that it is inadequately representative, insufficiently accountable, and structurally biased toward its most powerful members in ways that require reform. This critique I fully accept. The Security Council should be expanded to include India, Brazil, Germany, Japan, Nigeria, and South Africa, at minimum, with reformed veto arrangements. IMF voting shares should be rebalanced to reflect actual economic weight, not 1944 power distributions. Calling the UN "empire in disguise" does not advance this reform agenda — it encourages abandonment rather than transformation of the only system of collective security management we have, and leaves the realistic alternative, pure great power competition, unaddressed.

Thank you.

3.The realistic alternative to an imperfect UN is not a better multilateral order — it is unilateral great power competition, which systematically disadvantages weaker states far more than the current system does.
∴The correct response to the UN's structural biases is reform, not condemnation; the "empire" framing is analytically imprecise and politically counterproductive.

Mr President,

I want to offer a different kind of opposition to the proposition — one that accepts much of its empirical description while rejecting its causal and conceptual framework. The UN system has genuine and serious pathologies. It has failed catastrophically in Rwanda, in Srebrenica, in Syria. It has applied its standards inconsistently. It has imposed governance frameworks that reflect Northern theoretical commitments more than Southern developmental realities. But my argument, developed jointly with Martha Finnemore in Rules for the World, is that these pathologies have causes that the "empire in disguise" framing systematically misidentifies — and that getting the diagnosis wrong means the remedy will also be wrong.

"The proposition asks us to see the United Nations as an instrument of great power will — as empire with better public relations. Our research suggests a different and in many ways more troubling diagnosis: the UN is frequently not controlled by powerful states at all. It is controlled by its own bureaucratic culture — its internal rules, professional norms, and organisational pathologies — which produce failures that serve no one's interests and which powerful states are often unable to prevent."
i. international organisations as autonomous bureaucracies

Bureaucratic autonomy — IOs not reducible to state interestsThe realist account of international organisations — which the proposition essentially adopts — treats them as instruments of their most powerful member states: tools that powerful states use to advance their interests and discard when inconvenient. This is empirically untenable. International organisations develop their own institutional interests, professional cultures, and normative commitments that lead them to behave in ways their creators neither intended nor desired. The IMF expanded its conditionality mandate from short-term balance-of-payments stabilisation into long-term structural governance of domestic economies — not because the United States instructed it to, but because its professional economists, trained in a particular theoretical tradition, genuinely believed this was what development required. Bureaucratic autonomy, not imperial instruction, explains the overreach.

ii. the Rwanda case — bureaucratic failure, not imperial design

Rwanda — UNAMIR failure was organisational pathologyThe failure of UN peacekeeping in Rwanda — where UNAMIR stood by during the genocide that killed 800,000 people in three months — is the most devastating indictment of UN institutional performance. The proposition would explain this as serving imperial interests: the major powers had no interest in Rwanda, so the UN did nothing. But this explanation is too convenient. General Dallaire, the UNAMIR commander, had intelligence identifying genocide preparations and requested authorisation to act. The refusal came not from P5 political direction but from the DPKO's organisational culture of impartiality — a bureaucratic norm that treated intervention as inconsistent with peacekeeping's self-defined mandate, regardless of what the political consequences were. The failure was produced by the internal logic of a bureaucracy following its own rules, not by powerful states instrumentalising the organisation against Rwanda's interests.

iii. the "empire" framing overestimates coherence

Coherence problem — empire implies coordinated purposeEmpire, as a concept, implies coordinated purposive action: a centre directing the system toward particular ends. The UN system does not display this coherence. The Security Council is routinely paralysed by P5 disagreements. The General Assembly passes resolutions the P5 oppose. UN agencies work at cross-purposes: UNICEF's development mandate conflicts with UNHCR's refugee protection mandate; the OHCHR's human rights work conflicts with the political calculations of the Secretariat. The Bretton Woods institutions adopt frameworks that the UN development agencies criticise. This is not what a coordinated imperial system looks like — it is what a dysfunctional international bureaucracy looks like, populated by professionals with genuine but competing normative commitments and organisational interests. The proposition's analysis is too coherent. It imports an explanatory tidiness that the evidence does not support.

iv. the OECD governance norm — pathology or imperialism?

Good governance norm — bureaucratic ideology, not imperial instructionThe proposition correctly identifies the "good governance" agenda — pushed through World Bank and UN development programmes — as encoding primarily Western liberal-democratic and neoclassical economic norms. This is a real and serious problem. But the mechanism through which it operates is bureaucratic socialisation, not imperial direction. World Bank economists are trained in particular institutions, use particular theoretical frameworks, and genuinely believe that the development models they promote are the correct ones. They are not, for the most part, cynically serving American interests — they are doing what professionals trained in a particular intellectual tradition do: applying that tradition. The problem is that the tradition itself is not neutral, and its institutional embedding in the development apparatus makes it effectively hegemonic without anyone intending that outcome. This is worse than empire in one respect: it is harder to reform, because there is no imperial centre to negotiate with.

v. implications for reform — get the diagnosis right

Reform implications — accountability, not just power redistributionIf the UN's pathologies were produced by great power direction, the remedy would be straightforward: redistribute power. Give the Global South more seats, more votes, more veto-equivalent tools. And indeed, some problems — the Security Council's composition, IMF voting weights — do require exactly this. But many of the UN's most serious failures — the Rwanda genocide, the UNHCR repatriation of refugees into danger, the IMF's expansion beyond its mandate — were produced by bureaucratic pathology that would persist under any composition of powerful states. These failures require accountability mechanisms: transparency obligations on international organisations, civil society oversight, independent evaluation functions with genuine authority. An analysis that attributes all pathologies to empire will consistently prescribe the wrong remedies for the problems that are not caused by empire.

Thank you.

Argument IV — Good governance ideology: bureaucratic hegemony, not imperial direction
1.The good governance agenda encodes Western liberal-democratic norms not through imperial instruction but through bureaucratic socialisation: professional training, theoretical frameworks, institutional culture.
2.Professionals who genuinely believe in their models, and whose institutions embed those beliefs, produce hegemonic effects without conspiratorial intent — this is harder to reform than empire because there is no centre to negotiate with.
3.The proposition's framing — empire — implies a remedy (redistribute power) that does not address hegemony produced by professional ideology rather than political direction.
∴The good governance agenda is produced by bureaucratic ideological hegemony, not imperial direction; understanding this requires different analytical tools and different remedies than the "empire" diagnosis offers.
Argument V — Get the diagnosis right: accountability, not only power redistribution
1.Some UN failures — Security Council composition, IMF voting weights — do require power redistribution; the proposition is correct on these.
2.But many of the most serious failures — Rwanda, refugee repatriation into danger, mandate expansion — were caused by bureaucratic pathology that persists under any power composition and requires accountability mechanisms to address.
3.An analysis that attributes all pathologies to empire consistently prescribes incomplete remedies for non-imperial problems, leaving the most dangerous institutional failures unremedied.
∴The "empire" diagnosis is partially correct but systematically incomplete; the UN requires both power redistribution and accountability reform — and only a non-imperial diagnosis of bureaucratic pathology identifies the second need.
