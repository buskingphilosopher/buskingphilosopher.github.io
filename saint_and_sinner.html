Saint & Sinner

Published March 14, 2026

Saint lives a saintly life, sacrificing many personal goods in order to be kind and charitable to all. Sinner, by contrast, lives a life of selfish gain, profiting at the expense of others. Saint and Sinner die.

At the gates of heaven they are told of their fates. Unfortuantely, a mistake has been made and their fates have been assigned incorrectly. Saint will reside in heaven, living happily proportional to the good that he did on earth. Sinner, however, will also reside in heaven, living even happier than Saint.

This is philosopher Larry Temkin’s example of Saint and Sinner. He thinks this is a bad scenario. But the problem is, who is it bad for?

It isn’t bad for Saint because he is rewarded for all the good he did on earth. All his sacrifices are rewarded proportionally to the good that he did on earth.

Nor is it bad for Sinner since he gets to live a way better life than he would if his fate was assigned properly and he lived out his afterlife in hell.

Surely for a scenario to be bad, it has to be bad for somebody. If it is bad for absolutely nobody then it’s a little mysterious how the scenario can be considered to be bad as a whole. And if this is the case, then this purgatorial mistake, which is bad for neither Saint nor Sinner, cannot reasonably be considered a bad state of affairs after all.

But clearly there is something bad about this situation, and Temkin uses this example to argue states of affairs can be bad even when they are bad for no one. Which sounds strange.

Yet consider the following. We elect a government on the pledge to improve social services. When in office, they decide to improve only one social service by a minor amount. This is better for us because it’s better for at least some people. But it’s not what we elected them for.

The government response? It’s not a bad state of affairs because it is worse for nobody. After all, the only other option was to reelect the government which never intended to improve any social service. So how can it be bad state of affairs that now at least there is a minor improvement to one of the social services?

Perhaps the distinctive wrong is explained by what is known as the counterfactual account of harm. We are harmed under the newly elected government because there is a counterfactual world in which it certainly was possible the elected government stuck to its pledges and improved all social services. In other words, we are worse off than we feasibly could have been.

But this account of harm runs into the following problem. Imagine I hire two hitmen to kill you. The first is known to be unreliable so I hire the second as a back up. As it turns out, the first hitman successfully kills you.

According to this account of harm, hitman 1 didn’t harm you since hitman 2 would have killed you anyway if he’d missed. But to say he didn’t harm you is surely absurd!
