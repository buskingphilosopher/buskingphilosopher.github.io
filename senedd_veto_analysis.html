Wales Has Five Parties, No Majority and One Deal That Works

Senedd election 2026  ·  Analysis via veto player theory and spatial voting models

The 2026 Senedd election produced exactly what veto player theory predicts when ideological distance between parties is high and no single actor commands a majority: an empty all-party winset, two feasible coalition zones, and a Borda winner that nobody loves but everyone can live with.

Borda count — possible coalition agreements

Each party ranks the five possible deals. Score = Σ (n−1−rank), where n = 5. A deal scores 4 if ranked 1st, 3 if 2nd, and so on. The winner is the deal with the highest aggregate score — the broadest consensus, even if nobody's first choice.

Borda winner: Plaid minority + Labour confidence and supply. Not because anyone loves it — Plaid would prefer to govern alone, Labour would prefer to have recovered enough to govern itself — but because it is the least-bad outcome for the most parties. This is the defining quality the Borda count measures: not enthusiasm, but distributed tolerance.
How to read the diagram
01

Party positions

Each dot represents a party's ideal point in 2D policy space. The horizontal axis runs from Welsh nationalist/left to unionist/right. The vertical axis runs from statist/interventionist to market liberal. Dot size is proportional to projected seats. The key spatial fact: Reform and Plaid are nearly diametrically opposite, making any all-party deal geometrically impossible.

02

Indifference circles

Each dashed circle marks a party's indifference threshold — the furthest policy position it would accept over the status quo of no government at all. Radius is derived from ideological distance. Labour's circle is medium-sized despite only 12 seats, because it is the only party whose indifference curve intersects with Plaid's on the left and stops before Reform's on the right — making it an indispensable kingmaker.

03

Coalition winsets

A winset is the set of policies all members of a given coalition prefer to the status quo simultaneously. It is the intersection of their indifference circles. The green zones show the two viable configurations: Plaid + Labour C&S (55 seats) and Plaid + Labour + Greens (60 seats). The Reform + Conservative zone reaches only 38 — below the 49-seat threshold. The all-party yellow zone is empty: there is no policy all five parties would simultaneously accept.

04

Borda count on deals

When the full winset is empty, Borda count asks: which deal minimises aggregate dissatisfaction? Each party ranks five possible arrangements. The Borda winner accumulates points from every party's ranking — it wins not by being anyone's favourite, but by being tolerable to the most parties. The Reform + Conservative right bloc scores high with those two parties but zero with the others, sinking its total. Plaid minority + Labour C&S collects points from every relevant party.

Three predictions from veto player theory
Prediction 1 — empty all-party winset

With five parties distributed across the full ideological spectrum, no single policy exists that all parties prefer to the status quo of no government. The all-party winset is empty. This is confirmed in the diagram: the yellow zone does not appear.

Prediction 2 — kingmaker disproportionality

Labour holds only 12.5% of seats but is the decisive veto player because its indifference circle is the only one that connects the Plaid-led left bloc to a working majority. Tsebelis predicts that a player whose removal eliminates the winset holds disproportionate bargaining power regardless of seat share.

Prediction 3 — conservative deals

With a small winset, any deal that emerges must be close to the status quo — modest, incremental, heavily constrained by the most cautious player's indifference curve. Expect a Plaid programme for government with significant policy dilution: independence referendum deferred, fiscal ambitions trimmed to what Welsh Labour can accept.

Theoretical verdict

The 2026 Senedd result is a near-perfect illustration of Tsebelis's core theorem: policy change requires a non-empty winset, and the winset shrinks as the number of veto players and the distance between their ideal points increases. With five parties, two ideologically opposed blocs, and a proportional system that denies any single party a majority, the result is a structurally constrained government that will struggle to deliver its most ambitious policies — not because of political cowardice, but because the geometry of the policy space makes radicalism impossible.

A note on Borda count limitations

The Borda count identifies the consensus deal but carries three known weaknesses in this context:

Manipulability

Any party knowing the rankings can bury a rival deal by ranking it last, shifting the aggregate result. The Gibbard–Satterthwaite theorem proves this is unavoidable.

Irrelevant alternatives

Adding a decoy deal that cannot win changes which real deal wins. A government controlling the agenda can introduce options to shift the Borda scores of deals it dislikes.

Intensity blindness

Borda treats all rank steps as equal. Labour might be barely indifferent between its 1st and 2nd choice, but Reform might find its 2nd choice genuinely catastrophic. The count cannot see this.
