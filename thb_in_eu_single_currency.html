Logical Arguments

Let me say at the outset what this debate is and is not about. It is not about Britain’s identity. It is not about whether we are European or British. It is about whether British businesses, British workers, and British consumers would be better off in a currency union with our largest trading partners than permanently outside it. I believe they would. And I want to explain why I believe the economic arguments — carefully assessed — point clearly in that direction, even if they have not yet passed every one of Gordon’s tests.

Britain’s trade with the European Union accounts for roughly 55 percent of our total trade. Every day, tens of thousands of transactions are conducted across the Channel in two currencies, with all the costs and risks that entails. Currency hedging is not free. Exchange rate uncertainty is not costless. The volatility of sterling against the deutschmark and then the euro — with swings of 20, 30, even 40 percent over a decade — has created real uncertainty for British manufacturers trying to plan investment over anything longer than a two-year horizon. These are not abstract costs. They are the daily reality of running a business in Britain.

i. the trade and investment case: the city test was always positive

City test passed both years — financial services gains from euro membership; trade gains from elimination of transaction costs and exchange rate riskGordon Brown’s Treasury assessed five tests. On the most commercially important one — whether membership would benefit the City of London and Britain’s financial services sector — the verdict was positive in both 1997 and 2003. This is highly significant, because it is the sector in which Britain has the greatest comparative advantage in Europe, the sector that employs directly and indirectly the most high-value workers, and the sector that contributes most to our balance of payments. Euro membership would make London the natural settlement and trading centre for the eurozone’s entire capital market. Exclusion from the zone means that Frankfurt and Paris have a permanent structural argument for regulatory and settlement functions to be located on the Continent rather than in a non-euro jurisdiction. The irony of the anti-euro position in the City is that the City’s long-run interest in remaining Europe’s dominant financial centre is better served by membership than exclusion.

ii. exchange rate volatility and manufacturing: the hidden cost of staying out

Sterling volatility — pound overvalued in late 1990s; damaged UK manufacturing; fixed rate would eliminate this recurring pathologyThe opponents of euro membership consistently understate the cost of exchange rate volatility. Britain’s manufacturing sector was devastated in the late 1990s and early 2000s by a pound that was, by most purchasing power parity measures, substantially overvalued. The pound was roughly 20 percent overvalued against the euro through 1999–2003. British exporters faced a choice between accepting lower sterling margins and losing market share. Many chose the latter, permanently. If Britain had entered the euro at a properly assessed rate — which would have required care and the preparation of the economy — it would have locked in a competitive rate and insulated its tradeable sector from the kind of sustained overvaluation that has contributed to the long-run decline of British manufacturing. This is a real cost of staying out, and it is rarely quantified.

iii. convergence was increasing and the trajectory was positive

Convergence trajectory — UK business cycle converging with eurozone through late 1990s; 2003 assessment shows near miss not fundamental failureThe 2003 Treasury assessment is consistently described as a failure. It should be described as a near miss. Brown himself said that Britain had made “significant progress” on convergence since 1997. The outright failures of 1997 had become near misses by 2003. On the convergence test specifically — whether business cycles were sufficiently aligned that a common interest rate could be sustained — the correlation between UK and eurozone business cycles had risen substantially. The IMF and NIESR both produced estimates showing convergence trending upward. To characterise this as a stable, permanent failure is to freeze a snapshot into a verdict. The right question was not “have we converged?” but “are we converging, and at what pace?” On that question, the evidence was positive.

iv. the political economy argument: who governs the euro?

Political economy — outside euro, UK cannot shape ECB mandate, SGP rules, or eurozone fiscal architecture; exclusion is influence lossThere is a political economy dimension to this debate that pure economic analysis misses. The rules of the eurozone — the Stability and Growth Pact, the ECB mandate, the fiscal framework — are not set in stone. They are the product of ongoing political negotiation among member states. Britain, inside the zone, would have a seat at that negotiating table. Gordon Brown, with his formidable economic authority, would be one of the architects of whatever reformed fiscal framework emerged from the evident problems of the Growth Pact in its 2003 form. Outside the zone, Britain can lobby and argue, but it cannot decide. The nations that shaped the rules of the euro shaped the framework within which the largest economy in the world — the European economy — operates. That is not a seat at the table that a serious trading nation should willingly vacate.

v. the counterfactual: what does permanent exclusion actually mean?

Permanent exclusion — trade discrimination; regulatory divergence; eurosceptic ratchet; path to exit from EU itselfI want to close with the counterfactual that is almost never discussed. What does permanent exclusion from the euro mean in practice? It means that Britain’s largest trading partners share a currency from which we are excluded. It means that as the eurozone deepens its integration — as common fiscal rules, common banking supervision, and eventually common bonds develop — Britain will find itself increasingly outside the core institutional architecture of the European economy. It means that the pressure for full withdrawal from the EU itself becomes harder to resist, because a Britain that is neither in the euro nor meaningfully shaping European institutions is a Britain that can be argued to have no real stake in European membership. I believe — and I believe history will confirm this — that the road from euro exclusion to European exit is shorter than people imagine. And I believe Britain would regret both journeys.

I yield the floor.

2.Exclusion from the eurozone gives Frankfurt and Paris a structural regulatory argument for locating euro-denominated clearing, settlement, and primary issuance functions outside a non-member jurisdiction.
3.Financial services account for a greater share of UK GDP and high-value employment than any comparable economy; the sector in which Britain has greatest comparative advantage in Europe is the sector for which the Treasury's own assessment favours entry.

Argument II — Sterling's structural overvaluation in 1999–2003 inflicted real manufacturing costs that the euro would have prevented
1.Sterling was approximately 20% overvalued against the euro on purchasing power parity measures through 1999–2003, making British exports structurally uncompetitive throughout the first years of the eurozone's existence.
2.Entry into the euro at a properly assessed competitive rate would have locked in a sustainable exchange rate and ended the recurring cycle of pound overvaluation and manufacturing contraction that has characterised the British economy since the 1980s.
3.Exchange rate volatility and misalignment are real economic costs that the opponents of euro membership systematically fail to quantify; they appear on no balance sheet but they accumulate in the secular decline of British tradeable sectors.
∴The exchange rate cost of staying out is substantial and cumulative; the euro's elimination of sterling volatility and misalignment against Britain's main trading partners would constitute a permanent structural improvement for British manufacturing.

1.Brown's 2003 assessment showed "significant progress" since 1997; outright failures had become near misses; UK-eurozone business cycle correlation had risen substantially over the period.
2.The right question was not whether convergence was complete in 2003 but whether the trajectory was positive and the pace of convergence sufficient to justify entry on a planned timeline.
3.NIESR and IMF estimates showed convergence trending upward consistently; treating a near miss on a positive trend as a permanent structural failure overstates the case against entry.
∴The convergence test failure was a temporal judgment on a positive trajectory, not a structural verdict; the appropriate response was a planned entry timetable, not indefinite exclusion.
Argument IV — Outside the euro, Britain cannot shape its rules; exclusion is a permanent loss of economic governance influence
1.The Stability and Growth Pact, ECB mandate, and eurozone fiscal architecture are set by member states; Britain outside cannot determine these rules but must adapt to their indirect consequences as Europe's largest non-member economy.
2.Brown's economic authority, deployed inside the eurozone, could have shaped the fiscal framework toward the more flexible, growth-oriented rules he was already arguing for from the outside.
3.A trading nation that routes 55% of its exports through economies governed by rules it had no part in designing is not sovereign — it is merely unrepresented.
∴Euro exclusion is not sovereignty preserved but governance influence surrendered; Britain accepted subjection to eurozone economic rules without the compensating power to shape them.
Argument V — Permanent euro exclusion is the path from European marginality to European exit
1.As the eurozone deepens — common fiscal rules, banking union, common bonds — a Britain outside accumulates an institutional distance from the EU's core that makes a meaningful case for EU membership itself progressively harder to make.
2.The eurosceptic ratchet operates: each deepening of eurozone integration that Britain does not join becomes a further argument that Britain has no real stake in European membership.
3.The road from euro exclusion to EU exit is shorter than advocates of staying out acknowledge; the 2016 Brexit vote and subsequent events are consistent with this trajectory.
∴The political economy of permanent euro exclusion leads toward EU exit itself; this long-run trajectory should weigh heavily in the assessment of the decision, and it was systematically underweighted in 2003.

I published my theory of optimum currency areas in 1961. It has since been used by opponents of British euro membership as their primary theoretical weapon. I want to explain why this use of my theory is a misreading, and why the framework I developed in my less-known 1973 work points decisively in the opposite direction.

In 1961 I argued that the optimal size of a currency union is determined primarily by the mobility of factors of production, particularly labour. If workers can move freely from depressed regions to prosperous ones, an asymmetric shock — a recession in one part of the union and a boom in another — can be absorbed without needing an exchange rate adjustment. Britain’s opponents cite this argument correctly. But they stop reading my work in 1961.

The two Mundell models — and why they point in opposite directions.

Mundell I (1961, Stationary Expectations): An asymmetric shock hits region A but not region B. If workers cannot move from A to B, B’s currency must appreciate relative to A’s to restore equilibrium. Britain’s low labour mobility relative to the US makes the euro costly under this model.

Mundell II (1973, International Risk Sharing): If A and B share a currency and their residents hold diversified cross-border asset portfolios, an adverse shock to A is partially absorbed through the capital income channel — residents of A who hold claims on B's output continue to receive income even as A's production falls. The currency union's common capital market is itself an insurance mechanism. Britain's deep financial markets make it an ideal participant in this insurance mechanism.
i. why mundell ii changes the analysis for britain

Mundell II — financial integration as shock absorber; replaces labour mobility; UK financial depth ideal for euro risk-sharingThe key insight of my 1973 work is that a well-integrated capital market within a currency union provides a risk-sharing mechanism that can substitute for labour mobility. When residents of country A hold claims on country B’s output — through equities, bonds, and other financial claims — an adverse productivity shock to A reduces A’s output but not A’s income by the full amount, because A continues to receive dividend and interest income from its claims on B. This is consumption smoothing through financial diversification rather than factor mobility. The larger and deeper the common financial market, the more effective this insurance mechanism is. Britain has the deepest, most liquid, most internationally connected financial market in Europe. Under the Mundell II framework, Britain is not a marginal candidate for currency union membership — it is the ideal one.

ii. the asymmetric shock argument re-examined

Asymmetric shocks overstated — UK-eurozone shock correlation rising; financial integration reduces the cost of asymmetric shocks even when they occurCritics of British euro membership argue that the UK faces asymmetric shocks that the eurozone does not, principally because of its oil sector, its housing market’s sensitivity to interest rates, and its financial sector’s exposure to dollar-area business cycles. These are real differences. But two responses are needed. First, the correlation of business cycles between Britain and the eurozone increased substantially through the 1990s as trade integration deepened. Artis and Zhang (2001) show that UK cycles were significantly more correlated with eurozone cycles than with US cycles by the late 1990s. Second, under Mundell II, the cost of an asymmetric shock is reduced by financial integration. If British residents hold diversified euro-area assets and eurozone residents hold British assets, an adverse shock to Britain is partially insured through capital income flows. The asymmetric shock argument, even on its own terms, is weaker than it appears.

iii. labour mobility: not as different as critics claim

Labour mobility — US vs UK comparison overstated; UK internal mobility not obviously higher; EU free movement provides some substituteThe standard comparison between the eurozone and the United States holds that the US works as an OCA partly because American workers move between states in response to local recessions in a way European workers do not move between countries. This is true. But the comparison flatters the US and understates Europe. UK internal labour mobility between regions is not obviously higher than EU cross-border mobility once language barriers are controlled for — British workers are not notably more willing to move from the North East to the South East than French workers are to move to Germany. EU free movement of labour, extended to the UK as a member, provides a partial substitute for the kind of US interstate mobility that makes the American OCA work. The mobility argument for not joining is less decisive than its proponents claim.

iv. the exchange rate as shock absorber: theory vs evidence

Exchange rate as adjuster — theory says floating rate absorbs shocks; evidence suggests sterling primarily amplifies them via financial marketsThe classic case for maintaining an independent currency is that the exchange rate functions as a shock absorber: when Britain is hit by an adverse demand shock, a depreciation of sterling boosts export competitiveness and helps restore full employment. This logic is sound in theory. But it requires that exchange rate movements reflect and respond to real economic fundamentals, and that they do so promptly and in the right direction. Britain’s experience with floating sterling has not been this. Sterling in the late 1980s was overvalued relative to fundamentals, amplifying the boom. It collapsed in 1992 — below fundamental value — amplifying the subsequent recovery but also triggering the ERM crisis. It was overvalued again in 1999–2003, damaging manufacturing. The exchange rate, in practice, has functioned for Britain more as a source of volatility than as a tool of macroeconomic stabilisation. Giving it up would cost less than the theory of floating exchange rates suggests.

My theory has been misread. Properly understood, it argues for British membership. I rest my case.

Argument I — Mundell II: financial integration substitutes for labour mobility as an OCA criterion; Britain is ideal under this framework
1.Mundell (1973): when residents of currency union members hold cross-border financial claims, adverse shocks are partially insured through capital income flows — consuming from diversified asset portfolios smooths income even when output falls.
2.The effectiveness of this insurance mechanism increases with the depth and liquidity of the common financial market; Britain's financial sector is the deepest and most internationally connected in Europe.
3.Under Mundell II, Britain is the ideal candidate for euro membership, not a marginal one: its financial depth would make it the primary provider of cross-border risk-sharing services within the enlarged currency union.
∴The Mundell II framework, which is more relevant to financially integrated modern economies than the 1961 labour-mobility model, provides a strong argument for British euro membership based on Britain's unique financial depth.
Argument II — Asymmetric shocks are overstated; UK-eurozone cycle correlation was rising; financial integration reduces their cost
1.Artis and Zhang (2001) show that UK business cycles were more correlated with eurozone cycles than with US cycles by the late 1990s; the structural divergence cited by Brown was diminishing on a positive trajectory.
2.Under Mundell II, even genuine asymmetric shocks cost less in a currency union with deep financial integration, because income is partially insured through cross-border asset portfolios.
3.The asymmetric shock argument against entry was deployed as though the exchange rate is a perfectly efficient absorber of such shocks; it is not — sterling's historical volatility shows it amplifies rather than absorbs macroeconomic disturbances.
∴The asymmetric shock objection to British entry is weakened both by the evidence of rising cycle correlation and by Mundell II's demonstration that financial integration provides a superior alternative insurance mechanism.
Argument III — The US–eurozone labour mobility comparison overstates the case against entry
1.US interstate labour mobility is higher than EU cross-border mobility, but UK internal regional mobility is not obviously higher than EU cross-border mobility; the within-UK labour market is not the frictionless interregional market the comparison implies.
2.EU free movement of labour provides a partial substitute for the US model; and Mundell II's financial integration insurance reduces the cost of whatever labour immobility remains.
3.The mobility criterion, even on Mundell I, is necessary only insofar as it cannot be substituted by other adjustment mechanisms; Mundell II provides exactly such a substitute.
∴The labour mobility objection is less decisive than its proponents claim; UK internal mobility is not obviously superior to EU cross-border mobility, and Mundell II's financial integration mechanism reduces the cost of immobility regardless.
Argument IV — In practice, floating sterling has amplified UK economic shocks rather than absorbing them
1.Theory of floating exchange rates: the exchange rate adjusts promptly to absorb real shocks. British experience: sterling was overvalued in the late 1980s (amplifying boom), collapsed in 1992 (amplifying uncertainty), and was overvalued again in 1999–2003 (damaging manufacturing).
2.Financial market dynamics — carry trades, overshooting, speculative pressure — drive sterling movements that have little relation to macroeconomic fundamentals and function primarily as a source of volatility, not a stabiliser.
3.The empirical cost of surrendering the exchange rate instrument is therefore substantially below its theoretical value; Britain's floating exchange rate record suggests it is more curse than tool.
∴The exchange rate argument against euro entry assumes sterling's floating history has been stabilising; the historical record shows the opposite; this substantially reduces the practical cost of euro membership.

The most sophisticated argument against British euro membership goes like this: Britain’s business cycles are not sufficiently synchronised with the eurozone, its economy is not sufficiently integrated with the eurozone, and therefore the costs of sharing a common monetary policy are too high. This argument has one fatal flaw. It treats the OCA criteria as exogenous — as fixed structural properties of the British economy that the decision to join or not join leaves unchanged. They are not. They are endogenous. The act of joining changes them.

i. the frankel-rose endogeneity theorem

OCA endogeneity — joining creates trade; trade creates convergence; convergence satisfies OCA ex post; static assessment is wrongAndrew Rose and I published our key paper in the Economic Journal in 1998. The central result, which became known as the Rose Effect: countries that share a currency trade roughly three times as much with each other as otherwise comparable countries that do not. The gravity model of trade has been used for decades to predict bilateral trade flows as a function of economic size, distance, and trade barriers. When we added a currency union dummy to the gravity equation, it was enormous and highly significant. Countries sharing a currency — controlling for everything else — trade roughly 200 percent more with each other. This is a colossal effect.

Trade creates synchronisation — more trade means more correlated business cycles; joining improves OCA fit ex post even if not met ex anteThe second result is equally important. More bilateral trade is associated with more correlated business cycles. This is the mechanism linking the currency union to OCA criteria: currency union → more trade → more synchronised cycles → better fit with OCA convergence criterion. We showed empirically that a country that joins a currency union, and thereby increases its trade with the zone, should expect its business cycle to become more correlated with the zone over time. This is precisely what happened to the original eurozone members: their cycles converged further after 1999 than before. Britain, by staying out, preserved its cycle divergence — divergence that was itself partly a consequence of staying out.

ii. the self-fulfilling divergence of staying out

Self-fulfilling divergence — UK stays out → less trade with eurozone → less cycle synchronisation → fails OCA tests → stays out; a trapThere is a deeply troubling circularity in the Treasury’s five-test assessment. Britain fails the convergence test, in part, because its trade with the eurozone is conducted in two currencies rather than one — which suppresses the trade intensity that drives cycle synchronisation. Britain stays out because it fails the convergence test. Britain fails the convergence test in part because it stays out. This is not a stable equilibrium in any meaningful sense. It is a self-fulfilling divergence trap: the very act of waiting for convergence before joining prevents the convergence that joining would produce. The appropriate response to failing the OCA criteria is not to wait until they are met exogenously — they will not be met exogenously — but to join and thereby satisfy them endogenously.

iii. the rose effect and uk-specific estimates

UK-specific estimates — Frankel’s 2003 HM Treasury submission estimates 50% trade boost for UK from euro; investment boost on topI submitted to HM Treasury in 2003, at their invitation, my estimates of the trade effect of euro membership for the United Kingdom specifically. Using the gravity model with the panel estimates of the currency union effect, I found that UK trade with the eurozone could be expected to increase by approximately 50 percent over a decade following entry. Applied to UK-eurozone trade running at roughly £300 billion a year, this implies an increase of around £150 billion over ten years. Even if the Rose Effect is substantially overstated — and the most careful panel estimates reduce it from tripling to doubling — the trade gain from euro entry for an economy as trade-dependent as Britain is large relative to any plausible estimate of the macroeconomic stabilisation costs.

iv. why the stabilisation cost comparison must include the full trade counterfactual

Cost-benefit comparison — Treasury did not include Rose Effect trade gains in its cost-benefit; opponents undercount benefits; net assessment is positiveThe Treasury’s five-test assessment concentrated its analysis on the cost side: the risk of an ill-fitting common interest rate given insufficient convergence. It gave comparatively little weight to the benefit side: the trade and investment gains from currency union membership. But a complete cost-benefit analysis must compare the full expected cost — the risk of suboptimal monetary policy in an asymmetric shock — with the full expected benefit — the permanent reduction in transaction costs and exchange rate risk, plus the trade and investment gains from the Rose Effect. When these are properly set against each other, the net assessment is positive for an economy with Britain’s characteristics. The convergence test, however important, is not the only test that matters.

Staying out was not a neutral choice. It was a choice to remain in the divergence trap. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Rose Effect: currency union membership triples bilateral trade; this is the primary benefit of euro entry
1.Rose (2000): gravity model with currency union dummy finds countries sharing a currency trade approximately three times as much with each other as otherwise comparable non-union pairs.
2.Even the most conservative panel estimates (Baldwin 2006) find 5–10% trade increases attributable to currency union; applied to UK-eurozone trade of £300bn+/year, this represents £15–30bn in permanent annual trade gains.
3.The Treasury's five-test assessment gave insufficient weight to these trade gains relative to the monetary stabilisation costs of suboptimal interest rates; a complete cost-benefit analysis favours entry.
∴The Rose Effect trade gain is the primary economic benefit of euro membership; the Treasury's assessment systematically underweighted it relative to the monetary stabilisation costs that formed the basis of the failed convergence test.
Argument II — OCA criteria are endogenous: currency union membership produces the trade integration that satisfies OCA criteria
1.Frankel and Rose (1998): OCA criteria — trade integration and business cycle synchronisation — are not exogenous structural properties but are themselves produced by currency union membership through the trade channel.
2.More bilateral trade (produced by currency union) → more correlated business cycles → better fit with the OCA convergence criterion. Countries satisfying OCA criteria ex post need not satisfy them ex ante.
3.The original eurozone members' cycles converged further after 1999 than before; Britain's cycles diverged relative to the eurozone through the same period, consistent with staying out suppressing the trade channel.
∴OCA endogeneity means the Treasury's static OCA assessment in 2003 was conceptually flawed: it evaluated whether Britain already satisfied the criteria, not whether joining would cause Britain to satisfy them; on the endogenous assessment, the case for entry is much stronger.
Argument III — Staying out is a self-fulfilling divergence trap; the five-test framework perpetuates the condition it measures
1.Britain's cycle divergence from the eurozone in 2003 was partly a consequence of conducting trade with the eurozone in two currencies — suppressing the trade intensity that drives synchronisation.
2.The five-test framework conditions entry on achieving convergence, but staying out prevents the convergence that entry would produce; this is a logical trap, not a prudential framework.
3.The appropriate decision rule is not "join when converged" but "join and thereby converge"; the endogeneity result transforms the conditional from a prudential gate into a self-defeating obstacle.
∴The five-test framework is conceptually inconsistent with the endogeneity of OCA criteria; it requires Britain to pass tests that can only be passed by joining, creating a permanent barrier that the framework itself cannot exit.
Argument IV — UK-specific trade gain estimates from euro entry are large relative to stabilisation costs even on conservative assumptions
1.Frankel's 2003 HM Treasury submission estimates a 50% increase in UK-eurozone trade over a decade following entry; even at one-third of this estimate, the permanent annual trade gain exceeds £15bn.
2.The stabilisation cost of suboptimal monetary policy — the expected loss from having the wrong interest rate in an asymmetric shock — depends on shock frequency and severity, both of which are uncertain and probably declining as convergence increases.
3.On reasonable estimates of both sides, trade and investment gains exceed expected stabilisation costs; the Treasury's assessment did not perform this complete cost-benefit comparison.
∴When trade and investment gains are properly included in the cost-benefit assessment, the economic case for British euro entry is positive even on conservative estimates of the Rose Effect and pessimistic estimates of convergence.

I want to offer what I hope is the most measured and empirically grounded assessment on this side of the debate. I am not a committed European federalist. I am an economist who has spent the better part of a decade trying to assess, carefully and without prior commitment, whether the balance of evidence favours British euro membership. My conclusion, published in the National Institute Economic Review in 2000, is that it does — with important qualifications, and not overwhelmingly, but positively.

Let me work through the evidence category by category, because the debate has been bedevilled by selective quotation of particular data points in support of pre-formed positions.

Cycle correlation — UK-eurozone synchronisation overtook UK-US by late 1990s; trajectory strongly positive; convergence test was borderline, not fundamental failureThis table is the most important piece of evidence in the entire debate, and it is the evidence that Brown’s Treasury most consistently downplayed. By the late 1990s, UK business cycles were more correlated with German cycles than with US cycles — the first time this had been true since before Bretton Woods. The trajectory was unambiguously positive. This does not mean convergence was complete in 2003. It means that the 2003 assessment was a borderline near miss on a strongly positive trend, and that the appropriate inference was “entry on a planned timeline of two to three years” rather than “indefinite exclusion.”

ii. structural differences: real, but manageable

UK structural differences — housing market, oil, financial sector; real but smaller than claimed; other eurozone members face similar divergencesThe Treasury’s 2003 assessment cited three structural differences between Britain and the eurozone as the basis for its convergence failure: the UK housing market’s sensitivity to interest rates (due to higher variable-rate mortgage prevalence), North Sea oil, and the City’s financial-sector exposure to dollar-area cycles. These are real. But they need to be calibrated against the structural differences that exist between existing eurozone members. The Irish housing market was far more sensitive to the ECB’s interest rates than Britain’s would have been. Spanish construction was more exposed to rate cycles than British manufacturing. The existing eurozone contains economies that diverge from each other substantially more than Britain diverges from the core. The “too different to join” argument, applied consistently, would have excluded Ireland, Spain, and Portugal from the zone — none of which the proponents of that argument ever suggested.

iii. the costs of isolation: trade discrimination and speculative risk

Isolation costs — trade discrimination risk; regulatory divergence; speculative attack on sterling outside euro; these costs not in Treasury assessmentThe Treasury’s five tests focused almost entirely on the cost of joining. They gave insufficient weight to the cost of not joining. There are at least three such costs that the Treasury underweighted. First, trade discrimination: as eurozone members deepen their integration, non-members face increasing regulatory and administrative barriers to trade that are formally legal under EU rules but practically significant. Second, regulatory divergence: an increasingly integrated eurozone will develop common financial regulations in which non-members have no vote but by which, as participants in the European single market, they are substantially constrained. Third, speculative risk: sterling outside the eurozone is permanently subject to speculative attack of the kind that destroyed the ERM in 1992. These costs are not easily quantified, but they are real and cumulative.

iv. the verdict: not overwhelming, but positive

Balanced verdict — costs of joining modest given convergence trajectory; benefits real; isolation costs underweighted; balance of evidence positive for entry on planned timelineMy 2000 assessment, and my reassessment in the light of the 2003 Treasury report, reaches a conclusion that I want to state with appropriate precision. The economic case for British euro membership is not overwhelming. There are genuine costs, principally the loss of monetary autonomy at a moment when cycles are not perfectly synchronised. But the costs are manageable and declining as convergence increases. I want to make clear at the outset that neither Ed Balls nor I am opposed to British euro membership in principle. We are committed to it in principle, subject to our assessment of the economic conditions. What we are opposed to is membership on the wrong terms, at the wrong time, without the structural conditions that would make membership sustainable and beneficial for British workers and businesses. We designed the five tests precisely to prevent a repetition of the ERM debacle — Britain’s most catastrophic post-war monetary policy error — in which we entered a fixed exchange rate arrangement at the wrong rate and were forced out within two years at enormous economic and political cost.

The single most important structural difference between Britain and the continental eurozone economies is the housing market and its interaction with monetary policy. This is not a minor technical point. It is the core of the case against premature entry.

The UK housing market: why it makes a common interest rate genuinely costly.

In continental Europe, the majority of mortgages are fixed-rate for long periods (10–25 years in Germany and France). A change in the ECB interest rate has a limited, slow effect on household mortgage payments and therefore on consumer spending.

In Britain in 2000, approximately 70% of mortgages were variable-rate or short-term fixed (1–3 years). A 1 percentage point change in the Bank of England rate translated almost immediately into changed monthly mortgage payments for roughly 70% of homeowners.

This means the monetary policy transmission mechanism operates approximately 3–4 times faster and more powerfully in Britain than in Germany or France. A common ECB interest rate set appropriately for French and German conditions could be catastrophically wrong for Britain.

You enter without convergence: the common interest rate is wrong for Britain for years. You enter without flexibility: when a shock hits, labour markets and wages cannot adjust because they are insufficiently flexible. You enter without investment confidence: the business investment boost that should follow never materialises because the macroeconomic framework is not credible. iv. the entry rate problem: no good rate available in 2003

I want to make the case against British euro membership on the most fundamental theoretical grounds available. The euro is not merely a suboptimal arrangement for Britain in current circumstances. It is a fundamentally misconceived monetary framework that will eventually destroy itself in the absence of fiscal union, and Britain should not be inside it when that happens.

The theoretical case rests on Mundell’s 1961 analysis, which I believe is the correct framework despite what Mundell himself now argues about his 1973 work. A currency area without labour mobility and without fiscal transfers cannot sustainably absorb asymmetric shocks. It must either tolerate persistent regional unemployment and deflation, or it must evolve toward fiscal union. The eurozone in 2003 had neither sufficient labour mobility nor a fiscal transfer mechanism. The Stability and Growth Pact actually made things worse by imposing procyclical fiscal rules on member states that were already suffering from the loss of monetary autonomy.

i. the asymmetric shock mechanism

Asymmetric shocks — no fiscal transfers in eurozone; no exchange rate adjustment; only deflation or unemployment as adjustment mechanism; unsustainableLet me trace through the mechanism with precision. Country A is a eurozone member. Country A is hit by an adverse demand shock — say, a collapse in export demand for its products. Under a flexible exchange rate, A’s currency would depreciate, making its exports cheaper and stimulating demand. Under the euro, this mechanism is unavailable. What happens instead? Prices and wages in A must fall relative to the rest of the zone — internal devaluation. But wages and prices are sticky downward. The adjustment therefore occurs through unemployment: workers lose jobs, incomes fall, consumer spending contracts, and the economy depresses until relative wages have fallen enough to restore competitiveness. This is a painful, slow, and socially destructive adjustment process. In the United States, it is cushioned by automatic federal fiscal transfers — unemployment insurance, healthcare spending — that flow from thriving states to depressed ones. In the eurozone, no such mechanism exists. The depressed country must manage alone, with fiscal hands tied by the SGP.

ii. the stability and growth pact: procyclical fiscal folly

The purpose is to prevent free-riding on the zone’s collective monetary credibility through excessive national debt. But the mechanism is procyclical in a way that compounds the asymmetric shock problem catastrophically. When a member state is hit by an adverse shock and enters recession, its tax revenues automatically fall and its welfare spending automatically rises — the automatic stabilisers that normally cushion a recession. Under the SGP, these automatic stabilisers push the deficit toward or through the 3 percent limit, triggering compliance pressure precisely when the economy needs fiscal expansion most. Brown was right to object to the SGP from the outside. The right conclusion from this objection was not that Britain should join and try to reform the pact from inside — it was that the pact revealed the fundamental architectural flaw in the eurozone that makes it unsuitable as a monetary union without fiscal union.

iii. the britain-specific monetary policy record: why surrender it

Inflation remained within target. Unemployment fell from 10 percent to 5 percent. GDP growth was stable and above trend. The Bank could set rates for UK-specific conditions — responding to the housing market, to the oil price, to dollar-area financial conditions in ways directly relevant to Britain. The ECB, by contrast, sets rates for the eurozone aggregate. The Taylor Rule — the standard prescriptive rule for monetary policy — prescribed interest rates for Britain that were consistently different from ECB rates through the 2000s: sometimes 100, sometimes 200 basis points different. These are not trivial differences. They represent the macroeconomic stabilisation loss that Blair and Frankel want to dismiss as manageable. They are not manageable. They are the mechanism by which the Irish crisis was created.

iv. the endogeneity argument does not rescue the pro-entry case

Against Frankel — endogeneity means joining increases specialisation not just trade; increased specialisation means MORE asymmetric shocks not fewer; Krugman counter-argumentFrankel and Rose argue that joining the euro would increase trade integration and thereby synchronise business cycles, so that the OCA criteria would be satisfied ex post. This endogeneity argument sounds compelling but Krugman identified its fatal flaw in his 1993 contribution: increased trade integration within a currency union tends to increase industrial specialisation, not reduce it. If Britain trades more with Germany inside the euro, both countries will specialise further in their comparative advantage industries — Germany in high-precision manufacturing, Britain in financial services. More specialisation means more asymmetric shocks, not fewer. The Frankel-Rose endogeneity result assumes that increased trade leads to more synchronised cycles through demand linkages; the Krugman counter-argument is that it leads to less synchronised cycles through supply-side specialisation. The empirical evidence from the eurozone itself does not clearly support the Frankel-Rose prediction: shock correlation has not increased as dramatically as they projected.

The euro is an experiment in monetary economics whose outcome was predictable from first principles. Britain should not join the experiment at this stage of its development. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Without fiscal transfers and with low labour mobility, asymmetric shocks in the eurozone are absorbed through unemployment and deflation

∴The eurozone lacks both the exchange rate mechanism and the fiscal transfer mechanism that make currency unions sustainable under asymmetric shocks; this architectural flaw makes it an unsuitable monetary union for Britain, which faces identifiably asymmetric shocks relative to the eurozone core.

I come to this debate from an unusual position. I am not a eurosceptic. I am not opposed to European integration. I am opposed to the eurozone as constructed in the Maastricht Treaty and as it operated in 2003, because its fiscal architecture creates the very sovereign debt crises it was designed to prevent. And I believe Britain should not join a monetary union whose fundamental design flaw will eventually force either a disorderly fiscal integration under crisis conditions, or a disorderly partial breakup.

Let me explain the architecture problem with precision, because it is the argument that the proponents of entry least want to engage with.

The fundamental fiscal architecture problem of monetary union without fiscal union.

In the United States, states borrow in dollars. The federal government can always create dollars. If a state faces a solvency crisis, there is an implicit (and sometimes explicit) federal backstop — the sovereign borrowing in the federal currency cannot, in the end, be forced to default in the way a borrower in a foreign currency can.

In the eurozone, member states borrow in euros. But they do not control the euro. The ECB is prohibited from monetary financing of member state deficits (Article 123 TFEU). A member state facing a fiscal crisis is in exactly the position of a country that has borrowed in a foreign currency — it cannot print its way out, and it has no automatic fiscal backstop from the centre.

This is the configuration that produces sovereign debt crises. It is not a contingent problem that might or might not arise. It is structural, and it was built into the Maastricht architecture from the beginning.
i. the lender of last resort problem

Lender of last resort absent — no ECB monetisation of member state debt; member states borrow in currency they don’t control; sovereign debt crises structural not contingentEvery central bank in a national monetary system performs two related functions: it is the lender of last resort for the banking system — it provides emergency liquidity to solvent but illiquid banks — and it is, in the last analysis, the backstop for the sovereign debt of the state whose currency it manages. The Federal Reserve performs both functions for the United States. The Bank of England performs both for the United Kingdom. The ECB performs the first for the European banking system — imperfectly and after considerable political controversy — but it does not, as of 2003, perform the second. It is explicitly prohibited from monetary financing of member state deficits. This creates a structural gap: if a eurozone member state faces a sovereign debt crisis — if markets lose confidence in its ability to service its debt — there is no automatic backstop. The state must deflate, restructure, or seek politically conditional bailout from other members. This is not a theoretical risk. It is the exact configuration that produced the Greek, Irish, Portuguese, and Spanish crises of 2010–2012. And it was entirely predictable from the Maastricht architecture.

ii. why the SGP cannot substitute for fiscal union

SGP cannot substitute for fiscal union — pact is rule-based, not insurance-based; it prevents deficits in recessions but provides no transfers; not functionally equivalent to fiscal unionThe architects of the Maastricht system argued that the Stability and Growth Pact would prevent the fiscal crises that the absence of a lender of last resort would otherwise produce. This argument fails on two levels. First, as Minford has noted, the SGP is procyclical: it restricts deficit spending precisely when economies most need it. Second, and more fundamentally, the SGP is a rule that restricts behaviour; it is not a transfer mechanism that provides insurance. A federal fiscal union transfers resources from thriving regions to depressed ones automatically. The SGP imposes deficit limits on all members uniformly, regardless of their cyclical position. These are not functionally equivalent. The SGP can prevent fiscal profligacy. It cannot provide the stabilisation function of fiscal federalism. And without that stabilisation function, the currency union remains vulnerable to the sovereign debt crises that fiscal union would prevent.

iii. the british banking system and systemic risk

UK banking system — if UK joins euro, Bank of England loses lender of last resort capacity for UK banks; ECB not obligated to backstop; 2008-style crisis more severe inside euroThere is a further and specifically British dimension to the fiscal architecture problem. The United Kingdom has one of the largest banking systems in the world relative to the size of its economy. British banks’ total assets in 2003 exceeded UK GDP by a factor of four or five. The Bank of England, as lender of last resort, provides the ultimate backstop for this system. If Britain joins the euro, the Bank of England retains some limited lender of last resort capacity but loses the ability to conduct open-ended monetary operations in the currency in which UK banks would now be funded. The ECB has no obligation to backstop UK banks in a systemic crisis. The Northern Rock crisis of 2007 and the broader financial crisis of 2008 showed exactly how important the Bank of England’s emergency liquidity provision was for British financial stability. Inside the euro in 2008, the Bank of England could not have responded as it did.

iv. the correct conclusion: not anti-euro but anti-this-euro

Not anti-euro in principle — but the architecture as constructed in Maastricht is wrong; Britain should wait for fiscal union or ECB backstop capacity before joiningI want to be clear about what I am and am not arguing. I am not arguing that a European monetary union is inherently misconceived. I am arguing that the specific monetary union constructed under Maastricht, without fiscal union, without a credible lender of last resort for sovereigns, and with a procyclical fiscal rule that prevents the adjustment that the absence of exchange rate flexibility would otherwise require, is a flawed construction that will eventually require either full fiscal union or partial breakup to resolve. Britain’s correct position is to engage seriously with the reform of the eurozone’s fiscal architecture — demanding a genuine transfer mechanism, a European Monetary Fund, or an enhanced ECB backstop capacity as a condition of British entry. None of this was on offer in 2003, and Blair was wrong to proceed without securing it.

Don’t join the house before the foundations are laid. That is my submission.

3.The Northern Rock crisis (2007) and broader financial crisis (2008) demonstrated the essential importance of the Bank of England's emergency liquidity provision; inside the euro in 2008, this response would have been impossible.
∴British euro membership would transfer lender-of-last-resort responsibility for the world's largest-relative-to-GDP banking system to a central bank with no obligation to provide it; this is an unacceptable systemic risk for the UK financial system.
Argument IV — The correct position is not anti-euro but anti-this-euro; entry should be conditional on fiscal union or ECB backstop capacity
1.A monetary union with a genuine fiscal transfer mechanism, an ECB with sovereign backstop capacity, or a European Monetary Fund providing conditional emergency liquidity to member states would be a structurally sound monetary union worth joining.
2.None of these elements was present in the Maastricht architecture as of 2003; Blair's pro-entry position did not condition entry on securing them.
3.The appropriate position was to engage with reforming the eurozone's fiscal architecture as a condition of British entry, using Britain's bargaining power as the zone's most significant potential new member.
∴The correct anti-entry position is not that the euro is inherently misconceived but that the Maastricht architecture is structurally flawed; Britain should have made entry conditional on architectural reforms that were not on offer in 2003.

I want to address directly the economic arguments for euro entry rather than the constitutional or fiscal architecture arguments, important as those are. My case against entry rests on three propositions: that the trade gains from the Rose Effect are substantially overstated, that the Bank of England’s monetary policy record demonstrates the value of monetary autonomy, and that the interest rate that would have applied to Britain inside the euro through its main window of eligibility would have been deeply inappropriate for British macroeconomic conditions.

i. the rose effect: econometric fragility

Rose Effect fragility — selection bias; small/poor country sample; endogeneity; controls reduce estimate dramatically; Baldwin finds 5–10% not 200%Frankel and Rose’s original 2000 result — that currency union triples bilateral trade — was impressive econometrically but has not survived scrutiny. The problems are multiple and cumulative. First, selection bias: most currency unions in Rose’s original dataset involve very small, very poor countries — Pacific island states using the Australian dollar, Caribbean states using the US dollar. These are not comparable to a potential UK-euro union of large, wealthy, sophisticated economies. Second, omitted variables: countries sharing a currency often share other characteristics — colonial history, political integration, regulatory alignment — that independently promote trade; the currency union dummy partially captures these. Third, the euro-specific evidence is dramatically smaller than the Rose headline result: Micco et al. (2003), using the actual eurozone data from 1999–2003, found a trade effect of 10–20 percent, not 200 percent. Baldwin’s careful 2006 review found 5–10 percent after methodological corrections. The headline result has been revised down by a factor of 20 to 40.

Taylor Rule divergence — 100-300bps for 6+ years; this would have added fuel to UK housing boom; outcome: Ireland writ largeIf Britain had joined the euro in 1999 and faced ECB rates of 3 percent when the Taylor Rule prescribed 5 percent for UK conditions, the result would have been a credit boom far more severe than the one Britain actually experienced — and Britain’s actual boom, with the Bank of England setting appropriate rates, still ended in a significant housing-price correction. The Irish precedent is informative: Irish Taylor Rule rates in 2003 similarly diverged from ECB rates by 3–5 percentage points, and Ireland experienced an extreme credit and housing boom followed by one of the most severe financial crises in European history. Britain would have been Ireland with a financial sector ten times larger. The consequences for European financial stability would have been catastrophic.

iii. britain’s decade of stability: the value of what was at stake

UK decade of stability — inflation targeting delivered; GDP stable; unemployment halved; surrendering this for 5–10% trade gain is poor exchangeThe Bank of England’s inflation-targeting framework, formally adopted in 1997, delivered a decade of extraordinary macroeconomic stability. Inflation remained within its 2 percent target through the period. GDP growth averaged 2.7 percent per year. Unemployment fell from 7.5 to 5 percent. This was not the result of luck or of benign global conditions — the same period included the Asian financial crisis, the dot-com collapse, and the 9/11 shock. It was the result of having an independent central bank setting interest rates for UK-specific conditions with full accountability to British democratic institutions. The pro-entry camp asks Britain to exchange this demonstrably successful framework for membership in a monetary union with a one-size-fits-none interest rate, no fiscal backstop, a procyclical fiscal rule, and a trade gain that the best available evidence puts at 5 to 10 percent over a decade. This is not a trade I would make.

The numbers do not add up. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Rose Effect collapses from +200% to +5–10% when corrected for selection bias, omitted variables, and with euro-specific data
1.Rose's original sample was dominated by very small, very poor currency unions (Pacific/Caribbean states) not comparable to UK-eurozone; selection bias inflates the headline estimate dramatically.
2.Euro-specific estimates (Micco et al. 2003: 10–20%; Baldwin 2006: 5–10%; Bun and Klaassen 2002: 3% short-run) are 20–40× smaller than the Rose headline result after appropriate methodological corrections.
3.Frankel's own 2003 HM Treasury submission used estimates from the full Rose sample; applying the euro-specific corrections would have produced estimates too small to drive the cost-benefit analysis toward entry.
∴The Rose Effect is econometrically fragile and dramatically overstated when applied to large developed-economy currency unions; the trade case for British euro entry, properly estimated, is real but far too small to offset the established costs of inappropriate monetary policy.
Argument II — The Taylor Rule shows ECB rates would have been 100–300bps too low for UK conditions through 1999–2005
1.The Taylor Rule for the UK consistently prescribes rates 100–300bps above ECB rates through 1999–2005; had Britain joined the euro at launch, its monetary conditions would have been substantially too loose for its growth and housing cycle.
2.Rates 100–300bps below the Taylor Rule prescription for 6+ years would have fuelled a credit and housing boom far more severe than Britain actually experienced with appropriate Bank of England rates.
3.The Irish precedent — which experienced Taylor Rule divergences of 3–5pp and subsequently the worst financial crisis in its history — is the closest available natural experiment; Britain's financial sector is approximately 10× Ireland's relative to GDP.
∴The quantifiable cost of inappropriate ECB rates for British conditions — 100–300bps below Taylor Rule prescription for 6+ years — far exceeds the corrected trade benefits of euro membership; the macroeconomic arithmetic strongly favours retaining UK monetary autonomy.
Argument III — Britain's inflation-targeting record demonstrates the value of UK-specific monetary policy; surrendering it for small trade gains is a poor exchange
1.Bank of England inflation targeting (1997–2007): inflation within 2% target; GDP growth averaging 2.7% p.a.; unemployment falling from 7.5% to 5% — a superior macroeconomic record to the eurozone aggregate over the same period.
2.This record was achieved through UK-specific monetary policy responding to UK-specific conditions: appropriate rates for the UK housing market, labour market, and trade cycle, set by an accountable independent central bank.
3.The corrected trade gains from euro entry (5–10% of bilateral trade over a decade) do not plausibly offset the demonstrated value of UK-specific monetary policy, as evidenced by the decade of stability it produced.
∴Britain's decade of inflation-targeting success is the empirical demonstration of what was at stake; surrendering demonstrated macroeconomic stability for speculative trade gains of 5–10% over a decade is not a rational economic exchange.
Argument IV — Inside the euro in 2008, Britain's financial crisis response would have been impossible
1.The Bank of England's response to the 2008 financial crisis included emergency liquidity facilities, quantitative easing, and coordinated cuts in bank rate from 5% to 0.5% over six months — all requiring full monetary sovereignty.
2.Inside the euro, the Bank of England would have had no power to conduct quantitative easing in sterling, no ability to set UK-specific rates, and no independent capacity for emergency sterling liquidity provision to UK banks.
3.The 2008 crisis, which was centred on the UK financial system (Northern Rock, RBS, HBOS, Lloyds TSB), would therefore have required a eurozone response that the ECB had no mandate or obligation to provide.
∴Britain's crisis response in 2008 demonstrates retrospectively the essential value of UK monetary sovereignty; inside the euro, the same crisis would have been unmanageable without a degree of ECB intervention that neither the ECB's mandate nor its political conditions would have supported.
