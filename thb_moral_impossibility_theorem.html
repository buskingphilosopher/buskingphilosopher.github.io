First-Order Argument — Conceptual InconsistencyChristopher Cowie — “A New Argument for Moral Error Theory,” Noûs 56(2), 2022
Traditional error-theoretic arguments fail because they rest on contested metaphysical premises about the non-existence of categorical reasons or irreducibly normative properties. A better argument proceeds from within first-order morality itself: Arrhenius’s axiological impossibility theorems demonstrate that a small set of highly intuitive population-ethical principles are mutually inconsistent; since these principles have the status of conceptual truths about the concept of ‘goodness’ — they are constitutive of what it means to reason morally at all — their inconsistency infects the entire domain, yielding error theory not by metaphysical fiat but by the internal collapse of moral concepts.

Mr Speaker,

The error-theoretic debate has long been dominated by metaphysical arguments: there are no categorical reasons, no irreducibly normative properties, therefore no moral truths. These arguments are formidable but they face a structural disadvantage: they engage on ground that their opponents have already chosen and fortified. The naturalist simply denies that moral properties are irreducible; the relaxed realist simply denies that external metaphysical critique is the right approach; and the debate becomes an impasse of competing metaphysical commitments that will not resolve.

I offer a different kind of argument. It does not begin with metaphysics. It begins inside morality itself, with the results of first-order moral inquiry — specifically, with what I take to be the most troubling result in contemporary moral philosophy: Arrhenius’s axiological impossibility theorems.

The structure of the first-order argument.

The argument proceeds in three stages. First, there is a genuine inconsistency among highly intuitive moral propositions, demonstrated with mathematical rigour by Arrhenius. Second, these propositions have the status of conceptual truths — they are constitutive of what it is to reason morally about populations at all, in the way that “torturing innocents for fun is wrong” is constitutive of the moral concept of wrongness. Third, if concepts whose constitutive principles are mutually inconsistent are deployed in judgment, those judgments systematically fail to be true. The moral domain is in this predicament. Error theory follows — not because moral properties are metaphysically queer, but because moral concepts are internally inconsistent.
i. the master argument stated

Master argument — if the fixed-point conditions aren't all true, no moral propositions are; they aren't all true; therefore no moral propositions are trueThe formal structure of my argument is this. If the conditions in the impossibility theorem are not all true — and they cannot all be true, since they are demonstrably inconsistent — then no moral propositions are true. This conditional rests on the claim that the conditions have the status of conceptual truths about the moral domain. Inconsistent conceptual truths poison the concept they are constitutive of: just as the concept of a round square generates no truths because nothing can satisfy all its constitutive conditions, the concept of moral goodness generates no truths if its constitutive conditions are mutually contradictory. This is not scepticism — it is not the claim that we lack knowledge of moral truths. It is the stronger, error-theoretic claim that there are no moral truths to know.

ii. the fixed-points strategy — conceptual truths in ethics

Fixed points — Cuneo and Shafer-Landau; some moral truths are conceptual; Cowie uses this against realism: if they're conceptual and inconsistent, error theory followsI adopt, for the sake of argument, the moral realists’ own preferred methodology. Cuneo and Shafer-Landau have argued that some moral propositions have the status of ‘fixed points’ — substantive moral truths that are also conceptual truths, such that competent users of the relevant moral concepts are committed to them simply by virtue of understanding the concepts. They take this as a resource for moral realism. I turn it against them. The Egalitarian Dominance Condition, the Weak Non-Sadism Condition, the General Non-Extreme Priority Condition — these have all the hallmarks of fixed points. They seem obviously true to anyone who grasps them; their denials evoke bewilderment; they are knowable a priori. If fixed points can be inconsistent, then the conceptual apparatus of morality is internally defective, and error theory follows from the realists’ own commitments.

iii. advantages over traditional error-theoretic arguments

Dialectical advantages — engages relaxed realists; avoids metaphysical impasse; doesn't over-generalise to epistemic error theory; speaks to ordinary moral scepticismMy argument has decisive dialectical advantages. First, it engages relaxed realists who dismiss traditional error-theoretic arguments as ‘external scepticism’ — misconceived external metaphysical critique. The first-order argument is a form of internal scepticism; it operates within the moral domain itself and therefore cannot be dismissed as simply missing the point. Second, it avoids the over-generalisation problem: traditional error-theoretic arguments based on the denial of irreducible normative properties seem to entail error theories about epistemic normativity and prudential normativity as well — an unwelcome result. The first-order argument need not generalise in this way. Third, it speaks to ordinary philosophical motivations for moral scepticism: the sense that our deepest moral commitments pull irresolvably in different directions.

iv. the quarantine problem — deontic paradoxes

Quarantine fails — axiological paradoxes translate into deontic paradoxes; non-consequentialists cannot escape; the impossibility infects the right and virtue tooNon-consequentialists will object that even if the axiological domain is in trouble, they are not primarily concerned with axiology; their morality is about the right and the virtuous, not just the good. Parfit called this the attempt to ‘quarantine’ the paradoxes. But quarantine fails. Recent work has successfully translated the axiological paradoxes into deontic paradoxes — paradoxes about what we are obligated to do, not just about what is better. Even the most thoroughgoing deontologist assigns a significant and indispensable role to axiology in her moral theory. And the impossibility results, as Arrhenius has shown, do not depend on the transitivity of ‘better than’ alone — they survive reformulation in non-transitivity-friendly frameworks, making them even harder to quarantine.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The master argument: inconsistent conceptual truths yield a domain-wide error theory
1.Arrhenius’s impossibility theorem demonstrates that five highly intuitive population-ethical conditions are mutually inconsistent; at least one must be false.
2.These conditions have the status of conceptual truths about moral goodness: they are constitutive of the moral concept in the way that round-square conditions are constitutive of that concept.
3.If a concept’s constitutive conditions are mutually inconsistent, judgments deploying that concept systematically fail to be true — just as no judgment about round squares can be true.
∴No moral (axiological) judgment is true; error theory follows from the internal inconsistency of moral concepts, not from any external metaphysical claim.
Argument II — The fixed-points reductio: realists’ own conceptual commitments generate error theory
1.Cuneo and Shafer-Landau argue that some moral truths are conceptual: competent concept-users are committed to them a priori. The Egalitarian Dominance Condition and cognates satisfy all four criteria for fixed points (necessity, framework status, bewilderment-on-denial, a priori knowability).
2.But fixed points cannot all be true if they are mutually inconsistent; and Arrhenius proves they are inconsistent.
3.If the fixed points are inconsistent and yet are conceptually constitutive of goodness, moral realism is self-undermining on its own preferred metaethical methodology.
∴The fixed-points strategy, when combined with the impossibility theorems, yields error theory as a reductio of moral realism’s own conceptual commitments.
Argument III — Dialectical advantages: the first-order argument engages all parties and avoids over-generalisation
1.Relaxed realists dismiss external metaphysical arguments as misconceived; the first-order argument is internal and cannot be so dismissed.
2.Traditional arguments over-generalise to epistemic and prudential error theory; the first-order argument is specific to moral inconsistency and need not generalise.
3.The first-order argument speaks to ordinary philosophical motivations for scepticism, making it dialectically more persuasive than abstract metaphysical arguments.
∴The first-order argument is dialectically superior to traditional error-theoretic arguments: it engages all parties, avoids over-generalisation, and connects to real philosophical concerns.
Argument IV — Quarantine fails: the impossibility results translate into deontic paradoxes
1.Non-consequentialists hope to quarantine the axiological paradoxes by denying that axiology determines deontology; but recent work translates axiological impossibility results directly into deontic paradoxes about what we must do.
2.Arrhenius shows the impossibility results survive reformulation without dependence on transitivity of ‘better than’, removing the most common technical escape route.
3.No moral theory — consequentialist or otherwise — assigns axiology a negligible role; the inconsistency propagates throughout moral theory.
∴Quarantine fails: the impossibility infects deontic morality as well as axiology; error theory is not merely an axiological conclusion but a properly moral one.

Mr Speaker,

I am a moral philosopher and a mathematician, and it is in that combination that I have spent two decades working on what I believe is the most serious problem in ethics: the impossibility theorems of population axiology. I want to explain to this House what these theorems actually establish, why their implications are so troubling, and why I believe they constitute a genuine crisis for moral theory — though I am more cautious than Cowie about where exactly that crisis leads.

Arrow’s impossibility theorem in social choice theory showed, in 1950, that no system of aggregating individual preferences into a social preference can simultaneously satisfy a small number of highly compelling conditions. The theorem was revolutionary because it revealed a fundamental mathematical constraint on what rational collective choice can achieve. My six impossibility theorems in population axiology are analogous: they show that no population axiology can simultaneously satisfy a comparably compelling set of adequacy conditions.

Arrhenius’s Sixth Impossibility Theorem (the most powerful).

The following five conditions are mutually inconsistent — no population axiology can satisfy all of them:

Egalitarian Dominance: If population A is equal and same-sized as B, and every person in A has higher welfare than every person in B, A is better.
Weak Non-Sadism: Adding positive-welfare lives is at least as good as adding negative-welfare lives.
General Non-Extreme Priority: It is not always best to prioritise the worst-off individual, however marginally, at any cost to others.
Weak Quality Addition: Adding very high-welfare lives is at least as good as adding lives with negative welfare plus more very low positive-welfare lives.
Non-Elitism: A slightly better life in an otherwise worse population does not automatically make that population better.

Each condition seems obviously true. Together they are provably false.
i. the structure and rigour of the theorems

Rigorous proof — these are mathematical demonstrations not philosophical intuitions; inconsistency is proven, not argued; this is stronger than all previous moral paradoxesThe theorems are not thought experiments or intuition pumps. They are formal mathematical proofs. Given background assumptions about welfare and population structure that any population axiology must accept, I demonstrate with logical rigour that no axiology can satisfy all five conditions simultaneously. This is importantly different from previous work in the tradition — including Parfit’s Mere Addition Paradox, which involved background assumptions that could be questioned and conclusions that were not straightforward contradictions. My theorems establish genuine logical impossibility: the conditions are not jointly satisfiable in any model. This is a mathematical fact about the structure of the moral problem space, not a philosophical claim that can be finessed by clever theoretical moves.

ii. the intuitive weight of the adequacy conditions

Conditions are nearly undeniable — Egalitarian Dominance is as secure as any moral claim; abandoning any condition is costly; no cheap escapeWhat makes the theorems troubling is not just their logical character but the intuitive force of each condition. Consider the Egalitarian Dominance Condition: if two populations are the same size and perfectly equal, and every person in one has higher welfare than every person in the other, the first is better. Could anything be more obviously true? Or Weak Non-Sadism: adding people with positive welfare to the world is at least as good as adding people with negative welfare. These are not philosophers’ technical constructs; they are the moral bedrock from which any serious ethical theory must begin. When I ask audiences to identify which condition they would reject, the most common response is genuine paralysis: each condition seems more secure than any theoretical consideration that could be offered against it.

iii. the three options and why all are costly

Three options all costly — abandon a condition (but which?), accept scepticism, or explain away; no cost-free exit; realism must bite bullets it cannot stomachThe theorems leave moral theory with three options. First: abandon one of the conditions. But every condition is so intuitive that abandoning any of them requires endorsing something deeply counterintuitive. Abandoning Non-Sadism means accepting that adding negative welfare lives is sometimes at least as good as adding positive welfare lives. Abandoning Non-Extreme Priority means accepting that some version of extreme prioritarianism is correct — that it is always best to marginally benefit the worst-off individual even at enormous cost to everyone else. These are not theoretical costs easily paid; they are positions that virtually all moral philosophers find repugnant. Second: accept moral scepticism or error theory, as Cowie recommends. Third: explain away the results as showing that the domain of population ethics is somehow special, not representative of morality generally. I explore all three without fully endorsing any.

iv. from impossibility to metaethics

From impossibility to metaethics — theorems suggest at least one moral belief is false; consistency requires a justified theory; impossibility means none existsThe theorems have a straightforward metaethical implication: at least one of our deeply held, nearly undeniable moral beliefs is false. Since all five conditions seem secure, the theorem gives us no guidance about which to reject. And if our most secure moral beliefs include a false one we cannot identify, then the epistemic foundation of moral theory is in serious trouble. Consistency is, arguably, a necessary condition for moral justification — no inconsistent moral theory can be well-justified. If no consistent population axiology satisfies all adequacy conditions, then there is, as Arrhenius himself has noted, a real question whether any moral theory can be justified. The impossibility results are not a local problem in population ethics; they are a crisis at the foundations of moral theorising.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The theorems are rigorous mathematical proofs, not thought experiments; inconsistency is logically demonstrated
1.The six impossibility theorems are formal proofs: given background assumptions that any population axiology must accept, the five adequacy conditions are provably jointly unsatisfiable.
2.This is stronger than previous work (Parfit’s Mere Addition Paradox) which involved questionable background assumptions and conclusions that were not outright contradictions.
3.Mathematical impossibility cannot be evaded by philosophical cleverness; the conditions are not jointly satisfiable in any model.
∴The impossibility is mathematically established, not argued; this gives the result a kind of force that no philosophical counterargument can simply dissolve.
Argument II — Each adequacy condition is individually near-undeniable; no cheap escape by condition-rejection
1.Egalitarian Dominance: a same-sized equal population is better if every person in it has higher welfare. This is as secure as any moral intuition can be.
2.Weak Non-Sadism: adding positive-welfare lives is at least as good as adding negative-welfare lives. Denying this would seem to commit one to a form of sadism about population.
3.Any attempt to reject one condition to escape the impossibility incurs an enormous intuitive cost; there is no cost-free exit from the theorem.
∴The conditions’ individual intuitive force means that rejecting any of them is a serious moral cost, not a technical fix; the impossibility is a genuine crisis, not a puzzle to be solved.
Argument III — All three exit options from the impossibility are costly; moral theory is genuinely trapped
1.Option 1 (abandon a condition) requires endorsing a counterintuitive moral claim; every available condition-rejection is deeply repugnant to virtually all moral philosophers.
2.Option 2 (scepticism/error theory) denies that any moral theory can be justified, which is a radical and uncomfortable conclusion.
3.Option 3 (explain away by quarantining population ethics) fails because the results translate into deontic paradoxes and are not isolated in population axiology.
∴All exits from the impossibility are costly; moral theory is genuinely trapped; any position taken entails something deeply troubling for moral philosophy.
Argument IV — Consistency is necessary for justification; since no consistent population axiology satisfies all conditions, no moral theory can be justified
1.Consistency is a necessary condition for moral justification: an inconsistent theory cannot be the right account of the moral domain.
2.No consistent population axiology satisfies all five adequacy conditions; the theorem is proven.
3.A theory that fails to satisfy adequacy conditions as intuitive as these is not a satisfactory moral theory.
∴There is no satisfactory, justified population axiology; since consistency is necessary for justification and no consistent theory satisfies all conditions, a moral justification crisis follows.

Mr Speaker,

The argument before this House has distinguished historical roots. I speak for two predecessors who identified the problem of first-order moral inconsistency long before Arrhenius’s formal proofs were available — and whose anxiety about that inconsistency drove them to conclusions more radical than their successors have been willing to face.

Sidgwick, writing in 1874, identified what he called the ‘dualism of practical reason’: a deep and apparently irresolvable tension between the principle of universal benevolence — maximise aggregate welfare — and the principle of rational egoism — maximise one’s own welfare. Sidgwick regarded both as self-evident; neither could be derived from the other; both seemed to express fundamental demands of reason. The result was a contradiction. And Sidgwick was honest enough to contemplate the consequences: “if it cannot be overcome, the whole system must fall to the ground.”

“I am not prepared to deny that the cosmos of duty is really thus chaotic.” — Henry Sidgwick, The Methods of Ethics, 1874
i. sidgwick’s dualism and its error-theoretic implication

Sidgwick's dualism — self-evidence of both egoism and universalism; both cannot be true; system collapses; historical precedent for first-order error theorySidgwick’s dualism is an early instance of exactly the structure Cowie formalises. The methods of rational egoism and universal benevolence are both self-evident to Sidgwick — they both seem to express genuine, fundamental demands of practical reason. But they are inconsistent: situations arise in which what benevolence requires and what self-interest requires diverge, and there is no higher principle in Sidgwick’s system that resolves the divergence. Sidgwick himself contemplated an error-theoretic conclusion: if neither principle can be sacrificed and they cannot both be true, then perhaps the system of duty as a whole falls. He hoped for a theistic resolution — a God who would ensure the harmony of virtue and self-interest. But he acknowledged that without such resolution, the dualism was genuine chaos.

ii. parfit’s mere addition paradox and the repugnant conclusion

Mere Addition Paradox — A is not worse than A+; A+ is not worse than Z; but Z is worse than A; the repugnant conclusion is the forced result of plausible premisesParfit’s Mere Addition Paradox established the pattern that Arrhenius would later formalise with greater rigour. Consider three populations: A (a small population of very happy people), A+ (A plus an additional group of people with lives worth living but not as good as A’s), and Z (a very large population of people whose lives are barely worth living). Three plausible principles jointly entail that Z is better than A. First: it is not worse to add people with lives worth living to a population. Second: if two populations are the same size, the more equal and happier one is better. Third: ‘better than’ is transitive. These three principles force us to the Repugnant Conclusion: Z is better than A. But Z is a world of billions living lives barely worth living, while A is a world of flourishing. This is the heart of the paradox.

iii. the failure to quarantine: impossibility propagates

Propagation — Parfit himself tried to escape; every escape route leads to another repugnant conclusion; the impossibility cannot be localisedParfit spent decades attempting to find an escape from the Repugnant Conclusion. He tried every available axiology: total utilitarianism, average utilitarianism, critical level utilitarianism, person-affecting views, two-level accounts. Every escape route led to another repugnant conclusion. Average utilitarianism implies the Mere Addition Paradox from the other direction; critical level utilitarianism implies the Sadistic Conclusion; person-affecting views generate the Non-Identity Problem. The impossibility propagates: every position taken to avoid one counterintuitive result entails another. Parfit’s verdict, at the end of his life, was that population ethics was in genuine crisis — that we had not yet found the correct theory and that the search had uncovered something like a fundamental feature of the moral problem space that resists any satisfactory solution.

iv. the lessons for moral realism

Against realism — if moral truths exist, which horn does the realist bite? Every horn is repugnant; realism must own its counterintuitive commitments; error theory is the more honest responseThe lessons for moral realism are uncomfortable. If moral truths exist — as the realist insists — then one of the five conditions in Arrhenius’s theorem is false. But which? The moral realist must tell us. Enoch says simply: “bite the bullet.” But the bullets available are all repugnant. The realist must own that either adding billions of positive-welfare lives is sometimes worse than adding negative-welfare lives, or that extreme prioritarianism is correct, or that some similarly counterintuitive proposition is true. The moral realist cannot simply say “the facts are what they are even if counterintuitive” without acknowledging that the counterintuitive position she endorses is not a minor technical adjustment but a claim that overturns deeply held moral convictions. The error theorist’s position — that no moral claims are true — is at least honest about the crisis.

Argument I — Sidgwick’s dualism: self-evident principles of egoism and universalism are inconsistent; the system falls
1.Both rational egoism and universal benevolence are self-evident to Sidgwick: both seem to express fundamental demands of practical reason.
2.They are inconsistent: situations arise where what each requires diverges, and no higher principle resolves the divergence.
3.Self-evident inconsistent principles cannot all be true; if neither can be sacrificed, the system of duty collapses — as Sidgwick himself acknowledged.
∴Sidgwick’s dualism is the historical prototype of Cowie’s argument; first-order inconsistency in foundational moral principles is not a new discovery but a recurring and deepening crisis.
Argument II — The Mere Addition Paradox forces the Repugnant Conclusion from individually plausible premises
1.Three individually plausible principles (non-worsening of mere addition, preference for more equal same-size populations, transitivity of ‘better than’) jointly entail the Repugnant Conclusion.
2.The Repugnant Conclusion (that a vast population of lives barely worth living is better than a smaller flourishing one) is repugnant to virtually all moral intuition.
3.The paradox is not dissolved by rejecting transitivity alone: Arrhenius has shown the results survive without transitivity.
∴The Mere Addition Paradox demonstrates that individually compelling axiological principles generate a repugnant forced conclusion; the impossibility is inherent in the moral problem space, not a removable artefact.
Argument III — Impossibility propagates: every escape from one repugnant conclusion leads to another
1.Every major axiology tried to escape the Repugnant Conclusion leads to another repugnant result: total utilitarianism gives the Repugnant Conclusion; average utilitarianism gives the Mere Addition Paradox; critical-level utilitarianism gives the Sadistic Conclusion.
2.The propagation is not accidental but reflects the mathematical structure of the domain: Arrhenius’s theorems prove no consistent axiology avoids all repugnant results.
3.The impossibility is not a local puzzle but a fundamental feature of the moral problem space.
∴The propagation of impossibility across all axiological positions shows that the problem is not soluble by theoretical cleverness; the crisis is structural and genuine.
Argument IV — Moral realism must bite a repugnant bullet; error theory is more honest about the crisis
1.If moral truths exist, one of the five conditions in Arrhenius’s theorem is false; the moral realist must identify which.
2.Every available condition-rejection entails a deeply counterintuitive moral claim that overturns deeply held moral convictions, not a minor technical adjustment.
3.The error-theoretic position that no moral claims are true is at least honest about the scope of the crisis rather than claiming to have found the one bullet worth biting.
∴Moral realism faces the same crisis as error theory but is less honest about it; at least the error theorist does not claim to know which repugnant truth to endorse.

Mr Speaker,

Cowie’s argument is ingenious but insufficiently radical. He concludes from the impossibility theorems that moral error theory is true. I want to argue, first, that the paradoxes do not support error theory specifically but a more general position called moral nihilism; and second, that this is actually a stronger and more interesting conclusion than error theory alone.

Moral nihilism is the first-order view that nothing is good, bad, right, or wrong — that all moral predicates, across all their applications, yield nothing that is true. Moral error theory is one metaethical framework compatible with nihilism: it holds that moral sentences express genuine truth-apt beliefs that are all false. But moral nihilism is also compatible with moral non-cognitivism: the view that moral sentences express non-truth-apt attitudes rather than beliefs. Cowie’s argument, once properly understood, supports nihilism — and nihilism is a larger and more threatening conclusion than error theory.

“The logical space of moral nihilism is greater than the logical space of moral error theory. The threat that the argument from moral paradoxes poses to moral philosophy is therefore more serious than Cowie acknowledges.” — Lewis Williams, forthcoming
i. non-cognitivists can escape cowie’s argument

Non-cognitivist escape — Cowie says error theory uniquely avoids the repugnant horns; but non-cognitivists also avoid them; paradoxes don't uniquely support error theoryCowie argues that moral error theory is uniquely placed to avoid having to endorse any of the repugnant horns of the paradoxes, because error theorists simply deny all moral claims including the paradox conditions. But non-cognitivists can make exactly the same move: they express no moral attitudes toward any of the paradox conditions, treating them as expressions of attitude rather than truth-apt claims. Neither error theorists nor non-cognitivists are committed to endorsing any repugnant horn. Cowie therefore cannot argue that the paradoxes support error theory rather than non-cognitivism; they support the broader position of moral nihilism, which subsumes both.

ii. nihilism has a broader logical space and is the more precise conclusion

Broader logical space — nihilism is entailed by error theory but not vice versa; paradoxes target first-order claims; nihilism is the precise targetMoral nihilism is a first-order view: it holds that nothing has any moral property. Moral error theory is a metaethical view about the character of moral claims: they are truth-apt but all false. Error theory entails nihilism: if all moral claims are false, then nothing is good, bad, right, or wrong. But nihilism does not entail error theory: a non-cognitivist who expresses no attitudes about morality is a nihilist in the relevant first-order sense without being an error theorist in the metaethical sense. Since the paradoxes operate at the first-order level — they are paradoxes about what is better, worse, good, and bad — they target nihilism directly and error theory only indirectly. Nihilism is the more precise conclusion.

iii. the first unified argument for moral nihilism

First unified argument for nihilism — moral paradoxes across multiple domains support a single unified nihilistic conclusion; this is philosophically significantThe argument from moral paradoxes, once reconstructed as an argument for nihilism, becomes the first unified argument for that position. Previous arguments for nihilism have been either local — confined to specific moral domains — or dependent on contested metaethical premises. The paradox argument is neither: it operates from first-order moral claims that virtually all parties accept, and it covers a wide range of moral domains — not just population ethics but trolley problems, paradoxes of choice under uncertainty, and other domains where impossibility results can be generated. This breadth gives the nihilism argument a comprehensive force that no previous argument for nihilism has achieved.

iv. the threat to moral philosophy is more serious than cowie acknowledges

More serious threat — if any positive moral claim is made, counterintuitive companions follow; realists must own counterintuitive truths; the moral landscape is irreversibly darkenedCowie frames the argument primarily as a weapon for error theory in the metaethical debate. But the implications are more sweeping. If the paradoxes show that any consistent set of moral claims must include at least one deeply counterintuitive proposition, then anyone who makes any positive moral claim — not just population ethicists — is committed to counterintuitive companions. The moral realist who says “torturing innocents is wrong” is also, indirectly, committed to whatever counterintuitive proposition her consistent moral theory requires. The impossibility results mean there is no clean, internally consistent moral position that accommodates all our intuitions. The landscape of moral philosophy is irreversibly darkened by the paradoxes, in a way that nihilism alone honestly acknowledges.

1.Cowie argues error theory is uniquely placed to avoid endorsing any repugnant horn because error theorists deny all moral claims.
2.Non-cognitivists also avoid endorsing repugnant horns: they express no moral attitudes toward paradox conditions, treating moral sentences as non-truth-apt.
3.If both error theorists and non-cognitivists avoid the repugnant horns, the paradoxes do not uniquely support error theory over non-cognitivism.
∴The paradoxes support the more general position of moral nihilism, which is compatible with both error theory and non-cognitivism, rather than error theory specifically.

2.The impossibility paradoxes operate at the first-order level, targeting claims about what is better and worse; they therefore directly support nihilism as a first-order view.

∴Moral nihilism is the more precise conclusion from the paradoxes; error theory requires additional metaethical premises that the paradox argument alone does not supply.

Mr Speaker,

I want to examine both the sceptical and the anti-realist challenges that Cowie and Arrhenius derive from the impossibility results, and I want to argue that both fail — and that they fail for connected reasons. The key error in each case is an assumption about the epistemic status of the adequacy conditions that cannot survive scrutiny.

Let me distinguish the two challenges carefully. The sceptical challenge, primarily drawn from Arrhenius’s own comments on his theorems, claims that the impossibility results undermine our confidence in all moral intuitions, because if our most secure moral intuitions are inconsistent, our moral belief-forming methods are unreliable. The anti-realist challenge, developed by Cowie, claims that the conditions have the status of conceptual truths, and that their inconsistency therefore shows moral concepts to be defective, yielding error theory. I argue that both challenges fail.

The self-undermining structure of Cowie’s argument.

Cowie’s argument has a peculiar self-undermining structure. It requires:
(1) The conditions in Arrhenius’s theorem are conceptual truths about moral goodness.
(2) The conditions are mutually inconsistent.
(3) Therefore, moral concepts are internally defective and error theory follows.

But there is a tension between (1) and (2). Conceptual truths are necessarily true; mutually inconsistent propositions cannot all be necessarily true; therefore the conditions cannot all be conceptual truths. Cowie needs the conditions to be “almost” conceptual truths — secure enough to infect the domain but not so necessary that their inconsistency is impossible. This is an unstable position. Either they are conceptual truths (and so consistent, defeating premise (2)) or they are not (and so cannot yield the domain-wide error theory that premise (1) was meant to establish).
i. the self-undermining objection to cowie’s argument

Self-undermining — if conditions are conceptual truths they must be consistent; if inconsistent they can't all be conceptual; Cowie's argument defeats itselfCowie’s argument requires the conditions to be conceptual truths. But conceptual truths are necessarily true. And necessarily true propositions are consistent: mutually inconsistent propositions cannot both be necessarily true, because in any possible world at least one would be false, violating necessity. If the conditions are conceptual truths, they must be consistent — which contradicts Arrhenius’s theorem. If they are inconsistent — which Arrhenius’s theorem demonstrates — then at least one of them is not a conceptual truth. But if not all conditions are conceptual truths, the domain-wide infection that Cowie needs does not follow. The error-theoretic conclusion requires both (i) that the conditions are conceptual truths and (ii) that they are inconsistent. These two requirements are jointly incompatible.

ii. the sceptical challenge fails: the conditions lack the required epistemic privilege

Sceptical challenge fails — the conditions are not epistemically privileged enough to undermine all moral intuitions; we don't know which to reject but that's ordinary moral uncertaintyThe sceptical challenge holds that the impossibility results undermine our confidence in all moral intuitions. But this inference requires the adequacy conditions to have very high epistemic privilege — privilege sufficient to make the impossibility of jointly satisfying them a reflection on all other moral intuitions. In fact, the conditions, despite their initial plausibility, are not so epistemically privileged that failing to know which one to reject licenses general moral scepticism. The analogy to mathematics fails: we do not treat the discovery of a mathematical paradox as grounds for abandoning all mathematical belief, because the paradox is typically in a specialised domain. Similarly, the impossibility in population axiology gives us no reason to distrust our moral intuitions about, say, the wrongness of gratuitous cruelty.

iii. the analogy to philosophy generally: paradoxes do not breed scepticism

Paradoxes don't breed scepticism elsewhere — philosophy is full of paradoxes; the correct response is further inquiry not abandonment; scepticism from moral paradoxes proves too muchIf the argument from impossibility to scepticism were sound, a host of analogous arguments would be equally successful throughout philosophy. Our intuitions about personal identity generate paradoxes in philosophy of mind; our intuitions about sets generate paradoxes in logic; our intuitions about vagueness generate the Sorites paradox. If impossible-to-jointly-satisfy intuitions warranted abandoning confidence in the domain, we would be forced into scepticism about personal identity, set theory, and vagueness. But we are not; we treat these paradoxes as spurs to further inquiry, not as grounds for abandoning the domains. The moral impossibility results should be treated the same way: as difficult problems demanding theoretical resolution, not as grounds for error theory or scepticism.

iv. the correct response: identify which condition to reject

Correct response — further first-order inquiry to identify which condition to reject; not all conditions are equally secure; impossibility is a problem for axiology not metaethicsThe correct response to the impossibility results is not error theory or scepticism but further first-order inquiry into which condition to reject. The conditions are not equally secure on careful examination: the Non-Elitism Condition and General Non-Extreme Priority Condition, in particular, become less obvious once their technical formulations are properly understood. The impossibility results should motivate more careful population ethics, not a retreat to metaethical error theory. Moral realism survives: the existence of a hard and perhaps as-yet-unsolved problem in population axiology is compatible with the existence of moral truths and our ability to know some of them.

Cowie takes our framework — the moral fixed points — and wields it against us, arguing that the impossibility results show our own conceptual commitments to be self-defeating. I want to show that this is a misappropriation of our view that fails on its own terms, and that the moral fixed points framework, properly understood, is in fact robust against the impossibility challenge.

Our thesis is that some moral propositions are conceptual truths: propositions that competent users of the relevant concepts are committed to by virtue of their conceptual competence alone. Examples include: it is pro tanto wrong to engage in the recreational slaughter of a fellow person; it is pro tanto wrong to torture innocents just for one’s own amusement; it is morally admirable to express genuine gratitude to a benefactor. These are substantive moral truths, but their denial would betray a failure to understand the relevant moral concepts.

This is what happens in any domain when apparently secure principles turn out to be jointly unsatisfiable. I take the impossibility results more seriously than Moberger and more seriously than Cuneo and Shafer-Landau. I agree with Cowie and Arrhenius that the results reveal a genuine and deep tension in moral thought. But I disagree entirely about what the correct response is. The response is not error theory; it is radical revision of a theoretical assumption that virtually everyone in moral philosophy has made without question: that the relation ‘better than’ is transitive.

What I call the ‘essentially comparative view’ is this: what makes outcome A better than outcome B is constitutively comparative — it depends on which comparison is being made. The relevant features that bear on the comparison can change as we shift from comparing A with B to comparing B with C. If this is right, transitivity of ‘better than’ is not a conceptual truth but a false theoretical assumption, and abandoning it allows us to accept all the individual adequacy conditions in Arrhenius’s theorem without contradiction.

The essentially comparative view and intransitivity.

Compare three populations: A (ten very happy people), B (twenty people somewhat less happy), C (a thousand people with lives barely worth living).

The essentially comparative view says:
A is better than B: in this comparison, equality and quality favour A.
B is better than C: in this comparison, the sheer number of people with positive welfare in C exceeds B’s advantage in quality.
But: A might still be better than C, because in the direct comparison of A and C, different features become relevant — particularly the great qualitative gulf between A and C. Transitivity would require that if A > B and B > C, then A > C. But the essentially comparative view denies this: what makes A better than B might not be preserved in the comparison of A with C.
i. transitivity is not a conceptual truth about goodness

Transitivity is contestable — transitivity of betterness is a theoretical assumption not a conceptual truth; the essentially comparative view shows it can be coherently deniedVirtually everyone who works on population ethics assumes that the betterness relation is transitive: if A is better than B and B is better than C, then A is better than C. This seems so obvious as to be almost definitionally true. But the assumption is contestable, and I have argued that it is actually false. The essentially comparative view offers a coherent account of what makes one outcome better than another that entails non-transitivity: the relevant features bearing on comparison are context-sensitive, shifting as the comparison base shifts. This is not arbitrary or ad hoc; it reflects a genuine insight about the nature of comparative evaluation. And crucially: if transitivity is false, the impossibility results, which depend on transitivity in their proofs, are blocked. We can accept all five conditions without contradiction.

ii. the analogy to the sorites and vagueness

Sorites analogy — vagueness requires denying transitivity of “looks the same as”; this is familiar from philosophy; betterness intransitivity is the same kind of moveDenying transitivity might seem like a desperate theoretical move, but it has a distinguished precedent in philosophy. The Sorites paradox about vagueness generates an analogous impossibility: we cannot jointly hold that (i) a single grain of sand cannot make the difference between a heap and a non-heap, (ii) one grain of sand is not a heap, and (iii) heaps exist. The standard response is to deny that the ‘looks the same as’ relation is transitive: a and b might look the same, and b and c might look the same, without a and c looking the same. This denial of transitivity in the context of vagueness is widely accepted. My proposal that betterness is similarly non-transitive follows the same logical structure.

Revisionism vs error theory — revising a background assumption is not conceding error theory; we revise false theoretical posits all the time without abandoning the domainCowie might object that my revisionism is just a way of avoiding the error-theoretic conclusion by sacrificing one assumption — transitivity — rather than one of the listed conditions. But there is a fundamental difference between revisionism and error theory. Revisionism says: one of our background theoretical assumptions was false; we should revise it and proceed. Error theory says: the entire moral domain generates no truths; we should abandon it. These are radically different conclusions. Transitivity is a theoretical assumption about the formal structure of a relation; it is not itself a first-order moral claim. Abandoning it does not abandon any positive moral truth; it revises our meta-level understanding of how moral comparisons work. This is the kind of theoretical revision that all sciences undergo.

We cannot simply rank options on a single scale and choose the top-ranked. Moral realism survives: there are truths about which outcomes are better when compared pairwise, even if the betterness relation is not transitive over triples. The impossibility results have taught us something important about the nature of value and practical reasoning. They have not taught us that there is no such thing as value or that practical reasoning is systematically in error.

2.Nobody concluded from earlier intuition revisions that morality was in systematic error; the conclusion was that some intuitions were wrong and moral knowledge was advancing.
3.The same should hold for population ethics: discovering that one axiological condition is false is a step in moral learning, not evidence for error theory.
∴The track record of moral progress supports realism: moral intuitions are revisable, and revising them is compatible with moral knowledge advancing toward moral truth.
Argument III — Arrow’s theorem is an exact analogue; nobody concluded social choice error theory; the same should hold for moral theory
1.Arrow’s impossibility theorem shows no social welfare function jointly satisfies all compelling conditions; this is mathematically analogous to Arrhenius’s moral impossibility results.
2.Nobody concluded from Arrow that social choice theory generates no genuine claims, or that there are no truths about rational preference aggregation; we concluded that one of Arrow’s conditions must be relaxed.
3.The parallel argument in moral theory should yield the same conclusion: that one of Arrhenius’s conditions must be rejected, not that moral theory generates no truths.
∴Arrow’s theorem is a companion in guilt: impossibility results in a domain do not entail domain error theory; the same response appropriate for Arrow is appropriate for Arrhenius.
Argument IV — Near-undeniability is weaker than conceptual truth; the conceptual route fails
1.Cowie needs the conditions to be conceptual truths; near-undeniability alone does not establish this — history shows apparently necessary propositions can be false.
2.The axiological conditions may seem nearly undeniable while still being false in ways requiring deeper moral theory; near-undeniability is strong evidence but not proof of conceptual truth.
3.The impossibility shows only that one condition is false, not that the concept of goodness is incoherent; the conceptual route requires more than near-undeniability can supply.
∴The conceptual route fails; near-undeniability is insufficient to establish conceptual truth; the impossibility results show that one of our moral beliefs is false, not that moral concepts are defective.

Mr Speaker,

I want to examine the impossibility theorems more carefully from the inside — to look at the specific conditions that Arrhenius deploys and ask whether they are really as individually secure as the error theorists maintain. My thesis is that once we examine the technical formulations of the conditions with care, the picture changes considerably: some conditions are much less intuitively compelling than they initially appear, and rejecting them is less costly than Cowie and Arrhenius suggest.

The error-theoretic argument depends crucially on the claim that all five conditions are so intuitive that rejecting any of them would be a catastrophic cost. If I can show that some conditions are questionable on careful examination, the argument for error theory loses the dialectical grip it needs. The realist does not need to find all conditions equally questionable — she just needs to identify one that can be rejected at manageable cost.

“The response is to examine the adequacy conditions more carefully, not to abandon morality. Some conditions are less secure than they appear.” — Erik Carlson, Oxford Handbook of Population Ethics, 2022
i. the non-elitism condition is not as secure as it appears

Non-Elitism is questionable — the condition's technical formulation involves complex comparisons that may conceal a violation of egalitarian intuitions; it's less obvious than it first appearsThe Non-Elitism Condition is the most technically complex of the five conditions and the most difficult to parse without careful study. Recall its content: it holds, roughly, that if everyone in one population is much better off than everyone in a second population, then adding one person to the second population who is just marginally better off than anyone in the first population does not thereby make the second population better than the first. This seems plausible on first reading. But on careful examination, the condition embeds comparisons across populations of different sizes in ways that may not be as unambiguously compelling as simpler egalitarian conditions. Philosophers who have studied it carefully have found it far less obviously true than conditions like Egalitarian Dominance, and it has been questioned on grounds that it may itself violate egalitarian intuitions in certain cases.

ii. the general non-extreme priority condition embeds a contestable anti-prioritarian commitment

Non-Extreme Priority is contestable — it rules out extreme prioritarianism; but many moral philosophers find prioritarianism compelling; this is a contested first-order debate, not an obvious truthThe General Non-Extreme Priority Condition effectively rules out extreme prioritarianism: the view that it is always best to benefit the worst-off individual, however marginally, at any cost to others. The condition holds that for any welfare level A and any population X, there is a large enough group of high-welfare people such that adding one person with welfare just above A is at least as good as adding them with welfare only slightly above A — regardless of how many high-welfare people are affected. This is far from obviously true. Many philosophers find some form of prioritarianism highly compelling. The condition essentially builds a contested anti-prioritarian conclusion into an apparently neutral adequacy constraint. Rejecting it is not as costly as Cowie suggests; it reflects a live debate in first-order moral philosophy.

iii. the impossibility result does not hold universally across all formulations

Technical escape routes — some formulations of the conditions allow escape; the impossibility is framework-relative; a different framework may satisfy all conditions in weaker formThe impossibility results are proven within a particular formal framework with specific background assumptions about welfare levels, welfare ranges, and the structure of populations. Different background assumptions may yield different results. Theologians and philosophers have noted that certain modifications of the conditions — weakening them in ways that preserve their intuitive core while removing their technical extremes — may be jointly satisfiable. Non-Archimedean axiologies, lexical views, and vague welfare comparisons have all been proposed as ways of satisfying weaker versions of the conditions that retain their moral motivation without generating the impossibility. These are ongoing research programmes in population ethics, not merely desperate escapes.

iv. error theory is the least motivated response to a hard first-order problem

Error theory unmotivated — the correct response to a hard axiology problem is more axiology not less; error theory abandons too quickly; mathematicians don't abandon maths when problems are hardThe impossibility results pose a difficult challenge to population ethics. But the response that is least motivated — and most radical — is to abandon the moral domain entirely by endorsing error theory. Mathematicians did not abandon mathematics when Hilbert’s programme failed to resolve the consistency of arithmetic; they revised their conception of what mathematics is and what it can achieve. Economists did not abandon social choice theory when Arrow’s theorem showed the impossibility of an ideal social welfare function; they explored which condition to relax and built more sophisticated theories. The correct model for moral philosophy is exactly the same. The impossibility results demand more and better population ethics, not less morality. Error theory is the counsel of despair masquerading as philosophical rigour.
