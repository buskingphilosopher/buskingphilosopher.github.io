Motion before the House
"This House Believes Big Business Is Good for Society"
Proposition Speaking for the motion
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr President,

We live in an age of extraordinary ingenuity about what to resent. And at the top of the resentment hierarchy, occupying the position once held by monarchs and aristocrats and robber barons, sits the large corporation. Big business. The entity we love to hate even as we love what it makes.

I want to suggest to you that this hostility is not merely misplaced. It is a form of ingratitude so profound as to constitute a kind of civilisational amnesia.

On what big business actually is

The argument from coordinationA large corporation coordinates. It takes the dispersed knowledge, effort, and specialisation of thousands — sometimes hundreds of thousands — of human beings, and aligns them toward the production of something that none of them could produce alone. Your smartphone contains components designed in California, manufactured in Taiwan, assembled in China, shipped through Rotterdam, and delivered to your door in two days. That is not exploitation. That is a miracle of coordination that no government ministry, no cooperative, no artisanal collective has ever come close to replicating.

On trust

The argument from institutional reliabilityAmericans trust large corporations more than they trust Congress, more than they trust the media. When you board an aeroplane, you trust Boeing with your life. When you take a drug, you trust the pharmaceutical supply chain. When you eat from a supermarket, you trust Tesco or Walmart not to poison you. And they do not. Overwhelmingly, reliably, day after day, they do not. The large corporation has developed, over centuries of competitive pressure and reputational incentive, a culture of procedural reliability that is genuinely remarkable.

We have built institutions that reliably coordinate the behaviour of strangers toward mutually beneficial ends. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, the central problem of civilisation.

On innovation

The argument from innovationThe transistor was invented at Bell Labs — the research arm of AT&T, a monopoly. The internet was scaled by large corporations. The mRNA vaccine technology that ended a pandemic was developed with massive corporate investment and manufacturing infrastructure that no startup could have assembled. Large corporations are, in many domains, the primary engines of the kind of sustained, expensive, patient innovation that changes the world. The romance of the startup obscures the reality that most startups either fail, or if they succeed, become large corporations — at which point we begin to resent them.

On employment

The argument from labour conditionsLarge corporations employ people at scale, with benefits, with something resembling job security, with HR departments, legal protections, promotion ladders and pension schemes that small businesses cannot afford. When critics romanticise small business, they overlook what it is like to work for one. The boss is your cousin. There is no appeals process. There is no pension. If the owner takes against you, you are gone. The large corporation, for all its bureaucratic frustrations, represents a meaningful advance in the organisation of labour.

On the alternative

The argument from alternativesWe should always ask: compared to what? The critics of big business rarely specify what they would prefer. Government has a poor track record in complex goods and services. Non-profits do not scale. Small businesses fail at devastating rates. The question is not whether large corporations are perfect — of course they are not. The question is whether they are better than the available alternatives at the tasks they perform. And the honest answer, surveying human institutional arrangements, is: usually, yes.

On the critics

The argument from ingratitudeThe critics of big business write their critiques on Apple laptops, distribute them through Amazon, fund them with grants from large foundations, and reach their audiences through Google or Facebook — all large corporations. Big business has clothed us, fed us, medicated us, transported us, entertained us, and connected us to one another across distances that would have astonished our grandparents. It has done so imperfectly, often selfishly, sometimes destructively. But it has done so. And at a scale and with a reliability that no other human institution has matched. The least we can do is acknowledge that before we reach for the pitchfork.

Thank you.

Opposition Speaking against the motion
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr President,

My opponent has just delivered a love letter to the large corporation. It was eloquent. It was learned. And it was, at its core, a defence of power dressed in the language of coordination.

I want to suggest that the celebration of big business is a category error. It mistakes the performance of function for the justification of power. And it ignores, with remarkable consistency, those who bear the costs.

On coordination — and monopolisation

The argument from coordinationWe are told that large corporations coordinate. Yes. They also capture. They capture regulators, legislators, and the very competitive markets that are supposed to discipline them. Amazon undercuts competitors until they collapse, then raises prices. Google controls 92% of global search and uses that control to advantage its own products. Meta bought Instagram and WhatsApp not to serve users but to eliminate competitive threats. The word for what large corporations do to markets when left to their own devices is not coordination. It is monopolisation. When we celebrate big business, we are too often celebrating the winner of a race that was rigged after the fact.

On trust — a performed virtue

The argument from institutional reliabilityWe are asked to admire the reliability of large corporations. They do not poison us. They deliver on time. True. But the reliability of big business is not a moral achievement. It is a reputational constraint. Corporations do not poison their customers because poisoned customers do not return. They do, however, externalise carbon into the atmosphere, externalise risk onto taxpayers — as the 2008 financial crisis demonstrated — and externalise health costs onto communities without the political power to resist. The corporation is reliable where reliability is commercially rational and unreliable where it is not. This is not virtue. It is rational self-interest operating within whatever constraints society manages to impose.

On innovation — a selective history

The argument from innovationBell Labs is invoked. The iPhone is invoked. The mRNA vaccine is invoked. What is not invoked is the opioid crisis, engineered by Purdue Pharma, which spent decades suppressing evidence of OxyContin's addictiveness in pursuit of profit. What is not invoked is the tobacco industry's fifty-year campaign of deliberate deception about the health effects of smoking — corporate fraud that killed millions. What is not invoked is the fossil fuel industry's documented internal suppression of its own climate science. Big business innovates. Big business also suppresses, corrupts, and occasionally destroys. A full accounting must include both columns of the ledger. The selective presentation of the innovation column alone is not analysis. It is advocacy.

On employment — the view from below

The argument from labour conditionsThe warehouse worker at Amazon completes eleven hours of physical labour tracked by algorithm to the second, unable to take an unscheduled toilet break without it registering as time theft. The gig worker is classified — deliberately, legally, expensively — as an "independent contractor" so that the corporation can enjoy the productivity of an employee while denying them the protections of employment. The large corporation did not invent the HR department out of benevolence. It invented it to manage legal liability and to create the appearance of procedural fairness while retaining structural power over those who cannot afford to leave.

On the alternative — a false dilemma

The argument from alternativesWe are told: compared to what? This is a false dilemma. The alternative to unregulated large corporations is not the Soviet Union. It is regulated large corporations — with meaningful antitrust enforcement, without regulatory capture, with genuine liability for externalised costs, with tax obligations that reflect their use of public infrastructure rather than their ability to route profits through Ireland. We are not choosing between Amazon and subsistence farming. We are choosing what kind of rules govern powerful private institutions. And the record of big business in writing those rules for itself is not reassuring.

On the critics of the critics

The argument from ingratitudeMy opponent notes that critics of big business use Apple laptops and distribute their work through Google. This is true. It is also irrelevant. One may use the institutions available to one while arguing for their reform. The citizen who drives on public roads and pays taxes is not thereby endorsing every decision of the transport ministry. The argument from "you use it therefore you cannot criticise it" is a sophistication, not a refutation.

The verdict

Big business has produced genuine goods. I do not deny it. But the case for big business systematically ignores power — over markets, over employees, over democratic processes, over the natural world. Function is not justification. A society that mistakes the coordination of production for the justification of power has confused efficiency with justice. And efficiency without justice is simply a more productive form of exploitation.

Thank you.

Argument I — Coordination becomes monopolisationclick for image
1.Large corporations, once dominant, use market power to capture regulators and eliminate competition rather than serve consumers.
2.Amazon, Google, and Meta have demonstrably used dominance to crush rivals and entrench monopoly positions.
3.Coordination celebrated as efficiency is often monopolisation celebrated as scale.
∴The coordination function of big business cannot be separated from its tendency toward market domination and regulatory capture.
Argument II — Reliability is constraint, not virtueclick for image
1.Corporations behave reliably only where reliability is commercially rational; they externalise costs where they can avoid accountability.
2.Carbon externalisation, the 2008 financial crisis, and environmental damage near low-income communities all demonstrate this pattern.
3.Behaviour constrained by law and reputation is not the same as moral achievement.
∴Corporate reliability is a product of external constraint, not internal virtue — and disappears where that constraint is absent.
Argument III — Innovation is selectively invokedclick for image
1.A honest accounting of corporate innovation must include both what it produces and what it suppresses or corrupts.
2.Purdue Pharma, the tobacco industry, and fossil fuel companies all suppressed evidence of harm to protect profits — with catastrophic human cost.
3.Citing only beneficial innovation while ignoring corporate suppression of inconvenient science is advocacy, not analysis.
∴The innovation argument for big business is only valid when both columns of the ledger — creation and suppression — are honestly presented.
Argument IV — Labour protection is structural powerclick for image
1.Corporate HR and formal procedures exist primarily to manage legal liability, not to genuinely empower workers.
2.Algorithmic surveillance, gig classification, and non-compete clauses demonstrate the structural power corporations retain over workers despite formal protections.
3.Formal protection and genuine power parity are not the same thing.
∴The labour conditions argument for big business mistakes the appearance of procedural fairness for genuine worker empowerment.
Argument V — The alternatives aren't binaryclick for image
1.The choice is not between big business as currently constituted and some failed alternative — it is between different regulatory regimes governing large corporations.
2.Meaningful antitrust, genuine cost externalisation liability, and fair taxation are credible alternatives to current arrangements.
3.Big business has systematically worked to weaken exactly these constraints through lobbying and regulatory capture.
∴The "compared to what?" argument is a false dilemma; the real question is what rules should govern powerful private institutions.
Argument VI — Using ≠ endorsingclick for image
1.One may use the institutions available in a society while arguing for their reform without logical contradiction.
2.The argument "you benefit from it therefore you cannot criticise it" would make all institutional reform impossible.
3.The genetic fallacy holds: the origin or use of a critique does not determine its validity.
∴Pointing out that critics use corporate products is a rhetorical deflection, not a substantive rebuttal of their arguments.
