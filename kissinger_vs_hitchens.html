The Statesman's Defence
Henry Kissinger
The White House Years (1979) & Ending the Vietnam War (2003)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr Hitchens,

I want to begin by stating the problem as it actually presented itself in March 1969, rather than as it has been reconstructed, with the moral clarity of hindsight, by those who never had to make the decision. The United States had over half a million soldiers in Vietnam. American boys were dying — not in the abstract, but in specific, countable numbers, every week — from attacks launched by North Vietnamese and Viet Cong forces operating from sanctuaries inside Cambodian territory. These sanctuaries were not a secret to the enemy who used them. They were not a secret to the Cambodian government that tolerated them. They were a fact of the battlefield. The question that confronted the incoming Nixon administration was not whether to have a war in Indochina — that war had been underway for years and was inherited, not invented. The question was whether American forces would be permitted to engage an enemy that was already inside Cambodia, attacking American soldiers from a position the enemy itself had chosen as a sanctuary precisely because it believed itself immune from response.

I want to address this dispute on the terrain you have chosen — facts, decisions, consequences — because I believe that terrain, examined honestly, supports a defensible policy, even though its execution involved choices I do not pretend were costless and would not repeat in precisely the same form.

I. Cambodian neutrality was already compromised before Operation Menu began

Cambodian neutrality was already violated by North Vietnam, with Sihanouk's own tacit consent, before American bombing beganThe premise that the American bombing campaign violated a "stable and neutral" Cambodia requires correction by reference to the facts on the ground in 1969. Prince Sihanouk had, since at least 1966, permitted North Vietnamese and Viet Cong forces to establish base areas in eastern Cambodia and to receive supplies through the port of Sihanoukville, in exchange for an understanding that they would not foment internal rebellion against his government. This was not covert intelligence speculation. Operation Vesuvius, conducted in 1967, provided Sihanouk's own government with photographic evidence of North Vietnamese base areas inside Cambodian territory — evidence his government did not act upon, because acting upon it was contrary to the accommodation he had already reached with Hanoi. Cambodia's neutrality, by the time American bombing began, was a diplomatic fiction maintained for international consumption while a foreign army operated an entire logistics network inside its borders, supplied a major port for war matériel, and prepared attacks against American and South Vietnamese forces from within what the world's press continued, mistakenly, to call neutral territory. We did not violate Cambodian neutrality. We responded to a violation that had already occurred, conducted by an army that was not American.

II. The right of self-defence against forces using a third state's territory as sanctuary

International law and customary practice recognise the right to strike enemy forces sheltering in territory the host state cannot or will not controlThe legal question that I and my colleagues examined with genuine seriousness in early 1969 was whether the President had the right to act against concentrations of enemy troops that had already occupied neutral territory without the willing or effective consent of the territorial sovereign. Every legal opinion I received internally, and every serious examination of customary international law on this question before and since, supports the conclusion that this constitutes a legitimate exercise of the right of self-defence. A state cannot shelter behind a claim of neutrality while simultaneously permitting, or being powerless to prevent, its territory's use as a staging ground for attacks on a third party. This is not a novel or self-serving doctrine invented to excuse American conduct in 1969; it is a long-recognised principle that governs the conduct of war when belligerent forces seek sanctuary in the territory of states that cannot or will not enforce their own neutrality. The B-52 strikes targeted military base areas — supply depots, troop concentrations, command facilities — not Cambodian population centres or civilian infrastructure. The targets were chosen because intelligence indicated North Vietnamese military presence, not because of any indifference to Cambodian civilian life.

III. The secrecy was a response to a political reality, not a crime against democracy

The secrecy of the bombing campaign reflected a realistic assessment of what congressional and public opinion would permit, not a conspiracy against constitutional governmentI do not deny that Operation Menu was conducted with secrecy, including from members of Congress and from elements of the Pentagon bureaucracy whose discretion could not be assured. I want to explain, rather than disguise, the reasoning. By 1969, American public opinion regarding the Vietnam War had shifted decisively, and Congress would, in our assessment, never have authorised an extension of military operations into Cambodian territory through open legislative process — regardless of the military necessity, and regardless of the fact that American soldiers were dying from attacks launched from precisely the sanctuaries in question. This is, I recognise, an uncomfortable position for a government that claims democratic legitimacy. But I would ask you to consider the alternative: a president who, believing an action necessary to protect the lives of soldiers already committed to a war inherited from his predecessor, declines to take that action because the action would be unpopular if disclosed. That is not democratic accountability. That is paralysis disguised as principle. I do not say this resolves every concern about executive secrecy in wartime — it does not, and the subsequent history of executive overreach in this period, including matters wholly unrelated to Cambodia, has rightly generated lasting concern about the separation of powers. But the secrecy of Menu was not concealment of a crime. It was the concealment of a military operation whose disclosure would have generated a political reaction disproportionate to its actual military character.

IV. We did not create the Khmer Rouge, and the causal chain you assert is not as clean as you present it

The Khmer Rouge's rise had deep indigenous roots and was driven by Sihanouk's own political choices after the 1970 coup, not solely by American bombingThe most serious charge against the policy I pursued is that the bombing campaign directly produced the Khmer Rouge and therefore bears responsibility for the genocide that followed under Pol Pot. I want to take this charge with the seriousness it deserves rather than dismissing it, while also insisting on precision about causation. The Khmer Rouge existed before March 1969, as a small, largely ineffective Maoist insurgency with perhaps a few thousand adherents and no significant popular base. Its dramatic growth occurred specifically after March 1970, when Sihanouk was deposed by Lon Nol — an event in which, whatever the suspicions of the time, no credible evidence of direct American orchestration has ever been produced — and Sihanouk, in exile, made the decision to ally his considerable personal prestige and political following with the very insurgency he had spent years suppressing. It was Sihanouk's alliance with the Khmer Rouge, not American bombs alone, that transformed a marginal insurgent faction into a mass movement with access to the rural prestige of the monarchy. The bombing certainly created conditions of disruption and displacement that the Khmer Rouge exploited in their propaganda and recruitment — I do not deny this, and the documentary record on Khmer Rouge use of bombing damage in their recruitment messaging is genuine. But "exploited by" is a different causal claim from "created by," and the deeper roots of the Khmer Rouge's eventual ideology and the catastrophe of their rule lie in Maoist doctrine, indigenous Cambodian political conditions, and decisions made by multiple parties — including Hanoi, including Sihanouk, including the Khmer Rouge leadership itself — that cannot be reduced to a single American bombing campaign five years prior to their seizure of Phnom Penh.

V. The alternative to Menu was not peace — it was American casualties without American response

The realistic alternative to bombing the sanctuaries was continued American casualties from an enemy permitted unchallenged staging groundsI want to press on what I regard as the central evasion in every retrospective condemnation of this policy: the assumption that the alternative to Operation Menu was a peaceful, undisturbed Cambodia. This was never the realistic alternative. The realistic alternative was the continuation, without American military response, of North Vietnamese and Viet Cong operations from Cambodian sanctuaries that were already killing American and South Vietnamese soldiers in South Vietnam. We did not invent the war in Indochina; we inherited a war in which over thirty thousand Americans had already died before Nixon took office, in which hundreds of thousands more were still deployed, and in which the enemy had already demonstrated, through its use of Cambodian sanctuary, that it would exploit any constraint that the international laws of war or congressional caution placed upon American forces while incurring none of the same constraints itself. To decline to strike those sanctuaries was not a more moral policy. It was a policy of accepting continued American casualties in deference to a fiction of Cambodian neutrality that North Vietnam had already discarded. I do not ask you to find this calculation comfortable. I ask you to recognise that it was a calculation, weighing real lives against real lives, made by people responsible for the actual safety of soldiers already in the field — not an abstract moral seminar conducted at a comfortable distance from the consequences of inaction.

VI. The scale of civilian casualties has been substantially inflated in the retrospective indictment

The most extreme estimates of Cambodian civilian deaths from American bombing are not supported by the most careful subsequent scholarshipI want to address the casualty figures directly, because I believe the numbers most frequently cited in the retrospective indictment of this policy substantially overstate what the best available evidence supports. The bombing campaign targeted military base areas identified through intelligence as housing North Vietnamese and Viet Cong forces — areas that, by the nature of guerrilla warfare and the tactic of dispersal among rural populations that our enemy specifically adopted to complicate targeting, were not free of civilian presence. I do not deny that Cambodian civilians died in this campaign, and I do not minimise the gravity of civilian death in any military operation. But the methodology by which the most widely circulated mortality estimates have been produced — extrapolations from incomplete and disputed datasets, compiled decades after the events in question, frequently relying on interview-based retrospective estimation rather than contemporaneous documentation — has been criticised by serious demographers and historians as methodologically fragile. The honest position is that we do not know, with anything approaching the precision implied by figures cited as established fact, how many Cambodian civilians died specifically from American bombing as opposed to the subsequent civil war, the Khmer Rouge genocide, the famine and dislocation of the mid-1970s, or the Vietnamese invasion and occupation that followed. Conflating all of these causes of death under the single heading of "Kissinger's bombing" serves a rhetorical purpose. It does not serve historical accuracy.

VII. The moral asymmetry of judgment: North Vietnam's choices are erased from the indictment

The moral framing of the indictment erases the agency and choices of North Vietnam, which initiated the violation of Cambodian territory and chose to fight from within itI want to draw attention to an asymmetry in how this period of history has come to be narrated, an asymmetry that I believe reflects more about the politics of subsequent decades than about the events themselves. North Vietnam made the deliberate strategic choice to violate Cambodian neutrality by establishing base areas, depots, and a supply port within Cambodian territory specifically because doing so provided sanctuary from American and South Vietnamese forces. This was not a passive or incidental presence. It was a calculated military strategy, designed to exploit the legal and diplomatic protections of a neutral state while using that state's territory for active belligerent purposes against American forces. Every account of this period that begins the moral analysis with the American response to this strategy, rather than with the strategy itself, has already determined its conclusion before examining the facts. I am not asking that North Vietnam's strategic choices excuse every aspect of the American response — I have already acknowledged genuine costs and genuine errors in that response. I am asking that the moral accounting include the party that introduced foreign military forces into a neutral country's territory for offensive purposes, rather than reserving moral scrutiny exclusively for the party that responded to that introduction.

VIII. Statesmanship requires choices that cannot be judged by the standards of private morality

The responsibilities of statecraft require decisions that trade harms against harms in ways private ethical reasoning does not encounterI want to close with what I regard as the deepest disagreement between myself and my critics — one that goes beyond the specific facts of Cambodia to the nature of statesmanship itself. The responsibility of someone charged with the conduct of a great power's foreign policy during an ongoing war is not the responsibility of a private individual making an isolated ethical choice. It is the responsibility of weighing competing harms against one another under conditions of profound uncertainty, with the lives of one's own soldiers, the credibility of one's alliance commitments, the strategic position of one's country relative to its adversaries, and yes, the lives of civilians in the territories where the war is being fought, all simultaneously at stake — and weighing them not in the comfort of retrospective clarity but in the fog of an active, ongoing conflict whose further course could not be known with certainty at the time decisions had to be made. I do not say this to claim immunity from moral judgment. I say it to insist that the judgment, to be fair, must be rendered with some recognition of the actual conditions of decision rather than judged purely by its consequences as they are now known, decades later, with the benefit of information that was not, and in many cases could not have been, available to those who made the decisions. History will render whatever verdict it renders. I ask only that the verdict account for the actual alternatives that existed in March 1969, not the imagined alternatives constructed by those who never had to choose among them.

Thank you.

The Indictment
Christopher Hitchens
The Trial of Henry Kissinger (Verso, 2001)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Dr Kissinger,

I have read your defence with the attention it deserves, because the case for the prosecution must be built on facts, not merely on the moral disgust that your record so amply justifies. I wrote a book, The Trial of Henry Kissinger, that calls explicitly for your prosecution — not your condemnation in the court of opinion, where you have already been thoroughly condemned and have thoroughly survived it, but your prosecution under the law, for war crimes, for crimes against humanity, and for offenses against customary international law, including conspiracy to commit murder. I want to make the case as a lawyer would make it: with documents, with dates, with the testimony of those who were there, and with your own words, recorded in the conversations you did not know — or perhaps knew perfectly well — would someday be declassified.

I. The bombing began with full knowledge of its effect on civilians and full intent to deceive

The Menu bombing began with deliberate deception of Congress, the Pentagon's own chain of command, and the American public — and you knew, from the outset, what it would do to civiliansI wrote, of the bombing campaign you have just defended, that it "began as it was to go on — with full knowledge of its effect on civilians, and with flagrant deceit being employed in order to conceal the facts." I want to substantiate this rather than simply restate it. Operation Menu was structured from its inception around a dual-bookkeeping system: strikes conducted inside Cambodia were logged in official Air Force records as having occurred inside South Vietnam. This was not merely "keeping a secret from Congress" in the sense of declining to brief a committee. It was the deliberate falsification of military records, designed and maintained by your office, to prevent even the Pentagon's own internal chain of command from accurately tracking what was being done in its name. When Major Hal Knight, one of the officers who participated in this falsification, eventually became troubled enough by its legality to write to Senator Proxmire seeking clarification, the system you built had already been running for years. You did not stumble into secrecy as a regrettable necessity. You designed an apparatus of deception with the specific purpose of ensuring that the institutions constitutionally charged with oversight of the war power — Congress, and elements of the military itself — could not perform that oversight. This is not the conduct of a statesman making a difficult but defensible judgment under pressure. It is the conduct of a man who knew the action could not survive scrutiny and built a structure to evade it.

II. "Anything that flies on anything that moves": the documented disregard for civilian distinction

Your own recorded instructions reveal an explicit abandonment of the distinction between military targets and the general populationYou have told this audience that the strikes targeted military base areas, not Cambodian civilians, and that the targeting reflected careful intelligence rather than indifference. I want to put before this audience a phrase that has been attributed to you, on the basis of declassified conversations, and that I cited at length in my book: the instruction that American air power in Cambodia should strike "anything that flies on anything that moves." This is not the language of discriminating strikes against confirmed military concentrations. It is the language of a free-fire zone, in which the absence of certainty that a target is military is treated as insufficient grounds for restraint. The expansion of bombing under the subsequent "Freedom Deal" operation, after Congress had at least nominally constrained the targeting area, saw the tonnage dropped in 1973 alone — over 257,000 tons — approach the combined total of the previous four years, an escalation that occurred specifically as oversight mechanisms were tightening, not as military necessity demanded. A campaign that escalates its tonnage as legal constraint increases is not a campaign conducted with careful attention to discrimination. It is a campaign racing to do as much damage as possible before the door closes.

III. The 1970 coup and the deliberate destruction of the one path that might have spared Cambodia

The Sihanouk overthrow, immediately recognised by your administration, removed the one figure capable of containing the conflict and triggered the Khmer Rouge's transformation into a mass movementYou argue that no credible evidence of direct American orchestration of the March 1970 coup against Sihanouk has been produced, and on the narrowest reading of "orchestration" — direct CIA authorship of the coup plot itself — this is a defensible if convenient position. But I want to draw attention to what is not contested: the speed and enthusiasm with which your administration recognised the Lon Nol government that resulted from the coup, abandoning the diplomatic caution that recognition of an unconstitutional change of government normally requires, and the years of prior covert pressure your administration had exerted upon a Cambodian leader who, however imperfect his neutrality, had at least kept the worst of the war's violence away from Phnom Penh for most of a decade. Sihanouk himself stated, repeatedly and without equivocation, "there are only two men responsible for the tragedy in Cambodia: Mr Nixon and Dr Kissinger." You ask this audience to discount the testimony of the man whose overthrow you immediately recognised and whose country you had been secretly bombing for a year before that overthrow occurred. I ask this audience to consider whose account of causation deserves more weight: the man who lost his country, or the man who was bombing it.

IV. The CIA's own assessment confirms what you deny: the bombing built the Khmer Rouge

A declassified 1973 CIA cable confirms, in the agency's own contemporaneous assessment, that Khmer Rouge recruitment was built directly on the bombing's devastationYou argue that the bombing was "exploited by" rather than "created" the Khmer Rouge, and you ask this distinction to do a great deal of work. I want to put before you a document that your own government produced: a CIA cable from 1973, declassified decades later, which states plainly that Khmer Rouge cadres were using damage caused by B-52 strikes as the main theme of their propaganda, telling the rural population that the only way to stop the destruction was to remove Lon Nol and that the quickest way to accomplish this was to strengthen Khmer Rouge forces. This is not retrospective historical interpretation produced by your critics decades later. This is your own intelligence service, at the time, in a classified internal document not intended for public consumption or moral point-scoring, describing the precise mechanism by which the bombing functioned as the Khmer Rouge's most effective recruitment instrument. When the Khmer Rouge numbered some five thousand poorly organised adherents at the time Operation Menu began and controlled most of Cambodia's countryside by 1973, the CIA's own document tells us how that transformation occurred. You ask this tribunal to disregard your own agency's contemporaneous assessment in favour of a more comfortable causal account constructed after the fact.

V. The deceit of the American public was not incidental — it continued under oath

The deception extended to direct lies told to the American public, including by Nixon, and your own subsequent telephone conversations reveal a man managing political fallout, not wrestling with moral weightI want to draw attention to a specific lie, told not in a classified memorandum but directly to the American people, by the man you served. On 30 April 1970, announcing the ground invasion of Cambodia, President Nixon stated: "For five years neither the United States nor South Vietnam has moved against these enemy sanctuaries because we did not wish to violate the territory of a neutral nation." This was a year after Operation Menu had begun. It was a direct, deliberate falsehood, told to the American public by the President you served as National Security Advisor, about a policy you had personally administered. I have also placed before readers a transcript of your conversation with Secretary of Defense Melvin Laird in August 1973, as Congress investigated the secret bombing: you complain to Laird that you lack support from officials testifying in the hearings, and you say, "I mean, we saved American lives. There were North Vietnamese troops there. The government of the country we were bombing were asking us to do it. What the hell — one sometimes thinks we are in a madhouse." This is not the voice of a man wrestling, even years later, with the moral weight of mass civilian death. It is the voice of a man irritated that his political colleagues will not adequately defend a policy under congressional scrutiny. The tone of your own recorded words, available to anyone who consults the National Security Archive, does not match the solemn statesman's burden you have described to this audience today.

VI. The casualty estimates you dispute are corroborated by independent humanitarian assessment

Independent USAID and refugee data, not merely retrospective interview-based estimates, corroborate the scale of bombing-driven civilian death and displacementYou suggest that the casualty figures most commonly cited rest on fragile, retrospective methodology. I want to point to evidence that is neither retrospective nor reliant on the interview-based extrapolation you criticise. A report produced by the United States Agency for International Development — your own government's development arm, working contemporaneously and not decades later — found that the intensive American bombing of 1973 alone increased the cumulative number of Cambodian refugees to nearly half the country's population. This is a real-time bureaucratic assessment, not a retrospective historiographical reconstruction, and it documents displacement on a scale that corroborates rather than undermines the broader claims about bombing's devastating impact on the Cambodian population. The declassified Air Force bombing data itself — released by President Clinton in 2000, against the wishes of many in the foreign policy establishment who had spent thirty years minimising the scale of the campaign — documents 500,000 tons of ordnance dropped on 113,716 separate targets, a figure roughly three times larger than what had previously been acknowledged publicly. You ask this audience to doubt the casualty estimates. I ask this audience to notice that every revision of the documentary record, as it has been declassified over decades, has revised the scale of the campaign upward, not downward, and that the institution doing the revising has consistently been the United States government's own records, not the speculative reconstructions of your critics.

VII. The "moral asymmetry" argument is the oldest alibi in the history of state violence

The argument that the victim's prior provocation excuses the disproportionate response is precisely the reasoning that international law and basic moral reasoning exist to rejectYou argue that the moral accounting must include North Vietnam's prior violation of Cambodian neutrality, and that critics who begin their analysis with the American response rather than the North Vietnamese provocation have predetermined their conclusion. I want to be direct about what this argument actually is. It is the argument that a wrong committed by one party licenses a disproportionate response by another party against a third party who was not, primarily, responsible for the original wrong — namely, the Cambodian civilian population, who did not choose to have North Vietnamese sanctuaries established on their territory and who bore the overwhelming weight of the American response to that choice. This reasoning — that the existence of a provocation by combatant A justifies devastating harm to civilian population C, who happen to share a border with combatant B's chosen sanctuary — is not a serious principle of just war theory. It is the oldest alibi in the history of state violence: he started it, so what happened to the people in between is not really our doing. International humanitarian law exists precisely to prevent this reasoning from operating as a license, because if it is accepted, there is no war conducted on or near civilian populations that cannot be retrospectively justified by reference to what the enemy did first.

VIII. The "statesman's burden" argument is special pleading, and it has been rejected at Nuremberg

The claim that the responsibilities of high office generate a different moral standard than applies to ordinary persons is precisely the defence that the Nuremberg principles were established to rejectYou close your defence with the claim that statesmanship requires weighing harms in ways that private moral reasoning does not encounter, and that fair judgment must account for the actual conditions of decision rather than apply an absolute standard no wartime government could satisfy. I want to be precise about what this argument is, because it has a name and a history, and the history is not flattering to it. It is the argument that those who hold high office, and who weigh consequences across populations and strategic interests, are entitled to a different — a more permissive — moral and legal standard than applies to those who do not hold such office. This is precisely the defence that the Nuremberg principles, to which the United States itself was a primary architect and signatory, were established to reject: that the scale of one's responsibilities and the complexity of one's decisions does not exempt the decision-maker from accountability for the predictable human consequences of those decisions, and that "I was weighing many factors under difficult conditions" is not a defence available to those responsible for war crimes, however senior their office or however genuine their belief that they acted in their nation's interest. I called for your prosecution not because I doubt that you weighed many factors, Dr Kissinger. I called for it because the factors you weighed included, by your own and your government's contemporaneous account, the deliberate, sustained, and concealed bombardment of a population that bore no responsibility for the war being fought over its head — and the law does not recognise "I had a great many things to consider" as an answer to that charge.

Thank you.

1.Nixon's 30 April 1970 statement that the US had not moved against Cambodian sanctuaries "because we did not wish to violate the territory of a neutral nation" was a direct, deliberate falsehood — Operation Menu had been running for over a year.

∴The documentary record of what was actually said, at the time, by the decision-makers themselves, contradicts the retrospective characterisation of grave moral deliberation; it shows political management of a scandal, not the weight of conscience.
Argument VI — Contemporaneous US government data, not retrospective estimation, corroborates the bombing's devastating scaleclick for image
1.A contemporaneous USAID report found that the intensive 1973 bombing alone increased Cambodia's cumulative refugee population to nearly half the country — real-time bureaucratic data, not retrospective interview-based extrapolation.
2.The declassified Air Force bombing dataset, released in 2000, documents 500,000 tons dropped on 113,716 targets — roughly three times the figure previously acknowledged publicly, revising the scale upward as more records were declassified.
3.Every successive declassification of the documentary record has increased, not decreased, the known scale of the campaign — the opposite pattern from what Kissinger's "inflated estimates" argument requires.
∴The trajectory of the documentary record contradicts the claim that civilian impact estimates are methodologically fragile overstatements; it shows a consistent pattern of underestimation being corrected as more government records become available.
Argument VII — "They violated it first" is not a defence international law recognisesclick for image
1.Kissinger's moral-asymmetry argument holds that North Vietnam's prior violation of Cambodian neutrality licenses the disproportionate American response that followed.
2.This reasoning shifts responsibility for harm to a third party (Cambodian civilians) who did not choose the sanctuary arrangement between the two original combatants, onto whom the disproportionate response actually fell.
3.International humanitarian law exists specifically to prevent "the enemy provoked it" reasoning from operating as a license for harm to civilian populations, since accepting it would justify nearly any war conducted near civilians by reference to what an adversary did first.
∴The moral-asymmetry defence is not a serious principle of just war reasoning but the oldest rationalisation for disproportionate violence against populations who bore no primary responsibility for the underlying conflict.
Argument VIII — The "statesman's burden" defence is precisely what Nuremberg rejectedclick for image
1.Kissinger's closing argument claims that high office and the complexity of wartime decision-making generate a different, more permissive moral and legal standard than applies to private individuals.
2.The Nuremberg principles, which the United States itself was instrumental in establishing, were specifically constructed to reject the claim that scale of responsibility or complexity of decision exempts officeholders from accountability for the predictable consequences of their decisions.
3."I was weighing many factors under difficult conditions" is not, under the legal framework the United States itself championed after the Second World War, an available defence against charges of war crimes.
∴The call for prosecution does not rest on doubting that Kissinger weighed many factors; it rests on the principle, established at Nuremberg with American leadership, that weighing many factors is not a legal answer to a charge of deliberately concealed, sustained bombardment of a population not responsible for the underlying war.
