Mr President,

Let me begin with the paradox itself, stated with the precision the debate requires. Consider a sentence — call it L — that says of itself exactly that it is not true:

L: "This sentence is not true."

Now reason classically. Suppose L is true. Then what it says holds — so L is not true. Contradiction. Suppose L is not true. Then what it says holds — so L is true. Contradiction. Every assumption leads to contradiction. Something in the reasoning must be surrendered. The opposition will tell you it is self-reference that must go, or the T-schema, or a level distinction they can smuggle in. I want to argue that each of these manoeuvres fails — and that the correct response, which Hartry Field and I have developed in different but complementary ways, is to revise classical logic at the point where it most directly generates the paradox: the principle of bivalence, the assumption that every sentence is either true or false.

"The liar sentence is not true, and it is not false. It belongs to a third category — sentences that fail to reach a definite truth value through any grounded process of semantic evaluation. This is not a concession of defeat. It is the correct description of what the sentence is: semantically indeterminate, in a way that a rigorous formal theory can capture and reason about without generating contradiction."
i. the fixed-point construction — Kripke's solution

Fixed-point — grounded sentences; stages of evaluation; liar never stabilises; gaps are principledIn my 1975 paper "Outline of a Theory of Truth," I constructed a formal model in which sentences are evaluated in stages, beginning from a classical base of atomic sentences whose truth values are determined by the non-semantic facts. At each stage, the truth predicate is extended: a sentence gets the value true if it was grounded at some stage as true, false if it was grounded as false. The evaluation continues until a fixed point is reached — a stage at which no new assignments are made. Sentences like "Snow is white" reach their truth value early and stably. The liar sentence never stabilises: assigning it true requires subsequently assigning it false, and vice versa. It therefore falls outside the extension of the truth predicate at the fixed point. It is neither true nor false — it occupies what I call a truth-value gap. This is not an ad hoc stipulation; it is the natural outcome of the most principled bottom-up construction of a truth predicate that anyone has offered.

ii. the T-schema without bivalence

T-schema preserved for grounded sentences; bivalence restricted; T-schema only fails for paradoxical sentencesA central virtue of the fixed-point approach is that the T-schema — "P is true if and only if P" — is preserved for all grounded sentences. For "Snow is white," the sentence is true if and only if snow is white: the schema holds. The schema fails only for the liar and other semantically indeterminate sentences — and this failure is principled rather than ad hoc, because these sentences are precisely the ones that fail to be grounded. We do not restrict the T-schema globally. We restrict bivalence — the assumption that every sentence must be either true or false — in a way that is independently motivated by the fact that some sentences are not semantically connected to the world in any way that would determine their truth value. Hartry Field's extension of my approach adds a conditional operator satisfying most classical conditional laws, enabling reasoning about the liar without generating contradiction, and closing the most significant technical gap my original construction left open.

iii. why the opposition's hierarchy fails

Hierarchy critique — stratification is artificial; ordinary language is semantically closed; hierarchy cannot capture all truthsTarski's hierarchical solution — which the opposition will defend — stratifies language into levels: truth predicates at level n can only apply to sentences at levels below n. The liar paradox is blocked because a sentence cannot predicate truth of itself; it belongs to a lower level than its own truth predicate. This solution is technically impeccable within formal systems. As a description of natural language, it is a disaster. Ordinary English does not sort its truth predicates by level. When I say "everything on this list is true" I use a single truth predicate ranging over sentences of any level, including the one I am currently uttering. The hierarchy solution deals with the paradox by describing a language nobody speaks. It also cannot capture the generalisation "every truth is knowable" or "every sentence is either true or false" — generalisations that require quantifying over all levels simultaneously. These are things we want to say, and the hierarchy prevents us from saying them.

iv. the strengthened liar and Field's response

Strengthened liar — "this sentence is not true or gappy"; revenge; Field's indeterminate responseThe most serious objection to gap theories — including mine — is the strengthened liar. Consider the sentence:

S: "This sentence is not true or does not have a determinate truth value."

If S has no truth value (a gap), then it is not determinately true — which is exactly what S says. So S seems to be true after all. The gap has generated the paradox at a higher level — this is what theorists call "revenge." Hartry Field's response in Saving Truth from Paradox is subtle: he argues that the notion of "determinacy" used in the strengthened liar is itself indeterminate — that S generates not a new paradox but an ascent into a hierarchy of indeterminacies, each of which is itself indeterminate. This does not fully dissolve the revenge problem, and Field is honest about the remaining difficulties. But it demonstrates that the gap approach handles a substantially larger class of paradoxes than critics acknowledge, and that the revenge problem is a continuing challenge for all theories, not a decisive refutation of non-classical approaches specifically.

v. the naturalness of semantic indeterminacy

Semantic indeterminacy — vagueness; reference failure; indeterminate sentences are a natural categoryThe category of truth-value gaps is not invented for the liar paradox. It is the natural category for sentences that are semantically defective in a principled way: sentences involving reference failure ("The present King of France is bald"), sentences involving radical vagueness at the borderline ("This borderline heap is a heap"), and sentences like the liar that are not grounded in any non-semantic fact. These are genuinely different from sentences that are true and those that are false, and the refusal to admit a third category is an ideological commitment to bivalence, not a logical necessity. The opposition defends classical bivalence as though it were a law of nature. It is a simplifying assumption — useful for most purposes, but unable to accommodate the full complexity of semantic phenomena, of which the liar is the most vivid illustration.

Mr President,

My colleague Kripke has defended truth-value gaps — the view that the liar sentence is neither true nor false. I want to argue for the opposite revision of classical logic: that the liar sentence is both true and false simultaneously, and that this is not the reductio it appears to be, but the correct description of a genuine dialetheia — a true contradiction. This is the position of dialetheism, and I have defended it at length in In Contradiction and The Logic of Paradox. It requires us to abandon not the law of excluded middle, as Kripke does, but the principle of explosion — the classical inference from a contradiction to anything whatsoever.

"The liar paradox does not merely threaten our theory of truth. It reveals something deep and correct about the structure of reality: that some contradictions are true. The response is not to retreat into gaps or hierarchies — evacuating the problematic sentence of all truth value — but to accept the contradiction and build a logic strong enough to reason within it without collapsing into triviality."
i. the argument from the strengthened liar — the gap theory refutes itself

Strengthened liar — gap theories generate their own paradox; "not true" captures both false and gappyThe most powerful argument for dialetheism over the gap theory is that gap theories generate their own paradox more vividly than the dialetheist position does. Consider the strengthened liar:

S: "This sentence is not true."
On Kripke's theory, the liar sentence L is neither true nor false — it has no truth value. But if L has no truth value, then L is not true. But L says of itself that it is not true. So if L is not true, then what L says holds — so L is true. We have derived that L is both true and something — not true or gappy. The gap theory generates the contradiction it was designed to avoid. The dialetheist has a clean story: L is true and false, exactly as it appears to be. The "revenge" problem that Kripke's theory faces is internal to the gap approach. The dialetheist embraces the contradiction rather than being surprised by it.

ii. LP — the logic of paradox

LP — three values; both; LEM retained; Explosion rejected; paraconsistencyMy Logic of Paradox (LP) is a three-valued logic with values true, false, and both. Unlike Kripke's three-valued logic K3 — which has truth-value gaps — LP has truth-value gluts: the liar sentence receives the value both. LP retains the law of excluded middle (every sentence is either true or both, covering all cases) but rejects the principle of explosion — ex contradictione quodlibet — the inference from a contradiction to anything whatsoever. In classical logic, if both P and not-P are true, then every sentence is true — the system trivialises. LP blocks this by treating both as a designated value that does not propagate to arbitrary conclusions. This preserves the ability to reason within a theory containing contradictions without the theory implying everything — which is what makes paraconsistent logic a genuine logic rather than a mere concession to chaos.

iii. dialetheism and the T-schema

T-schema fully preserved — no restriction; dialetheia are genuine truths; the paradox is a solutionDialetheism's decisive advantage over both the hierarchy approach and the gap approach is that it preserves the T-schema without restriction. Every instance of "P is true if and only if P" holds — including for the liar. That the liar is true follows from the T-schema applied to the fact that the liar sentence asserts its own untruth. That the liar is false follows from the T-schema applied to the fact that if the liar is true, then what it says holds — that it is not true. Both assignments follow from an unrestricted T-schema. The contradiction is not a failure of the theory; it is what the theory correctly predicts. JC Beall's extension of this position into a coherent theory of transparent truth — in which the truth predicate is fully substitutable in any non-opaque context — shows that a fully naïve, unrestricted theory of truth is both formally consistent within paraconsistent logic and expressively complete in ways that the restricted theories are not.

iv. the inclosure schema — the paradox has a uniform structure

Inclosure schema — Russell, Burali-Forti, liar all share structure; dialetheism provides uniform solutionIn my analysis of the paradoxes of self-reference, I identify a common structure — the inclosure schema — that underlies the liar, Russell's paradox, Burali-Forti's ordinal paradox, Cantor's theorem, and others. Each paradox involves a totality Ω and a diagonalisation function δ that produces an object inside and outside Ω simultaneously: δ(Ω) ∈ Ω and δ(Ω) ∉ Ω. For the liar, Ω is the set of true sentences, and the liar sentence is both a member (because it asserts its own non-membership, making it true) and a non-member (because being true makes it false). Dialetheism provides a uniform solution to all inclosure paradoxes: wherever the inclosure schema applies, a dialetheia exists at the boundary. The alternatives — hierarchy solutions, gap solutions — must handle each paradox ad hoc, applying different restrictions to different cases. The structural uniformity of the dialetheist solution is a powerful point in its favour.

v. is explosion so obvious?

Explosion — not self-evidently valid; natural deduction suspicion; relevance logicians concurThe opposition will object that rejecting explosion — the inference from contradiction to anything — amounts to abandoning logic itself. This objection confuses classical logic with logic as such. Explosion is a theorem of classical logic, not a self-evident logical truth. Relevance logicians — following Anderson and Belnap — have independently argued that explosion is invalid on grounds of relevance: a conclusion is not validly inferred from premises if the conclusion shares no propositional content with the premises. "The liar is true and not true, therefore the moon is made of cheese" is not a valid inference on any plausible account of what makes an inference valid: the moon's constitution has nothing to do with the liar. If explosion is independently suspect, rejecting it to accommodate the liar is not special pleading — it is the application of an independently motivated revision to a case that clearly requires it.

Mr President,

The proposition's speakers have presented the liar paradox as a refutation of classical logic. I want to argue the opposite: that the liar paradox is a refutation of a specific and independently questionable feature of certain natural and formal languages — the feature of semantic closure — and that once this feature is correctly identified and eliminated, classical logic proceeds without modification. The paradox does not show that classical logic is wrong. It shows that a language cannot coherently contain its own truth predicate while also permitting unrestricted self-reference. That is a finding about the limits of semantic self-description, not a finding about the laws of logic.

"The source of the paradox is not in logic — it is in language. Natural languages are semantically closed: they allow sentences to express truths about their own truth values. This is an accident of natural language, not a necessity of logic. The correct response is to construct a hierarchy of formal languages in which each level can express truths about the level below it but not about itself. Within this hierarchy, every sentence has a determinate truth value, classical logic applies without restriction, and the liar paradox simply cannot be formulated."
i. semantic closure is the culprit — Tarski's diagnosis

Semantic closure — T-schema + self-reference = paradox; hierarchy eliminates semantic closureMy diagnosis of the paradox — presented in "The Concept of Truth in Formalized Languages" (1933/1936) — is precise. The paradox is generated by the conjunction of three features: (a) the language contains the T-schema as a logical law; (b) the language contains its own truth predicate applying to sentences of the same language; (c) self-referential sentences can be constructed within the language. Each feature alone is harmless. The combination is lethal. My solution is to separate the truth predicate from the object language: every formalised language has a truth predicate only in its metalanguage — the language used to talk about it. No sentence can apply a truth predicate to itself, because the predicate belongs to a higher-level language. The paradox is not blocked by adding a new axiom or revising a logical law; it is dissolved by correctly articulating the semantic architecture that a language must have to avoid self-undermining.

ii. Tarski's Undefinability Theorem

Undefinability Theorem — no language can define its own truth predicate; proved from Gödel's diagonalisationMy Undefinability Theorem gives a rigorous proof — using Gödel's diagonalisation technique — that no sufficiently expressive language can define its own truth predicate without contradiction. This is not a contingent finding about particular languages; it is a theorem provable within classical mathematics. If a language L contains arithmetic and a truth predicate for L, we can construct a sentence G of L that is true if and only if it is not in the extension of the truth predicate — replicating the liar's structure. Classical logic, applied within the metalanguage, then proves that L's truth predicate is indefinable within L. The proposition treats this as an embarrassment for classical logic. It is, correctly understood, a triumph: classical logic rigorously proves its own limits, which is precisely what a reliable logical system should do.

iii. Quine's dissolution — the paradox is a pseudo-problem in formal contexts

Quine — quotation and semantic ascent; the liar conflates use and mention; pseudo-problem for natural languageW. V. O. Quine approached the paradox from a different angle. In The Ways of Paradox, Quine argues that the liar paradox arises from a confusion between use and mention — between a sentence and its name. When we say "this sentence is not true," we are using the sentence while mentioning it: the sentence refers to itself. But self-reference of this kind — achieved through quotation or through devices like "this sentence" — is a trick of natural language that can be diagnosed and eliminated without revising logic. In formalised languages equipped with a proper use-mention distinction, the liar sentence either cannot be formulated or can be shown to violate a well-defined syntactic constraint. The apparent depth of the paradox is an illusion generated by the looseness of natural language. A sufficiently rigorous formal language has no liar.

iv. the charge of ad hocness — answered

Ad hoc charge — hierarchy is principled; fundamental principle of truth underlies it; T-schema restricted for good reasonThe proposition charges that the hierarchy solution is ad hoc: stratified to block the paradox rather than motivated independently. This charge is answered by the Undefinability Theorem itself. The hierarchy is not an arbitrary choice to avoid one bad sentence; it is the formal expression of a theorem about what truth predicates can do — namely, that they cannot consistently apply to their own language. The restriction on the T-schema that the hierarchy imposes — allowing "P is true" only when P belongs to a lower level — is justified by the same theorem that shows unrestricted self-application generates contradiction. The philosophical principle underlying the hierarchy is: truth is always truth-in-a-language, and the language that applies the truth predicate must be richer than the language to which it applies. This is a substantial philosophical claim, not a technical dodge.

v. against the non-classical alternatives

Against gaps and gluts — both accept paradoxical sentences as meaningful; hierarchy denies this; classical logic preservedBoth gap theories and dialetheism accept the liar sentence as meaningful and then dispute what truth value it receives. I take a more radical position: the liar sentence, in the context of a properly constructed formal language, is not meaningful in any way that generates paradox. It is a sentence that violates the syntactic constraints of the language — specifically, the constraint that truth predicates apply only to sentences of lower-level languages. Treating it as meaningful and then arguing about whether it has no truth value, both truth values, or some novel third status is to accept the question on the paradox's own terms. The correct response is to deny that the question arises: in any well-constructed language, the liar sentence is ill-formed or inexpressible. Both non-classical alternatives preserve the paradoxical sentence's meaningfulness at the cost of classical logic. The hierarchy dissolves the sentence's meaningfulness and preserves classical logic. This is the superior trade-off.

Mr President,

My opposition colleague Tarski has argued that the solution requires a formal hierarchy of languages. I want to offer a different classical response: one that preserves classical logic, allows unrestricted self-reference in natural language, but shows that the liar paradox rests on a misunderstanding of how truth predicates actually work in context-sensitive natural language. Two distinct but related approaches converge on this response: Tyler Burge's account of truth as an indexical predicate sensitive to pragmatic context, and Arthur Prior's argument that every statement implicitly asserts its own truth — an insight that, if correct, dissolves the paradox before it gets started.

"The liar paradox presents the sentence 'This sentence is false' as though it were a stable, context-independent object with a fixed content. But the truth predicate is not like that. When I utter 'That is true,' what I am applying the truth predicate to depends on context: which sentence I am talking about, which language I am in, what my conversational purposes are. The liar sentence, examined carefully, either has a perfectly determinate truth value given its context, or it changes the context in which it is evaluated and becomes determinate in that new context."
i. Burge — truth as a context-sensitive indexical

Burge — truth predicate indexical; context determines extension; liar shifts context; truth value determinate in each contextTyler Burge's 1979 paper "Semantical Paradox" argues that the truth predicate is context-sensitive in the way indexical expressions are: just as "I" refers to the current speaker and "now" refers to the current time, "true" applies to the extension determined by the current pragmatic context. Different contexts of utterance can assign different extensions to the truth predicate. When we reason through the liar paradox — "the liar is true, so the liar is false, so the liar is true" — we are not reasoning within a single fixed context. Each step of the reasoning shifts the context: the truth predicate is being applied at successively higher contextual levels. At any given context level, the liar sentence has a perfectly determinate truth value — it is false at the context at which it is first evaluated, because it asserts something that fails at that context. The paradox arises only if we conflate the truth predicate's extension across different contextual levels, which is the same mistake as thinking "I" means the same thing when different speakers utter it.

ii. the contextualist hierarchy — implicit not formal

Implicit hierarchy — context-shifting is natural; no formal level assignment needed; ordinary speakers navigate itBurge's crucial advantage over Tarski's hierarchy is that it does not require explicitly assigning formal level numbers to linguistic expressions. The hierarchy is implicit in the contextual dynamics of ordinary language. When I say "Everything John said is true" and John has said "Everything Mary said is false" and Mary has said "Everything John said is true," the apparent regress is navigated by the natural context-shifting of ordinary truth attribution: at each stage of evaluation, the truth predicate is applied at a higher contextual level than the one being evaluated. No sentence needs to be labelled "level 7 in Tarski's hierarchy"; the context-shifting happens automatically. This preserves the naturalness of ordinary truth-talk while blocking the paradox through the same mechanism Tarski employs — but at the level of pragmatics rather than formal syntax.

iii. Prior's implicit assertion — every statement implies its own truth

Prior — implicit self-assertion of truth; liar additionally asserts its falsity; net content is inconsistent; no paradox arisesArthur Prior's dissolution of the paradox, attributed by him to Charles Peirce and John Buridan, begins from the observation that every statement implicitly asserts its own truth. When I assert "It is raining," I am not only asserting that it is raining but also asserting that it is true that it is raining — the truth-assertion is carried by the act of assertion itself. Now consider the liar sentence: "This sentence is false." On Prior's account, this sentence asserts two things: (a) that it is true (by the implicit assertion involved in any statement), and (b) that it is false (by its explicit content). These two assertions are jointly inconsistent — but they are jointly inconsistent in a way that makes the sentence simply false. The sentence says it is true and says it is false; since it says something false (namely that it is true), it is false. The apparent paradox — that the liar's being false makes it true — does not arise because the implicit truth-assertion is built into the sentence's assertoric character, not into its semantic content evaluable for truth.

iv. the revenge objection — contextualism's strongest challenge

Revenge — "this sentence is false at every context"; contextualism faces its own strengthened versionThe revenge problem for contextualism is the sentence: "This sentence is false at every context." This sentence, if it is false at some context C, is true — because it says it is false at every context, including C. But if it is true, it is false at every context, including the one at which it is true. This strengthened liar apparently regenerates the paradox even after the context-shift move. I acknowledge this is the strongest objection to contextualist solutions. My response is that "at every context" is itself a context-sensitive quantifier: the contexts it ranges over are determined by the conversational context in which the sentence is uttered. When the liar sentence shifts us to a higher context, the quantifier "every context" shifts too — it now ranges over a larger collection of contexts, at which the sentence again receives a determinate value. The regress is potentially infinite, but at each stage the sentence has a determinate truth value relative to its evaluative context.

v. why classical logic should be preserved

Classical logic — minimal mutilation principle; track record; non-classical logics lack inferential strengthI want to close with a principled defence of preserving classical logic. The principle of minimal mutilation — revise as little as possible when confronted with a recalcitrant datum — applies with special force in logic, the most general of our inferential systems. Classical logic's track record is unparalleled: it underlies mathematics, scientific reasoning, formal linguistics, and computer science. Every non-classical alternative sacrifices inferential strength: gap theories lose the law of excluded middle; dialetheism loses the principle of explosion; both lose theorems that are useful and apparently valid in reasoning outside the paradox context. Preserving classical logic at the cost of expressive restriction or contextual complexity in truth attribution is the right trade-off: it keeps the powerful inferential machinery of classical logic intact while treating the paradox as a finding about language's semantic self-description rather than a refutation of logic itself.
