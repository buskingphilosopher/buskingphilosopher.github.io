Mr President,

I am not here to debate principles. I have been arguing principles for twenty years and I suspect most people in this room have heard them. What I want to do today is something simpler and more uncomfortable: I want to describe what the monarchy is actually doing — not in theory, but in practice — based on documented evidence that the institution has fought, through every legal mechanism available to it, to keep secret.

When you assemble these cases together, they do not describe an eccentric but harmless ceremonial institution. They describe a family that is systematically using its unique legal immunities to extract money from the public, suppress scrutiny, corrupt public appointments, and protect its own members from criminal consequences — while telling the rest of us it has no political role.

I. Queen's Consent: secret lobbying over a thousand bills

Queen's Consent: secret lobbying of ParliamentIn February 2021, the Guardian published documents from the National Archives revealing that the Queen had used a parliamentary procedure called Queen's Consent — which gives the palace advance sight of any legislation affecting Crown interests — to secretly lobby the government to change the 1973 Companies Act. Her private lawyer, Matthew Farrer of Farrer & Co., was dispatched to the Department of Trade to demand a clause exempting companies used by heads of state from new transparency measures. A civil servant recorded: the disclosure "would be embarrassing because of the risk of leaks." The government complied, inserting Section 27(9) into the Companies Act and then establishing Bank of England Nominees Limited as the vehicle for concealing the Queen's private shareholdings. The Queen's Consent had been invoked over a thousand times since 1953, on legislation as recent as the post-Brexit trade deal. Constitutional scholars told the Guardian that this gave the palace "the kind of influence over legislation that lobbyists would only dream of."

"The consent process, which gives the Queen and her lawyers advance sight of bills coming into parliament, has enabled her to secretly lobby for legislative changes." — The Guardian, 7 February 2021, David Pegg & Rob Evans

Buckingham Palace's response was to deny having "blocked" legislation — a response the Guardian's investigations editor noted was a deliberate mischaracterisation of the allegation, which concerned lobbying for changes, not vetoes. The palace has never answered the substance.

II. The black spider memos: Charles lobbies eight government departments

Charles's black spider memos: secret ministerial lobbyingBeginning in 2010, the Guardian's reporter Rob Evans spent five years fighting the government under the Freedom of Information Act to obtain copies of letters Charles had written to ministers in seven departments — letters that became known as the "black spider memos" for his distinctive scrawled handwriting. The government fought the disclosure through every level of the tribunal system. When the letters were finally published in 2015, after the Attorney General's attempt to suppress them was overturned, they revealed Charles lobbying on planning applications, health policy, architecture, badger culling, military equipment procurement, and herbal medicine regulation. A senior special adviser told the FOI tribunal that letters from Charles went "to the top of the pile" and were "treated with great reverence" — entirely unlike correspondence from the public. The tribunal found that Charles was using his access to ministers to "set up and drive forward charities and promote views" and ruled that this lobbying "cannot have constitutional status." His correspondence had secured, among other things, a £1.1 million government grant to his Foundation for Integrated Health, which his lobbying of Health Secretary Patricia Hewitt was found to have influenced.

"Ministers responded, and no doubt felt themselves obliged to respond... it was fundamental that Charles's lobbying cannot have constitutional status." — First-tier Tribunal ruling, September 2012; FOI Appeal Chamber III. The Duchy Files: charging the NHS, prisons and schools rent

Duchy of Cornwall and Lancaster: charging public bodies rentIn November 2024, a joint investigation by the Sunday Times and Channel 4 Dispatches revealed the full picture of how the Duchies of Lancaster and Cornwall — together generating more than £50 million a year for the King and Prince of Wales respectively — derive much of their income from public bodies. The NHS pays rent to the Crown. Schools pay rent. Prisons pay rent. Fire and ambulance services pay rent. The armed forces pay rent. Neither the King nor the Prince of Wales pays capital gains tax or corporation tax on these earnings. A separate Mirror/Dispatches investigation in the same month found that scores of Duchy of Cornwall properties rented to ordinary tenants contained damp and mould and failed minimum legal energy efficiency standards — the same standards that, had any private landlord been found in violation, would have triggered enforcement action. The Duchy is not subject to that enforcement regime. Republic's 2024 analysis calculates that the duchies alone cost the public £99 million a year in lost income that should be flowing to the Treasury rather than to the two most privileged private individuals in the country.

"The scale of profit and hypocrisy is striking... the real embarrassment lies in the detail of their tenants — covering some of the most venerated public institutions in Britain." — The Times, Libby Purves, November 2024; Sunday Times/Channel 4 Dispatches investigation IV. Cash for Honours: the CBE in the Blue Drawing Room

Cash for Honours: Mahfouz Marei Mubarak bin Mahfouz CBEIn 2021, Sunday Times and Guardian reporting revealed that a Saudi billionaire named Mahfouz Marei Mubarak bin Mahfouz received a CBE in a private ceremony in Buckingham Palace's Blue Drawing Room in 2016 — a ceremony that never appeared in the Court Circular and whose existence was concealed from the public. The honour had been arranged by Charles's aide Michael Fawcett, who wrote to an intermediary confirming that he would "support and contribute to" efforts to secure British citizenship and a higher honour for Mahfouz in exchange for millions in donations to Charles's charitable estates, Dumfries House and the Castle of Mey. Metropolitan Police investigated. Fawcett resigned. The Palace repeatedly refused to answer questions about what Charles knew and when. The investigation found that Charles had met Mahfouz personally on multiple occasions while the arrangements were being made, and that the Foreign Office had expedited its advice on the honour within 24 hours of one of those meetings. The full extent of Charles's knowledge has never been established, because the Palace declined to cooperate with the investigation's questions.

"Prince Charles's private secretary had promised a knighthood to a Saudi national in exchange for donations to the Prince's charities." — Sunday Times investigation, 2021; police inquiry confirmed by Metropolitan Police V. Andrew, Epstein, and the £14 million the family paid in secret

Prince Andrew: Epstein, settlement, and taxpayer-funded protectionVirginia Giuffre's civil lawsuit against Prince Andrew, alleging he sexually abused her when she was seventeen years old, was settled in February 2022 for an undisclosed sum — reported by The Sun to be approximately £12–14 million — that was funded substantially by the late Queen Elizabeth II (reported to have contributed around £9.5 million) and by King Charles (who reportedly lent Andrew the remainder). This money came from private royal funds, not the Sovereign Grant — but the same family that operates on public money, claims its finances are private, and maintains a £150 million taxpayer-funded security operation, used that private money to make a serious sex-trafficking-adjacent scandal disappear before the Platinum Jubilee. The settlement included a gagging clause on both parties until after the Jubilee. Andrew retained his taxpayer-funded police protection after being stripped of his royal duties, at a cost described by the government as "unknown." He had also previously used his Metropolitan Police protection officers during visits to Epstein, with released emails suggesting he directed an on-duty officer to investigate his accuser's background. In February 2026, Andrew was arrested on suspicion of misconduct in public office following the release of the Epstein files by the US Department of Justice. The monarchy's response to every stage of this scandal was to suppress, delay, and conceal.

"Gordon Brown demanded a police investigation into whether Mountbatten-Windsor used taxpayer-funded jets and RAF bases to meet Epstein." — CNN, February 2026; The Telegraph; DOJ Epstein file release VI. The true cost: half a billion pounds, almost none of it disclosed

The true cost: £510 million per yearThe Sovereign Grant — the official annual payment to the royal household — stood at £86.3 million in 2024–25 and is rising to £132 million in 2025–26, a 53% increase, because it is pegged to Crown Estate profits which have surged due to offshore wind income. But Republic's 2024 analysis calculates the true cost to the public at over £510 million a year. The components are: £132 million in Sovereign Grant; £150 million in estimated security costs (the government refuses to publish the actual figure); £99 million in Duchy income that should be public revenue; and £96 million in lost commercial revenue from royal residences. The King pays no capital gains tax and no corporation tax. He is exempt from inheritance tax. His private wealth — estimated in the hundreds of millions — has never been disclosed to the public, and the Companies Act clause that the Queen lobbied for in 1973 helped ensure it never would be. We are simultaneously being asked to cut the winter fuel allowance, raid NHS budgets, and tell public sector workers there is no money. There is money. It is in the Duchy of Cornwall.

"How can we talk about cutting the winter fuel allowance while wasting half a billion pounds on the royals? The Sovereign Grant is spiralling out of control." — Graham Smith, Republic, September 2024

The opposition will tell you the monarchy provides stability, continuity, and soft power. I will tell you what it actually provides: secrecy, immunity, and a family that has spent decades using every mechanism of state to enrich itself while evading the accountability it demands of everyone else. That is not a constitutional monarchy. It is a constitutional fraud.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleague Graham Smith has given you the ledger of abuses. I want to explain why the ledger is so long — why, in other words, these abuses are not accidents or isolated failures of individuals but expressions of a structural condition that is irremediable within the monarchical framework. My argument is drawn from the analysis I developed in The Enchanted Glass and from the broader tradition of republican political thought that has tried to make sense of Britain's peculiar failure to achieve constitutional modernity.

On the enchanted glass: monarchy as collective narcotic

Monarchy as ideological mystificationThe British monarchy is not merely a family that holds state offices. It is the central symbolic institution through which British society prevents itself from thinking clearly about power, accountability, and the organisation of the state. What I called the "enchanted glass" in 1988 is the mechanism by which the monarchy projects an image of national identity — continuity, dignity, sacrifice, uniqueness — that displaces the democratic imagination. When citizens look into it, they see themselves as subjects of an ancient lineage rather than as sovereign people who have delegated power to representatives. This is not mere sentimentality. It is a structural feature of the British constitutional settlement that has concrete political consequences: it makes radical democratic reform psychologically unintelligible to a population habituated to imagining political life through the categories of royalty.

On the absence of constitutional codification

Monarchy and the absence of a written constitutionBritain is exceptional among stable democracies in having no codified constitution. The Crown, prerogative powers, and the royal household occupy constitutional space that would, in any modern democracy, be governed by law. The result is precisely the situation my colleague has described: a parliamentary procedure — Queen's Consent — that has never been codified, that is not subject to judicial review, and that has been used to conduct secret lobbying over a thousand pieces of legislation since 1953. A system of financial arrangements — Duchy income, Sovereign Grant, security costs — that the government refuses to disclose in full. An honours system that is formally in the Crown's gift and that has proved, structurally, to be corruptible in ways that no codified system would permit. The monarchy and the absence of constitutional codification are not merely concurrent features of the British settlement. They are causally connected: the monarchy requires the absence of codification to maintain its immunities, and the immunities in turn protect the monarchy from reform.

On Tom Paine and the republican tradition

The republican tradition: Paine, rights, and sovereigntyTom Paine, writing in The Rights of Man in 1791, gave the definitive analysis of what the hereditary principle does to political life. A hereditary legislature, he wrote, is as absurd as a hereditary mathematician. Hereditary monarchy is not merely an inefficient way of selecting a head of state — it is a philosophical error that corrupts the entire constitutional settlement it inhabits. It embeds the claim that some human beings have a right to power by birth — a claim that is incompatible with the equal dignity of citizens that every modern democratic settlement nominally affirms. The British settlement attempts to square this circle by calling the monarch ceremonial. But as the evidence demonstrates, the monarch is not ceremonial. She has been, and he is, a political actor — lobbying ministers, concealing wealth, corrupting appointments — behind a ceremonial facade that makes scrutiny seem impolite.

On the Sovereignty problem: who does the monarch serve?

Parliamentary sovereignty and the CrownThe British constitutional fiction is that sovereignty lies with the Crown-in-Parliament. In practice, this means that the powers of the Crown — prerogative powers, the ability to advise on legislation before it reaches Parliament, the granting of honours, the appointment of ministers, the prorogue of Parliament — are exercised by the executive in the Crown's name, without meaningful democratic oversight. The prorogation crisis of 2019, when Boris Johnson used the Crown's prerogative to suspend Parliament, illustrated what happens when those powers are exercised aggressively. The Supreme Court ruled the prorogation unlawful — but only because it was egregiously so. Every day, Crown prerogative powers are exercised in ways that are constitutional but undemocratic, and the monarchy provides the symbolic legitimacy through which those exercises are naturalised. Abolishing the monarchy means codifying its powers, democratising their exercise, and ending the fiction that executive power exercised in the name of an unelected hereditary figure is somehow above politics.

On the opposition's stability argument

Against the stability argumentThe opposition will argue that the monarchy provides stability — a non-partisan head of state above the fray of politics. But the evidence assembled today shows that the monarchy is not above the fray. It is a participant in it, using its unique position to advance its financial interests, suppress scrutiny, and evade accountability. Germany has a non-partisan elected head of state. Ireland has one. Iceland, Portugal, Greece, Italy — all republics with strong democratic traditions and stable constitutional orders. The claim that abolishing the monarchy would produce instability confuses two things: the disruption of reform, which is temporary, and the structural damage of an uncodified constitution in which hereditary power operates with impunity, which is permanent. A country that cannot bring itself to reform an institution whose abuses are this well-documented is not stable. It is frozen — and it is frozen because the enchanted glass prevents it from seeing clearly.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to begin with a concession that I think the opposition — that is, those on my side — should make clearly and without equivocation. The cases described by the proposition are real. The Queen's Consent investigation uncovered evidence of genuine constitutional irregularity. The black spider memos demonstrated that Charles, as Prince of Wales, exceeded what his constitutional position permitted. The Duchy arrangements are opaque and in need of transparency. The Andrew scandal is a serious matter. I do not dispute the substance of a single case my opponents have cited. What I dispute is the conclusion they draw from it: that the institution should be abolished rather than reformed, and that a republic would do better.

On the constitutional functions of the monarch

The constitutional guardrail functionThe British monarchy performs a function that is genuinely difficult to replicate in a republic: it provides a non-partisan head of state whose legitimacy is entirely independent of the political process, and who therefore can, in constitutional crisis, act as a referee without being a player. Walter Bagehot identified the three rights of a constitutional monarch — the right to be consulted, the right to encourage, the right to warn. In the 2019 prorogation crisis, the Supreme Court's intervention was necessary precisely because those constitutional guardrails had been removed from the Crown by convention and not replaced. The proposition's own evidence demonstrates the constitutional danger of unchecked executive power exercised in the Crown's name. Their solution — abolition — removes the nominal check without explaining what replaces it.

On the dangers of an elected president

The elected president problemEvery republic that has attempted to establish a non-partisan head of state has had to answer the question of how to select one without that selection becoming political. The answer has invariably been either a popular election — which produces a figure with a democratic mandate who will be tempted to use it — or appointment by the legislature — which produces a figure whose legitimacy is derived from the same political process the head of state is supposed to oversee. Germany's Bundespräsident is elected by a special assembly, and German presidents have generally remained non-partisan. But the German model works because Germany has a codified constitution that strictly limits the president's powers. Without that codification — and the proposition has not committed to it — an elected British head of state would be either a constitutional redundancy or a new source of political conflict.

On Queen's Consent and the case for transparency, not abolition

Reform not abolition on Queen's ConsentThe Queen's Consent investigation is the proposition's strongest piece of evidence. Let me engage with it directly. The procedure was used improperly. The lobbying of the Companies Act was constitutionally improper. The remedy is the same as the remedy for any constitutional impropriety: codify it, make it transparent, and make it justiciable. The House of Commons Political and Constitutional Reform Committee examined Queen's Consent in 2014 and concluded that Parliament could abolish it by resolution — without any legislation. It has not done so, not because of monarchical resistance, but because Parliament has not chosen to. This is not a failure of the monarchy. It is a failure of Parliament. Abolishing the monarchy does not fix Parliament. Only Parliament can fix Parliament.

On the Duchy arrangements and the case for financial transparency

Duchy reform not abolitionThe Duchy of Cornwall and the Duchy of Lancaster are ancient landholdings whose relationship to the public finances is genuinely opaque and in genuine need of reform. But they are not an argument for abolition — they are an argument for treating them as what they are: de facto public assets whose income should be subject to the same transparency and tax obligations as any other large landowner. The relevant legislation could be passed tomorrow. Labour has a substantial majority. If the government chose to require the King to pay capital gains tax and corporation tax on Duchy income, it could do so. The fact that it has not done so is a political choice — made by elected politicians who are accountable to the public — not a constitutional barrier created by the monarchy.

On what abolition would actually require

What abolition actually requiresAbolition would require resolving questions that the proposition has not addressed. Who would be head of state? How would they be selected? What powers would they have? What would happen to the Crown Estate — worth £15 billion — whose profits currently fund both the Sovereign Grant and the Treasury? What would happen to the royal prerogative powers currently exercised by ministers in the Crown's name — the power to go to war, to sign treaties, to appoint ministers? All of these require codification before abolition is coherent. The proposition effectively argues for constitutional reform — and I agree that constitutional reform is needed. But constitutional reform does not require abolition; it requires what every serious democratic country eventually does: a codified constitution. Give us the constitution. The question of whether the head of state is hereditary or elected is secondary.

Mr President,

Professor Bogdanor has made the constitutional lawyer's case, and it is a careful one. I want to make a different case — a philosophical one — not because I dismiss the evidence the proposition has assembled, but because I think the proposition's response to that evidence reveals something about its own intellectual foundations that deserves examination.

The proposition's case is essentially rationalist: there is an institution, it has abuses, the rational response is to abolish it and design a better one. I want to question both the rationalism and the confidence that design can do what the proposition hopes.

On what the monarchy embodies that cannot be designed

Monarchy as embodied national continuityBurke argued in the Reflections on the Revolution in France that political institutions embody not merely the interests of the living but the accumulated commitments of generations — the dead, the living, and the unborn. The British monarchy is the most visible instance of this in English political life. It embodies continuity across seven hundred years of law, constitutional development, and national self-understanding. This is not mere sentimentality. It is an observation about what political legitimacy actually is — not a rational calculation of institutional efficiency, but a felt relationship between citizens and the historical identity that they share and that their institutions express. The proposition can produce no design for a replacement that carries this weight, because such weight cannot be designed. It must be accumulated, over time, through experience.

On the proposition's intellectual confidence

Against rationalist institutional designThe proposition's case has the structure of every rationalist reform argument since the Enlightenment: identify the flaws in an existing institution, propose a replacement, expect the replacement to be better. The French Revolution had this structure. The Bolshevik Revolution had this structure. I do not accuse the proposition of Jacobinism. But I do accuse it of the same intellectual vice: the confidence that institutions can be designed from scratch to produce the outcomes we desire, without inheriting the unintended consequences that every act of constitutional architecture produces. The British monarchy has abuses. A British republic would have different ones — ones we cannot currently anticipate, because the institutions that would produce them do not yet exist.

On what the proposition's evidence actually shows

What the evidence actually shows about reform vs abolitionLet me take the proposition's evidence seriously, because it is serious evidence. The Queen's Consent lobbying, the Duchy finances, the cash for honours, the Andrew scandal — these are genuine abuses by specific individuals in specific institutional contexts. But notice what they have in common: they are all cases where individuals used institutional opacity to serve private interests. The remedy for institutional opacity is transparency. The remedy for inadequate accountability mechanisms is better mechanisms. The remedy for tax exemptions that have outlived their justification is to legislate them away. None of these remedies requires abolishing the monarchy. They require the same thing as every other democratic reform: political will, legislative action, and the courage to use parliamentary power that already exists. The proposition has confused the case for those reforms with the case for abolition. They are not the same case.

On what Britons actually value and why it matters

Democratic legitimacy and popular attachmentDemocratic legitimacy requires that major constitutional change command genuine popular support. The consistent polling evidence — even in periods of acute royal scandal — shows that a substantial majority of the British public does not support abolition of the monarchy. The proposition may respond that this reflects the "enchanted glass" — that people have been ideologically mystified into supporting an institution that harms them. But this argument — that the people's preferences are not their real preferences, that they have been deceived into wanting what they want — is precisely the argument used to justify reform against the expressed will of the majority throughout the history of rationalist politics. If the proposition believes in democracy, it must take democratic preferences seriously. The British people have not asked for a republic. The proposition is asking Parliament to give them one anyway.

On what would actually be lost

What would be lost in abolitionI am not a naive monarchist. I accept that Queen's Consent should be codified and made transparent. I accept that the Duchies should pay tax. I accept that the honours system requires reform. I accept that the Andrew scandal is a serious matter requiring full accountability. All of these reforms are available without abolition. What would be lost in abolition is harder to quantify and therefore easier to dismiss — but no less real. It is the sense of historical continuity, of a national story embodied in living persons and ancient ceremonies, that gives British civic life much of its particular character. This is not nothing. It is, for most people, the pre-theoretical foundation on which their political life is built. Abolishing it to replace it with a designed institution that will have its own, as yet unknown, corruptions and failures, is not obviously progress. It is an experiment of enormous consequence and unknown outcome. And it is an experiment that cannot be undone.

1.Every rationalist reform — identify the flaws, design a replacement, expect improvement — has produced unintended consequences that the reformers did not anticipate.

3.The confidence that design produces better institutions than inheritance is an intellectual vice with a poor historical track record.
