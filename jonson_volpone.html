Volpone — Act V, Scene xii: Sentencing and Epilogue

Ben Jonson — Volpone, or the Fox (1606)

Structure & Context
The play's trick: The entire plot is a con — Volpone feigns dying to extract gifts from legacy-hunters; the epilogue extends the con to the real audience Poetic justice: Volpone is sentenced to experience the illness he faked — the fictional disease becomes literal punishment; Jonson calls this the moment comedy 'imitates justice' The structural inversion: Volpone has been supine on a sickbed for the entire play; he stands upright for the first time in the epilogue — which is itself a performance of standing The epilogue's trick: Volpone steps outside the fiction, addresses the audience directly, flatters them, uses the court's own legal language to appeal their verdict, and extracts applause Who is speaking: The epilogue is simultaneously Volpone (the character), the actor playing Volpone, and Jonson himself — all three occupy the same words at once The final gull: An audience that applauds has been manipulated into rewarding the man the court just condemned — they have become the play's last and most numerous victims
Act V, Scene xii — The Scrutineo, Venice — The Sentences
1st Avocatore
Thou, Volpone, By blood and rank a gentleman, canst not fall Under like censure; but our judgement on thee Is that thy substance all be straight confiscate To the hospital of the Incurabili: Since the most was gotten by imposture, By feigning lame, gout, palsy, and such diseases, Thou art to lie in prison, cramped with irons, Till thou be'st sick and lame indeed.
Volpone
This is called mortifying of a Fox.
[The court disperses. Volpone steps forward, out of the fiction, to address the audience directly.]
Epilogue — Volpone speaks to the audience
The seasoning of a play is the applause. Now, though the Fox be punished by the laws, He yet doth hope there is no suff'ring due For any fact which he hath done 'gainst you. If there be, censure him; here he doubtful stands. If not, fare jovially, and clap your hands.
