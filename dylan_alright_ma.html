It's Alright, Ma (I'm Only Bleeding)

Bob Dylan — Bringing It All Back Home (1965)
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
Structure & Context
Form: Twelve six-line stanzas, the first five lines built on dense internal rhyme, each stanza closing on a short, often single-word or single-phrase sixth line that lands as a verdict — "trying," "dying," "crying," "hatred," "phony," "lonely." This closing line is metrically and tonally distinct from the cascade that precedes it: the cascade builds momentum through accumulation, and the short line cuts it off, delivering judgement. Composition: Written in the summer of 1964 and recorded in a single take on 15 January 1965 for Bringing It All Back Home, the album's second side, on which Dylan moved from the electric instrumentation of side one to solo acoustic guitar and harmonica. Dylan has called it one of the songs that means the most to him, and has returned to it across decades of live performance, often substantially rearranged. The refrain: "It's alright, Ma" recurs five times across the song's twelve stanzas, each time followed by a different self-description — "I'm only sighing," "I can make it," "to live up to" (an inverted variant), "if I can't please him," and the final line, "it's life, and life only." The repetition with variation tracks an emotional arc: from suppressed distress through assertion to resignation to a closing acceptance that refuses any further explanation. Catalogue of targets: The song moves through an escalating series of social types and structures — advertisers, preachers, teachers, judges, the political "wise men and fools," conformists "baptized" to party platforms, a fellow singer "with his tongue on fire" — building an inventory of American institutional and social hypocrisy across the mid-1960s. The catalogue is not organised by argument but by accumulation, each stanza adding another figure to an unresolved indictment. The title's irony: The title phrase, repeated as refrain, performs reassurance — "it's alright" — while the parenthetical subtitle, "I'm Only Bleeding," undercuts that reassurance entirely. "Only" minimises a wound that the rest of the song insists is real and ongoing; the title itself enacts, in miniature, the song's larger strategy of saying one thing in a soothing register while meaning something considerably darker underneath. Existential turn: Although often read as a protest song — and it contains direct social and political targets — the song's final third pivots toward interior, existential territory: identity ("That you belong to"), authority ("I got nothing, Ma, to live up to"), and finally, in the closing stanza, an image of the speaker's own "thought-dreams" being punished by guillotine. The song's movement is from the external (advertising, institutions, hypocrisy) to the internal (the self under scrutiny by its own thoughts), arriving at an ending that offers no solution, only naming: "it's life, and life only."
