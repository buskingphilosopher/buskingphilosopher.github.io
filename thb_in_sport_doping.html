Autonomy, Safety & the Incoherence of the BanJulian Savulescu — Oxford • BJSM, 2004 (with Foddy & Clayton)
The prohibition on performance-enhancing drugs in sport is ethically indefensible. It rests on three failures: a failure to take athlete autonomy seriously, a failure to distinguish coherently between permitted and prohibited enhancements, and a failure to acknowledge that prohibition — not permissive legalisation — is the primary source of harm to athletes. Supervised legalisation would be safer, fairer, and more honest than the current hypocritical regime.

Addiction, Consent & the Limits of PaternalismBennett Foddy — Melbourne / Oxford • Bioethics, 2007; BJSM, 2004
The anti-doping movement's case rests crucially on the claim that athletes cannot genuinely consent to doping because addiction or social coercion vitiates their autonomy. This claim is false. Most performance-enhancing drugs used in sport are not significantly addictive in the relevant sense, and the social pressure to dope that already exists under prohibition is itself a product of prohibition rather than an argument for it. Fairness, Technology & the Natural Athlete MythClaudio Tamburrini — Stockholm • Values in Sport, 2000; Ethics & Sport, 2005
The appeal to “natural talent” as the foundation of sporting value is philosophically bankrupt. Elite sport already permits and celebrates the use of altitude chambers, surgical interventions, genetic screening, and cutting-edge nutritional science — all of which enhance performance by acting on the athlete's physiology. The prohibition on pharmacological enhancement is not the defence of nature against technology; it is an arbitrary line drawn for historical and commercial reasons, with no principled ethical foundation.
Speech
Logical Arguments

Let me begin with the most fundamental question: why do we ban performance-enhancing drugs in sport? The usual answer comes in three parts — they harm athletes, they are unfair, and they violate the “spirit of sport.” I will argue that each of these claims is either false, philosophically confused, or applies with equal force to things we already permit. The ban is incoherent, and incoherence is a poor foundation for a regime of punishment that has destroyed careers and reputations across generations of athletes.

Performance enhancement is not against the spirit of sport. It is the spirit of sport. Every athlete who trains harder, sleeps better, eats more carefully, or moves to altitude to stimulate erythropoietin production is enhancing performance by manipulating their biology. The question is never whether to enhance, but how. The distinction between “natural” and “artificial” enhancement is not a moral distinction; it is a historical accident dressed up as ethical principle.

The incoherence of the natural/artificial distinction in sport
Permitted enhancements that act on physiology:
Altitude tents / hypoxic chambers → stimulate EPO production → increase red blood cell count → improve endurance. Identical physiological mechanism to blood doping and exogenous EPO.
Caffeine (above threshold) → banned until 2004, now permitted. No principled basis for the reversal.
Therapeutic Use Exemptions → asthmatic athletes may legally use bronchodilators that improve lung function. Non-asthmatic athletes using the same compounds are banned.
The anti-doping regime does not prohibit physiological manipulation. It prohibits particular methods of physiological manipulation — selected historically, not on principled ethical grounds. The moral case for the prohibition must be made, not assumed from the existence of a rule.
i. the harm argument and its inversion

Harm inversion — prohibition causes harms it claims to prevent; clandestine use without medical supervision is more dangerous than regulated useThe harm argument against doping says: performance-enhancing drugs are harmful to athletes, therefore they should be banned. This argument has two critical flaws. First, many of the harms associated with doping are products of prohibition rather than of the drugs themselves. When athletes must dope covertly, without medical supervision, in jurisdictions where they cannot openly seek advice, they use higher doses than necessary, combine substances in dangerous ways, and receive no monitoring of their health markers. The harm is a feature of the clandestine regime, not of the pharmacology. Second, elite sport already tolerates and even celebrates activities that are medically demonstrably more dangerous than the drugs we ban. Contact sports produce chronic traumatic encephalopathy, catastrophic spinal injuries, and lifelong degenerative joint disease at rates that dwarf the risks of, say, supervised anabolic steroid use. If we genuinely cared about athlete welfare above all, we would ban rugby before we banned testosterone.

ii. the fairness argument and the genetic lottery

Genetic lottery — natural talent is itself profoundly unfair; Eero Mäntyranta had a natural EPO mutation; genetic advantages are celebrated, pharmacological ones bannedThe fairness argument says: doping is unfair because it gives some athletes advantages others do not have. But this argument, taken seriously, should lead us to ban genetic advantages, socioeconomic advantages, and geographical advantages — all of which produce far larger performance differentials than any drug. Eero Mäntyranta, the Finnish cross-country skier who won multiple Olympic gold medals, had a natural mutation in his EPO receptor that elevated his red blood cell count by approximately 25 to 50 percent. This natural genetic advantage — which produced exactly the effect that exogenous EPO achieves pharmacologically — was not merely permitted but celebrated. The athlete born with perfect fast-twitch muscle fibre composition, ideal cardiovascular architecture, or unusually efficient lactic acid metabolism enjoys advantages that no drug can replicate and that no anti-doping rule addresses. If fairness is the concern, the natural is no fairer than the pharmacological. Legalising drugs levels the playing field: it gives all athletes access to the same physiological tools, rather than concentrating advantage in those who happen to be born with the right genome or who can afford altitude camps and cutting-edge dietary programmes.

iii. autonomy and the anti-paternalist argument

Autonomy — adult athletes are entitled to take informed risks; the state and sporting bodies are not entitled to prohibit consensual, self-regarding choices; paternalism requires justificationThe most important argument for legalisation is the argument from autonomy. Adult athletes are entitled to make informed decisions about risks to their own bodies. They accept significant medical risks as a condition of elite competition — risks of injury, overtraining syndrome, eating disorders, and the long-term sequelae of extreme physical demands. We do not require that they justify these choices to sporting authorities. Why then do we single out pharmacological enhancement as the one category of risk that athletes may not accept? The answer cannot be that the risks are uniquely severe — we have seen that this is false. The answer cannot be that athletes cannot consent — the same argument would prohibit boxing. The paternalist who bans doping owes us a principled account of why this particular risk is different, and none of the standard accounts succeeds. The prohibition is not the protection of athletes; it is the imposition of a particular vision of sport on athletes who may not share it.

iv. the case for medically supervised legalisation

Supervised legalisation — open use with medical oversight would be safer than prohibition; WADA's zero-tolerance model is self-defeating; a performance policy replacing the banI am not advocating for an unregulated free-for-all. I am advocating for medically supervised legalisation within limits set by health considerations rather than by arbitrary appeal to “naturalness.” Under such a regime, athletes would declare their use openly to their medical teams; use would be monitored by physicians; doses would be calibrated to safe levels; and the entire practice would be governed by the same medical ethics that govern all other aspects of sports medicine. The current regime of zero tolerance achieves none of this. It drives use underground, removes medical oversight, encourages athletes to seek undetectable rather than safe alternatives, and imposes on governing bodies the impossible task of distinguishing permitted from prohibited physiological manipulation on a case-by-case basis. The ban is not only unjust; it does not even achieve its stated aim of protecting athlete health. A performance policy — guided by welfare, not moralism — would do both.

Performance enhancement is the spirit of sport. The ban should go. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The harm inversion: prohibition causes the harms it claims to prevent
1.Under prohibition, athletes who dope do so covertly, without medical supervision, combining substances, using doses calibrated for undetectability rather than safety.
2.The characteristic harms of doping — cardiovascular damage, hormonal disruption, contaminated products — are products of clandestine regimes, not of the pharmacology itself when used under supervised conditions.
3.Elite sport already permits activities (contact sports, extreme endurance events) with medically demonstrable risk profiles exceeding those of supervised pharmacological enhancement. Selective harm prevention is not principled harm prevention.
∴The harm argument for prohibition is self-undermining: prohibition produces the harms it cites as justification; medically supervised legalisation would reduce those harms while treating athletes as capable of informed consent to remaining risks.
Argument II — Natural genetic advantage is no fairer than pharmacological advantage; the fairness argument is self-defeating
1.Elite sport celebrates and rewards natural genetic advantages — extraordinary VO² max, favourable muscle fibre composition, anomalous EPO receptor mutations (e.g. Mäntyranta) — that produce performance differentials far exceeding those of banned drugs.
2.If pharmacological enhancement is impermissible because it gives some athletes unfair advantages, then natural genetic advantage is equally impermissible on the same grounds. But we do not ban natural talent. Consistency requires treating pharmacological and genetic advantage the same way.
3.Legalising drugs arguably levels the playing field: it gives athletes without favoured genetics access to the same physiological tools as those who happen to be born with them, producing a more genuinely equal competition.
∴The fairness argument for prohibition is self-defeating: natural sport is already massively unfair due to genetic and socioeconomic disparities that dwarf the effects of banned drugs; legalisation is at least as compatible with competitive fairness as the current regime.
Argument III — Autonomy requires permitting informed, consensual risk-taking; the anti-doping ban is unjustified paternalism
1.Adult athletes already accept significant medical risks as conditions of elite competition — injury, overtraining, long-term degenerative damage — without requiring justification to sporting authorities. This is consistent with respecting their autonomy.
2.Prohibiting pharmacological enhancement uniquely denies athletes the right to accept this category of risk. No principled distinction between this risk and other accepted risks has been established.
3.Paternalism — overriding a person's choices for their own good — requires strong justification: that the person cannot adequately assess the risk, or that the harm to others is severe. Neither condition is met for adult athletes making informed decisions about their own bodies.
∴The prohibition on doping is unjustified paternalism: it overrides the informed choices of adult athletes without principled justification; autonomy requires permitting medically supervised enhancement under conditions of genuine informed consent.
Argument IV — Supervised legalisation would be safer and more honest than prohibition; a performance policy should replace the ban
1.Medically supervised legalisation: athletes declare use to medical teams; doses calibrated for health; monitoring of biomarkers; governed by the same ethics as sports medicine generally.
2.Prohibition achieves none of this: it drives use underground, removes medical oversight, encourages undetectable over safe alternatives, and cannot coherently distinguish permitted from prohibited physiological manipulation.
3.A welfare-guided performance policy — permitting drugs below a health threshold, prohibiting those above it regardless of natural/artificial provenance — would protect athletes more effectively than zero tolerance.
∴Supervised legalisation dominates prohibition on both welfare and honesty grounds; a performance policy guided by health considerations rather than moralistic appeals to naturalness would better serve the athletes the current regime claims to protect.

I want to address what I consider the most sophisticated argument available to the opposition: the coercion argument. This argument, developed in various forms by Murray, Loland, and others, says that even if doping were permitted, athletes who preferred not to dope would be coerced into doing so by competitive pressure. Legalisation would therefore be not the liberation of athlete autonomy but its destruction. This is a serious argument. I believe it is ultimately wrong, and understanding why it is wrong illuminates the deep confusion at the heart of the anti-doping movement.

The coercion argument proceeds as follows. If doping is legalised, the performance improvements from doping will be significant enough that undoped athletes cannot remain competitive. Athletes who personally wish to remain drug-free will therefore face a forced choice: dope or lose. This forced choice undermines their autonomy. Therefore, prohibition — not legalisation — protects autonomy at the level of the athlete community as a whole.

The structure of the coercion argument and its decisive flaw.

The coercion argument assumes that the current status quo — prohibition — is itself free of coercion. But this is precisely what needs to be examined. Under prohibition, athletes face the following forced choice: dope secretly and risk your career, health, and reputation; or do not dope and compete against athletes who are almost certainly doping anyway. This is not an absence of coercion. It is coercion of a more dangerous kind — coercion into clandestine, unmonitored, medically unsupervised enhancement.

The coercion produced by a hypothetical legalisation is qualitatively different: it is the ordinary competitive pressure that pervades sport at every level. Training harder coerces other athletes to train harder. Better coaching coerces competitors to seek better coaches. The coercion produced by performance-enhancing drugs under legalisation is not relevantly different from the coercion produced by any other performance advantage — and we do not take the latter to justify prohibition.
i. addiction and the autonomy of the doping athlete

Addiction — most sports PEDs are not addictive in the philosophically relevant sense; informed consent is not vitiated by competitive pressure; autonomy is not automatically absentA deeper version of the anti-autonomy argument appeals to addiction: performance-enhancing drugs are addictive, and addicts cannot genuinely consent. But this argument, when examined carefully, does not apply to the vast majority of performance-enhancing substances used in sport. Anabolic steroids, EPO, human growth hormone, and most other prohibited substances are not significantly addictive in the neurological sense that opioids or nicotine are addictive. They do not produce compulsive seeking behaviour or withdrawal syndromes that override rational deliberation. The athlete who uses testosterone to recover more quickly between training sessions can straightforwardly choose to stop. The appeal to addiction as a general argument against legalisation mistakes the pharmacology. And even where there is some psychological dependence — as with stimulants — this does not automatically vitiate consent any more than the psychological dependence many athletes have on extreme training regimes vitiates their consent to those regimes.

ii. the reversal of the coercion argument

Coercion reversal — prohibition creates coercive conditions; legalisation replaces hidden coercion with transparent competitive pressure; transparency respects autonomy betterThe coercion argument, properly understood, tells against prohibition rather than for it. Under prohibition, athletes who would prefer to compete cleanly are coerced into a choice between doping secretly and accepting competitive disadvantage. This is a genuinely autonomy-undermining situation, because the choice is made under conditions of secrecy, misinformation, and medical risk. Under legalisation, athletes would face competitive pressure, as they always do — but they would face it transparently, with full information about what their competitors are doing, and with medical supervision of whatever choices they make. Transparent competitive pressure is consistent with autonomy; coerced clandestine risk-taking is not. The opposition's coercion argument, applied honestly, is an argument for the position they oppose.

iii. what genuine respect for autonomy requires

Genuine autonomy — requires information, not just absence of compulsion; medical disclosure and supervision enable genuine informed consent; prohibition denies athletes information about what they are takingGenuine respect for autonomy is not merely the absence of external compulsion. It requires that individuals have access to accurate information, that they are not deceived, and that they can make their choices in conditions that allow genuine deliberation. The current prohibition regime fails on every one of these criteria. Athletes who dope do not know with certainty what they are taking — contamination is endemic in the supplement industry and in the black-market supply chains that prohibition creates. They cannot seek transparent medical advice without risking their careers. They cannot openly compare notes with their peers or their physicians. This is not autonomy; it is autonomy denied in the name of its protection. Legalisation, with full medical disclosure, would produce the conditions in which genuine autonomous choice about enhancement is actually possible for the first time.

iv. the limits of sporting authority

Sporting authority — WADA's mandate does not extend to imposing a particular conception of the good life on athletes; anti-doping is an ethical position, not a neutral administrative oneThere is a final question about legitimacy: does WADA have the authority to impose the kind of restrictions it imposes? I think not. WADA's power derives from the consent of athletes to participate in a regulated sporting framework. That consent does not extend to consenting to a particular philosophical vision of what sport should be — one that privileges certain kinds of performance over others on the basis of how they were achieved. The anti-doping movement presents itself as a neutral, protective administrative enterprise. It is, in fact, an ethical position: the position that sport should be organised around the expression of natural talent, that pharmacological enhancement is categorically different from other enhancements, and that athletes who disagree should be excluded. This position requires justification, and none of the standard justifications succeeds.

The coercion argument backfires. Autonomy demands legalisation. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Most performance-enhancing drugs used in sport are not addictive in the philosophically relevant sense; the addiction argument fails
1.Addiction that vitiates autonomy requires compulsive seeking behaviour and withdrawal syndromes that override rational deliberation. This characterises opioids and nicotine, not anabolic steroids, EPO, or growth hormone.
2.Athletes using these substances retain the capacity to stop, to deliberate about risks, and to make genuine informed choices. The addiction argument does not apply to the drugs actually in dispute.
3.Even psychological dependence does not automatically vitiate consent — athletes are psychologically dependent on extreme training regimes, yet we do not take this to invalidate their consent to training.
∴The addiction argument against legalisation fails: it mischaracterises the pharmacology of performance-enhancing drugs and applies a standard of autonomy-vitiation that would invalidate consent to many other accepted sporting practices.
Argument II — The coercion argument reversed: prohibition creates coercive conditions; legalisation replaces hidden coercion with transparent competitive pressure
1.Under prohibition, clean athletes face: dope secretly (with medical risk, career risk, moral compromise) or compete against likely-doping competitors at a disadvantage. This is coercion into clandestine risk-taking.
2.Under legalisation, athletes face ordinary competitive pressure to use available enhancements — the same kind of pressure that compels training harder, seeking better coaching, or using superior equipment.
3.Transparent competitive pressure is consistent with autonomy; coerced clandestine risk-taking under conditions of secrecy and misinformation is not. Legalisation improves the autonomy situation, not prohibition.
∴The coercion argument, applied honestly, favours legalisation over prohibition: prohibition produces the more autonomy-undermining form of coercion; legalisation replaces it with ordinary transparent competitive pressure.
Argument III — Genuine autonomy requires information and transparency; prohibition denies both; legalisation enables them
1.Genuine autonomy requires accurate information, freedom from deception, and conditions for genuine deliberation. Athletes under prohibition lack all three: they cannot know exactly what they take, cannot seek open medical advice, cannot openly compare practices.
2.Legalisation with full medical disclosure would produce, for the first time, conditions in which genuine informed autonomous choice about enhancement is actually possible.
3.Claiming to protect autonomy through prohibition, while systematically denying athletes the informational conditions for genuine autonomous choice, is self-contradictory.
∴Prohibition denies the informational and deliberative conditions that genuine autonomy requires; legalisation with medical transparency would be the first regime actually consistent with athlete autonomy properly understood.
Argument IV — WADA's authority does not extend to imposing a particular conception of sporting value; anti-doping is an ethical position requiring justification
1.WADA presents itself as a neutral, protective body. In fact it enforces an ethical position: that sport should express natural talent, that pharmacological enhancement is categorically impermissible, that athletes who disagree are excluded from competition.
2.Athlete consent to participate in regulated sport does not entail consent to this particular philosophical vision of what sport should be.
3.An authority that enforces a contested ethical position requires justification for that position. None of the standard justifications — harm, fairness, naturalness — succeeds without circularity or inconsistency.
∴WADA lacks the authority to impose its conception of sporting value without principled justification; anti-doping is an ethical position that requires argument, and the arguments fail; the authority of the prohibition is therefore without foundation.

My contribution to this debate is to press the philosophical incoherence of the “naturalness” argument that underlies so much anti-doping advocacy, and in particular the work of Thomas Murray and Michael Sandel. The claim is that sport is valuable because it is the expression of natural talent perfected through dedicated training — and that pharmacological enhancement corrupts this because it bypasses or substitutes for genuine human achievement. I want to show that this argument fails on its own terms and rests on a romanticised and philosophically untenable picture of what elite sport actually is.

Consider what the training of a contemporary elite athlete involves. It involves altitude camps — not merely high-altitude residence but carefully engineered hypoxic environments designed to produce specific physiological adaptations. It involves genetic screening, through which athletes discover whether their natural profile is compatible with elite performance in their chosen discipline and adjust their training accordingly. It involves surgical interventions — laser eye surgery for shooters, reconstructive procedures that allow injured athletes to return to competition with enhanced structural integrity. It involves sophisticated nutritional science — intravenous vitamin drips, precisely calibrated macronutrient timing, creatine loading. Each of these practices is permitted. Each involves using science and technology to manipulate athletic physiology. The objection to pharmacological enhancement cannot be that it uses science to manipulate physiology. So what is the objection?

i. the myth of the natural athlete

Naturalness myth — elite sport is already thoroughly technologised; the natural/artificial line tracks historical accident not moral principle; no principled distinction can be drawnThe “natural athlete” is a myth. No elite athlete competes on the basis of natural talent alone. The entire infrastructure of modern sport — sports science, sports medicine, psychology, biomechanics — is dedicated to maximising the gap between what athletes can achieve naturally and what they achieve through scientifically optimised preparation. When Murray says that sport celebrates “the virtuous perfection of natural talents,” he is describing a romantic ideal that has no contact with the reality of elite competition. The question is not whether we use technology and science to enhance athletic performance; we always do. The question is which technologies and which sciences we permit, and on what grounds. The grounds cannot be naturalness, because naturalness is already absent. They must be something else — and that something else has never been coherently specified by the anti-doping movement.

ii. sandel’s giftedness argument and its failure

Sandel — giftedness argument claims enhancement displays hubris; but sport already is the radical refusal to accept natural talent as given; the argument refutes elite training as much as dopingMichael Sandel has offered a more philosophical version of the naturalness argument: the argument from giftedness. Sandel argues that enhancement reflects a problematic attitude of mastery and control over the given — a refusal to accept and appreciate our natural endowments as gifts. This attitude of hyper-agency, he argues, corrupts the human practices in which it appears. Applied to sport: the doping athlete refuses to accept the given gifts of their natural physiology and instead seeks to replace them with pharmacological supplements. This, for Sandel, is what is wrong with doping. But this argument has a decisive flaw: elite sport is already the most extreme expression of the refusal to accept the given. The entire project of athletic training is to refuse to accept what you were born with and to transform it, systematically and radically, through years of deliberate effort. Sandel's giftedness ethic is not the spirit of elite sport; it is the spirit of an afternoon jog. The elite athlete who accepts the gift of their natural talent as a gift — without radical transformation through training — is not a champion; they are a person who has not fully engaged with sport at the level we are discussing.

iii. the technology inconsistency argument

Technology inconsistency — altitude chambers and TUEs produce identical physiological effects to banned drugs; no principled basis for the distinction; the line is historically arbitraryThe most straightforward argument against the prohibition is what I call the technology inconsistency argument. Permitted altitude chambers stimulate erythropoietin production, increasing red blood cell count and oxygen-carrying capacity — exactly what EPO injections do. Therapeutic use exemptions for asthmatic athletes permit bronchodilator use that improves lung function — exactly what certain banned respiratory agents do for non-asthmatic athletes. Creatine supplementation enhances phosphocreatine replenishment in muscle — a less efficient version of what certain anabolic protocols achieve. In each case, the prohibited pharmacological intervention and the permitted technological or medical intervention produce the same physiological effect by the same or similar mechanisms. The only difference is the method of delivery. The anti-doping regime bans the injection but permits the altitude tent; it permits the inhaler for the asthmatic but not the stimulant for the non-asthmatic. These distinctions have no principled basis. They track historical accident, commercial interest, and moral panic rather than any coherent ethical principle.

iv. a new conception of sporting excellence

New excellence — sport reconceived as optimised human potential; this is coherent and admirable; it is closer to what actually draws audiences than the natural talent mythI want to close by sketching a positive vision rather than merely a critique. Sport can be coherently reconceived as the expression of optimised human potential — the achievement of the most extraordinary performance that human beings can reach through the combination of natural endowment, trained skill, tactical intelligence, and yes, medical and pharmacological support. This is a coherent and admirable sporting ideal. It celebrates human ingenuity and human limits. It does not require the fiction of the “natural athlete.” It does not require the arbitrary distinction between permitted and prohibited physiology manipulation. And it is, I would argue, closer to what actually draws audiences to elite sport — the astonishment at what a human body can achieve — than the romantic myth of the pure natural talent that the anti-doping movement pretends to protect. The question is not whether to allow enhancement. The question is how to do so in a way that is safe, transparent, and fair. That question is answered by legalisation, not by prohibition.

The natural athlete is a myth. The prohibition is incoherent. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The natural athlete is a myth: elite sport is thoroughly technologised; the natural/artificial distinction tracks historical accident, not moral principle
1.Elite athletes already use altitude chambers, genetic screening, surgical enhancement, intravenous nutritional protocols, and sports science methodologies explicitly designed to maximise the gap between natural and achieved performance.
2.None of this is regarded as a violation of the spirit of sport. The anti-doping regime does not prohibit technological physiological manipulation; it prohibits particular methods of it.
3.A prohibition that picks out pharmacological enhancement as uniquely impermissible, when permitted technologies achieve identical physiological effects, cannot justify that distinction by appeal to naturalness. Naturalness is already absent in elite sport.
∴The natural athlete is a myth; the natural/artificial distinction tracks historical accident rather than moral principle; the prohibition cannot be justified by appeal to sporting naturalness because elite sport is already thoroughly technologised.
Argument II — Sandel's giftedness argument fails: elite sport is already the extreme expression of the refusal to accept the given
1.Sandel: enhancement reflects a problematic attitude of mastery over the given; the doping athlete refuses to appreciate natural talent as a gift and seeks pharmacological substitution.
2.Counter: elite athletic training is precisely the most radical rejection of accepting the given. Years of deliberate physiological transformation — of diet, sleep, training load, biomechanics, psychology — constitute the wholesale refusal to accept natural endowment as given.
3.If Sandel's giftedness ethic condemns pharmacological enhancement, it condemns elite training regimes equally. Sandel's argument, applied consistently, is the spirit of recreational jogging, not elite sport.
∴Sandel's giftedness argument fails: elite sport is already the most extreme expression of the attitude of mastery that Sandel condemns; a giftedness ethic applied consistently would prohibit not just doping but the entire edifice of systematic athletic training.
Argument III — Technology inconsistency: permitted technologies produce identical physiological effects to banned drugs; no principled basis for the distinction
1.Altitude chambers → stimulate endogenous EPO → raise red blood cell count → same mechanism and effect as exogenous EPO. One permitted, one banned; both produce the same physiology.
2.TUEs for asthmatic athletes → permit bronchodilators that improve lung function → same class of effect as banned respiratory agents for non-asthmatics. The medical diagnosis, not the pharmacology, determines permissibility.
3.Same mechanism, same physiological effect, different delivery method. No principled ethical distinction separates the permitted from the prohibited. The distinction tracks administrative history and moral panic, not coherent ethical reasoning.
∴The technology inconsistency argument shows the anti-doping regime's distinctions are unprincipled: it bans the pharmacological delivery of physiological effects while permitting the technological delivery of the same effects; no coherent ethical principle justifies this asymmetry.
Argument IV — Sport reconceived as optimised human potential: a coherent positive vision replaces the incoherent naturalness ideal
1.Alternative sporting ideal: sport as the achievement of the most extraordinary performance that human beings can reach through the combination of natural endowment, trained skill, tactical intelligence, and medical support.
2.This ideal is coherent, does not require the fiction of the natural athlete, does not require arbitrary distinctions between permitted and prohibited physiological manipulation, and arguably better describes what spectators find astonishing in elite sport.
3.This ideal is served by medically supervised legalisation — enabling the pursuit of optimised human performance under conditions of safety and transparency — not by prohibition.
∴A coherent sporting ideal of optimised human potential is available and defensible; it does not require the natural athlete myth; it is served by supervised legalisation; the romantic naturalness ideal that grounds the prohibition is philosophically untenable and practically fraudulent.

Logical Arguments

I want to begin by acknowledging what Savulescu gets right, because his position is not without merit and the dishonesty of our opponents has sometimes led defenders of anti-doping to overstate their case. He is right that some anti-doping arguments are weak. He is right that the natural/artificial distinction is not self-evidently morally decisive. He is right that certain therapeutic use exemptions are difficult to justify. But he is profoundly wrong about the core of the anti-doping case, and wrong in a way that reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of what sport is and what it is for.

Sport is not merely a platform for the display of extraordinary physical performance. It is a social practice organised around a specific set of human excellences — the perfection of natural physical talents through dedication, discipline, and training. What draws us to watch a marathon runner cross the line, a gymnast complete a flawless floor routine, a sprinter break a world record is not merely the physical fact of what their body has done. It is the knowledge that this achievement is the product of years of human striving — of a person pushing the limits of what a naturally endowed human being can achieve through sustained effort and commitment. Remove that connection and you have something interesting, but you do not have sport in the sense that matters to us.

Murray's taxonomy of sporting values — what doping corrupts
The core connection in sport: natural talent × dedicated training → sporting achievement
Natural talent: the endowment of physical and cognitive capacities that each athlete brings to sport — speed, strength, coordination, tactical intelligence. These are not equal, but their inequality is morally legitimate because they are constitutive of the person who competes.
Dedicated training: the process of perfecting those endowments through sustained effort — the virtue of discipline and commitment expressed over years.
What doping does: it inserts a third variable — pharmacological manipulation — that short-circuits the talent × training relationship. The doped athlete's achievement cannot be fully attributed to their natural endowment and their training because a portion of it is attributable to the drug. The connection that gives sport its meaning is severed.
Savulescu's error: he analyses sport as a performance-maximisation enterprise and finds doping consistent with it. But sport is not a performance-maximisation enterprise; it is an excellence-display enterprise — and the excellence in question is human, natural, and earned.
i. sport as the display of human excellence

Excellence display — sport is valuable as display of perfected natural human capacities; doping corrupts it by inserting a non-human variable into the achievementWhy do we care about sport? Not merely for the physical spectacle — extreme physical performances are achievable by machines, and we do not care about them in the way we care about sport. We care because sport is the expression of something distinctly human: the capacity of persons to take their natural endowments — their bodies, their physical talents — and perfect them through years of concentrated effort and disciplined training. Just as surgery is the art of healing, sport is the art of perfecting natural physical talent. This is what Thomas Murray means, and Michael Sandel seconds, when they say the spirit of sport is the virtuous perfection of natural talent. This is not a romantic fiction; it is the explanation of why we build stadiums, why we watch, why we care when athletes win or lose. Doping corrupts this by introducing a variable that is not natural and not earned — a pharmacological supplement that contributes to the outcome in ways that cannot be attributed to the athlete's talent or their training. The doped athlete's achievement is partly chemical. And a partly chemical achievement is a diminished human achievement.

ii. the technology analogy fails

Technology analogy fails — permitted technologies enhance expression of natural talent; drugs substitute for it; the distinction is principled even if the line is sometimes hard to drawTamburrini and Savulescu press the technology inconsistency argument hard: permitted technologies also manipulate physiology, so what is the principled difference? I have two responses. First, there is a morally relevant distinction between technologies that enhance the expression of natural talent and technologies that substitute for it. Altitude training works by stimulating the athlete's own physiological systems to perform better — it is a conditioning tool, just like conventional training, that produces greater athletic capacity from the same natural endowment. EPO injection, by contrast, directly introduces a substance that produces the desired physiological effect without going through the process of athletic development. One is a more efficient form of training; the other is a pharmacological substitute for training. This distinction is principled even if individual cases are sometimes hard to classify. Second, the difficulty of drawing the line in some cases is not an argument for abandoning the line. Many important moral distinctions are difficult to apply in borderline cases; difficulty of application does not show the distinction lacks foundation.

iii. the fairness argument properly construed

Fairness properly construed — natural talent inequality is the subject of sport; pharmacological inequality distorts it; the genetic lottery objection misunderstands the practiceSavulescu's genetic lottery argument says: natural sport is already massively unfair because of genetic advantages, so legalising drugs would not make it less fair and might make it more fair. This argument misunderstands the point of sporting competition. The inequality produced by natural talent is precisely the kind of inequality sport is designed to express and celebrate. Sport is not a lottery; it is an excellence-display practice, and the relevant excellences are physical ones — the very endowments that genetics distributes. The inequality produced by pharmacological enhancement, by contrast, is of a different kind: it is not the expression of the athletic capacities being tested but an external input that inflates performance beyond those capacities. Genetic advantage is the subject of sport; pharmacological advantage distorts the subject of sport. This is the principled distinction that the genetic lottery argument ignores.

iv. what legalisation would actually produce

Legalisation consequences — pharmacological arms race; wealth-based inequality amplified; spectators lose the connection to human achievement that makes sport worth watchingSavulescu says legalisation would be safer and fairer than prohibition. I doubt both claims. On safety: the pressure to use increasingly powerful pharmacological agents, once the basic drugs are permitted, would drive athletes toward ever more aggressive enhancement regimes. The Nash equilibrium of a legalised doping market is not mild supervised steroid use; it is an escalating pharmacological arms race in which the pressure to use the most powerful available agent is irresistible to anyone who wishes to compete at the highest level. On fairness: access to the most advanced pharmacological enhancement would be distributed — as it always has been — by wealth, by access to specialist medical expertise, and by geography. The athlete from a wealthy programme would gain a new dimension of advantage over the athlete from a developing nation. Legalisation would not level the playing field; it would add a new and expensive dimension to the inequality that already exists in elite sport. Above all: what is lost is the connection to human achievement that makes sport worth watching. A competition between pharmacological optimisation programmes is something. But it is not sport in the sense we have inherited and value.

Savulescu and Foddy argue that the only legitimate basis for restricting doping is welfare: if drugs are safe, they should be permitted. I want to press an independent argument that their position systematically ignores: the argument from the balance of excellences. This argument holds that doping is objectionable not merely because of what it does to athletes’ bodies, but because of what it does to sport as an excellence-based activity. The argument is internal to the logic of sport, not a mere imposition of external morality.

Each sport is organised around a specific cluster of excellences — a set of physical and mental attributes that the sport is designed to test, develop, and display. Cycling tests aerobic capacity, muscular endurance, tactical intelligence, and pain tolerance. Weightlifting tests raw strength, explosive power, and technique. The 100 metres tests pure speed and reaction time. These are the excellences that define each sport, and the point of sporting competition is to determine which athletes best exemplify them. When a competitor uses a performance-enhancing drug that artificially elevates one element of this cluster, they disrupt what I call the balance of excellences — the relationship between the specific attributes that the sport is supposed to test.

The balance of excellences argument — formal statement.

Let E = {e⊂1, e⊂2, ... e⊂n} be the cluster of excellences that sport S is organised around testing.
Let D = a drug that dramatically enhances e⊂i without affecting e⊂j (j ≠ i).

Claim: the use of D in S disrupts the balance of excellences because:
(a) it inflates the contribution of e⊂i to competitive outcomes beyond the weight that the structure of S is designed to assign it;
(b) it thereby diminishes the relative contribution of all other excellences e⊂j that S is also designed to test;
(c) competition under D therefore does not test the cluster E that S is constituted to test, but a distorted surrogate cluster E* in which e⊂i is over-weighted.

Example: EPO in cycling dramatically enhances aerobic capacity (e⊂i = VO⊂2max efficiency). Under EPO, cyclists with superior aerobic capacity but inferior tactical intelligence, courage under pressure, or sprint power win at a higher rate than the sport's constitutive design intends. The balance of excellences is disrupted.
i. athletic excellence is not reducible to outcomes

Excellence irreducible to outcomes — sport tests a cluster of attributes, not just outputs; doping skews which attributes determine outcomes; the test is corruptedSavulescu's analysis of sport treats athletic excellence as identical with performance outputs — faster times, greater distances, heavier weights. On this view, anything that produces better outputs is excellence-enhancing and should be welcomed. But this analysis is wrong. Athletic excellence is not the output; it is the display of the specific capacities that each sport is designed to test. We do not simply want cyclists to go faster; we want the fastest cyclist to be the one who best combines aerobic capacity, tactical intelligence, courage, pain tolerance, and recovery ability — the specific constellation of excellences that cycling is organised to reveal. When EPO dramatically amplifies one element of this constellation, it changes what the race reveals. The athlete who wins is no longer necessarily the one who best exemplifies the full cluster of excellences that cycling is constituted to test. The outcome is the same kind of thing — a finishing time — but what it measures has changed. The test has been corrupted.

ii. not all performance enhancements disrupt the balance of excellences

Not all enhancements equal — proportional enhancements preserve the balance; drugs that inflate one excellence disproportionately disrupt it; the distinction is principledDevine's balance of excellences argument provides a principled basis for the distinction between permitted and prohibited enhancements. A permitted enhancement — better training methodology, improved nutrition, altitude conditioning — that raises the overall level of all excellences proportionally does not disrupt the balance. If all cyclists improve their aerobic capacity by 5 percent from better altitude training, the relative contributions of the different excellences remain the same, and the race still reveals the athlete who best exemplifies the cluster. A pharmacological enhancement that dramatically inflates one excellence — EPO inflating aerobic capacity, anabolic steroids inflating explosive strength — does disrupt the balance, because it changes the weights that different excellences receive in determining outcomes. This is the principled basis for the distinction that Savulescu demands and claims not to find.

iii. the excellence-based objection is independent of welfare

Independent of welfare — even if drugs were perfectly safe, the balance of excellences objection would still apply; Savulescu's welfare-only framing ignores the internal logic of sportA crucial feature of the balance of excellences argument is that it is entirely independent of welfare considerations. Even if we stipulated that EPO was perfectly safe — no cardiovascular risk, no side effects, no health consequences whatsoever — the balance of excellences argument would still apply. Safe EPO would still distort the cluster of excellences that cycling is designed to test. Safe anabolic steroids would still disrupt the balance of excellences in weightlifting. The excellence-based objection to doping cannot be addressed by making drugs safer; it can only be addressed by removing them. This shows that Savulescu's welfare-centred analysis — which identifies welfare as the only legitimate basis for restriction — systematically underestimates the resources available to the anti-doping case. There is an independent argument from the internal logic of sport that his framework simply ignores.

iv. the enhanced games and what they would actually be

Enhanced games — pharmacologically permissive competition would be extraordinary but categorically different; not more of sport but something else entirelyI want to close with a thought experiment. Imagine the Enhanced Games: an Olympic-style competition in which all pharmacological enhancement is permitted, medically supervised, and openly declared. Let us stipulate, with Savulescu, that the health risks are managed. What would such a competition be? It would feature extraordinary performances — times, heights, and distances beyond anything we have seen. But I submit that it would not be sport in the sense that matters. It would be a competition between pharmacological programmes as much as between athletes. The athlete who wins the Enhanced Games marathon would not be revealing who is the greatest runner; they would be revealing which combination of athlete and pharmacological support produces the fastest marathon. These are interesting questions. But they are different questions from the one that traditional sport asks. The Enhanced Games would not be an improvement on sport; it would be a different activity. And given that we already have sport, and that sport is valuable in the way I have described, replacing or competing with it is not a gain.

The balance of excellences matters. Welfare is not the only consideration. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Athletic excellence is not reducible to performance outputs; doping corrupts the test that sport is constituted to administer
1.Athletic excellence is the display of the specific cluster of capacities that each sport is designed to test — not simply the production of better outcomes. Sports are constituted by their tests, not by their outputs.
2.When a drug dramatically amplifies one element of the excellence cluster, it changes what the race reveals: the athlete who wins need no longer be the one who best exemplifies the full cluster.
3.The outcome (finishing time) is the same kind of thing, but what it measures has changed. The test has been corrupted even if the performance was higher.
∴Doping corrupts the test that sport is constituted to administer: it does not merely enhance athletic outputs but distorts the relationship between the excellence cluster that defines the sport and the outcomes that competition produces; higher performance is not the same as better sport.

1.Permitted enhancements (altitude training, nutrition, conditioning) that raise all excellences proportionally do not disrupt the balance: the race still reveals who best exemplifies the full excellence cluster, at a higher absolute level.
2.Pharmacological enhancement that disproportionately inflates one excellence (EPO → aerobic capacity; steroids → explosive strength) changes the weights that different excellences receive in determining outcomes. The balance is disrupted.

∴The balance of excellences provides the principled basis for the distinction: enhancements that preserve the balance are permitted; those that disrupt it are not; this is an internal criterion derived from the logic of sport, not arbitrary historical accident.

Argument IV — The Enhanced Games would be a different activity, not an improvement; sport's value is worth preserving
1.In a fully legalised competition, the athlete who wins would be the one who best combines natural talent, training, and pharmacological support. The question answered by the competition would be different from the question sport is constituted to answer.
2.This is a different activity — a competition between athlete-pharmacology combinations rather than between athletes as the bearers of natural talent and trained capacity.
3.Since sport in the traditional sense is valuable — for the reasons Murray articulates — replacing it with something that is not sport is a loss, not a gain.
∴The Enhanced Games thought experiment shows full legalisation would produce a categorically different activity — a competition between pharmacological programmes — that is not sport in the value-carrying sense; since sport is valuable, replacing it is a significant loss that the proposition has not reckoned with.

I want to press a structural argument that I believe is underappreciated in this debate: the Fair Opportunity Principle (FOP). This principle, which I have developed across my work in sports ethics, holds that competitive sport is ethically structured by the requirement that the inequalities between competitors arise from morally relevant sources. Performance-enhancing drugs violate this principle in a specific and important way — and understanding how they do so illuminates both what is wrong with doping and why Savulescu's legalisation proposal fails to solve the problem it identifies.

Fair competition does not require equal competitors. It requires that the inequalities between competitors arise from morally relevant sources — primarily the talent and effort of athletes. Natural differences in athletic capacity are morally relevant because they are constitutive of the persons who compete: they are not external advantages but expressions of who those people are. Differences that arise from wealth, access to superior equipment, or pharmacological enhancement are morally relevant in a different and more troubling way: they track contingencies external to the athlete's own development rather than the athlete's own capacities.

The Fair Opportunity Principle — formal structure
FOP: A sporting competition is fair if and only if the performance inequalities between competitors arise primarily from morally relevant sources.
Morally relevant sources of inequality: natural talent (constitutive of the person) × trained effort (the athlete's own dedication and discipline). These inequalities express who the athletes are as athletes.
Morally irrelevant sources of inequality: wealth of sports programme, access to medical expertise, pharmacological enhancement. These inequalities track contingencies external to the athlete's own development.
The FOP verdict on doping: pharmacological enhancement introduces inequalities that arise from morally irrelevant sources (access to drugs, wealth to purchase them, medical expertise to optimise their use) rather than from the athlete's own talent and training. This violates FOP even if the drugs are freely available to all — because equal access to drugs would produce inequalities tracking pharmacogenomics and wealth rather than athletic talent and effort.
i. why equal access does not solve the fairness problem

Equal access fails — pharmacogenomics means individuals respond differently to the same drugs; wealth inequalities in sophisticated regimes persist; the promised equality is illusorySavulescu's response to the fairness objection is to say that legalisation equalises access: if all athletes can use the same drugs, the advantage disappears. But this is wrong in two important ways. First, equal access to pharmacological agents does not produce equal pharmacological effects. Individual athletes respond differently to the same drugs due to their pharmacogenomics — their individual genetic profiles determine how efficiently their bodies metabolise and respond to performance-enhancing substances. Under legalisation, the athlete whose pharmacogenomics is most drug-responsive gains the greatest advantage, and this advantage is no more morally relevant than the advantage of the athlete who happens to respond best to altitude training. We would simply have replaced one form of natural lottery with another. Second, access to the most sophisticated pharmacological regimes will never be truly equal: it will always be distributed by the wealth of the athlete's programme, their access to specialist sports physicians, and their geographical location. The promised equality of legalisation is illusory.

ii. the biology of performance enhancement and the FOP

Biology and FOP — training exploits biological mechanisms of athletic development; drugs bypass those mechanisms; this maps onto the moral distinction the FOP requiresLoland and Hoppeler's 2012 paper establishes an important empirical underpinning for the FOP in the context of doping. They distinguish between performance enhancements that exploit the biological mechanisms of athletic development — the mechanisms by which training produces improvement — and enhancements that bypass or supplement those mechanisms through direct physiological intervention. Training stimulates adaptation; EPO delivers the result of adaptation without the training process. This biological distinction maps onto the moral distinction the FOP requires: training-based enhancements are expressions of athletic effort and talent; pharmacological enhancements that bypass the development process are not. The biology supports the ethics.

iii. the coercion argument for the FOP

Coercion and FOP — legalisation creates pharmacological gatekeeping; non-doping athletes face forced choice between values and sporting access; FOP is violatedThere is a further dimension of unfairness in legalisation that Savulescu's autonomy arguments obscure: the coercion of athletes who prefer not to enhance. Under legalisation, the competitive pressure to use drugs would be enormous. Athletes who for moral, medical, or personal reasons prefer not to use performance-enhancing drugs would face the choice between doping and accepting disqualifying competitive disadvantage. This is not an expression of their autonomy; it is a forced choice between their sporting ambitions and their values. The FOP is violated because the competitive access of non-doping athletes is curtailed not by their own talent or effort but by their unwillingness to use drugs. Legalisation does not liberate athlete autonomy; it creates a new form of pharmacological gatekeeping.

iv. a thin theory of sport versus a thick one

Thick vs thin — Savulescu's performance-maximising theory cannot explain why doped records differ morally from clean ones; the FOP's thick theory can; the framework must be replacedSavulescu's analysis of sport is what I call a thin theory: sport is a competition to see who can achieve the best performance outputs, and anything that improves those outputs is welcome. The Fair Opportunity Principle offers a thick theory: sport is a competition structured by the requirement that differences in outcome arise from morally relevant sources, primarily talent and effort. These two theories have very different implications for the permissibility of doping. The thin theory cannot explain why doping is worse than altitude tents; the thick theory can. The thin theory cannot explain why we care about records achieved under doping differently from records achieved without it; the thick theory can. The appropriate response to Savulescu is not merely to produce better arguments within his thin framework but to reject the framework and replace it with one adequate to the full moral complexity of sport as a human practice. The FOP is part of that replacement.

I want to address what I consider the deepest flaw in Savulescu’s project: a failure of philosophical method. Savulescu critiques the anti-doping position as if it were a collection of empirical claims and practical regulations, to be evaluated by evidence about harm and efficiency. But the anti-doping position is, at its core, an ethical stance rooted in a conception of what sport is and what makes it valuable. To evaluate it, you need to engage with that conception — and Savulescu, across multiple papers and fifteen years of advocacy, has consistently failed to do so. He attacks a straw man, and the real position stands unrefuted.

Let me be precise about what Savulescu mischaracterises. He treats the appeal to “naturalness” in anti-doping as if it were a crude metaphysical claim — that drugs are bad because they are artificial. But the sophisticated anti-doping position, as developed by Murray, Loland, myself, and others, does not rest on a metaphysical distinction between natural and artificial. It rests on a normative conception of what sport is: an excellence-based practice with internal goods, constituted by the norms and history of the practice itself, in which pharmacological manipulation is impermissible not because it is artificial but because it violates the internal standards of the practice.

i. sport as a Macintyrean practice

Macintyrean practice — sport has internal goods, virtues, and standards of excellence constituted by its history; anti-doping flows from the internal logic of the practice, not external impositionAlasdair MacIntyre’s account of practices provides the conceptual resources for understanding what sport is and why doping violates it. For MacIntyre, a practice is a complex, socially established cooperative activity with internal goods — goods that can only be achieved by participating in the practice according to its constitutive standards. The internal goods of sport are not the prizes, the contracts, or the public fame (these are external goods, achievable by other means). They are the specific forms of excellence that sport is designed to elicit and display: the extraordinary athletic achievements that are only possible through the sustained, disciplined development of human physical capacities within the structured context of sport. These goods are constituted by the norms and history of the practice — by what sport is — rather than by the preferences of individuals who happen to participate in it. Doping does not merely break a rule; it violates the internal goods of the practice by substituting a pharmacological shortcut for the genuine athletic development through which those goods are produced.

ii. the straw man in savulescu’s critique

Straw man — Savulescu attacks crude naturalness claim; sophisticated anti-doping is normative not metaphysical; his technology argument leaves the real position untouchedSavulescu’s critique of the naturalness argument proceeds as follows: he shows that elite sport already uses technology and science to manipulate physiology, and that the natural/artificial distinction is therefore arbitrary. This critique has force against a crude version of the anti-doping argument that says drugs are bad because they are artificial. But no sophisticated philosopher of sport holds this position. The sophisticated position says: drugs are impermissible not because they are artificial but because they violate the specific internal standards of excellence-based sporting competition — the FOP (Loland), the balance of excellences (Devine), or the Macintyrean internal goods of the practice (Murray, myself). None of these positions is touched by Savulescu’s technology inconsistency argument, because none of them rest on the natural/artificial distinction he is attacking. He is fighting a proxy war against an opponent who has already vacated that territory.

iii. the virtues of sport and what doping destroys

Sporting virtues — sport cultivates courage, resilience, and discipline; doping removes the conditions under which these virtues are developed; this is independent of health riskSport is not only valuable as a display of excellence; it is valuable as a cultivator of virtue. The virtues that elite sport develops — courage under pressure, resilience in the face of failure, discipline in training, the capacity to endure extraordinary physical discomfort in pursuit of a goal — are not merely means to athletic success. They are intrinsically valuable human capacities, developed through sporting practice and expressed in sporting competition. Doping undermines this cultivation. When an athlete uses pharmacological recovery agents to reduce the discomfort of hard training, or uses stimulants to mask fatigue during competition, they are not merely enhancing performance; they are removing the conditions under which the relevant virtues are cultivated and expressed. The capacity to endure — the virtue of athletic courage — requires the experience of genuine physical limit. Pharmacological manipulation of the limit does not develop the virtue of endurance; it sidesteps it. Sport as a virtue-cultivating practice is undermined by doping even when the doping is safe.

iv. integrity and the anti-doping stance

Integrity — anti-doping flows from internal goods and virtues of sport; Savulescu has never engaged with this dimension; the critique is systematically shallowI want to close by directly addressing Savulescu’s claim that anti-doping is merely an administrative rule imposed on athletes without principled justification. This is a serious mischaracterisation. Anti-doping is an ethical stance — one that flows from a coherent conception of sport as an excellence-based, virtue-cultivating practice with internal goods constituted by its history and norms. The case for anti-doping is not merely that rules exist and should be followed; it is that the rules exist because they protect the internal goods and virtues of a valuable human practice. Savulescu has never engaged adequately with this conception of sport. His critiques consistently address the surface of the anti-doping position — the harm claims, the fairness rules, the naturalness rhetoric — without engaging the philosophical foundation. A more philosophically serious engagement would require him to show either that sport does not have the internal goods and virtues we claim, or that doping does not violate them. He has shown neither. The anti-doping case stands.

3.Doping violates these internal goods by substituting a pharmacological shortcut for the genuine athletic development through which those goods are produced. It does not merely break a rule; it corrupts the practice itself.

3.Savulescu has never engaged adequately with these positions; he consistently addresses the surface rhetoric of anti-doping rather than its philosophical foundations.
∴Savulescu's critiques are directed at a straw man; the sophisticated anti-doping position — grounded in normative conceptions of sporting value — remains unrefuted by his technology inconsistency argument and his welfare-centred analysis.
Argument III — Sport cultivates virtues that doping undermines: pharmacological enhancement removes the conditions under which sporting virtues are developed
1.Sport develops intrinsically valuable virtues — courage, resilience, discipline, endurance — through the sustained experience of genuine physical limit, competitive pressure, and the possibility of failure.
2.Doping removes the conditions under which these virtues are cultivated and expressed: pharmacological recovery agents reduce the discomfort that cultivates resilience; stimulants mask the fatigue that cultivates endurance; the genuine physical limit is managed away.
3.Sport as a virtue-cultivating practice is undermined by doping even when the doping is safe, because the virtue-cultivating conditions are removed regardless of health risk.
∴Doping undermines the virtue-cultivating function of sport even when safe: it removes the conditions — genuine physical limit, unmedicated effort, real possibility of failure — under which sporting virtues are developed; this is an independent anti-doping argument that Savulescu's welfare framework ignores.
Argument IV — Anti-doping is an ethical stance, not an administrative rule; Savulescu has never engaged with its philosophical foundation
1.Anti-doping is an ethical stance flowing from a coherent conception of sport as an excellence-based, virtue-cultivating practice with internal goods. The rules protect those goods; they are not arbitrary administrative impositions.
2.A philosophically serious critique of anti-doping would require showing either that sport does not have these internal goods and virtues, or that doping does not violate them. Savulescu has shown neither.
3.His critiques address harm claims, fairness rules, and naturalness rhetoric — the surface of the anti-doping position — while leaving its philosophical foundation in the internal goods and virtues of sport entirely untouched.
∴Savulescu has never engaged the philosophical foundation of anti-doping: that sport has internal goods and virtues that doping violates; until he does, the anti-doping case stands; his advocacy is philosophically shallow because it consistently attacks the rhetoric rather than the argument.
