You Are Jeff

Richard Siken — Crush (Yale University Press, 2005)

Structure & Context
Form: 24 numbered prose-poem sections — Siken calls them "24 panels from a really difficult room" Central device: The sustained second-person "you" that addresses the beloved, the speaker's other self, and the reader simultaneously The name: Jeff is not glamorous. It is deliberately, pointedly ordinary — the reduction of desire to the most American, unremarkable name available The twins: Two men both called Jeff ride motorbikes through the poem; by section 20 Siken reveals they are one person — the revelation retroactively rewrites every previous section Prose rhythm: No line breaks — the prose form refuses the lyric pause, creating breathless accumulation; the sentences run as if they cannot stop themselves Context: Winner of the 2004 Yale Younger Poets prize; Louise Glück described the collection as a book about panic; Siken calls it a book about desire that drives you to panic
The Title
You Are Jeff
Sections 1–4 — The Two Jeffs on Motorbikes
Two men, both named Jeff, ride motorbikes. They are described as brothers, as twins. One is more aggressive. They pull over; one waits for the other with a lug wrench in his hand. The poem has not yet told us which one is "you."
They are brothers. They are twins.   One of them is you. Don't choose sides yet.
Section 5 — God and the Tuna Fish Sandwiches
God, hungry, makes two tuna fish sandwiches on sourdough, then realises the fish is bad. The sandwiches are already made. He does not know what to do with them.
the fish is bad. The sandwiches are already made.
Sections 6–7 — Three Jeffs Playing Cards
"You" plays cards with three men all called Jeff. They turn out to be your father, your brother, and your boyfriend. The same name holds three entirely different relationships.
your father, your brother, your boyfriend
Section 12 — The Direct Question
The speaker addresses "you" directly, having established that "you" loves to ride motorbikes — the physical freedom, the speed, the sensation of movement.
Who do you love, Jeff?
Sections 15 and 18 — The Self-Aware Passages
Two sections step back from the narrative and comment on the poem itself. Section 13 calls what we are reading "a love story." Section 18 comments on "how you make the meaning" — the act of interpretation is foregrounded as part of the poem's subject.
this is a love story  …  how you make the meaning
Section 20 — The Revelation
The twins are in a field of daisies or on their motorbikes, and they are talking to "you." But "you" are in pain and cannot understand them.
they're not brothers, they're just one guy, and he knows you, and he's talking to you, but you can-not understand him.
Section 23 — The Penultimate Promise
The speaker acknowledges that all the Jeffs in the poem are trying to tell "you" something. But not everything. If they wanted to tell you everything, they would do more — leave more traces, press harder.
All of these Jeffs are trying to tell you something — but not everything: if we wanted to tell you everything, we would leave more footprints in the snow or kiss you harder. One thing. Come closer. Listen…
Section 24 — The Closing Scene
You are in a car with a beautiful boy. You are trying not to tell him that you love him. You are trembling. He reaches over and touches you. Your heart is full of something for which you have no name.
You're in a car with a beautiful boy, and you're trying not to tell him that you love him, and you're trying to choke down the feeling, and you're trembling — and he reaches over and touches you, and your heart is filled with an emotion for which you have no name.
