Left Libertarianism without Chomsky

Published March 20, 2026

With Chomsky out of the picture after the latest release of the Epstein files exposed his scandalous involvement with the convicted sex offender, who should the left look to instead for intellectual guidance?

As many have pointed out, the libertarian socialist position he argued for all his life has much to say for it in countering the current of right-wing populism plaguing every corner of the planet.

Thankfully, there are other socialist libertarians we can look to for intellectual guidance instead.

One of those is philosopher Michael Otsuka. His book Libertarianism without Inequality argues for socialist libertarianism in a radically different way to Chomsky.

His strategy is to show that the very principle invoked by right-wing libertarians to defend their views actually undermines their view and supports left-wing egalitarian policies instead.

This principle is known as the principle of self-ownership. It says that we fully own ourselves and therefore everything we produce of our own accord we can rightly call ours too.

Most left-wing theorists, seeing that this principle leads to right-wing views, have attempted to reject this principle. But Otsuka noticed that this principle never truly justified right-wing views in the first place.

Instead, because virtually everything we produce involves the use of common resources, or the help of others, we owe others a share of the fruits of our production.

In granting the libertarian the principle, the left-libertarian can hold onto the intuitive force of the principle that we own ourselves, while at the same time showing that there are virtually no examples in real life where what we produce doesn't involve the help of others or the use of others' resources. This allows left-libertarians to have their cake and eat it (or eat their cake and have it, as the saying should go).

This gives left-libertarians an advantage over other left-wing political theories. In attempting to justify the right of the state to tax you or nudge you to behave in your supposed best interest, both communists and liberals have thought it necessary to bite the bullet and reject the principle, thereby subordinating our rights over ourselves to some degree to others.

There are two important dimensions to Otsuka’s left-libertarianism which make it preferable to Chomsky’s.

First, Otsuka's approach has promise for convincing the right that, by their own lights, they really ought to be egalitarians. Chomsky's own style of writing is not only highly divisive, but preaches only to the already converted.

Secondly, Otsuka's left-libertarianism does not make excuses for leaders who violate the self-ownership of their own citizens, even when they are vocally anti-imperialist on the global stage. Chomsky’s caustic anti-Western approach has been the culprit of excusing, and even supporting, regimes which are themselves committing flagrant human rights violations.

Stuck in the limbo of Chomsky's influence over the political left, Otsuka’s political philosophy promises to help us improve upon the many virtues Chomsky fought so successfully for.
