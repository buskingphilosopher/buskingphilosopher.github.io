The person-affecting restriction is not a mere intuition but the expression of a deep moral truth: value is always value for someone. The separateness of persons — the fact that there is no super-individual whose welfare is the sum of individual welfares — grounds a restriction on impersonal aggregation. Making People Happy vs. Making Happy PeopleJohann Frick — “Making People Happy, Not Making Happy People” (2014)
There is a fundamental moral asymmetry between making existing people happier and bringing new happy people into existence. The happiness of a person who already exists matters morally in a way that the putative happiness of a merely possible person does not. Possible people have no welfare interests that generate obligations on us: they cannot be benefited or harmed by our decisions about whether to create them. This asymmetry is constitutive of the person-affecting view and is independently compelling.

Against Substitution & the Incoherence of Impersonal ValueJoe Horton — “Always Agglomerate” (2021)
Impersonal axiology is not merely counterintuitive but incoherent: it requires treating people as substitutable for one another and treating the good of non-existent people as comparable to the good of existing ones. The person-affecting view is not a thesis about what makes outcomes worse; it is the denial that outcomes have impersonal value at all. Value is relational: it is always value-for, and there is no free-floating value that exists independently of the persons who bear it.

Chair,

I want to begin with what I take to be the deepest philosophical motivation for the person-affecting view — a motivation that goes beyond population ethics and touches on the most fundamental questions about what morality is for. The question is: what is the ultimate ground of moral value? My answer, which I share with the rest of this bench, is that value is always value-for-someone. There is no value that floats free of subjects who bear it.

“Each person’s life goes better or worse for that person. There is no super-individual for whom the sum of welfare across persons constitutes welfare. The separateness of persons is not a regrettable feature of reality but its moral foundation.”
i. the separateness of persons as a moral foundation

Separateness of persons — Rawls's point: persons are distinct, not parts of a super-individual; impersonal aggregation treats the moral universe as one person's life writ large; this is a category errorRawls made this point against utilitarianism and it applies with equal force against all impersonal axiology. Utilitarianism, Rawls observed, extends to society the principle of rational choice for one individual: just as it is rational for an individual to accept some sacrifice now for greater benefit later, so utilitarianism holds that it is rational for society to impose sacrifice on some for greater benefit to others. But this extension ignores the fundamental difference between the one and the many. In an individual life, there is one subject across whose experiences the trade-off is made. In a society, different experiences belong to different subjects. There is no super-individual for whom the aggregate welfare constitutes a welfare. When we aggregate welfare across persons and call it “total value,” we are not measuring anything that exists. We are performing a mathematical operation on numbers that represent real people’s welfare, but the result of the operation is not itself anyone’s welfare. The motion before this house states the implication: an outcome can only be morally worse if it is personally worse for a specific person, because only personal welfare is genuine welfare.

ii. the anti-aggregationist case

Anti-aggregationism — aggregating welfare across persons treats persons as vessels for welfare rather than as ends in themselves; it generates the conclusion that any number of headaches outweighs one death, which is absurdThe impersonal view is committed to aggregating welfare across persons. This commitment generates a series of deeply troubling conclusions. Derek Parfit himself noted the “repugnant conclusion” in population ethics. But the aggregation problem is equally severe in fixed-population cases. The aggregationist must hold that a sufficient number of very small benefits to very many people can outweigh a very large harm to one person — that enough headaches, summed together, can outweigh a death. This conclusion is independently objectionable, quite apart from the population cases. It follows from treating persons as mere containers for welfare rather than as individuals whose interests each demand separate attention. The person-affecting view, by contrast, assesses outcomes person by person: we ask whether each person is better or worse off, and we assess the outcome’s moral value in terms of those person-specific assessments. No one is sacrificed for an aggregate that exists only in a mathematical abstraction.

iii. the non-identity problem and its proper response

Non-Identity — Parfit's Problem shows that the narrow person-affecting view cannot condemn creating a worse-off person whose existence depends on the very choice that creates them; but this is a reason to refine PAV, not abandon itThe opposition will press the Non-Identity Problem: if we choose a policy that results in a worse-off person’s existence, we have not harmed anyone, since that person would not otherwise have existed at all. Therefore the narrow PAV cannot condemn the policy. I want to separate two responses. The first, which my colleague Melinda Roberts will develop, is that the Problem is soluble within the person-affecting framework through careful attention to the formulation of the principle. The second, which I want to press here, is that the Non-Identity Problem, even if we concede for argument’s sake that it poses a genuine challenge to PAV, is not a reason to abandon PAV and embrace impersonal axiology. It is a reason to develop a more refined version of PAV. The alternative — embracing impersonal totalism — generates the Repugnant Conclusion, the Mere Addition Paradox, the repellent verdicts in same-number cases, and a host of other deeply objectionable results. The theoretical cost of solving one problem by creating fifty others is not obviously worth paying.

iv. impersonal value and the reification of the void

Reification problem — impersonal axiology requires treating the welfare of non-existent possible people as contributing to an outcome's value; but non-existent people have no welfare; impersonal value reifies what does not existMy deepest objection to the opposition’s position is this: impersonal axiology requires that we treat the welfare of non-existent possible people as contributing to an outcome’s value. A world in which a possible happy person does not exist is, on the impersonal view, worse than a world in which they do — not worse for anyone, but worse simpliciter. But this requires that non-existent possible people have welfare — that there is something it would be like to be them, some good or bad that accrues to them, in worlds where they do not exist. There is not. Non-existence is not a state that anything is in; a possible person is not a person; the welfare of a non-existent entity is not welfare. The impersonal view reifies the void: it treats mathematical representations of counterfactual welfare as if they were morally real contributions to an outcome’s value. The person-affecting view simply declines to engage in this reification.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The separateness of persons grounds the person-affecting restriction: value is always value-for-someone
1.Persons are distinct individuals with separate lives; there is no super-individual for whom aggregate welfare constitutes welfare. This is Rawls’s fundamental objection to utilitarian aggregation.
2.Impersonal axiology treats the summed welfare of distinct persons as itself a welfare-like quantity whose magnitude determines an outcome’s moral value. This commits the category error of treating the sum of individuals’ welfares as if it were one individual’s welfare.
3.Since there is no super-individual to bear aggregate welfare, aggregate welfare is not welfare; it does not ground moral value. Only the welfare of specific persons grounds moral value.
∴The separateness of persons grounds the person-affecting restriction: an outcome has negative moral value only if it is worse for some specific person; impersonal aggregate value is a category error.
Argument II — Anti-aggregationism: summing welfare across persons treats persons as containers rather than as ends in themselves
1.Impersonal aggregation implies that sufficiently many small benefits to many can outweigh a large harm to one — that enough headaches can outweigh a death. This treats persons as mere vessels for welfare.
2.Treating persons as mere vessels for welfare fails to respect the separateness of persons and generates verdicts that are independently morally objectionable regardless of population-ethical commitments.
3.The person-affecting view assesses outcomes person-by-person and does not aggregate across persons; it therefore respects the separateness of persons and avoids these verdicts.
∴The anti-aggregationist implications of PAV are a feature: by refusing to sum welfare across persons, PAV respects each person as an end in themselves rather than a container for welfare.
Argument III — The Non-Identity Problem is a reason to refine PAV, not abandon it for a view with far worse implications
1.The Non-Identity Problem challenges the narrow PAV: a worse-off person whose existence depends on the policy creating them cannot have been harmed by that policy, since they would not otherwise have existed.
2.But abandoning PAV for impersonal totalism “solves” this problem only by generating the Repugnant Conclusion, the Mere Addition Paradox, and numerous other deeply objectionable verdicts.
3.Solving one problem by generating fifty others is not theoretical progress. The correct response to the Non-Identity Problem is to develop a more refined PAV, not to embrace impersonal axiology.
∴The Non-Identity Problem is not a decisive objection to PAV; it motivates refinement of PAV; the alternative of impersonal axiology creates more and worse problems than it solves.
Argument IV — Impersonal value reifies the void: non-existent possible people have no welfare and cannot ground moral value
1.Impersonal axiology treats the welfare of non-existent possible people as contributing positively to an outcome’s value. A world without a possible happy person is worse (impersonally) than one with them.
2.But non-existent possible people have no welfare. Non-existence is not a state; a merely possible person is not a person; there is nothing it would be like to be them in worlds where they do not exist.
3.Impersonal value therefore requires treating mathematical representations of counterfactual welfare as if they were genuine moral contributions — a reification of what does not exist.
∴Impersonal axiology commits the error of reifying non-existent possible people’s welfare as genuine moral value; since non-existent people have no welfare, impersonal value has no genuine foundation.

Chair,

I want to engage directly with the Non-Identity Problem, because the opposition treats it as a decisive refutation of the person-affecting view. I deny that it is. What it is, properly understood, is an invitation to formulate the person-affecting view more carefully. The narrow version of the view, which holds that an outcome is worse only if it is worse for someone who exists in both possible outcomes, is indeed challenged by Non-Identity cases. But the narrow version is not the only version, and it is not the best version.

Parfit’s Non-Identity Problem (canonical form). A woman can conceive now, producing a child with a moderately disadvantaged life, or wait two months, producing a different child with a life of full flourishing. The numerical identity of the child depends on when conception occurs. If she conceives now, the resulting person (call her A) will have a life worth living but below the flourishing level. A does not exist in the alternative outcome. A is not harmed by the choice to conceive now, since in the only alternative she does not exist. Yet there seems to be something wrong with the choice. The narrow PAV cannot explain what.
i. the actual person-affecting principle

Actual PAP — an outcome is worse only if it is worse for someone who actually exists in at least one of the outcomes; this covers the Non-Identity case without requiring impersonalismMy proposal — the Actual Person-Affecting Principle — holds that an outcome O1 is worse than O2 if and only if someone who actually exists in at least one of O1 or O2 is worse off in O1 than they would be in O2. This is slightly broader than the narrow PAV (which requires existence in both outcomes) but significantly narrower than full impersonal axiology (which considers possible people who exist in neither outcome). The Actual PAP covers the Non-Identity case: A exists in O1 (she is created), and she is worse off in O1 than she would be in O2 — but note that O2 does not exist for A because she does not exist in O2. The comparison must be with a counterfactual in which she does not exist, and non-existence is not worse for A. What the Actual PAP captures is the wrongness of the choice to create a worse-off person when a better alternative was available, without requiring that the worse-off person could have existed in the better alternative.

ii. the asymmetry and its vindication

Asymmetry — it is wrong to create a miserable life but not obligatory to create a happy one; this asymmetry is vindicated by PAV and violated by impersonal totalismThe standard person-affecting view vindicates what Benatar calls the asymmetry — or rather, the modest asymmetry that everyone finds intuitive: it is bad (wrong) to create a person who will have a life of suffering, but it is not obligatory to create a person who will have a life of happiness. This asymmetry is exactly what impersonal totalism denies: if adding a happy person to the world makes it better, then we are, all else equal, under some moral pressure to add happy people. The totalist must either accept this conclusion — which most people find deeply counterintuitive — or offer an ad hoc explanation for why the positive and negative cases are treated differently. The person-affecting view has a principled explanation: possible happy people have no welfare interests that ground obligations. Possible suffering people do not exist yet and cannot yet be harmed, but the person we will create — who will exist in the outcome — will be harmed by our choice. This is the kernel of the Actual PAP.

iii. responding to the argument from counterintuitive implications

Counterintuitive implications — PAV is said to imply that extinction is not bad because no one would exist to be harmed; PAV defenders accept a restricted conclusion: extinction harms those who exist, not those who will never existThe opposition will argue that the person-affecting view implies that human extinction is not bad — or at least not impersonally bad — because there would be no one in existence to be made worse off by it. Some versions of this objection are presented as devastating. I want to distinguish two questions. First: is extinction bad for those who currently exist? Yes: their lives are cut short, their projects are frustrated, their loved ones are lost. Second: is extinction bad because of the welfare of the people who never exist in the extinction scenario? My answer is no — not because those possible people do not matter, but because they are not people. They are possible people: mathematical constructions in an axiology. The person-affecting view’s verdict on extinction is that it is very bad — catastrophically bad, in fact, for all those who exist and whose lives it terminates — without any need to invoke the welfare of possible future people who will never be born.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Actual PAP handles Non-Identity without impersonalism: existence in one outcome is sufficient
1.The narrow PAV (existence in both outcomes required) fails Non-Identity cases: the worse-off person exists in only one outcome and cannot be compared across both.
2.The Actual PAP requires existence in at least one outcome: O1 is worse than O2 if someone who exists in O1 or O2 is worse off in O1. The Non-Identity person (existing in O1) is worse off than they would be in a counterfactual with better conditions.
3.This captures the wrongness of creating a worse-off person when a better alternative was available, without invoking the welfare of possible people who exist in neither outcome.
∴The Actual PAP handles the Non-Identity Problem within the person-affecting framework; the problem challenges the narrow PAV but not the correctly formulated Actual PAP.
Argument II — PAV vindicates the asymmetry between creating suffering and failing to create happiness; totalism violates it
1.The modest asymmetry: creating a person who will suffer is bad; failing to create a person who would flourish is not obligatory. This is near-universally accepted.
2.Impersonal totalism implies that adding a happy person improves the world and thus generates (some) moral pressure to add happy people. It violates the asymmetry.
3.PAV has a principled account of the asymmetry: possible happy people have no welfare interests; the person we create who suffers is made worse off (relative to non-existence being the baseline) by the conditions of their creation.
∴PAV provides a principled vindication of the asymmetry between procreative harm and procreative benefit; totalism violates this asymmetry and can offer only ad hoc explanations for it.
Argument III — Extinction is catastrophically bad for those who exist; PAV needs no possible-people welfare to condemn it
1.Extinction terminates the lives of all currently existing people, frustrated their projects and relationships. This is catastrophically bad for them — a verdict PAV delivers straightforwardly.
2.The further claim that extinction is bad because of the welfare of possible people who will never exist requires treating possible people as morally relevant — which PAV denies.
3.PAV’s verdict on extinction is severe enough to motivate strong obligations to prevent it, grounded entirely in the welfare of actual existing people.
∴PAV condemns extinction as catastrophically bad for actual people without requiring possible-people welfare; the objection that PAV is too permissive about extinction conflates the (very real) badness for actual people with a separate impersonal claim.

Chair,

I want to focus on what I believe is the most powerful positive argument for the person-affecting view: that all of its rivals, including those defended by the opposition, generate the Repugnant Conclusion or conclusions equally unacceptable. This is not merely a matter of intuition; it is a constraint that any adequate population axiology must satisfy. The Repugnant Conclusion is not merely repugnant in the sense of being distasteful. It is a reductio of any axiology that generates it.

The Repugnant Conclusion (Parfit). For any possible population of at least ten billion people, all with very high quality of life, there must be some much larger imaginable population whose existence, if other things are equal, would be better, even though its members have lives that are barely worth living. This follows from impersonal totalism: if we can add enough people with lives barely worth living, the total welfare eventually exceeds that of the smaller population. Parfit called this conclusion repugnant. He spent twenty years trying to avoid it. He could not.
i. why the repugnant conclusion is unacceptable

Repugnant Conclusion — a world of ten billion very happy people cannot be better than one of a trillion people with lives barely worth living; this is not a minor counterintuitive implication but a reductio of totalismThe Repugnant Conclusion is not a minor counterintuitive implication that can be set aside while the theory is otherwise accepted. It is a fundamental verdict about what matters morally: that the quality of individual lives is eventually overridden by sheer numbers, however low the quality and however high the numbers must go to achieve the override. A world in which every individual’s life is just barely worth living — in which every day is, on balance, marginally positive but contains no deep friendships, no great achievements, no love, nothing of genuine value beyond bare survival — is better than a world in which ten billion people live flourishing, rich, deeply meaningful lives, provided the former world contains enough people. This is not an implication we should learn to live with. It is a demonstration that totalism has the wrong theory of what makes an outcome better or worse. The person-affecting view, by restricting evaluative comparisons to the welfare of actual people in the outcomes being compared, avoids this conclusion entirely.

ii. weak person-affecting views and variable population cases

Weak PAV — outcomes are only impersonally comparable when they affect the same people; in variable population cases, comparisons are partial or indeterminate; this is honest about the limits of cross-existence comparisonI defend a weak person-affecting view: outcomes are impersonally comparable when they differ only in their effects on people who exist in both outcomes. When the outcomes involve different people — as in variable-population cases — the comparison is either partial or genuinely indeterminate. This is not a failure of the view; it is the honest recognition that comparing the welfare of people who exist in one outcome with the welfare of different people who exist in another outcome involves a kind of comparison for which there is no objective foundation. The opposition will say this is revisionary: it denies that there is a fact of the matter about whether a world with ten billion flourishing people is better than a world with a trillion barely-living people. I accept this implication. There is no such fact. Or rather, the question asks us to compare things that are not comparable: the welfare of people who are not the same people.

iii. responding to the impossibility theorems

Impossibility theorems — Arrhenius's theorems show no population axiology satisfies all plausible conditions; the PAV response is to reject totality conditions rather than person-affecting ones, since the latter are more fundamentalGustaf Arrhenius has proved a series of impossibility theorems showing that no population axiology can simultaneously satisfy all of the plausible conditions we would want it to satisfy. The opposition takes these theorems to show that the person-affecting view must be abandoned. I disagree. Impossibility theorems show that some condition must be abandoned; they do not show which. The person-affecting conditions — that morally relevant improvements must benefit actual people — are more fundamental and more secure than the totality conditions that generate the Repugnant Conclusion. When we must choose which plausible condition to abandon, we should abandon the one whose abandonment generates less moral catastrophe. Abandoning totality conditions generates indeterminacy in variable-population cases. Abandoning person-affecting conditions generates the Repugnant Conclusion, the Mere Addition Paradox, and the obligation to maximise population. The choice is clear.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Repugnant Conclusion is a reductio of impersonal totalism, not a counterintuitive implication to be accepted
1.Impersonal totalism implies that a world of a trillion people with lives barely worth living is better than one of ten billion with very high flourishing, provided the numbers are large enough. This is the Repugnant Conclusion.
2.The Repugnant Conclusion is not merely counterintuitive but a fundamental error about what matters morally: it implies that quality of individual lives is eventually overridden by quantity, however low the quality.
3.A theory whose core implication is that individual human flourishing is eventually morally overridden by quantity of bare existence has the wrong theory of moral value. The Repugnant Conclusion is a reductio.
∴The Repugnant Conclusion is a reductio of impersonal totalism; any axiology that generates it should be rejected; the person-affecting view avoids it by restricting comparisons to the welfare of actual people.
Argument II — Weak PAV: variable-population comparisons are partial or indeterminate; this is honest rather than a failure
1.Outcomes affecting the same people can be compared impersonally: one is better if it is better for more people and worse for none, or better for some and compensatingly worse for fewer. This is uncontroversial.
2.Outcomes involving different people cannot be compared by summing welfare across non-identical populations: there is no objective basis for comparing the welfare of person A in world W1 with the welfare of different person B in world W2.
3.Acknowledging indeterminacy in variable-population comparisons is the honest recognition of the limits of cross-existence comparison, not a failure of the theory.
∴Weak PAV is honest about the limits of population comparison; indeterminacy in variable-population cases is preferable to the false precision of totalism that generates the Repugnant Conclusion.
Argument III — Arrhenius’s impossibility theorems require abandoning some condition; person-affecting conditions should be retained over totality conditions
1.Arrhenius’s theorems prove no population axiology satisfies all plausible desiderata simultaneously. Some condition must be abandoned.
2.The choice of which condition to abandon is not determined by the theorems themselves; it requires a prior judgement about which conditions are most fundamental and whose violation generates less moral damage.
3.Person-affecting conditions (morally relevant improvements must benefit actual people) are more fundamental than totality conditions; abandoning totality generates indeterminacy; abandoning person-affecting generates the Repugnant Conclusion and population maximisation obligations.
∴When forced to choose which condition to abandon by impossibility theorems, we should abandon totality conditions rather than person-affecting ones; indeterminacy is preferable to the Repugnant Conclusion.

I want to press the distinction that I regard as the philosophical heart of this debate: the distinction between making people happy and making happy people. These are not merely different means to the same end. They are different ends, answerable to different moral considerations. The proposition’s motion expresses this distinction: an outcome is worse only if it is personally worse for a specific person. The crucial question is whether a person who does not yet exist — who exists in one possible outcome but not another — is a specific person in the morally relevant sense.

i. the interests of actual vs. merely possible people

Actual vs. possible — actual people have welfare interests that generate obligations; merely possible people are not yet people and have no welfare interests; the distinction is morally fundamental, not merely metaphysicalActual people — people who exist, or who will exist given the choices we do not vary in a given decision — have welfare interests that generate obligations on us. When I make a decision that will affect my existing children, their welfare is a direct moral constraint: I am obligated to take it into account, to weigh it seriously, to avoid harming it unnecessarily. Merely possible people — people who might exist depending on choices I might make — are not people in the morally relevant sense. They are not people at all; they are mathematical constructions in a welfare axiology. The possible child I might conceive if I choose a certain reproductive path does not yet exist; it has no welfare, no experiences, no interests, no standing in the moral community. I do not owe it anything, because there is no “it” to owe things to. The moral relevance of a person arises with the person’s existence; it does not precede it.

ii. why creating happy people is not obligatory

No obligation to create — if possible people have no welfare interests, creating a happy person generates no obligation from their perspective; the Repugnant Conclusion and the obligation to maximise population both presuppose that possible people's welfare mattersIf possible people have no welfare interests that generate obligations, then there is no obligation grounded in their perspective to create them. A possible happy person is not worse off if they are not created, because they are not a person, and non-persons have no welfare comparative states. The Repugnant Conclusion requires that the welfare of a trillion people with barely-worth-living lives grounds a positive verdict about that outcome. But those trillion people are, in the alternative outcome, merely possible. They have no welfare; they have no interests; their non-creation wrongs no one. The impersonal view generates the Repugnant Conclusion precisely by assigning welfare to possible people and then aggregating it. Remove the assignment, and the conclusion does not follow.

iii. the significance of coming into existence

Coming into existence — the moment of creation is morally significant: it creates a person with interests; prior to creation there is no person whose interests constrain our decisions; this is not a contingent fact but a conceptual oneThe opposition treats coming into existence as morally arbitrary — as if whether a person exists or not is merely a fact about which possible world is actual, making no difference to their moral status. I deny this. Coming into existence is morally significant. It is the creation of a new subject of welfare — a new locus of interests, experiences, and wellbeing that now makes genuine moral claims on us. Before creation, there is no such subject; after creation, there is. This is not a metaphysical contingency but a conceptual truth about what it is to be a person with welfare interests. The person-affecting view is not a special claim about population ethics; it is an expression of the general truth that obligations require subjects. There must be someone to whom one is obligated. Possible people are not someone.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Actual people have welfare interests; merely possible people do not; this distinction is morally fundamental
1.Actual people (existing or determinately future given fixed choices) have welfare: they can be benefited or harmed, and this welfare generates direct moral obligations on us.
2.Merely possible people are not people; they are mathematical constructions in a welfare axiology. They have no welfare, no experiences, no interests, and no standing in the moral community.
3.Moral relevance arises with existence; it does not precede it. The possible child I might conceive has no welfare to which I am obligated until it exists.
∴The distinction between actual and merely possible people is morally fundamental: only actual people’s welfare generates obligations; possible people have no welfare and therefore generate no obligations.
Argument II — Without possible-people welfare, the Repugnant Conclusion does not follow; totalism requires what PAV denies
1.The Repugnant Conclusion requires that the welfare of a trillion barely-living people grounds a positive impersonal verdict about an outcome. In the alternative outcome, those trillion people are merely possible.
2.If merely possible people have no welfare, their non-creation wrongs no one and their possible welfare grounds no impersonal verdict. The Repugnant Conclusion does not follow.
3.Totalism generates the Repugnant Conclusion precisely by assigning welfare to possible people and aggregating it. Remove the assignment and the conclusion is blocked at its source.
∴Denying welfare to merely possible people blocks the Repugnant Conclusion at its source; the conclusion is an artefact of the impersonal view’s assignment of welfare to possible people, not a brute fact about population ethics.
Argument III — Coming into existence creates a new subject of welfare; this is conceptually and morally significant
1.Coming into existence creates a new subject of welfare with genuine interests and experiences that now make moral claims. Before creation, no such subject exists.
2.This is not metaphysically contingent but conceptually necessary: a person with welfare interests is not the sort of thing that can exist in possible worlds where they have not come into existence.
3.Obligations require subjects; possible people are not subjects; therefore possible people generate no obligations. PAV expresses this general truth about the subject-relativity of obligations.
∴The moral significance of coming into existence is not arbitrary; it reflects the conceptual truth that welfare subjects must exist to have welfare; PAV correctly captures this by restricting moral comparisons to actual people.

I want to press a point about the logical structure of the impersonal view that I think is insufficiently appreciated: the impersonal view does not merely expand the class of morally relevant considerations. It makes a specific metaphysical claim about value — that there is free-floating, impersonal value that exists independently of any subject who bears it. I want to argue that this claim is either false or, if it can be made true, does so only by redefining “value” in a way that strips it of its moral relevance.

i. value as relational

Value is relational — value is always value-for; there is no free-floating impersonal value; the impersonal view mistakes a mathematical aggregation for a metaphysical fact about valueValue, I want to argue, is essentially relational: it is always value-for-someone. This is not merely a claim about moral value but about the structure of value in general. When we say that something is good, we mean either that it is good for someone — that it promotes their welfare, satisfies their preferences, enables their flourishing — or that it is instrumentally related to such goods. The idea of free-floating value — value that exists without being value for anyone, that does not contribute to anyone’s welfare — is not a coherent idea. The impersonal view, when it says that a world with more happy people is impersonally better, is making a claim that does not correspond to any real property of the world. It is saying that the sum of a certain mathematical operation over welfare values is larger. This is a fact about a mathematical operation, not a fact about value.

ii. the substitution objection

Substitution — impersonal totalism implies that persons are substitutable: replacing one happy person with an equally happy different person leaves value unchanged; but this treats persons as means to impersonal value rather than as ends in themselvesImpersonal totalism has a further implication that deserves explicit attention: it treats persons as substitutable for one another. If what matters is total welfare, then a world with person A at welfare level 10 is equally good as a world with different person B at welfare level 10. A and B are morally interchangeable: what matters is the welfare, not the person. This is not merely a counterintuitive implication; it is a fundamental failure of moral respect. I want to approach this debate from a formal angle: the question of what coherence conditions any adequate axiology must satisfy, and whether the person-affecting view satisfies them. My argument is that PAV fails basic formal requirements — requirements that are not themselves ethically contentious but that must be met for a view to count as a coherent axiology at all — and that these failures are not peripheral but central to the view’s structure.

i. the formal structure of betterness relations

Formal coherence — betterness must be transitive, complete (or at least semi-ordered), and consistent with Pareto; PAV violates these conditions in systematic, structural ways that cannot be patchedA betterness relation must satisfy at least transitivity and consistency with Pareto improvements. Transitivity: if A is better than B and B is better than C, then A is better than C. Pareto: if outcome A is better than B for at least one person and worse for nobody, then A is better than B. PAV’s person-affecting restriction generates systematic violations of these conditions when combined with the full set of our evaluative commitments. In cases where outcome A Pareto-dominates B, and B Pareto-dominates C, PAV must say A is better than C — but when A and C involve partially overlapping populations, PAV may render them incomparable or indeterminate, violating transitivity. These violations are not edge cases that a refined PAV can patch; they are structural consequences of the view’s commitment to requiring a specific beneficiary for every improvement.

ii. person-relative value and its limits

Person-relative limits — the proposition correctly identifies that value is often person-relative; but this does not entail that all value is person-relative; some value (equality, fairness) is agent-neutral; PAV overgeneralises from the person-relative insightThe proposition correctly identifies an important truth: much value is person-relative. An outcome is better-for-A if it improves A’s welfare; this person-relative betterness is genuine and important. But the proposition overgeneralises: from the fact that much value is person-relative, it concludes that all value must be person-relative. This does not follow. Consider the value of equality: a more equal distribution of welfare seems better than a less equal one, even when the total welfare is the same and some specific people are neither better nor worse off. Consider the value of fairness: a fair procedure seems better than an unfair one even when the outcomes for specific people are the same. These are genuine values that are not person-relative in the PAV sense: no specific person is made better off by greater equality or greater fairness in these cases. PAV cannot accommodate them without abandoning its core commitment.

iii. the trade-off problem in same-number cases

Same-number trade-offs — PAV cannot rationally evaluate trade-offs between welfare gains for some and welfare losses for others; it renders such cases incomparable or indeterminate, making it useless for most practical ethical reasoning even in fixed-population contextsEven in same-number cases — where PAV should be most tractable — the view faces a decisive problem: it cannot rationally evaluate trade-offs between welfare gains for some and welfare losses for others. Suppose we must choose between policy A (great benefit to persons 1–50, moderate cost to persons 51–100) and policy B (moderate benefit to persons 1–50, great benefit to persons 51–100). PAV renders these outcomes incomparable in many formulations, because neither is better for everyone. But public policy, medical resource allocation, and virtually all practical ethics requires making exactly these trade-offs. A view that renders them incomparable is not merely incomplete; it is useless for practical reasoning. The impersonal view, by contrast, provides a principled framework for evaluating these trade-offs, even if the resulting verdicts are sometimes counterintuitive. An axiology that cannot evaluate trade-offs is not a competitor to impersonalism; it is not an axiology at all.
