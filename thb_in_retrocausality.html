Mr Speaker,

The central question of this debate is whether the temporal asymmetry of causation — the apparent fact that causes precede effects — is a deep, objective feature of the physical world, or whether it is an artefact of our own temporally situated perspective. I shall argue for the latter, and I shall argue that taking this view seriously opens the door to retrocausality as not merely coherent but empirically motivated.

My starting point is what I call the Archimedean view: the view of the world from no particular point in time. From this perspective, the laws of fundamental physics are almost entirely time-symmetric. There is no preferred direction of time in quantum mechanics, electrodynamics, or general relativity. The temporal asymmetry we observe — the asymmetry between cause and effect, between records and anticipations, between the open future and the fixed past — is a macroscopic, thermodynamic phenomenon, not a fundamental physical one.

The central argument for time-symmetry.

The laws of physics, at the fundamental level, are time-reversal invariant (or CPT-symmetric in quantum field theory). The manifest asymmetry of our experience — that causes precede effects, that the past is fixed and the future open — is explained by the low-entropy initial conditions of the universe and the thermodynamic arrow of time. But this explanation is contingent: it tells us why we, situated as we are, experience causation as temporally directed. It does not establish that causation is metaphysically forward-directed. A being with the reverse thermodynamic orientation would experience a reversed causal arrow. Neither perspective is privileged.

Consequence: the asymmetry of causation is perspectival, not fundamental. Retrocausality is in principle as admissible as forward causation.
i. the source of the causal arrow

Causal arrow — thermodynamic, not metaphysical; perspectival not fundamental; Price's Archimedean viewI want to distinguish two questions. The first is an empirical question: why does our world exhibit the temporal asymmetries it does? The second is a conceptual question: is temporal direction a necessary component of the causal relation itself? My opponents run these questions together. They take the empirical fact of a thermodynamic arrow and use it to establish a metaphysical conclusion about the direction of causation. But the two questions are independent. The thermodynamic arrow explains why we, as agents embedded in time in a particular way, represent causation as forward-directed. It does not follow that causation, considered from the Archimedean perspective, is constitutively forward-directed. Once we separate these questions, the door to retrocausality opens.

ii. quantum mechanics as empirical motivation

Quantum non-locality — retrocausality resolves it without non-local signalling; EPR correlations best explained retrocausallyThe empirical motivation for retrocausality comes from quantum mechanics, specifically from the phenomenon of entanglement and the correlations revealed by Bell-inequality violations. These correlations cannot be explained by any local hidden variable theory with forward causation only — this is what Bell’s theorem establishes. The standard responses are to accept non-locality (Bohmian mechanics), to accept the many-worlds interpretation, or to accept that quantum mechanics is irreducibly non-classical. But there is a fourth option: to allow that the measurement settings can influence, retrocausally, the values of hidden variables at the time of preparation. This retrocausal model restores local causality. The correlations are not explained by spooky action at a distance but by ordinary local causation running partly backwards in time.

iii. the agent-relativist account of the causal asymmetry

Agent relativism — causal asymmetry explained by asymmetry of agency; no metaphysical asymmetry requiredWhy do we experience causation as temporally asymmetric? My answer is agent-relativist: the asymmetry of causation is the asymmetry of agency. We can intervene on the future but not on the past because of our thermodynamic situation: we have memories of the past and deliberate about the future, and our deliberations can make a difference to what comes after but not to what has already occurred, given the thermodynamic gradient. This explains the phenomenology of causation without entrenching its temporal direction as a metaphysical necessity. The asymmetry of causation is real for us, in our situation; it is not a fundamental feature of the universe.

iv. against the fixity of the past

Fixity of the past — conflates epistemic inaccessibility with causal impossibility; Price agrees with Dummett hereThe most common objection to retrocausality is the fixity of the past: the past has already occurred, so how could it be affected by what we do now? But this objection conflates two distinct claims. The first is an epistemic claim: we cannot change what we already know to have happened. The second is a causal claim: events in the past are causally inaccessible to present interventions. These come apart. If retrocausality obtains, then the past event was always going to be influenced by the future measurement; it was the outcome of a causal process that ran partly backwards. There is no contradiction in this, only the need to revise our folk-causal picture. The fixity of the past is an epistemic constraint, not a metaphysical one.

2.The asymmetry we observe in causation derives from the thermodynamic arrow; it reflects our embedded, temporally situated perspective, not a mind-independent metaphysical asymmetry.
3.From the Archimedean view (outside time), causal asymmetry is not a fundamental feature of physical reality; it is a perspectival projection.
∴Retrocausality is not ruled out by the fundamental physics; the apparent asymmetry of causation is perspectival and thermodynamic, not a metaphysical constraint on possible causal relations.

The Transactional Interpretation of quantum mechanics is the most direct and most naturally retrocausal of the major interpretations. I want to show why it deserves to be taken seriously as a physical proposal, and why its retrocausal structure is a strength rather than a weakness.

The Transactional Interpretation begins from a simple observation: the Schrödinger equation, like the classical wave equation, has both advanced (backward-in-time) and retarded (forward-in-time) solutions. Conventional quantum mechanics arbitrarily discards the advanced solutions. I argue that we should take both seriously. When we do, quantum measurement is no longer a mystery: it is the completion of a “transaction” between a retarded offer wave emitted by the source and an advanced confirmation wave emitted by the absorber. The transaction collapses the wave function and determines the outcome non-locally but without any violation of special relativity.

“The transactional interpretation provides an intuitively satisfying account of quantum mechanics in which the process of measurement is understood as a completed transaction between emitter and absorber, involving both advanced and retarded waves propagating across spacetime.” — Cramer, 1986
i. the advanced wave as physical

Advanced wave — both solutions physically real; discarding advanced solution is arbitrary; TI takes both seriouslyThe standard argument against the Transactional Interpretation begins with the claim that advanced solutions to the wave equation are unphysical: they correspond to waves propagating backwards in time and so cannot represent anything real. But this argument is question-begging. It assumes at the outset that nothing physical can propagate backwards in time. The Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory in classical electrodynamics provides a precedent: advanced electromagnetic waves were there shown to be physically necessary for a consistent treatment of radiation reaction. If we are prepared to take advanced electromagnetic waves seriously in the classical case, we should be prepared to take advanced quantum waves seriously in the quantum case.

ii. the measurement problem dissolved

Measurement problem — TI dissolves it; wave function collapse is a completed transaction, not an irreducible mysteryThe measurement problem — why quantum superpositions appear to collapse to definite outcomes upon measurement — is one of the deepest unsolved problems in the foundations of quantum mechanics. The Transactional Interpretation dissolves it: there is no mysterious collapse. The offer wave travels forward in time from the source, establishing a superposition of possible outcomes. Each potential absorber emits a confirmation wave backwards in time. The transaction is completed when the offer and confirmation waves reinforce each other at both the emission and absorption events. The outcome is determined by the completed transaction across spacetime: no collapse is needed because the definite outcome was always the result of the retrocausal transaction.

iii. non-locality without action at a distance

Non-locality — TI explains EPR correlations without FTL signalling; retrocausal transactions are locally causal in 4DThe EPR correlations — the fact that measurements on entangled particles yield correlated outcomes regardless of their spatial separation — seem to require action at a distance. The Transactional Interpretation explains these correlations without any action at a distance: the correlation is established by the transaction, which is a four-dimensional spacetime process. The transaction involves local interactions at both ends, connected by advanced and retarded waves that traverse the intervening spacetime. There is no instantaneous spooky influence; there is only a retrocausal structure that makes the correlation appear non-local when viewed from within the forward time direction only.

iv. against the many-worlds alternative

Against MWI — TI is more parsimonious; MWI multiplies entities without explanatory gain; TI explains outcomes, not just statisticsThe many-worlds interpretation is often presented as the natural alternative to collapse theories: it avoids wave function collapse by allowing all branches to be real. But many-worlds purchases this resolution of the measurement problem at the cost of an enormous ontological proliferation: an uncountably infinite branching of actual worlds. The Transactional Interpretation achieves the same resolution — no collapse, no mystery about measurement — without this proliferation. It postulates only the waves and transactions that are needed to explain the observed outcomes. And, crucially, it explains why specific outcomes occur, not just the probability distribution over outcomes: the completed transaction selects the outcome. Many-worlds cannot explain why this branch rather than any other is the one we observe; it can only say that all branches are real.

Argument I — Discarding the advanced wave solution is arbitrary; both solutions should be taken as physical
1.The Schrödinger equation, like the classical wave equation, has both retarded (forward-in-time) and advanced (backward-in-time) solutions; standard QM discards the advanced solution by assumption.
2.The Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory provides a precedent for taking advanced electromagnetic waves seriously in classical physics; the same move is available in quantum mechanics.
3.Discarding the advanced solution on the grounds that backwards propagation is unphysical begs the question against retrocausality; the assumption should be evaluated on explanatory grounds, not stipulated.
∴The advanced wave solution should be retained; discarding it is an arbitrary convention motivated by the assumption of forward causation, not by any independent physical or mathematical requirement.
Argument II — The Transactional Interpretation dissolves the measurement problem without wave function collapse
1.In the TI, a quantum event is a completed transaction: an offer wave (retarded, from source) and a confirmation wave (advanced, from absorber) mutually reinforce each other at both ends of the interaction.
2.The definite outcome is determined by which transaction is completed; there is no collapse of a superposition because the completed transaction always selects exactly one outcome.
3.The measurement problem is dissolved without positing a special collapse mechanism, a special role for observers, or a many-branches ontology.
∴The TI dissolves the measurement problem through its retrocausal structure; wave function collapse is replaced by a completed transaction, which is both physically intuitive and ontologically parsimonious.
Argument III — EPR correlations are explained locally by retrocausal transactions; no action at a distance is required
1.EPR correlations appear to require non-local influences because viewing the interaction from within the forward time direction only makes it seem as if distant measurements affect each other instantaneously.
2.In the TI, the correlation is established by a four-dimensional spacetime transaction involving local interactions at both ends; from the Archimedean view, the interaction is entirely local.
3.No superluminal signalling is required or implied; the non-locality is an artifact of the forward-time perspective, not a feature of the underlying spacetime process.
∴The TI explains EPR correlations without non-locality or superluminal signalling; apparent non-locality is dissolved by recognising the retrocausal structure of the transaction in four-dimensional spacetime.
Argument IV — The TI is more parsimonious than many-worlds and explains specific outcomes, not merely statistics
1.Many-worlds avoids collapse but at the cost of uncountably infinite branching worlds; TI avoids collapse without this proliferation, requiring only the waves and transactions needed to explain actual outcomes.
2.Many-worlds cannot explain why any particular branch is the one experienced; TI explains why specific outcomes occur through the mechanism of transaction completion.
3.Parsimony and explanatory completeness both favour TI over many-worlds; retrocausality is the price, but it is a smaller price than ontological proliferation.
∴The TI is superior to many-worlds on parsimony grounds and superior on explanatory completeness; retrocausality is less costly than the ontological multiplication required by many-worlds.

My contribution to this debate is primarily logical and metaphysical rather than physical. I want to establish that backwards causation — the causation of earlier events by later ones — is conceptually coherent, and that the counterfactual analysis of causation, which I have defended at length, does not preclude it.

Causal asymmetry — the fact that causes precede effects — is a contingent feature of our world, not a conceptual necessity. It is grounded, on my account, in the asymmetry of counterfactual dependence: earlier events do not counterfactually depend on later ones in our world, but this is a nomological and thermodynamic fact, not a logical truth. In other possible worlds, counterfactual dependence could run backwards, and in those worlds, causation would run backwards too.

Counterfactual analysis and temporal direction.

On my counterfactual analysis, C causes E if and only if, had C not occurred, E would not have occurred (with appropriate conditions to avoid backtracking counterfactuals). In our world, this analysis yields forward causation because the asymmetry of overdetermination and the low-entropy initial conditions make it the case that the occurrence of a future event counterfactually depends on its immediate past causes but not on its remote past causes or on future events.

But this is a contingent, world-relative fact. In a world with different thermodynamics or different initial conditions, the counterfactual asymmetry could reverse, and with it the direction of causation. Backwards causation is possible; it is simply not actual.
i. the counterfactual analysis permits backwards causation

Counterfactual analysis — permits backwards causation in other possible worlds; causal asymmetry is nomological not conceptualThe key insight is that the counterfactual analysis of causation is neutral with respect to temporal direction. What makes causes precede effects in our world is not the analysis itself but the particular pattern of counterfactual dependence that obtains in our world, which is itself grounded in thermodynamic and initial-condition facts. In a possible world where the thermodynamic gradient runs the other way, or where there is no thermodynamic gradient at all, the pattern of counterfactual dependence could reverse, and with it the causal direction. The counterfactual analyst is therefore committed to the conceptual possibility of backwards causation, even if backwards causation is not nomologically possible in our world.

ii. the asymmetry of overdetermination

Overdetermination asymmetry — explains why backtracking is blocked in our world; different in worlds with backwards causationIn our world, the past is overdetermined by the present: there are vastly more independent traces of past events than there are anticipatory traces of future events. This asymmetry of overdetermination is what blocks backtracking counterfactuals: we evaluate “if C had not occurred, what would have been different?” by holding fixed the past and varying the future, because the past is overdetermined and the future is not. But this is a contingent feature of our world. In a world with the reversed thermodynamic gradient, the future would be overdetermined by the present, and the past would be the open, underdetermined domain. In that world, causal counterfactuals would naturally run backwards.

iii. against the logical impossibility charge

Not logically impossible — no self-defeating loops arise if causal history is consistent; closed timelike curves are locally coherentIt is sometimes claimed that backwards causation leads to logical paradox: the “grandfather paradox,” or the ability to signal one’s past self with contradictory instructions. But these paradoxes arise only when backwards causation is combined with the assumption that the agent has the ability to create genuine inconsistencies. On a consistent-histories approach, backwards causation is not paradoxical: the causal influences from the future to the past are always part of the consistent history that actually obtains. There is no loop that could be exploited to create contradictions because the full causal history, including the backwards influences, is self-consistent by stipulation. The logical impossibility charge conflates causal inconsistency with the possibility of agent interference.

iv. possible worlds with backwards causation

Possible worlds — backwards causation worlds are coherent; nomological possibility is a separate question from conceptual possibilityEven if we grant that backwards causation does not occur in our world, the conceptual possibility of worlds with backwards causation is well-established. Consider a world in which the laws of nature are the time-reverse of ours: in that world, every causal process runs backwards relative to our temporal ordering. Agents in that world would experience a reversed causal arrow and would describe their own situation as one in which causes precede effects — in their temporal direction. From the Archimedean view, both worlds are equally coherent. The asymmetry between forward and backward causation is therefore not conceptual but nomological: it is a feature of the laws and initial conditions of particular worlds, not of the concept of causation itself.

2.Forward causation in our world is explained by the particular pattern of counterfactual dependence that obtains here, grounded in thermodynamics and initial conditions, not by the analysis itself.
3.In possible worlds with different thermodynamics or initial conditions, counterfactual dependence could reverse, and with it the causal direction.
∴The counterfactual analysis permits backwards causation in possible worlds with different patterns of counterfactual dependence; causal asymmetry is a contingent, nomological feature of our world, not a conceptual necessity.
Argument II — The asymmetry of overdetermination explains forward causation in our world and could reverse in other worlds

2.This is a contingent thermodynamic fact. In a world with reversed thermodynamics, the future would be the overdetermined domain and the past the open one.
3.In that world, causal counterfactuals would run backwards and causal direction would be reversed; the asymmetry of overdetermination is not metaphysically necessary.

2.In a consistent-histories framework, backwards causal influences are always part of the self-consistent history that obtains; there is no paradoxical loop because the full history, including backwards influences, is coherent by construction.

Argument IV — Worlds with backwards causation are fully coherent; temporal direction of causation is world-relative

My argument in the 1964 paper was primarily conceptual: I aimed to show that the concept of backwards causation is coherent, not that backwards causation actually occurs. The subsequent literature has, I think, vindicated this more modest claim while going further toward showing that backwards causation may be not merely coherent but physically real.

The standard objection to backwards causation — that you cannot affect what has already happened — rests on a confusion. It conflates the epistemological claim that we cannot now change what we already know to have happened with the metaphysical claim that earlier events cannot be causally influenced by later ones. Once this confusion is dissolved, the concept of backwards causation is seen to be perfectly coherent, and the question becomes an empirical one: does it occur?

“The question whether effects can precede their causes is no more senseless than the question whether effects can follow their causes. It is a substantive question about the way the world is, not a conceptual truth about the meaning of ‘cause’.” — Dummett, 1964
i. disentangling the objection

The standard objection — confuses epistemic and causal senses of “cannot affect the past”; Dummett's classic dissolutionWhen we say we cannot affect the past, we typically mean one of two things. We may mean: we cannot change the past as it is known — we cannot make it the case that what we know to have happened did not happen. This is a trivially true epistemic claim. Or we may mean: no event that occurs later than an event E can be a cause of E. This is the substantive causal claim that retrocausality denies. The standard objection to backwards causation moves from the epistemic claim to the causal claim without justification. The epistemic claim is true; the causal claim is precisely what is at issue. The objection begs the question by assuming the causal claim in the formulation of the objection.

ii. causal concepts do not require temporal priority

Temporal priority not constitutive of causation — regularity and counterfactual accounts allow temporal direction as contingentWhat are the constitutive features of causation? On regularity accounts, causation is a matter of constant conjunction between event types; on counterfactual accounts, it is a matter of counterfactual dependence; on process accounts, it is a matter of continuous physical processes connecting cause and effect. None of these analyses requires, as a matter of conceptual necessity, that causes precede effects. Temporal priority has been included in some definitions of causation, but this inclusion is either stipulative — simply building in the asymmetry by definition — or it rests on the contingent empirical fact that, in our world, causes do precede effects. Neither move establishes that backwards causation is conceptually impossible.

iii. the bilking argument considered and rejected

Bilking argument — only shows backwards causation is undetectable if always avoidable; does not show it is impossibleThe bilking argument, associated with Black and others, holds that backwards causation would allow us to “bilk” the supposed cause: if E at time t2 is supposed to cause C at an earlier time t1, we can wait until t1 has passed, observe whether C occurred, and then arrange or refrain from bringing about E at t2. If C occurred, we refrain; if C did not occur, we bring about E. This seems to show that backwards causation is self-defeating. But the bilking argument shows only that backwards causation cannot be exploited for free manipulation of the past: if backwards causation obtains, there will be constraints on what we can bring about that prevent the bilking. The argument does not show that backwards causation is impossible; it shows that, if it obtains, there are correlations between our choices and earlier events that prevent exploitation. This is exactly what we should expect from a consistent retrocausal world.

iv. the semantic case for coherence

Semantic coherence — backwards causation claims are truth-apt; verificationism blocks the argument from unintelligibilityOne might argue that claims about backwards causation are not merely false but unintelligible: that we simply do not know what it would mean for a later event to cause an earlier one, and that the appearance of sense is illusory. But this argument requires a strong verificationist semantics that is independently very difficult to defend. The claim that C (at t2) caused E (at t1, where t1 < t2) is perfectly well-formed and truth-apt; we know what it says, and we know what evidence would bear on it. The fact that such claims are unfamiliar and perhaps not true in our world does not make them semantically defective. The argument from unintelligibility to impossibility requires verificationism, and verificationism is false.

Argument I — The standard objection confuses epistemic with causal impossibility; it begs the question
1.“We cannot affect the past” means either: (a) we cannot change what we know to have happened (trivially true, epistemic); or (b) no later event can cause an earlier one (the substantive causal claim retrocausality denies).
2.The standard objection moves from (a) to (b) without justification; this conflates the epistemic with the causal claim and begs the question against retrocausality.
3.Once the two senses are distinguished, the standard objection dissolves; (a) is compatible with retrocausality, and (b) is the claim at issue.
∴The standard objection to backwards causation rests on a confusion between epistemic and causal senses of “cannot affect”; once dissolved, the objection fails and backwards causation remains conceptually open.
Argument II — No standard analysis of causation requires temporal priority as a conceptual necessity
1.Regularity, counterfactual, and process accounts of causation do not build temporal priority into the analysis as a conceptual requirement; they can be satisfied by backwards causal relations.
2.Definitions of causation that include temporal priority are either stipulative or rest on contingent empirical facts about our world; neither establishes conceptual impossibility of backwards causation.
3.Temporal priority is therefore a contingent feature of causal relations in our world, not a constitutive feature of causation as such.
∴No standard analysis of causation conceptually requires temporal priority; backwards causation is fully coherent under regularity, counterfactual, and process accounts.
Argument III — The bilking argument shows only that retrocausal worlds are consistent, not that backwards causation is impossible
1.The bilking argument: if E (t2) causes C (t1, t1 < t2), we can observe C at t1 and manipulate E at t2 to prevent correlation. This seems self-defeating.
2.But in a retrocausal world, correlations between choices at t2 and events at t1 are themselves part of the causal history; the bilking attempt would itself be correlated with the earlier event in a way that prevents contradiction.
3.The bilking argument shows that retrocausal worlds are self-consistent (bilking is always frustrated), not that they are impossible.
∴The bilking argument does not establish the impossibility of backwards causation; it establishes only that retrocausal worlds are self-consistent — which is exactly what the retrocausalist claims.

3.Without verificationism, the semantic argument against backwards causation fails; backwards causation claims are well-formed, truth-apt, and subject to empirical investigation.

My argument focuses on the specific context that makes retrocausality most pressing: the interpretation of quantum non-locality. I want to show that retrocausal models are the most defensible response to the challenge posed by Bell’s theorem, and that the standard objections to them misfire.

Bell’s theorem establishes that no local hidden variable theory with purely forward-directed causal connections can reproduce the statistical predictions of quantum mechanics. This is one of the most important results in the foundations of physics. The question is: what should we conclude from it? The answer I defend is that we should permit causal connections to run backwards in time, allowing measurement settings to influence the values of hidden variables at the time of particle preparation.

The retrocausal hidden variable strategy.

In a standard Bell experiment, two particles are prepared in an entangled state and sent to spatially separated detectors where their properties are measured along chosen directions. The correlations between measurement outcomes violate Bell inequalities and cannot be reproduced by any local hidden variable model with forward causation only.

The retrocausal strategy: allow the choice of measurement settings at each detector to influence, backwards in time, the hidden variable values at the moment of particle preparation. The correlation between the hidden variables and the measurement settings violates the “statistical independence” assumption in Bell’s theorem, escaping the no-go result. Crucially, this does not permit superluminal signalling: the retrocausal influences are constrained to be consistent with the observed statistics, not to enable exploitation for communication.
i. retrocausality and the assumptions of Bell’s theorem

Bell's theorem — statistical independence assumption; retrocausal models violate it legitimately; not fine-tuned conspiracyBell’s theorem derives its conclusion from several assumptions: locality, realism, and statistical independence (the measurement settings are uncorrelated with the hidden variables). Retrocausal models violate statistical independence: if measurement settings can influence past hidden variable values, then the settings and hidden variables are correlated. Critics argue that this correlation is a fine-tuned conspiracy — a suspicious coincidence that must be explained. But this criticism misidentifies what requires explanation. “The asymmetry of causation is grounded in the fork asymmetry: in our world, correlations are systematically explained by common causes in the past, not by common effects in the future. i. the fork asymmetry explained

Fork asymmetry — open forks ubiquitous, closed forks rare; objective statistical asymmetry; grounds causal directionAn open fork is a structure in which two events, C1 and C2, are correlated because they share a common cause in the past: there is a prior event A such that A causes both C1 and C2, and the correlation between C1 and C2 is screened off by A. A closed fork is the time-reverse: two events, E1 and E2, have a common effect in the future, F, and the correlation between E1 and E2 (if any) is screened off by F. In our world, open forks are extremely common — almost all correlations between events that are not causally related are explained by common causes in the past. Closed forks, by contrast, are extremely rare: it is unusual for two independent events to converge on a single common effect that screens off their prior independence. This asymmetry — the fork asymmetry — is what grounds the direction of causation.

The statistical fact that open forks are common and closed forks are rare is an objective fact about the world that holds independently of any observer’s perspective. An agent with the reversed thermodynamic orientation would observe the same fork asymmetry, described in their own temporal terms: they would find that correlations are explained by prior common causes (in their temporal direction) rather than posterior common effects. The fork asymmetry is not perspectival; it is objective and world-level.

My process theory of causation holds that causation is constituted by the transmission of conserved physical quantities — energy, momentum, charge — along worldlines in spacetime. I want to argue that this account of causation is more defensible than the counterfactual and transactional alternatives, and that it provides principled grounds for rejecting retrocausality.

On my account, a causal process is a worldline along which a conserved quantity is transmitted; a causal interaction is an intersection of causal processes at which conserved quantities are exchanged. Causation is constituted by these physical processes and interactions. This account has the virtue of being directly grounded in the laws of conservation in physics, and it explains why causation has the structural features it does — including, I shall argue, its temporal direction.

The conserved quantity process theory.

A causal process: a worldline along which a conserved quantity (energy-momentum, charge, lepton number, etc.) is transmitted continuously. A causal interaction: an intersection of two or more worldlines at which conserved quantities are exchanged between the processes.

Causation consists in these processes and interactions; causal relations are grounded in the physical structure of the transmission. This is not a reductive analysis of causation in terms of counterfactuals; it is an account of what causation physically consists in. The theory has direct empirical content: causal claims are answerable to the physics of conserved quantities.
i. causal processes are worldlines of conserved quantity transmission

Conserved quantity process — causal processes are physical worldlines; not abstract counterfactual relations; directly grounded in physicsThe key advantage of the process theory over counterfactual and regularity accounts is that it grounds causation directly in the physics. This grounding gives the process theory a directness and concreteness that counterfactual accounts lack. It also provides a natural account of why causal processes are continuous — because conserved quantity transmission is continuous — and why causal processes can be traced through space and time as physical entities. The retrocausal account, by contrast, relies on abstract counterfactual or wave-function-based relations that lack this physical concreteness.

ii. the directionality of conserved quantity transmission

Transmission direction — conserved quantities transmitted along worldlines in forward temporal direction; thermodynamic arrow grounds this; retrocausal transmission is unobservedConserved quantity transmission along worldlines has a direction in spacetime. In our world, this direction coincides with the forward temporal direction: energy and momentum flow from past to future along causal worldlines. This directionality is grounded in the thermodynamic arrow and in the boundary conditions of the universe. Retrocausal processes would require conserved quantities to be transmitted along past-directed worldlines — in the direction of decreasing entropy — which is observationally undetected and thermodynamically extraordinary. The process theory does not rule out retrocausal transmission on purely conceptual grounds, but it requires that retrocausal processes be identified with specific physical worldlines along which conserved quantities flow backwards; no such worldlines have been found, and positing them ad hoc is theoretically costly.

iii. against the transactional interpretation

The offer wave in the TI is essentially the quantum wave function — a mathematical object defined over configuration space, not a physical process in spacetime. Attributing physical reality to the wave function and its advanced counterpart is a substantial and contested metaphysical commitment. The process theory requires that causal claims be answerable to the physics of actual physical processes, and the TI’s abstract waves do not meet this standard.

iv. the absences objection and backwards causation

Absences objection — retrocausal models typically involve negative causal claims (absences); process theory struggles with these; but forwards causation handles them betterA final consideration concerns the role of absences in retrocausal models. In a retrocausal Bell experiment, the measurement settings influence past hidden variables not through the transmission of conserved quantities but through the absence of certain settings — through what is not chosen rather than what is. This reliance on absences is problematic for any process theory: absences are not physical processes, and their causal role is difficult to account for on a conserved quantity approach. But this is a problem for any causal model that relies on what could have been but was not done; retrocausal models that depend on the absence of certain future interventions inherit this problem in a particularly acute form. Forward causal models, which typically involve positive physical processes, are better positioned to accommodate the process theory’s requirements.
