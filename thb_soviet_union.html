Mr President,

I want to make a methodological point before I make a historical one. The opposition will tell you that the Soviet Union must be judged in context — the encirclement, the war, the backwardness that existed before 1917. That is a legitimate framework. What is not legitimate is to apply that framework selectively, using context to explain away the worst, while crediting the best without contextual qualification. I am a historian of archives. I work with the documents that the Soviet state itself produced. And those documents tell a story that context does not excuse.

“The mass operations of 1937–38 were not a paranoid response to real threats. They were a planned, quota-based, industrialised killing programme in which regional NKVD chiefs were assigned numerical targets for execution and imprisonment and competed to overfulfil them. This is what the operational orders say. This is what the signed authorisation sheets say. The documents are unambiguous.”
i. the great terror: what the archives actually show

Great Terror — NKVD Order 00447, quota-based mass killingNKVD Order 00447, signed by Nikolai Yezhov on July 30, 1937 and approved by the Politburo, is one of the most important documents in the history of political violence. It assigned every region of the Soviet Union a quota: a first category of people to be shot, a second category to be sent to camp. The initial order specified 76,000 to be shot and 186,500 to be imprisoned. Regional chiefs — knowing their careers depended on zeal — almost immediately requested “limit increases.” The final toll of the mass operations of 1937–38 was at least 750,000 executions and approximately 1.3 million arrests, documented in the “Stalin lists” — execution orders bearing Stalin’s personal signature on lists he signed in batches, sometimes hundreds of names at a sitting. This was not paranoia, not war, not encirclement. It was peacetime. The primary victims were Soviet citizens, not foreign agents. The operational files are in the Russian State Archive. I have read them.

ii. the planning mechanism of terror

Operational planning — terror as bureaucratic production processWhat makes the Soviet terror historically distinctive is not its scale alone but its administrative character. Execution was managed as a production process. Regional NKVD offices submitted weekly operational statistics. Troikas — three-man panels operating outside any judicial framework — processed sentences at rates of hundreds per sitting. The Butovo firing range outside Moscow, where 20,761 people were shot in 1937–38 alone, operated on a nightly schedule. These were not the chaotic massacres of a revolutionary moment. They occurred eleven years after the revolution, in a stable administrative state, planned by a functioning bureaucracy, and recorded with extraordinary precision by the perpetrators themselves. The precision of the record is itself an indictment: a system that counts its executions to the digit has institutionalised killing as normal administrative practice.

iii. the opposition's contingency argument and what it requires

Contingency argument — what it requires one to believe about Leninist structuresThe opposition will argue that Stalinism was contingent — that a different leadership after Lenin would have produced a different outcome, and that we should not judge the Soviet project by its worst expression. I take this argument seriously. But it requires one to explain why the structural features that made the terror possible — the party’s monopoly on power, the absence of independent courts, the press under party control, the secret police with unlimited detention authority, the elimination of all organised opposition by 1921 — were not themselves the product of Bolshevik doctrine and Leninist practice. Stalin did not build those structures. He inherited them. The contingency argument must account for why a system with no institutional constraints on executive power reliably produces the worst expressions of that power when a leader willing to use it arrives.

iv. what the documents establish beyond revisionist reach

Archival certainty — what post-Soviet documents settle definitivelySome historical questions remain genuinely contested. The precise death toll of the Gulag, the relative weight of different factors in the 1932–33 famine, the exact nature of the conspiratorial networks alleged in the show trials — these are live scholarly debates. But post-Soviet archival access has settled certain questions definitively. The existence of quota-based mass executions is not contested. The scale of Gulag mortality — approximately 1.5–1.8 million documented deaths in camps between 1930 and 1953, with serious scholars estimating considerably higher excess mortality — is not contested. The personal involvement of Stalin, Molotov, Kaganovich, and Yezhov in approving execution lists is not contested. The opposition may dispute interpretations; they cannot dispute these facts without abandoning scholarship.

Thank you.

Argument I — NKVD Order 00447: quota-based execution as documented state policy
1.NKVD Order 00447 (July 30, 1937) assigned regional execution and imprisonment quotas; regional chiefs immediately requested “limit increases,” reflecting competitive institutional incentives for killing.
2.The mass operations of 1937–38 produced at least 750,000 documented executions and 1.3 million arrests; the “Stalin lists” bear his personal signature on execution orders for hundreds of names at a sitting.
3.These operations occurred in peacetime, eleven years after the revolution, targeting Soviet citizens; the operational files documenting this are in the Russian State Archive and have been published and verified by multiple independent researchers.
∴The Great Terror was a planned, documented, quota-driven killing programme authorised at the highest level of the Soviet state; it is not a contested allegation but an archivally established fact that cannot be contextualised away.
Argument II — Terror as bureaucratic production process: the administrative character of Soviet mass killing
1.Executions were managed via weekly operational statistics; troikas processed sentences at hundreds per sitting; the Butovo firing range executed 20,761 people in 1937–38 on a nightly operational schedule.
2.These were not revolutionary chaos but the output of a stable administrative state managing killing as a normal production process, recorded with documentary precision by the perpetrators themselves.
3.A system that counts its executions to the digit and manages them through regular bureaucratic reporting has institutionalised political killing as standard state practice, not as emergency exception.
∴The administrative character of Soviet terror — its planning, quotas, statistical management, and meticulous documentation — distinguishes it as a systemic feature of the state, not an aberration explainable by emergency conditions.
Argument III — The contingency argument requires explaining why Leninist structures reliably produced Stalinist outcomes
1.The structures enabling terror — party monopoly, no independent courts, controlled press, unlimited secret police detention authority, elimination of all organised opposition by 1921 — were products of Leninist doctrine, not Stalinist invention.
2.Stalin inherited these structures; the contingency argument must explain why a system with no institutional constraints on executive power reliably tends toward its worst expressions when an unconstrained leader arrives.
3.The historical record of Leninist regimes — Soviet Union, Maoist China, Cambodia, North Korea — suggests these structural features produce extreme violence with a consistency that exceeds what contingency explanations can accommodate.
∴The contingency argument, to succeed, must account for why Leninist institutional structures without exception created the preconditions for mass violence; the structural pattern is more parsimonious than the “bad leader” explanation.
Argument IV — Post-Soviet archival access: what is now definitively beyond scholarly dispute
1.Quota-based mass execution under Order 00447 is documented in the Russian State Archive and verified by multiple independent research teams; it is not a contested allegation.
2.Gulag mortality of approximately 1.5–1.8 million documented deaths 1930–1953, with credible scholarly estimates of higher excess mortality, is established in camp records now partially available.
3.Stalin’s, Molotov’s, Kaganovich’s, and Yezhov’s personal signatures on execution lists are primary documents; these facts are not interpretations subject to revisionist correction.
∴The opposition may contest interpretations and contextual judgements; they cannot, without abandoning scholarship, contest the archivally established facts of planned mass execution, Gulag mortality, and Politburo authorisation of both.

Mr President,

The opposition will argue that the Gulag was a peripheral horror, a Stalinist deviation from a Soviet project whose core achievements — industrialisation, literacy, women’s education, the defeat of fascism — remain real and admirable. I want to show that this framing is precisely wrong. The Gulag was not peripheral to the Soviet economic achievement. It was constitutive of it. The canals, the railways, the gold mines, the timber operations — the infrastructure the opposition cites as Soviet achievement — were built in substantial part by the forced labour of Soviet citizens. You cannot separate the achievement from the system that produced it and then credit only one.

The Gulag economy — what it produced At its peak in the early 1950s, the Gulag held approximately 2.5 million prisoners. Gulag enterprises produced: 40% of Soviet chrome, 33% of platinum, significant shares of Soviet gold and coal output. The White Sea–Baltic Canal (1931–33), built by 100,000 forced labourers, 12,000 of whom died during construction, was completed in twenty months and presented to the world as a socialist triumph. The Kolyma gold fields — in the far north-east, accessible only by sea — were developed entirely by Gulag labour. The BAM railway, the “Road of Bones,” was built across Siberia by Gulag prisoners who died in such numbers that their remains are still in the embankments.
i. the gulag as productive institution, not aberration

Gulag as economy — constitutive of Soviet industrial achievementThe OGPU’s 1929 memo recommending the expansion of the camp system is explicit about its economic rationale: camps should be used for “colonising remote territories and exploiting their natural wealth through the labour of those deprived of freedom.” This is not a rationalisation applied after the fact. It is the founding logic of the Gulag as an economic institution. The Five Year Plans — the centrepiece of what the opposition calls Soviet achievement — were premised on extracting labour from a captive workforce in conditions that would have been commercially impossible with free workers. The opposition wants to credit the Plan without crediting the mechanism. That is not how causal responsibility works.

ii. the eastern european export of the system

Eastern Europe — Soviet system exported by force after 1945The Soviet project was not confined to the USSR. After 1945, it was exported at gunpoint to 100 million Europeans who had not chosen it. In my book Iron Curtain, I document the systematic, deliberate destruction of civil society in Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, and the other satellite states — the elimination of political parties, the takeover of youth organisations, the creation of secret police forces modelled on and trained by the NKVD. Hungary 1956: Soviet tanks killed approximately 2,500 Hungarian civilians and wounded 20,000 who had risen against a Soviet-imposed government. Czechoslovakia 1968: 500,000 Warsaw Pact troops invaded a country whose citizens had not been consulted. These are not edge cases. They are the Soviet project maintaining itself by force over populations that rejected it when given any opportunity to do so.

iii. the women's liberation argument turned inside out

Women's liberation — what Soviet record actually shows on genderThe opposition frequently cites Soviet advances in women’s education and workforce participation as a genuine achievement. These gains were real. What is not said is that they came alongside the systematic destruction of the family as an institution — the collectivisation-era requisitioning that destroyed households, the purge-era mass widowing, the enforced labour participation that was not liberation but compulsion. Soviet women worked because the state needed their labour, not because the state respected their autonomy. The same system that gave women access to universities denied them access to contraception, criminalised abortion until 1955, and produced some of the highest abortion rates in the world as the only available birth control. Liberation achieved through compulsion is a different moral category from liberation achieved through rights.

iv. what ‘on balance’ requires

Balance sheet — what intellectual honesty requires when counting costsThe motion asks us to judge “on balance.” To do this honestly, one must count both sides of the ledger. The proposition counts: 750,000+ executions in 1937–38 alone; 1.5–1.8 million documented Gulag deaths; 3.5–5 million dead in the Holodomor; the destruction of Ukrainian, Chechen, Crimean Tatar, Volga German, and Korean communities through deportation; the crushing of Poland, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, and the Baltic states. The opposition counts: literacy campaigns, industrialisation, Sputnik, the defeat of fascism. These are genuine achievements. But a system that achieves literacy through the labour of the Gulag and defeats fascism at the cost of the Katyn massacre of 22,000 Polish officers — a system that achieves and destroys simultaneously — must be judged on both columns of its ledger, not only the one it chose to publicise.

Thank you.

Argument I — The Gulag was the productive mechanism of Soviet industrialisation, not a peripheral aberration
1.The OGPU’s 1929 founding memo explicitly articulated the camp system’s economic rationale: forced labour for “colonising remote territories and exploiting their natural wealth.”
2.Gulag enterprises produced 40% of Soviet chrome, 33% of platinum, and large shares of Soviet gold and coal; the White Sea Canal, BAM railway, and Kolyma goldfields were built entirely or substantially by forced labour.
3.The Five Year Plans were premised on extracting labour in conditions commercially impossible with free workers; crediting Soviet industrial achievement while disavowing the mechanism that produced it is a logical inconsistency.
∴Soviet industrial achievement cannot be separated from the Gulag system that produced it; the opposition’s attempt to claim the achievement while excising the mechanism that enabled it is not intellectually coherent.
Argument II — The Soviet project was exported by force to 100 million Europeans who rejected it when able
1.After 1945, the Soviet model was imposed on Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, East Germany, Romania, Bulgaria, and the Baltic states through systematic destruction of civil society and Soviet-backed coups documented in archival detail.
2.Hungary 1956: Soviet tanks killed ~2,500 civilians who rose against a Soviet-imposed government. Czechoslovakia 1968: 500,000 Warsaw Pact troops invaded. Both interventions reveal a project that could only maintain itself by force over populations given any choice.
3.A system that requires military invasion to prevent its own abandonment by subject populations is not one those populations endorsed; the Soviet project’s geographic reach is not evidence of its appeal but of its coercive capacity.
∴The Soviet Union’s maintenance of its Eastern European empire required repeated military suppression of populations that rejected the system; this is conclusive evidence that the “project” was experienced as disaster by those who lived under it without choice.
Argument III — Soviet women’s ‘liberation’ was compulsion; achievements came alongside systematic family destruction
1.Soviet women accessed education and workforce participation because the state needed their labour, not as an exercise of recognised autonomy; workforce participation was largely compulsory, not voluntary.
2.The same system criminalised abortion until 1955, produced some of the world’s highest abortion rates as the only available birth control, and systematically destroyed family structures through collectivisation, purges, and deportations.
3.Liberation through compulsion — access to education as a labour extraction mechanism — is a different moral category from liberation through rights; conflating them requires ignoring the coercive structure that delivered the outcome.
∴Soviet gender achievements were real but delivered through compulsion rather than recognised rights, and accompanied by systematic destruction of family autonomy; counting them as unambiguous achievements requires ignoring the mechanism and context that produced them.
Argument IV — An honest balance sheet must count the Gulag, Holodomor, and crushed nations alongside Sputnik
1.The proposition’s ledger includes: 750,000+ executions in 1937–38; 1.5–1.8 million documented Gulag deaths; 3.5–5 million Holodomor deaths; deportations of Chechens, Crimean Tatars, Volga Germans, Koreans; crushing of Hungary and Czechoslovakia.
2.The opposition’s ledger includes: literacy, industrialisation, Sputnik, the defeat of fascism — genuine achievements that cannot be denied.
3.“On balance” requires both columns; a system that achieves Sputnik through Gulag labour and defeats fascism while massacring 22,000 Polish officers at Katyn has both entries and must be judged on both.
∴An honest balance sheet cannot credit the Soviet Union’s achievements while excluding their costs and mechanisms; when both columns are counted, the designation “disaster” is not hyperbole but the most precise available summary.

Mr President,

I study the part of Europe that I call the Bloodlands — Poland, Ukraine, Belarus, the Baltic states, western Russia — the zone where Nazi and Soviet power overlapped and killed, between 1933 and 1945, approximately 14 million people who were not soldiers. Of those 14 million, between 5 and 7 million were killed by Soviet policy. I want to make three specific arguments about what the archives tell us, and about why the Holodomor in particular demands to be taken seriously as a category of deliberate mass killing, not merely a tragic famine.

“The Holodomor is sometimes presented as one of the great historical controversies. But if we follow the method that historians actually use — if we ask what the internal Soviet documents show about the intentions and knowledge of decision-makers — the controversy substantially resolves itself. The question is whether you are willing to read the documents.”
i. the holodomor: what the specific archival evidence shows

Holodomor — blacklisting, internal passport system, targeted Ukrainian measuresIn November and December 1932, the Soviet authorities took three measures specific to Ukraine and the Ukrainian-populated Kuban region of the North Caucasus that are not explicable as generic famine management. First: blacklisting, in which entire villages that failed to meet grain quotas had all commodities — not just grain but matches, salt, kerosene, and food — removed and no further deliveries permitted. Second: the internal passport system of December 1932, which required urban residents to carry passports for movement; crucially, peasants were excluded from receiving passports, trapping the rural population — the population dying of famine — in place. Third: a January 1933 directive specifically prohibiting Ukrainian and Kuban peasants from leaving for other regions, even as Russian peasants were permitted to seek food elsewhere. These three measures — blacklisting, internal passport exclusion, travel prohibition — were targeted specifically at the Ukrainian population and were implemented as people were already dying. They are not explicable as neutral economic policy.

ii. the knowledge question: what moscow knew and when

Knowledge — internal reports proving Soviet leadership knew of mass death in real timeThe opposition sometimes argues that the scale of famine mortality was unknown to Moscow, or that reports were suppressed by local officials. The internal OGPU reports now available in Ukrainian archives demolish this position. Monthly OGPU reports to Stalin in late 1932 and early 1933 describe, with bureaucratic precision, the scale of death by starvation in Ukrainian villages — naming specific raions, providing mortality estimates, describing scenes of cannibalism. Stalin received these reports. The Politburo, which had approved the grain quotas, the blacklisting, and the travel restrictions, received these reports. The decision to continue requisitions through the winter of 1932–33, knowing what the reports showed, is documented. Whether this meets the specific legal definition of genocide under the 1948 Convention — which requires proof of intent to destroy a group as such — remains a genuine scholarly debate. That it constitutes deliberate mass killing of a specific national group, knowingly continued after its consequences were understood, does not.

iii. the naming imperative: why aggregate numbers are morally insufficient

Individual victims — the naming imperative against aggregate abstractionI want to make a point about method that I believe is also a moral point. The opposition will deploy numbers — death tolls that are genuinely uncertain, counterfactuals about what tsarist Russia would have produced, aggregate indices of literacy and industrial output. I do not dispute that these numbers matter. But there is something that aggregate numbers alone cannot do: they cannot account for the individual. In Bloodlands I tried to give names to the dead — specific people in specific places on specific dates. Oleksa Borysenko, who kept a diary of the starvation that killed him. The school inspector in Kharkiv who reported that children were too weak to attend class in the winter of 1933. These are not rhetorical devices. They are the irreducible minimum of historical honesty. A system is not redeemed by its average; it must be judged by what it did to specific human beings. And what the Soviet system did to specific human beings in 1932–33, in 1937–38, and across the breadth of its history was catastrophic.

iv. the comparative context: what makes Soviet killing historically exceptional

Comparative framework — Soviet killing in European 20th century contextThe opposition will invoke the comparative argument: British India, the Belgian Congo, Jim Crow America. These comparisons are appropriate and the body count of European imperialism is real and underweighted in Western consciousness. But the comparison does not exculpate; it may indict both. What is distinctive about Soviet killing in my comparative framework is that it was directed primarily inward — at the Soviet Union’s own citizens, in peacetime, by a state claiming to act in their name. Colonial killing was directed outward, at subject populations defined as racially inferior and explicitly excluded from the moral community of the perpetrators. Soviet killing targeted people who were, by the regime’s own ideology, comrades — workers and peasants whose emancipation was the system’s stated purpose. This internal character makes the betrayal constitutive of the catastrophe in a way that distinguishes it from, without excusing, colonial violence.

Thank you.

Argument I — Holodomor: three specifically targeted measures prove deliberate policy, not generic famine
1.November–December 1932: blacklisting removed all commodities (not just grain) from quota-failing Ukrainian villages; the internal passport system excluded peasants, trapping the starving rural population in place.
2.January 1933 directive specifically prohibited Ukrainian and Kuban (Ukrainian-populated) peasants from leaving for food; Russian peasants were not subject to the same prohibition.
3.These three measures — blacklisting, internal passport exclusion, travel prohibition — were targeted specifically at Ukrainians while people were already dying; they are not explicable as ethnically neutral economic management.
∴The specifically Ukrainian targeting of these three measures, implemented as mass death was occurring, establishes that the Holodomor was deliberately prolonged and intensified by policy directed at a specific national population, not a tragic but neutral famine.
Argument II — The knowledge question: OGPU reports prove Moscow knew of mass death in real time
1.Monthly OGPU reports to Stalin in late 1932 and early 1933, now available in Ukrainian archives, describe with bureaucratic precision the scale of starvation deaths in Ukrainian villages, including specific raions and mortality estimates.
2.The Politburo, which approved grain quotas, blacklisting, and travel restrictions, received these reports and continued requisitions through winter 1932–33 after the consequences were documented.
3.The decision to continue lethal policy with documented knowledge of its consequences meets the threshold of deliberate mass killing regardless of whether it meets the specific legal standard of genocide under the 1948 Convention.
∴Moscow’s real-time knowledge of mass death, proven by internal OGPU reports, establishes that continued requisitions represented a decision to continue lethal policy with full knowledge; this is deliberate killing regardless of the precise legal category applied.
Argument III — The naming imperative: aggregate statistics are morally insufficient
1.The opposition’s method relies on aggregate comparisons — death tolls, literacy indices, industrial output — that dissolve individual victims into statistical abstractions.
2.Historical and moral honesty requires accounting for specific individuals: the Ukrainian diarists who recorded their own starvation, the school inspectors who reported classrooms emptied by famine death, the named victims whose experiences the aggregate erases.
3.A system is not redeemed by its average output; it must be judged by what it demonstrably did to specific human beings, and the Soviet system’s record with specific human beings is catastrophic across multiple time periods and regions.
∴The opposition’s aggregate-level defence cannot discharge the moral and historical obligation to account for individual victims; a method that can only defend a system at the aggregate level while its record at the individual level is catastrophic has not successfully defended it.
Argument IV — Soviet inward killing: a distinctive moral category within the comparative framework
1.Colonial violence was directed outward, at populations defined as racially inferior and excluded from the perpetrators’ moral community; Soviet killing was directed inward, at the regime’s own citizens in peacetime.
2.By the Soviet system’s own ideology, the victims were comrades — workers and peasants whose liberation was the system’s stated purpose; the betrayal is constitutive of the catastrophe in a way colonial violence is not.
3.The comparative argument indicts imperialism and Stalinism simultaneously; noting that colonial powers also killed does not exculpate the Soviet state, it compounds the global indictment.
∴Soviet mass killing of its own citizens in peacetime, directed at people the system claimed to represent, constitutes a distinctive category of political betrayal; the colonial comparison simultaneously indicts both, not one at the expense of the other.

Mr President,

I am not here to defend Stalinism. I have spent forty years of my scholarly life documenting the human cost of Stalinist terror, including through the testimony of survivors. What I am here to contest is a motion so imprecisely framed that it can only produce a misleading verdict. “The Soviet Union” encompasses 1917 and 1991, Lenin and Gorbachev, the terror of 1937 and the social welfare state of 1960, the collectivisation famine and the universal healthcare system. To call this entire historical entity a “disaster,” on balance, is not a historical judgement. It is a Cold War verdict in historical clothing. And it requires one to do what no serious historian should do: treat as inevitable an outcome that was, at every stage, contingent.

“Stalinism was not the inevitable expression of Bolshevism. It was a political outcome that required the physical elimination of the Bolshevik alternatives to Stalin. Nikolai Bukharin was not a minor figure — he was, as Lenin described him, the favourite of the whole party. To understand what the Soviet Union could have been, one must take seriously what it killed.”
i. the contingency argument: what bukharin’s path would have meant

Bukharin — NEP path as real historical alternative to StalinismNikolai Bukharin, until his arrest in 1937, represented a coherent and politically substantial alternative to Stalinist forced collectivisation. His programme — continuation of the New Economic Policy, gradual socialist transformation through market mechanisms, preservation of the peasant economy while developing state industry, accommodation of cultural pluralism within a socialist framework — was not a fantasy. It had majority support in the party at various points in the 1920s, and the economic evidence suggests it was viable. The Soviet agricultural sector under NEP was recovering; the market socialist mechanism was generating growth without coercion. Stalin’s collectivisation was not an economic necessity. It was a political choice that required the physical liquidation of Bukharin, Rykov, Tomsky, and the entire alternative leadership. The proposition asks us to judge a project by the outcome of its worst leader making its worst choices. That is not historical analysis. It is attribution by association.

ii. the social welfare achievement: what soviets themselves valued

Social welfare — what Soviet citizens valued and the 1991 poll evidenceIn 1991, as the Soviet Union dissolved, Soviet survey data showed that approximately 80% of citizens opposed the introduction of free-market capitalism and preferred to preserve the social guarantees the system provided: universal healthcare, free higher education, guaranteed employment, subsidised housing, cradle-to-grave welfare provision. These preferences were not manufactured by propaganda — they were the response of people who had lived with the system and understood what they were being asked to give up. What followed — the shock therapy of the 1990s, the collapse of life expectancy by five years in Russia between 1991 and 1994, the asset stripping that produced the oligarchs, the privatisation that transferred the Soviet economic inheritance to a handful of politically connected men — validated those concerns entirely. The people who lived under the Soviet system, when given a choice, did not experience it as an uncomplicated disaster.

iii. the cold war corruption of american sovietology

Sovietology — Cold War ideology distorting Western academic scholarshipIn Sovieticus, published in 1985, I documented something that the proposition would prefer to ignore: that American academic Sovietology during the Cold War was systematically distorted by its institutional relationship to the US national security state. The field was funded substantially by foundations with intelligence connections, its practitioners were expected to produce findings compatible with containment policy, and scholars who reached revisionist conclusions faced professional consequences. This is not a conspiracy theory. It is documented in the funding histories of the major Sovietological institutions and in the career trajectories of dissenters. The proposition draws on a body of scholarship that was produced in conditions structurally hostile to findings favourable to the Soviet Union. That does not make it wrong. But it is the necessary context for evaluating why the field’s consensus so consistently aligns with American Cold War strategic requirements.

iv. what ‘disaster’ requires us to ignore

Achievements — literacy, healthcare, women's education, industrialisation, Nazi defeatTo call the Soviet Union a disaster, on balance, requires dismissing as irrelevant: the elimination of mass illiteracy in a country where 70% of the population could not read in 1917; the construction of a universal healthcare system that achieved life expectancy convergence with Western Europe by the 1960s; the industrialisation that transformed Russia from a peasant economy into a major industrial power in two decades; the defeat of Nazi Germany at Stalingrad, Kursk, and Berlin — a defeat achieved at a cost of 27 million Soviet lives that saved Western Europe from a Nazi hegemony. These are not Stalinist propaganda. They are historical facts that any honest balance sheet must include. The proposition’s case requires treating these achievements as either irrelevant or as tainted by association with terror. I submit that this is precisely the kind of Cold War reasoning that a serious historical symposium should resist.

Thank you.

Argument I — Stalinism was contingent: Bukharin’s NEP path was a viable and politically substantial alternative
1.Bukharin’s programme — NEP continuation, market socialism, gradual transformation, cultural pluralism — had majority party support at various points in the 1920s; the Soviet agricultural sector was recovering under NEP without coercion.
2.Stalin’s forced collectivisation required the physical liquidation of Bukharin, Rykov, Tomsky, and the entire alternative Bolshevik leadership; it was a political choice, not an economic necessity.
3.Judging the Soviet project by the outcome of its worst leader making its worst choices, while ignoring the alternatives that leader physically eliminated, is attribution by association rather than historical analysis.
∴Stalinism was a contingent outcome requiring the elimination of viable alternatives; to call the entire Soviet project a disaster on the basis of Stalinist outcomes ignores the historical roads not taken and the violence required to close them off.
Argument II — 80% of Soviet citizens in 1991 opposed free-market capitalism and preferred Soviet social guarantees
1.1991 Soviet survey data showed approximately 80% of citizens opposing free-market capitalism and preferring to preserve Soviet social guarantees: universal healthcare, free education, guaranteed employment, subsidised housing.
2.The post-Soviet shock therapy validated these concerns: Russian life expectancy fell five years between 1991 and 1994; oligarchic asset-stripping transferred the Soviet economic inheritance to a handful of politically connected men.
3.The preferences of people who lived under the system — informed by direct experience, expressed in a moment of genuine choice — are relevant evidence about whether the system was experienced as an uncomplicated disaster.

3.Scholarship produced in conditions structurally hostile to favourable findings about the Soviet Union requires methodological scepticism about its conclusions, without those conclusions being automatically wrong.
∴The proposition’s scholarly authority derives substantially from a field whose institutional conditions systematically rewarded anti-Soviet conclusions; this does not refute those conclusions but requires that they be evaluated against this structural context.
Argument IV — The balance sheet: literacy, universal healthcare, industrial transformation, and the defeat of Nazism
1.The Soviet Union eliminated mass illiteracy (70% illiteracy rate in 1917), built universal healthcare achieving life expectancy convergence with Western Europe by the 1960s, and industrialised a peasant economy in two decades.
2.The Soviet Union bore the primary burden of defeating Nazi Germany, suffering 27 million deaths; without this contribution, Nazi hegemony over Europe was likely. This is a positive historical fact of the first order.
3.A verdict of “disaster, on balance” requires dismissing or discounting these achievements as irrelevant or tainted; the motion asks us to do exactly what Cold War historiography did — count only the negative column.
∴An honest balance requires both columns; when both are counted — including the defeat of Nazism, universal healthcare, and the elimination of mass illiteracy — the verdict of “disaster, on balance” cannot be sustained without ideological pre-commitment.

Mr President,

I want to make a purely methodological point before I make a historical one. The proposition’s case rests on a foundation. That foundation is Khrushchev’s 1956 Secret Speech — the address to the Twentieth Party Congress in which Nikita Khrushchev catalogued Stalin’s crimes and launched the de-Stalinisation era. Everything the proposition argues — the terror, the show trials, the cult of personality, the unjust executions — flows from a narrative whose primary source document is Khrushchev’s speech. And that speech, when subjected to the evidentiary standards any serious historian must apply, turns out to contain a remarkable number of claims that are demonstrably false. I documented sixty-one of them. Let me tell you about the method and about some of the most important examples.

Khrushchev’s Secret Speech — the evidential problem Khrushchev’s 1956 speech is the primary document of the anti-Stalin narrative. It was delivered without supporting evidence, without witnesses, without documents, and without opportunity for rebuttal. Its claims about specific events were not verified before delivery. In Khrushchev Lied (2011), Furr catalogued the speech’s specific factual claims against available archival sources and found that the overwhelming majority of verifiable claims are either demonstrably false, contradicted by the archival record, or unverifiable.
i. the specific false claims in the secret speech

Khrushchev Lied — 61 specific false claims documented against archival sourcesConsider three specific examples from my catalogue. First: Khrushchev claimed that Stalin invented the concept of “enemy of the people” — a phrase used to justify executions of loyal Bolsheviks. The phrase was in fact used by Lenin from 1917 and appears throughout early Bolshevik documents; it was not a Stalinist invention. Second: Khrushchev claimed that the case against Marshal Tukhachevsky — the executed Red Army commander — was fabricated by German intelligence using forged documents. The archival record, including Soviet and German documents examined by multiple researchers, does not support the fabrication narrative; Tukhachevsky’s own confession, unlike many terror-era confessions, contains specific operational detail consistent with genuine conspiracy. Third: Khrushchev claimed that Stalin ordered the Leningrad Affair fabrications personally and against his wishes; archival documents show Khrushchev himself signed documents approving executions he claimed Stalin alone ordered. I raise these not to rehabilitate Stalin but to make an evidentiary point: the primary document of the prosecution’s narrative is unreliable, and building a historical verdict on an unreliable primary document is bad scholarship.

ii. the moscow trials: what the trotsky papers at harvard actually show

Moscow Trials — Houghton Library Trotsky papers and contact evidenceThe orthodox narrative of the Moscow Trials — that they were pure fabrications in which loyal Bolsheviks confessed to invented conspiracies after torture — requires one to ignore the Trotsky papers held at Harvard’s Houghton Library. Trotsky donated his papers to Harvard with a restriction: the most sensitive material was sealed for many years after his death. When those sealed materials became available, researchers found correspondence between Trotsky and individuals inside the Soviet Union of a character that made it difficult to maintain the standard narrative that the alleged contacts between Trotsky and Soviet oppositionists were entirely invented. I am not claiming the Moscow Trials were fair proceedings. They demonstrably were not — confessions obtained under duress are not reliable evidence. I am claiming that the standard narrative — total fabrication, no underlying reality — is harder to sustain than the orthodox position acknowledges. The historiographical question is more open than the proposition suggests.

iii. the yezhov vs. stalin question and the terror’s attribution

Yezhov vs. Stalin — archival evidence that Yezhov exceeded and falsified his instructionsThe proposition treats the Great Terror as Stalin’s programmatic killing of the Soviet population. The archival picture is more complicated. Documents I have examined, including materials from the Russian State Archive, show that Yezhov — the NKVD chief during the mass operations — systematically falsified reports to Stalin, inflated threat assessments, and in multiple documented cases fabricated the evidence on which Stalin authorised operations. This does not exculpate Stalin: he authorised mass operations and signed execution lists. But it substantially complicates the picture of a monolithic system executing a coherent programme. There is evidence that Stalin was, in some documented cases, deceived about the scale and character of what Yezhov was doing, and that when he discovered the deception, Yezhov himself was arrested and executed. This is not exculpation; it is the more complex archival reality that the proposition’s clean narrative requires suppressing.

iv. the methodological challenge to the prosecution

Methodology — evidence standards and the duty to follow the documentsMy methodological challenge to the proposition is simple: follow the evidence wherever it leads. If the evidence shows that Khrushchev’s speech contains sixty-one demonstrably false claims, that must be incorporated into your assessment of everything built on that speech. If the Trotsky papers contain material complicating the total-fabrication narrative of the Moscow Trials, that must be incorporated. If Yezhov’s documented falsification of reports to Stalin complicates the picture of a monolithic terror programme, that must be incorporated. I do not claim the result of this exercise is Soviet innocence. I claim it is a more complicated picture than the proposition can accommodate. And in a serious historical debate, complexity is not an evasion. It is the obligation.

Mr President,

I am a philosopher, and my contribution to this debate is philosophical before it is historical. I want to ask a question that the proposition has not asked and cannot answer without abandoning the premises of its argument: by what standard is the Soviet Union being judged? If the answer is a universal moral standard — one applied consistently across all political systems and historical periods — then the Soviet Union deserves the scrutiny it receives, and I am prepared to accept the most damning findings of the historical record. But if the standard being applied is a selective one — one invoked against the Soviet Union and not against the empires, the racial states, and the colonial powers whose body count rivals or exceeds it — then the motion is not a historical verdict. It is a political prosecution.

“British India, from 1876 to 1908, experienced famines that killed between twelve and twenty-nine million people. Mike Davis, in Late Victorian Holocausts, documents that these famines occurred while India was exporting food to Britain, that they were exacerbated by deliberate policy decisions, and that the death toll was known to the colonial administration. No symposium has ever been convened to debate whether the British Empire was, on balance, a disaster. The asymmetry is not accidental. It is ideological.”
i. the colonial body count: a standard never applied

Colonial comparison — British India, Belgian Congo, asymmetric condemnationMike Davis’s Late Victorian Holocausts (2001) documents 12–29 million famine deaths in British India between 1876 and 1908 — deaths that occurred while India exported food to Britain, that were exacerbated by colonial policy decisions documented in the archival record, and that were known to the colonial administration in real time. Adam Hochschild’s King Leopold’s Ghost documents 10 million deaths in the Belgian Congo under Leopold II, alongside systematic mutilation as a labour discipline mechanism. The transatlantic slave trade killed an estimated 1.8 million in the Middle Passage alone; the plantation system killed millions more over three centuries. None of these systems — the British Empire, the Belgian Congo state, the American slave system — is routinely described as “a disaster” in the discourse of Western liberal institutions. The Soviet Union is. The asymmetry is not explained by body count. It is explained by Cold War ideological priorities.

ii. the siege context: what the 1918–21 allied intervention established

Allied intervention — 1918-21 siege context establishing permanent security paranoiaBetween 1918 and 1921, fourteen foreign powers — including Britain, France, the United States, Japan, and Czechoslovakia — militarily intervened in the Russian Civil War against the Bolshevik government. British forces occupied Archangel. American forces occupied Vladivostok. This intervention, which killed more than a million people and came close to destroying the Bolshevik state, established a genuine existential threat that permanently shaped Soviet security thinking. I am not using this to justify the terror of 1937. I am insisting that any honest account of why the Soviet state developed the institutional paranoia it did must begin with the documented fact of foreign military invasion in its infancy. A state that was invaded by fourteen foreign powers within two years of its founding, and that spent its entire subsequent existence under the threat of another such invasion — culminating in 1941 in the largest military invasion in human history — made security decisions in a context that no Western liberal democracy has ever faced.

iii. the anti-colonial achievement: what the soviet union did for the colonised world

Anti-colonial support — Soviet backing for liberation movements globallyThe Soviet Union was the first major power to criminalise anti-Semitism, in 1917. It was the first major state to legally recognise and support the independence of colonial peoples at a time when every Western liberal democracy either owned colonies or tacitly accepted colonial rule. It provided education, technical assistance, and military support to anti-colonial movements across Africa, Asia, and Latin America at a time when those movements had no other major power willing to back them against European imperialism. The dozens of Central Asian peoples who received mass literacy campaigns, universities, and political representation as Soviet republics — Uzbeks, Kazakhs, Kyrgyz, Turkmen — came from societies where virtually no women were literate in 1917. Whatever one says about the brutality of Soviet rule in Central Asia, the counterfactual is not Swedish social democracy. It is continued colonial subordination to the Russian Empire, or to British and Chinese encroachment. The proposition must engage with this counterfactual, not simply catalogue Soviet crimes against a background of imagined liberal alternatives.

iv. the selective invocation of universalism

Selective universalism — the double standard as ideological symptomThe proposition’s deepest problem is philosophical. It invokes universal moral standards — the sanctity of human life, freedom from state terror, the right to political dissent — to condemn the Soviet Union. I accept these standards. But their selective application — to the Soviet Union and not to the British Empire, to Stalin and not to Churchill who oversaw the 1943 Bengal famine killing 2–3 million people, to the Gulag and not to the prison-plantation system of the American South — reveals that the invocation is not of universalism but of a particular political tradition defending itself against its rival. That is not moral philosophy. It is faction dressed as principle. A genuine universalism would produce a list of disasters that includes both the Gulag and the Congo, both the Holodomor and the Bengal famine. That list would not single out the Soviet Union. It would indict the modern world.

Thank you.
