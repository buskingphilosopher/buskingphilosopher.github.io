Moral responsibility does not require the ability to have done otherwise. Frankfurt-style cases demonstrate that an agent can be fully responsible for an action even when an external factor would have ensured the same outcome had they wavered. Valuation, Motivation & Free AgencyGary Watson, “Free Agency” (1975)
Free agency consists in the alignment of an agent’s valuation system (what they judge genuinely good) with their motivational system (what actually moves them to act). Unfreedom is not the mere existence of causal determination but the fracture between these two systems — the condition of the addict or the compelled, whose actions are driven by desires they do not value. Chair,

The incompatibilist’s case rests on a principle so widely assumed that it has rarely been examined carefully. That principle is the Principle of Alternative Possibilities: a person is morally responsible for what they have done only if they could have done otherwise. I want to show that this principle is false, and that its falsity dissolves the most powerful form of the incompatibilist argument before it gets started.

The Frankfurt Case (canonical formulation). Jones is deliberating about whether to vote for candidate A. Black, a neuroscientist, has implanted a device in Jones’s brain that monitors his deliberation. If Jones shows any sign of deciding to vote for B, Black will intervene and cause Jones to vote for A. As it happens, Jones decides entirely on his own to vote for A. Black’s device never activates. Is Jones responsible for his vote?
i. the flicker of freedom objection and why it fails

Frankfurt cases — PAP is false; Jones is responsible despite being unable to do otherwise; the counterfactual intervener makes no difference to the actual causal historyThe obvious incompatibilist response is to say that Jones could still have done otherwise — that prior to his decision, before the device activated, there was a “flicker of freedom” in the moment of deliberation during which he could have wavered. But this response is confused. The question is not whether Jones had any alternative possibilities whatsoever; it is whether the alternative possibilities he had are the kind that ground moral responsibility. Even if we grant that Jones could have initiated a wavering, that wavering would have immediately been corrected by Black’s device. The only alternative open to him is an alternative that is immediately cancelled — a flicker, not a fork in the road. The incompatibilist who relies on this flicker is in the embarrassing position of grounding moral responsibility in a causal process that makes no difference to the outcome and of which the agent is not even aware. If that infinitesimal and causally inert flicker is what grounds responsibility, responsibility is grounded in almost nothing.

ii. what actually grounds responsibility: the hierarchical will

Hierarchical will — freedom requires that effective first-order desires be endorsed by second-order volitions; this structural condition is what PAP misses and what determinism leaves intactMy positive account identifies what actually grounds moral responsibility: the structure of the will. A person acts freely when their action flows from a first-order desire that is endorsed by their second-order volition — when they want to want what they want. The contrast case is the unwilling addict: someone who is moved by a desire for the drug while also having a second-order volition not to be moved by that desire. Their will is, as I put it, split against itself. Their action does not express who they are at the level of what they care about. The willing addict presents a different case: someone who embraces their addiction, who identifies at the higher-order level with the desire that moves them. We may disapprove of their condition, but their action expresses their will in the relevant sense. Neither of these cases turns on whether determinism is true. The structure of the will is exactly the same whether or not the causal history of the desires is determined.

iii. the manipulation objection and the concept of identification

Manipulation — the worry that manipulation cases collapse mesh theories; Frankfurt's reply: what matters is whether the agent identifies with their desires at the time of action, not their causal historyThe opposition will press what is now called the manipulation objection: if a neuroscientist directly implants the higher-order volitions as well as the first-order desires, does the agent still act freely? The answer depends on a careful analysis of what identification consists in. My account does not say that freedom requires that desires have a particular causal history; it says that freedom requires that the agent, at the time of action, identify with the desire that moves them — that the desire is truly their own in the sense that they endorse it on reflection, that it coheres with what they care about. Whether or not it was implanted is a separate question from whether it is now genuinely the agent’s own. I acknowledge that the concept of identification is difficult, but its difficulty does not vindicate the incompatibilist’s alternative, which is to require uncaused origination — a condition nothing in nature can satisfy and that would, if satisfied, make actions random rather than responsible.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Frankfurt cases refute the Principle of Alternative Possibilities
1.The Principle of Alternative Possibilities (PAP): a person is morally responsible only if they could have done otherwise. This is the standard incompatibilist premise linking responsibility to alternative possibilities.
2.In a Frankfurt case, Jones votes for A entirely on his own; Black’s device never activates. Jones could not have done otherwise (Black would have intervened). Yet Jones is intuitively fully responsible for his vote.
3.The “flicker of freedom” response fails: any marginal alternative available to Jones is causally inert — immediately cancelled, unknown to Jones, and making no difference to the actual outcome. Grounding responsibility in such a flicker is both implausible and insufficient.
∴PAP is false: an agent can be fully morally responsible for an action even when no genuine alternative was available. The incompatibilist inference from the absence of alternatives to the absence of responsibility fails at its first premise.
Argument II — Hierarchical mesh theory: freedom requires endorsement of desires by higher-order volitions, not alternative possibilities
1.The unwilling addict is moved by a desire they do not endorse at the second-order level: they want not to want what they want. Their action does not express their deeper self.
2.The willing addict endorses their desire at the second-order level: their action expresses who they are at the level of what they genuinely care about. The structure of their will is coherent.
3.This distinction — between coherent and fractured wills — is exactly the distinction that grounds attributions of freedom and responsibility. It makes no reference to determinism and is entirely compatible with a determined causal history of desires.
∴The correct criterion for free action is hierarchical mesh: the agent’s effective desires are endorsed by their higher-order volitions. This criterion is compatible with determinism and correctly distinguishes free from compelled action.
Argument III — The manipulation objection does not vindicate incompatibilism; identification is what matters, not causal history
1.The manipulation objection: if both first- and second-order desires are implanted, the agent still seems unfree. This suggests causal history, not mesh, is what matters.
2.Reply: what matters for freedom is whether the agent currently identifies with the desire — whether it coheres with what they care about on reflection, not how it was caused. A desire can be implanted and then genuinely become one’s own.
3.The incompatibilist alternative — requiring uncaused origination — is worse: nothing in nature satisfies it, and if it were satisfied, the action would be random, not free. Identification is the better criterion even if imperfect.
∴Identification with one’s desires, not their causal history, is the criterion for freedom; the manipulation objection pressures mesh theories but does not vindicate incompatibilism, whose alternative condition of uncaused origination is both unsatisfiable and incoherent.

Chair,

I want to propose a way of thinking about this question that is quite different from most of what has been said on both sides. Both the compatibilist who argues that determinism is irrelevant because freedom is about the right kind of causation, and the incompatibilist who argues that determinism eliminates the metaphysical preconditions for responsibility, are engaged in the same mistake: they are treating moral responsibility as primarily a metaphysical question, to be resolved by analysis of causation and possibility. I want to suggest that it is primarily a human question — a question about the kind of beings we are and the kind of lives we live together.

“What I want to contrast is the attitude (or range of attitudes) of involvement or participation in a human relationship, on the one hand, with what I might call the objective attitude (or range of attitudes) to another human being, on the other.”
i. the reactive attitudes and the participant stance

Reactive attitudes — resentment, gratitude, indignation, love are constitutive of interpersonal life; they are responses to the quality of another's will, not to metaphysical properties of causationConsider the range of what I call reactive attitudes: resentment, gratitude, indignation, moral disapprobation, love, hurt feelings. These are not detached intellectual assessments of causation. They are responses to the quality of will that another person manifests in their dealings with us — to whether they showed goodwill, malice, indifference, care, contempt, or respect. When someone treads on my hand, my resentment or forgiveness depends on whether they did so deliberately, carelessly, or accidentally — on what their action expressed about their regard for me. These attitudes are not optional add-ons to interpersonal life; they constitute it. To live in the participant stance — to be in genuine relationship with other people — just is to be susceptible to these attitudes in response to others’ expressions of will.

ii. the objective stance and its limits

Objective stance — treating someone as an object of management rather than a subject of relationship is the real withdrawal of moral community; determinism cannot mandate this universallyThe contrast is with what I call the objective stance: the stance we take toward someone we regard as an object of management rather than a participant in relationship. We take the objective stance toward someone who is severely mentally ill, toward a very young child, toward someone in the grip of a compulsion they cannot control. We do not resent the obsessive-compulsive for their rituals; we try to understand and manage the condition. The objective stance involves a suspension of the reactive attitudes and a withdrawal from the participant relationship. Now, the incompatibilist’s position implies that if determinism is true, we ought to take the objective stance toward everyone, always — because everyone’s actions are, on the determinist picture, the causal outcome of prior states they did not control. But this implication is both psychologically impossible and humanly catastrophic. We cannot and should not treat each other as objects of management rather than subjects of relationship. The reactive attitudes are not a mistake we can be argued out of; they are the substance of human life.

iii. the irrelevance of the metaphysical question

Metaphysical irrelevance — the truth of determinism is simply irrelevant to the appropriateness of reactive attitudes; these are not grounded in metaphysics but in the participant stance itselfHere is the key claim: the appropriateness of the reactive attitudes does not depend on the truth of any metaphysical thesis about causation. We suspend the reactive attitudes not because we discover that someone’s action was causally determined, but because we discover something about the quality of their will — that it was distorted by compulsion, illness, or extreme deprivation. The metaphysical fact that everything is causally determined, if true, is simply not the kind of fact that licenses the universal adoption of the objective stance. It is not a discovery about the quality of anyone’s will. The question “is this person’s action causally determined?” is not the question we ask when we decide whether to resent or forgive. The question we ask is: “what did this action express about their regard for me?” Those are different questions, and the truth of determinism answers only the first.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Reactive attitudes constitute interpersonal life; they are responses to quality of will, not to metaphysical properties
1.Reactive attitudes (resentment, gratitude, indignation, love) are responses to the quality of will another person expresses — whether they showed goodwill, malice, or indifference. They are not assessments of causal history.
2.These attitudes are constitutive of the participant stance — of being in genuine human relationship. To live in relationship with others just is to be susceptible to these responses to their expressions of will.
3.What makes an action an appropriate target of resentment or gratitude is the quality of will it expresses, not whether it was causally determined. Determination is simply not the relevant property.
∴The reactive attitudes are grounded in the quality of will expressed in actions, not in the metaphysical causal history of those actions; the truth of determinism is therefore irrelevant to whether they are appropriate.
Argument II — Universal adoption of the objective stance is both impossible and humanly catastrophic
1.The objective stance — treating a person as an object of management rather than a subject of relationship — involves suspending reactive attitudes. It is appropriate for the severely ill, the very young, and the compelled.
2.The incompatibilist argument implies that if determinism is true, the objective stance is universally appropriate — that no one, ever, deserves reactive attitudes because all actions are causally determined.
3.Universal adoption of the objective stance is both psychologically impossible (we cannot genuinely suspend the reactive attitudes toward everyone) and morally catastrophic (it would destroy the fabric of human relationship).
∴The incompatibilist conclusion that determinism mandates the objective stance toward everyone is both psychologically and humanly unacceptable; the reactive attitudes cannot and should not be abandoned on metaphysical grounds.
Argument III — The metaphysical question of determinism is simply irrelevant to the conditions for reactive attitudes
1.We suspend reactive attitudes when we discover that someone’s will was distorted — by illness, compulsion, or severe deprivation. These are discoveries about quality of will, not causal structure.
2.The discovery that an action was causally determined is not a discovery about the quality of the agent’s will; it is a discovery about the metaphysics of causation. It belongs to a different category of fact entirely.
3.Since reactive attitudes are grounded in quality of will, and causal determination is not a property of quality of will, the truth of determinism cannot by itself make reactive attitudes inappropriate.
∴The truth or falsity of determinism is simply irrelevant to the appropriateness of reactive attitudes; the incompatibilist error is to treat a metaphysical thesis as if it were a fact about quality of will, which it is not.

My position is called semicompatibilism, and I want to explain why the “semi” is important. I am prepared to grant the incompatibilist something significant: I am prepared to grant that determinism may indeed be incompatible with the freedom to do otherwise — with what I call regulative control. Van Inwagen’s Consequence Argument is a serious argument, and I do not claim to have a decisive refutation of it. What I want to show is that this concession costs the incompatibilist nothing, because moral responsibility does not require regulative control. It requires only guidance control.

i. the distinction between guidance and regulative control

Guidance vs. regulative control — only guidance control (action from reasons-responsive mechanism) is required for responsibility; regulative control (ability to do otherwise) is not necessaryRegulative control is what the classical compatibilist defends and what the incompatibilist attacks: the ability to do otherwise, to select from among genuinely open alternatives. Frankfurt’s cases have already shown that this is not required for responsibility. But my positive claim goes further: what is required is guidance control — the agent’s action issuing from a mechanism that is the agent’s own and that is reasons-responsive. The mechanism need not be able to select among alternatives; it must be able to recognise reasons for acting differently and to respond to them in a suitable range of circumstances. An agent who acts from such a mechanism acts freely even if, given the actual circumstances, there was no alternative available.

ii. reasons-responsiveness and its two components

Reasons-responsiveness — requires both reasons-recognition (the mechanism can recognise sufficient reasons) and reasons-reactivity (it responds to those reasons); weak reasons-responsiveness is sufficient for responsibilityReasons-responsiveness has two components. Reasons-recognition: in some possible world with a sufficient reason to act otherwise, the mechanism would recognise that reason. Reasons-reactivity: in some possible world with a sufficient reason to act otherwise, the mechanism would actually respond by acting otherwise. I distinguish between strong reasons-responsiveness, which requires that the mechanism respond to every sufficient reason, and weak reasons-responsiveness, which requires only that it respond to some sufficient reason in some relevant possible world. Weak reasons-responsiveness is what is needed for responsibility. An agent who has never had a genuine reason to act otherwise in any possible world accessible to them is not an agent at all — they are a mechanism that processes inputs without genuine responsiveness. But this condition of genuine reasons-responsiveness is entirely compatible with determinism: a determined mechanism can be richly reasons-responsive.

iii. the own-mechanism condition and manipulation

Own mechanism — guidance control requires the mechanism be the agent's own: appropriately self-expressed and self-endorsed; this handles manipulation cases without requiring indeterminismGuidance control requires not merely that the mechanism be reasons-responsive but that it be the agent’s own mechanism. This is the condition that handles manipulation cases. A mechanism implanted by a neuroscientist five minutes ago, before the agent has had any opportunity to reflect on or endorse it, is not yet the agent’s own mechanism, even if it is reasons-responsive. A mechanism that has developed through the agent’s own history of deliberation, reflection, and self-constitution is their own, even if it is causally determined. Ownership of a mechanism is not a matter of its causal history being uncaused; it is a matter of the mechanism’s standing in the right relation to the agent’s own reflective self. The manipulation objection therefore does not show that we need indeterminism; it shows that we need a richer account of what makes a mechanism the agent’s own.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Semicompatibilism: moral responsibility requires guidance control, not regulative control
1.Regulative control (the ability to do otherwise) may be incompatible with determinism — this concession to van Inwagen’s Consequence Argument is granted for the sake of argument.
2.Frankfurt cases establish that moral responsibility does not require regulative control: Jones is responsible for his vote despite being unable to do otherwise.
3.Guidance control — the agent’s action issuing from their own reasons-responsive mechanism — is necessary and sufficient for moral responsibility. It does not require alternative possibilities.
∴Even granting incompatibilism about regulative control, moral responsibility is compatible with determinism because it requires only guidance control, which determinism does not threaten.
Argument II — Reasons-responsiveness: determined mechanisms can be richly responsive to reasons
1.A mechanism is reasons-responsive if, in some relevant possible world with sufficient reason to act otherwise, it would recognise that reason (reasons-recognition) and act on it (reasons-reactivity).
2.Weak reasons-responsiveness — responding to some sufficient reason in some relevant possible world — is sufficient for moral responsibility. It distinguishes agents from mere mechanisms.
3.A causally determined mechanism can be weakly reasons-responsive: determinism does not require that the mechanism be blind to reasons, only that its responses to reasons are themselves causally determined. Rich reasons-responsiveness is compatible with determination.
∴Determined mechanisms can satisfy the reasons-responsiveness condition for moral responsibility; determinism does not eliminate sensitivity to reasons, it merely determines how that sensitivity plays out; guidance control is therefore compatible with determinism.
Argument III — The own-mechanism condition handles manipulation without requiring indeterminism
1.A recently implanted mechanism is not the agent’s own even if reasons-responsive, because it has not been integrated into the agent’s history of self-constitution through deliberation and reflection.
2.A mechanism developed through the agent’s own reflective history is their own, even if causally determined. Ownership is a matter of the mechanism’s relation to the agent’s reflective self, not of its causal indetermination.
3.Manipulation cases show we need a richer account of mechanism-ownership; they do not show we need indeterminism.
∴The own-mechanism condition adequately handles manipulation cases within a compatibilist framework; the lesson of manipulation arguments is that ownership of mechanisms matters, not that indeterminism is required.

I want to offer a picture of free agency that takes seriously the phenomenology of unfreedom — the experience of being driven by impulses one does not endorse — without locating freedom in the absence of causal determination. My claim is that the distinction between free and unfree action is a distinction within the space of causally determined action, not between determined and undetermined action.

i. the valuation system and the motivational system

Two systems — the valuation system (what one judges good) and the motivational system (what actually moves one) can come apart; unfreedom is this fracture, not causal determinationEvery agent has at least two systems that bear on action. The valuation system is the system of evaluative judgements: what the agent takes to be good, worthwhile, desirable all things considered. The motivational system is the system of desires and impulses that actually move the agent to act. In the normal case, these systems are aligned: what one judges good is also what one wants, and what one wants is what one acts on. In cases of compulsion, addiction, and weakness of will, these systems come apart. The addict may judge that using the drug is bad, all things considered, and yet be overwhelmed by the desire for it. The phobic may judge that there is nothing to fear on the aeroplane and yet be consumed by terror. In these cases, the agent’s action does not express their evaluation of what is good. It expresses a motivational force that has, as it were, broken free from their evaluative governance. This is unfreedom: not determination, but the fracture of the two systems.

ii. why determinism is irrelevant to this distinction

Determinism irrelevant — the valuation/motivation fracture is a distinction within causal determination, not between determined and undetermined action; determinism cannot threaten free agency so understoodThe crucial point is that this distinction between free and compelled action is entirely internal to the space of causally determined action. The free agent whose valuation and motivation systems are aligned acts deterministically: their evaluative judgements cause their motivation which causes their action. The compelled agent whose systems are fractured also acts deterministically: their addictive craving causes their action despite their contrary evaluation. Both are determined. What distinguishes them is the internal structure of the causal process, not its presence or absence. Determinism as such is irrelevant: it does not create the fracture between valuation and motivation, and its absence would not repair it. The incompatibilist who says that free agency requires the absence of determination has simply changed the subject. What grounds our intuitions about compulsion and freedom is the internal structure of the agent’s motivational psychology, not the metaphysical question of whether any of it was determined.

iii. the connection to moral responsibility

Responsibility grounded in agency structure — we excuse those whose motivational systems overwhelmed their valuation systems; this explains our practices without invoking indeterminismThis picture explains our actual practices of excuse and attribution. We excuse the addict, the compelled, and the severely phobic not because we discover that their actions were determined but because we discover that their motivational systems overwhelmed their capacity for evaluative governance. We hold responsible the agent who acts from aligned systems — who wanted what they judged good and acted accordingly — not because their action was undetermined but because it expressed their evaluative agency. Our practices of responsibility are sensitive to the structure of agency, not to the metaphysics of causation. This is both the correct description of our practices and the correct normative account of what they should be sensitive to.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Free agency requires alignment of valuation and motivation systems; unfreedom is their fracture, not causal determination
1.The valuation system is what an agent judges to be good all things considered; the motivational system is what actually moves them to act. These are distinct systems that can be aligned or fractured.
2.In compulsion, addiction, and weakness of will, the motivational system overwhelms the valuation system: the agent acts against their own evaluative judgement. This fracture is the phenomenological core of unfreedom.
3.Free agency consists in the integration of these systems: acting from motivation that is in alignment with one’s evaluative judgements. This is entirely compatible with causal determination of both systems.
∴The correct account of free agency is the alignment of valuation and motivation systems; unfreedom is their fracture; causal determination is irrelevant to whether this fracture obtains.
Argument II — The valuation/motivation distinction is internal to causal determination; determinism cannot threaten it
1.Both the free agent (aligned systems) and the compelled agent (fractured systems) act deterministically. The difference between them is the internal structure of the causal process, not whether it is determined.
2.Determinism does not create or threaten the alignment of valuation and motivation systems; it merely specifies that whatever alignment or fracture obtains was causally necessitated. The structure can be determined and still be the relevant structure.
3.The incompatibilist who requires indetermination has changed the subject: our intuitions about freedom are grounded in the internal structure of agency, not the metaphysical presence or absence of causal determination.
∴Causal determination is irrelevant to the freedom-relevant distinction between aligned and fractured agency; the incompatibilist requirement of indetermination responds to the wrong property.
Argument III — Our excuse practices track agency structure, not metaphysical indetermination
1.We excuse the addict and the compelled because their motivational systems overwhelmed their evaluative governance — a fact about the structure of their agency, not about whether their action was determined.
2.We hold responsible agents whose actions express their evaluative judgements — again, a fact about agency structure.
3.Our practices are therefore tracking the correct property (agency structure) and are compatible with determinism, since agency structure is compatible with determination.
∴Our practices of excuse and responsibility are correctly sensitive to agency structure and are fully compatible with determinism; no metaphysical condition of indetermination is needed to underwrite them.

Chair,

Let me be direct about something that I think gets lost in the technical debates. The incompatibilists are in the grip of an image — a picture of what “real” free will would look like if we had it. It is the image of an uncaused self, originating its choices from nowhere, standing outside the causal order and selecting freely among genuinely open futures. I want to argue that this image is not merely unattainable: it is incoherent, and the free will it describes would not be worth wanting even if we could have it.

i. the free will worth wanting

Free will worth wanting — the incompatibilist's libertarian free will would, if coherent, make actions random rather than responsible; what we actually want and need is reasons-responsiveness and self-controlWhat do we actually want when we want free will? We want to be able to deliberate and have our deliberations make a difference. We want our actions to flow from our own reasoning, our own values, our own character, rather than from external compulsion or internal pathology. We want to be responsive to reasons: to be the kind of being who can be argued with, persuaded, educated, reformed. We want to be the authors of our lives in the sense of being genuinely in charge of them. None of these things requires indetermination. In fact, indetermination would undermine most of them. If my decision to keep a promise were indeterministic — if it were partially a matter of quantum randomness — it would be less mine, not more. The shot of randomness at the neural level adds nothing to my authorship. It subtracts from it, by introducing a factor that is not me at all.

ii. the manipulation of intuitions by thought experiments

Thought experiment bias — incompatibilist intuitions are generated by systematically misleading scenarios that suppress relevant features; our actual practices are the more reliable guideThe incompatibilist generates their intuitions with thought experiments: the replay argument, the Consequence Argument, the manipulation case. I want to raise a methodological worry about these experiments. They are designed to isolate the causal-deterministic feature and ask whether it alone is enough to undermine responsibility. But they do this by suppressing all the features that normally accompany determined action and that normally ground our attributions of responsibility: the agent’s history of deliberation, their character, their reasons-responsiveness, their self-control. The thought experiments work by stripping away everything that matters and then asking whether what is left is enough. Unsurprisingly, it is not. But this is a feature of the thought experiments, not of determinism. Our actual practices of responsibility are tracking all the relevant features together. They are a more reliable guide to what grounds responsibility than carefully constructed scenarios designed to elicit the intuition that nothing grounds it.

iii. the evolutionary naturalist account

Evolutionary account — free agency is a real, evolved capacity for self-control, anticipation, and avoidability; it emerged from natural selection and is fully explicable by science without invoking indeterminismMy positive account is evolutionary and naturalistic. Free agency is not a metaphysical mystery; it is an evolved biological capacity. Natural selection has produced creatures who can model their own future actions, anticipate consequences, evaluate alternatives, and modify their behaviour in light of those evaluations. This capacity for self-control and long-range planning — what I call “real” avoidability, the capacity to avoid bad outcomes by anticipating and adjusting — is the core of what makes us free agents. It is entirely explicable in evolutionary terms and entirely compatible with determinism. The incompatibilist who demands something more is demanding something that natural selection could not have produced, something that science cannot explain, and something that, if coherent, would make actions random. The free will worth having is the kind evolution gave us.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Libertarian free will is not worth wanting; indetermination would undermine authorship, not enable it
1.What we actually want from free will: deliberations that make a difference, actions flowing from our reasoning and character, responsiveness to reasons, genuine authorship of our lives.
2.Indetermination does not supply these goods. A decision that is partly a matter of quantum randomness is less attributable to the agent, not more; the random component is not the agent.
3.The incompatibilist demands indetermination on the assumption that it adds authorship; in fact it subtracts from authorship by introducing a causal factor that is not the agent at all.
∴The libertarian free will the incompatibilist demands is not worth wanting: if coherent, it makes actions partially random and thereby undermines rather than enables genuine authorship. The free will worth wanting is compatible with determinism.
Argument II — Incompatibilist thought experiments generate unreliable intuitions by suppressing all responsibility-grounding features
1.Incompatibilist thought experiments (rollback, manipulation, Consequence Argument) isolate the causal-deterministic feature by suppressing everything else that normally grounds responsibility: deliberation, character, reasons-responsiveness, self-control.

Argument I — The Basic Argument: true responsibility requires being causa sui; nothing can satisfy this; true responsibility is impossible
1.You act because of how you are: your character, values, and reasoning dispositions are the proximate mental causes of your action. For true responsibility, you must be responsible for these mental states.
2.But those mental states are the product of prior states and prior experience. For you to be responsible for them, you must be responsible for those prior states. This regress cannot terminate in any act of self-creation that was not itself caused by prior states.
3.True responsibility would require being causa sui — the cause of one’s own character. This is impossible for any finite being. Therefore true moral responsibility is impossible. This conclusion is independent of determinism: indeterminism adds randomness, not self-creation.
∴True moral responsibility is impossible: the regress of self-making cannot terminate; no being can be the ultimate cause of the character that causes its actions; this applies in both determinist and indeterminist worlds.
Argument II — Denying true responsibility is compatible with much that we value in moral life, properly reoriented
1.The catastrophe objection: abandoning true responsibility would destroy reactive attitudes and the fabric of interpersonal life. But what follows from the Basic Argument is more specific: it undermines basic desert, not all reactive attitudes.
2.Forward-looking practices (praise, encouragement, reform-directed censure) do not require true desert; they require only that behaviour be modifiable by these interventions. This condition can be met without true responsibility.
3.What must be relinquished is the satisfaction of ultimate retribution — the thought that someone truly deserves to suffer as the ultimate originator of their evil. This is a gain in moral clarity, not a loss of moral life.
∴The impossibility of true responsibility does not destroy moral life; it requires reorienting practices from desert to reform and forward-looking goals; the relinquishment of retributive desert is philosophically honest and humanly sustainable.
Argument III — Mesh theories describe local control but not ultimate responsibility; the Basic Argument’s regress applies at every hierarchical level
1.Frankfurt’s hierarchical theory: freedom requires first-order desires endorsed by second-order volitions. Watson: freedom requires aligned valuation and motivation. These conditions are genuine but insufficient for true responsibility.
2.At every level of the hierarchy, the same question recurs: where did this level come from? The second-order volition was formed by prior psychology and history. The valuation system was shaped by upbringing and experience. None of these were chosen.
3.Mesh theories describe self-expressive, coherent action. True responsibility requires that the self being expressed was itself ultimately the product of free choices. The regress shows this condition cannot be met.
∴Mesh theories succeed at the level of local agency description but fail at the level of true moral responsibility; the Basic Argument’s regress applies at every level of Frankfurt’s hierarchy; no mesh theory can ground the ultimate desert that true responsibility requires.

I want to press the compatibilists with a specific argument — the four-case argument — that I think goes directly to the heart of the reasons-responsiveness and guidance control accounts offered by Fischer and Watson. The argument proceeds by constructing a series of cases that are progressively more similar to ordinary deterministic agency, and asking at each stage whether there is a principled basis for distinguishing the earlier cases from the later ones.

The four cases.

Case 1. A team of neuroscientists directly programmes Plum to weigh reasons egoistically and to act accordingly. He kills Professor White for personal gain. He is reasons-responsive — he could have refrained if given strong enough reasons — but his values were directly implanted.

Case 2. The neuroscientists instead intervene in Plum’s development during childhood, through direct neural manipulation at a critical period, so that he has egoistic values as an adult. The intervention happened in the past, not immediately before the action.

Case 3. The neuroscientists do not manipulate Plum directly. Instead they design the social environment of his upbringing so that he will develop egoistic values. Everything proceeds as naturally as possible, through normal causal channels.

Case 4. Ordinary determinism. Plum’s egoistic character is the product of a deterministic causal history going back before his birth.
i. the argument from cases 1–3

Four-case argument — manipulation cases 1–3 all involve reasons-responsive agents who lack responsibility; the transition to case 4 (ordinary determinism) involves no morally relevant differenceIntuitively, Plum is not morally responsible for the murder in Case 1. He was directly programmed; his values were not his own. And in Case 2, though the manipulation is more distant, it seems equally clear that he is not responsible: the neural intervention at the critical developmental period means his egoistic values were just as externally determined as in Case 1. Case 3 is more controversial, but I want to argue that the same judgement applies: the neuroscientists designed the environment that produced Plum’s character. The causal mechanism is less direct but no less complete. In each case, Plum satisfies Fischer and Ravizza’s conditions for guidance control: he is reasons-responsive and his action issues from his own reasons-responsive mechanism in the relevant sense. But he is not responsible. If the reasons-responsiveness account is sufficient for responsibility, it should give the right verdict in Cases 1–3. It does not.

ii. the transition to ordinary determinism

No morally relevant difference — between cases 3 and 4, only the identity of the causal agent changes; this is not a morally relevant difference for the agent's responsibilityNow consider the transition from Case 3 to Case 4. In Case 3, the neuroscientists designed Plum’s social environment. In Case 4, Plum’s character was produced by the ordinary causal processes of nature and environment — processes that no one designed but that were nonetheless deterministic. The compatibilist wants to say that Case 4 is relevantly different from Case 3: that ordinary causal history, unlike neuroscientist-designed causal history, is compatible with genuine responsibility. But what is the morally relevant difference? The difference is not in Plum’s psychology, which is the same. The difference is not in the degree of causal determination, which is equally complete. The only difference is the identity of the causal agent: neuroscientists versus nature. But why should the identity of the agent who set the determining causal process in motion make any difference to Plum’s moral responsibility? It is Plum’s standing as a responsible agent that is in question, and his standing does not depend on who or what caused him to be who he is.

iii. living without free will and basic desert

Living without free will — a good life is possible without basic desert responsibility; we can ground forward-looking practices in wellbeing rather than desert; hard incompatibilism is liveableThe compatibilist will say that my conclusion is catastrophic. I deny this. What follows from hard incompatibilism is not the elimination of moral practice but its reform. We cannot justifiably hold that anyone basically deserves to suffer for what they have done — that retributive punishment is justified simply because the wrongdoer did wrong. But we can still hold wrongdoers responsible in a forward-looking sense: we can use practices of moral address — blame, censure, encouragement — as instruments for changing behaviour, protecting the innocent, and expressing our values. We can still feel hurt when someone treats us badly, and we can still communicate that hurt to them. We can still have close personal relationships in which we respond to the quality of others’ wills. What we must relinquish is the idea that retributive suffering is intrinsically deserved — an idea that, once its justification in basic desert is removed, we should be glad to lose. A criminal justice system that aims at protection, rehabilitation, and deterrence rather than desert is not a lesser system. It may well be a better one.

iv. responding to the reasons-responsiveness account

Against Fischer — guidance control requires the mechanism be the agent's own; but the four-case argument shows that what makes a mechanism 'one's own' cannot be causal history, since all four cases have fully determined causal historiesFischer and Ravizza require that for guidance control, the agent must take responsibility for their mechanism — must come to see it as their own through a process of reflection and self-endorsement. This condition is supposed to handle manipulation cases: a freshly implanted mechanism is not yet one’s own. But the four-case argument shows that this response is inadequate. In Cases 2 and 3, Plum’s mechanism developed through his own history of reflection and self-constitution; by Fischer and Ravizza’s lights, it is his own mechanism in the required sense. Yet we still judge him to be non-responsible. The own-mechanism condition does not draw the right line. The problem is that in a determined world, every mechanism — however long it has been part of the agent’s history — was fixed before the agent existed. The agent can never be the ultimate source of the mechanism in the way that genuine responsibility requires.

Argument I — Cases 1–3 all involve reasons-responsive agents who lack moral responsibility; guidance control is insufficient
1.In Cases 1, 2, and 3, Plum satisfies Fischer and Ravizza’s conditions for guidance control: he is reasons-responsive and his action issues from his own reasons-responsive mechanism. Yet in each case the intuition is strong that he is not morally responsible.
2.If guidance control were sufficient for moral responsibility, it should deliver the verdict that Plum is responsible in Cases 1–3. It does not. The cases reveal that guidance control is not sufficient for responsibility.
3.Something beyond reasons-responsiveness is required for moral responsibility — namely, that the agent be the ultimate source of the character and values expressed in the action. The guidance control account omits this condition.
∴The four-case argument demonstrates that guidance control is not sufficient for moral responsibility; the manipulation cases show that a fully reasons-responsive agent can lack responsibility if their character was causally determined by external factors.
Argument II — There is no morally relevant difference between Cases 3 and 4; ordinary determinism is on a par with neuroscientist manipulation
1.Between Cases 3 and 4, Plum’s psychology is identical. The degree of causal determination is identical. The only difference is whether a neuroscientist or impersonal natural processes caused his character to develop as it did.
2.The identity of the causal agent (neuroscientist vs. nature) is not morally relevant to Plum’s standing as a responsible agent. His standing depends on his own psychological properties and causal history, not on who set that history in motion.
3.If Plum lacks responsibility in Case 3 (as intuition strongly suggests), and there is no morally relevant difference between Cases 3 and 4, then Plum lacks responsibility in Case 4 (ordinary determinism) as well.
∴Ordinary deterministic agents are in the same moral situation as the manipulated agents in Cases 1–3; there is no morally relevant difference that would ground responsibility in Case 4 but not in Case 3; ordinary determinism is incompatible with basic desert moral responsibility.
Argument III — A good life is possible without basic desert responsibility; hard incompatibilism is practically liveable
1.Hard incompatibilism eliminates basic desert responsibility — the idea that wrongdoers intrinsically deserve to suffer. It does not eliminate all moral practices or reactive attitudes.
2.Forward-looking practices (blame as communication, deterrence, rehabilitation) can be grounded in wellbeing and harm-prevention rather than desert. These practices remain justified even without basic desert.
3.A criminal justice system oriented toward protection and rehabilitation rather than desert is not merely acceptable but potentially superior to a retributive system. The loss of basic desert is a philosophical and moral clarification, not a catastrophe.
∴Hard incompatibilism is practically liveable and morally defensible; a good life and a just society are possible without basic desert responsibility; the compatibilist charge of catastrophism is false.
Argument IV — The own-mechanism condition fails; in a deterministic world no mechanism can be the agent’s own in the required sense
1.Fischer and Ravizza require that the agent take responsibility for their mechanism through reflection and self-endorsement. This is supposed to distinguish responsible agents from manipulated ones.
2.In Cases 2 and 3, Plum’s mechanism developed through his own history of reflection and self-constitution; it satisfies the own-mechanism condition. Yet he still lacks responsibility. The condition does not draw the right line.
3.In a determined world, every mechanism was fixed before the agent existed. However long the agent has lived with and endorsed their mechanism, they were never the ultimate source of it. The own-mechanism condition cannot rescue guidance control from the four-case argument.
∴The own-mechanism condition is insufficient to rescue Fischer and Ravizza’s guidance control account from the four-case argument; in a deterministic world, no mechanism can be the agent’s own in the sense required for basic desert responsibility.
