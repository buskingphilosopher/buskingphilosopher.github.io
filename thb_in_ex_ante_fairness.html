Intrinsic Value of Equal ChancesMichael Otsuka • “Saving Lives” (2006) • “Determinism and the Value of Equal Chances” (2017)
When two people have equal claims to an indivisible life-saving resource, fairness requires giving each an equal chance — not because chances causally improve the probability of getting the good, but because equality of chances is itself a fair distribution of something rationally valuable. Even if a chance turns out to yield nothing, it is better to have had a genuine equal chance than to have been cut out entirely. This holds even under determinism, where “chances” are merely epistemic.

Fairness, Claims & Surrogate SatisfactionJohn Broome • “Fairness” (1990–91) • Weighing Goods (1991)
Fairness requires satisfying competing claims in proportion to their strength. When an indivisible good cannot be shared, a weighted lottery provides surrogate satisfaction: entering a person’s claim into a lottery proportionate to its strength is the morally required next-best substitute for direct proportional satisfaction. The chance is not nothing — it partially satisfies the requirement of fairness even if the claimant loses. If a person was never in with a chance, their claim was not respected at all.

Separateness of Persons & The Distribution of ChancesAlex Voorhoeve • Voorhoeve & Fleurbaey (2012) • “How Should We Aggregate” (2014)
The separateness of persons demands that the distribution of chances matters independently of expected outcomes. When individuals face competing risks, how those risks are distributed across distinct persons carries moral weight that cannot be captured by summing expected values. A policy that gives five people a 20% chance of being saved is not equivalent to saving one person with certainty, even when expected numbers saved are equal — because the distribution of who bears risk and who holds hope is morally significant.

Logical Arguments

Chair,

I want to begin with the simplest possible case, because it is the one that the opposition must confront most directly: two people with equal claims to one indivisible life-saving resource. Call them Alice and Bob. We can save one but not both. They have equally strong claims. What should we do?

The answer most people give immediately is: toss a coin. Give each a 50% chance. But the opposition will say this is mere procedure — a ritual that comforts but delivers no genuine moral value to the loser. If the coin comes up tails and Alice dies, her 50% chance did her no good. She is dead. The chance was an empty consolation.

I say this is wrong. And I want to show why through a series of cases.

The Rock Cases (Otsuka 2006).

Rock 1: Alice is stranded, will drown without rescue. You can reach her.
Rock 2: Bob is stranded, will drown without rescue. You cannot reach both.

Their claims are equal. You must choose. What does morality require?

Otsuka’s answer: you must toss a fair coin, giving each a 0.5 chance. This is not merely procedural. It is morally required because failing to give Bob any chance would fail to acknowledge his equal moral status as a person whose claim was as strong as Alice’s. If you simply walk past Bob without any coin toss, you have treated him as someone whose claim was never in the running — as if he were not equally worthy of rescue.
i. the coin toss is not merely procedural

Equal chances are intrinsically required — not just procedural comfort; failing to give Bob any chance treats him as having no claimThe opposition will say: the coin toss is merely expressive or procedural. It shows respect but delivers nothing real to the loser. But this misunderstands what we are doing when we toss the coin. Consider the counterfactual: suppose I decide in advance to save Alice and then, for appearances, toss a coin that I know is loaded — a coin that always lands heads and always selects Alice. I tell Bob “we tossed a coin.” Has fairness been achieved? Clearly not. The mere appearance of a chance has not satisfied anything. This shows that what matters is not the procedure but the actual distribution of the chance: the genuine 0.5 probability that it is actually Bob who gets saved. The chance is morally real, not merely expressive. And this means that when the coin lands tails and Bob dies, something of genuine moral value was given to him — a genuine 0.5 probability of survival — even though he did not in fact survive.

ii. the deterministic case and epistemic chances

Epistemic chances suffice — even if determinism is true and the coin was always going to land one way, the epistemic equal chance has moral value because it is rationally equal; the veil of ignorance makes things fairerHere is a deeper point that I develop in my 2017 paper. Suppose determinism is true. Then the coin was always going to land heads. Bob’s “50% chance” was never genuinely 0.5 in the objective, metaphysical sense — it was 0 all along. Does this mean the coin toss achieved nothing morally? I argue it does not. The epistemic probability — the probability from the perspective of our ignorance — is what matters for the rational value of a chance. Before the coin landed, neither Bob nor anyone else knew that the outcome was determined. From behind a veil of ignorance about the coin’s actual outcome, each person rationally valued their chance equally at 0.5. This epistemic equality is sufficient for the moral value of the equal chance. The descent of a veil of ignorance that deprives us of knowledge of who is fated to live and die makes things fairer — even if in objective terms nothing metaphysically probabilistic has occurred.

iii. the three-rock asymmetric case and weighted chances

Weighted chances — when claims differ in strength, proportional weighting of chances is required; giving a stronger claimant a higher probability respects both claimsNow consider a three-person case. Alice on Rock 1 will certainly drown (claim strength: 1.0). Bob on Rock 2 faces a 40% risk of drowning (claim strength: 0.4, since his claim is weakened by the lower severity of his situation). You can rescue at most one. What does fairness require?

Proportional chance allocation: Alice: p_A = c_A / (c_A + c_B) = 1.0 / (1.0 + 0.4) = 0.714 Bob: p_B = c_B / (c_A + c_B) = 0.4 / (1.0 + 0.4) = 0.286 Weighted lottery: a coin with P(heads)=0.714 in Alice's favour

This is not equivalent to simply saving Alice automatically. Bob’s 0.286 chance is morally real. It acknowledges that Bob too has a claim, even if a weaker one. If Bob loses the lottery, something was genuinely given to him — a 28.6% chance of rescue. This is better than nothing, even though he did not receive the good.

iv. the asymmetry with pure instrumentalism

Against pure instrumentalism — if chances had only instrumental value (value via getting the good), then a losing lottery would be worth exactly the same as no lottery at all; this is falseThe pure instrumentalist about chances says: a chance has value only as a means to getting the good. Its value = p × V(good). So Bob’s 0.5 chance of being saved has value 0.5 × V(saved). But if Bob loses, his chance’s actual contribution to his welfare is zero. He is as dead as if he had been given no chance. Therefore, says the instrumentalist, the loser should be indifferent between having had a 50% chance and having been excluded altogether.

Pure instrumentalist calculus (ex post evaluation): Bob given 50% chance, loses: Welfare gained = 0 Bob given 0% chance (excluded): Welfare gained = 0 Difference: 0 Verdict: The losing lottery and exclusion are identical in value.

This is the reductio of pure instrumentalism. It licenses an outcome in which the rescuer, knowing in advance who will survive (say, because the coin is already determined), may as well not bother giving Bob a chance at all. The pre-determined loser’s chance is literally worthless on this view. This consequence is morally unacceptable. It shows that chances must have some value independent of their causal role in producing the good.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Equal chances are morally required, not merely procedurally comforting: the biased coin shows that the content of the chance matters
1.A secretly biased coin that always selects Alice does not satisfy fairness, even if it looks like a fair toss. This shows that what matters is not the procedure but the actual probability distribution.
2.Therefore, genuine fairness requires that Bob actually has a 0.5 probability of being saved — not the mere appearance of it. The genuine probability is what fairness demands.
3.If genuine probability is what fairness demands, and Bob loses, then something of genuine moral value — a real 0.5 chance — was given to him. It was not empty consolation but a real distribution of a morally required good.
∴Equal chances are morally required in equal-claim cases because the genuine probability distribution is what fairness demands; when the loser loses, they received something of genuine moral value — the required equal chance — even though they did not receive the ultimate good.
Argument II — Under determinism, epistemic equal chances are rationally valuable and morally sufficient for the fairness of a lottery
1.Under determinism, the coin was always going to land one way. Bob’s “objective” chance was 0 or 1, not 0.5. Yet the epistemic probability — 0.5 from behind the veil of our ignorance about the outcome — is what each person rationally valued equally before the toss.
2.A thing is rationally valuable to the degree that it is rational to value it. Since both claimants rationally value the epistemic 0.5 chance equally, the epistemic chance is equally valuable to each of them, regardless of its objective metaphysical status.
3.Therefore, the descent of a veil of epistemic ignorance about the coin’s outcome makes the distribution fairer, even under determinism. Fairness operates on what we rationally know and can value, not on metaphysical facts about objective chance that may not exist.
∴Epistemic equal chances are morally sufficient for fair lotteries even under determinism; the rational value of a 0.5 epistemic probability is equal for both claimants; this equality is what fairness requires and delivers.
Argument III — Proportional chances respect unequal claims without nullifying weaker ones: c_A/(c_A+c_B) weighting is morally required
1.Alice: claim strength 1.0 (certain drowning). Bob: claim strength 0.4 (40% risk). Proportional allocation: P(Alice) = 1.0/1.4 = 0.714; P(Bob) = 0.4/1.4 = 0.286.
2.Broome’s principle: it is implausible that a claim marginally weaker than another should count for nothing. Bob’s 0.4 claim is real; it deserves a proportional positive chance, not zero.
3.A winning lottery outcome for Alice is consistent with Bob’s claim having been fully respected in the only way possible given scarcity: by entering it into the lottery at its proportional weight. Bob lost, but his claim was respected.
∴Proportional chance allocation respects unequal claims without nullifying weaker ones; a person whose weaker claim yields them 28.6% has been treated more fairly than one given 0%; the chance difference is morally real and significant.
Argument IV — Pure instrumentalism about chances reduces to the absurdity that a losing lottery is worth exactly as much as exclusion
1.Pure instrumentalism: V(chance) = p × V(good). If Bob loses, his chance’s realised value = 0. A 0% chance also yields realised value = 0. Therefore the pure instrumentalist must say: Bob excluded ≡ Bob given 50% chance and losing.
2.This consequence licenses a rescuer who knows in advance (say, under determinism) who will survive to skip the coin toss entirely. The pre-determined loser’s chance contributes nothing on the instrumentalist view.
3.But a person pre-determined to die who is excluded from the lottery without being given any chance has been treated worse than one who was given a genuine equal chance. This difference is morally real; the instrumentalist account cannot capture it.
∴Pure instrumentalism about chances is false: it entails that losing a fair lottery is no different from exclusion, which is plainly wrong; chances have value that is not exhausted by their causal role in delivering the ultimate good.

Chair,

My position on the value of chances is best understood through the framework of claims and their satisfaction. Fairness is, at its core, the proportional satisfaction of competing claims. When a good is divisible, this is easy: give each claimant a share proportional to their claim. But when the good is indivisible — one kidney, one ventilator, one lifeboat seat — proportional division is impossible. And this is where the moral value of chances becomes essential.

Broome’s kidney case (adapted from Fairness, 1990–91).

Alice needs a kidney. Her claim strength: 0.9 (she will die without it, matches perfectly).
Bob needs the same kidney. His claim strength: 0.6 (he will suffer severe disability without it).

The kidney is indivisible. Fairness ideally requires each person to receive a share of the kidney proportional to their claim — but a split kidney saves neither.

What to do? A weighted lottery with P(Alice wins) = 0.9/1.5 = 0.60 and P(Bob wins) = 0.6/1.5 = 0.40.
i. surrogate satisfaction: the lottery as fairness proxy

Surrogate satisfaction — the lottery partially satisfies fairness even though it cannot perfectly satisfy claims in outcome; each claim enters the lottery at its proportional weightThe weighted lottery does not give each person their proportional share of the kidney. What it does is give each person their proportional share of the chance of receiving the kidney. This is what I call surrogate satisfaction of fairness: it is the best available approximation of proportional satisfaction when the good is indivisible. Let me be precise about what “partially satisfying fairness” means.

Direct proportional satisfaction (impossible): Alice receives 0.9/1.5 = 60% of the kidney → infeasible (kidney is indivisible) Bob receives 0.6/1.5 = 40% of the kidney → infeasible Surrogate satisfaction via lottery: Alice receives P = 0.9/1.5 = 0.60 chance of getting the whole kidney Bob receives P = 0.6/1.5 = 0.40 chance of getting the whole kidney Fairness score under surrogate satisfaction: Each person's claim is entered at its proportional weight. The lottery is fair iff P_i ∝ c_i for all i.

The opposition says that because only one person will actually receive the kidney, the loser’s 40% chance contributed nothing to his welfare. But this is a confusion. The question is not whether the loser received the kidney (he did not) but whether his claim was respected (it was). The 40% chance is the respect owed to his claim. It is not a consolation prize for losing — it is the only form in which his claim could be satisfied given the indivisibility of the good.

ii. the complaint of one who was never in with a chance

Never on the cards — if a claimant was never given a positive chance, their complaint is not merely that they lost, but that they were never a genuine contender; this complaint is different in kind from the complaint of a lottery loserHere is the key asymmetry that the opposition must explain. Consider three scenarios for Bob, all of which end with Bob not receiving the kidney:

Scenario A: Kidney given to Alice with certainty (no lottery). Bob's chance: P = 0.0 Bob's complaint: "My claim was never considered. I had no chance." Severity of complaint: MAXIMAL Scenario B: Fair weighted lottery run, Bob loses. Bob's chance: P = 0.40 Bob's complaint: "I had a real chance but lost fairly." Severity of complaint: REDUCED — claim was respected Scenario C: Biased lottery (rigged in Alice's favour), Bob loses. Bob's stated chance: 0.40, actual chance: 0.0 Bob's complaint: Same as Scenario A, plus deception. Severity: MAXIMAL + DECEPTION

Scenario A and Scenario C are identical in moral complaint-severity. Scenario B is genuinely different. The difference between B and A is precisely the value of the 40% chance. If the opposition says that value is zero (because Bob didn’t get the kidney), they must say Scenario B is no better for Bob than Scenario A. But it clearly is better — Bob was treated as a genuine claimant in B, not in A.

iii. the Diamond case: equal claims, equal chances

Diamond (1967) — two equally deserving people, one indivisible good; a coin toss is better than choosing either person directly, even though expected outcome is equal in all three optionsPeter Diamond’s classic 1967 case provides the sharpest test. Two equally deserving people, one good. Three options: (i) give to person A; (ii) give to person B; (iii) toss a fair coin.

Option (i): Give to A A receives good: P = 1.0 → Expected value for A = 1.0 × V(good) B excluded: P = 0.0 → Expected value for B = 0.0 × V(good) Total expected welfare: 1.0 × V(good) Option (ii): Give to B A excluded: P = 0.0 → Expected value for A = 0.0 B receives good: P = 1.0 → Expected value for B = 1.0 × V(good) Total expected welfare: 1.0 × V(good) Option (iii): Fair coin toss A gets 50% chance: Expected value for A = 0.5 × V(good) B gets 50% chance: Expected value for B = 0.5 × V(good) Total expected welfare: 1.0 × V(good) All three options produce identical TOTAL expected welfare.

A pure expected-welfare maximiser is indifferent between all three options. Yet virtually everyone judges option (iii) to be better — fairer — than options (i) or (ii). Why? Because the equal distribution of the chance is itself a good that the pure welfare calculus does not capture. The equal-chance distribution in (iii) treats both claimants as equal in a way that (i) and (ii) do not, and this equality has genuine moral value over and above its contribution to expected welfare.

iv. the lexical structure of surrogate versus direct satisfaction

Lazenby's lexical objection — surrogate satisfaction via lottery is lexically posterior to direct satisfaction; but Broome disagrees: in equal-claim cases surrogate can outweigh directLazenby argues that fairness in outcome is lexically prior to fairness through lotteries: we should always prefer the option that is fairer in outcome, and only use lotteries as a tie-breaker. But this gets the structure of fairness wrong in equal-claim cases. Consider:

Option X: Give kidney to Alice (no lottery). Alice gets good: certain. Bob: nothing, no chance. Outcome fairness: ZERO (one person gets everything) Option Y: Run weighted lottery, Bob wins. Bob gets good. Alice: nothing, but had a 60% chance. Outcome fairness: ZERO (one person gets everything) But Option Y is fairer than Option X for the following reason: Under Y, Alice had a genuine P=0.60 chance. Under X, Bob had no chance (P=0.0). Lexical priority for outcome would say X and Y are equally unfair in outcome, but the lottery adds fairness value — contradicting Lazenby's view.

The reason Option Y is fairer overall, even though the outcome unfairness score is the same, is that the distribution of chances counts independently. The person who lost the lottery but was in with a genuine chance was treated more fairly than the person who was never given a chance at all. Lexical priority of outcome fairness cannot capture this distinction.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Fairness requires proportional satisfaction of claims; when the good is indivisible, weighted lotteries provide surrogate proportional satisfaction
1.Fairness requires claims to be satisfied in proportion to their strength. Alice (c=0.9) and Bob (c=0.6) should ideally receive 60% and 40% of the good respectively — but this is impossible for an indivisible good.
2.A weighted lottery with P(Alice)=0.60 and P(Bob)=0.40 gives each person a chance proportional to their claim strength. This is surrogate satisfaction: the best available approximation of proportional satisfaction under indivisibility.
3.If Bob loses, his claim has still been satisfied in the only way possible given indivisibility: his 0.40 claim was entered into the lottery at its full proportional weight. The satisfaction is of the fairness requirement, not of the claim in outcome.
∴Weighted lotteries provide genuine surrogate satisfaction of fairness: each claim is entered at its proportional weight; losing a fairly weighted lottery is consistent with having had one’s claim fully respected.
Argument II — The person never given a chance has a complaint categorically different from the person who lost a fair lottery
1.Scenario A (no lottery): Bob’s claim was never considered. He was never a genuine contender. His complaint: maximal severity. He was excluded from the morally required process entirely.
2.Scenario B (fair lottery, Bob loses): Bob had a genuine P=0.40 chance. His claim was respected proportionally. His complaint: reduced, because the fairness requirement was met even though the outcome went against him.
3.If the opposition says Scenario B is morally identical to Scenario A (because Bob received no good in either), they must say that whether a claim was respected is morally irrelevant. This is indefensible.
∴The person excluded entirely and the person who lost a fair lottery have categorically different moral complaints; this categorical difference is exactly the value of the chance; denying it makes the respect owed to claims morally vacuous.
Argument III — The Diamond case: all three options have equal expected welfare but option (iii) (coin toss) is fairer; the fairness of equal chances is a distinct value
1.In Diamond’s case, options (i), (ii), and (iii) all yield the same total expected welfare: 1.0 × V(good). A pure welfarist is indifferent. But virtually everyone judges (iii) to be fairer.
2.The only difference between (iii) and (i) or (ii) is the distribution of chances: (iii) gives each a 0.5 chance; (i) and (ii) give one person a 0.0 chance. This equal distribution of chances is what makes (iii) fairer.
3.Since the equal distribution of chances is what makes (iii) fairer, and since this is a moral advantage not captured by the welfare calculus, the equal distribution of chances has moral value independent of its contribution to expected welfare.
∴The Diamond case demonstrates that equal distribution of chances is a distinct moral value not reducible to expected welfare; all three options have equal expected welfare, yet the coin toss is fairer because the equal distribution of the chance is itself morally valuable.
Argument IV — Lazenby’s lexical priority of outcome fairness is wrong: surrogate satisfaction via lottery can outweigh worse outcome fairness
1.Under lexical priority: outcomes of equal unfairness (both involve one person getting everything) would make lotteries irrelevant. But this ignores the morally significant difference between having had a genuine chance and being excluded entirely.
2.Option X (give to Alice, no lottery) and Option Y (fair lottery, Bob wins): both have outcome unfairness = 0 by Lazenby’s metric. But Y is fairer overall because Alice had a genuine P=0.60 chance under Y and no chance under X.
3.The surplus fairness that Y has over X comes from the lottery’s distribution of chances. This cannot be lexically posterior to outcome fairness because it is a distinct dimension of fairness that outcome metrics cannot capture.
∴Lazenby’s lexical priority of outcome fairness is wrong; surrogate satisfaction via lottery adds genuine fairness value that is incommensurable with — not lexically posterior to — outcome fairness; the distribution of chances is an independent moral dimension.

Chair,

I want to extend the case for the value of chances in a direction that neither Otsuka nor Broome has fully developed: the claim that the separateness of persons demands sensitivity to how risks are distributed across individuals, not merely to expected sums of welfare or aggregate claim satisfaction. When we face choices under uncertainty that affect different persons differently, the distribution of chances is a morally fundamental datum that cannot be collapsed into expected-value comparisons.

The central case: certain one vs. uncertain five (Voorhoeve, adapted).

Option A: Save one person (Ernesto) with certainty from death.
Option B: Give five people (each currently facing a 20% risk of death) treatment that eliminates their risk entirely.

Expected lives saved: both options save exactly one expected life.
Option A: 1 × 1.0 = 1.0 expected lives saved.
Option B: 5 × 0.2 = 1.0 expected lives saved.

Are these options morally equivalent? I argue they are not, and the reason involves the value of the distribution of chances.
i. the separateness of persons and cross-person aggregation

Separateness — cross-person aggregation of risk-benefits ignores that each person faces her own risk alone; you can't pool Ernesto's certain death with five people's smaller risks without crossing the interpersonal boundaryThe utilitarian view says Options A and B are equivalent because they have the same expected welfare sum. But this aggregation crosses the separateness of persons in a morally problematic way. Ernesto faces certain death if Option B is chosen. Each of the five faces a 20% risk that the treatment would have eliminated. From Ernesto’s perspective, Option A is better. From each of the five’s perspective, Option B is better. The aggregationist says these individual perspectives can be summed: 5 × 0.2 = 1 × 1.0 = equal. But summing across persons is not like summing within a single person’s life. Ernesto cannot be compensated by the reduction in risk to five other people. He will be dead.

Interpersonal comparison forbidden by separateness: Ernesto's loss (certain death) vs. each of five's gain (20% risk eliminated) These cannot be directly compared because: - If Option B and treatment fails for all five, Ernesto is dead AND five had 20% risk - There is no mechanism by which the five's risk-reduction compensates Ernesto - The separateness of persons prohibits the cross-person arithmetic ii. who has the strongest interpersonal complaint?

Strongest complaint — Ernesto's certain-death complaint is stronger than any of the five's 20%-risk complaint; a contractualist cannot reasonably reject saving ErnestoThe contractualist question is: who can make the strongest individual complaint against which option? Ernesto’s complaint against Option B is categorical: it condemns him to certain death. Each of the five’s complaint against Option A is probabilistic: it leaves them with a 20% risk of death rather than none.

Complaints against each option: Option B (save five at risk): Ernesto's complaint: Certain death. Complaint strength = 1.0 × V(life). Each of five's complaint: None (they are saved from their 20% risk). Option A (save Ernesto): Each of five's complaint: 20% risk not eliminated. Complaint strength = 0.2 × V(life) per person. Aggregate complaint of five: 5 × 0.2 × V(life) = 1.0 × V(life). The aggregationist says these sum to equality. The contractualist says: compare INDIVIDUAL complaints, not sums. Strongest individual complaint against Option B: Ernesto, strength 1.0. Strongest individual complaint against Option A: any of the five, strength 0.2. Ernesto's complaint OUTWEIGHS any individual in the five. Therefore: Option A is required. iii. the value of a chance: why each of the five has a weaker claim even given aggregation

Value of chance in Voorhoeve case — each of the five has a genuine chance-based claim; but when chance is small (20%) and the alternative is certain death for one, the chance's moral weight is lessHere is where the value of chances enters most sharply. Each of the five does have a morally significant claim: a 20% chance of death is a real risk that deserves recognition. Their claims are not nothing. But the moral weight of a chance-based claim is proportional to the probability it concerns. A 20% chance of dying grounds a weaker claim than a 100% chance of dying.

Chance-weighted claim strengths: Ernesto (100% risk): claim = 1.0 × V(life) = V(life) Each of five (20% risk): claim = 0.2 × V(life) Ratio: Ernesto's claim is 1.0/0.2 = 5× stronger than each of the five. This is why a contractualist cannot reasonably reject saving Ernesto: no individual in the five group can say their claim outweighs his. The chance MATTERS in determining claim strength: A 20% chance of death grounds a claim of strength 0.2. A 100% chance of death grounds a claim of strength 1.0. These are not morally equivalent per person. iv. the Fleurbaey–Voorhoeve extension: competing claims view

Competing claims — chance-based inequality is morally significant independently; inequality in expected utilities grounds competing claims that have moral weight even before outcome is knownIn our 2012 paper with Marc Fleurbaey, I argue that inequality in individuals’ expected utilities — in the chances they hold — generates moral claims that matter independently of outcome. An egalitarian view that considers only outcome inequality misses something: the fairness of how risks are distributed before outcomes are known.

Competing Claims View (Voorhoeve & Fleurbaey 2012): Policy P1: Ernesto faces 100% risk; five face 0% risk. Expected utility inequality: Ernesto holds EU=0; five hold EU=1. Inequality in chances: Gini-like measure = very high. Policy P2: Equal risk distribution: each of six faces 16.7% risk. Expected utility: each holds EU = 0.833. Inequality in chances: zero. P2 is fairer even if P1 saves the same number in expectation, because inequality in the distribution of chances is itself unjust, grounds competing claims, and matters independently of outcomes. This is the Competing Claims View: how chances are distributed OVER PERSONS is a morally fundamental datum, not merely an instrumental means to welfare.

The opposition’s ex-post view cannot account for the moral significance of Policy P2’s equal risk distribution if no one ends up dying. If everyone survives, ex-post analysis says all policies were equivalent — no bad outcome occurred. But Policy P2 was fairer before outcomes were known, and this ex-ante fairness is real moral value.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The separateness of persons prohibits collapsing Ernesto’s certain death against five people’s 20% risk into an equal expected-value comparison
1.Ernesto faces certain death under Option B. He cannot be compensated by the five other people’s risk reduction. Their risk reduction happens to other persons; it does nothing for Ernesto.
2.Aggregating across persons treats the interpersonal boundary as morally irrelevant. But the separateness of persons requires this boundary to be morally significant: each person’s prospect matters independently.
3.Therefore Options A and B are not morally equivalent even though they have equal expected welfare sums. The separateness of persons demands we compare individual-level claims, not aggregated expected values.
∴The separateness of persons prohibits the aggregationist equivalence of Options A and B; each person’s individual prospect must be evaluated separately, and Ernesto’s certain death cannot be offset by the five’s risk reduction.
Argument II — Contractualist reasoning: Ernesto’s individual complaint strength (1.0) outweighs each of the five’s (0.2); Option A is required
1.Ernesto’s complaint against Option B: certain death; complaint strength = 1.0 × V(life). This is the strongest individual complaint in the case.
2.Each of the five’s complaint against Option A: 20% risk uneliminated; complaint strength = 0.2 × V(life). No individual can make a complaint stronger than Ernesto’s.
3.No one can reasonably reject Option A (save Ernesto) because Ernesto’s complaint against Option B outweighs any individual complaint against Option A. The contractualist test favours Option A.
∴Contractualist comparison of individual complaint strengths requires Option A; Ernesto’s claim (strength 1.0) outweighs each of the five’s (strength 0.2); numbers alone do not determine the outcome when individual claim strengths differ so dramatically.
Argument III — Chance-based claim strength is proportional to probability: a 20% risk grounds a 0.2-strength claim, not a 1.0-strength claim
1.Each of the five has a morally significant claim based on their 20% risk. This claim is real and has positive moral weight: 0.2 × V(life) per person.
2.But the moral weight of a chance-based claim is proportional to the probability it concerns. A 100% risk grounds a claim five times stronger than a 20% risk, per person.
3.This is why the value of chances matters for moral reasoning: the size of the chance determines the weight of the claim, and claim weight determines fairness obligations. Treating all positive chances as morally equivalent regardless of size is a theoretical error.
∴Chance-based claims have strength proportional to the probability they concern; a 20% chance of death grounds a claim of strength 0.2; a 100% chance grounds a claim of strength 1.0; this proportionality is essential to any adequate theory of fairness under risk.
Argument IV — The Competing Claims View: inequality in the distribution of chances is unjust independently of outcomes
1.Policy P1 (Ernesto faces 100% risk; five face 0%): maximum inequality in the distribution of chances across persons. Inequality measure is maximised.
2.Policy P2 (equal risk distribution at 16.7% each): zero inequality in the distribution of chances. This is fairer ex ante, before outcomes are known.
3.An ex-post view says P1 and P2 are equally fair if all persons survive (no bad outcome occurred). But P2 was fairer before outcomes were known; this ex-ante fairness is real moral value that the ex-post view cannot capture.
∴The Competing Claims View captures what the ex-post view misses: inequality in the distribution of chances across persons is unjust independently of outcomes; equal risk distribution is morally superior to concentrated risk even when all outcomes are equal.
You Cannot Eat ChancesDavid Wasserman • “Let Them Eat Chances” (1996)
Chances are a consolation that fails to console anyone who loses. They have no intrinsic value: a chance benefits its holder only if it results in the person receiving the good. An equal chance to a scarce resource is not a genuine good — it is a placeholder for the possibility of a good that may never arrive. My argument is more targeted than Wasserman’s. I am not denying that lotteries have fairness value of any kind. The lexical structure (Lazenby 2014).

First-order fairness (lexically prior): The direct satisfaction of claims in outcome. A is fairer than B in the first-order sense if more claims are directly satisfied in A than in B.

Second-order fairness (lexically posterior): The surrogate satisfaction of claims through lotteries, when direct satisfaction is impossible. This is valuable only as a tie-breaker when first-order fairness is equal across options.

Broome’s error: he allows second-order (lottery) fairness to outweigh first-order (outcome) fairness, producing scenarios in which we should run a lottery even when we could achieve a fairer outcome by not doing so.

Contractualism, as I develop it in What We Owe to Each Other, holds that an act is wrong if it fails to accord with principles that no one could reasonably reject. The test for a principle is whether any person affected by it could reasonably reject it.

∴The loser’s ex post complaint is not dissolved by ex ante procedural fairness; both dimensions of fairness are real; but this shows that ex ante chances are not the complete story of fairness, not that they are intrinsically valuable.
Argument IV — Occam’s razor: contractualism handles all the relevant cases without positing intrinsic chance value
1.Case 1 (equal claimants): Contractualism requires the lottery via reasonable-rejectability test. Same verdict as intrinsic-chance view, no extra postulation needed.
2.Case 2 (1 certain vs. 5 at 20% risk): Pairwise comparison gives Ernesto the stronger individual complaint; contractualism saves Ernesto without aggregating chance-weighted claims.
3.Case 3 (unequal claims): Contractualism gives the good to the strongest claimant without needing a weighted lottery, because the weaker claimant cannot reasonably reject this. Again, same or better verdict without positing intrinsic chance value.
∴Contractualism handles all the cases that motivate the intrinsic-chance postulation, giving the same or better verdicts with simpler theoretical apparatus; Occam’s razor favours the contractualist account; the intrinsic value of chances is theoretically unnecessary.
