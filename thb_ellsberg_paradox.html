Mr President,

The paradox before this house differs from the Allais paradox in a philosophically crucial respect. Allais concerned choices under risk — situations where probabilities are objectively known. Ellsberg concerns choices under uncertainty — situations where probabilities are unknown. The opposition will argue that this difference is precisely what defeats Savage's theory: that under genuine uncertainty, an agent simply cannot form a single additive probability over outcomes, and that any theory demanding they do is demanding something unreasonable. I want to argue that this response mistakes a psychological description of human discomfort under uncertainty for a normative account of what rationality requires.

"The Ellsberg urn contains 90 balls: 30 are red, and 60 are either black or yellow in unknown proportions. An agent who prefers to bet on red over black in the first gamble, but prefers to bet on black-or-yellow over red-or-yellow in the second, violates the sure-thing principle. In the first gamble they act as if red is more likely than black. In the second they act as if black-or-yellow is more likely than red-or-yellow. These cannot both be true. The agent has committed themselves to inconsistent probability judgements — and no appeal to 'ambiguity' rehabilitates a contradiction."
i. the sure-thing principle and its force

Sure-thing principle — Ellsberg violates it; inconsistent implicit probabilities; the argumentThe sure-thing principle, as I developed it in The Foundations of Statistics, says: if you prefer act A to act B regardless of whether event E occurs or does not occur, you should prefer A to B when you do not know whether E occurs. In the Ellsberg case, the four gambles are:

A: Win $100 if red drawn (30/90 balls red)
B: Win $100 if black drawn (black proportion unknown)
C: Win $100 if red or yellow drawn
D: Win $100 if black or yellow drawn
The modal preference is A≻B and D≻C. This violates the sure-thing principle. The yellow ball is a common consequence in both comparisons: whether or not yellow is drawn is the same for A versus B, and for C versus D. The sure-thing principle says the prospect of drawing yellow should be irrelevant to both comparisons. But if A≻B because the agent treats red as more likely than black, then they implicitly assign red higher probability than black — and they should therefore prefer C (red or yellow) over D (black or yellow). The preference D≻C reveals that the agent simultaneously treats black as more likely than red in the second comparison. These are inconsistent implicit probability assignments. That is not ambiguity aversion — it is incoherence.

ii. de Finetti's coherence argument — a unique prior must exist

de Finetti — coherence; Dutch book; unique prior; belief always representable as probabilityBruno de Finetti's coherence argument establishes that any agent whose betting dispositions are not representable by a unique, additive probability function over events is subject to a Dutch book: a sequence of bets each of which they accept as fair, but which together guarantee them a loss. This is not a threat an agent can dismiss by saying they have imprecise beliefs or multiple priors. It is a demonstration that the agent's own betting commitments are inconsistent — that they have, in effect, committed to prices for bets that cannot simultaneously be fair. An agent who refuses to bet on black in the Ellsberg urn at any odds above 50:50, but also refuses to bet on not-black at any odds above 50:50, is in exactly this position: there is no allocation of fair prices consistent with both refusals. De Finetti's theorem shows that coherent belief, whatever its psychological feel of uncertainty, is always representable as a unique additive probability. The agent who says "I cannot assign a probability to black" is saying something psychologically accurate about their state of discomfort but something normatively incorrect about what coherence requires.

iii. what "ambiguity" amounts to — a second-order confusion

Second-order confusion — first-order probability is all that matters for action; meta-uncertainty is representable as first-order uncertaintyThe standard philosophical motivation for rejecting the unique prior requirement is: in the Ellsberg urn, the agent genuinely does not know the probability that a black ball is drawn, and this genuine ignorance is different from knowing the probability is 0.5. I accept that genuine ignorance is psychologically different from precise knowledge of probabilities. What I deny is that this psychological difference requires a revision of the normative theory. Whatever meta-uncertainty the agent has about the probability of black — whatever range of second-order probability distributions they might have — this meta-uncertainty can be represented as first-order uncertainty about a compound lottery. The agent who is uncertain whether the black proportion is 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, or 0.8 has a second-order probability over these possibilities. The expected probability of drawing black, given these second-order beliefs, is 0.5 — identical to the probability of drawing red. A rational agent should therefore be indifferent between betting on red and black. The failure to be indifferent is not a manifestation of deep irrationality in the theory — it is a failure to integrate second-order uncertainty correctly into first-order probability judgements.

iv. the symmetry argument

Symmetry — by symmetry red and black are equally likely; Laplace principle of indifference; deviation from indifference is biasThere is a further argument for the indifference that the Ellsberg preferences violate. Given the agent knows 30 balls are red and 60 are black or yellow, and given the agent has no information that distinguishes any black-yellow ratio as more likely than any other, the principle of indifference applies: by symmetry, each possible black-yellow ratio is equally likely, so the expected number of black balls is 30. The expected probability of drawing black is therefore 30/90 = 1/3, identical to the probability of drawing red. An agent with Ellsberg preferences — who refuses to bet on black at what should be even odds — is not being appropriately cautious under uncertainty. They are exhibiting a bias toward the known that their own information does not justify. The principle of indifference, properly applied to the available information, yields a precise prior that makes the Ellsberg choices irrational rather than ambiguity-averse.

v. the normative status of SEU against revision

Normative status — SEU's axiomatic foundation is compelling; revisions sacrifice inferential power; the sure-thing principle cannot be abandonedI want to close with a defence of SEU's normative status against all revisions — maxmin EU, Choquet EU, imprecise Bayesianism. Each revision sacrifices something of the axiomatic foundation that makes SEU compelling: they violate consequentialism, or monotonicity, or partition invariance, or the sure-thing principle. The sure-thing principle is not a technical convenience. It is an expression of the most fundamental requirement of rational action: that you do not allow irrelevant factors to influence your decisions. Yellow balls are irrelevant to the comparison between red and black because the outcome is the same regardless of whether yellow is drawn. Allowing yellow's proportion to influence the red-black comparison is as irrational as changing your preference between two job offers based on an irrelevant third candidate you know you won't choose. The Ellsberg paradox does not reveal a limitation of SEU. It reveals a systematic human tendency to let irrelevant information distort preference — a tendency that a normative theory should diagnose as an error, not accommodate as a virtue.

Mr President,

My colleague Savage has made the orthodox Bayesian defence. I want to approach the same conclusion from a different angle — one that takes seriously the philosophical challenge posed by genuine ignorance while arguing that the right response to that challenge is not to abandon precise probabilities but to think more carefully about what the agent's utility function should look like when information has value. The Ellsberg paradox, on careful examination, does not reveal a new attitude toward risk or uncertainty that SEU cannot capture. It reveals that agents who care about being well-informed — who attach utility to epistemic states themselves, not just to monetary outcomes — may rationally exhibit behaviour that mimics ambiguity aversion without violating SEU.

"The standard Ellsberg presentation treats the agent's utility as a function of the monetary outcome only. But suppose the agent also cares about the epistemic quality of the basis on which they act — about whether their choice is well-grounded in reliable information. An agent with this additional epistemic value can rationally prefer the risky urn over the ambiguous one, not because they have multiple priors, but because choosing the known urn is associated with a higher-quality epistemic state that they value independently of the monetary outcome."
i. the utility of information argument

Utility of information — epistemic utility; preference for well-grounded choice; SEU explanation of ambiguity aversionThe standard Ellsberg presentation assumes agents care only about monetary outcomes. But this is an idealisation that many agents rationally violate. Consider an agent who values not only money but the epistemic quality of their decision process — who prefers to be in a state of making a well-grounded, well-informed choice over making a shot-in-the-dark choice, even if the expected monetary outcome is identical. This agent will rationally prefer to bet on the known urn: not because they assign lower probability to drawing black from the ambiguous urn, but because the act of choosing the known urn is associated with a better epistemic state — more information, more reliable expectation — that they value independently. This is a utility over epistemic states, and it is representable within SEU. No revision of the probability structure is required: only a richer specification of what the agent's utility function is defined over.

ii. the distinction between risk and uncertainty — epistemically, not normatively, important

Risk vs. uncertainty — Frank Knight's distinction; epistemic not normative significance; same probability theory appliesFrank Knight's distinction between risk (known probability) and uncertainty (unknown probability) is epistemically real: there is a genuine cognitive difference between knowing that a fair coin has 50% probability of heads and not knowing the probability of drawing a black ball from an ambiguous urn. What I deny is that this epistemic difference requires a revision of the normative theory of probability. The probability theory that applies to both cases is the same: the agent should form beliefs consistent with the information they have, using whatever principles of inference are appropriate. The cognitive difference between the two cases is a difference in the quality of the available evidence for belief formation — not a difference in the kind of probability that applies. An agent who can form a precise prior in both cases by correctly applying principles of indifference, updating, and integration has no need for imprecise or set-valued probabilities.

iii. the Machina paradox — ambiguity models face their own difficulties

Machina paradox — MEU and CEU violated by plausible preferences; ambiguity models have their own paradoxesThe opposition will invoke MaxMin EU and Choquet EU as the appropriate generalisations. But Mark Machina has demonstrated that these models face their own paradoxes: carefully constructed examples in which the natural, intuitive preferences violate maxmin EU's predictions in ways that are just as troubling as the original Ellsberg violation of SEU. If maxmin EU is itself subject to paradoxes that require further revision — smooth ambiguity models, variational preferences, confidence functions — we face an infinite regress of ever-more-complicated frameworks, each designed to accommodate an additional set of intuitions and each generating new cases that violate it. The simplicity and axiomatic elegance of SEU, combined with a richer account of utility functions, is preferable to this proliferation of frameworks.

iv. the multiplier preferences and robust decision theory

Hansen-Sargent multiplier preferences — robust control; penalty function; representable within SEU with entropy constraintEven within the ambiguity-theoretic tradition, there are approaches that preserve much of SEU's structure. Hansen and Sargent's multiplier preference model, developed for macroeconomics and robust control theory, represents ambiguity aversion as a penalty function on the deviation of beliefs from a reference prior — a formulation that is closely related to variational inference in statistical learning theory. This model is formally equivalent, under appropriate conditions, to SEU with a modified utility function that includes a penalty for model uncertainty. The interpretation is that the agent has a reference probability and penalises departures from it — not that they have a set of priors with no reference distribution. The practical applications of this framework in monetary policy and financial risk management demonstrate that it is operationally tractable in ways that set-valued imprecise probability frameworks are not.

v. against imprecise credences as a normative ideal

Against imprecise credences — decision paralysis; Elga's objection; permissiveness without action-guidanceImprecise Bayesianism — the opposition's preferred framework — faces a decisive objection: it generates decision paralysis in exactly the cases where decision guidance is most needed. If an agent's credences are represented by an interval [0.2, 0.8] for some event, and no act dominates all others across the whole interval, the imprecise framework gives no recommendation. Adam Elga's objection to imprecise credences is decisive: an agent whose credences are represented by a set of probability functions cannot use standard Bayesian updating to revise their beliefs — different updating rules applied to different elements of the credence set produce different posterior credence sets, and there is no general rule for combining them. A normative theory that leaves agents without action guidance in precisely the cases of deep uncertainty it was designed for has failed its practical mandate.

Mr President,

In Decision Theory with a Human Face, I argue for what I call imprecise Bayesianism — a framework in which an agent's doxastic state is represented not by a single probability function but by a set of probability functions, and in which rational action requires not maximisation of expected utility relative to a unique prior but something more nuanced: a relationship between the agent's set of credences and the available acts that respects the full range of their uncertainty. My argument is that this is not a retreat from rationality but a more accurate representation of what rationality actually requires for agents who are genuinely uncertain about probabilities — not just uncertain about outcomes.

"Savage and de Finetti demand that every rational agent have a unique additive probability over all events. But this demand conflates two fundamentally different epistemic states: equivocation — being equally confident in two hypotheses — and ignorance — having insufficient evidence to form any justified credence. The demand for a unique prior treats these as identical. They are not. An agent who assigns probability 0.5 to black because they know the urn is half-black has a different epistemic state from one who assigns 0.5 because they have no idea. Respecting this difference requires imprecise credences."
i. equivocation versus ignorance — the key distinction

Equivocation vs. ignorance — degenerate interval [0.5,0.5] vs. wide interval [0,1]; different doxastic states; unique prior collapses themThe most important conceptual contribution of imprecise Bayesianism is the distinction between equivocation and ignorance. An agent who believes that the probability of an event is 0.5 — because the urn is half-filled with red balls and half with blue, and each ball is equally likely to be drawn — is in a state of equivocation: equal credence in the two hypotheses, backed by definite information. This state is correctly represented by the degenerate interval [0.5, 0.5]. An agent who has no idea what proportion of black and yellow balls are in the Ellsberg urn is in a state of ignorance: they lack the evidence to form any justified credence. This state is correctly represented by a wide interval — in the limiting case of total ignorance, the interval [0, 1]. The demand for a unique prior forces both agents to assign the same number: 0.5. This collapses the distinction between evidentially grounded equivocation and uninformed ignorance — destroying information about the quality of the agent's epistemic state that is directly relevant to decision-making.

ii. the imprecise Bayesian framework — interval-valued credences

Interval credences — set of probability functions; lower and upper probabilities; representation of genuine ignoranceIn the imprecise Bayesian framework I develop in Decision Theory with a Human Face, an agent's belief state is represented by a set C of probability functions — all the probability functions the agent regards as consistent with their evidence and background beliefs. The agent's credence in a proposition A is not a single number but an interval [inf{p(A): p∈C}, sup{p(A): p∈C}] — the range of probability values assigned to A across all the probability functions in the set. For the Ellsberg urn, the agent who knows only that 60 balls are black or yellow would have C include all distributions consistent with this constraint — an interval [0, 2/3] for the probability of drawing black. This interval correctly represents genuine uncertainty about the proportion of black balls without artificially forcing a point estimate. The agent who knows the urn is half-red and half-other has a precise credence of 1/3 for red — and the imprecise framework represents both agents appropriately.

iii. against de Finetti's Dutch book argument

Against Dutch book — imprecise agent refuses bets outside interval; no Dutch book against cautious agent; Dutch book assumes bet acceptance dispositionDe Finetti's Dutch book argument assumes the agent has a disposition to accept or reject bets at specific prices — a disposition that reveals a unique precise probability. But an agent with interval credences [l, u] for event A rationally refuses to buy a bet on A at any price below l·V and refuses to sell a bet on A at any price above u·V, where V is the prize. There is no single price at which the agent is willing to bet both ways — and there is no Dutch book against such an agent, precisely because the agent refuses to commit to both buying and selling at the same price. The Dutch book argument does not establish that rational agents must have unique priors. It establishes that agents who are willing to bet both ways at a single price must have precise probabilities. An agent with imprecise credences who refuses such two-way commitment is not Dutch-bookable — they have simply declined the disposition the Dutch book argument assumes.

iv. updating imprecise credences — Bradley's solution

Updating — Jeffrey conditioning for imprecise credences; prior-by-prior updating; Bradley's revised rule; handles dilationJoyce's objection that imprecise credences cannot be consistently updated is a real challenge that deserves a serious response. In my framework, I adopt a generalisation of Jeffrey conditioning that applies prior-by-prior: each probability function in the credence set C is updated individually using standard Bayesian conditioning or Jeffrey conditioning, producing a posterior credence set C' that represents the agent's updated beliefs. This prior-by-prior updating rule is coherent: it preserves the agent's calibration across all the probability functions in the set. The phenomenon of dilation — where conditioning on information can expand the credence interval — is acknowledged and defended: dilation correctly reflects that learning some things can increase the extent of one's genuine uncertainty, which is an epistemically significant fact, not a failure of the framework. A precise Bayesian who gains evidence that expands the range of viable probability estimates should update their credences to reflect this, not force them into a narrower interval that misrepresents their epistemic state.

v. the human face of decision theory

Human face — bounded rationality; real agents cannot always form precise priors; theory must accommodate genuine epistemic limitsThe title of my book expresses my central conviction: a decision theory adequate to real agents — agents with genuine epistemic limits, incomplete information, and bounded computational resources — must accommodate the fact that those agents sometimes cannot justifiably form precise probability estimates, and that requiring them to do so anyway does not produce better decisions. It produces the illusion of precision where none exists. The Ellsberg paradox is precisely a case in which real agents are right to sense that their epistemic situation is different from the known-probability case, and right to let that sensing influence their choices. A theory that tells them they are simply wrong — that they should assign 0.5 to black by the principle of indifference and proceed — is not giving them better decision guidance. It is ignoring the genuine epistemic structure of their situation in favour of a mathematical convenience.

I want to make two distinct arguments that complement each other. The first is Itzhak Gilboa's and David Schmeidler's formal case that MaxMin Expected Utility is the axiomatically justified representation of ambiguity aversion — and that the sure-thing principle is the specific axiom that should be rejected when genuine uncertainty is present. The second is Alex Voorhoeve's and Thomas Rowe's moral argument that under severe uncertainty — the kind represented by the Ellsberg urn — there are not merely permissible but positively demanded reasons to guard against the worst case, especially when the burdens of uncertainty are distributed across persons with claims on equitable treatment.

"The sure-thing principle is often presented as if it were equivalent to the dominance principle — as if violating it meant endorsing dominated acts. It is not. The sure-thing principle is stronger than dominance. It requires not only that you not be dominated but that you make your choice independent of outcomes that are certain to be the same regardless of your choice. Under genuine uncertainty — where you do not know which states of the world obtain — this additional requirement is too strong. The MEU axioms give it up. This is not irrationality. It is appropriate sensitivity to the structure of the decision problem."
i. Gilboa-Schmeidler axioms — what is kept and what is given up

MEU axioms — weak certainty independence; uncertainty aversion; Gilboa-Schmeidler theorem; sure-thing principle replacedIn their 1989 paper "MaxMin Expected Utility with Non-Unique Prior," Gilboa and Schmeidler provide an axiomatic characterisation of MaxMin EU preferences. They retain most of Savage's axioms: weak ordering (completeness and transitivity), monotonicity, continuity, non-degeneracy. What they replace is the independence axiom — Savage's sure-thing principle — with two weaker axioms. First, uncertainty aversion: when an agent is indifferent between two acts, they weakly prefer a mixture of them — a hedge — over either alone. Second, weak certainty independence: the independence axiom is retained only for mixtures with a constant act — one whose outcome does not vary across states. These two weaker axioms, together with the retained Savage axioms, characterise exactly the MEU representation: maximising the minimum expected utility over a closed convex set of probability measures C. This is an axiomatic representation theorem, not an ad hoc accommodation of observed preferences. The weaker axioms are independently defensible: uncertainty aversion says you prefer to hedge under genuine uncertainty; weak certainty independence says that mixing with a certain prospect does not change the relative evaluation of two acts.

ii. why uncertainty aversion is rationally defensible

Uncertainty aversion — preference for hedging; information value; Knightian uncertainty vs. risk; rational cautionThe sure-thing principle says that if I prefer A to B in some states and am indifferent in others, I prefer A to B overall — and crucially, this should be the case regardless of the probability distribution over states. Under risk — known probability — this is compelling. Under Knightian uncertainty — unknown probability — it is not. Under uncertainty, I cannot be confident that the states in which A dominates are the more probable states. The hedge — a mixture of A and B — may outperform either pure act under the realised probability distribution, even if I cannot compute the expected performance of the hedge precisely. A preference for the hedge under genuine uncertainty is therefore not irrational fixation on the worst case: it is a rational response to the possibility that any given act is the wrong one under the true, unknown probability distribution. Uncertainty aversion is the formal expression of rational caution under ignorance about the probability of states.

iii. the sure-thing principle is too strong under genuine uncertainty

Sure-thing principle too strong — requires state-independence of preference under all distributions; appropriate only when distribution known; yellow's relevance under genuine uncertaintySavage's sure-thing principle requires that my preference between two acts be independent of outcomes in states where both acts yield the same result — the common consequence states. Under risk, this is appropriate: if I know the common states have probability 0.89, I can compute the expected value independently of what happens there. Under genuine uncertainty — where I do not know the probability of common consequence states — the principle is too strong. The probability of yellow balls is precisely what I am uncertain about in the Ellsberg urn. Whether yellow is drawn affects how much the 30 known red balls matter relative to the unknown black balls. Allowing yellow's unknown probability to influence my preference between red and black is not a violation of rational dominance reasoning — it is a rational response to not knowing how much weight the common consequence states deserve. The sure-thing principle demands state-independence of preference under all probability distributions, including distributions I have no reason to rule out. This is too demanding.

iv. Voorhoeve's egalitarianism under severe uncertainty

Voorhoeve-Rowe — severe uncertainty; egalitarian reasons to guard against worst case; moral dimension of distributing ambiguity burdensAlex Voorhoeve and Thomas Rowe in "Egalitarianism under Severe Uncertainty" (2018) extend the Ellsberg analysis in an important moral direction. Under severe uncertainty — where policymakers lack any reasoned basis for assigning probabilities to possible outcomes — there are not merely permissible but specifically egalitarian reasons to prefer policies that guard against bad outcomes for the worst-off, over policies that maximise expected value for the better-off. The moral dimension is this: when uncertainty is severe and outcomes are distributed across persons with unequal standing, the burden of getting the decision wrong is not distributed neutrally. A utilitarian who maximises expected value under severe uncertainty may systematically impose the worst-case burden on those least able to bear it. The ambiguity-averse preference — choosing the act that performs best under the worst plausible probability distribution — is not merely rational but morally required when the worst case falls on the already disadvantaged.

v. Bradley's 2022 Ethics paper — impartial evaluation under ambiguity

Bradley 2022 — impartial evaluation; ambiguity aversion justified from behind veil of ignorance; fairness requires attending to worst-case distributionsRichard Bradley's 2022 paper "Impartial Evaluation under Ambiguity" in Ethics provides a further normative foundation for ambiguity aversion that neither Gilboa-Schmeidler nor standard Bayesian approaches fully capture. Bradley shows that from an impartial perspective — analogous to Rawls's veil of ignorance but extended to cases of genuine probability uncertainty — rational agents who do not know which probability distribution governs the world should prefer acts that perform well under the worst-case distribution within the set of probability distributions they cannot rule out. This is not merely a decision procedure: it is a fairness argument. When we cannot know who will bear the costs of a choice made under genuine uncertainty, impartiality requires us to attend to the worst distributional outcomes. The MEU criterion is the formal expression of this impartial, fairness-sensitive evaluation under genuine uncertainty about probabilities.
