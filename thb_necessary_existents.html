Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I want to defend necessitism: the view that necessarily, everything is necessarily something. Formally:

□∀x□∃y(x=y)    [NNE: Necessary Non-Emptiness of Everything]

This is a view that many find immediately counterintuitive. Surely I could have failed to exist — if my parents had never met, I would never have been born. Surely this table could have been unbuilt — if the wood had burned before reaching the factory, no table would have resulted. How can I, or the table, exist necessarily? My argument is that the intuitive picture is confused, and that once the confusion is dissolved, necessitism emerges as the most theoretically virtuous and logically coherent position available.

Williamson’s Master Argument (reconstructed) (1) Necessarily, if I exist, then necessarily something is identical to me. [I am essentially self-identical]
(2) I exist. [actuality]
(3) Therefore, necessarily, something is identical to me. [from 1, 2 by necessitation + existential generalisation]
(4) Therefore, necessarily I exist. [from 3, interpreting “something is identical to me” as existence]

Contingentists must reject a step. They typically reject step (3) — claiming that existential generalisation fails in modal contexts, i.e. that from “I am self-identical” we cannot infer “something is identical to me” when operators are involved. Williamson argues this requires a free logic whose costs exceed those of accepting necessitism.
i. the master argument in first-order quantified modal logic

Williamson’s master argument — NNE follows from logicThe argument is deceptively simple but its logical force is difficult to resist without significant cost. Let “I exist” be formalised as “∃y(y = Williamson).” The claim is that this is a necessary truth. Why? Because self-identity is a necessary truth — necessarily, Williamson = Williamson. But if necessarily Williamson = Williamson, then necessarily something = Williamson, which just is the claim that I necessarily exist. The contingentist must deny one of two very plausible logical steps: either (a) that self-identity is a necessary truth, or (b) that from a necessarily true identity we can existentially generalise to “something is identical to me.” Denying (a) is very strange. Denying (b) requires adopting a free logic in which ∃ does not straightforwardly range over everything quantified over by the universal quantifier. This is a significant departure from standard first-order logic that requires independent motivation.

ii. the barcan formula and constant domains

Barcan Formula — BF and CBF both valid under necessitism; simplest QMLThe Barcan Formula and its converse together say that quantifier domains are constant across possible worlds — whatever exists in any world exists in all worlds:

BF: ◊∃xφx → ∃x◊φx    (if something possibly φs, something actually φs)
CBF: ∃x◊φx → ◊∃xφx    (if something actually possibly φs, possibly something φs)

The simplest quantified modal logic — the one that validates both BF and CBF — is S5 with constant domains. This is the logic that falls out naturally from combining standard first-order logic with standard propositional modal logic. The contingentist, who needs varying domains, must adopt a more complex modal logic with variable domains, free variables, or inner and outer domains. My argument is that the theoretical virtues of logical simplicity and elegance systematically favour the necessitist position. We should adopt the simplest logic adequate to our needs, and the simplest is necessitism’s constant-domain S5.

iii. concreteness is not essential

Concreteness not essential — objects exist but are not always concreteThe most powerful intuitive objection is: surely I could have failed to exist. But I want to question the intuition. When we say “Williamson could have failed to exist,” what we are imagining is a world in which Williamson is not concrete — not embodied, not causally efficacious, not spatiotemporally located. We are not thereby imagining a world in which there is nothing that is Williamson. The contingentist assumes that concreteness is essential to an object — that the table essentially occupies space. But essentiality claims require independent argument, and I do not see why an object’s spatiotemporal location should be essential to it rather than accidental. In a world where the wood burned, there is still an object — the would-be table — that is nonconcrete, not located, not causally engaged. This object necessarily exists; it is merely contingently concrete. Concreteness varies across worlds; existence does not.

iv. higher-order logic and comprehension

Higher-order logic — comprehension principles validate necessitism; contingentism cannot accept themMy strongest argument for necessitism comes from higher-order logic. In higher-order quantified modal logic, we want unrestricted comprehension principles: for any formula φ, there exists a property that is instantiated by exactly the things satisfying φ. This allows us to quantify over properties and relations without restriction. But Williamson shows that if we accept comprehension in a setting where existence is contingent, we face severe difficulties: the property of being Williamson — of being identical to me — would have to be a property that exists contingently, which means that in worlds where I don’t exist, there is no such property to serve as my individual essence. Necessitism avoids this: in every world, the property of being identical to Williamson exists as an abstract property, even if Williamson is not concrete there. Comprehension is thus most naturally and powerfully defended from within a necessitist framework.

Thank you.

Argument I — The Master Argument: NNE follows from self-identity plus standard logic
1.Necessarily, Williamson = Williamson. [self-identity is a necessary truth for any object]
2.From (1), by existential generalisation: necessarily, ∃y(y = Williamson). [something is necessarily identical to Williamson]
3.∃y(y = x) just is what it means for x to exist; so (2) says Williamson necessarily exists.
∴Williamson (and by analogous arguments, everything) necessarily exists. Contingentists must deny existential generalisation in modal contexts — a significant departure from standard logic requiring independent motivation.
Argument II — BF + CBF: constant domains give the simplest quantified modal logic
1.The simplest QML combines standard first-order logic with propositional S5; this validates the Barcan Formula (◊∃xφx → ∃x◊φx) and its converse, requiring constant domains.
2.Constant-domain QML is the necessitist logic: whatever exists in any world exists in all worlds; quantifier domains do not vary.
3.The contingentist needs variable domains, inner/outer domain distinctions, or free logic — all of which introduce complexity that the necessitist avoids.
∴Logical simplicity — a theoretical virtue — favours necessitism: the constant-domain S5 is the cleanest, most powerful modal logic, and it entails necessitism.
Argument III — Concreteness is not essential: objects are contingently concrete, not contingently existent
1.When we say “Williamson could have failed to exist,” we are imagining a world where he is not concrete — not spatiotemporally located, not causally efficacious.
2.This does not require imagining a world where nothing is Williamson; it requires imagining a world where Williamson is nonconcrete.
3.Concreteness is not essential to an object; the intuition that “I could have failed to exist” is correct when read as “I could have been nonconcrete,” which is compatible with necessary existence.
∴The most natural reading of “Williamson could have failed to exist” is satisfied by necessary existence plus contingent concreteness; the intuition does not force contingentism.
Argument IV — Higher-order comprehension: necessitism is required for unrestricted principles
1.Unrestricted higher-order comprehension: for any formula φ, there exists a property instantiated by exactly the φs. This is a natural and powerful principle.
2.Under contingentism, the property of being identical to Williamson would not exist in worlds where Williamson does not — but such a property is needed for individual essence and for higher-order quantification over individual properties.
3.Necessitism allows the property of being-Williamson to exist in every world as an abstract property, making comprehension unrestricted and natural.
∴Higher-order unrestricted comprehension is most naturally defended within necessitism; contingentism is forced into restricted comprehension principles that complicate the theory of properties.

Mr President,

Bernard Linsky and I arrived at our position independently of Williamson, though we reach very similar conclusions. Where Williamson is willing to bite many bullets in defence of necessitism, we prefer to defend the same core logical thesis — that the Barcan Formula and its converse are both valid and that quantifier domains are constant across worlds — while attempting to preserve more of common sense about ordinary objects. We do this through the distinction between existence and concreteness, which we develop differently from Williamson.

“The Barcan Formula is not the monstrous implication that contingentism takes it to be. It says: if there could be something that φs, then something is such that it could φ. But why should we assume the domain of ‘something’ in the consequent is restricted to currently concrete objects? Once we allow that the quantifier ranges over all objects — including contingently nonconcrete ones — the formula is simply true.”
i. the simplest quantified modal logic

Simplest QML — BF + CBF natural in constant-domain S5Our 1994 paper argues that the simplest quantified modal logic — the one that falls out directly from combining standard predicate logic with standard propositional modal logic — validates both the Barcan Formula and its converse. When you take S5 and add first-order quantifiers in the most natural way, you get constant-domain semantics and therefore both BF and CBF. The contingentist who rejects these formulae must use a less natural, more complicated system — variable domains, free logic, or inner/outer domain distinctions. We argue that the cost in theoretical simplicity is not justified by the metaphysical benefit of preserving pre-theoretical intuitions. Those intuitions may themselves be confused, as Williamson argues.

ii. contingently nonconcrete objects

Contingently nonconcrete — objects exist necessarily but are concretely present contingentlyOur distinctive contribution is the notion of a contingently nonconcrete object. In the actual world, you and I are concrete: we are spatiotemporally located, causally engaged, the right candidates for mental and physical predicates. In worlds where our parents never met, we exist but are not concrete: we are abstract objects of a kind — not Platonic abstracta but rather objects that are at those worlds what purely possible individuals are sometimes said to be. We exist at every world, but we are only concrete at some. This distinction allows us to say that ordinary objects are contingent in a meaningful sense: they are contingently concrete. It is a contingent fact that I occupy space here and now. But it is not contingent that I exist — I exist in every possible world, just not concretely in all of them.

iii. how our view differs from williamson’s

Linsky/Zalta vs. Williamson — preserve common sense via concreteness/existence distinctionWilliamson is primarily motivated by logic and theoretical virtue. He defends necessitism because it makes the best modal logic, and he is willing to let the metaphysical chips fall where they may. We agree that the logic supports necessitism, but we want to make the position more palatable to common sense. We do this by saying: when the common person says “Williamson could have failed to exist,” they are expressing a true thought — the thought that Williamson could have been nonconcrete. We preserve this common-sense intuition by identifying “existence” in ordinary discourse with “concrete existence.” On this reading, contingentism is true of concrete objects; necessitism is true of objects simpliciter. This is not equivocation but a recognition that ordinary existence-talk is indexed to concreteness.

iv. against the actualist alternative

Against Plantinga actualism — individual essences have the same commitments as nonconcrete objectsPlantinga argues that we can avoid necessitism through individual essences: for each possible person, there is an abstract property — a haecceity — that is necessarily instantiated by that person if they exist. This allows Plantinga to quantify over possible people through their essences rather than through the people themselves. But we argue that this view has the same ontological commitments as ours, only more obscurely expressed. Plantinga’s haecceity of a merely possible person is an abstract property that exists necessarily and would be instantiated if the person were concrete. This is just our contingently nonconcrete object described in terms of the property that would identify it. The actualist’s essences are the necessitist’s nonconcrete objects in disguise; our view is more honest about what the ontological commitments actually are.

Thank you.

Argument I — Simplest QML: BF and CBF fall out of combining standard predicate logic with S5
1.The most natural combination of standard first-order predicate logic with propositional S5 yields constant-domain semantics.
2.Constant-domain semantics validates both the Barcan Formula and its converse; these are not imposed from outside but fall out naturally.
3.Rejecting BF and CBF requires a less natural system (variable domains, free logic, inner/outer domains) whose complications are not independently motivated.
∴The Barcan Formula and its converse are validated by the simplest QML; rejecting them complicates modal logic without theoretical payoff that justifies the cost.
Argument II — Contingently nonconcrete objects: existence and concreteness come apart
1.Concreteness (spatiotemporal location, causal engagement) and existence (being in the quantifier domain) are distinct properties.
2.Objects exist necessarily but are concretely present only contingently; in worlds where our parents never met, we exist as contingently nonconcrete objects.
3.This distinction preserves the truth of necessitism while accommodating the common-sense intuition that we are contingent: contingent in our concreteness, necessary in our existence.
∴The existence/concreteness distinction allows necessitism to be true without implying that everything is concrete at every world; contingently nonconcrete objects are the necessitist’s answer to merely possible objects.
Argument III — Against Plantinga: individual essences are contingently nonconcrete objects in disguise
1.Plantinga’s individual essences (haecceities) are abstract properties that necessarily exist and would be instantiated by a person if they were concrete.
2.This has the same ontological commitments as Linsky/Zalta’s contingently nonconcrete objects: both posit necessarily existing abstract entities that track merely possible people.
3.The necessitist ontology is more transparent: it acknowledges directly that there are contingently nonconcrete objects rather than hiding them behind property-language.
∴Plantinga’s actualism has the same ontological costs as necessitism but is less explicit about them; necessitism is the more honest and parsimonious expression of the same underlying commitments.
Argument IV — Common sense: ordinary existence-talk is indexed to concrete existence
1.When ordinary speakers say “Williamson could have failed to exist,” they express the true claim that he could have been nonconcrete.
2.This ordinary existence-talk is indexed to concrete existence; under this reading, contingentism is true of concrete existence, necessitism of existence simpliciter.
3.No equivocation is involved: identifying what ordinary discourse tracks (concreteness) with what strict metaphysics tracks (existence) is a legitimate disambiguation.
∴Linsky/Zalta preserve both necessitism and the truth of common-sense contingency claims; the apparent conflict dissolves once ordinary existence-talk is correctly identified as concrete-existence-talk.

Mr President,

I want to present myself as a somewhat unusual advocate for necessitism — unusual because I have long been associated with the view, developed from Prior’s work, that contingentism is the natural position for an actualist who takes modal logic seriously. My earlier papers with Prior defended versions of actualist reduction of possibilist discourse. I now think those reductions fail, and that necessitism is correct — not reluctantly, but enthusiastically. Let me explain why I changed my mind, and why I think anyone in their right mind should accept necessitism.

“Suppose I show you three blades and three handles and ask: how many possible knives can be made from these materials? Surely the answer is nine. Yet, according to the contingentist, the correct answer is zero, since no blade is attached to any handle and none of these possible knives exists. This strikes me as absurd — perhaps not as absurd as denying that there are chairs and tables, but absurd all the same.”
i. the possible knives argument

Possible knives — necessitism required for numerical truths about possible objectsThe possible knives argument is designed to show that ordinary quantification over possible objects is unavoidable and carries necessitist commitments. We ordinarily say “there are nine possible knives that can be made from these blades and handles.” This is a numerical claim: it quantifies over nine distinct objects, none of which currently exists as a concrete knife. The contingentist must say either: (a) this claim is strictly false — there are no possible knives, only the materials from which knives could be made; or (b) the claim must be paraphrased into a form that does not quantify over merely possible objects. Both responses are costly. Response (a) denies something we firmly believe and say in ordinary reasoning about possibilities. Response (b) requires a systematic paraphrase that has never been satisfactorily completed, as Prior’s and my own earlier work shows.

ii. the failure of proxy reduction

Proxy reduction fails — no successful actualist paraphrase of possibilist discourseThe actualist programme — which I spent many years developing with Prior — attempts to reduce possibilist discourse to actualist discourse by proxy: replacing talk of merely possible objects with talk of actual objects that represent them (individual essences, haecceities, abstract properties). I now believe this programme fails. The problem is that possibilist claims have genuine singular content about the possible objects themselves — about this possible knife, made from this blade and that handle — that cannot be captured by any general actualist property. When I say “this possible knife could have been the murder weapon,” I am saying something about a specific object; no property of the actual materials captures the identity of that specific possible knife. The proxy reduction replaces a claim about an object with a claim about a property, and the two are not equivalent.

iii. against prior’s tense logic response

Against Prior — A-theory tense logic does not save contingentismPrior famously argued that the presentist can handle modal talk through tense logic: talking about what will or might have been is not quantifying over non-present entities but using primitive tensed or modal operators. This is precisely the strategy I helped develop, and I now think it fails at a crucial point. The tense/modal operator strategy works for claims about what could have happened; it does not work for numerical claims about how many possible objects there are. “There might be a knife made from this blade and that handle” can be expressed using modal operators. “There are nine possible knives that could be made from these materials” resists this treatment, because it requires counting distinct possible objects — and counting requires quantification over them. Prior’s strategy cannot handle the numerical dimension of possibilist claims.

iv. fine’s positive view: essence and necessity

Fine’s essentialist necessitism — essence grounds what exists necessarilyMy view is that necessitism is correct and that it is grounded in an essentialist framework. Every object has an essence — a set of properties constitutive of what it is. For mathematical objects like numbers, the essence includes necessary existence: the number 7 exists necessarily because existence is part of what it is to be the number 7. For concrete objects like persons and tables, the essence does not include concreteness: I exist necessarily but am contingently concrete. The essentialist framework explains why necessitism is true: every object’s essence determines its existence conditions, and for most objects those existence conditions hold necessarily. This is a richer account than Williamson’s logical argument — it grounds necessitism in the nature of things rather than in the structure of modal logic.

Thank you.

Argument I — Possible knives: ordinary reasoning requires quantification over merely possible objects
1.We ordinarily say “there are nine possible knives that can be made from these blades and handles” — a numerical claim quantifying over nine distinct possible objects.
2.The contingentist must say this is strictly false (absurd) or that it reduces to an actualist paraphrase (unachievable); both responses are costly.
3.Necessitism can accept the claim at face value: there are nine possible knives, each existing necessarily but currently nonconcrete.
∴Ordinary quantification over possible objects is unavoidable; necessitism accommodates it naturally while contingentism is forced into denial or unsatisfying paraphrase.
Argument II — Proxy reduction fails: possibilist claims have irreducible singular content
1.The actualist programme attempts to replace talk of merely possible objects with talk of actual abstract entities (essences, haecceities) that proxy for them.
2.Possibilist claims have genuine singular content about specific possible objects; no general actualist property captures the identity of a specific possible knife made from these specific materials.
3.Replacing a claim about an object with a claim about a property is not a reduction but a change of subject; the proxy reduction fails to preserve the content of possibilist claims.
∴The actualist programme of proxy reduction is a failure; possibilist discourse has irreducible singular commitments that necessitism alone accommodates honestly.
Argument III — Against Prior: tense/modal operators cannot handle numerical possibilist claims
1.Prior’s modal operator strategy expresses “there might be a knife from these materials” without quantifying over possible objects; this works for existential claims.
2.Numerical claims (“there are nine possible knives”) require counting distinct possible objects; modal operators do not individuate the objects to be counted.
3.The operator strategy cannot handle the numerical dimension of possibilist reasoning; it fails for exactly the cases that most clearly require quantification over possible objects.
∴Prior’s tense/modal operator strategy fails for numerical possibilist claims; the most natural account of how many possible objects there are requires quantification that necessitism provides.
Argument IV — Essentialist necessitism: essence grounds necessary existence
1.Every object has an essence constitutive of what it is; for mathematical objects, existence is part of the essence; for concrete objects, concreteness is not.
2.Since concreteness is not essential to persons and tables, they exist necessarily and are merely contingently concrete — essentialist necessitism.
3.This essentialist grounding is richer than Williamson’s logical argument: it explains why necessitism is true in terms of the natures of things, not merely the structure of modal logic.
∴Essentialist necessitism provides the deepest ground for necessary existence: objects exist necessarily because their essences do not include concreteness, and essence determines existence conditions.

Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I want to defend contingentism from the perspective of formal logic and common sense simultaneously. Williamson argues that necessitism follows from standard logical principles — that rejecting it requires abandoning existential generalisation in modal contexts and adopting a “free logic.” I accept this. I accept free logic. And I want to argue that free logic is not the monstrous complication Williamson makes it out to be. It is a principled, well-motivated logical system that is independently recommended by considerations in the logic of existence entirely separate from modal metaphysics.

Free Logic A free logic is one that does not assume that every singular term refers to an existing object — it allows terms that fail to refer. In classical logic, from “Fa” we can infer “∃x(Fx)” — existential generalisation (EG). In free logic, EG fails: from “Pegasus is a winged horse” we cannot infer “something is a winged horse.” The cost: EG is restricted. The benefit: we can talk about non-existents without ontological commitment. Applied to modal contexts: from “Necessarily, Williamson = Williamson” we cannot infer “Necessarily, something = Williamson” without the additional premiss that Williamson exists — which in some possible worlds he does not.
i. the case for free logic independent of modality

Free logic independently motivated — empty terms, fiction, past objectsFree logic is independently recommended by considerations entirely separate from modal metaphysics. We routinely use singular terms that fail to refer: “Pegasus,” “the present King of France,” “Sherlock Holmes,” and — crucially for the modal case — “Socrates” and “Caesar” as I use them now, where both are dead. In a classical free logic for ordinary discourse, we want to say that “Sherlock Holmes is a detective” is false but meaningfully so, without committing us to the existence of a detective named Sherlock Holmes. Free logic handles this naturally. Williamson says that adopting free logic for modal contexts is a significant departure from standard logic. But free logic is used in philosophy of mathematics, philosophy of language, and the logic of fiction entirely independently of modal considerations. The “departure” is already made; extending it to modal logic is a natural next step, not an ad hoc manoeuvre.

ii. the barcan formula is not a logical truth

BF not a logical truth — valid only in constant-domain semantics, not a metaphysically neutral resultWilliamson argues that rejecting the Barcan Formula makes modal logic more complicated. But the Barcan Formula is not a logical truth in any philosophically neutral sense — it is valid only in constant-domain semantics, which assumes necessitism. The choice between constant-domain and variable-domain semantics is precisely the metaphysical choice between necessitism and contingentism. Williamson cannot argue from the validity of BF in constant-domain semantics to the truth of necessitism without begging the question: the semantics that makes BF valid just is the necessitist semantics. The question of which semantics is correct — constant or variable domain — is the metaphysical question we are debating. It cannot be settled by pointing out that BF is valid in the constant-domain semantics that necessitists prefer.

iii. the tense logic analogy

Tense logic analogy — modal operators handle merely possible objects as tense operators handle past objectsMy positive account is that merely possible objects are handled by modal operators in the same way that past objects are handled by tense operators. We do not say that past objects like Caesar and Socrates currently exist; we say that they existed. The past-tense operator “it was the case that” allows us to make true claims about past objects without quantifying over them in the present-tense domain. Analogously: merely possible objects do not exist; we say about them that they could have existed. The modal operator “it could have been the case that” allows us to make true claims about possible objects without quantifying over them in the actual domain. Fine objects that this fails for numerical claims; I respond that numerical claims about possible objects are true in the same sense that numerical claims about past objects are true — under the scope of the appropriate temporal or modal operator.

iv. what is wrong with nonconcrete objects

Nonconcrete objects — explanatory idle, causally inert, metaphysically mysteriousLinsky and Zalta’s contingently nonconcrete objects are in a world where my parents never met: a nonconcrete object that is me, without spatiotemporal location, without causal powers, without mental states. What is this object? What individuates it from other nonconcrete objects? What makes it me rather than some other nonconcrete entity? Williamson says it is the one that would be me if it were concrete. But this counterfactual identity claim presupposes haecceitist facts about transworld identity that are at least as mysterious as the contingentist’s merely possible objects. Nonconcrete objects are explanatorily idle — they do no causal or constitutive work — and metaphysically mysterious. The contingentist who says I simply would not have existed in those worlds is making a cleaner, more intelligible claim. No mysterious nonconcrete entity is required.

Thank you.

Argument I — Free logic is independently motivated: not an ad hoc response to necessitism
1.Free logic is independently recommended by the logic of empty terms (fictional names, definite descriptions, past objects) entirely separate from modal metaphysics.
2.Adopting free logic for modal contexts is a natural extension of its application in philosophy of language and logic of fiction, not a desperate ad hoc manoeuvre.
3.The cost of free logic — restricting existential generalisation — is already paid in other domains; the modal application adds no new cost.
∴Free logic is independently motivated and the contingentist’s adoption of it for modal contexts is principled, not an ad hoc response to Williamson’s argument.
Argument II — BF is not metaphysically neutral: it is valid only in constant-domain (necessitist) semantics
1.The Barcan Formula is valid in constant-domain semantics and invalid in variable-domain semantics; the choice between these semantics is precisely the metaphysical choice between necessitism and contingentism.
2.Williamson cannot argue from BF’s validity in constant-domain semantics to the truth of necessitism without presupposing necessitism.
3.The simplicity argument for necessitism begs the question: the semantics that makes the logic simple just is the necessitist semantics.
∴BF is not a metaphysically neutral logical truth; its validity depends on accepting the necessitist semantics, making the simplicity argument for necessitism question-begging.
Argument III — The tense logic analogy: modal operators handle merely possible objects
1.Past objects (Caesar, Socrates) are handled by tense operators (“it was the case that”) without requiring that they exist now; past-tensed claims are true without quantifying over present-domain objects.
2.By analogy, merely possible objects are handled by modal operators (“it could have been the case that”) without requiring that they exist actually; modal claims are true without quantifying over actual-domain objects.
3.The modal-operator strategy is the systematic analogon of the temporal-operator strategy for past objects; if tense operators handle past objects, modal operators handle merely possible objects.
∴The tense logic analogy provides a principled contingentist account of merely possible objects that avoids quantifying over them; the modal-operator strategy is as well-motivated as the tense-operator strategy for past objects.
Argument IV — Nonconcrete objects are explanatorily idle and metaphysically mysterious
1.Linsky/Zalta’s nonconcrete objects lack spatiotemporal location, causal powers, and mental states; they do no causal or constitutive work.
2.What individuates nonconcrete objects from each other requires haecceitist transworld identity facts that are at least as mysterious as the contingentist’s merely possible objects.
3.The contingentist’s simple denial — in worlds where my parents never met, I simply would not have existed — is cleaner and more intelligible than positing mysterious nonconcrete entities.
∴Nonconcrete objects are explanatorily idle and presuppose haecceitist identity facts that are no less mysterious than contingentism; necessitism does not achieve genuine parsimony.

Mr President,

I want to defend actualism: the view that there are no merely possible objects — no non-existent things that might have existed. Everything that exists actually exists; possible worlds are not places populated by merely possible entities but are instead maximal possible states of affairs — maximal consistent ways the world could have been. This is the correct metaphysics of modality, and it is not committed to necessitism. I can accommodate all the modal truths that necessitists want to accommodate, without positing necessarily existing abstract surrogates for ordinary objects.

“There is no Thomas Aquinas who exists in this world but merely lacks concrete properties. In possible worlds where Aquinas’ parents never met, Aquinas does not exist. What does exist, in every possible world, is Aquinas’s individual essence: the property of being Aquinas, which is necessarily coinstantiated with him whenever it is instantiated at all.”
i. individual essences and actualist modal semantics

Individual essences — haecceities exist necessarily and ground transworld identityMy solution to the problem of merely possible objects is individual essences — what I call haecceities. For every actually existing object x, there is a property that is essentially individuative of x: the property of being x, which is necessarily such that it is instantiated by something if and only if that something is x. This haecceity exists in every possible world, even in worlds where x does not exist. In those worlds, the haecceity is uninstantiated but it is present. This allows me to make true claims about what would be the case if x had existed without quantifying over a nonconcrete version of x. The haecceity is the locus of modal facts about x in worlds where x does not exist; x itself is not.

ii. possible worlds as maximal consistent states of affairs

Possible worlds as states of affairs — not domains of objects but consistent descriptionsPossible worlds, on my view, are not domains populated by objects — actual, concrete, or nonconcrete. They are maximal consistent abstract states of affairs: maximally specific ways the world could have been. A state of affairs is possible if it could have obtained; it is actual if it does obtain. A possible world is a maximal possible state of affairs. This account is fully actualist: all the entities invoked — states of affairs, properties, propositions — are actual abstract objects that exist necessarily. There are no merely possible concrete objects and no nonconcrete objects that might become concrete. There are only actual abstract objects that represent possible configurations of actual and possible individuals through their essences.

iii. against williamson on haecceities

Against Williamson — haecceities are not unexemplified essences; Fine’s objection answeredWilliamson and Fine object to haecceities by arguing that they are problematic: a haecceity of a merely possible individual would be an uninstantiated essence with no object to be the essence of. But this objection misunderstands my account. Haecceities are not essences of non-existent objects; they are properties that necessarily co-instantiate with actual objects when instantiated. In worlds where Williamson’s parents never met, the property of being-Williamson exists as an abstract necessary entity, uninstantiated but present. It is not the essence of a nonconcrete Williamson; it is a property defined by the actual Williamson’s identity that is present in every possible world in virtue of its abstract necessary existence. Fine’s objection conflates the property with a possible individual; they are distinct.

iv. what distinguishes actualism from necessitism

Actualism vs. necessitism — distinct commitments; Plantinga preserves contingency of ordinary objectsNecessitism says that ordinary objects like Williamson and this table exist necessarily, albeit nonconcretely at some worlds. Actualism says that ordinary objects are contingent: Williamson might not have existed, full stop. What exists necessarily are abstract entities: properties, states of affairs, propositions, and among properties the individual essences that track ordinary objects across possible worlds. The crucial difference: on necessitism, there is a nonconcrete Williamson in worlds where his parents never met; on actualism, there is no Williamson at all in those worlds, only the property of being-Williamson. This is a substantive difference. Actualism respects the common-sense view that ordinary objects are genuinely contingent, while necessitism preserves ordinary objects in watered-down nonconcrete form. I maintain that genuine contingency is the more faithful account of what common sense intends.

Mr President,

I want to press two arguments against Williamson that complement what Prior and Plantinga have said. First, a parity argument: Williamson claims that necessitism is theoretically simpler and that this gives us reason to prefer it. I argue that contingentism is no worse off than necessitism on the simplicity dimension, and that the parity between them means simplicity cannot decide the debate. Second, a cardinality argument: Williamson’s commitment to the Barcan Formula, combined with plausible recombination principles, generates an implausible result about the number of necessary existents.

i. the parity argument

Parity argument — contingentism is no simpler or more complex than necessitismLouis deRosset and I have argued that contingentism and necessitism are on a par with respect to the relation between possible worlds semantics and modal reality. Williamson argues that necessitism provides a clearer account of the intended interpretation of QML: the quantifier domain is fixed across all worlds at a single set D. But necessitists themselves cannot take D to be a set of all objects, because there is no set of all objects (Russell’s paradox). Williamson must “pretend” that the model represents the intended interpretation even though no single set contains all necessitist objects. Contingentists can construct intended inhabited structures with the same degree of approximation. The intended-interpretation story is no cleaner for the necessitist than for the contingentist; the claimed simplicity advantage disappears on examination.

ii. the cardinality paradox for williamson

Cardinality paradox — BF + recombination implies too many necessary existentsMy 2009 paper identifies a paradox for Williamson. Williamson accepts the Barcan Formula: if something could exist, it does exist. If we also accept a plausible recombination principle — that for any objects and any arrangement of them, there is a possible world in which objects of those types exist in that arrangement — then Williamson is committed to there being more actually existing objects than there are natural numbers, more than there are real numbers, indeed more than any cardinal number could number. The argument: there is no limit to how many distinct objects could possibly exist (by recombination); by BF, all these possible objects actually exist; so the number of actually existing objects exceeds every cardinality. This is a set-theoretic problem that Williamson has not straightforwardly answered.

iii. the costs of free logic are manageable

Free logic costs — manageable and independently recommended; not a decisive objection to contingentismWilliamson argues that free logic, which contingentists must adopt to block his master argument, carries significant costs: it restricts existential generalisation, complicates the logical system, and is not the “natural” logic. But as Prior has argued, free logic is independently recommended. More importantly: the costs of free logic must be weighed against the costs of necessitism — nonconcrete objects, mysterious haecceitist identity facts, the cardinality problem, and the violation of the natural intuition that I could simply have failed to exist. When these costs are compared, free logic does not obviously lose. The contingentist’s logical complications are at the level of the proof theory; the necessitist’s metaphysical complications are at the level of ontology. Neither set of complications is obviously preferable.

iv. higher-order comprehension: the symmetry argument

Higher-order symmetry — contingentists can construct intended structures as well as necessitistsWilliamson argues that necessitists can accept unrestricted higher-order comprehension while contingentists cannot — that necessitism’s advantage at the higher-order level constitutes a decisive theoretical reason for it. DeRosset’s careful argument shows this is wrong. The contingentist can construct intended inhabited structures for higher-order QML that are exactly as adequate as the necessitist’s, with the same approximations and the same pretences required. The higher-order advantage that Williamson claims dissolves under careful formal examination: either both the necessitist and the contingentist can give clear accounts of the relation between QML models and modal reality, or neither can. The claimed asymmetry does not exist.
