Anti-Fundamentalism & Simulacrum AccountNancy Cartwright, 1983
The fundamental laws of physics — Newton’s law of gravitation, Coulomb’s law, the Schrödinger equation — are not true descriptions of how things behave in the real world. They are true only of idealised objects in models. Phenomenological laws, by contrast, are both true and empirically grounded. The correct account of explanation is the simulacrum account: we explain by constructing models, not by deriving from universal laws.

Chair,

I want to be precise about what I am claiming, because my view is radical but not nihilistic. I am not saying that physics is worthless, or that we should abandon fundamental theory. I am saying something more careful and more disturbing: that the fundamental laws of physics — those elegant, unifying equations that physicists revere as the deepest truths about nature — are not literally true descriptions of how things actually behave in the real world.

This is not a lament. It is a diagnosis. And the diagnosis is grounded not in sceptical philosophy but in a close examination of how physics is actually practised.

The central distinction: phenomenological versus fundamental laws.

Phenomenological laws are about observable regularities. They describe what actually happens in specific, controlled experimental conditions. They are derived from data, tested against measurements, and revised when they fail. They are, in the main, true.

Fundamental laws — Newton’s law of gravitation, Coulomb’s law, the Schrödinger equation, Maxwell’s equations — are different in kind. They are not inductive summaries of observed regularities. They are powerful, unifying theoretical structures. And it is precisely their power and unifying elegance that is the source of their falsity: to achieve generality, they abstract away the very features of the world that make the phenomenological laws true.
i. the truth-explanation trade-off

Truth–explanation trade-off — fundamental laws explain by losing truth; phenomenological laws are true but localHere is the core paradox I am pressing. We have two kinds of laws, and they trade off against each other. Phenomenological laws — Galileo’s law of free fall, Ohm’s law, the ideal gas law — are true of real physical systems in real experimental situations. But they are also local and limited: they apply only to the restricted class of systems they were designed to describe. Fundamental laws, by contrast, are magnificently general. Newton’s gravitational law tells us something about every massive body in the universe. The Schrödinger equation purports to govern every quantum system. But this generality is purchased at the cost of truth. F = Gm⊂1;m⊂2;/r² does not accurately describe the motion of any real planet, because no real planet is subject only to gravitational attraction: there are electromagnetic effects, thermal radiation, the gravitational pull of other bodies. The law applies literally only to a two-body system in a perfect vacuum with no other forces — and no such system exists. The law gains its sweeping generality by describing a fiction.

ii. idealization and the simulacrum account

Simulacrum account — explanation is model-building, not derivation from laws; models have form without substance of real systemsThe standard view of scientific explanation is the covering-law model, derived from Hempel: to explain a phenomenon is to derive it from a true law plus initial conditions. I reject this account. I propose instead what I call the simulacrum account. The word “simulacrum” is carefully chosen: it means something having the form or appearance of a thing without its substance. On the simulacrum account, to explain a phenomenon is to construct a model — an idealised representation — that fits the phenomenon into a theoretical framework. The fundamental laws are true of the objects in the model. But the objects of the model are not real objects: they are idealised abstractions that have only the “form or appearance” of real things. We explain the motion of a pendulum by modelling it as a simple harmonic oscillator with a massless, inextensible string and a point mass bob. No real pendulum is like this. But the model captures enough of the real system to be explanatorily useful. The fundamental laws are not lies about the real pendulum; they are truths about the idealised model of the pendulum. The lie is in thinking the model is a faithful representation of reality.

iii. ceteris paribus and the problem of the missing physics

Ceteris paribus — fundamental laws true only with hedging conditions that are never satisfied; they describe no real systemsDefenders of the standard view will say: of course F = Gm⊂1;m⊂2;/r² is idealized; the full law includes a ceteris paribus clause — all other things being equal. Very well. But then I ask: equal to what? The ceteris paribus clause is a promissory note for the rest of the physics. The law is true when all other forces are absent, when the bodies are perfectly spherical, when spacetime curvature is negligible. But in the real world, all other things are never equal. The conditions under which the fundamental law is strictly and literally true are conditions that are never instantiated in nature. The law applies to an infinite set of possible but non-actual systems. This is not a minor technical qualification. It means that the law, taken literally with its ceteris paribus clause, describes the behaviour of idealised ghost objects, not real ones. When the conditions are right for the law to be true, we are no longer in the real world.

iv. composition of causes and the problem of vector addition

Composition of causes — component forces are theoretical fictions; only resultant force is real; Cartwright’s classic objection to superpositionConsider the simplest case of multiple forces: a charged body subject to both gravitational and electromagnetic attraction. We have two fundamental laws, one for each force. We compose them by vector addition to get the resultant force. But what is the ontological status of the component forces? Neither force acts separately on the body: what acts is the resultant. The component forces are theoretical constructs introduced to make the mathematics tractable. They do not individually describe any real force acting on any real object. The covering-law account says we explain the body’s motion by deriving it from the two laws. But both laws, taken individually, describe a situation that does not exist — a body subject to only one of the forces. The explanation proceeds through a fiction. We use laws that are false of the actual situation to derive the true phenomenological result. This is not explanation by true laws; it is explanation by theoretical scaffolding that is dismantled once the phenomenological result is in hand.

v. entity realism against law realism

Entity realism — electrons are real; Coulomb’s law is not true of real electrons; realism about entities is consistent with anti-realism about lawsMy position is sometimes misread as global anti-realism. It is not. I am a realist about theoretical entities. Electrons are real. Electromagnetic fields are real. We have excellent grounds for believing in them: we use them as tools to manipulate other parts of the world. Ian Hacking’s argument for entity realism — if you can spray them, they are real — is one I endorse. What I am anti-realist about is the theoretical laws that are supposed to govern these entities. Coulomb’s law describes the force between two point charges in a perfect vacuum. No electron is a point charge; no space is a perfect vacuum. The law is true of a theoretical fiction, not of real electrons. We can believe in electrons without believing that Coulomb’s law accurately describes their behaviour in the real world. Entity realism and law anti-realism are not only compatible; they are jointly supported by attention to scientific practice.

vi. the dappled world: against fundamentalism

Dappled world — nature is disunified; no single set of laws covers all phenomena; patchwork of local truths rather than universal theoryBehind the specific arguments about idealization and composition lies a deeper claim about the nature of reality itself. The fundamentalist believes in what I call the image of the “vending machine” universe: you put in the initial conditions, out comes the trajectory, governed throughout by a small set of universal laws. I deny this image. The world is dappled — a patchwork of different kinds of systems governed by different local regularities, with no single overarching set of true laws that covers them all. Different domains — solid-state physics, fluid dynamics, population genetics, macroeconomics — require different models, different mathematical frameworks, different causal concepts. The fundamentalist dream of a single theory of everything that generates true descriptions of all phenomena is not a scientific achievement waiting to happen; it is a philosophical prejudice about what the world must be like.

vii. the radiometer and the failure of first-principles derivation

Radiometer case — Maxwell’s kinetic theory cannot derive the actual radiometer behaviour from first principles; phenomenological models fill the gapConsider the radiometer — the device with vanes that spin in partial vacuum when light falls on them. Maxwell himself struggled to account for its behaviour from first principles using kinetic theory. The problem is that the actual behaviour of the radiometer depends on a complex interplay of gas pressure, mean free path, thermal gradients, and surface properties. No derivation from the fundamental laws of kinetic theory gives the right result without a battery of additional assumptions, approximations, and model-specific parameters. Scientists who understand radiometers do not work from first principles down. They work from the phenomenological behaviour up, constructing models that fit the observed regularities. The fundamental theory provides a loose framework; the real explanatory work is done by the phenomenological model. This is not an isolated failure; it is the normal condition of physics.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The truth–explanation trade-off: fundamental laws achieve generality by sacrificing descriptive truth
1.Phenomenological laws (Galileo’s law, Ohm’s law, ideal gas law) are true of real systems but local and limited in scope.
2.Fundamental laws (Newton’s law of gravitation, Schrödinger equation) achieve sweeping generality by abstracting away the very features of real systems that make phenomenological laws true.
3.F = Gm⊂1;m⊂2;/r² does not accurately describe any real planet, which is subject to electromagnetic effects, other gravitational bodies, and thermal radiation. The law applies literally only to a two-body system in a vacuum — a system that does not exist.
∴The generality of fundamental laws is purchased at the cost of descriptive truth. The more general the law, the more idealized the objects it describes, and the further those objects are from anything real.
Argument II — The simulacrum account: explanation proceeds through idealized models, not from true fundamental laws
1.The covering-law model (Hempel): to explain is to derive from true laws plus initial conditions. But the laws used in physics explanations are not true of real systems.
2.Simulacrum account: to explain is to construct a model — an idealised object — such that the fundamental laws are true of the model, and the model captures enough of the real system’s structure to license a phenomenological description. The model has the “form or appearance” of the real system without its substance.
3.Explanation is a relation between a model and a real system, mediated by the theory. The theory is not directly applied to reality; it is applied to the idealised model. The covering-law account is therefore wrong about the structure of scientific explanation.
∴Scientific explanation is simulacrum explanation: we explain by building models that fit real phenomena into a theoretical framework. The fundamental laws are true of the model, not of the world. The covering-law account misrepresents the actual structure of scientific practice.
Argument III — The ceteris paribus problem: the conditions under which fundamental laws are strictly true are never instantiated
1.Any defence of the literal truth of fundamental laws must add ceteris paribus clauses: “all other things being equal,” “in the absence of disturbing factors,” etc.
2.The conditions specified by these clauses — perfect isolation, point masses, no other forces — are never satisfied in nature. The law is strictly true only of systems that do not exist.
3.A law that is true only under conditions that are never instantiated does not describe any real state of the world. It describes a theoretical fiction.
∴The ceteris paribus defence of fundamental laws fails: it saves the truth of the laws only by restricting their domain to systems that do not exist in nature. The laws are vacuously or fictionally true, not genuinely true of the real world.
Argument IV — Composition of causes: component forces are theoretical fictions; only the resultant force is real
1.When a body is subject to both gravitational and electromagnetic forces, we explain its motion by composing them via vector addition. But neither component force acts separately on the body; what acts is the resultant.
2.Each component force law (Newton’s, Coulomb’s) describes a situation that does not exist — a body subject to only one force. The laws are individually false of the actual situation.
3.We use these individually false laws to derive a true phenomenological result via mathematical composition. The explanation proceeds through theoretical scaffolding (the component forces) that is dismantled once the phenomenological result is obtained.
∴The composition of causes argument shows that covering-law explanation in multi-force cases proceeds through laws that are individually false of the actual system. The explanatory success is real but does not vindicate the literal truth of the fundamental laws.
Argument V — Entity realism without law realism: realism about electrons is consistent with anti-realism about Coulomb’s law
1.Grounds for believing in entities: we use them as tools to manipulate other parts of the world. If you can spray electrons, they are real. This justification is causal and experimental, not theoretical.
2.Grounds for disbelieving in the literal truth of fundamental laws: Coulomb’s law is true only of point charges in a perfect vacuum — idealized objects that do not exist. The justification for law-belief is theoretical, and the idealisation undermines the claim to truth.
3.Entity realism and law anti-realism are independently motivated by different features of scientific practice and are jointly consistent.
∴Entity realism and law anti-realism are jointly defensible. Belief in electrons is well-grounded in experimental manipulability; disbelief in the literal truth of Coulomb’s law is well-grounded in the idealized character of its domain of application.
Argument VI — The dappled world: the fundamentalist dream of a single covering theory is a philosophical prejudice, not a scientific result
1.Different scientific domains (solid-state physics, fluid dynamics, ecology, economics) require different models, mathematical frameworks, and causal concepts. There is no evidence that these domains reduce to a single set of fundamental laws.
2.The fundamentalist image — initial conditions in, trajectory out, governed by universal laws — is a philosophical prejudice about what the world must be like, not a demonstrated scientific fact.
3.The world is more plausibly “dappled”: a patchwork of locally governed regularities with no single overarching true theory.
∴Fundamentalism — the view that a single set of universal laws covers all phenomena — is a metaphysical prejudice unsupported by scientific evidence. The dappled world is the more accurate image: different domains governed by different local laws, with no guarantee of unification.
Argument VII — The radiometer case: actual explanatory work is done by phenomenological models, not first-principles derivation
1.Maxwell could not derive the radiometer’s behaviour from first principles using kinetic theory. The actual behaviour depends on a complex interplay of gas pressure, mean free path, and surface effects that resist derivation from fundamental laws alone.
2.Scientists who understand radiometers work from phenomenological behaviour up, constructing models that fit observed regularities. The fundamental theory provides loose scaffolding; the real explanatory work is done phenomenologically.
3.This is not an isolated failure but the normal condition of fundamental physics applied to real systems. First-principles derivation is an idealised description of scientific practice, not an accurate one.
∴The radiometer case illustrates the general point: explanatory work in physics is done by phenomenological models, not by deduction from true fundamental laws. The covering-law model misrepresents the actual structure of physical explanation.

Cartwright’s argument about the truth of fundamental laws intersects with a broader question I have pressed in The Scientific Image and Laws and Symmetry: what should the aim of science be, and what is the epistemic status of theoretical laws? My answer is constructive empiricism, and it converges with Cartwright’s diagnosis, though from a different angle.

“Science aims to give us theories which are empirically adequate; and acceptance of a theory involves as belief only that it is empirically adequate.” — van Fraassen, 1980
i. empirical adequacy vs. truth

Empirical adequacy — science aims at saving phenomena, not at true theories; belief in truth of laws goes beyond what evidence warrantsThe scientific realist claims that the aim of science is truth: that our best theories are approximately true descriptions of the world, including its unobservable features. I deny this. The aim of science is empirical adequacy: having theories that correctly describe all observable phenomena. Accepting a theory is not believing it is true; it is believing that it saves the phenomena — that its empirical consequences are correct. The realist goes beyond the evidence. We have excellent grounds for accepting theories as empirically adequate; we have no grounds for believing they are true of unobservable structures, including the laws that purportedly govern them. The history of science is a graveyard of theories that were once empirically successful but are now known to be false. The cautious conclusion is that empirical success is a poor guide to truth.

ii. the bad lot argument and inference to the best explanation

Bad lot argument — IBE does not license truth-belief; best of bad hypotheses may still be false; no reason to think true theory is in our hypothesis spaceThe realist’s main argument for the truth of fundamental laws is inference to the best explanation: our fundamental laws are the best explanation of the empirical success of physics, therefore they are approximately true. I call this the “bad lot” problem. Even if our current fundamental laws are the best explanation among the hypotheses we have considered, we have no reason to think the true theory is among them. We may be choosing the best of a bad lot. The history of science confirms this: Newtonian mechanics was the best explanation of an enormous range of phenomena, yet it turned out to be false. The inference from “best available explanation” to “approximately true” is not licensed unless we have independent reason to think the true theory is among the candidates, which we never do.

iii. laws as structural features of models

Laws as model-features — laws characterize mathematical structures; no commitment to their truth of unobservable world required; semantic view supports thisOn the semantic view of theories, which I develop and defend, a scientific theory is not a set of sentences but a collection of models — mathematical structures. The “laws” of a theory characterize the class of models that belong to it. To accept the theory is to believe that one of its models is isomorphic to some real system at the observable level. There is no commitment to the claim that the structural features of the models — the “laws” — describe the deep structure of unobservable reality. The laws are features of our representational tools; they need not be truths about the world. This is entirely consistent with using the laws as practically effective tools for prediction and intervention, which is what scientific practice actually requires.

iv. the observable/unobservable distinction and law anti-realism

Observable/unobservable — fundamental laws describe unobservable structures; warranted only at observable level; anti-realism about laws is principled, not scepticalMy anti-realism about fundamental laws is not a blanket scepticism. It is principled and discriminating. I am a thoroughgoing realist about observables: the experimental data of physics are real, the observed regularities are real, the phenomenological laws that describe them are approximately true. What I am sceptical about are the unobservable structures — the theoretical posits, the fundamental field equations, the symmetry groups — that the realist claims to know. This scepticism is not arbitrary: it reflects a principled epistemological distinction between what we can directly confirm and what we can only infer by theoretical reasoning that goes beyond the evidence. Fundamental laws describe these unobservable structures. The evidence for their truth runs out at the observable boundary.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Empirical adequacy is the proper aim of science; belief in the truth of fundamental laws goes beyond what evidence warrants
1.The evidence available for any theory consists in its observable consequences. Any theory that correctly saves all observable phenomena is empirically adequate.
2.Two theories that are empirically equivalent but differ in their claims about unobservable structures (including fundamental laws) are equally well supported by all evidence. No evidence can discriminate between them.
3.Accepting a theory as true (rather than empirically adequate) involves believing its claims about unobservable structures, which are underdetermined by the evidence. This is epistemically unjustified.
∴Belief in the truth of fundamental laws is epistemically unjustified: it goes beyond what the evidence supports. Empirical adequacy is the appropriate epistemic aim; accepting a theory as empirically adequate is all that scientific rationality requires.
Argument II — The bad lot argument: IBE does not license truth-belief in fundamental laws
1.Realist argument: our fundamental laws are the best explanation of the empirical success of physics; therefore they are approximately true (inference to the best explanation, IBE).
2.Bad lot objection: even if our laws are the best among considered hypotheses, we have no reason to think the true theory is among them. We may be choosing the best of a bad lot. The inference from “best available” to “approximately true” is not warranted.
3.Historical confirmation: Newtonian mechanics was the best explanation of an enormous range of phenomena and turned out to be false. IBE is unreliable as a guide to truth about unobservable structures.
∴IBE does not license truth-belief in fundamental laws; the bad lot problem shows that even the best available explanation may be false; the realist inference from explanatory success to approximate truth is not warranted.
Argument III — On the semantic view, laws are structural features of models; acceptance requires no commitment to their truth of unobservable reality
1.A scientific theory is a collection of models — mathematical structures. The “laws” of a theory characterize the models belonging to it, not sentences about the world.
2.Accepting a theory is believing one of its models is isomorphic to some real system at the observable level. No commitment to the deep structural truth of the laws is required.
3.Laws are features of representational tools; they can be practically effective and epistemically valuable as such without being truths about unobservable reality.
∴On the semantic view of theories, laws are structural features of models, not truth-apt sentences about unobservable reality. Accepting a theory involves no commitment to the truth of its laws beyond the observable domain.
Argument IV — The observable/unobservable distinction grounds principled anti-realism about fundamental laws
1.Observables can be directly confirmed. Fundamental laws describe unobservable structures: field equations, symmetry groups, quantum amplitudes. These are confirmed only by indirect theoretical inference.
2.Indirect theoretical inference goes beyond the evidence in a principled, discriminable way. The evidence runs out at the observable boundary.
3.Realism about observables and anti-realism about unobservable laws is therefore a principled, discriminating position, not a blanket scepticism.
∴The observable/unobservable distinction grounds principled anti-realism about fundamental laws: we are warranted in believing claims about observables but not in believing the unobservable structural claims of fundamental theory.

In Science Without Laws I develop a position that is complementary to Cartwright’s from a different philosophical angle. My claim is that the very concept of a “law of nature” — in the traditional sense of a universal, exceptionless, metaphysically necessary regularity — is philosophically empty and scientifically unnecessary. Scientists do not discover laws; they construct models.

i. the model-based view of science

Model-based science — scientists construct models of limited aspects of the world; laws-talk is shorthand for model claims, not metaphysical commitmentsThe dominant image of science in philosophy is law-based: science discovers universal regularities and formulates them as laws. I argue that this image is mistaken. What scientists actually do is construct models — abstract theoretical structures that represent specific aspects of specific systems. A textbook statement like “F = ma” is not a universal law applying to all matter everywhere; it is a claim about a family of models. To say Newton’s second law is true is to say that certain mechanical models accurately represent certain physical systems in certain respects and to certain degrees of accuracy. The law-language is a convenient shorthand for a complex system of claims about how well-constructed models represent real systems. Strip away the rhetorical inflation, and there are no laws — only models of varying fidelity and scope.

ii. perspective and the limitations of representation

Perspectivism — models represent from a perspective; no model captures all features of a system; the perspective is never view-from-nowhereAll scientific models represent from a perspective. A model of planetary motion represents the solar system as a gravitational system, ignoring electromagnetic effects and relativistic corrections that are irrelevant at the intended scale of accuracy. A model of electrical circuits represents a network of conductors and resistances, ignoring the quantum mechanical processes that underlie conductance. Models are like maps: they represent selected features of reality, at a chosen scale, for a particular purpose. No map is the territory; no model is the world. The perspective is always chosen — by the purposes of the investigation, the mathematical tools available, the phenomena deemed relevant. There is no “view from nowhere” that would give us the one true law-governed description of reality.

iii. against the metaphysics of laws

Against laws metaphysics — Humean regularity view and necessitarian views both fail; philosophers cannot agree on what laws are; scientists need no such conceptPhilosophers have proposed two main accounts of laws: Humean regularity accounts (laws are just true universal regularities, nothing more) and necessitarian accounts (laws are grounded in objective necessity, natural kinds, or dispositions). Both are problematic. The Humean account makes laws extensionally equivalent to any accidental true generalization; it cannot distinguish laws from coincidences. The necessitarian account invokes metaphysical necessities that go well beyond anything science can establish. Neither account is scientifically well-grounded. Scientists themselves manage perfectly well without the concept of a law of nature: they talk about models, equations, symmetries, and mechanisms. The philosophical concept of a law is excess metaphysical baggage that can be safely discarded.

iv. local models and the disunity of science

Disunity — different domains require incommensurable models; reduction to unified fundamental theory is a myth; disunity is the actual structure of scienceScience is not a unified pyramid with fundamental physics at the base. It is a collection of overlapping, partially inconsistent, domain-specific models. The models of quantum field theory, general relativity, statistical mechanics, fluid dynamics, and evolutionary biology cannot all be derived from a single unified set of fundamental laws. Attempts at unification consistently produce new models with restricted domains of applicability, not the universal laws the fundamentalist dreams of. The disunity of science is not a temporary deficiency to be overcome; it reflects the actual structure of nature, which is itself a patchwork of different kinds of systems, mechanisms, and regularities. Cartwright is right: the world is dappled, and our models accurately reflect that dappling.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The model-based view: laws-talk is shorthand for claims about models, not metaphysical commitments to universal regularities
1.“F = ma” is not a universal law applying to all matter everywhere; it is a claim about a family of mechanical models that represent certain systems in certain respects.
2.To say a law is “true” is to say that certain models accurately represent certain systems at a certain level of approximation. The truth-claim is about model-world fit, not about universal necessity.
3.Laws-language is a convenient rhetorical inflation of claims about models. Once the inflation is stripped away, there are no laws — only models of varying fidelity and scope.
∴Laws-talk in science is best understood as shorthand for complex claims about how well models represent real systems; it carries no metaphysical commitment to universal, exceptionless regularities; the concept of a “law of nature” is scientifically unnecessary.
Argument II — Models represent from a perspective; no model captures all features; there is no view-from-nowhere
1.All scientific models represent selected features of a system, at a chosen scale, for a particular purpose. The selection is always perspectival: made by the purposes and tools of the investigation.
2.There is no model that captures all features of any system; no map is the territory. The perspective is never the “view from nowhere” that would give us the one true law-governed description.
3.The realist demand for the one true fundamental theory is a demand for the view from nowhere — a philosophically incoherent ideal that science cannot and does not attempt to realise.
∴Scientific representation is irreducibly perspectival; the demand for a non-perspectival “true theory” from which all phenomena follow is incoherent; the model-based view is the accurate description of scientific practice.
Argument III — The metaphysics of laws is philosophically unresolvable; scientists need no such concept
1.Humean regularity accounts cannot distinguish laws from accidental generalizations. Necessitarian accounts invoke metaphysical necessities that science cannot establish.
2.The philosophical debate about what laws “really are” is unresolved after decades of sustained effort. This suggests the concept is defective.
3.Scientists manage perfectly well using the language of models, equations, symmetries, and mechanisms. The philosophical concept of a law of nature is excess metaphysical baggage that scientific practice does not require.
∴The concept of a law of nature is philosophically unresolvable and scientifically dispensable; the model-based view of science needs no such concept; talk of “laws” should be understood as a loose and metaphysically unloaded shorthand.
Argument IV — The disunity of science: incommensurable domain-specific models reflect the actual patchwork structure of nature
1.Quantum field theory, general relativity, statistical mechanics, and evolutionary biology cannot all be derived from a single set of fundamental laws. Attempts at unification produce new domain-specific models, not universal laws.
2.The disunity of science is not a temporary deficiency but reflects the actual structure of nature: different domains are governed by different local regularities, mechanisms, and capacities.
3.The fundamentalist dream of a single unified theory is a philosophical prejudice not supported by the actual history and practice of science.
∴The disunity of science is real and reflects the patchwork character of nature; the fundamentalist ideal of unification is not a scientific achievement in progress but a philosophical projection unsupported by the actual development of science.

My argument in Representing and Intervening distinguishes two different questions that are run together in the scientific realism debate: the question of theoretical entities, and the question of theoretical laws. Cartwright and I developed our positions together, and they are jointly more powerful than either alone.

“If you can spray them, they are real. If you can use them to investigate something else, they are real enough.” — Hacking, 1983
i. the experimental argument for entity realism

Experimental entity realism — manipulation is the criterion; entities used as experimental tools are real regardless of the truth of the laws describing themMy case for entity realism is experimental, not theoretical. The realist argument I find compelling is not inference to the best explanation — “electrons are the best explanation of the photoelectric effect, therefore they are real.” It is the manipulation argument: we use electrons as tools to create other effects, to investigate other entities, to build new technologies. Particle physicists use positron beams to study the weak force. Engineers use electron tunnelling in transistors. We manipulate electrons in ways that produce predictable effects in novel contexts. This is precisely the kind of causal evidence that justifies belief in a real, causally efficacious entity. The entity’s reality is demonstrated by our ability to intervene on the world using it as a tool — not by the truth of the equations in which it appears.

ii. why entity realism does not require law realism

No law realism needed — we can manipulate electrons without believing Coulomb’s law is literally true; practical knowledge ≠ theoretical truthThe crucial point is that our grounds for believing in electrons are entirely independent of our grounds for (or against) believing in the literal truth of Coulomb’s law. We know how to use electrons because we have accumulated practical, experimental knowledge of their causal behaviour in specific contexts. This knowledge is phenomenological — it is knowledge of what electrons do in particular situations, encoded in engineering formulae and experimental protocols. It does not require, and is not grounded in, the literal truth of the fundamental equations that purport to govern electron behaviour in all possible circumstances. Coulomb’s law describes point charges in a perfect vacuum — a theoretical fiction. Real electrons are not point charges; real spaces are not perfect vacuums. The practical knowledge is real and grounded; the theoretical law is a useful fiction that systematises it.

iii. the theory-experiment gap

Theory-experiment gap — experimental practice is autonomous from theory; experimenters develop local knowledge that outruns theoretical lawsOne of the most important features of mature experimental science is that experimental practice becomes autonomous from theory. Experimenters develop local, hands-on knowledge of how to make things work — knowledge of systematic experimental errors, instrumental artefacts, materials behaviour, and technique — that is not derivable from any fundamental theory and that outruns what the theory predicts. The best particle physics detectors are designed and operated by people who know how real detectors behave, which is not always what the fundamental equations say they should behave. This gap between theoretical predictions and experimental reality is not a temporary deficiency; it is a permanent feature of the relationship between theory and experiment. Cartwright is right that the phenomenological is doing the real work; I add that even within the experimental domain, practical knowledge is prior to theoretical law.

iv. representing vs. intervening

Our paper “Ceteris Paribus Lost” and its companion piece “Ceteris Paribus, There Is No Problem of Provisos” make a targeted technical argument against the core of Cartwright’s position. i. the triviality problem for ceteris paribus laws

Triviality problem — CP laws are either trivially true or vacuously unfalsifiable; neither vindicates Cartwright’s claim that fundamental laws lieCartwright claims that fundamental laws are true only with ceteris paribus modifiers. But what does “all other things being equal” actually mean? There are two interpretations. On the first, the ceteris paribus clause is an implicit quantification over all interfering factors: the law holds “when there are no other forces” or “when other forces are negligible.” This makes the law trivially true: of course the gravitational two-body equation correctly describes a two-body gravitational system with no other forces. ii. the laws of fundamental physics are not ceteris paribus

The total description of a system with both electromagnetic and gravitational fields is given by the conjunction of the two sets of equations. No law is false; no law requires hedging. What is complicated is the application of the laws to derive the behaviour of specific systems, but that complication reflects the complexity of the systems, not the falsity of the laws.

The law remains true of the real pendulum — it accurately predicts the pendulum’s motion to the degree of accuracy appropriate for the engineer’s purposes. The idealization is in the model, not in the law. Cartwright confuses the engineer’s approximation with a failure of the fundamental law.

2.The success of the BCS model is success within the quantum mechanical framework; it confirms the framework’s applicability to the domain of superconductivity, not the inadequacy of fundamental theory.
3.Cartwright’s use of BCS therefore cuts against her own conclusion: the partially autonomous model succeeds because of the truth of the underlying framework, not despite it.
∴The BCS model is evidence for the truth of the quantum mechanical framework, not against it; Cartwright’s use of this example to support anti-realism is self-defeating.
Argument IV — Phenomenological disunity is compatible with fundamental law-governed unity at the structural level
1.The dappled world claim: different domains require different models, with no single phenomenological theory covering them all. This may be correct at the phenomenological level.
2.But this does not entail that no fundamental laws are true of the underlying structures. Disunity at the phenomenological level is consistent with unity at the level of fundamental physical structure.
3.Cartwright conflates phenomenological disunity (real and important) with fundamental lawlessness (a much stronger claim unsupported by the evidence). The dappling is at the level of appearances; fundamental laws may still govern the underlying reality.
∴Phenomenological disunity is compatible with fundamental law-governed unity; the dappled world at the level of phenomena does not entail a lawless world at the level of fundamental physical structure; Cartwright’s inference from the former to the latter is invalid.
