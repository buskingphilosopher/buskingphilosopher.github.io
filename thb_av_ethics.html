Chair,

Autonomous vehicles will kill people. Not many — far fewer than human drivers kill today — but some. When a crash is unavoidable, the vehicle’s algorithm will determine who dies. This is not a philosophical thought experiment. It is an engineering reality. And it is a reality that requires us to confront, head-on, precisely the kind of moral trade-off that philosophers have been studying through the trolley problem for fifty years.

The social dilemma of autonomous vehicles — the core finding In six Amazon Mechanical Turk studies (Bonnefon et al., Science 2016), participants rated utilitarian AVs — those that sacrifice passengers to save a larger number of pedestrians — as the moral course of action. But they would not buy such a car for themselves. They approved of utilitarian AVs for society; they wanted passenger-protective AVs for their own use. This free-rider problem was confirmed at global scale in the Moral Machine Experiment (Nature 2018): 40 million decisions from 233 countries and territories, the largest cross-cultural study of moral preferences ever conducted.
i. the free-rider problem and why it is a genuine trolley problem

Free-rider problem — people want others to ride utilitarian AVs; they want their own AVs to protect them; this is a prisoner's dilemma requiring public policyThe scenario facing autonomous vehicle policy is not hypothetical. It is a classic social dilemma — specifically a prisoner’s dilemma — of exactly the kind the trolley problem was designed to illuminate. Each individual, reasoning from self-interest, prefers to ride in a passenger-protective AV while everyone else rides in utilitarian ones. If everyone acts on this preference, all AVs will be passenger-protective, which is collectively worse than the alternative: a world of utilitarian AVs in which each person occasionally bears the risk of being sacrificed but lives in a much safer traffic environment overall. The trolley problem is the right frame precisely because it asks: when is it permissible to harm one person to save many? That is the question facing anyone who programs or regulates autonomous vehicles. The philosophical literature on trolley cases is not an academic curiosity here; it is the primary resource for thinking through the structure of the dilemma we face.

ii. the moral machine: what 40 million decisions showed

Moral Machine — 40 million decisions from 233 countries; three universal preferences; significant cross-cultural variation; provides empirical foundation for policyOur Moral Machine Experiment presented 40 million moral decisions to participants in 233 countries and territories, making it the largest cross-cultural study of moral preferences in history. Three elements emerged with near-universal approval: sparing humans over animals; sparing more lives over fewer; and sparing children over the elderly. But there was substantial cross-cultural variation that challenges simple universalism. Participants from countries with strong collectivist cultural norms showed weaker preference for sparing the young; those from higher-inequality societies showed stronger preferences for sparing the wealthy; participants from countries with greater legal enforcement showed stronger preferences for sparing law-abiding pedestrians over jaywalkers. This variation matters: it tells us that no single moral algorithm can simply be exported globally, and that the trolley problem framing — which makes the trade-offs explicit — is the only rigorous way to surface these disagreements and work toward cross-culturally acceptable standards.

iii. the premeditation objection addressed

Premeditation — objectors say programmed AV decisions are premeditated unlike human crash decisions; the response: premeditation is a virtue not a vice in safety-critical systemsCritics like Patrick Lin argue that programming a vehicle to make lethal decisions in advance is morally different from a human making a split-second choice — that it involves premeditation and therefore makes the manufacturer liable for a planned killing. We dispute this framing. Premeditation in safety-critical systems is not a moral failing but a design virtue. Aviation safety is built on elaborate advance planning for failure modes, including scenarios in which some loss of life is inevitable. Medical triage protocols specify in advance which patients will receive scarce resources and which will not. We do not condemn anaesthesiologists for pre-specifying what happens if a patient codes on the table. The alternative — leaving the algorithm to make purely random or unguided choices in crash scenarios — is morally worse, not better. Intentional, deliberated harm-minimisation is preferable to unintentional, arbitrary harm.

iv. what the data tells us about how to proceed

Policy implications — cross-cultural variation means no single algorithm; but some preferences are universal; a tiered global framework is possible and necessaryOur Moral Machine data does not, by itself, generate a policy prescription. Moral preferences are not the same as moral facts, and cross-cultural variation does not establish moral relativism. What the data does is ground the discussion in actual human values rather than philosopher’s intuitions. It shows which elements of trolley-problem intuitions are culturally universal — sparing more lives, sparing children, sparing humans — and which are culturally variable. This provides the empirical foundation for a tiered global framework: a small set of universal principles forming a floor, with locally-adjustable parameters above it. The point is that without the trolley-problem framing — without making the trade-offs explicit and measurable — none of this empirical grounding is possible. The trolley problem is not merely an analogy. It is a decision-theory framework that allows us to represent these dilemmas precisely enough to study them scientifically and legislate them democratically.

Chair,

Bonnefon and colleagues documented the dilemma. My contribution is to resolve it. The question I address is: given that people simultaneously approve of utilitarian AVs for society and want passenger-protective AVs for themselves, what policy should regulators adopt? I use a game-theoretical approach to show that the apparently paradoxical answer — mandatory utilitarian settings for all — is in fact what rational self-interest, properly understood, recommends.

“There is a difference between what an individual deems the right course of conduct and whether she wants that particular course of action to become a law applied to everyone. Rational self-interest, under conditions of ignorance about one’s position in a future accident, favours a mandatory utilitarian setting — because this is what you would choose behind the veil of ignorance.” — Gogoll & Müller, Science and Engineering Ethics (2017)
i. the prisoner’s dilemma structure

Prisoner's dilemma — personal ethics settings produce a collectively worse outcome; the unique Nash equilibrium of PES is mutual passenger-protection, which is Pareto-dominated by mandatory utilitarian settingsConsider a simplified scenario with two drivers, A and B, each choosing between a utilitarian (U) and passenger-protective (P) setting for their AV. If both choose P, both are protected by their own vehicles and each will be killed by the other’s vehicle if a collision occurs — a wash. If A chooses U and B chooses P, A is at risk of being sacrificed by their own vehicle while B benefits from A’s utilitarian programming without bearing any of its costs. The dominant strategy for each individual, reasoning from self-interest, is P: whatever the other driver chooses, you are better off with a protective car. The result is a Nash equilibrium in which both choose P — the worst collective outcome. A regime of mandatory U settings, by contrast, is Pareto superior: both drivers are safer overall in a world where all AVs are utilitarian, because the reduction in pedestrian deaths and the broader traffic-safety benefits outweigh the individual risk of being occasionally sacrificed. The game-theoretic argument for a mandatory ethics setting is not a concession to paternalism; it is the rational response to a collective action problem.

ii. the rawlsian argument: behind the veil of ignorance

Rawlsian argument — behind the veil of ignorance, not knowing whether you are the one pedestrian or the five, rational agents would choose a utilitarian algorithmThere is a second, independent argument for mandatory utilitarian settings that does not rely on game theory but on Rawlsian reasoning. Suppose you do not know, at the time of choosing which AV ethics setting to implement, whether you will be the passenger of the car that faces a crash dilemma, or the pedestrian in its path. From this position of ignorance, rational self-interest recommends a utilitarian setting. If you are one of five pedestrians, you prefer the car to swerve to save you. If you are the one passenger, you prefer the car to protect you. But since you do not know which you will be, and since the five pedestrians collectively benefit more from the utilitarian setting than you lose as the passenger, you should choose the utilitarian setting as a policy applicable to all. The Kantian universalisability point reinforces this: you can only consistently will a policy that you would accept from any position in the system it governs. Passenger-protection fails this test; utilitarian harm-minimisation passes it.

iii. the personal ethics setting objection answered

PES objection — some argue people should be free to choose their own ethics settings; Gogoll-Müller reply: this produces a prisoner's dilemma and violates what individuals would choose under ideal reasoningSome have proposed allowing drivers to customise their AV’s ethics — to purchase a “protect me at all costs” setting as a product feature. This Personal Ethics Setting (PES) regime sounds appealing because it respects individual autonomy. But it has a fatal structural flaw. PES produces the prisoner’s dilemma we have described: every individual has a dominant strategy to choose the self-protective setting, and the result is a collectively worse outcome for everyone. Moreover, the PES consumer is not choosing freely in the relevant sense: they are choosing under conditions of ignorance about where they will be in any given future accident, and their choice reflects a known psychological bias towards their own immediate interests rather than their considered judgement under ideal conditions. A genuine respect for autonomy — one that takes seriously what agents would choose under conditions of full information and impartiality — points toward the mandatory setting, not the personal one.

iv. mandatory settings and democratic legitimacy

Democratic legitimacy — mandatory settings require democratic authorisation; the trolley-problem framework makes the moral content of AV regulation transparent and democratically contestableA mandatory ethics setting imposed by manufacturers without democratic process would be deeply problematic. The game-theoretic and Rawlsian arguments show that mandatory utilitarian settings are what rational citizens would choose — but they do not show that manufacturers may impose them unilaterally. The correct conclusion is that the trolley-problem framing should be brought into democratic deliberation: citizens, through their legislators, should decide which mandatory standard to adopt, with the game-theoretic case for utilitarian settings laid before them as the principled recommendation. What the trolley-problem framework enables here is the transparency required for legitimate democratic choice: it makes visible exactly which moral trade-offs are being embedded in AV regulation, rather than hiding them in opaque technical specifications. Without this framework, democratic accountability for AV ethics is impossible.

Chair,

My contribution is different from my colleagues on the proposition. I want to use the trolley problem not to vindicate harm minimisation — which I believe is the wrong conclusion to draw from trolleyology — but to show how careful engagement with trolley-problem philosophy moves us beyond the simplistic utilitarian harm-minimisation approach toward something philosophically more defensible: a rights-based constraint that prohibits the deliberate targeting of persons, even to minimise overall harm.

“We argue that a careful engagement with the trolley problem and its literature actually provides reasons to reject a harm minimisation rule for autonomous crash algorithms. The philosophical insights generated by trolley cases — in particular, the distinction between doing and allowing harm, and the moral relevance of using a person as a means — show that crash algorithms should be governed by duties of non-interference rather than by a maximising principle.” — Hübner & White, Ethical Theory and Moral Practice (2018)
i. the doctrine of double effect and crash algorithms

Double effect — the DDE distinguishes intended from merely foreseen harm; crash algorithms that deliberately target a person to minimise casualties violate DDE and are impermissibleThe Doctrine of Double Effect, which has been central to trolley-problem debates since Philippa Foot’s original 1967 paper, holds that there is a morally significant difference between harm that is intended as a means and harm that is merely foreseen as a side effect. Foot’s own intuition — that it is permissible to divert a trolley toward one person to save five, but impermissible to push a fat man off a bridge to stop it — tracks exactly this distinction: in the bridge case, you are using the man’s body as a means; in the trolley case, you are merely redirecting a threat. Applied to crash algorithms: a harm-minimisation algorithm that calculates which pedestrian group is smaller and steers into them is not merely redirecting a threat — it is using those persons as the means by which overall casualties are reduced. This is impermissible under DDE, regardless of the numerical outcome. The trolley problem’s philosophical apparatus reveals why pure harm-minimisation is morally unacceptable.

ii. duties of non-interference and the passive car principle

Non-interference — crash algorithms should obey duties of non-interference: never deliberately steer to harm anyone; brake and hold course; this is derivable from trolley-problem philosophyIf crash algorithms should not be governed by harm maximisation, what principle should govern them? Our answer is a duty of non-interference: crash algorithms should be designed to avoid actively targeting any person, even if this means accepting a worse aggregate outcome. In practice, this means that when a crash is unavoidable, the AV should perform its best emergency braking procedure — which, as Rebecca Davnall’s vehicle dynamics analysis confirms, is always to brake hard in a straight line — rather than selecting a target by calculated harm-minimisation. The non-interference principle is not a counsel of passivity; it is a rights-based constraint derived directly from the trolley-problem insight that there is a morally significant difference between doing harm and allowing it. The AV that brakes and holds course allows some harm it cannot prevent; the AV that steers into a smaller group actively does harm to produce a better aggregate.

iii. the contractarian case for non-interference

Contractarian case — every potential victim can veto being deliberately targeted; non-interference is the only principle no one could reasonably rejectGogoll and Müller argue for a contractarian approach using Rawlsian reasoning. We use a Scanlonian contractarian argument that reaches a different conclusion. Scan lon’s contractualism asks: which principle no one could reasonably reject? Any potential crash victim can reasonably reject a principle that allows them to be deliberately targeted — to have their life traded away by an algorithm — even if the aggregate outcome of such a principle is better. No one can reasonably reject a principle of non-interference, because non-interference does not actively use anyone as a means; it merely declines to improve on the accident’s arbitrary outcome by deliberate selection. The non-interference principle is therefore the unique contractually permissible crash algorithm. Harm-minimisation, because it can always be reasonably rejected by the individual it targets, is not.

iv. where this leaves us on the motion

Verdict — the trolley problem is the right framework because it reveals that harm-minimisation is wrong; the opposition is wrong to dismiss trolleyology; they should use it more carefullyMy argument is a defence of the trolley-problem framework and a critique of the harm-minimisation conclusion that framework is taken to support. The opposition wants to dismiss trolleyology entirely and focus on mundane driving situations. I argue they are too hasty. Careful engagement with trolley-problem philosophy — specifically, with the Doctrine of Double Effect, the doing/allowing distinction, and Scanlonian contractualism — yields a substantive and defensible answer to the crash algorithm question: adopt a principle of non-interference. This answer is not available from the “focus on mundane situations” approach, which tells us nothing about what to do when an unavoidable crash actually occurs. The trolley-problem framework is not only useful; it is indispensable for the specific question of principled crash algorithm design.

Chair,

The trolley problem is one of the most productive thought experiments in the history of moral philosophy. But its productivity in abstract ethics does not automatically transfer to concrete engineering problems. In our 2016 paper, we identified three fundamental ways in which the ethics of accident-algorithms for self-driving cars differs from the philosophy of the trolley problem — differences that are not incidental but structural, and that mean applying trolley-problem intuitions to AV design will systematically generate the wrong answers.

The three disanalogies — Nyholm & Smids (2016) (i) The decision-making situation: Trolley problems involve an individual making a split-second choice; AV algorithms involve designers making policy decisions in advance for a statistical distribution of future scenarios. (ii) Moral and legal responsibility: Trolley problems abstract away from questions of responsibility; AV accidents involve manufacturers, regulators, software engineers, and passengers in complex responsibility relationships that the trolley analogy erases. (iii) Decision-making under uncertainty: Trolley problems stipulate complete knowledge of the scenario; AV sensors will never provide the certainty required by trolley-style calculations.
i. the decision-making situation is fundamentally different

Decision situation — trolley involves individual spontaneous choice; AV involves advance policy-making for statistical distributions; the moral structure is completely differentIn the standard trolley problem, a single agent faces a sudden dilemma and must choose in the moment. The moral question is: what may this individual do, given this specific situation, right now? AV accident algorithms involve an entirely different moral structure. Designers must choose, months or years before any accident, which rules the vehicle should follow across a statistical distribution of future accident scenarios that cannot be individually specified. They are not asking: what should this driver do right now? They are asking: what policy should govern a fleet of thousands of vehicles across millions of miles of driving? These are different moral questions requiring different moral frameworks. A rule utilitarian or contractarian analysis of a fleet-wide policy is not the same as asking what an individual may do in a dilemma. The trolley problem’s moral apparatus was designed for the latter question; it is not obviously suited to the former.

ii. moral and legal responsibility is erased by the analogy

Responsibility — trolley erases questions of culpability; AV accidents involve complex manufacturer/regulator/passenger responsibility chains that trolleyology cannot addressThe trolley problem is deliberately constructed to abstract away from questions of moral and legal responsibility. There is no question of who is to blame for the trolley’s being out of control, of what prior obligations the agent has, or of what legal consequences will follow from their choice. Real AV accidents are saturated with exactly these questions. When a self-driving car kills someone, who is responsible? The manufacturer who specified the algorithm? The software engineers who implemented it? The regulator who approved it? The owner who purchased it? The passenger who was present? These questions are not subsidiary to the ethical question of how the car should behave; they are constitutive of it. A framework that systematically erases them — as the trolley problem does by design — will generate answers that look principled in the abstract but cannot be implemented in the actual legal and institutional environment of AV regulation.

iii. decision-making under radical uncertainty

Uncertainty — trolley problem stipulates complete knowledge; AV sensors will never know how many people are in a vehicle or whether they are children; calculations required by trolleyology are unavailableThe trolley problem stipulates that you know exactly how many people are on each track, what their characteristics are, and that your action will certainly produce the specified outcome. AV sensors, even in ideal conditions, cannot provide this information. In a crash scenario at traffic speed, a vehicle’s sensors may not be able to distinguish one pedestrian from three, or a child from an adult, or whether an oncoming vehicle is occupied. The Moral Machine Experiment asked participants to choose between scenarios with specified numbers and types of people — five scientists versus one criminal, three elderly versus two children. These specifications are not available to real AVs in real crash scenarios. A trolley-problem-derived algorithm that requires this information — to calculate which group has more people, which group includes more valuable lives — cannot actually be implemented in an AV, because the input data required will not be available.

iv. what should replace the trolley analogy

Alternative — risk management under uncertainty; traffic engineering standards; legal frameworks for acceptable risk imposition; these are the right toolsWe do not claim that AV accident ethics is easy, or that crash scenarios have no moral dimension. We claim that the trolley problem is not the right framework for addressing them. The right frameworks are: risk management under uncertainty (how should risk be distributed across the population of road users?); traffic safety engineering (what ex ante standards minimise expected casualties across all driving scenarios?); and legal frameworks for acceptable risk imposition (what level of residual risk may manufacturers impose on pedestrians without their consent?). Each of these frameworks addresses the actual moral questions posed by AV design: policy questions about statistical risk distribution, not individual-choice questions about specific dilemmas. The trolley problem illuminates the latter and cannot illuminate the former.

i. four problems with trolley cases for avs

Four problems — technical tension with assumptions; limited range of issues; political not moral questions; epistemic problems; all four undermine the trolley frameworkMy paper identifies four distinct problems with the trolley-case approach to AV ethics. First, trolley cases rest on assumptions — complete knowledge, certain outcomes, binary choices — that are in tension with the technical limitations of real AV systems. i. the premeditation problem

Premeditation — programming an AV to kill is premeditated homicide; human crash decisions are spontaneous; the legal and moral status is completely differentWhen a human driver swerves instinctively and kills a pedestrian, we do not typically hold this to be murder. There was no plan, no deliberation, no intent to kill. Criminal law distinguishes very carefully between spontaneous and premeditated harm. Now consider an AV programmed to steer into a smaller group of pedestrians to avoid a larger group. The manufacturer has, in advance, specified that under condition C, the vehicle will steer toward person P and kill them. This is premeditation in the legal sense. The proposition says this is morally unproblematic — that advance planning for harm minimisation is a virtue in safety engineering. But there is a significant legal and moral difference between specifying a policy that will, across a statistical distribution of scenarios, reduce expected casualties, and specifying that the vehicle shall kill this specific person when this specific condition is met. The second is what crash algorithms actually do, and it is what no one has demonstrated is legally permissible or morally acceptable.

ii. the manufacturer liability problem

Manufacturer liability — if AVs are programmed to kill in certain conditions, manufacturers may be criminally liable; this creates perverse incentives against deploying safe AVsThe premeditation problem generates a severe practical problem for AV deployment. If manufacturers program their vehicles to make deliberate targeting decisions, they risk criminal liability for the resulting deaths. This is not a theoretical concern: in most jurisdictions, intentionally designing a product to kill a person under specified conditions, even for good reasons, involves the manufacturer in a way that spontaneous accident does not. The threat of criminal liability will incentivise manufacturers to specify no crash algorithms at all — to rely on unguided emergency braking regardless of the situation — not because this is morally better but because it eliminates the evidentiary record of deliberate targeting. Paradoxically, the proposition’s insistence on morally sophisticated crash algorithms may, if implemented, produce legally defensive algorithms that are morally less sophisticated than what the trolley framework recommends. Regulatory reform to resolve this liability problem is necessary before trolley-problem ethics can be implemented in practice.

In a crash scenario at highway or even city speed, tyre traction limits and braking distances determine that a vehicle can apply its maximum deceleration force in a straight line, or it can steer, but it cannot do both simultaneously with any precision. Swerving while braking at maximum force causes loss of control, unpredictable behaviour, and typically worse outcomes than straight-line braking. More fundamentally, the sensor information required to choose between trolley-style alternatives — count the pedestrians in the target zone, assess their characteristics, calculate which option minimises casualties — cannot be obtained, processed, and acted upon within the time available before impact. Davnall calculates that the entire trolley-type deliberation is physically impossible within the real-world constraints of crash dynamics.
