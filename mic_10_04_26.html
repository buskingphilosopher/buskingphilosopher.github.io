More in Common MRP — April 2026

Published March 21, 2026

What this poll is and why it is different

Every poll examined in this series so far has been a national voting intention survey: a snapshot of aggregate sentiment, useful for tracking trends but unable to answer the question that actually matters for who governs Britain — how do those vote shares translate into seats in the 650-member House of Commons?

More in Common's April 2026 MRP answers that question. Based on 15,482 respondents surveyed throughout March 2026 — more than six times the typical weekly poll sample — their Multilevel Regression with Post-stratification model produces a projected vote share for every party in every one of the 631 GB constituencies. The sample size is large enough to give genuine constituency-level statistical power rather than the wide uncertainty bands that characterise smaller MRPs.

The national vote shares implied by this model (Reform ~27.9%, Conservative ~20.7%, Labour ~19.8%, Liberal Democrat ~12.8%, Green ~12.3%) are broadly consistent with where the poll-of-polls has been sitting through March 2026. What the MRP does is translate those shares into seats using the geographic distribution of that support and the mechanics of first-past-the-post. That translation is where the story really begins.

The headline projection: Reform 324, Labour 101

The central finding: Reform UK is projected to win 324 of 631 GB seats, with Labour on 101, Conservatives on 81, Liberal Democrats on 62, SNP on 26, Greens on 22, Others on 10, and Plaid Cymru on 5.

To contextualise that number: 324 seats out of 650 total (adding 19 Northern Ireland seats which are allocated to other parties) gives Reform approximately 49.8% of Westminster seats on approximately 27.9% of the vote. They would form a government with a working majority. This is the projection that the headline "Reform to win majority" shorthand attempts to convey, and it is accurate as far as it goes. But like every headline this series has examined, it conceals more than it reveals.

The disproportionality: the most important number in the dataset

Reform's projected vote-to-seat conversion rate is the most politically significant figure in this entire model. Converting 27.9% of votes into 51.3% of seats represents a disproportionality ratio of 1.84 — for every percentage point of vote share, Reform wins nearly two percentage points of seat share. No major party has achieved this level of vote efficiency in recent British electoral history at this vote share.

To understand why: first-past-the-post rewards parties whose support is geographically concentrated in winnable seats. Reform's support — heaviest in post-industrial towns in the North and Midlands, coastal constituencies, and areas of economic stagnation — is concentrated precisely in constituencies where the combined Labour-Conservative vote has been fracturing for a decade. Reform does not need to win seats by large margins. They need to come first in a three or four-way split. In Ashfield, the model projects them at 50.7% with nothing close to them. In Sefton Central, they win with 29.4% — barely edging Labour on the same share. These two seats count identically in the House of Commons.

The contrast with the Greens is instructive. The Greens are projected at 12.3% of the vote but only 22 seats — 3.5% of the GB total. Their votes are concentrated in university cities, inner-city seats, and progressive coastal areas where they pile up large majorities in a small number of constituencies (Brighton Pavilion 64.5%, Bristol Central 63.4%) while failing to compete elsewhere. Their votes-to-seats ratio of 0.28 makes them by far the most electorally inefficient major party in this model.

The Liberal Democrats, with a similar national vote share to the Greens at 12.8%, win 62 seats — 9.8% of the total. Their ratio of 0.77 reflects a deliberate targeting strategy: concentrating resource in winnable rural and suburban English seats rather than accumulating large irrelevant majorities in safe ground. This is what vote efficiency looks like in practice, and it is the key lesson of every electoral system analysis: under first-past-the-post, where you win votes matters as much as how many you win.

The fragility beneath Reform's majority

The projection of 324 seats and a working majority is technically correct but analytically misleading without the margin distribution. Of Reform's 324 projected wins, 95 are by a margin of less than 5 percentage points, and 198 — more than 60% of their projected seats — are by less than 10 percentage points. Their median win margin is 7.6 percentage points.

This matters because the margin of error on individual constituency projections in any MRP is substantially wider than the national figure. The More in Common model gives a national margin of error of approximately 1.4%; at the constituency level, with sample sizes of perhaps 50-100 respondents per seat before post-stratification, the effective uncertainty is considerably higher. Seats that the model projects Reform winning by 2-3 points could realistically go either way.

More concretely: if tactical voting against Reform were to materialise at scale, and if that tactical voting were efficiently deployed in the right seats, the seats at the narrow end of Reform's distribution are precisely where it would have the most effect. The 95 Reform seats won by less than 5 points represent the fragile outer ring of their majority. If half of those flipped to other parties, Reform would fall below 277 seats — short of a majority.

The ten narrowest Reform wins in the model include Melton and Syston (0.004 points over the Conservatives), Chester South and Eddisbury (0.042 points), Stroud (0.219 points, beating both Labour and Conservative), and Leicester West (0.229 points). At these margins, the model is essentially saying "we cannot call this seat" while assigning it to Reform by the slenderest of conventions.

Who Reform is beating: the electoral geography of the takeover

The second-place party in Reform wins reveals the geography of the political realignment with precision.

Reform beats the Conservatives in approximately 140 of their projected seats — predominantly in rural England, the Home Counties, and provincial market towns where the Conservative vote has fractured rightward. Seats like Newark (Conservative 32.4%, Reform 32.3%), Ely and East Cambridgeshire (Conservative 26.4%, Reform 26.2%), and North Somerset (Conservative 27.7%, Reform 27.4%) are traditional blue-ribbon seats that the Conservative Party would need to hold to have any realistic path back to government.

Reform beats Labour in approximately 155 of their projected seats — the Red Wall and beyond. This is the category that produces the model's most dramatic individual results. Ashfield (Reform 50.7%, Labour 10.4%), Barnsley South (Reform 50.6%, Labour 17.7%), Easington (Reform 47.5%, Labour 19.1%), Hartlepool (Reform 46.5%, Labour 17.3%) — these are seats where Labour's traditional coalition has not merely weakened but collapsed. The model projects Reform winning these seats not narrowly but decisively. They are the constituencies that form Reform's core, and they are unlikely to be retrieved by Labour within a single electoral cycle regardless of leadership or policy.

The most analytically significant category is Reform beating the Liberal Democrats or Greens in seats where neither Labour nor the Conservatives are competitive. Stroud — Reform 23.8%, Conservative 23.4%, Labour 23.6%, Liberal Democrat 8.2% — illustrates a different dynamic: a four-way split in a prosperous English town constituency where Reform's diffuse support happens to be sufficient. These are the seats where tactical voting coordination could most effectively deny Reform a win, because the combined non-Reform vote substantially exceeds Reform's share.

Labour's 101: a parliamentary rump or a foundation?

Labour's projection of 101 seats — down from 412 won in July 2024 — is a reduction of 311 seats in less than two years. To contextualise the scale: this would be Labour's worst result since 1935. Their worst result of the postwar era was 1983, when they won 209 seats. This projection puts them well below that.

Their 101 seats are concentrated in a specific geographic and demographic profile: inner London (Bermondsey and Old Southwark, Bethnal Green and Stepney, Hackney), safe urban Northern seats (Bootle, Bolton South East), their Welsh strongholds (Cardiff South and Penarth), and Scottish gains from the SNP. The average Reform vote share in Labour-held seats is 21.4% — meaning even in the seats Labour holds, Reform is not absent. They are the second or third party in most Labour strongholds.

The most marginal Labour seats in the model — Sefton Central (Labour 29.4%, Reform 29.4%, margin 0.0), Wolverhampton West (Labour 26.8%, Reform 26.7%, margin 0.1), Leeds North West (Labour 26.3%, Reform 26.0%, margin 0.2) — are seats Labour would hold by a rounding error. A slight overestimate of Reform's support by the model, or a slightly higher Reform turnout on the day, would flip them.

What is analytically important about Labour's 101 is not the scale of the loss — which any serious analyst has been anticipating — but the character of what remains. The rump Labour parliamentary party that this model projects would be heavily urban, heavily Southern, and heavily graduate. The working-class towns, coastal communities, and post-industrial cities that formed Labour's historic core would be almost entirely gone. This is not a recoverable position in the way that opposition parties recover from ordinary defeats. It represents a structural transformation of Labour's geographic base that would require rebuilding constituency organisations, candidate pipelines, and local government infrastructure from near-zero in the places that matter most.

The Green breakthrough: 22 seats and what they mean

The Greens' projected 22 seats is a number that demands careful interpretation. Their two clearest seats — Brighton Pavilion (64.5%) and Bristol Central (63.6%) — have been building for years and are not surprising. But the model projects Green wins in Waveney Valley (43.3%), North Herefordshire (40.0%), and Sheffield Heeley (28.4%), which represent a different kind of breakthrough: non-urban constituencies where progressive voters are concentrating without a viable Labour presence.

The 22-seat projection puts the Greens within range of becoming a significant parliamentary force for the first time — not merely a pressure group with one or two MPs but a bloc with genuine parliamentary arithmetic significance. In a hung parliament scenario, 22 Green MPs would have real leverage. Even in a Reform majority parliament, 22 Green MPs would give the left-progressive opposition a concentrated voice it has lacked since the Liberal Democrat collapse of 2015.

The Green wins in Northern cities — Birkenhead, Huddersfield, Manchester Rusholme, Gorton and Denton, Leeds South — are the most analytically interesting. These are not traditionally Green territory. They represent the vacuum left by Labour's collapse in constituencies where the progressive vote has migrated Green rather than to Reform. If the model is correct about these seats, it suggests the Greens are successfully establishing themselves as Labour's main competitor in specific urban progressive environments — a foothold from which further expansion becomes geometrically easier.

The Conservative 81: survival but not recovery

The Conservative projection of 81 seats is simultaneously better and worse than it looks. Better, because it avoids the near-extinction scenario that some modelling earlier in the parliament suggested — they remain the official opposition if Labour falls to 101. Worse, because 81 seats on the best possible face of the data represents a second consecutive catastrophic defeat.

Their retained seats cluster in rural southern England, wealthy Home Counties, and some Scottish seats. The pattern — Beaconsfield, Arundel and South Downs, Aberdeenshire North and Moray East, Berwickshire Roxburgh and Selkirk — describes a party reduced to its wealthiest and most geographically peripheral strongholds. The urban and suburban English constituencies that a serious national party must contest have largely gone.

The most vulnerable Conservative seats in the model are those where Reform is within 2 points: Newark, Ely and East Cambridgeshire, North Somerset, North Norfolk, Melksham and Devizes, Sussex Weald, Welwyn Hatfield. These are the seats that would tip Conservative into near-extinction territory if the model has understated Reform's support. They are also the seats where Conservative-Reform tactical coordination — in either direction — would be most consequential.

What this model cannot tell you

An MRP projection is not a prediction. It is an answer to a specific question: if an election were held today on these vote shares, what would the result be? Three things the model cannot capture are worth stating explicitly.

Tactical voting. If the Greens, Labour, Lib Dems, and Conservatives coordinate — formally or through informal voter guidance — in the 95 narrowest Reform seats, the outcomes in those seats would be different from what this model projects. The model takes declared voting intention as input. It does not model the cascading second-order effects of large-scale tactical coordination. There is YouGov survey evidence suggesting substantial appetite for anti-Reform tactical voting among Labour, Green, and Lib Dem voters. If that appetite translates into action in the right seats, Reform's 324 projection could fall meaningfully.

Campaign effects. A general election campaign is not a poll. Parties raise money, target resources, motivate ground operations, and expose each other's weaknesses in ways that systematically shift vote shares in competitive seats. Reform in particular has very limited local campaign infrastructure outside a handful of areas. Their projected wins in seats like Stroud and Aylesbury depend on holding a fragile multi-way plurality with minimal organisational presence.

Time. This model uses March 2026 fieldwork. The next election will not be held until 2029 unless called early. Three years is an enormous window for political conditions to change. Reform's poll position has already declined from a 2025 peak of around 36% to the mid-to-high twenties in most houses. If that decline continues, or if economic conditions improve sufficiently to partially rehabilitate Labour or the Conservatives, the seat projection changes non-linearly — a small national vote shift can flip dozens of marginal seats.

Summary: the five findings that matter

First: Reform's majority is real but fragile. 324 seats is a projected majority, but 95 of those wins are by less than 5 points and could flip on modest vote share movements or tactical coordination.

Second: the vote-to-seats disproportionality is historically extreme. Reform winning 51.3% of seats on 27.9% of votes represents a level of FPTP amplification that would produce a government with enormous parliamentary power on a minority of public support.

Third: Labour's floor is not yet visible. 101 seats is not a floor — it is a projection. If Reform's support grows further, or if Labour fails to arrest its collapse among working-class voters, the number could fall further. What is analytically clear is that the seats Labour retains are fundamentally different in character from the seats they lose, and rebuilding requires solving a geographic problem as much as a political one.

Fourth: the Greens at 22 seats are the most structurally significant finding outside the Reform headline. It represents the possible emergence of a genuine second opposition force with parliamentary leverage — a development that would transform the political dynamics of any hung or minority parliament.

Fifth: the Conservative rump at 81 is their existential test. If Reform continues to squeeze them from the right while Labour recovers their urban losses, the path to Conservative revival narrows to a question of whether they can find a distinct coalition that neither Reform nor Labour addresses. The model does not show that coalition yet.

Analysis based on More in Common MRP, April 2026. Fieldwork 1st–30th March 2026. N=15,482 GB adults. 631 GB constituencies (excludes Northern Ireland). Constituency-level projections carry substantially wider margins of error than the 1.4% national headline figure.
