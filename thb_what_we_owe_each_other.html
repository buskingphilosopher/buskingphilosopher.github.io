A Debate on Social Obligation
"What We Owe Each Other" — Two Visions
Baroness Minouche Shafik · What We Owe Each Other: A New Social Contract (Princeton UP, 2021)
T. M. Scanlon · What We Owe to Each Other (Harvard UP, 1998)
The Economist
Baroness Minouche Shafik
What We Owe Each Other: A New Social Contract (2021)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Professor Scanlon,

We have been given, by some happy editorial accident, the same title. I take that convergence seriously. It suggests that we are both trying to answer the same question — what do we owe one another? — and that the question is genuine enough to attract serious thinkers working in very different traditions. But I want to suggest, respectfully, that the way you have framed the question — and the tools you have brought to it — leave out almost everything that matters most when real people, in real societies, try to answer it.

I am an economist and a policymaker. I have spent my career at the World Bank, the IMF, the Bank of England, and the London School of Economics. I have watched social contracts succeed and fail across dozens of countries, from Egypt to Germany, from Scandinavia to the United States. What I bring to this question is not a theory of moral wrongness but an account of how the systems through which we actually fulfil — or fail to fulfil — our obligations to one another work, why they break down, and how they might be rebuilt. I believe that conversation is at least as important as yours, and that without it, moral philosophy risks becoming a beautifully constructed answer to a question that nobody asked.

I. The social contract is not an abstraction — it is an infrastructure

The social contract as lived infrastructure of mutual obligationLet me begin with what I mean by the social contract. I mean the real, historically evolved, institutionally embodied set of arrangements through which societies pool risks, share resources, and assign responsibilities across their members. It is not a hypothetical agreement between abstract rational agents. It is the tax system. It is the pension scheme. It is the provision of education to children who had no say in where they were born. It is the public health system that treated you during the pandemic regardless of your ability to pay. These institutions embody answers — imperfect, contested, evolving — to the question of what we owe each other. They are the social contract made concrete, and they are under severe strain. Technology has disrupted labour markets. Demographic ageing has destabilised pension systems designed for a different population structure. Climate change has created intergenerational obligations that our existing institutions are wholly unequipped to honour. The social contract I write about is not a thought experiment. It is the infrastructure of civilised life, and it is breaking.

II. The three pillars: security, opportunity, and shared risk

Security, opportunity, and risk-sharing: the three pillars of a functional social contractDrawing on evidence from across the globe, I argue that a functional social contract rests on three interconnected pillars. The first is security: every person needs a floor below which they cannot fall — access to basic income, healthcare, and shelter regardless of labour market fortune. The second is opportunity: the social contract must actively invest in people's capacity to contribute, beginning with early childhood, where the evidence from James Heckman and others shows that the returns to investment are highest and the inequalities most consequential. The third is shared risk: modern life is full of risks — illness, unemployment, longevity, climate disruption — that individuals cannot bear alone. A sound social contract pools these risks collectively, spreading them across the population and across time, so that no individual is destroyed by events she could not have prevented. These are not merely redistributive preferences. They are conditions of a functioning society. Countries that fail to provide them pay the costs in political instability, lost productivity, and eroded trust.

III. The care economy: the invisible contract we must make visible

The care economy: unpaid care work is the hidden subsidy of every social contractOne of the most important arguments in my book concerns what economists call the care economy — the vast, largely invisible system of unpaid work through which societies raise children, care for the elderly, and sustain their members through illness and vulnerability. Every social contract has been built on the implicit assumption that this work — historically done almost exclusively by women — would be provided for free. This is not a minor accounting error. It is a structural injustice embedded in the foundations of modern welfare states. When women entered the paid labour force — as they did, rapidly and irreversibly, across all advanced economies during the second half of the twentieth century — the free subsidy on which the social contract depended began to disappear. Nobody redesigned the contract to replace it. Instead, women are now expected to do both paid work and unpaid care, the old and the young are less cared for than before, and the care economy runs on a combination of guilt, exhaustion, and low-paid female labour. A new social contract must make the value of care visible, share responsibility for it more equitably between men and women and between families and the state, and compensate those who provide it appropriately.

IV. The transformation of work: platform labour and the fraying safety net

Technology and the gig economy have dissolved the employment-based social contractThe social contract of the mid-twentieth century was built around the employment relationship. The employer provided not just wages but health insurance, pension contributions, and a degree of income stability. The state supplemented this through unemployment benefits and public pensions. Between them, the employer and the state provided a reasonably comprehensive architecture of security. Technology has dissolved this architecture. The gig economy, platform work, and the broader casualisation of labour have created a class of workers who are formally self-employed but substantively dependent on algorithmic employers who bear none of the social contract obligations that regulated employment would carry. When a delivery driver is injured, the platform does not pay. When she has no work in January, there is no employer contribution to draw on. The risk that was once shared between employer, employee, and state has been entirely shifted onto the individual. This is not a minor market adjustment. It is the dismantling of the employment-based social contract without replacement. My argument is that the obligations the social contract once assigned to employers must now be reassigned — to platforms, to government, or to new collective institutions — and that refusing to do so is a political choice dressed as economic necessity.

V. Ageing and intergenerational obligation

Demographic ageing requires a renegotiated intergenerational contractDemographic ageing is the quiet revolution that is breaking social contracts across the developed world. Every pension system designed in the post-war era was calibrated for a population with more workers than retirees, with relatively short retirement periods, and with children who could be expected to care informally for their parents. None of these conditions holds. We are moving toward a world in which the old outnumber the young in many societies, in which retirement may last thirty years, and in which the paid labour market that women have entered makes informal elder care impossible at its previous scale. I argue that this demands a renegotiation of the intergenerational contract: one that asks more of the old — working longer where health permits, contributing more through wealth taxation, and accepting reforms to pension systems that currently transfer wealth from younger, poorer generations to older, wealthier ones — and that also invests more in the young, whose productivity will ultimately determine whether any of us can be supported in old age. This is not a zero-sum political fight between generations. It is a recognition that the current intergenerational compact is fiscally and morally unsustainable and must be consciously redesigned.

VI. Climate and the obligation to the future

Climate change as an intergenerational social contract failureClimate change is the most severe social contract failure in human history, because it is a contract between the present and the future — between those who bear the costs of reducing emissions and those who will bear the consequences if we do not — and the future has no vote, no voice, and no representation in the institutions through which we make these decisions. I argue that the social contract must be extended explicitly to future generations: that carbon pricing, green investment, and the reform of international financial institutions to prioritise climate are not merely good policy but obligations that a coherent social contract imposes on the present. The evidence that the costs of inaction vastly exceed the costs of action is by now overwhelming. The barrier is not knowledge or technology. It is the political failure of social contracts that discount the future because the future does not vote. Redesigning these contracts to take the long run seriously is the central challenge of social contract reform in this century.

VII. The comparative method: learning from what actually works

Comparative evidence: the Nordic model, Singapore, and what we can learnThroughout my book, I draw on the comparative evidence of what different social contract models have actually achieved. The Nordic countries have demonstrated that it is possible to combine high levels of labour market flexibility with comprehensive social insurance — to have markets that work efficiently while ensuring that no one is left without a floor. The evidence on early childhood investment — from Perry Preschool in the United States, to Heckman's returns calculations, to the experience of Nordic countries — shows that the social contract's obligations to children are not merely humanitarian but economically rational: investment in early childhood produces returns in productivity, reduced crime, and lower long-run welfare costs that exceed almost any other public expenditure. Singapore, despite its different political model, demonstrates how a government can build substantial mandatory savings systems — the Central Provident Fund — that pool risk across a lifetime while preserving individual agency. These are not theoretical ideals. They are working systems, from which we can learn, adapt, and improve.

VIII. Reciprocity and conditionality: asking more of everyone

The new social contract must be reciprocal: security in exchange for contributionI want to address a critique that my argument attracts from the right of the political spectrum: that a more generous social contract will erode the incentive to work and contribute, producing a dependency culture that undermines the productivity on which the contract depends. My answer is that the new social contract I propose is explicitly reciprocal. It offers more — more security, more investment, more risk-sharing — in exchange for more contribution. This means expecting people to work, to retrain, to participate in civic life, and to contribute taxes proportionate to their means and their use of common resources. The Scandinavian social contract works not despite but because of its reciprocity: it is generous, but it also has high rates of labour force participation, strong civic norms, and expectations of contribution that are embedded in its culture and enforced by its institutions. Generosity and reciprocity are not in tension. They are the two sides of a sustainable social contract.

IX. The gap between moral philosophy and social contract practice

The limits of ideal moral theory for real social contract designI want to close by acknowledging, and pressing, a tension that runs between Professor Scanlon's project and mine. Scanlon asks what principles govern what we owe each other and gives an account of the wrongness of actions that fail to live up to those principles. This is important philosophical work. But real social contracts are not designed by individuals applying a test of reasonable rejectability to their own conduct. They are the product of political negotiation, historical accident, institutional inertia, and collective action problems that moral philosophy alone cannot resolve. The question I address is not only "what is the morally correct answer?" but "what institutional arrangements can actually get societies to approximate that answer, given that people are not purely moral agents and that political systems are not moral reasoning machines?" This requires evidence about what works, comparative analysis of what different countries have achieved, and political economy thinking about what reforms are achievable. I do not think Scanlon's contractualism is wrong. I think it is incomplete — that it provides a standard without a mechanism, a destination without a map, and that the map is at least as important as the destination.

Thank you.

The Philosopher
T. M. Scanlon
What We Owe to Each Other (Harvard UP, 1998)

Logical Arguments

Baroness Shafik,

I am genuinely glad that we share a title, and I take the coincidence as seriously as you do — though perhaps for different reasons. You are right that we are both trying to answer the same fundamental question. But I want to suggest that the question has a shape that your approach, for all its practical wisdom and empirical richness, tends to obscure rather than illuminate. I do not think that moral philosophy is a luxury supplement to real policy work. I think that without a clear account of the grounds of our obligations to each other — without understanding what makes our social arrangements just or unjust, right or wrong — the policy conversation lacks its own foundations. You need to know what you are aiming at before you can argue about how to get there.

Let me try to show what I mean.

I. The wrongness formula: what makes our obligations genuine

Contractualism: wrongness as unjustifiability to others on principles none could reasonably rejectMy central claim is about the nature of moral wrongness. An act is wrong, I argue, if its performance under the circumstances would be disallowed by any set of principles for the general regulation of behaviour that no one could reasonably reject as a basis for informed, unforced, general agreement. This is not a description of social contract history. It is an account of what makes conduct genuinely wrong — of why you have a moral reason to behave one way rather than another toward other people. The moral domain I am concerned with — what we owe to each other — is the domain of interpersonal justification: of what it means to act in ways that can be justified to others who have their own lives to live and their own interests to pursue. When Baroness Shafik argues that the social contract must be redesigned, she is making claims about what we owe each other. My project asks what makes those claims true or false, and provides an account of the moral reasoning through which they can be assessed.

II. Against aggregation: the separateness of persons

Contractualism refuses aggregation: each person's complaint must be answered individuallyOne of the deepest commitments of my contractualism is the refusal to aggregate. The reasons that count in moral deliberation are the reasons that individual persons have — one by one, each considered on her own terms — not the aggregate or sum of those reasons across persons. This is a direct challenge to utilitarian and purely consequentialist reasoning, which allows large aggregate benefits to one group to outweigh serious complaints from another. Consider Baroness Shafik's approach to the social contract: much of its policy reasoning turns on aggregate outcomes — the total economic return to early childhood investment, the fiscal sustainability of pension systems, the collective benefits of shared risk. These are real and important considerations. But they are in constant danger of obscuring what contractualism insists we cannot lose sight of: the individual person who is being asked to accept a policy, and whether the principle underlying that policy could be reasonably rejected from her particular standpoint. The person whose pension was reformed to balance the demographic accounts has a complaint. That complaint must be answered — not buried in the aggregate fiscal improvement that the reform produces.

III. Reasons beyond welfare: the pluralism of what matters

Contractualism admits plural reasons: welfare is not the only thing that matters morallyMy contractualism holds that the reasons relevant to moral reasoning are not limited to welfare or wellbeing. People have reasons that go beyond their interests in good outcomes: reasons of fairness, of self-respect, of procedural justice, of personal relationships, of what it means to be treated as a person rather than as a unit in a calculation. When Baroness Shafik designs the new social contract, she is working largely within an economic welfare framework — security, opportunity, risk-sharing are valued for the wellbeing they enable and the productivity they support. But some of what is most important about how we treat each other cannot be captured in these terms. The person who is helped through a conditional welfare scheme that requires her to demonstrate reciprocal contribution may be materially better off than she would have been without it, yet something may still have gone wrong in how she was treated — something about the way her dignity was handled, about whether she was treated as a citizen or as a liability. Contractualism insists on taking these non-welfare reasons seriously as independent considerations, not merely as factors that affect welfare outcomes.

IV. Against utilitarianism and its policy descendants

Against utilitarian policy reasoning: the separateness of persons forbids trading individuals against aggregatesMuch of the policy analysis in Baroness Shafik's book — and in the tradition of empirical policy economics she represents — is implicitly utilitarian in structure: it assesses arrangements by their aggregate outcomes, looks for policies that produce the highest total returns, and treats distribution as a secondary concern to be addressed through corrections to the basic productive system. I want to argue that this framework systematically undervalues what is most important in the treatment of persons. When we permit a principle that causes serious harm to some identifiable individuals on the grounds that it produces sufficient aggregate benefit to a larger group, we are doing something morally specific — we are treating those individuals as means to the collective benefit rather than as ends in themselves. This cannot be justified to them from a standpoint of mutual recognition. They could reasonably reject the principle. And if they could reasonably reject it, the policy is wrong in a sense that the aggregate welfare calculation does not capture and cannot correct.

V. The motivation for morality: mutual recognition, not self-interest

The moral motivation: acting from the desire to justify oneself to others, not from incentive structuresBaroness Shafik's social contract is held together by a combination of incentives and institutions — by the self-interest of each party in the cooperative gains that the contract produces, disciplined by the rule of law and the coercive power of the state. This is a serviceable account of how social contracts are maintained in practice. But it is not an account of what makes them morally binding. My account of moral motivation is different: the moral point of view is constituted by a desire to act in accordance with principles that can be justified to others — by what I call the desire for mutual recognition. This is not a calculating preference for cooperative outcomes. It is an intrinsic orientation toward others as persons whose standing demands justification. The force of moral obligation does not derive from the incentive structure that the social contract creates. It derives from the value of the relationship of mutual recognition itself — from what it means to be a person who acts in ways she can justify to others, and who treats others as persons who are owed such justification.

VI. The limits of reciprocity as a moral foundation

Against reciprocity as the ground of moral obligation: we owe things to those who cannot reciprocateBaroness Shafik argues that the new social contract must be explicitly reciprocal — more support in exchange for more contribution. I want to press on the moral foundations of this claim. Reciprocity is a coherent basis for cooperative arrangements among parties who are roughly equal in their capacity to benefit from and contribute to the arrangement. But the social contract does not govern only relations between such parties. It governs our obligations to the severely disabled, to infants, to the elderly who can no longer contribute in the economic sense, to future generations who cannot reciprocate at all. If reciprocity is the moral foundation of what we owe each other, then our obligations to these groups become difficult to explain. My contractualism grounds obligations not in the mutual benefit of cooperative partners but in the equal standing of persons as objects of moral concern — in what principles could be reasonably rejected by anyone, regardless of their capacity to contribute to the cooperative surplus. This provides a basis for obligations to the non-reciprocating that pure reciprocity cannot supply.

VII. Ideal theory and real practice: why the distinction matters

Ideal theory is not irrelevant to practice: without it we cannot assess what practice falls short ofBaroness Shafik ends with what she presents as a limitation of my project: that it provides a standard without a mechanism. I want to accept half of this characterisation and resist the other half. I accept that my project is primarily in the domain of ideal theory — it asks what our obligations are, not primarily how to achieve political conditions for honouring them. But I want to resist the implication that ideal theory is therefore secondary or dispensable. Without an account of what we owe each other — of what the correct standard is — we cannot assess whether any particular social arrangement falls short of it, by how much, or in what direction it should be reformed. Baroness Shafik knows that the care economy is unjust, that platform workers are being wronged, that future generations are being treated badly by our climate arrangements. She knows this because she has, implicitly, a moral standard — a conception of what we owe each other. My project makes that standard explicit, tests it for coherence, and asks what it actually requires. Far from being a luxury supplement to policy work, this is the foundation on which the entire policy argument rests.

VIII. What contractualism adds to the climate and intergenerational problem

Contractualism and future generations: the reasonable rejection test applied to climateI want to take Baroness Shafik's climate argument and show what contractualism adds to it. She argues that future generations are harmed by current climate decisions and that this generates obligations on the present. This is morally correct. But the contractualist framework asks a more precise question: could future persons reasonably reject a principle that licenses present generations to impose on them the costs of carbon emissions in the pursuit of current welfare? The answer is clearly yes — the principle licenses serious harm to identifiable others for the benefit of those doing the licensing, and there is no offsetting benefit to the future people that would make the principle acceptable to them. This is not the same as saying future generations have a vote. It is saying that their moral standing — their standing as persons to whom principles must be justifiable — requires that we consider whether any principle governing our climate behaviour could be reasonably rejected from their standpoint. Contractualism supplies the moral precision that Baroness Shafik's political economy, for all its power, lacks.

IX. The unity of the moral domain: why "what we owe each other" is not a policy question

The unity of the moral domain: contractualism unifies our obligations under a single standardI want to close with what I regard as the deepest point of difference between Baroness Shafik's project and mine. She treats the social contract as a series of domain-specific institutional problems — the care economy, the gig economy, pensions, climate — each to be addressed through evidence-based policy design. This is genuinely valuable. But it treats our obligations to each other as a collection of separate practical questions rather than as expressions of a unified moral relationship. My project argues that what we owe each other — across all these domains — is unified by a single principle: the requirement to act in accordance with principles that none of our fellow persons could reasonably reject. This principle gives the separate obligations their moral force and explains why failing to honour them is wrong, not merely inefficient. It provides the common currency in which obligations to the poor, the disabled, the future, the platform worker, and the cared-for elderly can all be assessed and compared. Without this unifying framework, policy reform is empirically driven but morally adrift. With it, we know not only what works but what we owe.

Thank you.
