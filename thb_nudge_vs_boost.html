Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I want to begin with an uncomfortable fact about human cognition that most people in this room accept in theory but whose implications they have not fully worked through. Human beings are not the homo economicus of classical economic theory. We have evolved biases, heuristics, and cognitive shortcuts that served us well in ancestral environments and that sometimes serve us poorly in the environments we actually inhabit. We are loss averse. We are present-biased. We are susceptible to framing effects. We anchor on irrelevant numbers. We follow defaults without examining them.

On what a nudge is and why it works

Libertarian paternalism — nudge preserves choice while improving outcomesA nudge is a feature of the choice environment that predictably alters people's behaviour without forbidding any options and without significantly changing economic incentives. The placement of healthy food at eye level in a school cafeteria is a nudge. The automatic enrolment of employees in pension schemes is a nudge. Nudges work by operating on the choice environment rather than on the deliberative capacities of the person making the choice — they are System 1 interventions, operating on the automatic, intuitive processing system. The philosophical foundation is libertarian paternalism: paternalistic in the sense that we want to steer people toward choices that make them better off as judged by their own values; libertarian in the sense that we preserve freedom of choice — nobody is required to follow the default. A well-designed choice environment helps people act on their values. A poorly designed one — shaped by whatever happens to serve commercial interests — makes it harder.

On the comparison with boost

Nudge is immediate, universal and equitable; boost is slow, resource-intensive and unequally distributedBoost — the alternative framework — holds that the appropriate response to bounded rationality is to train people's deliberative capacities: to teach statistical reasoning, to cultivate risk literacy. I want to make three observations. First, boost is slow — training deliberative competences takes time, resources, and sustained engagement that most real-world policy interventions cannot provide. Nudge works immediately, at scale, and for everyone. Second, boost is unequally distributed — the people who most benefit from boost are people who are already relatively advantaged. The people most systematically harmed by poorly designed choice environments — who are most subject to exploitation by default settings, manipulative framing, and dark patterns — are people with less education, less time, and less cognitive bandwidth. Nudge helps everyone, immediately. Boost helps those already well-positioned to help themselves.

On misidentifying the problem

Choice architecture is inevitable — the question is who designs it and for whose benefitThird, and most importantly, boost misidentifies the problem. The problem is not that people lack the capacity to reason well in some abstract sense. The problem is that they face choice environments systematically designed — by commercial interests — to exploit their biases. The solution is not to teach everyone to resist exploitation. The solution is to design choice environments that do not exploit. Nudge does this. Boost leaves people in the same exploitative environment, merely more resistant to it — and less resistant than the commercial interests arrayed against them, who have unlimited resources and every incentive to stay one step ahead.

Mr President,

My colleague Thaler has made the case for nudge from the psychology of bounded rationality. I want to make the case from democratic legitimacy and institutional realism — the argument that nudge is not merely effective but appropriate for democratic governance in ways that boost is not.

On the choice architecture problem

Choice architecture is inevitable — the question is who designs it and for whose benefitChoice architecture is ubiquitous and inevitable. Whenever a government, institution, or private entity designs a system in which choices are made, it must decide on defaults — what happens if the person does nothing. It must decide on the order and presentation of options. These design decisions affect behaviour systematically — they are nudges whether or not the designer intends them to be. The question is not whether to nudge but whether to nudge well or badly. My opponents argue that boost is preferable to nudge because it respects human agency and cultivates genuine deliberative competence. But they have not answered the prior question: while we are waiting for boost to cultivate deliberative competence — a process that takes years or decades — what do we do about the choice architecture that exists right now and is currently shaping behaviour? The choice is not between nudging and not nudging. It is between well-designed nudges that serve people's interests and poorly-designed defaults that serve commercial interests.

On democratic legitimacy

Nudge does not bypass agency — it helps people act on their own values; opt-out rights are genuineBoost advocates argue that nudge is paternalistic — that it substitutes the planner's judgement for the individual's. I want to challenge this characterisation. A nudge that changes the default from non-enrolment to automatic enrolment in a pension scheme is not substituting the planner's preferences for the employee's. It is recognising that the employee has a preference — to save for retirement — that they are systematically failing to act on because of present bias and inertia. The nudge helps the employee act on their own preference. What makes a nudge democratically legitimate is transparency. NICE's guidance on default opt-in for organ donation in England was publicly debated for years before implementation. The automatic enrolment policy was subject to Parliamentary scrutiny and public consultation. These are democratically chosen policy instruments, subject to the same scrutiny as any other policy. Boost, by contrast, requires prior decisions about which competences to cultivate and which heuristics to teach — normative choices that also require democratic legitimation, and which the boost framework has not engaged with.

On the empirical record

Nudge has a strong empirical record; boost's evidence of durable cross-domain transfer is mixedThe evidence that nudge works is overwhelming. Automatic enrolment in the UK increased pension saving participation rates from below 55% to over 85% within three years. Default organ donation in Wales produced a measurable increase in donation rates. Simplified application processes for financial assistance dramatically increased take-up among those entitled to it. These are large effects, achieved quickly, at low cost, without restricting anyone's freedom. The evidence that boost works — that it produces durable improvements in decision quality that transfer across contexts and persist over time — is considerably more mixed. Gigerenzer's studies show improved statistical reasoning in specific trained domains. They do not show that this improvement transfers reliably to untrained domains or persists without reinforcement. The effect sizes for real-world decision improvement from statistical reasoning training are modest compared to the effect sizes from well-designed defaults.

Mr President,

I want to make the philosophical case for nudge — the argument that the boost framework, however attractive it sounds, rests on a mistaken picture of what good public policy should be trying to achieve.

On the goal of policy

Policy should target outcomes not rational cultivation — boost confuses the instrument with the goalThe boost framework holds that good policy should cultivate the rational agency of citizens — help people reason better, deliberate more carefully, and make choices they would endorse on reflection. This sounds admirable. But it gets the goal of policy wrong. The goal of public policy is not to cultivate rational agency. It is to produce good outcomes for the people whose wellbeing it is responsible for. These are different goals, and they can conflict. Consider a simple case: a public health policy wants to increase physical activity among sedentary adults. A nudge approach might redesign office environments to default to standing desks, make stairs more prominent, provide walking-friendly meeting routes. A boost approach might provide training in the cognitive habits of health-conscious decision-making. Which approach is more likely to increase physical activity? Almost certainly nudge. The behaviour change is immediate, large, and does not depend on the acquisition and maintenance of cognitive skills that many people will find difficult to develop and sustain. Boost produces people who think more carefully about physical activity. Thinking more carefully about physical activity is not the same as doing more of it.

On the dignity objection

Nudge does not bypass agency — it helps people act on their own values; opt-out rights are genuineThe most powerful philosophical argument for boost over nudge is the dignity objection: that nudge treats people as objects to be manipulated rather than agents to be engaged, that it bypasses rational capacities rather than appealing to them. I accept the importance of dignity and deny that nudge violates it. The dignity objection assumes that the appropriate way to respect a person's agency is to engage with their deliberative capacities — to give them information, arguments, and training that help them reason to better choices. But this picture of agency is too narrow. People do not only exercise agency through deliberation. They exercise agency through habit, through environment, through the embedding of values in routines and contexts that make acting on those values easier. A person who has set up automatic transfers to their savings account has not abdicated their agency — they have used their agency wisely by creating an environment in which they do not need to re-decide to save every month. Nudge, understood correctly, is the public policy equivalent of this kind of self-design.

On boost as paternalism in disguise

Boost is paternalism about which cognitive capacities to cultivate — not less normative than nudgeBoost advocates characterise nudge as paternalistic but claim boost is not. But boost is also paternalistic. It decides which cognitive capacities to cultivate, which heuristics to teach, and which domains of decision-making to prioritise. These choices embed normative views about what counts as good reasoning and what goals people should be equipped to pursue. The difference between nudge and boost is not that one is paternalistic and the other is not. It is that nudge is transparent about using the structure of choice to promote specific outcomes, while boost presents its paternalism as the cultivation of neutral rational capacities — capacities that are, on examination, shaped by normative views that are every bit as contestable as the nudge designer's view of what outcomes to promote.

Mr President,

I want to challenge the proposition at its foundation — not merely at the level of policy effectiveness but at the level of the psychological theory on which nudge rests. The nudge framework is built on a picture of human cognition that I believe is fundamentally wrong. And a policy framework built on a wrong theory of the mind will produce worse outcomes than one built on a correct theory.

On ecological rationality — the correct picture of human cognition

Libertarian paternalism — nudge preserves choice while improving outcomesThe nudge framework inherits its psychological foundations from the heuristics-and-biases research programme of Kahneman and Tversky — which identifies systematic deviations from rational choice theory as cognitive biases. But this programme judges human cognition against the wrong standard. Human cognition did not evolve to satisfy the axioms of rational choice theory. It evolved to make good decisions under uncertainty, with limited time and limited information, in uncertain, complex, rapidly changing environments. And for this purpose — making good decisions in real environments under real constraints — simple heuristics often outperform the full machinery of rational calculation. This is ecological rationality. The take-the-best heuristic — consult one good reason and ignore the rest — outperforms multiple regression in environments with limited information. The recognition heuristic — prefer the recognised option — outperforms complex deliberation where name recognition tracks quality. The 1/N heuristic — divide resources equally — outperforms complex portfolio optimisation when the future is uncertain.

On what nudge gets wrong about heuristics

Choice architecture is inevitable — the question is who designs it and for whose benefitThe nudge framework treats heuristics as cognitive deficiencies to be routed around. It identifies the bias — status quo bias, present bias — and designs choice architecture that exploits the bias to produce a better outcome. The person's heuristics are used against them, in service of an outcome the designer has determined is beneficial. But if heuristics are adaptive responses to environmental conditions rather than cognitive deficiencies, then routing around them is not obviously beneficial. A heuristic that makes the person accept the default without deliberation may reflect not laziness but a reasonable judgment that deliberation in this domain is not worth the cognitive investment. Overriding this judgment — exploiting the person's disposition to accept defaults — is not helping them act on their own values. It is substituting the designer's values for the person's implicit assessment of the value of deliberation.

On what boost gets right — genuine competence transfer

Nudge has a strong empirical record; boost's evidence of durable cross-domain transfer is mixedThe evidence for the efficacy of boost is more impressive than my proposition colleagues suggest. Studies on natural frequency representations — replacing probability statements like "10% of patients will experience adverse effects" with frequency formats like "10 out of 100 patients will experience adverse effects" — show large and robust improvements in statistical reasoning across populations. These improvements are genuine — they reflect a real change in people's ability to reason about probabilistic information. And crucially, the improvement transfers. People trained in natural frequency reasoning apply the insight to novel problems they have not seen before. This is the fundamental advantage of boost over nudge. Nudge produces compliance in the specific domain where it is applied. Boost produces competence that transfers across domains. A person automatically enrolled in a pension scheme has not acquired any capacity that helps them make better financial decisions in other contexts. A person trained to reason about long-term consequences has acquired a tool applicable wherever present bias is relevant.

Mr President,

My colleague Gigerenzer has challenged the psychological foundations of nudge. I want to make the ethical case for boost — the argument that nudge is not merely less effective than its advocates claim but morally problematic in ways that its elegant philosophical packaging conceals.

On what nudge actually does to agency

Nudge does not bypass agency — it helps people act on their own values; opt-out rights are genuineLet me be precise about the mechanism through which nudge achieves its effects. A nudge works by operating on System 1 — the automatic, fast, intuitive processing system — rather than on System 2 — the deliberate, slow, reflective system. It changes behaviour without engaging the person's deliberative capacities. The person automatically enrolled in a pension scheme does not deliberate about whether to save. The person who chooses the healthier food because it is at eye level does not deliberate about their diet. This is presented as a virtue. But the capacity to deliberate — to reflect on options, to weigh considerations, to make choices that express one's values — is not merely a cognitive capacity. It is constitutive of moral agency. A person who has been nudged to a particular choice has not expressed their agency. They have been steered by someone else's design of their choice environment. The fact that they could have opted out does not vindicate their agency — opting out requires precisely the deliberative engagement that the nudge was designed to bypass.

On learned helplessness and the erosion of deliberation

Policy should target outcomes not rational cultivation — boost confuses the instrument with the goalThere is a further concern about nudge that I think is insufficiently appreciated: the risk of learned helplessness. If people are systematically nudged toward good choices — if their defaults are always set correctly, their options always presented in the most beneficial order — they will, over time, lose the habit of deliberation. They will become dependent on the design of their choice environment rather than on their own judgment. The muscle of deliberation atrophies if it is never exercised. The social psychology literature on self-efficacy is clear: people's sense of competence and self-efficacy is built through the experience of making decisions, sometimes getting them wrong, learning from mistakes, and developing judgment through practice. A person who is systematically nudged toward correct choices is denied the experience of developing judgment. They are like a person kept on a moving walkway — they arrive at the right destination, but they have not developed the capacity to walk.

On transparency and the limits of opt-out

Nudge is immediate, universal and equitable; boost is slow, resource-intensive and unequally distributedSunstein argued that nudge is democratically legitimate because it is transparent. But there is a structural difference between persuasion and nudge that makes transparency insufficient to address the manipulation concern. Persuasion works through the person's deliberative capacities — it presents reasons, evidence, and arguments that the person can evaluate and accept or reject. The person's rational agency is engaged, and the person's acceptance of the persuasion is an expression of their rational assessment. Nudge works through mechanisms that bypass deliberation. Changing the default exploits inertia. Placing healthy food at eye level exploits salience bias. Announcing that you are exploiting someone's inertia does not make the exploitation less exploitative. Moreover: the nudge systematically favours those whose genuine preferences happen to align with the default. Those whose genuine preferences differ face extra friction. The nudge is not neutral between preferences — it is systematically biased toward the designer's view of what preferences people should have.

Mr President,

I want to close the opposition's case with an argument that goes beyond psychological and ethical concerns to the level of political theory. My argument is that the choice between nudge and boost is not merely a choice between two policy instruments. It is a choice between two conceptions of the relationship between state and citizen — and that the nudge conception is dangerous for self-governing democratic societies.

On what nudge assumes about citizens

Boost is paternalism in disguise, not an escape from itThe nudge framework rests on a particular conception of the citizen: as a bundle of preferences and biases, to be managed by expert designers of choice architecture, so as to produce outcomes that are good for the bundle. The citizen is not, in this picture, a self-governing agent who participates in collective decisions about the direction of public policy. The citizen is a subject of governance — a person whose behaviour is to be shaped by the state, however benevolently. This conception is politically dangerous for two reasons. First, it concentrates power: enormous power accrues to whoever designs the choice architecture — determining defaults, information presentation, and option framing for millions of people. These decisions are made by experts, not by the people whose choices are being shaped. Second, it undermines the epistemic conditions for democracy: democratic self-government requires citizens capable of forming, revising, and expressing genuine preferences about public matters — who can evaluate evidence, weigh arguments, and participate in collective deliberation. A citizenry systematically nudged toward good choices — rather than cultivated in the competences required for good judgment — is poorly equipped for democratic participation.

On boost and democratic competence

Nudge has a strong empirical record; boost's evidence of durable cross-domain transfer is mixedThe boost framework, by contrast, is a framework for building democratic competence. When we teach people statistical reasoning, we are not merely equipping them to make better personal decisions about health and finance. We are equipping them to evaluate evidence about public policy — to assess claims about the effectiveness of government programmes, to recognise the manipulation of statistics in political debate, to be better informed citizens. When we cultivate risk literacy, we are equipping citizens to participate meaningfully in public debates about environmental risk, public health policy, and technological governance. The competences that boost cultivates — statistical reasoning, risk literacy, recognition of cognitive biases — are precisely the competences that effective democratic citizenship requires. A society that invests in boosting its citizens is investing in the conditions of its own democratic health. A society that invests in nudging its citizens is investing in their manageability.

On the political economy of nudge

Choice architecture is inevitable — the question is who designs it and for whose benefitThe nudge framework developed in academia and migrated to government through behavioural insights units — staffed by psychologists and economists with strong institutional interests in the expansion of nudge as a policy instrument. Behavioural insights teams are most useful to governments when nudge is the primary policy instrument — they have institutional incentives to advocate for its expansion and to resist the substitution of boost for nudge even where boost might be more appropriate. This is a standard public choice observation about the interests of bureaucratic actors. Boost is harder to institutionalise. It requires sustained investment in education, in training, in the development of new curricula and pedagogical approaches. It does not produce a new class of state actors with interests in its expansion. Its benefits accumulate slowly and are harder to measure. These are political disadvantages that have made boost less attractive to governments — but they are not moral disadvantages. They are the costs of taking seriously the project of building citizens rather than managing subjects.
