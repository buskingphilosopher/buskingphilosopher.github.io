Mr President,

I want to begin this debate with what I take to be the central case — the case that strips everything back to its simplest elements and asks the question most directly. I have a supply of a life-saving drug. David needs all of it to survive. Five others — call them the five — each need one-fifth. I cannot divide it further. I cannot save all six. What, morally, should I do?

The expected answer is: save the five. More lives saved. The numbers favour it overwhelmingly. I want to resist this answer — not because I am indifferent to the five, but because I believe the argument for it rests on a fundamental philosophical error about what moral reasons are and who they are addressed to.

On the impersonal standpoint — and its absence

No impersonal standpoint for summing lossesThe argument for saving the five typically runs as follows: five deaths are worse than one death; therefore we should prevent them. But consider the premise: five deaths are worse than one death. Worse for whom? For each of the five, of course — but each of the five faces the same total loss as David. His death is the end of everything for him. Her death is the end of everything for her. There is no person for whom five deaths are experienced as five times worse than one. There is no impersonal ledger in which losses accumulate. Each person faces only their own loss, and their own loss is total. The claim that five deaths are worse than one, in a sense that generates a reason to save the five, presupposes a standpoint — some vantage point from which all losses are visible and summable — that simply does not exist. Moral reasons are addressed to persons. They are reasons for someone to do something. And no person has a reason grounded in a combined loss that no one experiences.

On the friend case — and what it reveals

The friend case: no strict requirement to save the fiveSuppose David is my close friend. I give him the drug. I save one person over five. Have I done something morally wrong — violated a strict moral requirement? I do not think most people, on reflection, think so. I may have a special reason deriving from friendship. But even if I had no such special reason, even if David and the five were strangers to me, it does not seem that there is a strict moral requirement compelling me to save the five. If there were such a requirement, the friendship could not override it — the permissibility of saving David-my-friend would be a merely personal permission that has nothing to do with the fundamental moral question. But the intuition that saving David is not a moral violation is not about friendship. It is about the absence of a strict numerical requirement. The friend case is a diagnostic: if you think saving David is permissible even for a stranger who simply chose him, it reveals that you do not really believe the five generate an overriding claim grounded merely in their number.

On what equal concern actually requires

Equal concern: a coin-toss or weighted lottery, not automatic saving-of-the-manyEqual concern for persons requires that I give each person's situation equal weight in my moral reasoning. It does not require that the output of that reasoning be determined by a headcount. If I genuinely attend to David's situation — his life, his interests, his facing death — I am giving him equal concern. If I then attend equally to each of the five, I am giving each of them equal concern. What I am not required to do is multiply five separate instances of equal concern into a combined force that overrides the one. The appropriate expression of equal concern, when claims are equal, is to give each person an equal chance: a fair lottery or a coin-toss between the two groups. This is not procedural cowardice. It is the only procedure that actually treats each person's claim as having equal weight rather than treating some claims as ballast for others.

On the headache case and what it shows about the aggregative picture

The headache case: aggregation leads to absurdityThe opposition will tell me that the numbers must count because five deaths are worse than one death. But once we accept that numbers count — once we accept that the moral weight on a side increases with the number of people on that side — we have accepted a principle with no natural stopping point. If five deaths outweigh one death because five is greater than one, then a sufficiently large number of headaches must outweigh one death, because headaches are at least some bad thing and enough of them must sum to something worse than one death. This is the conclusion Parfit himself has been willing to accept in some moods — that at some enormous number, headaches outweigh a death. I find this conclusion not merely counterintuitive but a reductio of the aggregative picture entirely. The fact that aggregationism leads here is evidence that something has gone wrong at its foundations, not that we should revise our intuition about the headache case.

On what the motion asks us to affirm

What numbers-scepticism actually claimsI want to be clear about what I am and am not claiming. I am not claiming that the five people's lives do not matter. I am not claiming that David's life matters more than theirs. I am claiming that there is no strict moral requirement generated by the mere number of people on one side of a rescue situation — that the right procedure, when all else is equal, is to give each person an equal chance. This is the motion. This House believes that numbers and aggregation across persons are not always morally decisive. They are not. Each person's claim is their own. No one's claim is strengthened by the accident of who else happens to be drowning beside them.

Mr President,

I disagree with Taurek's conclusion. I believe we ought to save the five, and I believe the lottery is not required. But I reach this conclusion by a route that is entirely different from the aggregationists on the other side — and the difference in route matters enormously, because the opposition's route leads to conclusions that contractualism must reject.

The motion asks whether numbers and aggregation are always morally decisive. I am on the proposition not because I think the numbers never count, but because I think they count only in a specific and limited way — a way that respects the separateness of persons and does not permit the direct comparison of summed welfares across distinct individuals.

On the individualist restriction

The individualist restriction: moral reasoning is person by personThe contractualist framework I defend in What We Owe to Each Other is built around a fundamental constraint: the individualist restriction. When we ask whether a principle can be reasonably rejected, we ask what any one person could reasonably object to — not what the combined objections of many people aggregate to. This restriction rules out the utilitarian move of summing welfare across persons and comparing the sum on one side to the welfare on the other. It rules out Parfit's direct comparison of impersonal losses. And it captures something morally important: each person's claim is owed a response that she herself could accept, not merely a response that maximises some aggregate that no one experiences.

On the tie-breaking argument

Tie-breaking: saving the many without aggregationSo why should we save the five? Not because five deaths aggregate into something worse than one death. Because a principle of saving the one over the five can be reasonably rejected by each member of the five, considered individually. Consider any one of the five. She faces death. She knows that one other person also faces death. After we match her claim against the one's claim, the claims are equal — neither is stronger than the other. But there are four further people on her side whose claims face no competing claim. A principle that saves the one leaves these four people to die without their claims having been addressed by any competing claim. Each of these four can reasonably reject a principle that fails to address their claims at all. This is not aggregation. It is a sequential procedure — match equal claims, attend to the unmatched — that respects each person as an individual.

On why this is not aggregation in disguise

Tie-breaking vs aggregation: a genuine distinctionOtsuka will argue that my tie-breaking procedure is simply aggregation under another name. I want to resist this forcefully. Aggregation takes claims from multiple people, sums them, and compares the sum to the claims on the other side — smaller claims can pile up until they outweigh larger ones. Tie-breaking does nothing of the sort. The procedure requires that claims be matched against equal opposing claims before any tie is broken. A thousand headache-claims cannot break a tie between a death-claim and another death-claim, because the headache-claims are not equal to the death-claim and therefore do not participate in the matching procedure. This is the individualist restriction at work: no claim does aggregative work; each claim is individually compared. The opposition's aggregative frameworks, by contrast, allow headaches to sum into something that outweighs a death — which the individualist restriction forbids and which is the central conclusion this House should reject.

On the World Cup case

The World Cup case: no individual viewer can reasonably demand we leave the engineerConsider this case: an engineer is trapped and suffering agonising electric shocks. Rescuing him requires interrupting a live football broadcast for fifteen minutes. Millions of viewers would be inconvenienced. The utilitarian view — comparing aggregate viewer discomfort to the engineer's agony — implies that at some number of viewers, we should leave him to suffer. This conclusion is morally monstrous. And it follows directly from the aggregative framework the opposition defends. My contractualist approach avoids it cleanly: no individual viewer can reasonably insist that we leave a man in agony for fifteen minutes of football. The individualist restriction — ask what any one person can reasonably demand, not what the aggregate demands — produces the right result without any ad hoc modification. The World Cup case is not an exception to be explained away. It is evidence that aggregation is the wrong framework and contractualism is the right one.

On where I part ways with Taurek

Against the lottery: tie-breaking justifies saving the manyTaurek concludes that equal concern requires a lottery. I reject this. A lottery treats the situation as one where no further moral work can be done after attending to each individual claim — as if the equal claims simply cancel out and chance must decide. But the tie-breaking procedure shows that further moral work can be done: the unmatched claims of the four, each individually attended to, give us reason to save the five without a lottery and without aggregation. The lottery is the procedure for cases where no such further work is available. Here, it is available. Giving David a one-in-six chance when we have strong non-aggregative reasons to save the five is not equal concern — it is indifference dressed as principle.

Mr President,

My colleagues Taurek and Scanlon represent, respectively, the hardest and the most sophisticated forms of the proposition's case. I sit between them — I think Taurek is right that no aggregative conclusion follows from the mere numbers, and I think Scanlon's tie-breaking argument, while genuinely more sophisticated, ultimately either collapses into the aggregation it claims to avoid, or fails to justify saving the many at all. I want to press this dilemma as sharply as I can, because I think it is the most important unresolved problem in this debate.

On the pairwise comparison argument

Pairwise comparison: no aggregative conclusion follows from equal claimsThe fundamental question is: what follows from comparing the claims of individuals against each other, one by one? Consider any member of the five — call her Ann — against David. Ann faces death. David faces death. Their claims are identical in strength. Pairwise comparison yields: equal claims, no advantage to either side. Now consider a second member of the five — Beth — against David. Again: equal claims, no advantage. We can run this procedure for all five members of the five and for every pairing with David. In no pairing does any member of the five have a stronger claim than David. If pairwise comparison is the procedure, and pairwise comparison yields equality in every case, then pairwise comparison yields no conclusion that favours saving the five. The aggregationist must be doing something other than pairwise comparison to reach their conclusion — and that something is aggregation.

On Scanlon's tie-breaking — the dilemma

Scanlon's dilemma: aggregation in disguise or failure to justify saving the manyScanlon says: match equal claims against each other, then attend to unmatched claims. The four unmatched members of the five provide reasons to save the five. But here is my dilemma for Scanlon. Either the unmatched claims are doing the same kind of work as aggregated claims — accumulating on one side and providing more and more reason as their number grows — in which case Scanlon's procedure is just aggregation with extra steps. Or the unmatched claims do not accumulate — the four unmatched claims count the same as one unmatched claim — in which case the procedure merely shows that there is one person more on one side than the other, which is a reason to save the five only if numbers count, which is precisely what is in question. The dilemma: aggregation in disguise, or question-begging. I do not see a third option.

On what the pairwise argument reveals about Voorhoeve

Pairwise comparison and ARC: relevance is not enoughAlex Voorhoeve's Aggregate Relevant Claims makes a more refined move: only relevant claims — claims comparable in severity — aggregate. Five death-claims are each relevant to the one death-claim; they aggregate. Headache-claims are not relevant to a death-claim; they do not. But the pairwise comparison argument applies to ARC just as it applies to Scanlon's procedure. In any pairwise comparison between a member of the five and David, the claims are equal. No individual member of the five has a stronger claim than David. For the five's death-claims to aggregate against David's, they must be doing something that pairwise comparison alone cannot justify. Voorhoeve has specified when aggregation is permissible — within the relevance threshold — but he has not shown that permissible aggregation yields claims that are genuinely stronger than the opposing individual claim. The relevance criterion permits the aggregation; it does not explain how aggregation produces stronger individual claims.

On what follows for the lottery

The lottery is the only procedure consistent with equal individual claimsIf pairwise comparison yields equal claims in every matchup, and if no aggregative procedure can be shown to produce genuinely stronger individual claims rather than merely a larger number of equal claims, then the appropriate procedure is the one that treats equal claims equally: the lottery. Taurek is right about this. A coin-toss between David and the group gives each person — David included — an equal chance grounded in their equal individual claim. The only objection to the lottery is that it ignores the number of lives at stake. But that objection assumes what is in question: that the number of lives at stake, beyond what is captured in the individual claims, generates additional reasons. The pairwise argument shows it does not.

On what saving the greater number would require

What saving the many would require: a genuine account of aggregationI am not arguing that we can never justify saving the greater number. I am arguing that none of the accounts on offer — Scanlon's tie-breaking, Voorhoeve's ARC, Parfit's impersonal badness, Kamm's contextual interaction, Brown's residue argument — has yet provided a satisfying account of how individual equal claims combine into something that is stronger than any individual equal claim on the other side. Until that account is provided, the proposition stands: numbers and aggregation across persons are not always morally decisive, and where individual claims are equal, the lottery is the only procedure consistent with taking each person's claim seriously as an individual claim.

Mr President,

I want to approach the numbers problem from a direction that cuts deeper than the formal machinery my colleagues are deploying. I want to ask: what is the fundamental philosophical error in Taurek's position? And I want to argue that the error is a commitment to a purely personal conception of reasons — a conception that fails on independent grounds, and that once abandoned makes the case for counting the numbers not merely defensible but compelling.

On what Taurek's argument actually establishes

Taurek shows no combined subject; he does not show numbers are irrelevantTaurek is right that there is no single person for whom five deaths would be worse than one death — no one experiences five deaths as a combined loss. He is right that each person faces only their own loss. What he is not entitled to conclude is that we have no stronger reason to save the five. The non-existence of a combined subject of loss does not show that numbers are morally irrelevant. It shows only that the numbers do not matter because some super-person would experience a larger loss. They matter for a different and more fundamental reason: because each additional person we could save has their own claim not to be left to die — a claim grounded in the agent-neutral badness of their death — and the existence of that claim generates an additional reason that we do not face when there are fewer people to save.

On agent-neutral reasons and the impersonal badness of death

Agent-neutral reasons: death is impersonally bad; five deaths worse than oneWhat grounds our concern for any person who faces death? It is not merely that her death is bad for her — though it is. It is that death is bad, and she faces it. If we take seriously the claim that death is genuinely bad — that there is something terrible about a person ceasing to exist, losing everything they value, everything they will ever experience — then the question of whose death it is cannot be the only morally relevant question. The badness of death is not a property that varies with whose death it is. From the standpoint Sidgwick called the point of view of the universe, five deaths are worse than one death — not because anyone experiences them as a sum, but because they represent five instances of something genuinely terrible, and five instances are worse than one. Taurek's purely personal account cannot accommodate this, and that is a defect in his account of reasons.

On the separateness of persons — correctly understood

Separateness of persons: constraint on aggregation, not prohibition on counting claimsThe proposition will invoke the separateness of persons — the Rawlsian claim that gains for one do not compensate losses for another. I accept this principle. But I deny that it entails anti-aggregationism. The separateness of persons prohibits treating a group's combined welfare as belonging to a single subject, so that gains anywhere compensate for losses elsewhere. That kind of aggregation genuinely fails to respect persons as separate. But counting the number of people we could save — recognising that each additional person has their own separate claim not to be left to die — does not violate the separateness of persons. It honours it. Each person is counted separately. Each claim is attended to individually. The separateness of persons is a constraint on how we aggregate, not a prohibition on recognising that more separate people have more separate claims.

On Otsuka's pairwise argument

Reply to Otsuka: pairwise comparison misses agent-neutral reasonsOtsuka's pairwise argument is powerful but it proves less than he claims. He shows that in any matched pairing between a member of the five and David, the claims are equal — neither individual has a stronger claim than the other. This is correct. But from this he concludes that the aggregative procedure must be doing something beyond pairwise comparison, and that this something is impermissible aggregation. The error is in thinking that pairwise comparison exhausts the relevant moral considerations. Pairwise comparison captures agent-relative claims — what each person is owed from the perspective of their own interests. It does not capture agent-neutral reasons — reasons grounded in the impersonal badness of outcomes. Five deaths are impersonally worse than one death, and this gives us reason to prevent them that is not captured by any pairwise comparison between individual claims. Otsuka's pairwise procedure assumes that agent-relative claims are the only moral considerations. That assumption is precisely what I am denying.

On the headache case and incommensurability

Headache case: incommensurability, not a relevance thresholdThe hardest case for my position is the headache case. If numbers always count and impersonal badness always aggregates, does it follow that a billion headaches outweigh a death? I want to resist this conclusion without invoking Voorhoeve's relevance threshold. The resistance lies in genuine incommensurability: death and headache relief are not on the same scale of value. There is no fact about how many headache-minutes of relief add up to a death-equivalent, because the two kinds of value are incommensurable — they cannot be placed on a single measuring scale at all. Where genuine incommensurability obtains, aggregation is not merely blocked by a threshold — it is undefined, inapplicable. Within the domain of comparable harms, numbers count and aggregation applies. The headache case is one where comparability fails, not one where aggregation is otherwise correct but blocked.

I agree with Parfit that numbers count and that we should save the five. And my colleagues Scanlon and Otsuka, in their different ways, have pointed to a genuine problem with aggregative accounts — a problem that can only be answered if we attend more carefully to the structure of the moral situation itself, rather than either summing individual claims or comparing impersonal values.

My approach is built around what I call the Principle of Contextual Interaction.

On why Taurek's lottery fails as an expression of equal concern

Against the lottery: equal concern does not require equal chancesTaurek's lottery is the most seductive part of his case. Give David a one-in-six chance. Give each of the five a one-in-six chance. Symmetry. But this is not equal concern — it is a profound misidentification of what equal concern requires. Equal concern requires that I take each person's situation seriously and respond to it appropriately. The five people's situation involves each of them facing death. Taking that situation seriously — genuinely attending to what it means for five people to be about to die when I could prevent it — does not lead to the conclusion that the appropriate response is to give them all a one-in-six chance. That response treats certainty of saving five as morally equivalent to a one-sixth probability of saving any one. No account of equal concern that deserves the name implies this equivalence. The lottery is indifference masquerading as fairness.

On the Principle of Contextual Interaction

Contextual Interaction: each person's claim is constituted partly by who else is presentThe key move in my account is that claims do not exist in moral isolation from one another. When five people need saving alongside one, each member of the five is not in the same moral situation as she would be if she were the only person needing help. Her claim exists in a context — a context that includes four other people with equally urgent claims on the same scarce resource. This context is morally constitutive of her situation. Specifically: each member of the five can point to her claim as being part of a set of claims that, taken together as individually urgent claims in a shared predicament, generates a stronger reason for the rescuer to act. This is not Parfit's impersonal addition. It is not the sum of five losses. It is a claim about the internal structure of what each person's situation amounts to when others are present in the same predicament.

On Otsuka's pairwise argument and contextual interaction

Reply to Otsuka: pairwise comparison misses contextual structureOtsuka argues that in any pairwise comparison, claims are equal, and no aggregative conclusion follows. But pairwise comparison, conducted as a sequence of isolated two-person comparisons, misses the contextual structure I am pointing to. The five are not facing David one at a time in isolated encounters. They are all present in the same situation, with claims that interact with one another as a set of individually urgent claims in a shared predicament. Asking what Ann's claim is in isolation from Beth, Carol, Diana, and Eve mischaracterises the moral situation. Ann's claim, in context, is different from Ann's claim in isolation — because the context of others sharing her predicament is morally constitutive of what her situation amounts to. Pairwise comparison, conducted without attending to this context, reaches the wrong result because it is asking the wrong question.

On the complex cases — uninvolved parties

Contextual interaction and uninvolved parties: the limits of the principleContextual interaction explains not only why we should save the five but also why we cannot simply aggregate across all persons in all situations. The contextual interaction of claims occurs among people who are in the same predicament — who share a situation of needing help with the same scarce resource at the same time. It does not automatically extend to people outside that predicament. If saving the five would require imposing a harm on an uninvolved sixth party — using their property, say — the uninvolved party's claim has a different character from the claims of those in the shared predicament. She is not in contextual interaction with the five; she stands outside it. Pure aggregation, by contrast, treats all welfare on one side the same way — it cannot distinguish between a claimant in the predicament and an uninvolved bystander whose interests are merely affected. Contextual interaction makes this distinction naturally, and it is a distinction that matters morally.

On where Voorhoeve is right and where the grounding needs revision

ARC: right shape, wrong foundation; contextual interaction is the correct groundVoorhoeve's ARC captures a genuine moral phenomenon — that claims of radically different severity do not interact in the way that comparable claims interact. A headache-claim does not enter into contextual interaction with a death-claim in the way that a paralysis-claim does. On this Voorhoeve is right, and his framework captures something that pure aggregationism misses. Where I differ is on the grounding of the relevance criterion. Voorhoeve grounds it in prudential self-sacrifice reasoning — what a rational agent would accept as a personal trade-off. But the relevant question is not about individual risk attitudes. It is about whether two claims are of a kind that can enter into genuine contextual interaction — an objective structural question about the nature of claims themselves, not a subjective question about any individual's psychology. The foundation of ARC needs revision; contextual interaction provides it.

Mr President,

I hold an unusual position. I think Taurek is wrong that numbers never count. I think Parfit is wrong that numbers always count without qualification. I think Scanlon's contractualism, although it reaches the right verdict in the drug case, struggles to explain why numbers count in the way that they do without covertly relying on the aggregative reasoning Otsuka has exposed. My position — Aggregate Relevant Claims — is that numbers count when and only when the competing claims are sufficiently comparable in severity to be morally relevant to each other. This is not a compromise. It is the correct theory.

On the two intuitions that any adequate theory must accommodate

Two intuitions: numbers count in the lives case; not in the headache caseThere are two intuitions about this problem that command extremely widespread assent. The first: we ought to save five people from death rather than one, and part of the reason is that there are five of them. Numbers count. The second: we ought to save one person from death rather than cure any number of people of mild headaches — no matter how large that number grows. Numbers do not always count across different severities of harm. Taurek cannot accommodate the first. Full aggregationism, including Parfit's impersonal badness account, struggles genuinely with the second — even Parfit's incommensurability response is, as I shall argue, underdeveloped. Only partial aggregation, in the form of ARC, accommodates both simultaneously. This double adequacy is not a happy accident. It is the primary virtue of the theory and the primary defect of the alternatives.

On what ARC says

ARC: aggregate only relevant claims — those in the same moral conversationARC holds that when we face competing claims, we may aggregate only those claims that are mutually relevant — that are sufficiently comparable in severity to be, as I put it, in the same moral conversation. Five death-claims are each relevant to one death-claim: each of the five can point to her own death as being in the same conversation as David's. They aggregate; we save the five. A billion headache-claims are not relevant to one death-claim: no headache sufferer can plausibly claim her harm is in the same conversation as a death. They do not aggregate; we save the one. The relevance criterion is not an arbitrary threshold. It is grounded in what persons can reasonably demand of one another in a contractualist framework — specifically, in what they could demand that others take seriously as a comparable burden.

On why Parfit's incommensurability response is underdeveloped

Against Parfit's incommensurability: the notion does too little workParfit handles the headache case through incommensurability: death and headache relief cannot be placed on a common scale, so aggregation is inapplicable rather than blocked. This is a philosophically attractive move but it is underdeveloped as a response to the full range of cases. What about moderate harms — chronic severe pain, permanent disability, serious illness? Are these commensurable with death or not? Incommensurability gives us no answer. It requires a theory of when incommensurability obtains and when it does not — and that theory, when spelled out, will look very much like a relevance criterion. Parfit has deferred the problem to the theory of incommensurability without solving it. ARC solves it directly by specifying a principled criterion for when claims can aggregate.

On Otsuka's challenge to ARC

Reply to Otsuka's pairwise argument against ARCOtsuka argues that pairwise comparison never yields an aggregative conclusion, and that ARC's relevance criterion permits aggregation without explaining how it produces claims stronger than any individual opposing claim. This is the hardest challenge to my position and I want to address it directly. The relevance criterion does not produce stronger individual claims through aggregation in the way that Otsuka imagines. What it does is specify a domain within which the reasons generated by multiple individual claims combine — not because any one claim is made stronger, but because the total moral reason in that domain is greater when more comparable claims are unaddressed. This is a claim about reasons, not about the strength of individual claims, and it is this distinction that Otsuka's pairwise procedure, which focuses only on individual claim strength, cannot capture.

On health policy and the practical stakes

ARC in health policy: a floor of severity in QALY-maximisationThe debate between full and partial aggregationism is not merely academic. In health care priority-setting — in bodies like NICE in the UK — a commitment to full aggregation implies that we should always maximise QALYs, regardless of severity. At sufficient numbers, treating moderate conditions should always crowd out treating severe ones. ARC implies something different: there is a floor of severity below which claims simply do not trade off against larger numbers of milder claims. This has direct policy implications for how we structure health resource allocation — specifically, for the question of whether we should ever allow sufficient numbers of mild-condition treatments to take priority over life-saving interventions. Partial aggregation says no. Full aggregation, consistently applied, says yes. The stakes are not theoretical.

My role in this debate is somewhat different from my colleagues'. I am not primarily here to defend a particular verdict — though I share the intuition that saving the five is the right thing to do, and I think the motion as stated goes too far in the sceptical direction. I am here to examine, with some rigour, whether the moderate position that most participants in this debate find attractive — that numbers count in some cases but not others — can be given a formally precise and consistent articulation.

My paper "Is Close Enough Good Enough?" attempts this investigation. What I found is that moderate aggregation faces serious structural problems. I want to be honest about them, because I think the philosophy progresses better through acknowledged difficulties than through confident assertions that a view's problems are merely apparent.

On what the Close Enough View formally requires

The Close Enough View: formal precision reveals substantive commitmentsThe moderate position — what Parfit calls the Close Enough View — holds that aggregation is permissible when, and only when, conflicting harms are sufficiently similar in severity. Five deaths against one death: close enough, aggregate. A billion headaches against one death: not close enough, do not aggregate. But making this formally precise immediately reveals complications. We need a significance function f that measures the severity of harms on a common scale, a ratio threshold ρ below which a harm is too different in severity to aggregate, and a decision rule specifying what to do with the resulting values. These are not mere implementation details. Different choices produce radically different moral verdicts across cases, and the choice is itself a substantive theoretical commitment that the Close Enough View, stated abstractly, does not settle.

On Generalised Collectivism and Transposition Pareto

Generalised Collectivism violates Transposition ParetoThe most natural formalisation of ARC and similar moderate views is what I call Generalised Collectivism: minimise the total significance of all relevant unprevented harms, where claims below the threshold have no role. This captures the intuitive core of Voorhoeve's framework. The problem is that it generates violations of a principle I call Transposition Pareto: if outcome A is Pareto-superior to a permutation of outcome B — better for some people and worse for none, under any re-labelling of individuals — then A should be preferred. In certain three-person cases, Generalised Collectivism is indifferent between outcomes where one is straightforwardly better for two people and worse for no one. This is a serious defect. Even committed non-aggregationists should find it unacceptable.

On Weighted Numbers and ubiquitous weak-trumping

Weighted Numbers generates weak-trumping across negligible differencesAn alternative formalisation — Weighted Numbers — achieves moderation by using a strictly concave bounded function of the number of claimants, so that additional persons carry diminishing weight. This avoids some problems of Generalised Collectivism. But it generates a different and arguably worse problem: for any harm x slightly more significant than harm y, there exists some n such that n instances of x outweigh any number of instances of y. A ten-minute headache weakly trumps a nine-minute headache, at sufficiently large numbers. This is not moderate aggregation — it is ubiquitous weak-trumping between arbitrarily similar harms, dressed in moderate-looking clothing. It captures neither the spirit of the Close Enough View nor the intuitions that motivated it.

On obligation cycles and the structural problem facing all moderate views

Obligation cycles: moderation entails intransitive better-than relationsThe most fundamental structural problem is that moderation necessarily generates obligation cycles. Because the "close enough" relation is intransitive — harm A may be close enough to harm B, B close enough to C, without A being close enough to C — there will be triples of outcomes where each is better than one of the others in a cycle. This forces any moderate theory to choose between prohibition dilemmas — situations where no option is permissible — and violations of contraction consistency — where an option permissible in a larger menu becomes impermissible in a smaller one. Neither is comfortable. This is not a problem for a particular implementation of the Close Enough View. It is a structural consequence of moderation itself.

On the best available candidate: ICH combined with TCM

ICH + TCM: best moderate candidate, with acknowledged costsMy investigation concludes that the most defensible formalisation is what I call the Individualist-Collectivist Hybrid combined with Transitive Closure Maximisation. ICH first applies a lexical individualist criterion — minimising the most significant unprevented harm, with further tiebreakers — and falls back on standard aggregation only when the individualist criterion leaves the choice undecided. Transitive Closure Maximisation handles obligation cycles by declaring all options in a cycle permissible. This combination satisfies Transposition Pareto and avoids prohibition dilemmas. But I want to be honest: ICH+TCM lacks a unified rationale, its hybrid structure looks ad hoc, and its handling of cycles by making all cycle members permissible is at best an evasion of the underlying intransitivity. The costs of moderation are real. Whether they are worth paying is a question I deliberately leave open — but the opposition's instinct that numbers generally count is I think correct, and it is the articulation, not the verdict, that remains to be perfected.

I want to approach this problem from the perspective of the theory of weighing — a framework I have developed in Weighing Goods and related work — and to argue that both the proposition's sceptical position and the more naive forms of aggregationism on this side of the chamber rest on confusions about the nature of value, the structure of moral reasons, and what it means to give persons equal consideration.

On Taurek's mistake about value

Against Taurek: impersonal value and the separability of personsTaurek's central argument rests on the claim that there is no standpoint from which five deaths can be worse than one death, since no one experiences a combined loss. This argument conflates two things: the subjective badness of an outcome for those who experience it, and the impersonal value of an outcome considered from outside. I accept, with Parfit, that death has impersonal badness — that a world with more deaths in it is, other things equal, a worse world. But I want to be more careful than Parfit about how this impersonal badness relates to the separability of persons. The separability of persons means that the value of an outcome can be decomposed into contributions from separate individuals. This decomposition is what allows us to say that five deaths are worse than one death — not because five losses sum into a super-loss, but because the outcome with five deaths has five separate negative contributions to its value, one per person, and the outcome with one death has one. Separability, not aggregation in the objectionable sense, explains why numbers count.

On Scanlon's tie-breaking and why it is unsatisfying

Against Scanlon's tie-breaking: it does not explain what does the moral workScanlon's tie-breaking argument is ingenious but it does not explain what is doing the moral work. He says: match equal claims, attend to unmatched claims. But why do unmatched claims matter? Either they matter because they represent additional persons whose interests have weight — in which case Scanlon is committed to something like the claim that more interests have more weight, which is a form of aggregation. Or they matter for some reason that Scanlon has not specified. The tie-breaking procedure produces the right verdict in the drug case, but it does so by deferring the explanation rather than providing one. The explanation that does the actual work is that additional persons' interests have additional weight — which is precisely what the theory of weighing says, and precisely what the proposition needs to resist if it is to maintain that numbers are not always decisive.

On fairness and the role it plays in the numbers problem

Fairness as a separate consideration in the numbers problemThere is a dimension of the numbers problem that both the proposition and much of the opposition has neglected: fairness. In Weighing Goods I argue that fairness is a consideration separate from and potentially in competition with the aggregative considerations about value. A fair lottery, as Taurek suggests, gives each person an equal chance. This is not nothing — it is a genuine expression of a genuine moral consideration. But fairness competes with the consideration of doing as much good as possible. When we must choose between a procedure that is maximally fair and a procedure that produces the best outcome, we face a genuine moral trade-off between two real considerations. The numbers problem is partly a problem about how to weigh fairness against value — and the answer, I think, is that value wins in cases like the drug case, but the claim of fairness is real and not merely confused.

On the claim that people matter equally — and what it implies

Equal concern implies numbers count: each person matters, therefore more persons matter moreThe deepest argument for the numbers counting is the simplest: each person matters equally. If each person matters equally, and five persons each matter equally, then five persons matter more than one person — not because they have greater individual worth, but because there are five of them, each with equal worth. This argument does not require an impersonal standpoint, and it does not require treating the five as a composite. It requires only that we take seriously what it means to say that each person matters. If that commitment is genuine, we cannot be indifferent between saving five and saving one when saving five is available. The proposition's claim that numbers are not always decisive is, at its core, a denial of this implication — a claim that equal individual worth does not translate into greater reasons when there are more individuals. I find this denial hard to sustain.

On where I part company with unrestricted aggregationism

Against unrestricted aggregation: the repugnant conclusion and limited aggregationI do not endorse unrestricted aggregationism. The repugnant conclusion — that a world of very many people with lives barely worth living can be better than a smaller world of very happy people — follows from unrestricted aggregation and strikes me as genuinely repugnant. And in the interpersonal trade-offs at issue in this debate, I accept that there are constraints on aggregation: we cannot sacrifice a person's vital interests for sufficiently diffuse benefits to sufficiently many. But these constraints do not support the proposition's position that numbers are not always decisive. They support a more nuanced conclusion: that numbers count, that aggregation is generally valid, and that the cases where it goes wrong are those where it violates a separable person's vital interests rather than merely their diffuse preferences. This is closer to Voorhoeve's ARC than to Taurek's scepticism, and I regard it as the most defensible position in the space.

2.These constraints apply where aggregation would violate a separable person's vital interests for diffuse benefits to many — not where claims are of comparable severity.
3.Within the domain of comparable claims — lives against lives — these constraints do not apply and the numbers are decisive.
∴The correct position is constrained aggregationism: numbers count and aggregation is generally valid, constrained by vital interests at the boundaries — closer to ARC than to Taurek, and firmly against the motion.
