Sonnet 3

William Shakespeare — Sonnets (1609)

Structure & Context
Form: Shakespearean sonnet — ABAB CDCD EFEF GG; iambic pentameter throughout Sequence: Third of the procreation sonnets (1–17); extends the mirror conceit introduced in Sonnet 1 Central image: The mirror — it governs lines 1, 9, and 11, each time in a different grammatical role Argument: The young man owes it to his mother as well as the world to reproduce; refusing is a triple injury — to himself, to potential heirs, and to her Agricultural register: 'Unear'd,' 'tillage,' 'husbandry' — all Elizabethan terms for uncultivated land applied to the female body and male sexual duty Volta: Line 9 — shifts from accusation and rhetorical question to the positive case for reproduction, via the mother argument

Look in thy glass, and tell the face thou viewest Now is the time that face should form another; Whose fresh repair if now thou not renewest, Thou dost beguile the world, unbless some mother. For where is she so fair whose unear'd womb Disdains the tillage of thy husbandry? Or who is he so fond will be the tomb Of his self-love, to stop posterity? Thou art thy mother's glass, and she in thee Calls back the lovely April of her prime: So thou through windows of thine age shall see Despite of wrinkles this thy golden time. But if thou live, remember'd not to be, Die single, and thine image dies with thee.
