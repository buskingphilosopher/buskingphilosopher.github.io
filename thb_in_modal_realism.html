Modal Realism — The Full DoctrineDavid Lewis — On the Plurality of Worlds (1986)
Possible worlds are genuine, concrete, spatiotemporally unified universes, as real as the actual world. The actual world is distinguished only indexically — it is the world we inhabit. Modal truths are truths about what holds at these worlds: “possibly P” means P holds at some world; “necessarily P” means P holds at all worlds. The ontological extravagance of this view is justified by the extraordinary theoretical unification it achieves across the analyses of modality, counterfactuals, properties, propositional content, and causation. No rival view approaches this unification.

Isolation, Actuality & the Defence of ConcretenessPhillip Bricker — “Island Universes and the Analysis of Modality” (1987) & subsequent work
Lewis’s modal realism faces objections about the causal isolation of worlds, the epistemological access to other worlds, and the indexical analysis of actuality. I want to defend and develop the Lewisian position: the isolation of worlds is not a defect but a consequence of the correct metaphysical picture; the epistemological problem dissolves once we see that knowledge of modal truths does not require causal contact with other worlds; and the indexical analysis of actuality is correct and independently motivated. The alternatives — ersatzism, combinatorialism, actualism — all fail to provide genuine analyses of modal notions rather than primitive stipulations.

Theoretical Unification & the Cost-Benefit AnalysisDaniel Nolan — David Lewis (2005) & “Recombination Unbound” (1996)
The case for modal realism is fundamentally a cost-benefit argument. The cost is ontological extravagance: uncountably many concrete universes. The benefit is theoretical unification of an extraordinary range of phenomena. No rival view achieves this unification without invoking either primitive modality (which is no explanation at all) or abstract objects of a kind at least as ontologically puzzling as Lewis’s concrete worlds. The staggering objection from van Inwagen and others mistakes the nature of theoretical justification in metaphysics: a theory that unifies more with simpler primitives is better even if its ontology is larger, because quantitative parsimony is less important than qualitative parsimony and explanatory power.

Chair,

I want to begin with the theoretical situation before I get to the metaphysics, because the opposition will spend most of their time expressing incredulity at my ontology, and incredulity is not an argument. The question I want to press is: what does the alternative cost? Every alternative to modal realism either takes modal notions as primitive — which is no analysis at all — or analyses them in terms of abstract objects that are, on reflection, no less puzzling than my concrete worlds. The question is not whether modal realism is strange. Everything in metaphysics is strange. The question is whether it is the best theory we have.

“I believe, and so do you, that things could have been different in countless ways. I believe permissive conception of possibility: if something can be the case, I say it is the case, at some world.” — Lewis, On the Plurality of Worlds
i. the theoretical benefits of modal realism

Theoretical unification — modal realism provides uniform analyses of modality, counterfactuals, properties as sets of possible individuals, propositions as sets of possible worlds, and causal relations; no rival achieves this rangeLet me catalogue what modal realism gives us. First and most obviously: an analysis of modal discourse. Possibility, necessity, and contingency are analysed as quantification over possible worlds — genuinely informative analyses that reduce modal notions to non-modal ones. Second: counterfactual conditionals, via the similarity semantics we discussed in the previous session. Third: properties are analysed as sets of possible individuals — not sets of actual individuals, which would make co-extensive properties identical, but sets of individuals across all worlds, which makes the property of being a horse and the property of being a renate (kidney-possessing creature) come out distinct as they should. Fourth: propositions are sets of possible worlds — again, a genuine analysis that explains the identity conditions of propositions without primitive intentionality. Fifth: causation, via counterfactual dependence, analysed in turn via the possible worlds semantics. No rival theory analyses more than one or two of these. Ersatz theories take over my worlds but treat them as abstract objects and inherit all my modal analyses at the cost of failing to ground them in genuinely distinct entities. Armstrong’s combinatorialism cannot account for alien properties. Adams’s actualism cannot provide genuine analyses of modal notions without circularity.

ii. the indexical analysis of actuality

Indexical actuality — 'actual' is an indexical like 'here' and 'now'; it picks out whichever world the speaker inhabits; this explains the logical behaviour of actuality without requiring a metaphysically special worldThe opposition will say that I cannot account for the special status of the actual world — the world we inhabit — without admitting that it is different in kind from other worlds. On my view, “actual” is an indexical: like “here” and “now,” it picks out whatever world (or place, or time) the speaker is at. When I say “the actual world,” I mean the world I am in — this world, the one whose history includes my writing On the Plurality of Worlds and your reading it. Every world is actual from the perspective of its inhabitants; our world is no more metaphysically special than a place is metaphysically special by being “here” from the speaker’s perspective. This indexical analysis captures perfectly the logical behaviour of “actual” in modal discourse. When we say “it is actually the case that P,” we mean P holds at the world of the context of utterance — which is exactly what the indexical analysis delivers. Adams will object that this makes it a contingent matter whether we exist, since we could have failed to be in the actual world. But this gets the logic wrong: we could not have been in a different world, any more than I could have been in a different location from the location I am in. “Actual” is world-rigidified in context just as “here” is place-rigidified.

iii. the incredulous stare and the methodology of metaphysics

Incredulous stare — van Inwagen's objection is a methodological refusal to follow an argument to its conclusion; in metaphysics, theoretical virtues can justify conclusions that conflict with pre-theoretical intuitionVan Inwagen will give what I call the incredulous stare — the response that modal realism is simply too bizarre to take seriously, that the sheer quantity of concrete universes it posits is beyond rational acceptance. I have three replies. First: incredulity is not an argument. The pre-theoretical intuition that there is only one world is a data point, but it is not inviolable. Pre-theoretical intuitions have been revised by scientific theory repeatedly — about the age of the universe, the size of the cosmos, the nature of matter. Metaphysical theory can revise pre-theoretical intuitions too. Second: the benefits of modal realism are so great that the cost in intuitive incredulity may well be worth paying. We are not choosing between modal realism and a comfortable intuitive picture; we are choosing between modal realism and theories that achieve far less theoretical unification. Third: the quantitative size of my ontology should not be the metric. The relevant metric is qualitative parsimony: how many fundamental categories of entity does a theory posit? My worlds are all of the same fundamental kind — concrete spatiotemporal systems. I posit no new fundamental categories; I just posit more of the same kind.

iv. counterpart theory and the problem of trans-world identity

Counterpart theory — individuals exist in only one world; de re modal claims are about their counterparts in other worlds; this avoids the problems of trans-world identity while preserving the content of ordinary modal thoughtKripke will object that my theory cannot account for de re modal claims — claims about what a specific individual could or could not have been. When we say “Humphrey could have won the election,” we seem to be saying something about Humphrey himself, not about some counterpart of his in another world. My response is counterpart theory: individuals exist in only one world, but they have counterparts in other worlds — individuals that resemble them in relevant respects. When we say “Humphrey could have won,” we say that some world contains a counterpart of Humphrey who wins. Kripke protests that Humphrey is not comforted by the thought that some similar individual in another world wins. But this misunderstands the claim. We are not saying the winning event is good for Humphrey. We are saying something about what kind of world is metaphysically possible — a world sufficiently similar to ours in having a Humphrey-like individual who wins. The complaint that counterpart theory fails to capture what “could have” means is either a begging of the question against modal realism, or a request that I vindicate pre-theoretical intuitions that are themselves data to be weighed against the theory.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Theoretical unification: modal realism analyses modality, counterfactuals, properties, propositions, and causation in a single framework
1.Modal realism provides: analyses of possibility and necessity as world-quantification; counterfactual semantics via world-similarity; properties as sets of possible individuals (avoiding co-extension collapse); propositions as sets of possible worlds; causation via counterfactual dependence.
2.No rival theory analyses more than one or two of these. Ersatzism inherits the analyses but fails to ground them non-circularly. Combinatorialism cannot handle alien properties. Actualism cannot provide non-circular modal analyses.
3.Theoretical unification is the paramount theoretical virtue. A theory that analyses five major philosophical phenomena with a single ontology is overwhelmingly preferable to five theories each invoking primitive notions for one phenomenon.
∴Modal realism’s theoretical unification across modality, counterfactuals, properties, propositions, and causation is its decisive advantage; no rival theory approaches this range; this unification justifies the ontological cost.
Argument II — The indexical analysis of actuality correctly captures the logical behaviour of “actual” without metaphysical special-pleading
1.“Actual” is an indexical like “here” and “now”: it picks out whichever world the speaker inhabits. Every world is actual from its inhabitants’ perspective.
2.This analysis correctly captures the logical behaviour of “actual” in modal logic: “Actually P” is true iff P holds at the world of the context of utterance.
3.The objection that our world is metaphysically special confuses the indexical with the non-indexical. Our world is no more metaphysically special than a place is special by being “here.”
∴The indexical analysis of actuality is correct and independently motivated; it explains the logical behaviour of “actual” without positing a metaphysically special world; the intuition that our world is privileged is an indexical fact, not a metaphysical one.
Argument III — The incredulous stare is not an argument; qualitative parsimony, not quantitative size, is the correct metric
1.Incredulity at the quantity of concrete universes is a pre-theoretical intuition, not an argument. Pre-theoretical intuitions have been repeatedly revised by good theory in science and philosophy.
2.Quantitative parsimony (fewer entities) is less important than qualitative parsimony (fewer fundamental categories). Lewis’s worlds are all of one fundamental kind — concrete spatiotemporal systems. No new fundamental categories are introduced.
3.A theory that posits many entities of one kind to achieve great unification is more parsimonious, in the relevant sense, than a theory that posits few entities of many puzzling kinds.
∴The incredulous stare is not an argument; qualitative parsimony, not quantitative size, is the correct measure of ontological economy; Lewis’s theory is qualitatively parsimonious and theoretically powerful.
Argument IV — Counterpart theory handles de re modality without trans-world identity; Kripke’s objection begs the question
1.Counterpart theory: individuals exist in only one world. De re modal claims are about an individual’s counterparts in other worlds — individuals relevantly similar to them. “Humphrey could have won” means some world contains a Humphrey-counterpart who wins.
2.Kripke objects that Humphrey is not comforted by a counterpart winning. But this confuses the metaphysical claim (what kind of world is possible) with a personal welfare claim (whether Humphrey benefits). The comfort objection is a non-sequitur.
3.The alternative — trans-world identity of individuals across concrete universes — faces severe metaphysical problems (the problem of accidental intrinsics, the logic of identity across worlds). Counterpart theory avoids them.
∴Counterpart theory provides a coherent account of de re modality without trans-world identity; Kripke’s comfort objection is a non-sequitur; counterpart theory is superior to trans-world identity accounts.

Chair,

I want to develop and defend Lewis’s modal realism against two objections that are often pressed as decisive: the epistemological objection and the isolation objection. These objections are frequently presented as if they immediately refute modal realism, but careful examination shows that neither succeeds.

i. the epistemological objection

Epistemological objection — if other worlds are causally isolated from us, we can have no knowledge of them; but we do have modal knowledge; therefore either modal realism is false or modal knowledge is inexplicableThe epistemological objection runs as follows: we have genuine modal knowledge — we know that it is possible for there to be talking donkeys, that it is necessary that 2+2=4, that it is contingent that I am sitting. If modal realism is true, this knowledge is knowledge about other concrete universes. But those universes are causally isolated from us: we cannot perceive them, interact with them, or receive causal signals from them. How, then, do we know anything about them? The objection has a Benacerrafian structure: it mirrors the objection to Platonism about mathematical objects that Paul Benacerraf pressed in 1973. I want to show that the epistemological objection fails for modal realism for the same reason the Benacerrafian objection, properly understood, does not refute mathematical Platonism. The objection presupposes that all knowledge is grounded in causal contact with its objects. This presupposition is wrong. We know a priori truths without causal contact with their truth-makers. We know logical truths, mathematical truths, and — on Lewis’s view — modal truths, by a combination of conceptual analysis, inference from the structure of our concepts, and philosophical argument. None of this requires causal contact with other worlds.

ii. the isolation objection

Isolation objection — other worlds are causally isolated from the actual world; this means they are irrelevant to what happens here; but modal facts are relevant to actual deliberation and explanation; therefore isolated worlds cannot ground modal factsThe isolation objection is different and sharper: causal isolation does not merely prevent knowledge of other worlds; it makes those worlds irrelevant to what happens in our world. If no causal influence passes between worlds, how can facts about other worlds be relevant to whether an action is possible for me, or to whether a counterfactual about actual events is true? My response has two parts. First: causal isolation does not entail explanatory irrelevance. Mathematical facts about numbers are causally isolated from physical events but are explanatorily relevant to why physical systems behave as they do. Facts about other worlds are similarly causally isolated but explanatorily relevant: they ground the truth conditions for modal and counterfactual claims that figure in our explanations of actual events. Second: the isolation objection, pressed consistently, refutes ersatz theories as well. Abstract ersatz worlds are no more causally connected to the actual world than Lewis’s concrete worlds. If isolation makes worlds irrelevant, it makes all theories of possible worlds irrelevant, not just modal realism.

iii. the recombination principle and plenitude

Recombination — Lewis's Principle of Recombination: for any individuals and any arrangement of them in space and time, there is a world that contains such individuals in such an arrangement; this grounds the plenitude of worlds without ad hoc stipulationOne of modal realism’s most important assets is the Principle of Recombination: roughly, for any individuals — from this world or any other — and any arrangement of them in space and time, there is a possible world containing them in that arrangement. This principle grounds the plenitude of Lewis’s worlds: they contain all the individuals and arrangements that are genuinely possible. Ersatz and actualist theories struggle to match this plenitude. Armstrong’s combinatorialism, for instance, can only recombine existing actual universals and particulars; it cannot account for alien properties — properties that are genuinely possible but not instantiated in the actual world. This is a severe limitation. If there could have been a colour that nothing in the actual world instantiates — and there surely could — Armstrong’s theory has no resources to accommodate it. Modal realism accommodates it straightforwardly: there is a world whose inhabitants have that colour. The concreteness of Lewis’s worlds is precisely what enables this plenitude.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The epistemological objection fails: modal knowledge does not require causal contact with possible worlds
1.The epistemological objection: other worlds are causally isolated from us; causal contact is required for knowledge; therefore we cannot know modal truths.
2.But causal contact is not required for all knowledge. We know a priori truths (logical, mathematical, modal) by conceptual analysis and philosophical argument, not by causal interaction with their truth-makers.
3.The Benacerrafian structure of the objection shows it applies equally to mathematical Platonism; since mathematical Platonism is not thereby refuted, neither is modal realism.
∴The epistemological objection fails: a priori knowledge does not require causal contact; modal knowledge is a priori; the objection applies equally to mathematical Platonism and does not refute it.
Argument II — The isolation objection fails: causal isolation does not entail explanatory irrelevance; and it applies to all possible worlds theories equally
1.Mathematical facts are causally isolated from physical events but explanatorily relevant to them. Facts about other worlds are similarly isolated but explanatorily relevant: they ground the truth conditions of modal and counterfactual claims.
2.Abstract ersatz worlds are no more causally connected to the actual world than Lewis’s concrete worlds. If isolation makes worlds irrelevant, it refutes all possible worlds theories, not just modal realism.
3.The isolation objection is therefore either too weak (failing to refute modal realism) or too strong (refuting all possible worlds theories). Neither horn benefits the opposition.
∴The isolation objection fails: it applies equally to ersatz and actualist theories; causal isolation does not entail explanatory irrelevance; the objection is either too weak or too strong to count against modal realism specifically.
Argument III — The Principle of Recombination grounds plenitude and accommodates alien properties; combinatorialism cannot match this
1.Lewis’s Principle of Recombination: for any individuals and any arrangement, there is a world containing them in that arrangement. This grounds the plenitude of worlds non-stipulatively.
2.Armstrong’s combinatorialism can only recombine actual universals and particulars; it cannot accommodate alien properties — properties genuinely possible but uninstantiated in the actual world.
3.The possibility of alien properties is strongly intuitive: there could have been a colour nothing actual instantiates. Modal realism accommodates this; combinatorialism cannot. This is a decisive advantage for modal realism.
∴The Principle of Recombination grounds plenitude non-stipulatively and accommodates alien properties; combinatorialism’s inability to handle alien properties is a decisive disadvantage; modal realism handles what combinatorialism cannot.

Chair,

I want to press the cost-benefit argument for modal realism more explicitly, because I think the opposition consistently understates the benefits and overstates the costs. The case for modal realism is not that it is the most immediately intuitive theory — it is not — but that it is the best theory: the one that achieves the most theoretical work with the fewest primitive notions and the most unified ontological basis.

i. the ersatz theories and their hidden costs

Ersatz costs — ersatz theories substitute abstract worlds for concrete ones but inherit Lewis's modal analyses without grounding them; the abstract objects they invoke are either mysterious or secretly modalAdams, Plantinga, and other actualists propose ersatz possible worlds: maximally consistent sets of propositions (world-stories), or Plantingian states of affairs, or linguistic descriptions of how things might be. These theories seem to avoid the ontological extravagance of modal realism by replacing concrete worlds with abstract objects. But what is an abstract object? At minimum, it is something that exists necessarily, is not spatiotemporally located, and has no causal powers. This ontology is not obviously less puzzling than Lewis’s concrete worlds. Moreover, the ersatz theories inherit all of Lewis’s modal analyses — they analyse modal notions in terms of possible worlds — without being able to ground those analyses. What makes a set of propositions “maximally consistent”? Consistency is a modal notion: a set is consistent if it is possible for all its members to be true together. The ersatz theory analyses modality in terms of worlds, which are themselves defined in terms of modality. The analysis is circular. Lewis’s concrete worlds, by contrast, allow a genuine non-circular analysis: “possible” just means “holds at some world,” and worlds are entities of the same fundamental kind as our own, requiring no further modal analysis.

ii. qualitative vs. quantitative parsimony

Qualitative parsimony — the relevant measure of ontological economy is the number of fundamental categories, not the number of entities; Lewis posits one fundamental category (concrete spatiotemporal systems); rivals posit many (concrete world + abstract entities + primitive modality)The standard objection to modal realism is that it posits too many entities. But as Lewis himself argues, the relevant measure of ontological economy in theory choice is qualitative parsimony — the number of fundamental kinds of entity — not quantitative parsimony — the total number of entities. Consider the alternatives. The materialist about the actual world already accepts the existence of particles, fields, spacetime regions, and their combinations — a very large number of entities — without thinking this makes materialism unacceptable. Lewis adds more entities of the same fundamental kind. Actualist theories, by contrast, add new fundamental categories: abstract propositions, states of affairs, or properties, none of which is obviously less mysterious than a concrete universe, all of which require their own account of their nature and our access to them. If we measure parsimony qualitatively, modal realism is surprisingly economical: it invokes one fundamental kind of entity and generates its modal analyses from that base.

iii. the analysis of properties

Properties as sets — modal realism analyses properties as sets of possible individuals; this avoids the co-extension problem and the problem of uninstantiated properties; rivals need primitive universals or tropes, adding to their ontological commitmentsOne of the most compelling advantages of modal realism is its analysis of properties. On the standard account, properties are universals — abstract entities that are instantiated by multiple objects. But universals face the problem of co-extension: if two properties are necessarily co-extensive in the actual world, the universal account cannot distinguish them without invoking modal notions. Lewis’s account identifies properties with sets of possible individuals — all the individuals, across all worlds, that have the property. This immediately distinguishes co-extensive properties: the property of being a horse and the property of being a creature with a horse’s DNA are co-extensive in the actual world but pick out different sets of possible individuals. Armstrong’s theory of universals is one of the main alternatives: it posits fundamental universals as an additional ontological category. But this addition is a cost, not an advantage over modal realism. Lewis’s analysis of properties in terms of sets of possible individuals is one of the clearest demonstrations that his theory actually simplifies, rather than complicates, our fundamental ontology.

Argument I — Ersatz theories are circular: they analyse modality in terms of worlds defined using modal notions
1.Ersatz theories define possible worlds as maximally consistent sets of propositions (or states of affairs, or descriptions). Consistency is a modal notion: a set is consistent if it is possible for all its members to be true together.
2.The ersatz analysis of modality is therefore circular: it analyses “possible” in terms of possible worlds, which are defined in terms of “possible.”
3.Lewis’s analysis is non-circular: possible worlds are concrete entities of the same kind as the actual world; “possible” just means “holds at some world” with no further modal analysis needed.
∴Ersatz theories are circular in their analysis of modality; Lewis’s concrete worlds provide a genuinely non-circular analysis; this is a decisive advantage for modal realism over all ersatz alternatives.
Argument II — Qualitative parsimony favours modal realism: one fundamental category versus many
1.Quantitative parsimony (fewer entities) is less important than qualitative parsimony (fewer fundamental categories) in theory choice. A theory with more entities of one kind is more economical than one with fewer entities of many puzzling kinds.
2.Modal realism invokes one fundamental category: concrete spatiotemporal systems. Rivals invoke concrete worlds plus abstract propositions, universals, states of affairs, or primitive modal operators — many additional fundamental categories.
3.Measured by qualitative parsimony, modal realism is more economical than its rivals despite its quantitative extravagance.
∴Qualitative parsimony favours modal realism: it invokes one fundamental category while rivals invoke many; the ontological extravagance objection mistakes quantitative for qualitative parsimony.
Argument III — Modal realism analyses properties as sets of possible individuals, avoiding co-extension collapse without primitive universals
1.Properties as sets of actual individuals collapse co-extensive properties to the same property. Properties as universals add a primitive ontological category requiring its own analysis.
2.Lewis’s analysis: properties are sets of possible individuals (across all worlds). Co-extensive-in-actuality properties come apart across worlds, avoiding co-extension collapse without primitive universals.
3.Armstrong’s universals are an additional ontological commitment; Lewis’s analysis achieves the same work with entities already posited — sets of possible individuals.
∴Modal realism’s analysis of properties as sets of possible individuals is superior to universals: it avoids co-extension collapse without adding a new primitive category, using resources already available in the framework.

Combinatorialism & the Naturalist AlternativeDavid Armstrong — A Combinatorial Theory of Possibility (1989)
Possible worlds can be analysed as combinatorial constructions from actual particulars and universals, without postulating a vast plurality of concrete universes. The Combinatorial Theory of Possibility holds that the possible states of affairs are all and only the recombinations of actual particulars and universals. This is ontologically economical in the relevant sense: it invokes only the actual entities we already have reason to believe in. Lewis’s appeal to alien properties — properties not instantiated in the actual world — is a symptom of his over-permissive modal ontology, not a genuine constraint that any adequate theory of possibility must satisfy.

Actualism & Possible Worlds as World-StoriesRobert Adams — “Theories of Actuality” (1974) & “Actualism and Thisness” (1981)
Only actual things exist. Possible worlds are not further concrete universes but maximal consistent sets of propositions — world-stories — that describe how things could be. The actual world is not merely the world we happen to inhabit; it is the only world there is, and “actual” is not an indexical but a substantive predicate that picks out the unique world that genuinely exists. The Incredulous Stare, Talking Donkeys & the Limits of OntologyPeter van Inwagen — “Two Concepts of Possible Worlds” (1986) & subsequent work
Modal realism is not merely counterintuitive; it is incredible. The theory requires the existence of uncountably many concrete universes, most of which contain no mathematics, no logic, no philosophical argument, and no reason for believing in them. Chair,

I want to make a point about methodology before I get to the substance. Lewis presents modal realism as the outcome of a cost-benefit analysis of competing theories, and he says the extraordinary theoretical benefits of his view justify the ontological extravagance. But this methodology presupposes that modality is the sort of thing that admits of reductive analysis in terms of some more fundamental ontological base. I want to challenge this presupposition directly. Modal facts — facts about what is possible and necessary — may not be reducible to any more fundamental facts. They may be primitive.

“Possible worlds are stipulated, not discovered by powerful telescopes. There is no reason to believe that the actual world is one of a vast plurality of concrete universes. When I ask whether Nixon could have lost, I am asking about Nixon — this very man — not about someone in another universe who merely resembles him.” — Kripke
i. possible worlds are stipulated, not discovered

Stipulation vs. discovery — we stipulate possible worlds to represent modal possibilities; we do not discover them; the ontological question of whether stipulated worlds are real is answered by the way we introduce possible worlds talk, not by metaphysical investigationLewis says possible worlds are as real as the actual world, discovered through the same methods of philosophical and scientific inference. I deny this. Possible worlds are introduced into philosophical discourse by stipulation. We say: let a possible world be a way things could have been. This is a technical device for representing modal facts, not a discovery of new concrete entities. The question “but are possible worlds real?” is like asking whether the number four is real after we have stipulated the natural numbers. The question makes sense only if we have a prior understanding of what it would mean for a possible world to be real — which requires an understanding of the modal notion of what could have been the case. But then the modal notion is already understood prior to, and independently of, the possible worlds framework. We do not need the framework to understand modality; we use our prior understanding of modality to construct the framework. Lewis has the explanatory order backwards.

ii. rigid designation and the failure of counterpart theory

Rigid designation — names pick out the same individual in every possible world; this makes de re modal claims genuinely about specific individuals, not their counterparts; counterpart theory replaces this with similarity, losing the genuine content of de re claimsConsider the statement “Aristotle might not have been a philosopher.” On my view, this is a claim about Aristotle — this very man — and his possible careers. It is true because there is a possible world in which Aristotle, the very same individual, pursues a different vocation. Names are rigid designators: “Aristotle” picks out the same individual in every possible world in which Aristotle exists. On Lewis’s counterpart theory, by contrast, “Aristotle might not have been a philosopher” is a claim about some other individual in another world who merely resembles Aristotle sufficiently to count as his counterpart. This is not what we mean. When I say Aristotle could have been a carpenter, I am saying something about Aristotle, not about some carpenter in another universe who happens to be similar to him. The counterpart theory — as Humphrey himself would notice, if he could think about these things — gives the wrong truth conditions for de re modal claims. It replaces claims about specific individuals with claims about similar individuals, changing the subject.

iii. the anti-reductionist alternative

Anti-reductionism — modal notions are primitive; they cannot be and do not need to be reduced to non-modal facts about concrete universes; taking modality as primitive is not a theoretical failure but an accurate recognition of its irreducibilityMy positive position is anti-reductionist: modal facts are primitive. They cannot be and do not need to be reduced to non-modal facts about concrete universes or anything else. When we say it is possible for there to be unicorns, we mean something irreducible: it is genuinely the case that the world could have included unicorns, and this is a modal fact about the actual world, not a non-modal fact about some other universe. Lewis says taking modality as primitive is no analysis at all — it leaves modal discourse unexplained. But “unexplained” is not the same as “ungrounded” or “unintelligible.” We understand modal discourse perfectly well without a Lewisian analysis. We knew what “possible” and “necessary” meant before Lewis invented modal realism, and our understanding did not become less clear when we learned about it. The demand for a reductive analysis of modality is a demand Lewis imposes, not a constraint that arises from the phenomena themselves.

iv. identity across worlds without trans-world individuals

Identity across worlds — possible worlds can include the same individuals as the actual world without requiring trans-world spatiotemporal connections; individuals are identified across worlds by their essential properties, not by spatiotemporal continuityLewis argues that trans-world identity — the same individual existing in multiple worlds — faces the problem of accidental intrinsics: if Humphrey is six feet tall in the actual world but five foot ten in another, then he both is and is not six feet tall. Lewis says this is contradictory and therefore trans-world identity is impossible. Counterpart theory avoids the contradiction by making different-world individuals numerically distinct. But the contradiction is only apparent. Modal properties are properties relativised to worlds, not absolute properties. Being six feet tall in the actual world and being five foot ten in a possible world are not contradictory: they are world-indexed properties. I am six feet tall in the actual world, and Humphrey is five foot ten in the possible world where he stops growing at fifteen. There is no contradiction any more than there is a contradiction in my being taller than someone in London and shorter than someone else in Paris. Lewis’s problem of accidental intrinsics dissolves once we see that height, like many properties, is world-indexed.
