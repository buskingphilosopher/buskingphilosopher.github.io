Mr President,

I want to begin with what I take to be the most striking fact about the contemporary debate: the emergence of epistemicism as its dominant position. My colleagues across the floor will tell you that vague predicates draw sharp boundaries — that there is a precise number of grains of wheat that constitutes the minimal heap, a precise number of hairs at which baldness begins, a precise moment at which a child becomes an adult — and that our failure to know these boundaries is simply ignorance, explicable on general principles about the limits of knowledge. This view is, in my judgment, the philosophical equivalent of a conjuring trick: it produces a solution by refusing to engage with the actual problem. The problem is not merely that we do not know where the boundary is. The problem is that the very idea of a sharp boundary for a vague predicate is inconsistent with what it is to be a competent user of that predicate.

"A vague predicate is one that is tolerant with respect to some dimension of comparison: it is such that if it applies to one item, it applies equally to another item that differs from the first only negligibly along that dimension. 'Bald' is tolerant with respect to hair-count. 'Tall' is tolerant with respect to height. 'Heap' is tolerant with respect to grain-number. The sorites argument merely makes this tolerance explicit. It does not generate an error — it inherits one from the predicate itself."
i. the tolerance principle and why it cannot be abandoned

Tolerance — constitutive of vague meaning; not an optional extra; the sorites premise is analytic of vaguenessIn my 1975 paper "On the Coherence of Vague Predicates" and in subsequent work including Truth and Objectivity and my papers on quandary, I have consistently argued that the tolerance of a vague predicate is not a contingent feature of it that might be surrendered in the face of the paradox. Tolerance is constitutive: what makes a predicate vague is precisely that it does not discriminate between cases that are only negligibly different along the relevant dimension. To deny tolerance — to assert, with the epistemicist, that there is after all a single grain of wheat whose addition converts a non-heap into a heap — is to change the subject. It is to introduce a concept that has the surface grammar of 'heap' but lacks the semantic character that makes 'heap' the word it is. The epistemicist's "solution" dissolves the paradox by replacing the vague predicate with a precise one and pretending they are the same. Competent speakers would reject any proffered sharp threshold as simply incorrect — and their rejection is criterially decisive for questions of meaning.

ii. the quandary of borderline cases — neither truth-apt nor ignorance

Quandary — borderline cases produce genuine impasse; neither true nor false; not mere ignorance; the phenomenology of vaguenessIn my paper "On Being in a Quandary" I introduced the notion of quandary to describe the distinctive epistemic state that borderline cases produce. When a man is borderline bald — forty or fifty hairs distributed across his scalp — I am not in the state of an agent who lacks information and could, in principle, obtain it. I am in a state of genuine impasse: there is no method of inquiry that would settle the matter, no evidence I could gather that would resolve it. This is not because the fact is too far away from me — it is because there is no fact to be found. The epistemicist tells me I am simply ignorant of a fact that is there. But the phenomenology of vagueness — the feeling that a borderline question is unanswerable in principle, not merely in practice — is itself evidence against the epistemicist picture. An agent who genuinely believes there is a sharp boundary for 'bald' should feel motivated to seek information that would locate it; the universal absence of this motivation is a datum that demands explanation.

iii. why classical logic must be revised — the case for intuitionism

Intuitionism — reject excluded middle for borderline statements; no assertion without warrant; logic revised not abandoned; Wright's revisionismThe correct response to the quandary of borderline cases is not to abandon logic but to revise it in the direction of intuitionism. Classical logic validates the law of excluded middle: for any proposition P, either P or not-P. Applied to borderline cases, this demands that either the borderline man is bald or he is not bald. But we have no warrant for asserting either disjunct. A position — the intuitionist one — that permits us to say "neither is assertable" without requiring us to assert "neither is true" provides exactly the resources we need. The logic of borderline assertions is one in which the assertion conditions are not equivalent to the truth conditions. I can withhold assertion of "P or not-P" about a borderline case without committing myself to the truth-value-gap claim that the supervaluationist makes. This is a more careful, more philosophically honest position than either asserting excluded middle (classical) or asserting a third truth-value (many-valued logic).

iv. against supervaluationism — the penumbral connections problem and higher-order vagueness

Against supervaluationism — higher-order vagueness; the sharp boundary problem relocates; 'definitely' is itself vague; Fine's own abandonmentSupervaluationism — the view that borderline statements lack a truth-value and that logical truths hold when true on every permissible "sharpening" of the predicate — faces a devastating problem of higher-order vagueness. The theory draws a sharp line between what is "definitely" true and what is borderline. But the operator 'Definitely' is itself vague: there are cases that are neither definitely definitely bald nor definitely not definitely bald. The sharp line between the definite and the indefinite reintroduces the very problem it was supposed to solve, now at a higher order. Kit Fine himself — the architect of supervaluationism's canonical 1975 formulation — has in his recent work abandoned it for precisely this reason, developing instead his "global" approach to indeterminacy. The failure of supervaluationism to handle higher-order vagueness shows that any theory that tries to preserve classical semantics while accommodating borderline cases by introducing a new operator will face this regress without terminus.

v. the forced march and why epistemicism fails its own test

Forced march — step-by-step compulsion; epistemicist has no explanation of the phenomenology; seamless transition is real; sharp threshold is incredibleConsider the "forced march" sorites: a competent speaker is confronted, one by one, with members of a sorites series — 1 hair, 2 hairs, 3 hairs, ... — and asked at each step whether the man is bald. The epistemicist says there is a sharp threshold. At step n the man is bald; at step n+1 he is not. But any competent speaker forced to march will experience a seamless transition, not an abrupt threshold. The epistemicist must explain why — if the threshold is real — no competent speaker has ever found it, why every competent speaker experiences the transition as gradual, and why the very idea of a sharp threshold strikes us as linguistically improper. Williamson's answer — that the threshold exists but is unknowable — does nothing to explain the phenomenology of seamless transition. It predicts that there should be a threshold we cannot locate; it cannot predict that there should be no threshold that can even be approximately located. The phenomenology demands a semantic explanation, not an epistemological excuse.

Argument I — Tolerance is constitutive: the sorites premise is analytic of vagueness
1.A predicate is vague if and only if it is tolerant: it applies to one item whenever it applies to another that differs from it only negligibly along the decisive dimension.
2.The sorites major premise ("if n grains make a heap, so do n+1") simply articulates this tolerance; it is not a contingent empirical claim but an analytic consequence of the predicate's meaning.
3.To deny the sorites premise — as the epistemicist does — is to replace the vague predicate with a precise one; this dissolves the paradox not by solving it but by changing the subject.
∴The tolerance of vague predicates cannot be abandoned without abandoning vagueness itself; epistemicism fails to solve the sorites and instead covertly eliminates the phenomenon it purports to explain.
Argument II — Quandary: borderline cases are genuinely unanswerable, not merely unknown
1.Genuine ignorance produces motivation to inquire: an agent who believes a fact obtains but is hidden from them will seek evidence, undertake investigation, and feel the gap as a deficiency to be remedied.
2.Borderline cases produce no such motivation: the universal absence of attempts to locate the precise threshold of baldness, or the exact moment of manhood, is itself a semantic datum.
3.The state competent speakers are in when confronted with borderline cases — quandary — is phenomenologically distinct from ignorance: it is an impasse, not a gap to be filled.
∴The phenomenology of borderline cases evidences genuine semantic indeterminacy, not ignorance; epistemicism misclassifies the state, predicting an investigative impulse that speakers universally lack.
Argument III — Intuitionism: excluded middle should be withheld for borderline cases
1.Classical logic's law of excluded middle (P ∨ ¬P for any P) demands that for any borderline case, either the predicate applies or it does not; but we have no warrant for asserting either disjunct about a borderline case.
2.Intuitionist logic permits withholding assertion of "P ∨ ¬P" without requiring the assertion of a third truth-value or the claim that both disjuncts are false — it simply demands warrant for assertion.
3.This position is more honest than classical logic (which over-asserts) and more careful than many-valued logic (which over-determines truth-values); it respects the actual assertability conditions for vague predicates.
∴Intuitionist revision of classical logic is the appropriate response to vagueness: it withholds commitment where no warrant exists without generating false precision or spurious third truth-values.
Argument IV — Higher-order vagueness defeats supervaluationism
1.Supervaluationism divides cases into "definitely true," "definitely false," and "borderline" (gaps); but the predicate "definitely bald" is itself vague — there are borderline cases of definiteness.
2.Any operator 'Def' introduced to demarcate the definite from the borderline will itself generate higher-order borderline cases, producing an infinite regress without resolution.
3.Kit Fine, the theory's architect, has himself abandoned supervaluationism for this reason — the theory cannot avoid reintroducing the sharp boundaries it sought to eliminate, now hidden inside the 'Def' operator.
∴Supervaluationism fails its own test: it relocates the sharp boundary into the operator 'Definitely' rather than eliminating it; higher-order vagueness exposes this as an irresolvable internal failure.
Argument V — The forced march: epistemicism cannot explain seamless transition
1.The forced march sorites requires a speaker to classify each member of a sorites series in sequence; every competent speaker experiences a gradual, seamless transition, not an abrupt threshold.
2.Epistemicism predicts a threshold exists; it cannot predict that the threshold would feel indiscoverable even approximately — an approximation to the sharp boundary should be locatable even if its exact location is not.
3.The universality of seamless phenomenology — across all vague predicates, all competent speakers — demands a semantic explanation (there is no threshold) rather than an epistemological excuse (we just cannot find it).
∴The forced march provides decisive evidence against epistemicism: the phenomenology of gradual transition, universal and robust, is incompatible with the existence of a sharp boundary however well-hidden.

Mr President,

My approach to vagueness differs from Wright's in one crucial respect: I believe bivalence can be preserved. I do not think we need to revise classical logic, introduce truth-value gaps, or embrace intuitionism. What we need is a better account of what vague predicates actually mean — and of why the sorites argument, though apparently valid, is in fact based on a misunderstanding of how vague expressions function semantically. My account — the Multiple Range Theory — holds that vagueness consists essentially in a predicate's possession of multiple equally permissible ranges of application, and that this plurality is not a defect but a constitutive feature of how such predicates work.

"A competent speaker who classifies a borderline-bald man as bald on one occasion, and then classifies a man with the same hair as not bald on another occasion, has not made an error on either occasion. Both classifications are equally correct. The predicate 'bald' has multiple permissible ranges of application, and the speaker has exercised different, equally competent choices on each occasion. This is not inconsistency — it is the expression of the predicate's semantic character."
i. the multiple range theory — vagueness without gaps

Multiple Range Theory — multiple permissible ranges; semantic arbitrariness; bivalence preserved; no gaps or third truth-valuesThe Multiple Range Theory, which I develop in Unruly Words (2014), holds that a vague predicate like 'bald' is associated not with a single range of application but with multiple ranges, any one of which a competent speaker may legitimately employ on a given occasion. These ranges overlap in the clear cases — all of them include obviously bald men and exclude obviously non-bald men — but diverge in the borderline region, where some ranges include a case and others exclude it. For any borderline case, it is true (relative to some ranges) that the predicate applies and true (relative to other ranges) that it does not. There is no truth-value gap, no indeterminacy, no third truth-value. Bivalence is preserved: every statement is either true or false relative to whichever range is in play. What is distinctive about vague predicates is that the choice among these equally permissible ranges is semantically arbitrary — no competent fact about meaning determines which range must be used.

ii. borderline cases as incompatibilist — green vs. not-green, not green vs. blue

Incompatibilist analysis — borderline cases defined by contraries not contradictories; blue-green cases; not a truth-value gap but a competition between positive classificationsA crucial innovation in my account is what I call the Incompatibilist Analysis of borderline cases. Standard accounts define borderline cases as those to which neither the predicate nor its negation applies — a truth-value gap. But this gets the phenomenology wrong. A borderline blue-green tile does not feel like a case where neither 'blue' nor 'not blue' applies; it feels like a case where 'blue' and 'green' — two positive classifications, contraries rather than contradictories — are both in competition. The difficulty is not that neither applies; the difficulty is that both seem to apply, and applying one excludes the other. This shift from the contradictory analysis (gap between P and not-P) to the contrary analysis (competition between P and Q) transforms our understanding of what borderline cases are. They are not gaps in our classifications — they are sites of genuine competition between incompatible but equally valid positive descriptions.

iii. the sorites refuted — semantic arbitrariness breaks the inductive step

Sorites refuted — the major premise assumes a single range; multiple ranges make the inductive step fail relative to any fixed range; no sharp boundary within any rangeThe sorites argument takes the form: (1) n grains make a heap; (2) if n grains make a heap, so do n+1; (3) therefore any number of grains makes a heap. The multiple range theory shows exactly where (2) fails. Within any single permissible range, there will be a sharp boundary — some n such that n grains make a heap on this range and n+1 do not. There is no vagueness within a range: ranges are precise. What makes 'heap' vague is that multiple ranges are equally permissible, so there is no single n at which the boundary falls across all permissible ranges. The inductive premise is false relative to any fixed range. The sorites paradox rests on a conflation: it treats the predicate as if it had a single range with no sharp boundary, when in fact it has multiple ranges each of which is internally precise. The apparent force of the inductive premise derives from failing to hold fixed which range is in play.

iv. against contextualism — the variability is not context-sensitivity

Against contextualism — Raffman's earlier view rejected; multiple ranges are fixed features of meaning not context-shifts; context-sensitivity insufficient to explain semantic arbitrarinessI want to distinguish the Multiple Range Theory from contextualism — a view I myself once held and have since rejected. Contextualists such as Hans Kamp and Scott Soames argue that vague predicates are context-sensitive: the range that determines their application shifts with context, including the comparison class, the conversational stakes, and what has been said before. This view has genuine appeal, but it is not sufficient. Even when all contextual factors are held fixed — same comparison class, same conversation, same stakes — competent speakers may apply a vague predicate to a borderline case on one occasion and withhold it on another. The variability is not explained by context-shifts because no context-shift has occurred. It is explained by the fact that the predicate itself has multiple equally permissible ranges, and the choice among them is irreducibly semantically arbitrary. Vagueness is not context-dependence — it is constitutive plurality of application ranges.

v. judgmental hysteresis and the evidence for multiple ranges

Hysteresis — path-dependent classification; same object classified differently depending on ordering; empirical evidence for multiple ranges; not mere inconsistencyThere is striking empirical evidence for the Multiple Range Theory in the phenomenon of judgmental hysteresis. When subjects are asked to classify a sorites series in sequence — from one extreme to the other — their classification of borderline cases depends on the path by which they arrive at them. A subject proceeding from the clearly bald end will classify as bald borderline cases that the same subject, proceeding from the clearly non-bald end, classifies as not bald. This path-dependence — hysteresis — is precisely what the Multiple Range Theory predicts. Different paths through the sorites series engage different permissible ranges; the range in play determines the classification of borderline cases; and the engagement of different ranges on different traversals produces different, equally correct classifications. This is not inconsistency — it is the mark of a predicate whose application is genuinely plural, whose ranges are multiple and equally legitimate, and whose borderline region is a domain of semantically arbitrary but correct choice.

Mr President,

My opponents on this side of the house take vagueness to be a semantic phenomenon — something in language, in meaning, in the relation between words and the world — and conclude that classical logic must be abandoned or revised accordingly. I want to argue for the opposite view: that vagueness is an epistemic phenomenon, a form of ignorance, and that the preservation of classical logic and bivalence is both possible and theoretically preferable. The proposition invites us to solve the problem of vagueness by distorting our logic. I invite the House to consider that the problem of vagueness is better solved by understanding our ignorance.

"Every borderline case of 'bald' is either bald or not bald. There is a fact of the matter. What we lack is knowledge of the fact. This is not a retreat to a commonsense platitude. It is a philosophically substantive claim: that the mechanisms which fix the extensions of vague predicates — use, context, causal history — determine sharp boundaries, but determine them in a way that makes their precise location inaccessible to us. The inaccessibility is explained by independently motivated epistemological principles. The sharpness is a consequence of bivalence, which should be preserved."
i. the abductive case for epistemicism — simplicity and logical conservatism

Abductive argument — classical logic preserved; bivalence; no revision required; epistemicism is theoretically simpler than semantic alternativesIn Vagueness (1994), I argue for epistemicism on grounds of theoretical simplicity and logical conservatism. Classical logic and bivalence are among the most successful, most thoroughly tested, most widely applicable formal frameworks in the history of thought. Any proposal to revise them — to abandon excluded middle, to introduce truth-value gaps, to allow sentences to be "true to a degree" — bears an enormous burden of justification. The proposition has produced no such justification. Wright's intuitionism and Raffman's multiple-range theory both incur theoretical costs: the former requires a fundamental revision of logic; the latter requires a semantics in which truth is range-relative in a way that cannot be stabilised. Epistemicism requires no such revision. It requires only that we acknowledge that our access to semantic facts is limited — a claim independently supported by our general epistemology of meaning and mind.

ii. the margin-for-error principle — why ignorance in borderline cases is inevitable

Margin for error — knowledge requires safety; nearby worlds with different meaning; unknowability is epistemically explained; not a mysteryThe central challenge to epistemicism is to explain why, if there are sharp boundaries, we cannot know where they are. My answer uses the margin-for-error principle, which I develop both in Vagueness and in Knowledge and Its Limits. The principle: if one knows that P, then P must be true not only in the actual world but in nearby possible worlds in which one's evidence and cognitive state are the same. For vague predicates, the boundary is determined by the complex statistical pattern of use among all speakers of the language — a pattern that is extraordinarily sensitive to small variations. A world in which the pattern of use is very slightly different — in which one speaker uses 'bald' very slightly differently — may be a world in which the boundary has shifted. But such a world is, for an individual speaker, epistemically indistinguishable from the actual world. Hence even a true belief that a borderline man is bald fails to count as knowledge: it would fail the safety condition in nearby worlds where the boundary is slightly different. The ignorance is principled, not mysterious.

iii. against the tolerance principle — it generates explicit contradiction

Against tolerance — generates contradiction; Wright cannot embrace it without absurdity; the paradox shows tolerance is false; competence does not require itWright argues that tolerance is constitutive of vagueness. But if tolerance is true, the sorites argument is sound — and the conclusion of the sorites argument, that no number of grains makes a heap, is false. No move available to Wright avoids this: he cannot embrace both tolerance and the falsity of the sorites conclusion without contradiction. His proposal — to withhold assertion of the inductive premise rather than deny it — is logically insufficient. If tolerance is not assertable, it is not an analytic truth of the predicate's meaning; if it is assertable, the sorites conclusion follows. Wright attempts to hold a position that is neither fish nor fowl: tolerance is apparently analytic but cannot be asserted. This is not philosophical subtlety — it is evasion. The correct response is to deny the tolerance principle: competent use of 'bald' does not require that the predicate fail to discriminate between minimally different cases. It requires that we have no reliable method of locating the point of discrimination — which is exactly what epistemicism predicts.

iv. bivalence and the argument from metalinguistic reflection

Bivalence — metalinguistic argument; "neither true nor false" is itself a truth claim that contradicts bivalence; the gap theorist's self-refutationThe proposition's semantic theories — supervaluationism, many-valued logic, gap theories — all involve the claim that certain sentences are neither true nor false. But this claim is itself a truth claim: to say that "Harry is bald" is neither true nor false is to say something true about that sentence. If bivalence fails for object-language sentences, it apparently applies at the metalinguistic level — making the metalanguage classical even as the object language is revised. This is an unstable position. Williamson's argument in Chapter 7 of Vagueness runs as follows: if "P" is neither true nor false, then it is not the case that P, and it is not the case that not-P — but "not the case that P" just is the same as "not-P." So the gap theorist must assert "not-P" while denying that "not-P" is true. This is a contradiction from which no semantic manoeuvre escapes. The correct response is to preserve bivalence: every statement is either true or false, including borderline statements, and our failure to know which reflects our epistemic limitations, not reality's indeterminacy.

v. the instability of meaning — why semantic facts are unknowable

Meaning instability — use-dependence; butterfly effect on semantic content; aggregate patterns determine boundaries; individual access is limited by the complexity of collective useThe distinctive feature of epistemicism — what makes it a positive account rather than a mere denial — is its theory of why semantic facts are unknowable for vague predicates. I argue that the extension of 'bald' is determined by the complex aggregate pattern of use among all speakers of English. This aggregate pattern is extraordinarily sensitive to variation: small changes in how speakers use 'bald' can shift the sharp boundary significantly. I compare this to Lorenz's butterfly effect in meteorology: long-range weather prediction is impossible not because weather is indeterminate but because the relevant causal system is chaotically sensitive to initial conditions. Similarly, the location of the semantic boundary of 'bald' is unknowable not because it does not exist but because it depends on the aggregate use-pattern in a way that exceeds any individual's epistemic access. This explains the appearance of vagueness — the felt absence of a fact — without requiring any revision of logic or semantics.

Argument I — Abductive case: epistemicism is theoretically simpler than semantic revision
1.Classical logic and bivalence are maximally tested, maximally general, and carry no known counter-instances from outside the vagueness literature; revising them requires extraordinary justification.
2.Semantic alternatives — intuitionism, gap theories, many-valued logic, multiple-range theory — each require substantial additional theoretical apparatus (truth-value gaps, range-relative semantics, revised inference rules) that epistemicism avoids.
3.The abductive argument favours the theoretically simpler view that preserves classical logic; epistemicism requires only an independently motivated epistemological principle (margin for error), not a revision of logic or semantics.
∴Epistemicism is abductively superior: it preserves classical logic and bivalence while explaining vagueness using independently motivated epistemological principles, avoiding the theoretical costs of all semantic alternatives.
Argument II — Margin for error: unknowability of vague boundaries is epistemically explained
1.The margin-for-error principle: if one knows that P, then P must be true in all nearby worlds where one's evidence and cognitive state are the same — knowledge requires safety.
2.Vague predicate boundaries are determined by complex aggregate use-patterns, sensitive to small variations; nearby worlds where use-patterns are marginally different are epistemically indistinguishable from the actual world.
3.A true belief that a borderline man is bald fails the safety condition: in nearby worlds where the boundary has slightly shifted, the same belief with the same evidence would be false. Hence it is not knowledge.
∴The margin-for-error principle provides a principled epistemic explanation of why we cannot know the precise boundaries of vague predicates, without positing any semantic indeterminacy.
Argument III — Tolerance generates contradiction: the principle must be denied
1.If tolerance is true and is the analytic content of 'heap', then the sorites inductive premise is analytically true; combined with the true premise "1 million grains make a heap," the conclusion "1 grain makes a heap" follows validly.
2.The conclusion is false; modus tollens applied to the sorites shows that tolerance — the inductive premise — must be false, whatever its intuitive appeal or apparent connection to meaning.
3.Wright's manoeuvre — withholding assertion of tolerance without denying it — is insufficient: if tolerance is not assertable as true, it is not analytic; if it is analytic, it is assertable and the contradiction stands.
∴The tolerance principle must be denied; it generates explicit contradiction with known facts about heaps; epistemicism correctly identifies it as false and replaces it with a margin-for-error explanation of our apparent attraction to it.
Argument IV — Bivalence: gap theories generate metalinguistic contradiction
1.Gap theories assert: "P is neither true nor false" for borderline P. But this assertion is itself a truth-apt claim — a true claim about P — and must be true in the classical metalanguage.
2.If P is neither true nor false, then it is not the case that P; but "not the case that P" is equivalent to "not-P." The gap theorist must assert "not-P" while denying that "not-P" is true — a contradiction.
3.No semantic manoeuvre escapes this: redefining 'true' or 'not the case that' pushes the problem to the next metalevel; the instability is pervasive and shows that gap theories are internally inconsistent.
∴Gap theories — the foundation of all semantic alternatives to epistemicism — generate metalinguistic contradiction; bivalence must be preserved; every statement about borderline cases is true or false.
Argument V — Meaning instability: the butterfly effect in semantics explains ignorance
1.The extension of a vague predicate is determined by the aggregate pattern of use across all speakers; this aggregate is chaotically sensitive — small changes in individual usage can shift the sharp boundary.
2.No individual speaker has epistemic access to the full aggregate use-pattern; hence no individual can reliably locate the exact boundary; this is a principled, non-mysterious source of ignorance.
3.The felt absence of a fact — the phenomenology Wright calls "quandary" — is explained by this epistemic inaccessibility, without any need to posit semantic indeterminacy or revise the logic.
∴Meaning instability provides a positive epistemicist account of why vague boundaries are unknowable; the phenomenology of quandary is fully explained by epistemic principles, with no semantic revision required.

Mr President,

I want to occupy an unusual position in this debate. I am not a defender of epistemicism — I have argued in my published work that Williamson's specific explanation of our ignorance is self-defeating. But I am equally unconvinced by Wright's intuitionism and Raffman's multiple-range theory. My role here, taken alongside Rosanna Keefe's defence of supervaluationism, is to show that there is a third path: a rigorous semantic theory of vagueness that preserves much of classical logic, accommodates borderline cases without truth-value gaps in a contradictory-generating sense, and handles the critical phenomena — penumbral connections, higher-order vagueness — better than any alternative. The terrain of the debate, properly surveyed, reveals epistemicism as philosophically implausible and intuitionism as logically profligate — and supervaluationism, suitably developed, as the best available framework.

i. Mahtani's instability critique — Williamson's explanation is self-defeating

Instability critique — Mahtani 2004; self-defeating; same principles that explain ignorance undermine the premise; margin for error collapsesIn my 2004 paper "The Instability of Vague Terms" and my 2008 paper "Williamson on Inexact Knowledge," I argue that Williamson's explanation of why we are ignorant of vague predicate boundaries is self-defeating. Here is the structure of the problem. Williamson says: the boundary of 'bald' is sharp but unknowable, because the boundary is determined by the aggregate use-pattern, which is sensitive to small variations, so nearby possible worlds have slightly different boundaries — violating the safety condition. But this explanation requires that the actual use-pattern has determined an actual boundary. For the safety condition to apply, it must be the case that in the actual world there is a boundary at some precise n. But the same instability that makes nearby worlds have different boundaries makes it uncertain — given Williamson's own principles — whether the actual world's pattern has produced precisely the boundary needed. The instability cuts both ways: it undermines not only our knowledge of where the boundary is, but Williamson's own claim that there is a boundary to be known.

ii. Keefe's supervaluationism — penumbral connections vindicated

Keefe supervaluationism — Theories of Vagueness 2000; penumbral connections; truth on all sharpenings; classical logic preserved for logical truthsRosanna Keefe's book Theories of Vagueness (2000) provides the most systematic defence of supervaluationism and addresses many of Wright's objections. The core of the view: a sentence is true if it is true on every admissible sharpening of the vague predicate (every precise way of drawing the borderline that is compatible with the predicate's meaning); false if false on every sharpening; borderline otherwise. This preserves all logical truths — "Harry is bald or Harry is not bald" is true on every sharpening even if "Harry is bald" is true on some sharpenings and false on others — while accommodating borderline status. Crucially, it handles penumbral connections: the connections among vague predicates that constrain their admissible sharpenings. 'Red' and 'orange' cannot both apply to the same item; any sharpening of 'red' that extends it into the orange range violates a penumbral connection. Classical logic is preserved for all logical truths; revision is required only for certain inference rules (like contraposition applied to conditionals with vague predicates).

iii. the higher-order vagueness objection addressed — columnar and bounded approaches

Higher-order vagueness responses — Bobzien columnar; Wright's illusion argument; higher-order vagueness may not iterate indefinitely; supervaluationism can accommodate bounded iterationWright's most powerful objection to supervaluationism is the regress of higher-order vagueness: the operator 'Definitely' is itself vague, generating an endless hierarchy. Susanne Bobzien's 2015 work "Columnar Higher-Order Vagueness" and my own analysis suggest this regress may be structurally bounded rather than genuinely infinite. The vagueness of 'Def' at the second order does not automatically require a 'Def-Def' operator that is similarly vague at the third order, and so on. Wright himself, in his most recent work, argues that higher-order vagueness may be illusory — that there may be no genuine borderline borderline cases. If Wright is right about this, the most powerful objection to supervaluationism collapses. More importantly: the supervaluationist can insist that the regress is a feature of any theory with a boundary between the definite and the indefinite — including Wright's own quandary theory, which must draw a line between genuine quandary states and decisive non-quandary ones. The objection is not a distinctive problem for supervaluationism.

iv. against Wright's intuitionism — quandary without logic

Against quandary — Wright's intuitionism revisionism is costly; no action-guidance; quandary is phenomenologically described but not explained; supervaluationism explains quandary semanticallyWright's quandary theory describes the phenomenological state of competent speakers confronted with borderline cases — a state of genuine impasse, neither asserting P nor not-P. But it fails to explain this state semantically. Why do speakers feel quandary? The supervaluationist has an answer: because the sentence is true on some admissible sharpenings and false on others, no competent speaker has epistemic access to a ground for asserting either. Wright's intuitionism says: speakers feel quandary because they have no warrant for assertion. But this is a redescription of the phenomenon, not an explanation. It also incurs the costs of intuitionistic logic: the loss of double-negation elimination, the loss of proof by contradiction in unrestricted form, the loss of the law of excluded middle. These are severe logical costs for a theory whose only distinctive prediction — that speakers will feel quandary — is also predicted by supervaluationism without any revision of logic.

v. higher-order vagueness and the limits of any theory

Meta-theoretical humility — all theories face their own regress; supervaluationism is not uniquely disadvantaged; Raffman's ranges face boundary questions; epistemicism faces nested ignorance; the problem is universalI want to close with a point of meta-theoretical honesty that cuts against all parties in this debate, including the proposition. Higher-order vagueness is a genuine and deep problem. But it is not a problem that uniquely afflicts supervaluationism. Raffman's multiple-range theory faces an immediate question: are the boundaries of each range sharp? If they are, the ranges have precise limits that multiple-range theory was designed to avoid. If they are not, we need a meta-theory of vague ranges — and the same regress reappears one level up. Epistemicism faces a nested ignorance problem: we are not only ignorant of where the first-order boundary is, but ignorant of the scope of our ignorance — whether our margin-for-error is itself sharp. Wright's quandary theory must distinguish genuine quandary states from borderline-quandary states. Every theory faces a higher-order version of its own characteristic problem. Supervaluationism is not uniquely disadvantaged; and among available theories, it handles the first-order phenomena — penumbral connections, the sorites, logical truths — most systematically.

Argument I — Mahtani's instability critique: Williamson's explanation is self-undermining
1.Williamson's explanation requires: (a) there is a sharp boundary at some precise n in the actual world; (b) nearby worlds with slightly different use-patterns have different boundaries; (c) hence our actual-world true belief fails the safety condition.
2.The same instability that generates nearby worlds with different boundaries makes it uncertain whether the actual use-pattern has produced a determinate boundary at n rather than n+1 — Williamson's own principles prevent him from knowing his own premise (a).
3.The margin-for-error explanation requires knowing something about the actual boundary's location that Williamson's instability thesis renders unknowable; the explanation undermines its own presupposition.
∴Williamson's instability explanation of vague-boundary ignorance is self-defeating: it requires a determinate actual boundary whose determinacy is undermined by the very instability the explanation invokes.
Argument II — Keefe's supervaluationism: penumbral connections vindicate the framework
1.Supervaluationism: a sentence is true if true on all admissible sharpenings; false if false on all; borderline otherwise. All logical truths hold on all sharpenings; hence classical logic is preserved for logical truths.
2.Penumbral connections — semantic constraints relating vague predicates — are naturally handled by restricting admissible sharpenings to those that respect these constraints; no other theory handles penumbral connections as elegantly.
3.The supervaluationist framework accommodates borderline status without truth-value gaps in the contradictory-generating sense; it preserves logical truth while explaining why borderline statements have no determinate truth value.
∴Supervaluationism handles the central phenomena of vagueness — borderline cases, sorites, penumbral connections — more systematically than any alternative while preserving classical logical truths.
Argument III — Higher-order vagueness is not a uniquely supervaluationist problem
1.Every theory of vagueness faces a higher-order version of its own characteristic problem: Raffman's ranges need sharp or vague boundaries; epistemicism faces nested ignorance; Wright's quandary states have borderline cases.
2.Wright himself now argues higher-order vagueness may be illusory; Bobzien's columnar analysis shows the regress may be bounded rather than infinite; the objection is not as decisive as it initially appears.
3.If all theories face an equivalent version of the higher-order regress, it cannot serve as a decisive objection to supervaluationism specifically; the comparison must be made at the level of first-order explanatory success.
∴Higher-order vagueness is a shared challenge for all theories; supervaluationism is not uniquely disadvantaged and its first-order explanatory resources are superior to available alternatives.
Argument IV — Against intuitionism: costly revision without distinctive explanatory gain
1.Intuitionist revision of logic incurs severe costs: loss of double-negation elimination, loss of proof by contradiction in unrestricted form, loss of excluded middle — all theorems of classical logic with wide application.
2.Wright's theory's distinctive prediction — that speakers will experience quandary — is also predicted by supervaluationism without any logical revision; the quandary state arises naturally when a sentence is true on some sharpenings and false on others.
3.A theory with the same predictive content but lower logical cost is preferable; intuitionism is not distinctively required by any phenomenon that supervaluationism cannot explain.
∴Intuitionistic revision of logic is not warranted by the phenomena of vagueness; supervaluationism explains the same phenomena at lower logical cost; the revision is philosophically unjustified.
Argument V — Mahtani on higher-order vagueness: can vagueness cut out at any order?
1.In "Can Vagueness Cut Out at Any Order?" (2008), Mahtani shows there is no a priori constraint on which order of vagueness is the highest; vagueness could in principle cut out at the first, second, or any finite order.
2.This result undermines the claim that higher-order vagueness generates a genuine infinite regress that no theory can handle; the regress may be finite and bounded, contrary to Wright's assumption.
3.If the regress is bounded, supervaluationism needs only to handle finitely many orders of vagueness — a tractable task — and the Wrightian objection based on infinite regress fails.
∴Mahtani's result shows that higher-order vagueness need not generate an infinite regress; supervaluationism faces a tractable finite challenge rather than an in-principle unresolvable one.
