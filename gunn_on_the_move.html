On the Move

Thom Gunn — The Sense of Movement (1957)

Structure & Context
Form: Five eight-line stanzas of iambic pentameter, rhyming ABABCDCD — the interlocking scheme, unlike a closing couplet, refuses to lock shut; each stanza ends with momentum still open, which is the point Occasion: Written in California in spring 1955, shortly after Gunn moved from England; the poem responds to the motorcycle culture he encountered there, epitomised by the Marlon Brando film The Wild One (1953) Argument: The poem moves from birds (pure instinct) to motorcyclists (will overlaid on instinct, the machine as chosen identity) to the philosopher-speaker, who finds in the gang's motion a model for how humans must act without guaranteed purpose — choosing movement in a valueless world The Hughes parallel: Hughes was publishing The Hawk in the Rain (1957) at the same moment, finding raw energy in English landscape and animal violence. Gunn found the same energy on an American highway. Their Selected Poems appeared jointly in 1962 — the transatlantic pairing was institutionally recognised Existentialism: The poem draws directly on Sartre's concept of radical freedom — that human beings have no fixed essence and must define themselves through action. The motorcyclists are Sartrean heroes: they cannot justify their movement but they move anyway The speaker: The poem uses the indefinite pronoun 'One' rather than 'I', raising the observation from personal confession to universal proposition — a characteristic Gunn move, the coldly philosophical distanced from the emotionally particular

The blue jay scuffling in the bushes Follows some hidden purpose, and the gust Of birds spurts across the field, the wheeling Swallows have nested in the trees: wheeling Swallows have nested in the trees And both are happy in their hiding, But when you see the silver baffled sense Yet movement urgent certainty Comes of itself, and implies On motorcycles, durst. They from far away Come small and valid. In goggles, dust, They strap in doubt — goggles, donned impersonally In gleaming jackets trophied with the dust, They ride in rows, with gloves and sideburns On motorcycles, in a valid world: Small, black, as flies hanging in heat, the Boys Exact conclusion of their hardiness Has no shape yet, but from known whereabouts They ride, direction where the tires press. They scare a flight of birds across the field: Much that is natural, to the will must yield. Men manufacture both machine and soul, And use what they imperfectly control To dare a future from the taken routes. It is a part solution, after all. One is not necessarily discord On earth; or damned because, half animal, One lacks direct instinct, because one has Starved the imprecision of the self. As with the dull thunder of approximate words On motorcycles, durst: astride the created will They burst away; the towns they travel through Are home for neither bird nor holiness, For birds and saints complete their purposes. At worst, one is in motion; and at best, Reaching no absolute, in which to rest, always toward, toward. One joins the movement in a valueless world, Choosing it, till, both hurler and hurled, One moves as well, always toward, toward. At worst, one is in motion; and at best, Reaching no absolute, in which to rest, One is always nearer by not keeping still.
