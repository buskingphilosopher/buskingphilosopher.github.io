Mr President,

I want to defend the utilitarian social welfare function not as an ideological commitment but as the conclusion of the most rigorous axiomatic analysis available to welfare economics. My thesis is that the utilitarian SWF is uniquely distinguished by satisfying a complete and internally consistent set of axioms that its rivals — however morally appealing — must violate.

W = u1 + u2 + … + uN    (sum of individual well-being levels) i. harsanyi’s aggregation theorem

Harsanyi's aggregation theoremHarsanyi proved in 1955 that if individual preferences satisfy the von Neumann-Morgenstern axioms, if social preferences likewise satisfy those axioms, and if the Pareto Indifference principle holds — that society is indifferent when all individuals are — then the social welfare function must be a weighted sum of individual utility functions. With equal weights, this is the utilitarian SWF. This is not a philosophical argument susceptible to intuition-based objections. It is a mathematical theorem. Any SWF that departs from sum-weighting must either violate individual rationality, violate social rationality, or violate Pareto Indifference. Each of those violations carries a significant axiomatic cost that the utilitarian SWF does not bear.

ii. the unique axiom satisfaction under uncertainty

Utilitarianism: the only SWF satisfying all axioms under uncertaintyUnder uncertainty — which is the normal context of policy evaluation — the utilitarian SWF is the only member of the SWF family that simultaneously satisfies: Strong Pareto, Anonymity, Continuity, Separability, the Ex Ante Pareto axioms, and Dominance (stochastic dominance: if one policy is certain to yield a better outcome than another, it is ranked better). Adler himself acknowledges this: there is no "Theory X" for prioritarianism under uncertainty — no prioritarian module can satisfy both Dominance and Ex Ante Pareto. The utilitarian SWF is uniquely privileged in the axiomatic landscape. To choose prioritarianism under uncertainty is to knowingly accept an axiom failure. That is a significant choice that requires justification.

iii. the diminishing marginal utility reply

DMU — utilitarianism already redistributes to the poorThe most common objection to utilitarianism is distributional insensitivity: it is indifferent between outcomes with the same total well-being regardless of how that total is distributed. But this objection misunderstands what the utilitarian SWF operates on. The well-being measure — constructed via von Neumann-Morgenstern theory — already incorporates diminishing marginal utility of resources. An additional unit of income raises the well-being of a poor person more than a rich one. The utilitarian SWF, summing well-being rather than income, therefore already systematically redistributes toward the poor through the diminishing marginal utility embedded in the well-being measure. The prioritarian's additional concave transformation of well-being is layering redistribution on top of redistribution already present in the measure itself.

iv. the determinacy advantage

Determinacy — utilitarianism specifies a unique functionThe utilitarian SWF is fully determined by the equal-weighting requirement: W = Σui. The continuous prioritarian SWF requires specification of a concave transformation g(·), and the prioritarian principle itself does not determine which g to use. An isoelastic function g(u) = u(1-η)/(1-η) requires choosing η, the inequality aversion parameter. Different values of η produce dramatically different policy rankings, and there is no principled basis internal to prioritarianism for choosing η over any other value. This indeterminacy is not a peripheral problem. It means the prioritarian SWF cannot give a definite policy recommendation without an external value judgment that the framework itself cannot supply. The utilitarian SWF requires no such additional specification.

Thank you.

Mr President,

Adler identifies the continuous prioritarian SWF as his preferred framework, and I want to defend it as the position that best balances the utilitarian's axiomatic virtues against the genuine moral force of the insight that benefiting people matters more the worse-off those people are. I will argue that the utilitarian's distributional indifference is not a technical limitation but a moral failure, and that the prioritarian concave transformation g(u) is not an arbitrary addition but the formal expression of a compelling ethical principle.

W = g(u1) + g(u2) + … + g(uN)    g strictly increasing, strictly concave i. the pigou-dalton principle

Pigou-Dalton — transfers to the worse-off improve social welfareThe Pigou-Dalton principle holds that a pure transfer from a better-off person to a worse-off person — one that does not reverse their relative positions — makes the outcome better, ceteris paribus. This principle captures the intuition that equality of distribution matters: a society in which well-being is distributed more equally is, all else equal, a better society. The utilitarian SWF does not satisfy Pigou-Dalton — it is indifferent between two outcomes with the same total well-being regardless of how that total is distributed. The continuous prioritarian SWF does satisfy Pigou-Dalton: because g is strictly concave, g(upoor + ε) − g(upoor) > g(urich) − g(urich − ε), so the transfer increases W. The Pigou-Dalton principle is the key axiomatic difference between the utilitarian and prioritarian SWFs; it is the formal expression of the moral weight of equality.

ii. the priority view — not mere egalitarianism

Priority view — absolute welfare, not equality as an endContinuous prioritarianism must be distinguished carefully from egalitarianism. The egalitarian values equality as such — a reduction of inequality is good even if it comes at the cost of levelling everyone down. The continuous prioritarian does not value equality as such. It values absolute welfare levels, weighted by a concave function of their position. Levelling down — reducing everyone's well-being uniformly — is unambiguously bad under prioritarianism: W decreases. Prioritarianism says not that equality matters intrinsically but that benefiting the worse-off matters more. This is the insight Parfit articulated as "the Priority View": it is not that we care about the gap between persons, but that benefits to worse-off people are simply more valuable, full stop, given their absolute welfare level. The concave g captures this: the marginal social value of an increment of well-being decreases as the recipient's welfare level rises.

iii. the DMU reply is insufficient

DMU insufficient — normative and empirical independenceThe utilitarian argues that diminishing marginal utility already performs the redistributive work of prioritarianism. But this conflates a normative claim with an empirical one. Diminishing marginal utility of resources is an empirical fact about the relationship between resources and well-being. The Pigou-Dalton principle and the Priority View are normative claims about how increments of well-being at different welfare levels should be weighted for policy purposes. These are logically independent. Consider a thought experiment: suppose — contrary to fact — that marginal utility were constant, so rich and poor gained equal well-being from an extra unit of resources. In that world, the utilitarian would be indifferent between giving resources to rich or poor. The prioritarian would still weight gains to the poor more heavily, because their absolute welfare level is lower. The prioritarian intuition remains compelling even when DMU is removed; therefore DMU is not doing the normative work the utilitarian claims.

iv. indeterminacy: a manageable challenge

Indeterminacy — tractable through sensitivity analysisThe utilitarian raises indeterminacy: the prioritarian SWF requires choosing the inequality aversion parameter η. I accept this challenge but deny it is fatal. Policy analysis under the SWF framework can conduct sensitivity analysis across a range of values of η, reporting which policies are robust to different degrees of inequality aversion. Where a policy is preferred under a wide range of η values, confidence in the recommendation is high. Where rankings are sensitive to η, that sensitivity is itself informative — it shows where distributional values are doing the decisive work in the ranking. This is not a defect of prioritarianism; it is a feature: it makes visible the distributional value choices that the utilitarian obscures by pretending they are not being made.

Thank you.

1.The Pigou-Dalton principle: a transfer from a better-off to a worse-off person (not reversing relative positions) makes the outcome better, ceteris paribus.
2.The utilitarian SWF does not satisfy Pigou-Dalton; the continuous prioritarian SWF does, because the strict concavity of g ensures that the transfer raises W.
3.A policy evaluation framework indifferent between equal-sum distributions of radically different equality is morally inadequate; Pigou-Dalton is the axiomatic expression of why.
∴Pigou-Dalton compliance is a compelling requirement for any adequate SWF; the continuous prioritarian SWF satisfies it while the utilitarian does not; this is a decisive advantage.

2.Continuous prioritarianism formalises this: the marginal contribution g′(u) of a well-being increment to W decreases as u rises, so the same increment is worth more to social welfare when it accrues to a worse-off person.

Mr President,

The debate between ex ante and ex post prioritarianism is one of the most technically demanding in the Adlerian framework, and I want to present the ex ante position as the superior approach to handling the moral dimension of risk in policy evaluation. The core issue is this: when policy outcomes are uncertain — which is always — the question of when to apply the priority weighting matters fundamentally. Ex ante prioritarianism applies it to expected well-being, before outcomes are realised. Ex post prioritarianism applies it after, to realised well-being. I will argue the ex ante approach is correct.

WEAP = Σi g(E[ui])    (transform expected well-being, then sum) i. the ex ante pareto axioms

Ex Ante Pareto — respect for individual preferences under uncertaintyThe Ex Ante Pareto principle holds that if every individual prefers policy A to policy B — taking into account the full probability distribution of outcomes each faces — then society should prefer A to B. This is a compelling requirement: it means that policy choices respect what individuals themselves prefer given their actual risk situation. Ex ante prioritarianism satisfies the Ex Ante Pareto axioms. Ex post prioritarianism does not: it can rank policy A above B even when every individual prefers B to A, because the ex post priority weighting can favour outcomes that produce more equal distributions of realised well-being, overriding each person's own preferences. This violates a fundamental requirement of welfarism: that social evaluation be responsive to what individuals actually prefer for themselves.

ii. the interpersonal fairness of ex ante evaluation

Ex ante fairness — equality of opportunity, not outcomeEx ante prioritarianism treats individuals fairly in the sense of equality of opportunity: the priority weighting responds to each person's expected welfare — what they can reasonably expect their life to contain. This is analogous to judging fairness ex ante in distributive theory: behind a veil of ignorance, what matters is the lottery each person faces, not which ticket they happen to draw. Ex post prioritarianism responds to realised outcomes — it gives extra weight to whoever happens to end up worst-off, regardless of whether their initial lottery was fair. If two people face identical lotteries but one draws the bad outcome by chance, ex post prioritarianism treats them very differently at the point of policy evaluation, even though their situations were equally good from an ex ante perspective. This seems wrong: the source of the bad outcome (bad luck in a fair lottery) is morally relevant.

iii. what ex post gets wrong — the ex post paradox

Ex post paradox — ex post can endorse dominated policiesEx post prioritarianism can violate Dominance: it can rank policy A below policy B even though A is certain to produce a better outcome than B. This happens when the worse policy B happens to produce more equal distributions of well-being in some states, and the ex post priority weighting on the worst-off gives those states extra weight sufficient to flip the ranking, even though A dominates B state by state. This is a serious problem: a policy evaluation framework that can prefer a certainly worse policy because it distributes badly in a more equal way has violated a minimal rationality condition. The ex ante prioritarian SWF does not face this problem; it ranks B above A only when the expected priority-weighted welfare is genuinely higher under B.

iv. why the ex ante / ex post conflict matters in practice

Policy implications — fatality risk regulation caseAdler’s case study of fatality risk regulation makes the stakes concrete. A policy that reduces mortality risk for all people at some income cost produces different rankings under ex ante and ex post prioritarianism. Under ex ante, each person’s expected lifetime well-being is computed and the priority weighting applied; the result responds to each person’s integrated expected life situation. Under ex post, the weighting responds to who actually dies — a matter of realised luck. In Adler’s analysis, ex ante prioritarianism generates smoother, more predictable policy rankings that better track the actual welfare impacts of risk reduction. The ex post approach introduces a volatility driven by distributional outcomes in states of the world that may be very low probability, distorting the policy ranking in ways that are neither predictable nor clearly morally justified.

1.Ex Ante Pareto: if every individual prefers policy A to policy B given their full probability distribution of outcomes, social welfare ranks A above B.
2.Ex ante prioritarianism satisfies Ex Ante Pareto; ex post prioritarianism violates it — it can rank B above A even when all individuals prefer A, if B produces more equal realised distributions.
3.A SWF that overrides the unanimous preferences of all individuals for the sake of distributional equality in outcomes violates the foundational welfarist commitment that social evaluation be responsive to individual welfare.
∴Ex ante prioritarianism is the prioritarian approach that respects individual preferences under uncertainty; ex post prioritarianism sacrifices this respect for distributional symmetry in outcomes.

1.Fairness under uncertainty requires that each person faces an equally good lottery, not that each person obtains an equally good outcome — identical lotteries generating different outcomes by chance are not unfair.
2.Ex ante prioritarianism weights by each person’s expected well-being — responsive to the quality of their lottery, not the luck of their draw.
3.Ex post prioritarianism weights by realised well-being — treating bad luck outcomes as generating strong distributional claims even if the underlying lottery was perfectly fair.
∴The ex ante approach captures the morally relevant notion of fairness under uncertainty; the ex post approach conflates bad luck with injustice, generating unwarranted distributional claims from morally neutral chance events.
Argument III — Ex post violates Dominance: a rationality failure

Mr President,

My colleague defending ex ante prioritarianism has argued that the ex ante approach is superior because it satisfies Ex Ante Pareto while the ex post approach does not. I want to argue that this is precisely backwards: it is Ex Ante Pareto that should be abandoned, not Dominance, and that the ex post prioritarian SWF is the correct approach to applying the Priority View under conditions of uncertainty.

WEPP = E[Σi g(ui)]    (expected value of sum of transformed realised well-being) i. the priority view is about actual welfare, not expected welfare

Actual vs. expected well-being — Priority View applies to what is, not what was expectedThe Priority View holds that it is more important to benefit people the worse-off they are. The morally relevant question is: how well-off are they? And the answer to that question is a fact about their realised welfare — what actually happens to them — not about their expected welfare before outcomes are determined. A person who draws the bad outcome from a fair lottery is genuinely worse-off; the fairness of their initial lottery does not change their actual welfare level. If the Priority View is correct — if benefits to worse-off people matter more — then it must apply to actual welfare, not probabilistic expectations of welfare. Ex ante prioritarianism applies the priority weighting to an expectation, a number that may correspond to nobody’s actual welfare level in any realised state. It weights a statistical abstraction; ex post prioritarianism weights actual people in actual states of the world.

ii. ex ante pareto should be rejected, not treated as inviolable

Ex Ante Pareto — not a compelling axiom under prioritarianismThe Ex Ante Pareto axioms require that if all individuals prefer policy A in terms of expected utility, society should prefer A. But Ex Ante Pareto is compelling only under a consequentialist framework indifferent to distribution. Under prioritarianism, Ex Ante Pareto can generate problematic results: it can require endorsing a policy that imposes serious risks of very bad outcomes on the worst-off individuals, because the expected values happen to favour all individuals compared to an alternative. Adler himself notes that Ex Ante Pareto can require endorsing policies that a continuous prioritarian SWF would clearly reject — specifically, policies that impose risks of catastrophically bad outcomes on individuals who are already worse-off. A framework that is compelled to endorse such policies by an axiom about expected values has let the tail wag the dog. Dominance is the more compelling axiom.

iii. dominance is a minimum rationality requirement

Dominance — a more compelling axiom than Ex Ante ParetoDominance states: if policy A produces a better outcome than policy B in every state of the world, A is better than B. This is a minimum rationality condition for decision-making under uncertainty — it is simply irrational to prefer a certainly worse outcome over a certainly better one. Ex post prioritarianism satisfies Dominance; ex ante prioritarianism satisfies Ex Ante Pareto. When we must choose which axiom to satisfy and which to violate — and Adler’s impossibility result shows we cannot satisfy both under prioritarianism — the rational choice is to satisfy Dominance. Ex Ante Pareto is a preference condition that can generate perverse results under prioritarianism; Dominance is a rationality condition that cannot be compromised without abandoning the framework’s claim to be a coherent decision procedure.

iv. equity dominance and social risk aversion

Equity Dominance — social risk aversion over distributionsEx post prioritarianism adds a dimension of social risk aversion to the individual risk aversion already present in the well-being measure. In addition to caring about each person’s risk exposure, ex post prioritarianism cares about the distribution of well-being across persons in each realised state: it is worse if outcomes are very unequally distributed across the population, beyond what each individual’s own risk aversion captures. This social risk aversion is not redundant — it captures a genuine moral concern about societies in which bad luck concentrates suffering on those already worst-off. The ex ante approach is blind to this concern: it evaluates each person’s lottery independently and then sums, missing the correlation between bad luck and low welfare that matters for distributive justice.

Mr President,

The utilitarian and prioritarian SWFs share a common structure: they aggregate across persons, they make trade-offs across persons, and they permit the interests of the worst-off to be sacrificed for sufficiently large gains to better-off individuals. I want to argue for a fundamentally different approach: the leximin SWF, which maximises the well-being of the worst-off person first, then the second worst-off, and so on lexicographically. This is not a soft preference for the worse-off — it is a categorical priority that cannot be overridden by aggregation.

Leximin: max min{u1,…,uN}; then max min of remaining; iterate lexicographically i. the rawlsian moral foundation

Rawlsian foundation — veil of ignorance selects leximinRawls argued that rational agents behind a veil of ignorance — not knowing their position in society — would choose principles that maximise the position of the worst-off, because they cannot risk ending up at the bottom of a distribution optimised for the majority. The leximin SWF is the welfare-economic formalisation of this insight. Harsanyi argued that rational agents behind the veil would choose expected utility maximisation — i.e., utilitarianism. But Rawls’s reply is decisive: behind the veil, rational agents do not have determinate probability distributions over social positions, and in conditions of genuine uncertainty about one’s position, maximising the minimum is the rational strategy. The leximin SWF implements this: it asks, for any policy, what is the lowest well-being level it produces, and prefers policies that make the worst-case as good as possible.

ii. no trade-offs across persons

No trade-offs — leximin refuses inter-personal welfare sacrificeThe prioritarian SWF, for all its advantages over utilitarianism, still permits trade-offs across persons: a sufficiently large gain to a better-off person can outweigh a loss to the worst-off, if the gain is large enough. Only the rate of trade-off changes, not the fact of trade-off. The leximin SWF refuses this entirely. No amount of gain to better-off persons can justify a loss to the worst-off, because the worst-off person’s well-being is lexicographically prior. This is not excessive rigidity — it is the correct response to the separateness of persons. Persons are distinct; their interests cannot be traded off across persons any more than one can trade off a person’s severe loss against a redistribution of gains across others who are already better-off.

iii. the continuity objection answered

Continuity objection — leximin is not indifferent between close outcomesThe standard objection to leximin is that it violates Continuity: it would prefer a world with 1,000 people at welfare level 5 and one person at welfare level 5.0001 over a world with 1,000 people at welfare level 1,000,000 and one person at welfare level 4.9999, because the worst-off person is marginally better-off in the first world. This seems absurd. But the objection, pressed too hard, proves too much. Any framework that gives significant weight to equality will produce counterintuitive results in extreme cases constructed to exploit that weight. The leximin’s real advantage is in normal policy analysis, where we are comparing outcomes with similar aggregate welfare but different distributions — and there, maximising the position of the worst-off is exactly the right framework. The extreme cases are designed to make the leximin look bad; they do not reflect the structure of real policy choices.

iv. what distinguishes leximin from continuous prioritarianism

Leximin vs. continuous prioritarianism — categorical vs. weighted priorityThe continuous prioritarian gives extra weight to the worse-off through the concave function g — but the weight, while higher for the worse-off, is finite. For any loss to the worst-off, there is some large enough gain to a better-off person that outweighs it under the prioritarian SWF. Leximin gives infinite priority to the worst-off: no such outweighing is possible. This difference matters morally: the prioritarian is effectively saying "we care more about the worst-off, but not infinitely more." The leximin says "we will not permit the worst-off to be worse-off if we can avoid it." I argue that the second position is more faithful to what the Priority View actually requires when taken seriously. Parfit’s original formulation does not specify a finite priority weight; leximin takes the Priority View to its natural conclusion.

Argument II — No trade-offs: leximin takes separateness of persons seriously

2.The leximin SWF refuses inter-personal welfare trade-offs entirely: the worst-off person’s welfare is lexicographically prior to all others’.
3.The separateness of persons — that persons are distinct and their losses cannot be compensated by others’ gains — supports categorical rather than weighted priority.
∴Leximin is the only SWF that takes the separateness of persons fully seriously by refusing all inter-personal trade-offs; the prioritarian’s finite weighting is a weakened, compromised expression of the same underlying intuition.
Argument III — The continuity objection is a constructed extreme, not a real-world problem
1.The standard continuity objection uses extreme cases (marginal gain to worst-off vs. enormous gain to many) designed to make leximin appear absurd.
2.Real policy choices involve comparing outcomes with broadly similar aggregate welfare but different distributions — in this domain, leximin generates intuitively correct verdicts.
3.Any equity-sensitive framework produces counterintuitive results in extreme constructed cases; the continuity objection proves too much, defeating prioritarianism and egalitarianism along with leximin.
∴The continuity objection is not decisive against leximin; it exploits the framework’s sensitivity to distribution in extreme artificial cases that do not reflect the structure of real policy analysis.
Argument IV — Leximin vs. prioritarianism: categorical vs. weighted priority
1.Continuous prioritarianism gives finite extra weight to the worse-off — for any loss to the worst-off, some large enough gain to others outweighs it under g(u).
2.Leximin gives lexicographic priority — no gain to better-off persons can justify a worsening of the worst-off position.
3.Parfit’s Priority View does not specify a finite priority weight; leximin takes it to its natural conclusion, fully implementing what it means to prioritise the worst-off.
∴Leximin is the full and faithful implementation of the Priority View; continuous prioritarianism implements a compromised version, arbitrarily specifying a finite priority weight that the Priority View itself does not determine.

Mr President,

All the SWFs debated so far — utilitarian, prioritarian in its various forms, and leximin — share a common assumption: that the moral value of increments of well-being varies continuously with the welfare level of the recipient. The utilitarian says all increments are equal; the prioritarian says increments to the worse-off are worth more, smoothly; the leximin says they are worth infinitely more. I want to defend a fundamentally different approach: sufficientism, which holds that there is a morally significant threshold of well-being — a level of "enough" — below which priority concerns are strongly operative, and above which ordinary aggregation applies. The sufficientist SWF formalises this discontinuity.

Frankfurt’s sufficiency — "enough" is morally fundamentalHarry Frankfurt argued in 1987 that what matters morally is not whether people have equal amounts of goods, but whether they have enough. Equality, for Frankfurt, is not a morally fundamental value — what matters is that people have sufficient well-being to live decent lives. Below the threshold of sufficiency, the moral urgency of improving people’s welfare is very high; above it, further increases still matter, but their urgency is substantially reduced. This is not merely an intuition. It reflects a deep structural feature of human welfare: the difference between barely surviving and living a minimally decent life is qualitatively more significant than the difference between a good life and an excellent life. Sufficientism captures this moral discontinuity that continuous prioritarianism smooths over.

ii. the threshold as a policy instrument

Policy relevance — thresholds map onto real welfare conceptsThe sufficientist threshold maps naturally onto real concepts in policy analysis: the poverty line, the minimum income standard, the threshold of food security, the basic package in healthcare policy. These are not arbitrary administrative cutoffs — they represent genuine transitions in the quality of human life. A sufficientist SWF operationalises this by giving much greater weight to policies that move people from below to above the threshold than to policies that improve the lives of those already above it. This generates policy rankings that track genuine human urgency more accurately than smooth prioritarianism. In Adler’s framework, a sufficientist SWF is formalised by using a highly concave gL(u) below threshold τ and a less concave (or linear) function above — producing a kink in the transformation function at the threshold.

iii. what distinguishes sufficientism from prioritarianism

Sufficientism vs. prioritarianism — discontinuity vs. smooth weightingThe continuous prioritarian gives extra weight to gains to the worse-off, but the weighting varies smoothly and continuously with welfare level. A person at welfare level 10 gets more weight than one at level 100, but marginally more than one at 11, and there is no discontinuity at any threshold. Sufficientism holds that there is a genuine moral discontinuity at the sufficiency threshold — that moving someone from level 9 to level 11 (across the threshold) is qualitatively different from moving someone from level 10 to level 12 (within the above-threshold range), even if the welfare increments are identical. This discontinuity is not arbitrary: it reflects the genuine moral salience of the transition from inadequate to adequate welfare. Continuous prioritarianism is too smooth; it cannot capture this transition.

iv. the threshold indeterminacy objection answered

Threshold specification — less indeterminate than the prioritarian’s ηThe standard objection to sufficientism is that the threshold τ is arbitrary — where exactly does "enough" begin? But this objection applies with equal force to the prioritarian’s η: both require a value judgment that neither framework can supply internally. The difference is that the sufficientist threshold τ corresponds to a welfare level with independent moral and empirical grounding — minimum income standards, poverty lines, basic needs assessments — while the prioritarian’s η corresponds to no such grounded concept. Sensitivity analysis across a range of threshold values τ will often reveal that policy rankings are robust: policies that perform well for the below-threshold population tend to perform well across a range of threshold specifications. The indeterminacy objection is less damaging to sufficientism than to prioritarianism.
