If—

Rudyard Kipling — Rewards and Fairies (Macmillan, 1910)
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
"If—" (1910) is in the public domain. First published in Rewards and Fairies (Macmillan, 1910). The poem was reportedly written with Leander Starr Jameson in mind as its model.
Structure & Context
Form: Four octaves of iambic pentameter, rhyming ABABCDCD; the entire poem is a single suspended conditional sentence that does not resolve until its final two lines The addressee: "My son" in the final line reveals a paternal speaker throughout — every "you can" is an instruction from father to child, which retroactively frames all 30 preceding lines as parental counsel The model: Kipling said the poem was written with Leander Starr Jameson in mind — leader of the failed Jameson Raid (1895–96) into the Transvaal, who bore public humiliation with apparent equanimity The "If": The governing conjunction is stated 14 times across 32 lines; the repetition defers the main clause across three full stanzas, keeping the reader syntactically suspended until the volta at line 31 Triumph and Disaster: The poem's most quoted antithesis; Kipling capitalises both — giving abstract qualities the status of proper nouns, making them figures rather than conditions Reception: Voted Britain's favourite poem in a 1995 BBC poll; the first two lines are inscribed above the players' entrance at Wimbledon's Centre Court
Stanza I · Self-possession
If you can keep your head when all about you Are losing theirs and blaming it on you, If you can trust yourself when all men doubt you, But make allowance for their doubting too; If you can wait and not be tired by waiting, Or being lied about, don't deal in lies, Or being hated, don't give way to hating, And yet don't look too good, nor talk too wise:
Stanza II · Mind and Fortune
If you can dream—and not make dreams your master; If you can think—and not make thoughts your aim; If you can meet with Triumph and Disaster And treat those two impostors just the same; If you can bear to hear the truth you've spoken Twisted by knaves to make a trap for fools, Or watch the things you gave your life to, broken, And stoop and build 'em up with worn-out tools:
Stanza III · Risk and Endurance
If you can make one heap of all your winnings And risk it on one turn of pitch-and-toss, And lose, and start again at your beginnings And never breathe a word about your loss; If you can force your heart and nerve and sinew To serve your turn long after they are gone, And so hold on when there is nothing in you Except the Will which says to them: 'Hold on!'
Stanza IV · Society and the Apodosis
If you can talk with crowds and keep your virtue, Or walk with Kings—nor lose the common touch, If neither foes nor loving friends can hurt you, If all men count with you, but none too much; If you can fill the unforgiving minute With sixty seconds' worth of distance run, Yours is the Earth and everything that's in it, And—which is more—you'll be a Man, my son!
