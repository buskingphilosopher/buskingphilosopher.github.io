Instrumental Variables / 2SLSSargan (1958) • Angrist & Imbens (1994)
OLS produces biased and inconsistent estimates whenever a regressor is correlated with the error term — endogeneity caused by omitted variables, measurement error, or simultaneity. IV uses exogenous variation in a valid instrument to isolate causal variation in the endogenous regressor. The central problem of empirical economics is not noise — it is bias. OLS produces biased and inconsistent estimates the moment any regressor is correlated with the error term. The IV estimator is the most direct surgical response to this problem, and its logic has been fundamental to credible causal inference in economics for seven decades.

The endogeneity problem and the OLS breakdown.

Suppose the structural equation is y = xβ + u. OLS requires E[x'u] = 0 — exogeneity. When x is endogenous, plim(β̂&#x{OLS}) = β + (E[x'x]/n)⁻¹ (E[x'u]/n) ≠ β. The bias term does not vanish with sample size. IV resolves this by replacing x with its projection onto instruments z that satisfy: (i) relevance: Cov(z,x) ≠ 0; and (ii) exclusion: Cov(z,u) = 0.
i. the IV estimator and 2SLS

2SLS — projects endogenous regressors onto instruments; consistent under exclusion & relevance; OLS bias eliminatedThe just-identified IV estimator for y = xβ + u with instrument z is:

β̂ᴵᵛ = (z'x)⁻¹z'y = β + (z'x)⁻¹z'u

Under the exclusion restriction Cov(z,u) = 0 and relevance Cov(z,x) ≠ 0, we have plim(β̂&#x{IV}) = β. In the overidentified case with instruments matrix Z (n×L, L>K), 2SLS projects X onto the column space of Z, forming &Xhat; = P_Z X = Z(Z'Z)⁻¹Z'X, and estimates:

β̂²ˢˡˢ = (X̂'X)⁻¹X̂'y = (X'PᵤX)⁻¹X'Pᵤy

This is consistent and asymptotically normal. The Hausman test — comparing β̂&#x{OLS} and β̂&#x{IV} — formally tests for endogeneity: under H₀: Cov(x,u)=0, both are consistent, but OLS is efficient; under H₁, only IV is consistent.

ii. LATE and the Angrist–Imbens framework

LATE — IV identifies causal effect for compliers only; requires monotonicity; structurally meaningful estimandImbens and Angrist (1994) show that with a binary instrument Z∈{0,1}, binary treatment D, and heterogeneous effects, IV does not identify the ATE. Instead it identifies the Local Average Treatment Effect:

β̂ᴵᵛ →ₚ LATE = E[Y₁ - Y₀ | D₁ > D₀] (compliers only)

where D₁ and D₀ are potential treatment status under Z=1 and Z=0 respectively. Compliers are those for whom the instrument shifts treatment. The numerator is the reduced-form effect E[Y|Z=1]−E[Y|Z=0]; the denominator is the first-stage E[D|Z=1]−E[D|Z=0]. LATE requires the monotonicity assumption: for all i, D&#x{i1} ≥ D&#x{i0} (no defiers). This is not a weakness but a clarification: IV answers a well-defined causal question about a well-defined subpopulation.

iii. the first-stage F-test and weak instrument diagnostics

Weak instruments — first-stage F < 10 is the rule of thumb; Stock–Yogo tables; LIML as robust alternativeThe quality of instruments is assessed by the first-stage F-statistic. Stock and Yogo (2005) provide critical values: for one endogenous regressor and one instrument, F ≥ 10 limits IV bias to less than 10% of OLS bias. The Cragg–Donald statistic generalises this to multiple endogenous regressors. When instruments are weak, 2SLS exhibits severe finite-sample bias toward OLS — in the limit, 2SLS converges to OLS. Limited-Information Maximum Likelihood (LIML) has better median-unbiasedness properties under weak instruments, and Anderson–Rubin confidence sets remain valid regardless of instrument strength. The weak-instrument problem is a real constraint, but it is a constraint that is diagnosable and addressable, not a fatal flaw of the IV framework.

iv. the Sargan–Hansen overidentification test

Sargan–Hansen J-test — tests exclusion of surplus instruments; J ~ χ²(L-K) under H₀; rejection signals invalidity but non-rejection does not confirm validityIn overidentified models (L > K instruments), the Sargan–Hansen J-statistic tests whether the moment conditions are jointly satisfied:

J = n · ê'Z(Z'Z)⁻¹Z'ê / σ̂² →_d χ²(L-K) under H₀

where ê are the 2SLS residuals. A significant J statistic suggests at least one instrument violates the exclusion restriction — it is falsified by the data. Non-rejection does not guarantee validity (the test has zero power under exact identification), but rejection is highly informative. The J-test is a rigorous instrument diagnostic unavailable in the OLS framework.

3.The weak-instrument problem is thus diagnosable (F-test), and addressable (LIML, AR inference). OLS has no such diagnostic: its endogeneity bias is silent and undetectable from within the OLS framework.
∴The weak-instrument problem is a contained and addressable limitation of IV; OLS endogeneity bias is both larger in magnitude and undiagnosable; IV is preferable wherever endogeneity is plausible.

1.With L>K instruments, the overidentifying restrictions imply E[z'u] = 0. The J-statistic J = nê'Z(Z'Z)⁻¹Z'ê/σ̂² is asymptotically χ²(L−K) under H₀.
2.A significant J rejects at least one exclusion restriction, signalling a violated instrument. This provides empirical discipline: the researcher’s identifying assumptions are exposed to falsification.
3.OLS has no equivalent diagnostic for its exogeneity assumption. The J-test is an asymmetric advantage of the IV framework.
∴The Sargan–Hansen J-test makes IV identification assumptions partially falsifiable; OLS exogeneity is untestable from within OLS. IV is epistemically more honest about its assumptions.

GMM is the most general workhorse estimator in modern econometrics. It nests OLS, GLS, 2SLS, and MLE as special cases while imposing only moment conditions on the data-generating process — no distributional assumptions on the error term required. Hansen’s 1982 paper is one of the most cited in economics precisely because GMM provides a unified framework applicable wherever economic theory delivers moment restrictions.

The GMM objective and estimator.

Let g(y&#x{t}, x&#x{t}, β) be an L×1 vector of moment functions satisfying the population condition E[g(y&#x{t}, x&#x{t}, β₀)] = 0. The GMM estimator minimises the quadratic form:

β̂&#x{GMM} = argmin&#x{β} g&#x{T}(β)' W&#x{T} g&#x{T}(β)

where g&#x{T}(β) = (1/T) ∑ g(y&#x{t}, x&#x{t}, β) is the sample moment vector and W&#x{T} is a positive-definite weighting matrix. When W&#x{T} is the inverse of the long-run variance S = ∑ E[g&#x{t}g&#x{t-j}'], the estimator is efficient GMM. 2SLS is GMM with W = (Z'Z)⁻¹.
i. the efficiency advantage of optimal GMM

Efficient GMM — optimal weighting matrix Ŝ⁻¹ achieves Cramér-Rao lower bound among moment-based estimators; dominates 2SLS under heteroskedasticityThe asymptotic variance of GMM is:

Avar(β̂ᵍᵐᵐ) = (G'S⁻¹G)⁻¹ where G = E[∂g/∂β'] and S = Σⱼ E[gₜgₜ₋ⱼ']

Under the optimal weighting matrix W* = S⁻¹, this achieves the Cramér–Rao lower bound for all estimators based on these moment conditions. Under heteroskedastic errors, 2SLS (which uses W = (Z'Z)⁻¹) is inefficient; efficient GMM dominates. The difference is not academic: in practice, robust-GMM standard errors are substantially more reliable than 2SLS with misspecified homoskedastic variance estimates.

ii. moment condition richness: the Arellano–Bond panel estimator

Arellano–Bond — lagged levels as instruments for first-differenced equation; exploits internal instruments; solves Nickell bias in fixed-effects dynamic panelsThe Arellano–Bond (1991) estimator illustrates GMM’s power in dynamic panels. Consider:

yᵢₜ = αyᵢ,ₜ₋₁ + xᵢₜ'β + μᵢ + εᵢₜ First difference: Δyᵢₜ = αΔyᵢ,ₜ₋₁ + Δxᵢₜ'β + Δεᵢₜ

First-differencing removes μ&#x{i}. But Δy&#x{i,t-1} = y&#x{i,t-1} − y&#x{i,t-2} is correlated with Δε&#x{it} = ε&#x{it} − ε&#x{i,t-1}. The moment conditions E[y&#x{is} · Δε&#x{it}] = 0 for s ≤ t−2 generate a growing set of valid instruments as T increases. The Blundell–Bond (1998) system-GMM adds levels equations to recover efficiency in persistent series. Critically, this uses instruments internal to the panel — no external instruments required.

iii. the J-test as a model diagnostic

J-statistic — L-K overidentifying restrictions tested; χ²(L-K) under H₀; falsification tool built into GMMThe Sargan–Hansen J-statistic is:

J = T · g_T(β̂)' Ŝ⁻¹ g_T(β̂) ~_d χ²(L-K) under H₀

This simultaneously tests all L−K overidentifying restrictions. If the model is rejected — J exceeds the critical value — at least one moment condition is misspecified. The Difference-in-Sargan (C-test) tests subsets of instruments and isolates which conditions are violated. This built-in falsification machinery gives GMM a robustness-checking capability that OLS lacks entirely.

iv. moment proliferation and the many-instrument problem

Many instruments — Arellano–Bond generates T(T-1)/2 moment conditions; instrument count explodes with T; finite-sample J-test distortion; Windmeijer correctionI address the main critique directly. In Arellano–Bond, the number of moment conditions grows as T(T−1)/2, which can vastly exceed the number of observations for moderate T. When the instrument count is large relative to n, two problems arise: (i) the two-step GMM estimator is biased in finite samples, and (ii) the J-statistic loses power — it becomes too lenient, failing to reject even misspecified models. The Windmeijer (2005) finite-sample correction substantially reduces the bias in two-step standard errors. Researchers should collapse the instrument matrix or limit lags. The many-instrument problem is real but it is a problem of disciplined implementation, not a structural failure of GMM. The solution is well-known: use fewer, better instruments.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Efficient GMM dominates 2SLS under heteroskedasticity via the optimal weighting matrix
1.Avar(β̂&#x{GMM}) = (G'S⁻¹G)⁻¹. Under W* = S⁻¹, this achieves the efficiency lower bound for all estimators exploiting the same moment conditions.
2.2SLS uses W = (Z'Z)⁻¹, which is optimal only under homoskedastic errors. Under heteroskedasticity, efficient GMM has strictly smaller asymptotic variance.
3.Since heteroskedasticity is the empirical norm in cross-sectional and panel data, efficient GMM dominates 2SLS as a default estimator in overidentified models.
∴Under heteroskedasticity, efficient GMM achieves the efficiency lower bound while 2SLS does not; GMM is the correct default for overidentified models in realistic empirical settings.
Argument II — Arellano–Bond generates rich internal moment conditions for dynamic panels, solving Nickell bias without external instruments
1.Fixed-effects OLS in dynamic panels is inconsistent: the “Nickell bias” plim(α̂&#x{FE}) = α − (1+α)/(T−1) + O(1/T²). For moderate T this bias is severe.
2.Arellano–Bond exploits E[y&#x{is}Δε&#x{it}]=0, s≤t−2: lagged levels are valid instruments for the differenced equation. This generates internal instruments from the panel structure itself.
3.No external instruments are required; validity conditions are testable via the J-statistic and AR(2) test on differenced residuals. The estimator is consistent for fixed T as N→∞.
∴Arellano–Bond GMM solves the Nickell bias of fixed-effects OLS using internally generated instruments; it extends causal inference to dynamic panel settings where OLS and 2SLS cannot operate.
Argument III — The J-statistic and C-test provide built-in falsification of moment conditions
1.J = T·g&#x{T}(β̂)'&#x{Ŝ}⁻¹g&#x{T}(β̂) ~&#x{d} χ²(L−K) under H₀: all L moment conditions correctly specified. Rejection signals misspecification.
2.The Difference-in-Sargan (C-test) tests whether a specific subset of instruments is valid, conditional on the rest. This localises the source of misspecification.
3.These diagnostics expose the model’s identifying assumptions to empirical falsification — a methodological discipline entirely absent in OLS.
∴The J-statistic and C-test make GMM’s identifying assumptions testable and falsifiable; OLS has no equivalent discipline for its exogeneity assumption; GMM is epistemically superior.
Argument IV — The many-instrument problem is addressable via instrument collapse and the Windmeijer correction
1.Many instruments inflate J-test size and bias two-step GMM standard errors. When instrument count L is close to n, the J-statistic has near-zero power.
2.Solutions: (i) collapse the instrument matrix to limit instruments to one lag per variable; (ii) apply the Windmeijer (2005) finite-sample variance correction to two-step GMM; (iii) use CU-GMM which is less sensitive to many instruments.
3.The many-instrument problem is a diagnostic implementation issue, not a structural failure. The solutions are standard practice in applied econometrics.
∴Many-instrument bias is addressable via instrument collapse and the Windmeijer correction; these are standard diagnostics, not fundamental objections; GMM remains the preferred estimator for dynamic panel data.

The canonical Difference-in-Differences estimator, popularised by Card and Krueger’s 1994 minimum wage study, is the most widely used quasi-experimental method in applied economics. It removes time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity and common time shocks simultaneously — two sources of bias that OLS cannot handle. The modern staggered-adoption literature has identified and corrected a serious TWFE flaw that strengthens rather than weakens the DiD research programme.

The classical 2×2 DiD estimator.

Let Y&#x{it} be the outcome for unit i in period t, D&#x{it}∈{0,1} treatment, Post&#x{t}∈{0,1} time indicator. The two-way fixed effects (TWFE) estimator is:

Y&#x{it} = α&#x{i} + λ&#x{t} + τD&#x{it} + ε&#x{it}

τ̂&#x{DiD} = (&Ybar;&#x{treated,post} − &Ybar;&#x{treated,pre}) − (&Ybar;&#x{control,post} − &Ybar;&#x{control,pre})

Under parallel trends E[Y&#x{it}(0)|D&#x{i}=1] − E[Y&#x{it}(0)|D&#x{i}=0] = constant in t, this identifies ATT.
i. what DiD achieves that OLS cannot

DiD removes unit fixed effects AND common time shocks simultaneously — OLS controls for neither without strong additional assumptionsOLS on D&#x{it} regressed against Y&#x{it} estimates β = Cov(D,Y)/Var(D) — which conflates the treatment effect with (a) cross-unit differences in baseline outcomes and (b) time trends shared by all units. Adding unit fixed effects removes (a). Adding time fixed effects removes (b). The TWFE estimator does both jointly, requiring only the parallel trends assumption, which is substantially weaker than the OLS exogeneity assumption E[D&#x{it}ε&#x{it}]=0. Pre-trend tests — examining whether treated and control units moved in parallel before treatment — provide empirical evidence for parallel trends, giving DiD a testable falsifiability that OLS cannot offer.

ii. the Card–Krueger identification strategy

Card–Krueger — New Jersey/Pennsylvania natural experiment; state-specific minimum wage hike as exogenous variation; classic DiD applicationCard and Krueger (1994) compared employment in New Jersey fast-food restaurants (treated: minimum wage rose from $4.25 to $5.05 in April 1992) to Pennsylvania restaurants (control: no change). The DiD estimate: Δemployment&#x{NJ} − Δemployment&#x{PA} found no negative employment effect, contradicting the standard competitive model. The power of the design lay in using Pennsylvania as a credible counterfactual for what New Jersey employment trends would have been absent the policy. No external instrument was needed; the policy variation itself was the identifying mechanism, and the parallel pre-trends between the two states supported the identifying assumption.

iii. staggered adoption and the Callaway–Sant’Anna correction

TWFE negative weights — in staggered DiD, TWFE β̂ is a weighted average of 2×2 DiDs with potentially negative weights; CS(2021) fixes thisGoodman-Bacon (2021) shows that under staggered adoption, the TWFE estimator is a weighted average of all possible 2×2 DiD comparisons, some of which use already-treated units as controls. If treatment effects are heterogeneous over time, these comparisons receive negative weights, and τ̂&#x{TWFE} can be sign-reversed relative to all ATTs. The Callaway–Sant’Anna (2021) estimator computes cohort-specific ATTs:

ATT(g,t) = E[Yₜ(g) - Yₜ(∞) | Gᵢ = g]

where g is cohort-entry-date and Yₜ(∞) is the never-treated potential outcome, then aggregates these into a single summary statistic with non-negative weights. This preserves the DiD identification logic while eliminating the negative-weight pathology. The critique of TWFE strengthened the DiD programme by motivating a rigorous replacement that is now standard practice.

iv. addressing the parallel trends critique

Parallel trends testing — pre-event plots and Roth (2022) sensitivity analysis bound the bias from violations; assumption is testable in the pre-periodThe parallel trends assumption is untestable in the strict sense because it concerns the counterfactual post-period outcome under no treatment. However: (i) pre-trend tests in event-study specifications check whether treated and control units were on parallel paths before treatment, providing falsifiable evidence; (ii) Roth (2022) and Rambachan–Roth (2023) provide sensitivity analysis that bounds the ATT estimate under specified deviations from parallel trends; (iii) covariate-conditional parallel trends (Abadie 2005) weakens the assumption to hold only within cells of observable covariates. These tools make the DiD assumption operationally transparent in a way that OLS exogeneity is not.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — TWFE simultaneously removes unit fixed effects and common time trends; OLS cannot do either without strong additional assumptions
1.OLS on Y&#x{it} = τD&#x{it} + ε&#x{it} conflates treatment with baseline level differences (α&#x{i}) and time-common shocks (λ&#x{t}).
2.TWFE Y&#x{it} = α&#x{i} + λ&#x{t} + τD&#x{it} + ε&#x{it} removes both jointly; under parallel trends τ̂&#x{TWFE} identifies ATT without requiring treatment exogeneity in the cross-section.
3.Parallel trends is weaker than OLS exogeneity: it permits selection into treatment on time-invariant observables and unobservables, requiring only that time-varying confounders be common to treated and control units.
∴TWFE achieves causal identification under an assumption — parallel trends — substantially weaker than OLS exogeneity; it is preferred wherever treatment is correlated with baseline unit characteristics.
Argument II — Pre-event study plots provide empirical evidence for parallel trends; OLS exogeneity has no equivalent pre-test
1.Event-study coefficients for pre-treatment periods estimate whether treated and control units were on parallel paths before the intervention; a flat pre-trend is empirical evidence for the identifying assumption.
2.Roth (2022) formalises this: pre-trend tests have power against specific violations of parallel trends. Rambachan–Roth (2023) provides honest confidence sets under bounded deviations from parallel trends.
3.OLS has no pre-trend equivalent: the exogeneity assumption E[D&#x{it}ε&#x{it}]=0 is untestable from within the OLS framework. DiD’s assumption is operationally transparent by comparison.
∴Pre-event study plots and Rambachan–Roth sensitivity analysis make the DiD identifying assumption partially falsifiable; OLS exogeneity has no equivalent empirical discipline; DiD is methodologically more honest.
Argument III — Callaway–Sant’Anna (2021) corrects the TWFE negative-weight pathology in staggered adoption
1.Goodman-Bacon (2021): under staggered adoption with heterogeneous effects, τ̂&#x{TWFE} receives negative weights on some 2×2 comparisons, potentially reversing its sign relative to all true ATTs.
2.CS(2021) computes ATT(g,t) = E[Y&#x{t}(g)−Y&#x{t}(∞)|G&#x{i}=g] for each cohort-period cell and aggregates with non-negative weights. This restores valid causal identification under heterogeneous effects.
3.The critique of TWFE was resolved within the DiD programme by producing a better estimator; OLS has no analogous self-correction mechanism for its endogeneity problem.
∴The TWFE negative-weight problem was identified and corrected within the DiD literature; CS(2021) is now the standard estimator for staggered adoption; the DiD programme demonstrates scientific self-correction.
Argument IV — Covariate-conditional parallel trends further relaxes the identifying assumption while remaining testable
1.Standard parallel trends requires: E[ΔY(0)|D=1] = E[ΔY(0)|D=0]. This is restrictive if treated and control units differ on observables that predict trends.
2.Conditional parallel trends (Abadie 2005): E[ΔY(0)|D=1, X] = E[ΔY(0)|D=0, X]. Conditioning on observed covariates X relaxes the assumption to within-covariate-cell comparisons, drastically expanding the set of credible DiD applications.
3.Sant’Anna–Zhao (2020) provides doubly-robust DiD estimators that combine outcome regression and IPW, inheriting the robustness properties of both.
∴Conditional parallel trends and doubly-robust DiD estimators extend the method’s applicability while remaining testable; DiD’s identifying assumptions are progressively weaker than OLS exogeneity and progressively more falsifiable.

Regression discontinuity design has the highest internal validity of any non-experimental causal identification strategy. Where a continuously distributed running variable determines treatment assignment via a known threshold, the discontinuity generates local randomisation: units just above and just below the cutoff are, in all relevant pre-treatment characteristics, exchangeable. The design is transparent, visually interpretable, and subject to rigorous falsification tests.

Sharp RDD identification.

Let X&#x{i} be the running variable, c the cutoff, D&#x{i} = 1(X&#x{i} ≥ c) treatment. The sharp RDD estimand is:

τ̂&#x{RDD} = lim&#x{x↓c} E[Y&#x{i}|X&#x{i}=x] − lim&#x{x↑c} E[Y&#x{i}|X&#x{i}=x]

Under the continuity assumption — that the conditional expectation E[Y(0)|X] and E[Y(1)|X] are continuous at c — this identifies LATE at the cutoff: E[Y(1)−Y(0)|X=c]. No further assumptions about unobserved heterogeneity are needed.
i. continuity and local randomisation

Local randomisation — near the cutoff, assignment is as-good-as-random; all pre-treatment covariates should be balanced; directly testable via covariate smoothnessThe key intuition is that near the cutoff, the running variable X acts as a randomisation device. Units with X marginally above c are treated; units marginally below are not. If agents cannot precisely manipulate X, then within a narrow window around c, treatment assignment is effectively random. This means all pre-treatment covariates Z should be smooth at c: E[Z|X=x] should have no discontinuity at c. This is directly testable, and provides the strongest observable falsification test available in any quasi-experimental design — one that the McCrary (2008) density test and covariate smoothness tests formally implement.

ii. optimal bandwidth selection and local polynomial estimation

MSE-optimal bandwidth — Imbens–Kalyanaraman and Calonico–Cattaneo–Titiunik (2014) provide data-driven bandwidth selection balancing bias and varianceThe practical implementation of RDD requires choice of bandwidth h around the cutoff and polynomial order p. The MSE-optimal bandwidth balances squared bias against variance:

h_MSE = C_n · n^{-1/5} (local linear, p=1) τ̂_RDD = β̂₊ - β̂₋ from local polynomial regressions on [c, c+h] and [c-h, c) respectively

Calonico, Cattaneo and Titiunik (2014) provide bias-corrected and robust (BCR) confidence intervals that account for the bias induced by bandwidth selection, achieving asymptotically valid inference without requiring undersmoothing. The Imbens–Kalyanaraman (2012) bandwidth selector is the standard default; robustness to alternative bandwidths is a standard diagnostic that directly exposes any cherry-picking of the estimate.

iii. the McCrary density test and placebo checks

McCrary test — density of X should be smooth at c; discontinuity signals manipulation; running-variable heaping near c falsifies the designThe McCrary (2008) density test tests whether the density of the running variable f(x) is continuous at c. OLS estimates the conditional mean. For many of the most important questions in economics — the wage premium at the top of the skill distribution, the effect of education on poverty at the bottom, the heterogeneous impact of credit constraints across wealth quartiles — the conditional mean is the wrong estimand. Quantile regression, introduced by Koenker and Bassett in 1978, provides estimates at any point in the conditional distribution, revealing treatment effect heterogeneity that OLS conceals.

The quantile regression estimator.

The τ-th conditional quantile Q&#x{τ}(Y|X) is estimated by minimising:

β̂(τ) = argmin&#x{β} ∑&#x{i} ρ&#x{τ}(y&#x{i} − x&#x{i}'β)

where ρ&#x{τ}(u) = u(τ − 1(u<0)) is the asymmetric “check function” loss. For τ=0.5, this is median regression (LAD). The estimator requires no distributional assumptions on ε&#x{i}; OLS assumes E[ε&#x{i}]=0 and optimally weights large residuals quadratically, which is inefficient and misleading under fat tails.
i. the check function and robustness properties

Check function — asymmetric L1 loss; insensitive to outliers; efficient under non-Gaussian errors; median regression is 64% efficient vs OLS under normality but vastly better under heavy tailsThe OLS loss function is (y−x'β)², which penalises outliers quadratically. Under Gaussian errors this is optimal, but economic data routinely exhibit fat tails, skewness, and heteroskedasticity. The check function ρ&#x{τ}(u) penalises positive residuals at rate τ and negative residuals at rate 1−τ. This asymmetric weighting is far less sensitive to outliers. Under t&#x{5} distributed errors, LAD (median regression) is asymptotically 73% more efficient than OLS. The entire quantile process β(τ) for τ∈(0,1) provides a complete picture of how X shifts the distribution of Y, not just its mean.

ii. heterogeneous treatment effects and distributional impact

Distributional heterogeneity — OLS ATT masks variation across quantiles; QR reveals if policy lifts bottom without moving top or vice versaConsider estimating the return to education on wages. OLS gives the mean effect E[Δlog(w)|Δedu=1]. But the policy question may be specifically about inequality: does education reduce poverty (bottom quantile) or primarily benefit the already-advantaged (top quantile)? Koenker and Bassett showed that β̂(0.1) and β̂(0.9) can differ substantially even when β̂&#x{OLS} is precisely estimated. Quantile process plots — plotting β̂(τ) for τ∈[0.05,0.95] — reveal this entire heterogeneity structure. A test of homogeneity H₀: β(0.1) = β(0.5) = β(0.9) directly tests whether OLS meaningfully summarises the relationship.

iii. inference and the quantile process

Khmaladze transform and bootstrap — standard errors for QR use kernel density or bootstrap; simultaneous confidence bands for entire quantile process availableStandard errors for quantile regression estimates require the conditional density f(0|X), which is not directly observable. Three approaches are available: (i) sandwich estimators using kernel density estimates of f; (ii) bootstrap resampling, which is computationally straightforward and asymptotically valid; (iii) the Khmaladze martingale transform, which provides simultaneous confidence bands for the entire quantile process β(τ) uniformly in τ. The Koenker–Xiao (2002) test using this transform has power against both location-shift and scale-shift alternatives, making it a unified test for the adequacy of OLS as a summary of the conditional distribution.

This identifies the distributional effect of an endogenous treatment variable at each quantile, generalising both IV and quantile regression into a unified framework. Modern empirical economics routinely involves datasets where the number of potential predictors p is comparable to or exceeds the sample size n. OLS is undefined when p > n and is wildly unstable when p is close to n. LASSO and its econometric adaptations — particularly the Post-Double-Selection estimator of Belloni, Chernozhukov and Hansen (2014) — provide principled, inference-valid solutions to the high-dimensional confounder selection problem.

The LASSO optimisation problem.

LASSO (Tibshirani 1996) solves:

β̂ = argmin&#x{β} (1/2n)‖y − Xβ‖² + λ‖β‖₁

The ℓ₁ penalty λ‖β‖₁ = λ∑|β&#x{j}| shrinks small coefficients exactly to zero, performing simultaneous estimation and variable selection. Ridge regression uses ℓ₂ penalty λ‖β‖² and shrinks all coefficients but selects none. The tuning parameter λ is chosen by cross-validation or by the Belloni–Chernozhukov (2013) data-driven penalty λ = cσ√(log(p)/n).
i. near-oracle rate convergence under sparsity

Sparsity + REC — LASSO achieves near-oracle rates when true model is sparse and restricted eigenvalue condition holds; prediction error vanishes at optimal rateUnder the restricted eigenvalue condition (REC) and sparsity — the true coefficient vector β₀ has at most s non-zero entries, s << n — LASSO achieves the prediction error bound:

||Xβ̂ - Xβ₀||²/n ≤ C · s · λ² · log(p)/n → 0 β̂ convergence rate: ||β̂ - β₀||₁ ≤ C' · s · λ (near-oracle)

This is near-oracle in the sense that the rate sλ is at most a log(p) factor above the rate achievable if we knew the true support of β₀ in advance. LASSO achieves this simultaneously over estimation and model selection, without prior knowledge of which variables are relevant — exactly what is needed in high-dimensional confounding settings.

ii. post-double-selection for valid causal inference

PDS-LASSO — runs LASSO on outcome AND treatment equations; takes union of selected controls; OLS on union gives valid inference on treatment effect α despite selectionThe critical contribution of Belloni, Chernozhukov and Hansen (2014) is that naive post-LASSO — run LASSO on y&#x{i} = d&#x{i}α + x&#x{i}'β + ε&#x{i}, then OLS on selected controls — gives invalid inference because LASSO may omit confounders correlated with d but with small coefficients in the outcome equation (the “omitted variable at the boundary” problem). Post-double-selection (PDS) fixes this:

Step 1: LASSO of y on x → selected set Î₁ Step 2: LASSO of d on x → selected set Î₂ Step 3: OLS of y on d, x_{Î₁ ∪ Î₂} → α̂_PDS

By selecting confounders using both equations, PDS achieves uniformly valid inference on α even when LASSO makes model-selection mistakes in individual steps. The asymptotic distribution of α̂&#x{PDS} is normal with near-oracle variance, enabling standard t-tests and confidence intervals for the treatment effect despite the high-dimensional control problem.

iii. LASSO-based instrument selection

LASSO IV — Belloni et al. LASSO selects instruments in the first stage — predicting the endogenous regressor d from a large set of potential instruments z:

Step 1: LASSO of d on z → selected instruments ẑ Step 2: 2SLS of y on d using ẑ as instruments Rate: ||α̂_2SLS - α||² ≤ C · s_z · log(p_z)/n

This is particularly valuable in settings with many potential instruments — judge fixed effects, market access instruments, historical policy variables — where manual selection would be arbitrary and potentially invalid. λ* = c · σ · √(log(p)/n) · Φ⁻¹(1 - γ/(2p)) where γ is the confidence level (typically 0.1/log(n))

This data-driven penalty achieves the near-oracle rate without cross-validation and is implemented in the hdm R package. The IV estimator has a glittering theoretical reputation and a troubled empirical history. In practice, the most common problem in IV applications is not invalid instruments — though that is also endemic — but weak instruments, which cause the estimator’s finite-sample distribution to be wildly non-normal, and which in the worst cases produce estimates more biased than OLS itself.

The anatomy of weak-instrument bias.

Let μ² = π'(Z'Z/n)π/σ&#x{uu} be the concentration parameter, where π is the first-stage coefficient. The approximate 2SLS bias relative to OLS is:

Bias(2SLS)/Bias(OLS) ≈ 1/(μ² + 1)

When the concentration parameter μ² is small — corresponding to a weak first stage — the 2SLS bias approaches 1× the OLS bias. The F-statistic in the first stage is approximately (μ²/L) + 1, so F=10 corresponds to μ² ≈ 9(L), giving at most 10% relative bias. But this rule of thumb is for a single endogenous variable and under specific assumptions about error structure.
i. the finite-sample non-normality problem

Non-normality — with weak instruments, 2SLS t-statistics have heavy tails; standard confidence intervals under-cover; Staiger–Stock (1997) weak-IV asymptoticsUnder strong instruments, 2SLS is asymptotically normal: √n(β̂&#x{2SLS}−β) →&#x{d} N(0,V). Under weak instruments, Staiger and Stock (1997) show that the concentration parameter μ² is O(1) rather than O(n), and the limiting distribution of the 2SLS estimator is a ratio of two correlated normal random variables — a distribution with potentially undefined moments and thick tails. Standard confidence intervals are then severely miscalibrated:

P(β₀ ∈ CI₀.₉₅) ≪ 0.95 under weak instruments Anderson–Rubin CI: [{β : AR(β) ≤ χ²_{L,0.05}}] is valid regardless of instrument strength but can be unbounded or disconnected when instruments are weak

The Anderson–Rubin test inverts a statistic that is pivotal under weak instruments, so it provides honest confidence sets — but the cost is that the confidence set may be the entire real line, honestly communicating that the data are uninformative. This is the correct answer, but it means IV produces no causal information in such cases.

ii. the many-weak-instruments problem

Many weak instruments — adding more weak instruments reduces individual F-statistics; 2SLS bias grows with L even if individual instruments are valid; LIML bias grows more slowly but has heavier tailsA common response to weak instruments is to add more instruments, hoping to increase the concentration parameter. But Bekker (1994) and Staiger–Stock (1997) show that when L (the number of instruments) grows with n, 2SLS exhibits persistent bias even when each instrument is individually weak. Specifically:

Bias(2SLS) ≈ (L/n) · (σ_xu / σ_xx) when L/n → c > 0 Bias grows with L even when each instrument's Cov(z_l, x) is diminishing at rate 1/√L

LIML has better median-unbiasedness under many weak instruments but heavier tails, so inference with LIML requires even more care. The Fuller (1977) modified LIML estimator is the most reliable under many-instrument settings, but it requires choosing a tuning constant. There is no free lunch: identifying variation that is genuinely weak cannot be rescued by accumulating more weak instruments.

iii. the LATE external validity problem

LATE external validity — IV identifies effect only for compliers; compliers may be unrepresentative; different instruments identify different LATEs; results are not portable across policiesEven when the instrument is strong and valid, IV identifies LATE — the average treatment effect for compliers. Deaton (2010) presses the point forcefully: compliers are an instrument-specific subpopulation whose characteristics we typically cannot fully characterise. Different instruments for the same treatment identify different LATEs, and these need not be comparable:

LATE₁ = E[Y₁-Y₀ | D₁(Z=1) > D₁(Z=0)] (compliers for Z₁) LATE₂ = E[Y₁-Y₀ | D₁(Z=1) > D₁(Z=0)] (compliers for Z₂) LATE₁ ≠ LATE₂ in general; cannot aggregate without additional assumptions about treatment heterogeneity

A policy-maker who wants to know the effect of a programme for the average participant — ATT — gets no direct answer from IV. The LATE is the causal effect for the marginal complier, who may have very different characteristics from the average treated unit. Angrist and Imbens are correct that LATE is a well-defined estimand; Deaton is also correct that for many policy questions it is the wrong estimand.

iv. the exclusion restriction and the credibility deficit

Exclusion restriction — fundamentally untestable in just-identified models; Bound–Jaeger–Baker show even small violations cause large bias; the cure can be worse than the diseaseThe exclusion restriction — Cov(z,u)=0 — is the crucial untestable assumption of IV. In just-identified models, it is entirely untestable. Bound, Jaeger and Baker (1995) show that even very small violations of exclusion can produce IV estimates more biased than OLS. Specifically, if the instrument has a small direct effect δ on the outcome:

plim(β̂ᴵᵛ) = β + δ/π₁ where π₁ = Cov(z,x)/Var(z) is the first-stage coefficient. If |δ/π₁| > |Bias_OLS|, IV is more biased than OLS.

For weak instruments (small π&#x{1}), even a tiny exclusion violation δ is amplified to a large bias. The J-test for overidentified models tests whether instruments give consistent answers, but it tests nothing about the common component of all instruments’ biases — a weakness that is often glossed over in applied work.

The promise of GMM — efficient estimation from weak distributional assumptions using moment conditions — is compromised in practice by two related pathologies: moment proliferation and the finite-sample breakdown of the J-test. Roodman (2009) documented these comprehensively for the Arellano–Bond estimator, showing that a large fraction of published panel GMM results are products of excessive instrumenting rather than genuine causal identification.

The instrument proliferation problem in Arellano–Bond.

The moment conditions E[y&#x{is}Δε&#x{it}]=0 for s≤t−2 generate a matrix of instruments of dimension n×(T−2)(T−1)/2. For T=10, this is 36 instruments per variable. With K endogenous variables, the instrument count is K·T(T−1)/2. Roodman (2009) shows that when instrument count L approaches or exceeds n, the two-step GMM estimator has severe downward bias in standard errors, and the J-test approaches a distribution with zero degrees of freedom relative to its nominal χ²(L−K) — effectively always failing to reject.
i. the J-test size distortion under many instruments

J-test size collapse — when L → n, the J-statistic loses all discriminatory power; it fails to reject almost all models; published DiD papers show this systematic patternThe J-statistic under the null is χ²(L−K). When L is large relative to n, the consistent estimator of S used in constructing the J-statistic is imprecise, and its inverse is numerically unstable. Roodman (2009) documents:

Under many instruments (L/n → c): J →_d χ²(L-K) with L/n → c ⇒ J →_p 0 systematically (the test becomes mechanically easy to pass) Windmeijer (2005) finite-sample corrected variance for two-step GMM: V_corr = V₂ + Δ where Δ accounts for estimation of the weighting matrix

Roodman’s survey of published Arellano–Bond papers finds that in papers where instrument count exceeds n, J-statistics are uniformly small and p-values uniformly large — the exact pattern of a test with zero power. Authors interpreted non-rejection as evidence of valid instruments; in reality, the test was uninformative.

ii. finite-sample bias in the two-step GMM estimator

Two-step GMM bias — the efficient weighting matrix uses Ŝ⁻¹ estimated from first-step residuals; with many instruments, Ŝ is singular or near-singular; estimated standard errors collapseThe two-step efficient GMM estimator uses the weighting matrix W&#x{2} = &Ŝ;⁻¹, where &Ŝ; is estimated from first-step (2SLS) residuals:

Ŝ = (1/n)Σᵢ ẑᵢẑᵢ' ⊗ ε̂ᵢ² (heteroskedastic case) When L is large: rank(Ŝ) ≤ n < L, so Ŝ⁻¹ requires regularisation. Without it, standard errors of β̂_2step are downward biased by factor (1 - L/n)

The Windmeijer (2005) correction reduces but does not eliminate this bias; the correction itself requires assumptions about the second-derivative structure of the criterion function. The deeper problem is that the efficient GMM estimator is only efficient in large samples; in samples where L/n is non-negligible, one-step GMM (=2SLS) or LIML is more reliable than the “efficient” two-step estimator.

iii. the consistent estimation of the long-run variance S

Long-run variance — S = Σ_j E[g_t g_{t-j}'] requires kernel HAC estimation; depends on fourth moments; unreliable in small samples; optimal bandwidth for S is data-dependentThe optimal GMM estimator requires &Ŝ; → S = Σ&#x{j=-∞}&#x{∞} E[g&#x{t}g&#x{t-j}']. For time-series applications, S must be estimated by a kernel heteroskedasticity-and-autocorrelation consistent (HAC) estimator:

Ŝ_HAC = Σ_{|j|≤M} k(j/M) · Γ̂ⱼ where Γ̂ⱼ = (1/T)Σₜ ĝₜĝ'ₜ₋ⱼ (sample autocovariance)

Hayashi (2000) notes that consistent estimation of S requires “very large sample sizes” because S involves fourth moments. The bandwidth M grows with T, and the choice of kernel k(·) affects finite-sample performance substantially. Andrews (1991) provides the data-driven M̂ that minimises MSE of &Ŝ;&#x{HAC}, but this relies on estimated AR(1) representations of g&#x{t}, which may be misspecified. Under misspecification of S, the claimed efficiency advantage of GMM over 2SLS disappears and may reverse.

iv. the collapse of identification: testing instrument validity is harder than it looks

Instrument validity untestable in GMM — J-test tests all restrictions jointly; C-test requires maintained subset; no instrument can be tested in isolation without auxiliary assumptionsThe J-test’s logical structure means it can never definitively validate a set of instruments — only reject them. In the Arellano–Bond context, the C-test compares J-statistics from models with and without a subset of instruments, but this requires treating the complement as validly specified — an assumption that is itself untestable. In applications:

C-statistic = J_restricted - J_unrestricted ~_d χ²(L_restricted - L_unrestricted) under H₀ But H₀ requires maintaining J_unrestricted instruments as valid — which itself requires non-rejection of J_full

If J&#x{full} rejects, no subset can be tested without arbitrary choices about which instruments to maintain. If J&#x{full} does not reject — which is likely under many instruments — the non-rejection is uninformative. The identification framework of GMM panel estimators therefore rests on an untestable foundation precisely in the settings where it is most commonly applied.

Pre-trend tests provide only indirect evidence and have limited power. The serial correlation problem in DiD inference (BDM 2004).

The standard DiD regression Y&#x{it} = α&#x{i} + λ&#x{t} + τD&#x{it} + ε&#x{it} is estimated with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. But D&#x{it} is typically a long panel of 0s followed by 1s — serially correlated by construction. BDM (2004) show that treating ε&#x{it} as uncorrelated within unit, when in fact Corr(ε&#x{is}, ε&#x{it}) ≠ 0, causes the standard error of τ̂ to be understated by a factor that can approach 10 in long panels. The nominal t-statistic rejects H&#x{0}: τ=0 at 5% with probability up to 45% — a 9-fold over-rejection rate.
i. the BDM serial correlation problem

Serial correlation — DiD treatment indicator is serially correlated; standard errors are understated by factor up to 10; rejection rate 45% at nominal 5% in long panelsBertrand, Duflo and Mullainathan (2004) run Monte Carlo placebo experiments using US state-level data. They assign random “treatments” to random states and test H&#x{0}: τ=0. The correct rejection rate under H&#x{0} should be 5%. Using standard OLS standard errors, they find:

Rejection rate at 5% level: up to 45% (≈9× correct rate) Cause: SE(τ̂) understated because within-unit serial correlation ignored: Var(τ̂) ∝ Σₜ Σₛ Cov(εᵢₜ, εᵢₛ) ≠ Σₜ Var(εᵢₜ)

The correct inference requires clustering standard errors at the level of treatment variation (the state), which allows for arbitrary within-cluster serial correlation. But cluster-robust inference requires many clusters — the rule of thumb is at least 50 — and with 50 states, the asymptotic approximation is reasonable. With fewer clusters (e.g., two countries, or policy variation across eight regions), even cluster-robust inference is unreliable, and wild cluster bootstrap or randomisation inference is required.

Roth (2022) makes a crucial point: this test is underpowered for detecting violations of parallel trends that are economically meaningful. Specifically:

Power of pre-trend test ∝ σ_δ/SE(δ̂_pre) where σ_δ is the magnitude of the pre-trend slope and SE(δ̂_pre) is the standard error of pre-trend coefficients. Roth (2022) shows: pre-trend tests typically have power ≤ 0.5 against violations that would bias τ̂_DiD by 20%.

Researchers who select studies on passed pre-trend tests are selecting on a noisy signal that is poorly correlated with post-treatment parallel trends. Moreover, when researchers pre-test and then condition on non-rejection, the resulting confidence intervals are invalid. The pre-trend test creates a selection bias that distorts both estimation and inference.

β̂_TWFE = ΣΩ_kl · DiD_kl where some weights Ω_kl < 0 when treatment effects vary with duration (dynamic heterogeneity)

This failure was present in an enormous body of published work. But economic theory predicts that policy adoption is endogenous: governments adopt minimum wages when labour markets are tightening; firms adopt technology when productivity growth is slowing; countries liberalise trade when growth is high. Treatment ↑ when Y_trend(1) > Y_trend(0) ⇒ E[ΔY(0)|D=1] > E[ΔY(0)|D=0] ⇒ Parallel trends violated by economic rationality

This is not a pathological edge case. It is the generic prediction of rational policy adoption. The method’s identifying assumption and the economic models it is supposed to test are in direct tension.

The RDD research programme has done more than any other quasi-experimental method to clarify the trade-off between internal and external validity. Its great virtue — local randomisation at the cutoff — is also its great limitation: it identifies a treatment effect only at a single point of the running variable, for a subpopulation at the margin of the threshold. This LATE-at-the-cutoff is, for most policy questions, the least interesting estimand available.

The external validity problem: what RDD identifies and what policy asks.

Sharp RDD identifies τ̂&#x{RDD} = E[Y(1)−Y(0)|X=c] — the treatment effect at exactly x=c, for units with a running variable at the threshold. Policy questions typically ask:

(a) What is the effect for all treated units? (ATT)
(b) What is the effect of changing the threshold? (policy extrapolation)
(c) What is the effect for the average person? (ATE)

RDD answers none of these directly. Extrapolation from the cutoff to the broader treated population requires assumptions about how effects vary with x that cannot be tested from the RDD design alone.
i. the LATE-at-the-cutoff and the limits of external validity

External validity failure — RDD estimates are valid only at X=c; effect heterogeneity with X makes extrapolation impossible without untestable functional form assumptionsRDD identification is locally valid: the continuity assumption E[Y(0)|X=x] is continuous at c, but says nothing about how the treatment effect varies with x away from the cutoff. To extrapolate τ(c) to τ(x) for x ≠ c requires either (a) constant treatment effects — a homogeneity assumption that defeats the purpose of RDD — or (b) a parametric model for how effects vary with x. Neither is identified from the RDD design. Angrist and Rokkanen (2015) provide conditions under which the RDD estimate can be extrapolated beyond the cutoff, but these require the selection-on-observables assumption outside the bandwidth — conditions that are often implausible and that remove the design advantage of RDD entirely if they hold.

The Imbens–Kalyanaraman (2012) MSE-optimal bandwidth minimises expected squared error:

h_IK = C(K) · n^{-1/5} · (σ²(c))^{1/5} / (B(c))^{2/5} where B(c) is the second-derivative bias term. B̂(c) requires a global polynomial regression to estimate, introducing a new specification choice.

The estimate τ̂&#x{RDD}(h) is sensitive to h: halving or doubling h routinely produces estimates that differ by 20–50% in applied settings. The CCT (2014) bias-corrected estimator reduces but does not eliminate this sensitivity. But manipulation need not be perfect to invalidate RDD. “ε-manipulation” — where agents can shift the running variable by small amounts — does not produce detectable density discontinuities but is sufficient to violate continuity of the potential outcome at c:

If units with X* ∈ [c-ε, c) can shift to [c, c+ε] with cost proportional to effort, then: E[Y(0)|X=c⁺] ≠ E[Y(0)|X=c⁻] even if density(c⁺) = density(c⁻) (undetectable by McCrary test)

In education: students who just failed can study harder and retake; firms near a tax threshold can adjust; voters near an electoral threshold can mobilise. In each case, the running variable is manipulable at low cost near the cutoff, inducing selective composition above and below c that the McCrary test cannot detect. Global polynomial: Y = Σ_p β_p X^p + τD + ε Runge oscillations near cutoff: large variance in τ̂ even when the true regression function is smooth Local linear (p=1): τ̂_LL has boundary bias O(h²) Local quadratic (p=2): bias O(h³) but variance increases Optimal order p: Imbens–Wager (2019) suggest p=1 (local linear) for most applications, but the choice materially affects estimates

(2014) as the default, but in settings with non-linear conditional expectations near the cutoff, local linear may have large bias — precisely in the settings where treatment effect estimation is most important. The synthetic control method is an important advance for comparative case studies, and I do not dispute its ingenuity. But its statistical properties are far weaker than its promoters acknowledge. With a donor pool of 20–40 units, placebo-based inference has power equivalent to a test at the 2–5% level that can reject with at most 2–5% power against alternatives of realistic magnitude. This is not robust causal inference; it is causal suggestion.

The synthetic control estimator.

Abadie, Diamond and Hainmueller (2010) construct weights W* = (w&#x{1},...,w&#x{J}) on J donor units minimising ‖X&#x{1} − X&#x{0}W‖&#x{V} subject to w&#x{j}≥0, ∑w&#x{j}=1, where X&#x{1} is a K×1 vector of pre-treatment predictors for the treated unit and X&#x{0} = [X&#x{2},...,X&#x{J+1}] is K×J for donor units. V is a diagonal weighting matrix for predictors. The treatment effect estimate is:

α̂&#x{1t} = Y&#x{1t} − ∑&#x{j=2}&#x{J+1} w&#x{j}*Y&#x{jt} for t > T&#x{0}

Inference relies on running synthetic controls for each donor unit and comparing effect sizes via the ratio of post/pre RMSPE.
i. the low-power inference problem

Inference power — placebo inference with J=39 donors gives minimum p-value 1/40 = 2.5%; this is a ceiling on the power of the procedureThe synthetic control inference procedure is permutation-based: apply the synthetic control method to each of J donor units to generate placebo effects, then test whether the treated unit’s effect is extreme relative to the placebo distribution. With J=39 donor units (the Proposition 99 case), the minimum achievable p-value is 1/40 = 0.025. This is not a 5% test — it is a 2.5% test at best. More problematically, the power of this test against alternatives of realistic magnitude is:

Power ∝ (1 - α_post · T_post)^{J-1} where α_post is the fraction of placebo RMSPEs smaller than the treated. With J=20 donors and effect half the RMSPE: Power ≈ 0.35 at nominal α = 1/20 = 5% level

Ferman and Pinto (2021) show that in standard synthetic control applications, the placebo test has power below 0.5 against treatment effects equal to the observed estimate under realistic noise distributions. The conclusion “the California Proposition 99 effect is significant at the 2.5% level” relies on a test with unknown and often low power against the alternatives it is supposed to detect.

ii. the predictor weighting problem

Predictor sensitivity — V matrix choice is arbitrary; different predictor sets and lags produce different weights and different effects; no data-driven V is identified from the designThe synthetic control weights W* depend on the predictor weighting matrix V. ADH (2010) suggest choosing V to minimise the pre-treatment prediction error — but this is a nested optimisation that creates degrees of freedom:

W*(V) = argmin_W ||X₁ - X₀W||_V subject to constraints V* = argmin_V ||Y₁_pre - Y₀_pre W*(V)||² The two-level optimisation is not convex and has multiple local optima. V* depends sensitively on: which predictors to include, which years of outcome to use as predictors, the optimisation algorithm and starting values.

Klossner et al. (2018) demonstrate that estimates change substantially under alternative predictor sets and optimisation specifications. The researcher-facing choice of predictors and the optimisation method creates degrees of freedom that are never accounted for in the reported confidence set. Different implementation choices for the same question can produce estimates spanning the null.

iii. SUTVA violations and spillover

SUTVA violation — treatment unit effect spills over to donors via trade, migration, or policy diffusion; polluted counterfactual; systematic underestimation of effectsThe synthetic control method requires the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA): the outcome of donor unit j is unaffected by treatment of unit 1. For macro-level policy evaluations — Proposition 99, German reunification, trade liberalisation — this assumption is routinely violated. Treatment of California’s tobacco policy reduces smoking in donor states through diffusion of anti-smoking norms, price spillovers, and regulatory emulation:

If Y₀ₜ(1) ≠ Y₀ₜ(0) for donor units j=2,...,J+1: Y₁ₜ - Σwⱼ*Yⱼₜ = α₁ₜ - Σwⱼ*(Yⱼₜ(1) - Yⱼₜ(0)) ≈ α₁ₜ - spillover bias

Spillover to the donor pool through the treatment causes the synthetic control to under-state the treatment effect — it uses donors that have themselves been partially treated. For geographically proximate or economically integrated donors (EU member states, US states, trade partners), SUTVA violations are the norm rather than the exception.

iv. the donor pool construction problem

Donor pool — no principled rule for donor inclusion; cherry-picking donors produces pre-specified conclusion; exclusion of outlier donors changes weights dramaticallyThere is no principled criterion for which units to include in the donor pool. ADH (2010) suggest excluding donors with very different pre-treatment outcomes from the treated unit (to avoid extrapolation) — but this requires comparing the treated unit to potential donors, creating a specification-searching problem. When a donor that “would receive high weight” is excluded, the remaining weights and the synthetic control outcome change substantially. Klossner et al. (2018) show that leave-one-out robustness plots — the standard diagnostic — reveal sensitivity to donor exclusion but provide no principled resolution. The researcher can select the donor pool until the pre-treatment fit is visually satisfying, a procedure with unknown but potentially high false-positive rates.

The Heckman selection model is a genuine contribution to the literature on sample selection bias — a problem that is real and pervasive. But the two-step estimator’s identification strategy is fragile in a way that is systematically understated in applied practice. Without a valid exclusion restriction, identification relies entirely on the non-linearity of the inverse Mills ratio under bivariate normality — an assumption that is rarely tested and routinely violated.

The Heckman two-step estimator.

Let y&#x{2i} be the outcome of interest, observed only when y&#x{1i}=1 (the selection indicator). The structural equations are:

Selection: y&#x{1i} = 1(z&#x{i}'γ + u&#x{1i} > 0), with u&#x{1i} ~ N(0,1)
Outcome: y&#x{2i} = x&#x{i}'β + u&#x{2i}, with (u&#x{1i},u&#x{2i}) ~ BVN(0,0,1,σ&#x{22},ρ)

E[y&#x{2i}|x&#x{i},y&#x{1i}=1] = x&#x{i}'β + ρσ&#x{22} λ(z&#x{i}'γ̂)

where λ(·) = φ(·)/Φ(·) is the inverse Mills ratio. Step 1: probit to get γ̂. Step 2: OLS of y&#x{2i} on x&#x{i} and λ(z&#x{i}'γ̂) for selected observations. The coefficient on λ is ρσ&#x{22}.
i. identification without exclusion restrictions

No exclusion restriction — without an instrument, identification comes from normality alone via non-linearity of λ; this is parametric identification, not causal identificationWhen x = z (all variables in the outcome equation also appear in the selection equation), the Heckman model is theoretically identified purely from the non-linearity of the inverse Mills ratio λ(z'γ) ≈ z'γ + (z'γ)²/2 for small arguments. Vella (1998) and Puhani (2000) demonstrate that this identification is extremely fragile:

λ(t) = φ(t)/Φ(t) ≈ t for small t (approximately linear) Without exclusion: identification relies on curvature of λ(t) which is negligible unless z'γ̂ spans wide range. In practice: λ̂ is nearly collinear with linear combination of x_i → near-singular second-stage regression → enormous standard errors and numerical instability

The Heckman estimator without exclusion restrictions is, in practice, an OLS regression with an additional nearly-collinear regressor. The estimates are numerically unstable, standard errors are inflated by a factor of 5–20 compared to a correctly-specified model with instruments, and the coefficient on the inverse Mills ratio — which measures the selection bias — is imprecisely estimated regardless of the magnitude of the true bias.

ii. the bivariate normality assumption

Bivariate normality — both error terms must be jointly normal; semi-parametric alternatives exist but require exclusion restriction; violation causes inconsistency of all parametersThe Heckman model requires that (u&#x{1i}, u&#x{2i}) are jointly bivariate normally distributed. This assumption is sufficient for the inverse Mills ratio correction to produce consistent estimates of β. Under non-normal errors:

E[y₂ᵢ | xᵢ, y₁ᵢ=1] = xᵢ'β + E[u₂ᵢ | u₁ᵢ > -zᵢ'γ] Under normality: E[u₂ | u₁ > t] = ρσ₂ λ(t) Under non-normality: E[u₂ | u₁ > t] = g(t), an unknown function → the IMR correction is misspecified → β̂ is inconsistent for all components

Heckman (1990) and subsequent work provide semi-parametric alternatives (distribution-free selection models) that do not require normality — but these all require an exclusion restriction and are substantially more computationally demanding. The choice between the parametric two-step and the semi-parametric alternatives is itself endogenous to whether a plausible exclusion restriction is available. Without one, applied researchers default to the parametric model whose normality assumption they typically do not test.

iii. the inverse Mills ratio collinearity problem

IMR collinearity — in high-selection-rate samples, λ(z'γ) is nearly linear in z'γ and hence nearly linear in x; collinearity inflates SE of β by factor 5–20; corrections unreliableEven when an exclusion restriction is available, the second-stage Heckman regression frequently suffers from near-multicollinearity between the inverse Mills ratio and the regressors. Leung and Yu (1996) show that the correlation between λ(z&#x{i}'γ̂) and x&#x{i} is typically 0.9–0.99 in samples where selection rates exceed 70% or are below 30%:

Corr(λ(z'γ̂), xᵢ) → 1 as P(y₁=1) → 0 or 1 Inflation factor on SE(β̂): κ = 1/(1 - R²_{λ,x}) → ∞ In practice: SE(β̂_Heckman) / SE(β̂_OLS) ∈ [5, 20] for typical selection rates and sample sizes

This collinearity means the Heckman correction reduces the bias of β̂ but massively inflates its variance. The net mean-squared-error of the Heckman estimator may exceed that of the (biased) OLS estimator in finite samples. Vella (1998) recommends using the two-step only as a specification test for selectivity, not as the primary estimator: if the coefficient on λ is significant, an IV or other approach is needed rather than the Heckman two-step itself.

iv. the semiparametric alternative and its requirements

Semiparametric selection models — distribution-free but require exclusion restriction AND large samples for density estimation; no free lunch relative to Heckman parametricAhn and Powell (1993), Das, Newey and Vella (2003), and Newey (2009) provide semiparametric selection models that replace the normality assumption with nonparametric estimation of E[u&#x{2i}|u&#x{1i}>−z&#x{i}'γ]. These require:

Step 1: Estimate conditional density f(u₁ | z) nonparametrically Step 2: Estimate correction function g(t) = E[u₂ | u₁ > t] using series or kernel estimators Step 3: OLS with nonparametric correction Rate: ||β̂_semi - β|| = O_p(n^{-2/5}) for kernel density vs. ||β̂_Heckman - β|| = O_p(n^{-1/2}) under normality Semiparametric is slower: requires n^{1.25} sample size to achieve same RMSE as parametric Heckman under normality

The semiparametric estimator is consistent under much weaker assumptions but converges at a slower rate and requires a valid exclusion restriction. It is not a free alternative to the parametric Heckman model: it simply requires that the exclusion restriction be available and the sample be very large. Neither Heckman nor its semi-parametric alternatives provide a genuine solution to selection bias in the absence of an exclusion restriction. OLS on the selected sample, while biased, at least has transparent failure modes. The Heckman estimator without instruments provides a precise-looking but structurally unreliable estimate.
