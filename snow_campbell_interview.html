Alastair Campbell interview with Jon Snow

Channel 4 News interview over the government's row with the BBC about its Iraq coverage.

Published March 21, 2026

[00:00-01:35]

Jon Snow: Now we are joined by Alastair Campbell - a rare moment - thank you for coming in. This is row between you and the BBC. Many will see it as a diversionary tactic to prevent people from actually seeing the real issue there, which is that MPs are not getting to the root of whether in fact the intelligence we were provided with was the real intelligence provided by the intelligence services.

Alastair Campbell: Well, if people wish to see it as a diversionary tactic, they may. The media are constantly telling people never to take things at face value. This is not a row between me and the BBC. This is an attempt by the government to get the BBC to admit that a fundamental attack upon the integrity of the government, the Prime Minister, the intelligence agencies, let alone people the evil spin doctors in the dark who do their dirty works in the minds of the journalists. Let them just accept for once that they have got it wrong. The allegation, let's just understand what this allegation amounted to. And these weasel words in Richard Sambrook's letter today that says to me, we didn't make the allegation. We reported a source making the allegation. What does that say about journalism? You have been in journalism for decades, I was a journalist for quite a long time, I respect a huge number of journalists including many at the BBC, but they now say you can say anything you want on the television because somebody said it to you. It doesn't matter if it's true. It doesn't matter if you check it. It doesn't matter if it's corroborated. You can say it.

Snow's opening gambit frames the entire interview as a diversion. Campbell's response is an elegant demonstration of 4c [query the presupposition]. Rather than deny the charge, Campbell turns the media's own epistemological rule against them by saying "the media are constantly telling people never to take things at face value." It's a presupposition attack disguised as philosophical agreement. He then proceeds with a 5a [attack the interviewer's framing] in which he avoids saying "it is not a diversion" by saying "you may call it that if you like," which implicitly questions whether Snow's framing has merit while refusing to concede it does not.

[01:35-02:29]

Jon Snow: However, the BBC's riposte to you is very reasoned. It is set in the context of all the other information which was in the public domain. It is entirely consistent with that information. It credits the Guardian, the Observer, the Independent, The Times, I mean most of Fleet Street had similar accounts of what intelligence sources were telling them. The BBC doesn't seem to be out of step with anybody else.

Alastair Campbell: The BBC in their letter to me, and it is fascinating, they have post facto justification of a story by citing sources in newspapers which wrote stories subsequent to their, to the story they had done. Some of those stories, I know for a fact are incorrect. One of them, there is no point in going through all the detail, and I think the public are probably bored rigid with this already. One of those stories I know for a fact is wrong, and I have addressed in evidence to the Select Committee.

Here, Campbell hits Snow with a triple-stack. (1) Campbell refuses to name which story is wrong, a confidentiality/selectivity move which is a typical 6c. (2) He then pre-emptively dismisses the public's interest — "bored rigid" — which trivialises the line of questioning, a clear use of 4d. (3) Campbell tops this off with a 10a, deflecting to a prior answer given to a different forum (the Select Committee) where the answer allegedly already exists. Two other things to note about this answer. First, at first he claims "Some of those stories I know for a fact are incorrect" but then in the next sentence he subtly amends this to "one of those stories I know for a fact is wrong," moving almost unnoticed between 'some' which suggests multiple and 'one' which is a lot less convincing. By the time he settles on 'one,' he has already put 'some' in people's heads, ready to establish this with official credibility by claiming he "addressed it in evidence to the Select Committee", leaving it to seem as if he has presented multiple, not just one, to the Select Committee without having ever actually said this. The second thing to note is the phrase "there is no point in going through all the detail." This phrase is particularly telling because it asserts that more detail exists while refusing to supply it.

[02:29-03:25]

Jon Snow: I think the public is more likely to be concerned at the extraordinarily intemperate language which is coming out on behalf of the Prime Minister in your name. 'The story was a lie, it is a lie.' 'Weasel words,' weasel not incidentally spelt correctly in consistent terms of the original fake dossier...

Alastair Campbell: If I may say so, the statement that you are reading from was read to the Press Association. So that, that, I would not get hung up on the spelling mistake by somebody who's typed it, although I know that you, you also Jon, reported that the four people in my office were responsible for writing the so-called dodgy dossier when they were not. However, put that to one side, the reason that is weasel words is that it does not answer the questions that I put. I asked the BBC whether they were standing by the allegation they made, the BBC made, as John Humphrys described it, the BBC made the allegation that we deliberately exaggerated, abused, distorted.

Snow tries to use the spelling error in the "dodgy dossier" as a point of attack. Campbell absorbs the blow and immediately counter-attacks with Snow's own alleged inaccuracy — the claim that Snow reported the wrong people wrote the dossier. This is a classic 5a move: you are questioning my accuracy, but you have been inaccurate too. The personal attack on Snow is then nominally withdrawn ("put that to one side") — but the damage is done, Snow is now briefly the defendant. Campbell then pivots back to his own preferred ground: the BBC's failure to directly answer his questions.

[03:25-03:51]

Jon Snow: The answer to the question you put to the BBC, do they stand by it? The answer is yes, a robust yes.

Alastair Campbell: Excuse me, that letter is about as robust as Blackburn Rovers were when they played Trelleborgs (sic). I'll tell you, the answer to the question - yes or no? Did we abuse British intelligence? The answer to that question is no.

This is Campbell's most technically sophisticated move in the interview. He substitutes his own question for Snow's. Snow's question is broad: did the government misuse intelligence? Campbell narrows it to a very specific formulation — "did we abuse British intelligence?" — and then answers that. This is Bull's literalism category at its finest: by choosing the word "abuse" (a very high threshold) rather than "sex up," "exaggerate," or "distort," Campbell answers a question no one was asking in quite that form. The Blackburn Rovers aside is also notable — it ridicules the BBC's letter and Snow's endorsement of it without engaging with either's content.

[05:54-06:34]

Jon Snow: But you have heard the Foreign Secretary himself tell that committee that that dossier was a Horlicks.

Alastair Campbell: Well, I am sorry, again, deliberate conflation of two things. Correct yourself. Correct yourself, it is not the same document. Do you accept that?

Jon Snow: Which is not the same document?

Alastair Campbell: You just said that the Foreign Secretary described the dossier as a Horlicks

Jon Snow: The dodgy dossier.

Alastair Campbell: Excuse me we were talking about the weapons of mass destruction dossier. This is the problem. The people who have been opposed to this conflict from the word go are now seeking to change the ground and to say the Prime Minister led the country into conflict on a false basis and you are deliberating conflating the issues.

Campbell catches Snow conflating the two dossiers, the September WMD dossier and the "dodgy dossier" (the February 2003 document) and interjects with a 4b [Question based on false premise]. This is a legitimate factual correction, but Campbell weaponises it with a 5a [Accuses interviewer of bad faith / bias]. Rather than accepting the correction and moving on, he uses it to impugn Snow's entire credibility and motivation. "You are deliberately conflating the issues" carries the charge of bad faith, accusing him not of error but of ill intention. He then broadens this into a 7a [Attacks the opposition] to attack all opponents of the war, framing the entire line of questioning as anti-war advocacy dressed up as journalism.

[06:34-07:19]

Jon Snow: The issue in play here today is absolutely that this war was fought on the basis of intelligence information. That intelligence information firstly; the charge that in the first document in September there were serious errors of fact.

Alastair Campbell: Sorry the first document in September? There were serious errors of fact? And what were they Jon?

Jon Snow: The Niger allegation in which the Minister who was supposed to have signed the nuclear purchasing order had himself resigned many years before.

Alastair Campbell: You know do you Jon that that was the basis on which British intelligence put that in the dossier? You know that, do you? Because if you think that, you are wrong. There were no errors of fact in the WMD dossier in September 2002.

Three moves in rapid succession. First, Campbell interrupts with feigned surprise — "serious errors of fact? And what were they, Jon?" — forcing Snow to commit to a specific allegation. Once Snow names the Niger allegation, Campbell deploys a competence attack: "you know, do you Jon?" — repeated twice — implies Snow does not actually know what he thinks he knows. Then comes the literalism: Campbell's defence is that the Niger claim was based on British intelligence assessment, not the forged documents. This is a technical truth used to deflect a broader accuracy question. "There were no errors of fact" is an absolute categorical denial that sidesteps the question of whether the underlying intelligence was reliable.

[07:33-08:03]

Jon Snow: Well, one, one fact is one is absolutely incontrovertible and that is the second dossier, the dodgy dossier, was indeed just that. And the last question...

Alastair Campbell: And I have acknowledged that. As I have acknowledged. And I have admitted. Let me just draw this contrast between myself, between the government and the BBC. On Wednesday, at that Select Committee, I acknowledged we had made a mistake. I accepted responsibility for that on behalf of the junior.. The official in my office who made the mistake.

Campbell concedes the "dodgy dossier" point — but he concedes it in a way that generates credit rather than accountability. By emphasising that he has already acknowledged this (three times in one sentence: "I have acknowledged... as I have acknowledged... and I have admitted"), he frames the concession as a sign of his own integrity. He then immediately pivots to the BBC as a contrast: he admitted his mistake; the BBC has not admitted theirs. The concession becomes a weapon. Bull would classify this as a rare example of acknowledgement deployed strategically as part of a political point.

[08:11-08:45]

Jon Snow: I want to ask you one last question. The last question is, you are now part of the story. When the government's communications' chief is himself part of the story, isn't it time he resigned?

Alastair Campbell: Oh for heaven's sake. The reason I am part of the story is that a BBC journalist made an allegation about me.

Jon Snow: Against whom the BBC believe you have a vendetta because he's caused you trouble.

Alastair Campbell: I have never met the guy. I have never met Andrew Gilligan. I don't have a vendetta against him.

The resignation question receives three moves. "Oh for heaven's sake" is a dismissal of the question's premise — it signals contempt without engaging the substance, Bull's category of treating the question as undeserving of a real answer. Campbell then reframes the premise: "the reason I am part of the story is that a BBC journalist made an allegation about me" — repositioning himself as victim rather than subject of legitimate scrutiny. Finally, the vendetta charge is deflected with literalism: "I have never met the guy" technically addresses whether he has a personal relationship with Gilligan, but says nothing about whether he has conducted a professional or institutional campaign against him.
