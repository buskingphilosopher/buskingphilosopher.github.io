Mr Speaker,

The debate about the nature of welfare is one of the deepest in practical philosophy. I want to make clear what is genuinely at stake, and why the subjective tradition — properly refined — is better placed to answer it than the objective alternatives.

In Reasons and Persons I distinguished three broad theories: hedonism, desire theories, and objective list theories. I raised difficulties for all three. But the difficulties I raised for desire theories — particularly the problem of informed versus actual desires — are ones that desire theorists can address, and have addressed, through the move to idealised or informed desire accounts. The difficulties I raised for objective list theories are harder to resolve, because they concern the very foundations of the view: the question of what entitles us to say that certain things are good for a person independently of whether that person values or enjoys them.

The structure of the debate.

The central question is: what is the relationship between a person’s welfare and their own perspective? Hedonism and desire theories locate welfare within the subject’s experiential or conative life. Objective list theories deny that this is sufficient: certain things — knowledge, achievement, deep personal relationships — are good for you whether or not you experience them as good.

The fundamental objection to objective list theories: if something is good for you despite being neither experienced as good nor desired by you in any informed way, the claim that it is good for you becomes mysterious. It becomes good simpliciter, not good-for. This collapses the distinction between welfare and impersonal value.
i. the informed desire view as a refinement of hedonism

Informed desire — corrects extensional failures of simple desire theory; retains subjectivityThe simple desire theory — that welfare consists in getting what you want — faces well-known counterexamples. I may want to count the blades of grass on a lawn, a desire whose satisfaction makes my life no better. I may want something based on a factual mistake. I may have adaptive preferences formed under conditions of deprivation. The move to informed desires addresses all three. What makes my life go best is what I would want if I were fully informed, fully rational, and had vividly imagined how the satisfaction of each desire would feel. This is preference hedonism: it is both a desire theory (welfare is grounded in what the subject would want) and hedonistic (the imagined-feeling test keeps experiential quality in view). It is more defensible than either simple hedonism or simple desire theory because it incorporates the insights of both.

ii. the alienation problem for objective list theories

Alienation objection — objective goods not experienced as good fail to benefit the subjectConsider a person who achieves great things — publishes important books, forms lasting friendships, contributes to science — but who, through depression or temperamental incapacity, experiences none of this as good. Does her life go well? Objective list theorists say yes: achievement and deep relationships are on the list, she has achieved them, therefore she is benefited. But this seems wrong. If the achievement is not experienced as good and not desired by her in any reflective sense, it is hard to understand in what sense it is good for her. It is good in the world; it is not obviously good for her. The objective list theory cannot distinguish between what is impersonally valuable and what constitutes welfare, and this is a fundamental failure.

iii. the experience machine revisited

Experience machine — intuition misfires; shows we care about more than welfare, not that welfare is non-experientialNozick’s experience machine is often taken to refute hedonism: we would not plug in, therefore welfare is not merely experiential. But this inference is too quick. The intuition that we would not plug in may reflect not a belief that non-experiential goods contribute to welfare, but rather a belief that other things matter beyond welfare — that we care about really doing things, not merely having the experience of doing them. This is consistent with welfare being entirely experiential. The experience machine shows that we value more than welfare, not that welfare is constituted by more than experience. Crisp makes this point with particular force; it is one of the strongest responses available to the hedonist.

iv. against the objectivist alternative

Against objectivism — no principled account of list contents; risks paternalism; collapses welfare into impersonal valueObjective list theories owe us an account of what is on the list and why. Hurka’s perfectionism grounds the list in the development of essential human capacities. Arneson’s perfectionism grounds it in species-typical functioning. But these accounts are either too demanding (the great artist who neglects friendship is thereby harming herself), or too permissive (any species-typical capacity counts), or question-begging (the list turns out to contain exactly what we antecedently found plausible). Worse, objective list theories risk a form of paternalism: they say that a person who finds deep satisfaction in simple pleasures and has no desire for knowledge or achievement is nonetheless missing something crucial for her welfare. The subjective tradition, properly refined, avoids this implication.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Informed desire theory corrects the extensional failures of simple hedonism and simple desire theory
1.Simple desire theory fails: satisfying arbitrary, misinformed, or adaptive preferences does not constitute welfare benefit.
2.Simple hedonism fails: it cannot account for the intuition that some pleasures (e.g. malicious ones) contribute less to welfare than their hedonic intensity would suggest.
3.The informed desire view corrects both: welfare is what the fully informed, rational agent would want for their actual self, incorporating the experiential test via vividly imagined consequences.
∴The informed desire view is a principled synthesis that inherits the subjectivity of desire theories and the experiential grounding of hedonism while correcting the extensional failures of both; it is more defensible than either simple account.
Argument II — Objective list theories conflate welfare with impersonal value via the alienation problem
1.If X is good for a person P despite being neither experienced as good nor desired by P in any reflective or informed sense, then X’s goodness does not depend on P’s perspective at all.
2.But welfare is a person-relative concept: what is good for P must stand in some constitutive relation to P’s own experiential or conative life.
3.Objective list theories cannot explain this relation: they collapse the distinction between what is impersonally valuable and what constitutes welfare.
∴Objective list theories face the alienation problem: goods that are neither experienced as good nor desired are not plausibly good for the person; the list collapses into impersonal value, abandoning the person-relative character of welfare.
Argument III — The experience machine objection misfires: it shows we value more than welfare, not that welfare is non-experiential
1.Nozick’s experience machine: we would not plug in, therefore welfare is not merely experiential. But the intuition to decline may reflect valuing things beyond welfare (e.g. really doing things, having genuine relationships) not a belief that non-experiential goods constitute welfare.
2.If the refusal to plug in reflects caring about more than welfare — a plausible hypothesis — then the machine shows only that welfare is not all we value, not that welfare is non-experiential.
3.This reading of the intuition is at least as plausible as the objectivist reading; the experience machine is therefore not a decisive refutation of hedonism.
∴The experience machine objection is compatible with hedonism: the refusal to plug in reflects a plurality of values beyond welfare, not a belief that welfare is constituted by non-experiential achievements.
Argument IV — Objective list theories lack a principled account of list contents and risk paternalism
1.Objective list theories require a principled account of what is on the list. Extant accounts (Hurka’s perfectionism, Arneson’s species-typical functioning) are either too demanding, too permissive, or question-begging.
2.Worse, objective list theories entail that a person who finds deep satisfaction in simple pleasures but lacks the listed goods (knowledge, achievement) is thereby failing to flourish — a paternalistic implication that runs counter to ordinary welfare attributions.
3.Subjective theories avoid this implication: what is good for a person is constrained by what they actually or would reflectively care about.
∴Objective list theories face twin failures: they lack a principled basis for their list contents, and they imply a paternalistic verdict on lives that subjectively flourish without the listed goods; the subjective tradition is more parsimonious and less revisionary.

Hedonism has been too hastily dismissed in the post-Parfit literature. I want to revive it in a form that is genuinely defensible against the standard objections.

The core hedonist claim is that welfare consists entirely in pleasant experience: what makes your life go better is the occurrence of pleasant states, and what makes it go worse is the occurrence of unpleasant ones. This sounds crude, but its crudeness is in the statement, not in the theory. A sophisticated hedonism acknowledges qualitative distinctions among pleasures, avoids simple additive aggregation, and provides a richer account of what pleasure itself is. With these refinements, hedonism is more defensible than any rival.

“Hedonism is, I believe, the most plausible theory of well-being available. Its rivals either secretly rely on hedonist considerations at crucial points, or generate conclusions that are more counterintuitive than those they were designed to avoid.”
i. the experience machine reconsidered

Experience machine — intuition misidentifies what we care about; doesn't refute hedonismThe standard presentation of the experience machine asks whether you would plug in to a life of simulated pleasures indistinguishable from real ones. Most people say no. But notice what the machine presents: it removes your real relationships, your real achievements, your real causal engagement with the world. The intuition to refuse may be tracking the loss of these things — which we value for non-welfare reasons — rather than a belief that the simulated pleasures would fail to benefit you. When we separate the question carefully — would you be better off plugged in, all else equal? — the intuition that you would not is much less clear. The experience machine does not refute hedonism; it exploits our tendency to conflate what is good for us with everything we care about.

ii. qualitative distinctions do not require objectivism

Qualitative hedonism — qualitative distinctions internal to hedonism; no objectivist commitments neededMill noticed that some pleasures seem qualitatively superior to others: better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied. This observation is often taken to require an objective list: the higher pleasures are higher because they involve the exercise of capacities that are objectively valuable. But this inference is mistaken. Qualitative distinctions among pleasures can be internal to hedonism: a pleasure is qualitatively higher not because it involves an objectively valuable activity but because it is a richer, more complex, more deeply satisfying kind of felt experience. The felt quality of intellectual engagement differs from the felt quality of sensory comfort in a way that can be evaluated on hedonist grounds, without reference to any objective list. Qualitative hedonism preserves the Millian intuition without the objectivist commitment.

iii. objective list theories rely on hedonist considerations at crucial points

Hidden hedonism in OLT — achievement counts only when felt; lists implicitly track experiential valueWhen we probe why achievement is on the objective list, the most plausible answer makes implicit reference to the experienced satisfaction of achievement. An achievement that is never experienced as such — that leaves no positive trace in the subject’s experiential life — is much less plausibly a welfare benefit than one that is experienced as an achievement. Similarly, knowledge that the subject does not experience as illuminating or satisfying contributes far less, if anything, to welfare. Objective list theories, to the extent that they generate plausible verdicts, do so because the listed goods are correlated with experiential benefits. Where they diverge from experiential considerations, their verdicts become counterintuitive. This suggests that the experiential dimension is doing most of the real work.

iv. the adaptive preference problem does not defeat hedonism

Adaptive preferences — hedonism can distinguish genuine from deformed pleasure; the distinction is internalThe most powerful objection to hedonism from within the subjective tradition is the adaptive preference problem: people raised under conditions of deprivation may take genuine pleasure in what should not satisfy them. Their pleasures are real but their preferences have been deformed by their circumstances. Does this show that pleasure is insufficient for welfare? I deny it. Hedonism can accommodate a distinction between authentic and deformed pleasures without importing objective content: a deformed pleasure is one that would not survive exposure to better alternatives, or one that is experienced against a background of suppressed preferences that, if expressed, would conflict with it. This distinction is internal to hedonism and does not require an objective list to adjudicate it.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The experience machine exploits conflation between welfare and all-things-considered value
1.The experience machine deprives the agent of real relationships, real achievements, and real causal engagement. The intuition to refuse tracks the loss of things valued for non-welfare reasons.
2.When the question is carefully reformulated — would you be better off, all else equal? — the intuition is much less clear. The machine objection exploits a conflation between welfare and everything we care about.
3.If the refusal reflects caring about more than welfare, it is consistent with hedonism about welfare: we value more than welfare, but welfare itself is experiential.
∴The experience machine does not refute hedonism; it exploits a conflation between welfare and broader all-things-considered value; once the conflation is resolved, hedonism remains unscathed.
Argument II — Qualitative distinctions among pleasures are internal to hedonism; no objectivist commitment is needed
1.Millian qualitative distinctions among pleasures are real. But they need not be grounded in objective activity-values; they can be grounded in the richer, more complex felt quality of certain experiences.
2.Intellectual pleasure differs from sensory comfort in its felt character: it is more complex, more self-sustaining, more deeply satisfying. This difference is evaluable on hedonist grounds.
3.Qualitative hedonism therefore preserves the Millian intuition without any objectivist commitment: the higher pleasures are higher in felt quality, not because they involve objectively valuable activities.
∴Qualitative distinctions among pleasures do not require an objective list; they are grounded in the comparative felt complexity and richness of different experiential types; this supports hedonism, not objectivism.
Argument III — Objective list theories covertly rely on hedonist considerations at their most plausible points
1.Why is achievement on the list? The most plausible answer references the experienced satisfaction of achievement. An achievement never experienced as such is a far weaker candidate for a welfare benefit.
2.Objective list theories generate plausible verdicts precisely where the listed goods correlate with experiential benefits. Where they diverge from experiential considerations, their verdicts become counterintuitive.
3.This suggests the experiential dimension is doing most of the genuine welfare-constitutive work; the objective list is a theoretical superstructure resting on a hedonist foundation.
∴Objective list theories secretly rely on hedonist considerations at their most plausible points; their genuine explanatory work is done by the experiential dimension they officially supplement or replace.
Argument IV — Adaptive preferences are distinguishable within hedonism without importing objective content
1.A deformed pleasure is one that would not survive exposure to better alternatives, or that is experienced against a background of suppressed preferences. This distinction is internal to hedonism.
2.Hedonism can therefore accommodate the adaptive preference intuition: a pleasure that reflects adaptation to deprivation is not a full welfare benefit because it is conditional on ignorance of alternatives.
3.This response does not require importing an objective standard of what the agent should enjoy; it remains within the subjective framework by appealing to counterfactual experiential comparisons.
∴Hedonism can distinguish authentic from adaptive pleasures without objectivist commitments; the adaptive preference objection does not defeat hedonism but refines it.

My aim in the 1986 paper was to find a view that is robustly subjective — that keeps welfare anchored in the agent’s own perspective — without the extensional failures of simple desire theory.

The key move is to distinguish between what the agent actually wants and what an ideally informed, instrumentally rational version of that same agent would want for their actual self. Welfare is constituted by the latter, not the former. This is subjective because the idealised agent is a projection of the actual agent’s own psychology under conditions of full information and rationality; it is not an external standard imposed from outside. But it avoids the extensional failures of simple desire theory because idealisation filters out misinformed and irrational desires.

“What is intrinsically valuable for a person is what he would want himself to want, or to pursue, if he were to contemplate his present situation from a standpoint of full information.” — Railton, 1986
i. the subjective internalist structure

Subjective internalism — ideally informed rational counterpart; avoids alienation; avoids extensional failuresThe central structural claim is this: for each actual agent A, we can construct a counterpart A+ who is fully informed about all relevant facts, instrumentally rational, and who deliberates about what is best for A — the actual agent, not for A+ as a hypothetical person. Welfare is what A+ would want for A. This structure avoids the alienation problem that afflicts objective list theories: the ideally informed counterpart is a projection of A’s own psychology, not an externally imposed standard. It also avoids the extensional failures of simple desire theory: A+ filters out desires based on ignorance or irrationality. What remains is a robustly subjective but extensionally adequate account of welfare.

ii. the objectivity of welfare facts is compatible with subjective constitution

Objective facts, subjective constitution — welfare facts can be objectively true without objective list contentOne might object that if welfare facts are to be action-guiding, they must have some objective standing. How can what is good for me be merely what an idealised version of me would want? My answer is that the objectivity of welfare facts does not require objective list content. The facts about what A+ would want for A are perfectly objective facts — they are facts about a specific idealised psychology in a specific situation — even though their content is determined entirely by A’s own subjective perspective under idealisation. Objectivity of statement is compatible with subjectivity of constitution. This is what makes the view both normatively respectable and genuinely respectful of the individual’s own perspective.

iii. against the objective list: the problem of alien goods

Alien goods — objective goods that the agent cannot recognise as good are not plausibly beneficialConsider an agent who, through no cognitive failure, does not value knowledge, achievement, or deep personal relationships. She finds genuine satisfaction in routine, in sensory pleasure, in simple social bonds. According to objective list theories, she is missing crucial welfare goods. But there is no cognitive or rational failure here: her values are the product of her psychology, fully informed. On my view, if A+ would not want knowledge or achievement for A, then knowledge and achievement are not welfare goods for A. This respects the individuality of welfare in a way that objective list theories cannot. Objective list theories impose alien goods — goods the person cannot recognise as good from within their own perspective — and this is a fundamental failure of the concept of welfare.

iv. responding to the extensional objections to desire theory

Extensional objections handled — full information screens out misinformed, adaptive, and external desiresThe standard objections to desire theory concern desires that seem clearly insufficient for welfare: the desire to count blades of grass, desires formed under conditions of deprivation, desires about strangers whose satisfaction I will never know. The informed desire account handles all three. The grass-counting desire: A+ would not want A to count blades of grass once fully aware of the opportunity costs. Adaptive desires: A+ has full information about alternatives; deformed desires do not survive idealisation. Future strangers: A+ wants things for A, not for unnamed third parties; desires about strangers are filtered as outside the scope of A’s welfare. The view is both extensionally adequate and theoretically unified.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The subjective internalist structure avoids both alienation and extensional failure
1.Welfare is what A+, the fully informed rational counterpart of A, would want for A (the actual agent). A+ is a projection of A’s own psychology under idealisation, not an external standard.
2.This avoids alienation: the welfare standard is derived from A’s own perspective, not imposed from outside. It avoids extensional failure: idealisation filters out misinformed and irrational desires.
3.The result is a robustly subjective but extensionally adequate account of welfare that inherits the person-relativity of desire theory while avoiding its failures.
∴The subjective internalist account is the best-positioned theory in the welfare debate: it is subjective without being extensionally inadequate, and objective without being alien to the individual’s own perspective.
Argument II — Objectivity of welfare facts is compatible with subjective constitution
1.Facts about what A+ would want for A are perfectly objective facts about a specific idealised psychology in a specific situation.
2.These facts are objectively determinable even though their content is fixed by A’s subjective perspective under idealisation; objectivity of statement does not require objective list content.
3.The view therefore satisfies the normative requirement that welfare facts be action-guiding and objective without importing any objective list commitments.
∴Subjective constitution of welfare is compatible with the objectivity of welfare facts; the informed desire view avoids both the alienation of objectivism and the normativity deficit of simple subjectivism.
Argument III — Objective list theories impose alien goods that the individual cannot recognise from within their own perspective
1.An agent who does not value knowledge, achievement, or deep relationships through no cognitive or rational failure is, on objective list theories, missing welfare goods she cannot recognise as good.
2.On the informed desire view, if A+ would not want these goods for A, they are not welfare goods for A. This respects the individuality of welfare.
3.The concept of welfare is person-relative: what is good for a person must stand in some constitutive relation to their own perspective. Alien goods violate this constraint.
∴Objective list theories fail by imposing alien goods; the informed desire view avoids this by grounding welfare entirely in what the ideally informed version of the agent would want for their actual self.
Argument IV — Standard extensional objections to desire theory are handled by the full-information condition
1.Grass-counting, adaptive, and stranger-directed desires are the standard counterexamples to desire theory. A+ filters each: opportunity-cost awareness eliminates grass-counting; full information about alternatives dissolves adaptive desires; A+-for-A scope excludes stranger desires.
2.The filtering mechanism is theoretically unified: full information and instrumental rationality are the only idealisation conditions required; no ad hoc additions are needed.
3.The view is therefore both extensionally adequate (it gives the right verdicts) and theoretically parsimonious (it achieves this with minimal theoretical apparatus).
∴The informed desire account handles all standard extensional objections to desire theory through a single unified mechanism; it is more parsimonious than rivals and equally extensionally adequate.

My aim in Well-Being and Death is to defend hedonism as a complete and strict account of welfare — all welfare facts reduce without remainder to facts about pleasant and unpleasant experiences.

This is a bold claim, and I am aware that the post-Parfit literature is full of apparent counterexamples. I want to show that each of the major counterexamples either fails when scrutinised carefully, or is accommodated more elegantly by hedonism than by its rivals. Hedonism is not merely defensible; it is, I submit, the correct theory of welfare.

The hedonist framework.

Welfare is constituted entirely by pleasant and unpleasant experiences. A life goes better to the extent that it contains more net pleasure (pleasures minus pains, each weighted by intensity and duration). No non-experiential fact — no achievement, no knowledge, no relationship as such — contributes to welfare independently of its experiential manifestations.

This is strict: there are no other welfare goods. It is axiological: the claim is about what constitutes welfare, not merely what usually correlates with it.
i. posthumous harm and the timing problem

Posthumous harm — hedonism denies it; this is a virtue, not a vice; the intuition is unreliableOne of the most discussed apparent counterexamples to hedonism concerns posthumous harm: if my reputation is destroyed after my death, or if my wishes are betrayed, I seem to be harmed. But hedonism entails that I cannot be harmed after death: once experience ceases, welfare ceases. I accept this consequence and argue it is a virtue of hedonism, not a vice. The intuition that posthumous harm is possible is unreliable: it conflates a violation of someone’s interests or projects with harm to their welfare. A person’s projects can be frustrated without their welfare being set back, if the frustration is unfelt. This is not a failure of hedonism; it is a clarification of the concept of welfare that hedonism correctly forces upon us.

ii. the experience machine: the best available response

Experience machine — best response: intuition tracks what we want beyond welfare; hedonism about welfare survivesThe experience machine case is the most discussed objection. My response is in two parts. First, the intuition to refuse may track concerns outside welfare: real achievement, genuine relationships, causal engagement with the world. These are things we care about for non-welfare reasons. The refusal therefore does not show that welfare is non-experiential; it shows that welfare is not everything we care about. Second, there is a further consideration: many people’s refusal to plug in is partly explained by risk aversion and uncertainty about what the machine would actually deliver. In ideally specified conditions, where we are certain the pleasures would be as described, the refusal intuition weakens considerably. Neither consideration requires abandoning hedonism.

iii. against desire theory: the problem of future and distant desires

Against desire theory — future desires and stranger desires generate implausible verdicts; hedonism avoids theseDesire theories face a problem that hedonism elegantly avoids: the problem of future and distant desires. Suppose I have a strong desire that some event occur far in the future, after my death, or in a distant place I will never visit. If that desire is satisfied, desire theory entails I am thereby benefited. But this seems implausible: a benefit to my welfare must make some difference to my experiential life. Hedonism correctly predicts that satisfaction of desires whose outcomes I will never experience contributes nothing to my welfare. The desire theorist must add complications — temporal constraints, experience requirements, idealisation conditions — to handle this. Hedonism handles it simply: no experience, no welfare.

iv. objective list theories fail the parsimony test

Parsimony — OLTs multiply welfare goods without genuine explanatory gain; hedonism more parsimoniousObjective list theories multiply welfare goods: knowledge, achievement, deep relationships, aesthetic experience, and more. But each addition requires justification. Why is knowledge on the list rather than, say, curiosity-satisfaction or the exercise of rational capacities? The lists proliferate and become increasingly difficult to adjudicate. Hedonism, by contrast, recognises a single fundamental welfare good — pleasant experience — and explains the apparent plurality of welfare goods as a plurality of sources of pleasant experience. This is a straightforwardly more parsimonious explanation. The parsimony consideration is not decisive, but when parsimony favours hedonism and the arguments against hedonism are as inconclusive as I have shown, parsimony tips the balance in favour of the hedonist.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Posthumous harm: hedonism correctly denies it; the intuition conflates welfare with interest-violation
1.Hedonism entails that no welfare harm is possible after death, since welfare requires experience. The posthumous harm intuition seems to contradict this.
2.But the intuition conflates two things: violation of a person’s interests or projects (real) with harm to their welfare (requires experience). These come apart; hedonism is right to separate them.
3.The denial of posthumous welfare harm is a clarification of the concept of welfare that hedonism correctly forces; it is a virtue of the view, not a counterexample to it.
∴Hedonism’s denial of posthumous harm is correct: it correctly distinguishes welfare harm (experiential) from interest-violation (non-experiential); the intuition that posthumous harm occurs conflates these distinct notions.
Argument II — The experience machine: the refusal tracks non-welfare values; hedonism about welfare survives
1.The refusal to plug in may reflect caring about real achievement, genuine relationships, and causal engagement with the world — things valued for non-welfare reasons. This does not show welfare is non-experiential.
2.Under ideally specified conditions (certainty about the pleasures delivered), the refusal intuition weakens considerably, suggesting it is partly explained by risk aversion and uncertainty.
3.Neither consideration requires abandoning hedonism about welfare; both are consistent with welfare being entirely experiential.
∴The experience machine does not defeat hedonism; under careful analysis, the refusal intuition reflects concerns beyond welfare rather than a belief that welfare is non-experiential.
Argument III — Desire theory generates implausible verdicts about future and distant desires; hedonism avoids these simply
1.Desire theory entails that satisfaction of desires whose outcomes I will never experience contributes to my welfare. This is implausible: a welfare benefit must make some experiential difference.
2.Hedonism simply predicts that no experience = no welfare benefit; no additional complication is required.
3.Desire theorists must add temporal constraints or experience requirements to handle this case — adding complexity that hedonism handles for free.
∴Hedonism handles the future and distant desire problem more elegantly than desire theory; this is a point in its favour relative to the most prominent subjective rival.
Argument IV — Objective list theories fail the parsimony test; hedonism explains apparent plural goods more economically
1.Objective list theories multiply fundamental welfare goods without a unified explanatory principle. Each addition requires separate justification; the lists proliferate and become difficult to adjudicate.
2.Hedonism recognises one fundamental welfare good — pleasant experience — and explains the apparent plurality of welfare goods as a plurality of sources of pleasant experience.
3.When the arguments against hedonism are as inconclusive as the foregoing has shown, parsimony tips the balance in hedonism’s favour.
∴Hedonism is more parsimonious than objective list theories without being less extensionally adequate; parsimony, combined with the inconclusiveness of the counterexamples, supports hedonism as the correct theory of welfare.

I want to defend a hybrid account that takes seriously both the subjective and objective dimensions of welfare, while insisting that the objective dimension is constrained by the subject’s own perspective in a way that preserves the core insight of the subjective tradition.

My view begins from a question about desert: when is it fitting that a person’s welfare be at a particular level? I argue that welfare and desert are systematically connected, and that this connection reveals the structure of welfare itself. But the route through desert is a device for illuminating welfare, not for replacing it; my ultimate claim is about what makes lives go well, not about justice.

“A complete theory of well-being must make room for both the subject’s own endorsement of her life and for the genuine contribution of certain objective achievements — but the objective elements are constrained by the requirement that the subject can recognise them as her own.”
i. why pure subjectivism is insufficient

Against pure subjectivism — achievements matter even unfelt; endorsement constraint preserves subjectivityPure hedonism and simple desire theory share a common limitation: they cannot explain why certain achievements matter to welfare independently of whether they are experienced as good or desired. Consider a person who makes a genuine scientific discovery that goes unrecognised in her lifetime and is never experienced as an achievement by her. Hedonism says this contributes nothing to her welfare. But intuitively, something is missing from this verdict: the achievement seems to matter to how well her life goes, even unfelt. My hybrid account says: objective achievements contribute to welfare, but only when they can be recognised as such by the subject in the sense of fitting into her own self-understanding and endorsable by her from within her own perspective.

ii. the endorsement constraint: preserving the subjective core

Endorsement constraint — objective goods count only when subject-endorsable; preserves person-relativityThe crucial constraint is endorsability: an objective achievement contributes to welfare only if the subject could in principle recognise it as hers, endorse it as valuable from within her own perspective, and integrate it into her self-understanding. This constraint preserves the core insight of the subjective tradition: welfare remains person-relative in a robust sense, because the objective contributions are filtered through the subject’s own perspective. An alien good — one that the subject could not endorse from any reflective standpoint continuous with her actual psychology — contributes nothing to her welfare. This distinguishes my view from pure objective list theories and from Hurka’s perfectionism: I do not impose external standards of human excellence; I require subject-endorsability as a constitutive condition.

iii. responding to the pure objectivist

Against pure objectivism — endorsement constraint blocks alien goods; hybrid is more plausible than pure OLTPure objective list theorists like Hurka and Arneson do not require subject-endorsability: if the exercise of rational capacities is on the list, it contributes to welfare whether or not the subject endorses it. My hybrid view denies this. An objective achievement that is genuinely alien to the subject’s perspective contributes nothing to her welfare. This is more plausible than pure objectivism: it explains why we resist saying that a person is benefited by achievements she finds meaningless, while preserving the intuition that unfelt achievements can matter. The hybrid view occupies a principled intermediate position between pure subjectivism (achievements only matter when experienced as good) and pure objectivism (achievements matter regardless of the subject’s perspective).

iv. the hybrid view and the opposition

Against Nozick and Kahane — experience matters; nihilism too extreme; capabilities too impersonalI want to note briefly how my view relates to the opposition. Nozick’s anti-mental-state realism correctly insists that real engagement with the world matters, but goes too far in dismissing the role of subjective states. A life of genuine achievement that is experienced as meaningless is impoverished in a way that Nozick’s view struggles to capture. Kahane’s prudential nihilism — the view that there are no genuine welfare facts — is too extreme: we have a robust pre-theoretical understanding of welfare that cannot be dismissed. Sen’s capabilities approach captures important dimensions of what enables welfare but conflates the conditions for welfare with welfare itself. The hybrid view holds the middle ground: subjective endorsement and objective achievement are both constitutive of welfare, neither reducible to the other.

The proposition has offered us a sophisticated defence of the subjective tradition. I want to show that, however sophisticated, it cannot account for the most basic facts about what makes human lives go well.

My perfectionist objective list account holds that welfare consists in the development and exercise of distinctively human capacities. This is not an arbitrary list: the items on it are grounded in a defensible account of what humans essentially are — rational, social, and animal beings with distinctive capacities for reason, relationship, and achievement. The hedonist and desire-theoretic traditions cannot do justice to this structure because they reduce welfare to a subjective residue: to what feels good or what is wanted. But welfare is not merely a matter of how life feels or what one happens to want; it concerns what one genuinely is and what one genuinely does.

The central objection to subjectivism.

Consider two people: one who develops her rational and relational capacities to a high degree, achieving genuine understanding and forming deep personal bonds, but who, through temperamental melancholy, experiences relatively modest hedonic wellbeing; and another who, through lucky temperament, experiences high hedonic wellbeing but exercises minimal rational or relational capacity — she drifts pleasantly through life without deep engagement.

Hedonism says the second person has higher welfare. This verdict is deeply implausible. The first person lives a life that is in a clear sense better, more fully human, more genuinely flourishing. Any theory that misses this has misdescribed the phenomenon.
i. the depth objection to hedonism

Depth objection — hedonic welfare misses genuine human flourishing; quality of engagement matters objectivelyHedonism misses what I call the depth dimension of welfare. A life of shallow but intense pleasures — a life of constant entertainment, sensory gratification, and social pleasantry — scores well on hedonist measures. But it is missing something that a life of genuine intellectual achievement, moral seriousness, and deep personal engagement possesses. This something is not just a further pleasure or a further desire-satisfaction; it is a quality of engagement with the world that is objectively superior independently of how it feels. Crisp’s qualitative hedonism attempts to capture this by distinguishing higher from lower pleasures, but this move covertly introduces the objective distinction I am making under a hedonist label. The quality of a pleasure is assessed by reference to the activity that produces it, and the activity is assessed by non-hedonist standards.

ii. the endorsement constraint fails: subjects can be wrong about their welfare

Subjects can be wrong — endorsement constraint too permissive; welfare requires more than subject-endorsabilityKagan’s endorsement constraint holds that objective goods contribute to welfare only when endorsable by the subject. But this constraint is too permissive: it makes welfare depend on the subject’s endorsements, which can be systematically deformed. A person raised in a culture that suppresses intellectual development may genuinely be unable to endorse the value of theoretical understanding; the endorsement constraint then implies she does not benefit from intellectual engagement. But this seems wrong: the capacity for understanding is part of what it is to flourish as a human being, whether or not any particular subject can endorse it. The endorsement constraint makes welfare hostage to contingencies of upbringing and culture in a way that a principled account of human flourishing should resist.

iii. the objective list is not arbitrary: it is grounded in human nature

List grounded in human nature — not arbitrary; grounded in essential human capacities; Parfit’s list charge failsParfit objects that objective list theories lack a principled basis for their list contents. But this objection underestimates the resources of the perfectionist account. The list is not arbitrary: it is grounded in an account of what humans essentially are. Humans are, essentially, rational beings with the capacity for theoretical and practical reason; social beings with the capacity for deep personal relationships; and animal beings with a range of physical capacities and pleasures. The items on the welfare list are those that develop and exercise these essential capacities. This is a principled ground that explains and constrains the list in a way that does not simply codify antecedent intuitions. iv. against the alienation objection

Alienation objection fails — welfare is partly objective; the alienated achiever still benefits from genuine achievementParfit and Railton press the alienation objection: if something is good for you despite being neither experienced as good nor desired, in what sense is it good for you? Just as a plant in excellent condition flourishes whether or not it “experiences” its flourishing, a human being who genuinely develops her capacities benefits from this development even if she cannot experience it as a benefit. The person-relativity of welfare is preserved because the standard applied is the standard appropriate to the kind of being she is — it is not an impersonal standard but a person-kind-relative one.

My experience machine argument was designed to be a clean test of hedonism, and I believe it succeeds. The responses it has generated in the post-Parfit literature reveal more about the strength of hedonism’s grip on philosophical imagination than about any genuine weakness in the argument itself.

The core of the argument is simple: if welfare consisted entirely in pleasant experiences, then a life in the experience machine — which delivers maximal pleasant experiences — would be the best possible life. But we would not choose it. Therefore welfare does not consist entirely in pleasant experiences. The proposition has offered several responses; I want to show why none of them succeeds.

“We want to do certain things, and not just have the experience of doing them. We want to be a certain way, and not just have the experience of being that way. We want to touch reality, to live in contact with a deeper reality than is humanly constructed.” — Nozick, 1974
i. the argument stated and defended

Experience machine — clear refutation of hedonism; three reasons to refuse are all non-hedonistI offer three reasons why we would not plug in to the experience machine, each of which identifies a non-experiential component of what we care about. First: we want to actually do things, not merely have the experience of doing them. Second: we want to be a certain kind of person — courageous, honest, creative — and this is not merely a matter of having certain experiences. Third: we want to live in contact with a reality that is not of our own construction. Each of these reasons identifies something that matters to us beyond experience. Together they constitute a powerful prima facie case that welfare is not purely experiential. The proposition’s response — that these concerns are non-welfare values — is simply an ad hoc re-drawing of the boundary between welfare and other values to preserve hedonism at the cost of explanatory power.

ii. the “we care about more than welfare” response fails

Proposition’s response fails — re-drawing welfare/other-values boundary is ad hoc; pre-theoretical welfare includes real achievementParfit and Crisp respond that the refusal to plug in reflects caring about things beyond welfare, not a belief that welfare is non-experiential. But this response is ad hoc. It re-draws the boundary between welfare and other values precisely where needed to save hedonism, without independent motivation for the re-drawing. The pre-theoretical concept of welfare — of what is good for a person, what makes their life go well — includes caring about whether one actually achieves one’s goals, has real relationships, and makes genuine contributions to the world. These are not separate values adjacent to welfare; they are part of the ordinary understanding of welfare. The hedonist response excises them from welfare by definitional fiat.

iii. the desire theory is not the answer

Against desire theory — desire theory incomplete without constraint to ground-level desires; objective content still neededOne might think that desire theory, rather than hedonism, is the correct subjective theory. But desire theory faces its own version of the experience machine problem. Suppose a person in the experience machine has all their desires satisfied — including desires to have real friendships, make real achievements, and engage with reality. The machine can be modified to satisfy the desire for real friendship by actually providing real friendships as part of the simulation. But this modification reveals that what we care about is not merely that our desires be satisfied but that the objects of our desires be real. Desire theory cannot capture this without importing an objective constraint on which desires count for welfare. And once we import objective constraints, we are on the road to objective list theory.

iv. contact with reality as a welfare good

Contact with reality — real engagement constitutive of welfare; not merely instrumentally valuedThe third reason for refusing the machine — the desire to live in contact with reality — is the deepest. It is not merely that we value real achievement instrumentally, as a means to pleasant experience or desire-satisfaction. We value it constitutively: real engagement with the world is part of what makes a life go well, not merely part of what produces the experiences that make a life go well. This is the fundamental insight that hedonism and desire theory both miss. A complete theory of welfare must include real contact with reality — genuine achievement, genuine relationships, genuine knowledge — as constitutive components of welfare, not merely as causal antecedents of beneficial experiences.

My contribution in Perfectionism was to develop the most principled and systematic version of the objective list view available in the post-Parfit literature. I want to show why it is more defensible than the subjective alternatives and why the objections to it misfire.

The perfectionist thesis is this: what is good for a human being is the development and exercise of the properties that constitute human nature. Humans are rational and animal beings; the properties that are distinctively and essentially human are those capacities for reason, relationship, achievement, and understanding that differentiate us from other animals. Developing these properties to a high degree, and exercising them effectively, is what constitutes human flourishing. This is not an arbitrary list; it is a list derived from a defensible account of what humans essentially are.

The perfectionist derivation.

Premises: (1) What is good for a being is what perfects it — develops its essential properties to a high degree. (2) The essential properties of humans include rational capacities (theoretical and practical reason) and social-relational capacities (deep personal relationships, participation in human communities). (3) These are the properties that appear in the best characterisation of human nature.

Conclusion: What is good for a human being is the development and exercise of rational and social-relational capacities — not subjective experience or desire-satisfaction as such.
i. the non-arbitrariness of the perfectionist list

Non-arbitrary list — derived from human nature; not a codification of intuitions; principled derivationThe standard objection to objective list theories is that they are arbitrary: they simply codify the values of their proponents under the guise of a principled account. I deny that perfectionism is vulnerable to this objection. The perfectionist list is derived, not stipulated. The derivation proceeds through a theory of human nature: we identify the essential properties of humans by the best available account of what differentiates humans from other kinds of beings. The derivation is revisable in the light of better accounts of human nature. It is not merely a matter of what we find intuitively plausible; it is a matter of what follows from the best account of what humans are. This provides the kind of principled basis that Parfit demands and that purely intuition-based objective list theories lack.

ii. perfectionism avoids the alienation objection

Alienation — perfectionist goods are goods for beings of the relevant kind; person-kind-relativity is sufficientThe alienation objection holds that goods that are neither experienced as good nor desired cannot be good for a person. Perfectionism has a principled response: its goods are good for persons not because they are experienced or desired but because they are goods for beings of the kind that persons are. Just as being in good physical condition is good for a body regardless of whether the body experiences this as good, developing one’s rational and relational capacities is good for a person regardless of whether they experience it as such. The relevant sense of “for” is person-kind-relative: it is good for persons qua persons, qua beings of the relevant natural kind. This is a robust and principled sense of person-relativity that does not require experiential or conative grounding.

iii. against hedonism: the flourishing without pleasure case

Flourishing without pleasure — genuine perfectionist flourishing without hedonic wellbeing still constitutes high welfareConsider a person of great intellectual achievement and deep personal relationships who, through temperamental melancholy, derives relatively modest hedonic satisfaction from these activities. Hedonism says she has lower welfare than a person who drifts pleasantly through life without deep engagement. But this verdict is deeply implausible. The achieving, relating person flourishes in the sense that matters most: she develops and exercises her distinctively human capacities at a high level. The pleasant drifter does not. Welfare judgements that systematically favour the pleasant drifter over the melancholy achiever have misdescribed what welfare is. Perfectionism gives the intuitively correct verdict; hedonism gives the wrong one.

iv. against the desire theory: the flourishing of the adaptive preference holder

Adaptive preferences — desire theory cannot explain why adaptive preference holder fails to flourish; perfectionism canA person whose desires have been formed under conditions of systematic deprivation may have desires that are satisfied by her deprived circumstances: she has learned not to want what she cannot have. And what she would want if she knew what she was missing is precisely the perfectionist goods: rational engagement, genuine relationship, achievement. The desire theory’s idealisation therefore covertly relies on a perfectionist standard to generate the right verdicts. Perfectionism is the foundational theory; informed desire is a downstream approximation.

My position in this debate is different from all the others. I do not offer a rival theory of welfare. I argue that the concept of welfare itself — the concept of what is good for a person — is too problematic to support the theoretical edifice that both sides of this debate are trying to build on it.

Both the proposition and my fellow opposition speakers assume that there are genuine welfare facts: facts about what makes lives go well for the people who live them. I want to challenge this assumption. The post-Parfit literature has produced a proliferation of theories — hedonism, informed desire, objective list, perfectionism — each of which generates compelling counterexamples to the others. I want to argue that this theoretical impasse is not merely a sign of incomplete theorising but is evidence that the concept of welfare is itself defective.

“The concept of personal good or welfare — of what is good for a person — is not the foundational concept in practical philosophy that it is usually taken to be. It may be deeply confused, or at minimum much more problematic than the literature assumes.” — Kahane, 2011
i. the conceptual problem: what does “for” mean?

Conceptual problem — “good for” is irreducibly ambiguous; no single concept underlies all welfare talkThe expression “good for a person” is used in multiple ways that do not converge on a single concept. Sometimes it means: what the person experiences as good. Sometimes it means: what satisfies the person’s (informed) desires. Sometimes it means: what enables the person to function well as a human being. These are not merely different theories of the same concept; they track genuinely different phenomena. The philosophical literature treats welfare as a single concept that admits of a single correct analysis. But the evidence from the post-Parfit debate is that there is no single concept to be analysed: welfare talk is irreducibly plural, and attempts to unify it generate the endless cycle of counterexamples that characterises the field.

ii. the persistent counterexample problem as evidence of conceptual defect

Counterexample proliferation — persistent pattern across theories; best explained by conceptual defect, not incomplete theorisingEvery major theory of welfare has counterexamples that are compelling and that resist resolution within the theory’s framework. Hedonism: the experience machine, adaptive pleasures, malicious satisfactions. Desire theory: grass-counting, stranger-desires, posthumous satisfactions. Objective list: alien goods, paternalism, list arbitrariness. This pattern has persisted for decades despite increasingly sophisticated theorising. The natural explanation is theoretical incompleteness: we have not found the right theory yet. But an alternative explanation has more explanatory power: the concept of welfare is itself defective, generating a persistent pattern of failure that no theory within the concept can resolve.

iii. the normativity problem: why should welfare matter?

Normativity problem — welfare must be normatively significant; but its normative significance is disputed; concept presupposes contested metaethicsThe concept of welfare is supposed to be normatively significant: facts about welfare are supposed to provide reasons for action, both for the welfare-bearer and for others. But the normative significance of welfare is disputed at every level. Why should I care about my own welfare in the relevant sense? Why should others care about it? The hedonist says: because pleasant experience is intrinsically valuable. But why is that? The desire theorist says: because you are getting what you want. But why does that matter normatively? Each answer to the normativity question presupposes a contested metaethical position. The concept of welfare, far from being foundational in practical philosophy, depends on answers to metaethical questions that are at least as contested as the first-order welfare questions it was supposed to ground.

iv. prudential nihilism: the least bad option

Prudential nihilism — rejects welfare concept; moral deliberation proceeds without it; not practically eliminativistPrudential nihilism holds that there are no genuine welfare facts because the concept of welfare is defective. This does not mean that nothing matters or that we cannot make sound practical judgements. It means that welfare, as a theoretical concept, should be eliminated from our best moral theory and replaced by more tractable concepts: experiential states, preference-satisfactions, capabilities, functioning — taken separately rather than unified under a single welfare concept. Practical deliberation can proceed without the welfare concept: we can ask directly what experiential states people are in, what they prefer, what they can do and be, without asking the unanswerable question of what is “good for” them. Prudential nihilism is not practically eliminativist; it is theoretically eliminativist.

Argument I — The concept of “good for” is irreducibly ambiguous; welfare talk tracks genuinely different phenomena
1.“Good for a person” is used to mean: what the person experiences as good; what satisfies informed desires; what enables good functioning as a human being. These are genuinely different phenomena, not merely different analyses of one concept.
2.The philosophical literature treats welfare as a single concept. But the evidence from the post-Parfit debate suggests it is not: attempts to unify welfare talk generate the persistent cycle of counterexamples that characterises the field.
3.If welfare talk is irreducibly plural, then the search for a single correct theory of welfare is misguided from the start.
∴The concept of welfare is irreducibly ambiguous: it tracks genuinely different phenomena that resist unification under a single analysis; the search for a correct theory of welfare is misguided because there is no single concept to theorise about.
Argument II — Persistent counterexample proliferation is better explained by conceptual defect than by incomplete theorising
1.Every major welfare theory has counterexamples that resist resolution within the theory’s framework: hedonism, desire theory, and objective list theories each face compelling and persistent objections.
2.Two explanations: (a) theoretical incompleteness — we have not found the right theory; (b) conceptual defect — the concept of welfare itself generates failure that no theory within the concept can resolve.
3.The persistence and diversity of the failures across decades of sophisticated theorising is better explained by (b): it is unlikely that so many distinct theories have all failed through mere incompleteness.
∴The persistent counterexample proliferation is best explained by conceptual defect in the welfare concept itself, not by mere theoretical incompleteness; this supports prudential nihilism over continued theorising within the welfare framework.
Argument III — The normativity of welfare presupposes contested metaethical positions; welfare is not foundational
1.Welfare is supposed to be normatively significant: welfare facts are supposed to provide reasons for action. But why? Each account of why welfare matters presupposes a contested metaethical position.
2.The concept of welfare, far from being foundational, depends on answers to metaethical questions at least as contested as the first-order welfare questions it was supposed to ground.
3.A concept that depends on unresolved foundational questions for its normative significance cannot play the foundational role in practical philosophy that it is usually assigned.
∴The normativity of welfare is not foundational but dependent on contested metaethical positions; welfare cannot serve as the theoretical bedrock of practical philosophy that both the proposition and the other opposition speakers assume.
Argument IV — Prudential nihilism: eliminate the welfare concept and replace with more tractable first-order concepts
1.If the welfare concept is defective, we should eliminate it from moral theory and replace it with more tractable concepts: experiential states, preferences, capabilities, functioning — taken separately.
2.Practical deliberation can proceed by asking directly what experiential states people are in, what they prefer, and what they can do — without asking the unanswerable question of what is “good for” them.
3.Prudential nihilism is theoretically eliminativist, not practically eliminativist: it eliminates a theoretical concept, not a domain of practical concern.
∴Prudential nihilism is the least bad option: it eliminates the defective welfare concept while preserving practical deliberation by replacing welfare with more tractable first-order concepts that do not presuppose a unified concept of prudential benefit.

Mr Speaker,

My argument is that both sides of this debate — the hedonists and desire theorists on the proposition side, and the objective list theorists among the opposition — have been theorising at the wrong level of abstraction.

Both camps focus on what constitutes welfare — on whether it is pleasant experience, desire-satisfaction, or objective achievement. But this debate is conducted in abstraction from the actual conditions that enable or prevent human flourishing. What we need, for purposes of moral and political assessment, is not a theory of what welfare is but an account of what enables it: of the real freedoms and capabilities that allow people to live lives they have reason to value. This is the capabilities approach, and I believe it is more adequate than any welfare-constitution theory for the practical purposes that matter most.

The capabilities approach.

Capabilities are substantive freedoms to achieve what one has reason to value — to be well-nourished, to live in good health, to participate in the life of one’s community, to exercise practical reason, to experience and produce art. These are not welfare states but enabling conditions: they are what allows a person to achieve the functionings that constitute a good life.

The appropriate currency for welfare assessment is capabilities, not utility (pleasant experience) or resources (means to desire-satisfaction). Utility is blind to the conditions that enable it; resources are blind to how people convert them into functionings.
i. the problem of adaptive preferences

Adaptive preferences — hedonism and desire theory both fail; capabilities approach correct; utility blind to deprivationThe adaptive preference problem is the most powerful objection to both hedonism and desire theory. A person who has been raised in conditions of severe deprivation may experience genuine satisfaction — real pleasure, real desire-satisfaction — in circumstances that should not satisfy her. Her pleasures and satisfied desires are real, but they are formed against a background of suppressed possibilities that she cannot see. Hedonism and desire theory both ratify her welfare as adequate, or even high. But this is wrong. The capabilities approach correctly identifies what is missing: she lacks the capabilities that would enable her to convert her circumstances into genuine functionings. Utility is systematically blind to the conditions that produce it; the capabilities approach looks beneath utility to the real enabling conditions.

ii. the informational poverty of utility metrics

Utility informationally poor — fails to distinguish genuine from deformed flourishing; capabilities are informationally richerThe hedonic and desire-satisfaction metrics that the proposition defends are informationally impoverished: they tell us only what a person experiences or desires, not what they are able to do and be. Two people with the same utility level may be in radically different situations with respect to their real freedoms: one may be unable to move freely, to participate in political life, or to pursue the relationships she values, while the other has all these freedoms. The utility metric treats them as equally well-off; the capabilities approach correctly distinguishes them. This is not merely a theoretical point; it has direct implications for development policy, healthcare allocation, and the assessment of social justice.

iii. against the objective list: the diversity of human flourishing

Against OLT — single list too rigid; capabilities approach respects human diversity; freedom to choose among functioningsI am also critical of the objective list theorists, including Hurka and Arneson. Their perfectionist lists are too rigid: they specify which functionings constitute welfare, leaving no room for the diversity of human ways of flourishing. The capabilities approach is more flexible: it identifies which capabilities people must have — which substantive freedoms — without specifying which functionings they must achieve. A person with the capability for intellectual engagement may choose not to exercise it; this is her freedom. The objective list denies this freedom by specifying that intellectual achievement is constitutive of welfare regardless of the person’s choices. The capabilities approach respects human diversity and individual freedom in a way that objective list theories cannot.

iv. capabilities as the appropriate currency for assessment

Capabilities currency — avoids fetishism of utility and resources; directly assesses what people can do and beThe fundamental argument for the capabilities approach is that it identifies the appropriate currency for welfare assessment: what people are actually able to do and be, in the full range of domains that matter for a good life. Utility fails because it ratifies deformed and adaptive responses. Resources fail because they are blind to conversion factors: differences in disability, age, health, and social position mean that the same resources enable very different real freedoms. The capabilities approach directly assesses the real freedoms people have, avoiding both the utility fetishism of the hedonist tradition and the resource fetishism of rawlsian liberalism. It is not a theory of what welfare is constituted by; it is a theory of what the appropriate metric for welfare assessment is. And this is the question that matters for practical and political purposes.
