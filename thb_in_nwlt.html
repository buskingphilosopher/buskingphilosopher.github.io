Let me begin with a theorem. A theorem is a claim that follows necessarily from its assumptions. If the assumptions are denied, the theorem does not follow. I want to explain why every one of Arrhenius’s impossibility theorems depends on an assumption that New Wave Lexical Totalism legitimately denies — and that denying it is not a desperate escape move but a principled claim about the structure of welfare.

The NWLT framework, formally stated
Welfare level: w = (h, l) ∈ ℤ × ℤ
h = quantity of higher goods (autonomy, meaning, intellectual and aesthetic depth)
l = quantity of lower goods (sensory pleasure, comfort, hedonic experience)
Ordering on lives: (hx, lx) ≥ (hy, ly) ⇔ hx > hy, or [hx = hy and lx ≥ ly]
Population value: V(X) = (Σ hi, Σ li) for all lives i ∈ X
Population ordering: V(X) ≥ V(Y) ⇔ ΣhX > ΣhY, or [ΣhX = ΣhY and ΣlX ≥ ΣlY]
Key property: no finite accumulation of l-goods ever bridges the gap between lives that differ in h-goods. The h-dimension is categorically prior. This is the denial of Finite Fine-Grainedness.
i. what finite fine-grainedness says and why nwlt denies it

FFG — assumes finite chain between any two welfare levels; NWLT denies this for cross-tier comparisons; denial is principled not evasiveFinite Fine-Grainedness (FFG) is the assumption that for any two welfare levels w₁ and w₂, there exists a finite sequence w₁ = v₀, v₁, ..., vₙ = w₂ such that each consecutive pair (vᵢ, vᵢ₊₁) differs only slightly. Arrhenius formalises “slightly” through a standard of smallness; the key intuitive idea is that you can get from any welfare level to any other by a finite number of marginal steps, each plausibly small. NWLT denies this for pairs of welfare levels that differ in h-goods. The welfare level (1, 0) — one unit of higher good, no lower goods — is not reachable from (0, 10⁶) — a million units of lower good — by any finite chain of slight increments, because every step that increases l by one while leaving h at 0 does not approach (1, 0) in the lexical ordering. The “gap” between tiers is categorical, not gradual.

Escape is genuine — theorem fails without FFG; NWLT satisfies all other conditions; this is not hand-wavingLet me show concretely why the Sixth Theorem fails without FFG. The theorem’s proof requires a “small steps” argument: it constructs a sequence of populations each slightly better than the previous, connecting a high-quality population A to the repugnant population Z by a finite chain. This chain depends on FFG: each link in the chain requires that you can always find a welfare level slightly below the current one that is still strictly positive. Under NWLT, this chain cannot be constructed, because there is no welfare level “slightly below” (1, 0) that has any positive h-component — the nearest level with h = 0 is categorically worse, not slightly worse. The chain snaps. The proof does not go through.

iii. the haydn and oyster case: NWLT’s motivation
The Haydn and Oyster case (Parfit, 1984).

Suppose Haydn can choose between two lives. In Life A, he is Haydn: he composes, experiences deep intellectual and aesthetic joy, forms profound personal relationships — a life of genuine flourishing. In Life B, he is transformed into an Oyster that lives a hundred years of simple, pleasant, unthinking sensory experience. Which life is better?

Most people judge: Life A is better, and no quantity of additional oyster-pleasure makes Life B as good. This is the lexical intuition in its purest form. NWLT formalises it: Life A has h > 0; Life B has h = 0. No accumulation of l-goods in Life B brings it to the value of Life A. The intuition that motivates NWLT is not a technical convenience; it is the core Parfitian intuition about what makes lives go well.

Haydn case motivates NWLT — lexical intuition is strong and independent; NWLT formalises it without ad hoc additionsThe Repugnant Conclusion says that a world of billions of Oyster-lives, each barely worth living, is better than a world of ten billion Haydn-lives. This conclusion is repugnant precisely because it denies the lexical intuition the Haydn and Oyster case reveals. NWLT blocks it: a world of Haydn-lives has a positive h-sum; a world of Oyster-lives has h-sum = 0. No number of Oyster-lives makes h-sum positive, so no number of Oyster-lives makes the Oyster world better. The escape from the Repugnant Conclusion is automatic, principled, and derivable from the same intuitive source as the Haydn and Oyster judgement. This is not a mathematical trick. It is the formalisation of what the repugnance was always about.

Add one nanosecond of genuine philosophical reflection to an otherwise purely hedonic life: does h suddenly become positive? This looks absurd. But the right response is not to abandon lexical structure; it is to embrace vagueness. There is genuine vagueness about which lives have positive h-goods. A supervaluationist treatment makes the correct claim: in all admissible sharpenings of “has positive h-goods,” excellent lives are in the positive tier and lives barely worth living are not. The borderline is vague but the classification of the extreme cases that drive the Repugnant Conclusion is sharp. That is all NWLT requires.

Thomas has given the formal demonstration. I want to give the substantive ethical argument: the vector structure of welfare is not a mathematical convention but a reflection of how value actually works. Higher goods are not merely quantitatively greater than lower goods. They are categorically superior — superior in kind, not merely in degree.

Consider what we mean when we say that a life of genuine intellectual and moral engagement is better than a life of intense but vacant pleasure. The claim is not that the engagement life scores higher on some common currency of welfare-units. It is that the engagement life has something the pleasure life lacks entirely — a mode of being in the world that constitutes a qualitatively different kind of flourishing. The vector representation captures this: h-goods are not large-l-goods. They are goods of a different mathematical dimension.

Why categorical superiority grounds the lexical ordering.

J.S. Mill argued that “it is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied.” His point was not that Socrates’ pleasures score higher on some hedonist scale. His point was that the pleasures available to Socrates — the pleasures of philosophical understanding, moral seriousness, authentic friendship — are pleasures of a categorically different kind from the fool’s. A competent judge, knowing both, prefers the higher.

NWLT formalises Mill’s claim. The h-dimension represents the class of goods that are categorically preferred — preferred in kind, not merely in quantity — over goods of the l-dimension. The lexical ordering represents the categorical preference: some quantity of h-goods makes a life better than any quantity of l-goods alone.

Two-tier motivation — categorical preference for higher goods; Mill, Parfit, epistemic support; h-goods not just large l-goodsThe case for a two-tier welfare structure rests on a claim about what makes lives go well that is older than the paradoxes of population ethics. When Parfit introduced the Haydn and Oyster case, he was drawing on a tradition — running from Aristotle through Mill to G.E. Moore — that identifies qualitative differences among goods that resist reduction to a common scale. Moore’s ideal values — knowledge, aesthetic experience, personal affection — are not just large quantities of pleasure. They are goods that a competent judge would prefer regardless of hedonic quantity. The two-tier vector structure is the formal representation of this philosophical tradition, not a technical innovation imposed on population ethics from outside it.

ii. lexical threshold totalism: handling borderline cases with incommensurability

The key modification is that lives near the h-threshold are not strictly ranked but are on a par: incommensurable with each other in a way that resists precise comparison. This handles the spectrum cases that motivate sharp-cut-off objections. Rather than claiming that a life with 0.001 units of h-good is categorically superior to a life with 0 h-goods, LTT allows that lives near the threshold are genuinely comparable only with difficulty, and that precise verdicts in these cases may not be available. The Repugnant Conclusion is still avoided: lives clearly above the threshold — which includes all the lives we would naturally describe as genuinely flourishing — are still categorically superior to lives clearly below it.

In my 2000 Noûs paper, “Parfit’s Puzzle,” I argued that the Repugnant Conclusion reveals not a problem for population ethics as a whole but a specific problem with theories that treat welfare as a single-dimensional quantity. The puzzle Parfit identified is the puzzle of commensurability across qualitatively different kinds of lives. My claim was then, and remains now, that the puzzle dissolves once we recognise that qualitatively different kinds of lives are not commensurable on a single welfare scale.

The non-compensation principle is the core of the NWLT programme: no accumulation of lives in the lower tier can compensate for the elimination of a population of lives in the higher tier. This is not an arbitrary axiom. It is what we already believe when we judge that a world of flourishing human lives is not made better by replacing it, however many times over, with a world of creatures who experience only uncomplicated hedonic pleasure.

i. parfit’s puzzle and the non-compensation principle

Non-compensation — no l-lives compensate for loss of h-lives; this is what RC repugnance tracks; NCP is the core of NWLTParfit named the Repugnant Conclusion “repugnant” because he found it genuinely, deeply wrong. He did not merely find it counterintuitive in the way that some surprising theorem might be counterintuitive. He found it morally monstrous: the idea that a world of persons living lives barely worth living could be better than a world of ten billion lives of profound flourishing. What makes it monstrous? The non-compensation principle is what makes it monstrous. When we judge that no number of Z-lives compensates for the A-lives, we are not making a quantitative error about how many units of welfare Z-lives contain. We are expressing a qualitative judgment about what kinds of lives matter categorically. The Non-Compensation Principle captures this judgment precisely and NWLT gives it formal expression.

ii. the classical sources: aristotle, mill, and moore on qualitative value plurality

Classical motivation — Aristotle on eudaimonia, Mill on higher pleasures, Moore on ideal values; all support qualitative plurality that NWLT formalisesThe Non-Compensation Principle is not a philosophical innovation. It is the formal expression of a conviction that runs through the entire history of moral philosophy. Aristotle distinguished eudaimonia — the flourishing of the rational soul — from mere pleasure, and insisted that eudaimonia could not be reduced to or compensated by any quantity of pleasure. Mill distinguished higher and lower pleasures, insisting that a competent judge would prefer a smaller quantity of higher pleasure to any quantity of lower pleasure. Moore distinguished ideal values from mere hedonic goods, grounding the distinction in the self-evident value of knowledge, aesthetic experience, and personal affection. Each of these thinkers was asserting something structurally identical to what NWLT asserts: that some goods are not commensurable with others on a single scale, and that a threshold quantum of the higher good defeats any finite quantity of the lower.

iii. how the non-compensation principle appears in ordinary moral judgment

Ordinary judgment — we actually apply NCP when comparing lives; not a theoretical stipulation but a formalisation of common moral senseThe Non-Compensation Principle is not a philosopher’s stipulation. It appears constantly in ordinary moral judgment about lives. When we advise a young person not to sell their intellectual development for material comfort, we are applying it. When we judge that a life of creative engagement is more admirable than a life of vacant pleasure even if the latter contains more subjective satisfaction, we are applying it. When we express discomfort with the idea of wireheading — direct brain stimulation to produce permanent maximal hedonic pleasure without any engagement with the world — even if the subject wants it, we are applying it. The Non-Compensation Principle is not a theoretical construct imposed on ordinary moral sense; it is the formalisation of ordinary moral sense about the categorical importance of certain kinds of value.

iv. against huemer: the RC is not simply true because the intuition is not simply wrong

Against Huemer — RC follows from plausible principles via small steps; NCP blocks each step by denying substitutability across tiers; not just an intuition but a principled judgmentHuemer argues that the Repugnant Conclusion is simply true, and that the intuition opposing it is a distorting cognitive bias. He offers the Benign Addition Argument: start with world A, add happy lives to get A+, then equalise welfare to get B, and repeat — eventually reaching Z. Each step seems unobjectionable, so Z is better than A. NWLT breaks the chain. Specifically, it breaks at the equalisation step: when we equalise by reducing some lives from the higher tier and raising others in the lower tier, we are trading h-goods for l-goods, and no such trade preserves value. The Non-Compensation Principle says that equalisation across tiers is not a neutral operation; it destroys value. The chain argument fails because it treats welfare as one-dimensional when NWLT shows it is not.

The puzzle is solved by recognising what value is. I yield the floor.

I want to address the concern that NWLT abandons total utilitarianism — that it replaces the simple, compelling idea of maximising total welfare with a complex, two-dimensional structure that no longer honours the core totalitarian intuition. This concern is mistaken. NWLT is not an abandonment of total utilitarianism; it is an enrichment of it.

The core totalitarian intuition is this: one population is at least as good as another if and only if it contains at least as much total welfare. NWLT preserves this intuition entirely — it just models welfare as a pair of real numbers rather than a single real number, and orders those pairs lexically rather than by scalar addition. The conclusion that NWLT is a departure from totalism rests on a confused identification of totalism with scalar totalism, when the real content of totalism is the formula “more total welfare is better,” which NWLT straightforwardly preserves.

NWLT as vector totalism: the mathematical structure
Scalar totalism: V(X) = Σi wi ∈ ℝ
One population is better than another iff its scalar welfare sum is higher.
Vector totalism (NWLT): V(X) = (Σi hi, Σi li) ∈ ℤ × ℤ
One population is better iff its vector welfare sum is higher in the lexical ordering.
Both are versions of the same formula: “more total welfare is better.” Vector totalism just models welfare as a two-dimensional quantity. The totalitarian intuition is preserved; only the geometry of welfare is enriched.
i. how nwlt preserves separability and additive structure

Separability preserved — NWLT satisfies Separability of lives; adding a life increases the population value by exactly the welfare of that life; additivity maintained in each dimensionOne of the most attractive features of scalar totalism is its additive structure: the value of a population is the sum of its members’ welfare, and adding one person contributes exactly that person’s welfare to the total. NWLT preserves this structure in each dimension. Adding a life with welfare (h, l) to a population increases the population’s h-sum by h and l-sum by l. The population value is the vector sum of all lives’ welfare vectors. This means NWLT satisfies Separability of lives — the principle that the contribution of any individual to population value depends only on their own welfare, not on the welfare of others. Separability is one of the most attractive features of totalism, and NWLT does not abandon it.

ii. nwlt and the strong totalist intuition

Strong totalism — NWLT satisfies the strong intuition that more total welfare is better in each dimension; dominance in both dimensions is unambiguousThe strong totalitarian intuition is that, other things equal, adding welfare to the world makes it better. I want to present the argument I gave at the PPE Seminar with its full force. The proposition has told us that the existing objections to NWLT can be defused by appeal to vagueness: the spectrum cases that seem to require sharp cuts between the h-tier and the l-tier are handled by acknowledging that the boundary is vague, applying supervaluationism, and observing that the extreme cases — the Haydn life and the oyster life — are determinately classified. I accept that this response is adequate for spectrum cases. My objection does not rely on spectrum cases.

I want to show that NWLT is committed to a verdict in a case that has nothing to do with borderline lives at the tier threshold — and that the verdict is clearly, determinately wrong. The vagueness response cannot touch it.

The Overwhelming Bad case (Cowie’s non-spectrum objection).

Population OB: one person with welfare level (1, 0) — the barest minimum of higher goods, no lower goods — plus ten billion people each with welfare level (0, −1,000,000): vast lower-good suffering and no higher goods at all.

Population AG: ten billion people each with welfare level (0, 1,000): substantial lower-good wellbeing, no higher goods.

NWLT verdict: OB is better than AG. The h-sum of OB is 1; the h-sum of AG is 0. Since h-sum determines the primary ranking, OB > AG — one life with minimal higher goods outweighs ten billion lives of positive wellbeing, even when the OB world also contains ten billion lives of extreme suffering.

Note: this case does not depend on any borderline classification. The person with (1,0) has determinately positive h-goods. The AG people have determinately zero h-goods. No vagueness can rescue this verdict.
i. why the overwhelming bad case is not a spectrum case

Not spectrum — no borderline lives; person with (1,0) is determinately in h-tier; AG people are determinately not; verdict is determinate and clearly wrongThe proposition’s response to spectrum objections is as follows: any case that requires a precise cut-off between h-good-possessing lives and h-good-lacking lives is a case where vagueness makes the cut-off acceptable. Concede the cut-off’s imprecision and apply supervaluationism. But the Overwhelming Bad case does not require any cut-off determination. The person with welfare (1, 0) in OB has a positive h-good — one unit of genuine higher-tier wellbeing. This is determinately true. The AG people each have welfare (0, 1,000) — substantial lower goods but zero higher goods. This is determinately true. The NWLT verdict that OB is better than AG is therefore not generated by borderline classification; it is a determinate verdict from the logic of NWLT applied to two populations with unambiguous tier positions.

ii. what makes the verdict clearly wrong

It is monstrous in a way that is, I submit, considerably worse than the original Repugnant Conclusion that NWLT was designed to avoid. The Repugnant Conclusion says that a world of vast numbers of barely-worth-living lives is better than a world of excellent lives. That is counterintuitive, but it has a certain coherence: the positive welfare of the barely-worth-living lives accumulates. The OB verdict says that a world containing one person with minimal higher goods plus ten billion people in extreme agony is better than a world containing ten billion people living well. This gives absolute priority to one unit of higher goods over any amount of lower-tier suffering. It is not merely counterintuitive; it is a verdict that virtually any reflective person would find obviously, decisively wrong.

In the OB case, this response is unavailable. On every admissible sharpening of the h-predicate, (1, 0) falls in the positive h-tier — it has at least one unit of higher goods, which is clearly enough to qualify on any reasonable precisification. On every admissible sharpening, (0, 1,000) falls outside the positive h-tier. The verdict OB > AG is therefore determinate under supervaluationism. The vagueness machinery does not generate indeterminacy here; it generates a determinate wrong verdict.

My objection is that NWLT produces no verdict at all in a wide range of cases — and that this incompleteness is not a principled feature but a catastrophic failure of the theory as a guide to action and policy.

The proposition acknowledges that NWLT generates incommensurability in some cases. They present this as a feature: appropriate indeterminacy in response to genuine philosophical uncertainty. I want to show that the incommensurability zones are not confined to the philosophically uncertain borderline cases, but infect all comparisons between populations where different lives involve different h-l compositions, and that this makes NWLT practically useless for the comparative judgments population ethics is supposed to guide.

By a duplication argument (taking n copies of each population and grouping them), WLT can be shown to produce contradictions with plausible aggregation principles. Cowie develops this argument in detail. The point is that the easy modification — adding a threshold δ — does not solve the OB problem; it generates a new problem in the same formal neighbourhood.

The NWLT verdict in virtually every real-world comparison will be determined entirely by which option produces more h-goods, regardless of how much l-good is produced or destroyed in the process. This is not a principled guide to policy; it is a capitulation to lexical absolutism that ignores the overwhelming majority of what makes lives go well or badly. A theory that tells us to ignore all l-welfare considerations whenever h-welfare can be marginally increased by any means is not a theory that can guide real decisions.

I want to raise a difficulty for NWLT that goes beyond the specific paradoxes and impossibility results that Cowie, Baker, and Arrhenius have developed. My concern is foundational: NWLT is not merely an axiology with some counterintuitive implications. It is an axiology that, by abandoning the Archimedean axiom, makes rational decision-making under uncertainty impossible. And population ethics that cannot guide rational choice under uncertainty cannot guide us at all, since all population-affecting decisions are made under uncertainty.

The Archimedean axiom is a condition on rational preference under risk. It says: for any outcomes A > B > C, there is some probability p such that a prospect that gives A with probability p and C with probability 1-p is exactly as good as a certain B. This axiom is a necessary condition for expected utility theory — without it, no utility representation of preferences over lotteries exists. NWLT violates it.

NWLT violates the Archimedean axiom: formal demonstration
Let A = population of Haydn-lives: V(A) = (10, 0)
Let B = population of mixed lives: V(B) = (5, 5000)
Let C = population of oyster-lives: V(C) = (0, 1,000,000)
NWLT ordering: A > B > C (h-sums: 10 > 5 > 0)
Archimedean axiom requires: ∃ p ∈ (0,1) such that p·A + (1−p)·C ~ B
NWLT verdict: For any p < 1, the prospect p·A + (1-p)·C has expected h-sum = 10p and expected l-sum = 1,000,000(1-p). B has h-sum = 5, l-sum = 5000. Under NWLT: the prospect is better than B whenever 10p > 5, i.e. p > 0.5. It is worse than B when p < 0.5. No value of p makes them exactly equal (tied h-sum requires exactly p=0.5, but then expected l-sum = 500,000 >> 5,000, so p·A + (1-p)·C > B). The indifference point does not exist. The Archimedean axiom fails.
i. why the archimedean axiom matters for decision theory

Archimedean axiom essential — without it, no utility representation exists; no expected value calculation; no rational decision framework under riskThe Archimedean axiom is one of the four axioms of expected utility theory (along with completeness, transitivity, and independence). Without it, the expected utility representation theorem fails: there is no real-valued utility function whose expectation represents the preference ordering over lotteries. This means that under NWLT, there is no coherent way to compute the expected value of a population prospect, because the lexical ordering of population values cannot be represented by a real-valued utility function over which expectations can be taken. NWLT-users therefore cannot rationally choose between uncertain population-affecting policies by calculating expected value. They lack the basic apparatus of rational decision-making under risk.
