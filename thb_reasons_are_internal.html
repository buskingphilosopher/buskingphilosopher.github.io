Mr President,

The debate between internalists and externalists about practical reasons is, at bottom, a debate about the nature of normativity itself — about what it means for something to count as a reason for action at all. The externalist picture is that there are normative facts out there in the world, independent of us, that bind us whether or not we are in any position to be motivated by them. I want to argue that this picture is incoherent, and that the internalist alternative — properly understood, as I have sought to develop it in the Kantian tradition — is the only account that makes sense of normativity as a practical phenomenon.

Internalism: A has a practical reason to φ only if there is a sound deliberative route from A's subjective motivational set S(A) to φ-ing (Williams); or: reasons must be capable of motivating the agent from the inside
Externalism: there are practical reasons that apply to an agent regardless of whether her motivational set contains anything that could motivate her to act on them
Korsgaard's version: Kantian constitutivism — normativity is grounded in what is constitutive of practical agency itself; the moral law is not an external constraint but the form of a unified will On the sceptic about practical reason

Constitutivism: normativity grounded in reflective agencyIn my 1986 paper "Skepticism about Practical Reason," I distinguished two forms of scepticism: content scepticism, which questions whether rational considerations bear on deliberation, and motivational scepticism, which questions whether reason can motivate action. My central claim was that motivational scepticism — the worry that normativity cannot move us — has no force independent of content scepticism. And my response to content scepticism is constitutivism: the normativity of practical principles derives from the fact that they are constitutive of practical agency. When you deliberate, you must deliberate under the presupposition that some considerations count as reasons and others do not. The form of that presupposition — the structural commitment to act on principle that is itself the form of willing — is what the moral law articulates. Normativity is not an external imposition. It is what it is to have a will at all.

On constitutivism and self-constitution

Self-constitution: the agent constitutes herself through her choicesIn Self-Constitution I developed this Kantian picture further. A person is not simply given to herself as a unified agent; she constitutes herself as such through her choices and actions. The principles she acts on are the principles by which she makes herself into a unified self. The categorical imperative is not an external law imposed on the agent from without: it is the form that a unified will must take. An agent who acts from the categorical imperative is not obeying an external constraint; she is expressing what it is to be a self-governing rational agent. This is why the normativity of moral reasons is internal to agency: it is grounded in what you must be if you are to be a unified practical agent at all, not in any facts about the external world that you might happen to lack the motivation to care about.

On Williams and the subjective motivational set

Against the Humean restriction: rational deliberation can extend the motivational setBernard Williams, who initiated this debate, held that an agent has a reason to φ only if she has something in her subjective motivational set S that a sound deliberative route would lead to φ-ing. I accept the internalist structure of this view but not its Humean restriction. Williams tied the motivational set too closely to brute desires as its fundamental elements. The Kantian insight is that rational deliberation is not merely a process of working out how to satisfy pre-given desires. It involves evaluating and endorsing those desires from a reflective standpoint — a standpoint that is itself practical reason. The norms of that reflective standpoint are constitutive of agency, not contingent additions to a desire-set. So reasons can be internal without being grounded in contingent desires.

On the amoralist and the externalist challenge

The amoralist: internalism explains why morality is genuinely authoritativeThe externalist's favourite challenge is the amoralist: a person who simply does not care about morality. The externalist says the amoralist has moral reasons that she is not motivated by — and that this shows reasons can be external to motivation. I say the constitutive account handles the amoralist better. The amoralist is not a fully rational agent who simply happens to lack moral motivation. She is someone who has failed to constitute herself as a unified practical agent, who acts on merely contingent impulses without subjecting them to the self-governance that practical agency requires. Her failure is not merely psychological but practical-rational. The externalist picture, by contrast, simply asserts that the amoralist has reasons she is indifferent to — which tells us nothing about where the authority of those reasons comes from or why she should care.

On the normativity challenge to externalism

Against externalism: normative facts without motivational grip are normatively idleThe deepest problem with externalism is this: if practical reasons are facts about the world that are entirely independent of the agent's motivational constitution, what makes them reasons at all? The externalist owes an account of why certain mind-independent facts generate requirements on action — why they are normative in the practical sense, rather than merely evaluative facts about how things are. If these facts can obtain even when the agent has absolutely no capacity to be moved by them, in what sense are they reasons for her? A reason, in the practical sense, must be something that can function in practical deliberation — that can move an agent from deliberation to action. Normative facts that are in principle disconnected from all motivational capacity cannot do this work. They are normatively idle.

Internalism is not the view that anything goes because reasons are whatever we happen to want. It is the view that the source of normative authority lies in the structure of rational agency itself, not in a domain of mind-independent facts that we are mysteriously required to care about.

Mr President,

I want to make a different case for internalism from Professor Korsgaard's — one that does not depend on constitutivism and that engages more directly with the externalist challenge on its own terms. My argument is that internalism is the correct account of what reasons are, and that it can be defended without assuming the Humean theory of motivation, and without conceding the externalist's charge that internalism leads to moral relativism.

On what internalism actually claims

Internalism: procedural account of what reasons areInternalism, as I defend it, is not the view that an agent has a reason to φ just in case she desires to φ. It is the view that what an agent has reason to do depends fundamentally on what she is antecedently motivated by, subject to corrections for misinformation and procedural irrationality. This is a procedural account of reasons: reasons are considerations that a procedurally rational deliberator — one who reasons without false beliefs, who has systematically coherent motivations, who has full information about her situation — would be motivated to act on. The key move is that the corrections for rationality are procedural, not substantive: they concern the form of deliberation, not its content.

On the motivating intuition for internalism

Motivating intuition: reasons must be capable of motivating the agent whose reasons they areThe core intuition behind internalism is this: practical reasons must be capable of being motivating for the agent whose reasons they are, when she is reasoning well. If a consideration is supposed to be a reason for me to act, but there is no possible deliberative route — even under ideal conditions — by which I could come to be motivated by it, in what sense is it a reason for me? It is a reason for some other agent, perhaps, or a moral fact about what would be good. But a practical reason must have purchase on my deliberation. This is not a claim about what actually motivates me now, with all my current irrationalities. It is a claim about what could motivate me under conditions of full procedural rationality. That modest claim is what internalism asserts.

On the analogy to epistemic reasons

Analogy to epistemic reasons: internalism has independent motivation beyond HumeanismI want to defend internalism without relying on the Humean theory of motivation. Consider epistemic reasons — reasons for belief. There is a plausible case that epistemic reasons are procedural: a consideration is a reason for me to believe p just in case a procedurally rational enquirer in my situation would be moved to believe p by it. We do not think epistemic reasons are whatever brute desire happens to make us believe things. We think they are what rationality recommends from the inside. Practical reasons are structurally parallel: the correct account is that they are what procedural practical rationality recommends, not what brute desire happens to motivate. This gives internalism an independent motivation entirely separate from the Humean theory of motivation that Williams relied upon.

On Kantian internalism and universal moral reasons

Kantian internalism: Kant's formula of humanity grounds universal moral reasons internallyThe deepest worry about internalism is that it leads to moral relativism: if reasons depend on what agents are motivated by, and agents are motivated by different things, then different agents will have different moral reasons, and perhaps some will have no moral reasons at all. In Moral Reason I argued that this worry can be met within the internalist framework by drawing on Kant's argument for the formula of humanity. Kant shows that any rational agent, whatever her contingent desires, is committed to treating humanity as an end in itself — because the unconditioned value of humanity is the presupposition without which the agent's own conditional values make no sense. This is a Kantian argument that generates universal moral reasons from within the internalist framework, without appeal to external normative facts.

On the externalist charge that internalism cannot explain moral authority

Against externalism: moral authority explained without external factsThe externalist — Enoch in particular — will say that internalism cannot explain why moral reasons have authority over agents who happen not to be motivated by them. I can explain it. Moral reasons have authority over agents because they are what any procedurally rational agent would be motivated to act on, in virtue of Kant's argument about the value of humanity. The agent who is not currently motivated to act morally is not in the position of someone correctly perceiving external normative facts and choosing to disregard them. She is in the position of someone whose deliberation is procedurally defective — who is failing to track what she herself, under conditions of full rationality, would be motivated to do. That explains authority without positing mysterious external normative facts.

Internalism does not reduce reasons to brute desires. It grounds them in procedural rationality, which is itself normatively structured — and Kantian philosophy shows that this structure is sufficient to generate universal moral reasons without abandoning the internalist framework.

Mr President,

I have spent my career arguing that a sophisticated version of reasons internalism can accommodate everything the externalist wants — objective moral truths, universal moral requirements, the authoritativeness of moral reasons over all agents — while remaining true to the internalist insight that reasons must connect with the motivational constitution of the agent. My approach, developed in The Moral Problem, combines internalism about the nature of reasons with a substantive account of what full rationality requires, and shows that these two commitments generate objective moral reasons without any appeal to mysterious external normative facts.

On the Moral Problem and the internalist solution

The Moral Problem: reconciling objectivity, practicality and Humean motivationThe Moral Problem, as I frame it, arises from the apparent tension between three plausible claims: that moral judgements express beliefs about objective facts; that such judgements are necessarily motivating for rational agents; and that the Humean theory of motivation is true — that beliefs alone cannot motivate without the cooperation of a desire. An error theorist dissolves the problem by denying the first claim. An expressivism dissolves it by denying the first in a different way. I propose to resolve it rather than dissolve it: by showing that there are facts about what fully rational agents would desire, and that these facts are the moral facts. On this view, moral judgements express beliefs about objective facts, those facts are constituted by facts about ideal rational desire, and recognising those facts motivates rational agents — because recognising that your fully rational self would desire something rationally commits you to desiring it.

On the ideal advisor account

Ideal advisor: reasons are what a fully rational version of you would be motivated byThe ideal advisor account says: an agent A has a normative reason to φ if and only if A's fully rational counterpart — A as she would be if she had all true beliefs, no false ones, and maximally coherent and informed motivations — would be motivated to φ. This is an internalist account because the reasons are grounded in A's own motivational constitution, corrected for all irrationality and ignorance. But it generates objective reasons because the facts about what a fully rational counterpart would desire are objective facts — not mere reports of current preferences. The fully rational self is not a different person; she is an idealisation of the actual agent that reveals what the agent herself has reason to do.

On convergence and the objectivity of moral reasons

Convergence argument: fully rational agents converge on moral requirementsA crucial part of my account is the claim that fully rational agents would converge in their motivations on moral requirements. This is a contingent but robust empirical claim: the corrections required for full rationality — eliminating false beliefs, achieving coherence of motivations, achieving full information — are precisely the corrections that bring agents' motivations into alignment with moral requirements. This is not a lucky coincidence. It is because moral requirements are themselves constituted by what fully rational agents would desire: the convergence is built into the account. This explains why moral reasons are authoritative for all agents: the fully rational counterpart of any agent, whatever her current desires, would be motivated by moral requirements.

On why internalism does not concede too much to Hume

Against Humean reduction: internalism does not reduce reasons to brute desiresThe externalist charges that internalism reduces reasons to mere desire, making ethics subjective. My account shows that this charge misidentifies what internalism claims. The ideal advisor account does not say that reasons are whatever you happen to desire. It says reasons are what you would desire under conditions of full rationality. Those conditions are substantial: they include full information, coherent motivations, and the elimination of all false beliefs. The agent who wants to eat a saucer of mud because she mistakenly believes it is good does not have a reason to eat it — because her fully rational counterpart, freed from that false belief, would not want to. Internalism with rational idealization is not Humean reduction.

On why externalism fails to explain normativity

Against robust realism: external normative facts cannot explain normativity for agentsThe externalist — Enoch, Shafer-Landau — holds that there are mind-independent normative facts that generate reasons for action regardless of agents' motivational constitutions. But this account has a fatal explanatory gap. Even if there are such facts, why do they give agents reasons to act? What is the connection between a mind-independent normative fact and the agent's practical deliberation? The externalist answer tends to be: they just do; that is what normative facts are. But this is not an explanation. It is a label. The internalist account explains why reasons generate requirements on deliberation: because they are grounded in what the agent's own fully rational self would be motivated to do. That is a genuine explanatory connection between the normative fact and the agent it applies to.

Internalism, properly developed, is not a concession to subjectivism. It is the only account that explains why normative facts are genuinely reasons — for agents, as matters of practical deliberation — rather than merely evaluative facts about how things stand.

Mr President,

I want to begin by expressing a certain sympathy for the internalist intuition. There is something right in the thought that reasons must be capable of engaging with an agent's practical life — that they cannot be entirely external to the standpoint from which she deliberates. But the way Williams formulated internalism, and the way the Kantian internalists have developed it, makes the connection between reasons and motivation far too tight, in a way that makes external reasons not merely theoretically unmotivated but in fact incoherent. My paper "Might There Be External Reasons?" argues that they might — indeed, that they must.

On Williams and the internalist orthodoxy

Against Williams: the subjective motivational set is too restrictiveWilliams holds that an agent has a reason to φ only if there is a sound deliberative route from her current subjective motivational set S to φ-ing. On his picture, if nothing in S can be connected by sound deliberation to φ-ing, there is simply no reason for the agent to φ — and any claim that there is such a reason is either false or a mere rhetorical device. But this restriction faces a fundamental problem. It makes reasons entirely dependent on the contingent contents of an agent's current motivational set. An agent who simply does not care about others, who has no element in her motivational set that could be extended by sound deliberation to concern for others, has, on Williams's view, no reason to be fair or honest or kind. This result is not a reductio of external reasons. It is a reductio of Williams's internalism.

On conversion as a counterexample

Conversion: acquiring new reasons through moral transformation not grounded in prior desiresConsider the phenomenon of moral conversion: an agent who begins with no motivation to care about others and is, through an encounter with the suffering of another person, genuinely transformed — who comes to see that she has reason to act differently. Williams says that if this conversion is rational, there must have been a latent element in her prior motivational set that the encounter activated. But this seems wrong. Sometimes conversion is genuinely new: a person comes to see the world differently, comes to perceive moral reasons she previously had no capacity to perceive, and her motivations change as a result of what she has come to see. The new motivation is a response to the perceived reason, not the activation of a pre-existing desire. Williams's demand that there always be a prior motivational element begs the question against external reasons.

On moral perception and second nature

Moral perception: the virtuous agent perceives moral saliences that constitute genuine reasonsMy broader account of practical reason, developed through my work on virtue and mind, holds that the virtuous agent perceives moral saliences in her situation — features of the world that are genuinely reason-giving, that she is trained by moral education to see clearly. These perceptions are not mere projections of her pre-existing desires onto a neutral world. They are responses to features of the world that are really there to be perceived. The moral education that produces this perceptual capacity is a process of developing second nature — a nature that goes beyond mere first-nature animal desires. The reasons the virtuous agent perceives have their source in the moral facts, not in her prior motivational set. This is external reasons in the sense that matters.

On the internalist's ideal advisor and its limitations

Against Smith's ideal advisor: convergence is not guaranteed and begs the questionMichael Smith argues that fully rational agents converge in their motivations on moral requirements, and that this convergence is what makes moral requirements objective. But this claim is either question-begging or false. If the ideal advisor is defined as someone who would be motivated by moral requirements, then the convergence is trivially true but circular — it builds morality into the idealisation. If the ideal advisor is defined independently of morality, there is no guarantee of convergence. Different agents, ideally informed and coherent, may simply have different motivations. The convergence Smith requires is an empirical claim that has not been established, and there are strong reasons to think it is false: reflective, fully informed agents do in fact disagree about what morality requires.

On why externalism is compatible with the practicality of reasons

Externalism and practicality: external reasons can become motivating through moral educationThe internalist says that external reasons cannot function in practical deliberation because they have no motivational grip. But this misunderstands the relationship between perceiving a reason and being motivated by it. The virtuous agent, trained by moral education, perceives moral reasons and is thereby motivated to act on them. The perception itself is motivating, not because it activates a pre-existing desire, but because seeing that there is a reason to act, for a person of virtuous character, just is being motivated to act. The external reason becomes practically effective through the development of appropriate moral sensibility — the development of second nature. Externalism is not committed to reasons that are necessarily and permanently disconnected from motivation. It is committed to reasons that are not constituted by motivation.

Might there be external reasons? Yes. The conversion case, the moral perception account, and the failure of Williams's account to handle moral transformation all point in the same direction.

∴Externalism is compatible with the practicality of reasons; external reasons become motivating through moral education without being constituted by prior motivation.

Mr President,

I want to make the case for moral realism and against internalism from a different angle from Professor McDowell. My argument is not primarily about conversion or moral perception. It is about the structure of moral rationalism — the thesis that morality necessarily provides reasons for action — and about whether internalism can accommodate it. I will argue that it cannot, and that the only adequate account of moral reasons is an externalist one.

On moral rationalism and what it requires

Moral rationalism: moral facts are intrinsically normative, providing reasons independently of desiresMoral rationalism is the view that moral facts necessarily provide reasons for action — that if it is morally wrong for you to φ, then you have a reason not to φ, full stop, regardless of what you desire. This is a powerful and widely held view. It captures the sense in which morality makes demands on us: not conditional demands that apply only when we happen to care about morality, but categorical demands that apply whether or not we care. Moral facts are, on this view, intrinsically normative — they carry their reason-giving character essentially, not contingently. Internalism cannot accommodate this. If reasons depend on an agent's motivational set, and an agent simply has no element in her motivational set that connects to moral requirements, then moral facts generate no reasons for her. Moral rationalism is false on internalism.

On the amoralist

The amoralist: internalism implies the amoralist can lack moral reasons entirelyThe amoralist is someone who fully understands moral requirements but simply does not care about them — who has no motivation to act morally and nothing in her motivational set from which sound deliberation could generate such motivation. On Williams's internalism, and on any version that ties reasons to the motivational set, the amoralist has no moral reasons. She is not irrational; she simply has different motivations from the rest of us. But this is deeply implausible. The amoralist who tortures innocents for fun is not merely differently motivated from the rest of us. She has reasons not to do what she does; they apply to her whether or not she feels them. The intelligibility of moral condemnation of the amoralist depends on this. Internalism makes moral condemnation a mere expression of our different motivations — which is precisely what moral realism denies.

On why Korsgaard's constitutivism does not help

Against constitutivism: the shmagency objection; constitutive norms of agency are not obviously moralProfessor Korsgaard argues that moral reasons are constitutive of agency itself — that to be a rational agent just is to be subject to the categorical imperative. But this faces what David Enoch has called the shmagency objection. Even if there are constitutive norms of agency, why should the amoralist care about being an agent in the constitutive sense Korsgaard has in mind? She could simply embrace being a "shmagent" — an entity that acts from impulse and contingent desire rather than from the categorical imperative — and deny that the norms of Korsgaardian agency have any grip on her. The constitutive norms of agency have no bootstrapping power to generate motivation in someone who does not already care about rational agency. This is the shmagency objection, and it is formidable.

On moral facts as intrinsically normative

Intrinsic normativity: moral facts carry their reason-giving character essentiallyMy alternative is that moral facts are intrinsically normative: they carry their reason-giving character not contingently, in virtue of some relation to agents' motivations, but essentially, in virtue of what they are. The wrongness of cruelty is not wrong because some agent or set of agents is motivated to avoid it. It is wrong, and its wrongness makes it the case that agents have reason to avoid cruelty, whether or not they are motivated to do so. This is what moral rationalism requires. And moral rationalism is not merely a philosopher's thesis: it is built into our ordinary practices of moral assessment, moral demand, and moral condemnation. When we say "you have reason not to do that," we are not saying "you happen to be motivated in a way that connects to not doing that." We are making a categorical demand.

On the epistemological objection to external reasons

Against the normativity objection: external reasons can be known and responded toThe internalist objects that external reasons cannot function in practical deliberation because they have no motivational grip. But this confuses the metaphysical claim about what reasons are with the epistemological claim about how agents come to respond to them. External reasons can be known: we can come to perceive that cruelty is wrong, that fairness makes categorical demands, that others' suffering generates genuine requirements. And knowing these things, agents of good character are motivated to respond to them. The external reason is what generates the moral requirement; the moral perception and its motivational upshot explain how agents respond to it. These are different questions. The externalist has coherent answers to both.

If morality makes categorical demands on all agents regardless of their motivations, then moral reasons must be external to those motivations. Internalism cannot accommodate moral rationalism. Externalism can and must.

Mr President,

I want to make what I take to be the deepest case for externalism: the argument from what I call deliberative indispensability. My thesis is that when we take practical deliberation seriously — when we genuinely ask "what should I do?" and treat this as a real question with a real answer — we are committed to the existence of normative facts that are external to our own motivational sets. Internalism, whatever its philosophical ingenuity, is inconsistent with taking morality seriously.

On deliberative indispensability

Deliberative indispensability: genuine deliberation commits us to external normative factsHere is the argument from deliberative indispensability. When an agent genuinely deliberates about what to do, she is asking a real question: not "what do I happen to want?" or "what would my fully rational self desire?" but "what should I do?" — where the answer is something she discovers rather than creates or constructs. The deliberating agent treats the question as having an answer that is not made true by her own attitudes. She is committed, in her deliberation, to the existence of normative facts that are not constituted by her preferences, desires, or motivational set. These are external normative facts. If internalism is true, this commitment is systematically mistaken. But we cannot give up this commitment without giving up genuine practical deliberation. Therefore internalism is false.

On the shmagency objection to constitutivism

Shmagency: constitutivism cannot bootstrap normativity from the bare fact of agencyI introduced the shmagency objection to Korsgaard's constitutivism: even if acting morally is constitutive of being a rational agent in Korsgaard's sense, why should the amoralist care about being a rational agent in that sense? She can simply embrace being a shmagent — an entity that acts from contingent desires rather than the categorical imperative — and deny that the constitutive norms of rational agency have any grip on her. Constitutivism tries to bootstrap normativity from the bare fact of agency: from the fact that deliberation has constitutive norms, it tries to derive that those norms give agents reasons. But the bootstrapping argument does not work. Whether the norms of an activity give one reasons depends on whether one has reason to engage in that activity. And constitutivism cannot answer this question without circularity.

On Smith's ideal advisor and why it underdelivers

Against Smith: ideal advisor is either trivially internalist or smuggles in externalismMichael Smith's ideal advisor account says reasons are what a fully rational version of you would be motivated to do. But consider what "fully rational" means in this account. If it means maximally coherent given your current motivational starting points, then the ideal advisor delivers merely the coherentisation of current desires — which gives no reason to the genuinely amorally motivated agent to be moral. If it means something more substantive — if "fully rational" includes being appropriately responsive to moral facts — then the account has smuggled in external normative facts through the back door of the idealization. The ideal advisor account either underdelivers on objectivity or is secretly externalist. There is no stable middle ground.

On robust realism and taking morality seriously

Robust realism: only externalism vindicates our practice of taking morality seriouslyThe main motivation for robust realism — my view — is that no other metaethical position can vindicate our practice of taking morality seriously. When we deliberate about what to do, when we condemn wrongdoing, when we hold each other accountable, we are not merely reporting facts about our own or others' motivational sets. We are making claims whose truth is independent of what anyone happens to want or be motivated by. To take morality seriously is to treat it as making categorical demands that are not constituted by our attitudes — demands that apply even to those who do not care about them. Only robust realism — only externalism about reasons — can vindicate this practice. Internalism, however sophisticated, reduces the authority of moral demands to facts about agents' (idealised or constitutive) motivational states, and in doing so fails to take morality seriously.

On the existence-internalism objection to external reasons

Against existence-internalism: external normative facts need not be metaphysically extravagantThe internalist will say that positing external normative facts commits us to metaphysically extravagant entities — to non-natural properties or queer facts that have no place in a naturalistic worldview. I respond by distinguishing existence-internalism from judgment-internalism. Judgment-internalism — the plausible claim that moral judgements are necessarily connected to motivation — does not entail existence-internalism — the claim that external normative facts do not exist. Moreover, the ontological parsimony argument against external normative facts is not significantly stronger than the analogous argument against mathematical or logical facts. We accept the existence of mathematical facts despite their mind-independence. The case for external normative facts is analogous: we should posit them because their existence is deliberatively indispensable, not deny them because they are ontologically unfamiliar.

Taking morality seriously means believing that when you ask "what should I do?" you are asking a real question with an answer that is not made true by your own attitudes. That belief commits you to external normative facts. That is robust realism.
