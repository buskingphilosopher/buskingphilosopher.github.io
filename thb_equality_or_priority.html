Logical Arguments

Let me begin with a case.

Suppose you are the parent of two teenage children. One of them, let us call her Ann, is healthy and able-bodied. The other, Ben, has recently been diagnosed with a serious physical disability. You have lost your job but have two offers: one in a city, where Ben would gain access to specialist medical facilities that would substantially, though not wholly, alleviate the effects of his disability; the other on the outskirts of town, in a suburb with excellent schools and open spaces, where Ann would flourish. The gain to Ann from the suburb is greater, let us stipulate, than the gain to Ben from the city. A utilitarian would therefore move to the suburb. What should you do?

The Two-Child Case (Nagel 1979).
Ann (healthy) in city: 60  •  Ben (disabled) in city: 39
Ann (healthy) in suburb: 70  •  Ben (disabled) in suburb: 30

Utilitarian verdict: move to the suburb (total = 80 > 69). Nagel’s verdict: move to the city. It is more urgent to benefit Ben, even though the benefit we can give him is smaller.
i. the egalitarian ground: it is in itself bad if some are worse off

Egalitarian principle — inequality intrinsically badThe principle I am invoking is this: it is in itself bad — bad over and above any bad effects it may have on individuals — if some people are worse off than others through no fault or choice of their own. This badness resides in the inequality itself. It is the unfairness of some being less well off than others without having done anything to deserve or incur that disadvantage. When I say it is in itself bad, I mean that this badness does not derive from the suffering it causes, from the envy it generates, or from any further consequence. The inequality is bad in the same way that injustice is bad: not because it makes someone feel worse but because it is a departure from a condition of fairness. Ben is worse off than Ann through no fault of his own. That inequality has a moral weight that is independent of, and can override, the utilitarian calculation that slightly favours the suburb.

ii. urgency, not merely size of benefit

Urgency — the worse-off have a stronger claim, independent of benefit sizeThe key concept is urgency. It is more urgent to benefit Ben, even though the benefit we can give him — a move from 30 to 39 — is smaller than the benefit we can give Ann, which is a move from 60 to 70. The reason it is more urgent is not simply that Ben benefits more in percentage terms, or that his needs are more pressing in some medical sense. It is that improving the situation of someone who is already worse off than others has a priority that improving the situation of the better-off does not. This priority is grounded in the badness of inequality itself. The urgency of Ben’s claim is a function of his relative position: the fact that he is worse off than Ann is precisely what generates the additional weight.

iii. pluralism: equality is not the only value

Pluralist egalitarianism — equality matters but need not always dominateI am not claiming that equality always overrides every other consideration. Equality is not the only value; it must be weighed against the total welfare of individuals and against other moral considerations. In this case, I claim, the egalitarian consideration is sufficient to override the small utilitarian advantage of the suburb: a gain of one unit in total welfare, against a very substantial inequality reduction. But I readily acknowledge that cases can be constructed where the utilitarian advantage is so great that moving to the suburb would be justified even against the egalitarian consideration. My claim is that the egalitarian principle constitutes a genuine moral reason — one that is not simply reducible to the welfare of individuals — and that in this case that reason is decisive. Parfit will say I am really a prioritarian. I deny this: my reason for moving to the city is comparative. It depends essentially on the fact that Ben is worse off than Ann.

iv. in reply to parfit: the comparison is essential, not incidental

Against Parfit — egalitarian reason is essentially comparativeParfit suggests that my reasoning, properly understood, appeals only to Ben’s absolute level of welfare, not to his comparative position, and that I would have equally strong reason to benefit Ben even if Ann did not exist and there were no inequality between them. I contest this. The urgency I feel to benefit Ben is heightened precisely because he is worse off than Ann. If Ben were the only child and had the same absolute level, I do not believe my reason to benefit him rather than pursue some other good would have the same moral character. The egalitarian reason is relational: it is grounded in the comparative fact that someone is worse off than another. Separating it from that comparative fact and calling it “priority” leaves behind the very thing that made the reason morally distinctive.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Inequality is intrinsically bad: the principle of equality
1.It is in itself bad — bad over and above any effects on individuals — if some are worse off than others through no fault or choice of their own. This badness is the unfairness of undeserved inequality.
2.Ben is worse off than Ann through no fault or choice of his own. The inequality between them is therefore bad in itself, independently of the utilitarian calculus.
3.This intrinsic badness of inequality constitutes a genuine moral reason to reduce it that is independent of, and can override, the utilitarian case for the suburb.
∴The egalitarian principle provides an independent moral ground for moving to the city that is not captured by welfare maximisation, and in this case is sufficient to override the small utilitarian advantage of the suburb.
Argument II — Urgency is grounded in comparative position, not absolute level
1.It is more urgent to benefit Ben than Ann, even though the benefit he would receive is smaller.
2.This urgency is grounded in Ben’s comparative position: the fact that he is worse off than Ann is precisely what generates the additional moral weight of his claim.
3.The urgency is therefore essentially relational: remove the comparison with Ann and the distinctive moral weight of the egalitarian reason is lost.
∴Urgency in the egalitarian sense is irreducibly comparative: it cannot be restated as a non-relational claim about Ben’s absolute level without losing the distinctively egalitarian ground of the moral reason.
Argument III — Pluralist egalitarianism: equality can be outweighed but is not reducible to welfare
1.Equality is not the only value: it must be weighed against the welfare of individuals, liberty, and other moral considerations. It can be outweighed.
2.But equality is a genuine, irreducible moral value — not merely an instrument for promoting welfare — and its weight in a given case is a matter of judgment, not formula.
3.In the two-child case, the egalitarian reason (substantial inequality reduction) outweighs the utilitarian reason (a gain of one welfare unit to the total). The verdict: move to the city.
∴Pluralist egalitarianism yields the correct verdict in the two-child case without commitment to the view that equality always overrides welfare — a flexibility that the Priority View purchases only by abandoning the essentially comparative nature of the egalitarian reason.
Argument IV — Against Parfit: the comparative dimension is essential, not eliminable
1.Parfit: the urgency of benefiting Ben can be fully accounted for by his absolute level of welfare, independently of any comparison with Ann. One can re-describe the egalitarian reason as prioritarian without loss.
2.But if Ann did not exist, or if she were equally badly off, the distinctively egalitarian reason to benefit Ben would have a different character. The relational fact that Ben is worse off than Ann is not merely background context; it is constitutive of the moral reason.
3.Parfit’s re-description preserves the verdict (move to the city) while losing the ground (comparative unfairness); this is not a neutral re-description but an elimination of what makes the reason distinctively egalitarian.
∴The egalitarian reason cannot without distortion be restated as prioritarian: the relational, comparative character of the reason is not an optional framing but the moral substance of the position.

Our argument proceeds in two stages.

In the first stage we accept, with Parfit, that there is a powerful objection to egalitarianism: the Levelling Down Objection. If inequality were intrinsically bad, then it would be in at least one respect better if the better-off were brought down to the level of the worse-off, even if no one thereby gains. This seems deeply counterintuitive: there is nothing good about blinding the sighted in order to produce equality among the blind. The Priority View avoids this objection entirely. We therefore take it seriously as an alternative to egalitarianism.

In the second stage, however, we argue that the Priority View fails a different test — one that is, we believe, decisive. The test is provided by the One-Child Case, a transformation of Nagel’s two-child case that Parfit’s view cannot handle without generating deeply counterintuitive implications.

The One-Child Case (Otsuka & Voorhoeve 2009). You have a single teenage child, Cathy, who is healthy but faces a 50% chance of developing the same disability as Ben, and a 50% chance of remaining able-bodied. You must now — before knowing how her health develops — choose permanently between the city and the suburb. Moving to the suburb maximises Cathy’s expected welfare (50). Moving to the city is recommended by final-utility prioritarianism because the move from 30 to 39 at a low absolute level has greater priority-weighted value than the move from 60 to 70 at a higher level.
i. the one-child case defeats final-utility prioritarianism

One-child case — prioritarianism requires you to act against Cathy’s expected interestIn the One-Child Case, moving to the suburb maximises Cathy’s expected welfare. No matter how things turn out, you can offer Cathy a complete prudential justification for the suburb: you balanced the two possible outcomes from the perspective of her self-interest alone, and the suburb was better for her in expected terms. Final-utility prioritarianism nevertheless requires you to move to the city, because the priority-weighted expected value of the city is higher. This means the prioritarian is requiring you to act against the expected interests of the only person whose welfare is at stake, for reasons that are entirely impersonal — reasons that have nothing to do with Cathy’s own good. This is a deeply counterintuitive implication. When only one person’s well-being is at issue, it is bizarre to make decisions on the basis of impersonal moral weights rather than on the basis of what is good for that person.

ii. prioritarianism fails to track the separateness of persons

Separateness of persons — two-child and one-child cases are morally different; priority ignores thisThe fundamental problem is that final-utility prioritarianism applies the same moral calculus to the two-child case and the one-child case. In the two-child case, Ann and Ben are separate persons: no prudential justification for forgoing a gain to Ben can be offered from the perspective of Ben’s interests alone, because the gain that compensates for it goes to someone else. In the one-child case, Cathy’s two possible futures are unified because they are both potential futures of hers. This unity makes it appropriate to balance the potential advantages and drawbacks of each option from the perspective of her interests alone. The Priority View, by applying the same priority weights in both cases, fails to track this morally crucial difference between what happens within a single life and what happens across the boundary between two lives.

iii. egalitarianism does track the difference

Egalitarianism under risk — comparative fairness requires the city in the two-child case but not the one-child caseThe egalitarian view, by contrast, handles the difference correctly. In the two-child case, there is genuine inequality: Ben is worse off than Ann through no fault of his own. The egalitarian principle — that it is bad if some are worse off than others without deserving to be — gives strong reason to move to the city. In the one-child case, there is no inequality between separate persons: Cathy faces a risk of being worse off in one of her possible futures, but this is not an inequality in the morally relevant sense, because both futures belong to the same individual. The egalitarian principle does not apply to risks within a single life in the same way it applies to inequalities between separate persons. This means the egalitarian can recommend the suburb in the one-child case (maximising Cathy’s expected welfare) while recommending the city in the two-child case (reducing inequality between Ann and Ben). The Priority View cannot make this distinction.

iv. in reply to parfit’s defence: the one-child case is not parochial

Reply to Parfit’s defence — one-child objection not answered by hybrid prioritarianismParfit, in his later defence of the Priority View, suggests that hybrid prioritarianism — which gives priority to people’s prospects for welfare as well as their actual welfare — can handle the one-child case better. Even hybrid prioritarianism, he suggests, recommends the city. We have three replies. First, even if hybrid prioritarianism reaches the same verdict (city) in the one-child case, it does so for the wrong reasons — impersonal priority-weighting rather than Cathy’s expected good. The prudential justification we can offer for the suburb is available only if we maximise expected welfare; hybrid prioritarianism forecloses it. Second, Parfit does not explain why priority should apply to prospects as well as outcomes — the extension multiplies the complexity of the view without providing a principled ground for it. Third, the structural problem remains: the Priority View applies the same moral framework to intrapersonal and interpersonal cases, and this is the error that egalitarianism does not make.

We yield the floor.

Argument I — Final-utility prioritarianism requires acting against the only person’s expected interest
1.In the One-Child Case, moving to the suburb uniquely maximises Cathy’s expected welfare (50 vs 49.5). Someone solely concerned with Cathy’s interests would prefer the suburb on her behalf.
2.Final-utility prioritarianism requires the city because the priority-weighted expected value of the city is higher: gains from lower absolute levels receive greater moral weight.
3.This means the prioritarian is requiring the parent to act against the expected welfare of the only person whose well-being is at stake, for entirely impersonal reasons. If you chose the suburb, you could offer Cathy a complete prudential justification; if you chose the city, no such justification is available no matter how things turn out.
∴Final-utility prioritarianism generates the deeply counterintuitive result that one must act against the expected interests of the only affected person for impersonal moral reasons — a result that is both implausible in itself and a symptom of the deeper structural problem with the Priority View.
Argument II — Prioritarianism fails to respect the separateness of persons
1.In the Two-Child Case, Ann and Ben are separate persons. No prudential justification for the suburb can be offered to Ben from the perspective of his interests alone, because only a different person (Ann) benefits. The separateness of persons makes it hard to justify forgoing a gain to Ben in order to benefit Ann more.
2.In the One-Child Case, Cathy’s two possible futures are unified because they are both potential futures of hers. It is appropriate to balance them from the perspective of her interests alone.
3.Final-utility prioritarianism applies the same priority weights to increments in welfare in both cases, thereby failing to track the morally crucial difference between trade-offs within a life and trade-offs across lives.
∴The Priority View fails to respect the separateness of persons by treating intrapersonal and interpersonal trade-offs as morally equivalent; this is a fundamental flaw, not a technical difficulty.
Argument III — Egalitarianism correctly distinguishes the two cases
1.The egalitarian principle applies to inequalities between separate persons: it is bad if some are worse off than others through no fault of their own. In the Two-Child Case, this principle applies and generates strong reason to move to the city.
2.In the One-Child Case, there is no inequality between separate persons: Cathy faces a risk within her own life, not an unchosen disadvantage relative to another person. The egalitarian principle does not apply in the same way.
3.The egalitarian therefore recommends the suburb in the One-Child Case (maximising Cathy’s expected welfare) and the city in the Two-Child Case (reducing inequality between Ann and Ben).
∴Egalitarianism correctly tracks the morally crucial distinction between intrapersonal risk and interpersonal inequality; it recommends different choices in the two cases for principled reasons, while the Priority View treats them the same.
Argument IV — Against hybrid prioritarianism: it answers the wrong question
1.Parfit’s hybrid prioritarianism extends priority-weighting to prospects as well as outcomes, and therefore recommends the city even in the One-Child Case. Parfit claims this shows that prioritarianism can handle the one-child objection.
2.But even if hybrid prioritarianism reaches the same verdict (city), it does so for the wrong reasons: impersonal priority-weighting of prospects rather than concern for Cathy’s expected good. The verdict is the same; the justification is not. And no prudential justification for the city can be offered to Cathy from the perspective of her own interests.
3.Moreover, hybrid prioritarianism provides no principled ground for why priority should apply to prospects as well as outcomes; the extension multiplies complexity without solving the structural problem of failing to distinguish intrapersonal from interpersonal trade-offs.
∴Hybrid prioritarianism does not resolve the one-child objection; it reaches the right verdict by the wrong route and fails to explain why the intrapersonal/interpersonal distinction should be ignored.

I want to defend egalitarianism from a direction somewhat different from Nagel’s.

The Levelling Down Objection, as Parfit presents it, is taken by many to be simply devastating to egalitarianism. If inequality is intrinsically bad, then its removal must be in at least one respect a change for the better, however that removal is achieved. But if we blind the sighted to produce equality among the blind, that seems in no respect an improvement. Therefore — so the argument goes — inequality cannot be intrinsically bad, and egalitarianism is false. I want to resist this conclusion at every step.

“Isn’t it unfair for some to be worse off than others through no fault of their own? Isn’t it unfair for some to be blind while others are not? And isn’t unfairness bad? Does this mean I think it would be better if we blinded everybody? No. Equality is not all that matters. But in one respect — the respect of comparative fairness — a world of universal blindness is better than a world of unequal sight.”
i. the slogan and why it is questionable

The Slogan — person-affecting principle; Temkin’s challengeAt the heart of the Levelling Down Objection is what I call the Slogan: an outcome can be better in some respect only if it is better for some particular person. The Slogan is a narrow person-affecting principle: it holds that the only genuine improvements are improvements in the welfare of actual individuals. This principle has great intuitive appeal. But it is not obvious that it is correct, and the Levelling Down Objection inherits all of its force from the Slogan’s assumed truth. If we are entitled to doubt the Slogan — if we are entitled to believe that an outcome can be better in some respect even if it is better for no one — then the Levelling Down Objection loses its force. And I submit that we are so entitled, because the Slogan presupposes a welfarist view of what can be morally valuable that is itself controversial and contestable.

ii. comparative fairness as a genuine moral consideration

Comparative fairness — relational value; unchosen inequality is genuinely badThe egalitarian’s fundamental concern is with comparative fairness: with how individuals fare relative to each other. It is bad, I maintain, when some are worse off than others through no fault or choice of their own, not because this makes any individual worse off than they would otherwise be, but because it is unfair — and unfairness is bad even when no individual is made worse off by the unfair state of affairs. The Levelling Down case forces a choice: accept the Slogan and reject egalitarianism, or reject the Slogan and accept that the equal-blindness world is better in one respect (comparative fairness) even though it is worse in overall welfare. I choose the latter. This is not absurd. There genuinely is something bad about the unequal world — the unfairness of some being blind while others are not through no fault of their own — and that bad thing is absent in the equal world. We need not and should not conclude that equalisation by levelling down is on balance better. Equality is not the only value. But it is a value.

iii. inequality is complex and multi-dimensional

Complexity of inequality — many principles, no algorithm; judgement requiredOne source of the Levelling Down Objection’s apparent force is the assumption that egalitarianism must yield a simple algorithm: the worse the inequality, the more reason to reduce it, always and everywhere. But the moral assessment of inequality is, I argue, inherently complex. Inequality may be worse when it affects a greater proportion of the population; it may be worse at the bottom than at the top; it may be worse when the gap is wider; and it may be worse when those who are worse off are significantly below some reasonable standard of sufficiency. These dimensions do not reduce to a single measure, and no single principle of equality can capture all of them. This complexity is not a defect in egalitarianism; it reflects the genuine complexity of the moral phenomenon we are trying to assess. The fact that the resulting theory is not a simple formula does not mean it is no theory at all.

iv. against parfit: priority cannot replace equality

Against Parfit — altitude analogy misses comparative unfairnessParfit’s altitude analogy is famous. People at higher altitudes find it harder to breathe, not because they are higher than others but because of their absolute altitude. Similarly, Parfit claims, benefits to the worse-off matter more, not because they are worse off than others, but because they are at a lower absolute level. The comparison with others is irrelevant. But the analogy fails in a crucial respect: breathing difficulty is not a matter of comparative fairness. Nobody is treated unfairly by finding it hard to breathe at high altitude; there is no wrongdoer, no relationship of advantage and disadvantage between persons. Inequality of welfare is different: it involves some people being worse off than others, and when that is unchosen and undeserved, there is a genuine complaint about comparative treatment. Parfit’s altitude analogy precisely strips away the relational, moral dimension of inequality and replaces it with an impersonal fact about absolute levels. That is not a neutral re-description; it is a substantive elimination of what makes inequality morally objectionable.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Levelling Down Objection rests on the Slogan, which is questionable
1.The Levelling Down Objection: if inequality is intrinsically bad, levelling down must be in some respect an improvement; but levelling down (e.g. blinding the sighted) is in no respect an improvement; therefore inequality is not intrinsically bad.
2.The objection rests on the Slogan: an outcome can be better in some respect only if it is better for some particular person. The Slogan is a narrow person-affecting principle of contested validity.
3.If we are entitled to doubt the Slogan — to believe that fairness can be a genuine moral improvement even when it benefits no one — the Levelling Down Objection loses its force. We are so entitled: the Slogan presupposes a welfarism that is itself controversial.
∴The Levelling Down Objection is not as decisive as it appears; it presupposes the Slogan, which is independently contestable, and the egalitarian is entitled to reject the Slogan and accept that levelling down is in one respect (comparative fairness) better, even if worse overall.
Argument II — Comparative fairness is a genuine moral value, independent of individual welfare
1.It is bad when some are worse off than others through no fault or choice of their own, not because this makes any individual worse off than they would otherwise be, but because it is unfair — and unfairness is bad even when no individual is made worse off by it.
2.In the universal-blindness world, the comparative unfairness of the unequal-blindness world is absent. This is a genuine moral improvement in the dimension of comparative fairness, even if the world is worse overall in welfare terms.
3.Pluralism: equality of welfare matters, but so does total welfare and other values. Levelling down is not on balance better; but the reduction in unfairness is a real moral good that is real and present in the levelled-down world.
∴Comparative fairness is a genuine moral value that the Levelling Down Objection illegitimately dismisses; the egalitarian is entitled to say that the equal-blindness world is better in one respect, without being committed to saying it is better all things considered.
Argument III — Inequality is complex; no single principle captures all its dimensions
1.The moral badness of inequality varies across multiple dimensions: the proportion of the population that is worse off, the size of the gap, whether the worse-off are near or far from a sufficiency threshold, and others.
2.No single formula can reduce these dimensions to a single measure; the assessment of inequality requires judgment across multiple incommensurable considerations.
3.This complexity is a feature, not a bug: it reflects the genuine moral complexity of the phenomenon. A theory that yielded a simple algorithm would be suspicious precisely because it ignored morally relevant distinctions.
∴The complexity of egalitarianism does not impugn it; the Priority View’s simplicity is achieved by systematically ignoring morally relevant features of comparative advantage and disadvantage that egalitarianism correctly tracks.
Argument IV — Against Parfit: the altitude analogy strips away the moral dimension of inequality
1.Parfit: benefits to the worse-off matter more because of their absolute level, not their comparative position. The altitude analogy: one finds it hard to breathe at high altitude regardless of whether others are at lower altitudes.
2.But breathing difficulty at altitude is not a matter of comparative fairness; there is no wrongdoer, no relationship of advantage and disadvantage between persons. Inequality of welfare is different: it involves some being worse off than others, and when unchosen, this generates a genuine complaint about treatment.
3.Parfit’s analogy strips away the relational, comparative dimension of inequality and replaces it with a non-comparative fact about absolute levels. This is not a neutral re-description but the substantive elimination of what makes inequality morally distinctive.
∴The altitude analogy fails because it ignores the morally crucial relational character of inequality; comparative fairness — how some fare relative to others through no fault of their own — is the heart of the egalitarian concern and cannot be recast in non-comparative terms without moral loss.

I want to begin with what I take to be the clearest version of the problem.

Nagel is right that we should move to the city. He is right that the urgency of benefiting the disabled child is greater than the benefit we could give the healthy child, even though the latter benefit is larger. But I believe he has misdescribed his own reason. His reason is not, I will argue, the egalitarian reason he supposes — not the intrinsic badness of the inequality between the two children. It is the prioritarian reason: that benefiting the disabled child matters more because that child is worse off in absolute terms.

“People at higher altitudes find it harder to breathe. Is this because they are higher up than other people? In one sense, yes. But they would find it just as hard to breathe even if there were no other people who were lower down. In the same way, on the Priority View, benefits to the worse off matter more, but that is only because these people are at a lower absolute level. It is irrelevant that they are worse off than others.” — Equality or Priority? (1995)
i. the levelling down objection is decisive

Levelling Down Objection — decisive against telic egalitarianismTelic egalitarianism — the view that it is in itself bad if some are worse off than others — is subject to the Levelling Down Objection. If inequality is intrinsically bad, then its removal must be in at least one respect a change for the better, however it is achieved. Suppose the better-off suffer misfortune and become as badly off as everyone else. Their misfortune removes the inequality. Egalitarianism must say this is in one way good. But this seems absurd. When we blind the sighted to produce equality among the blind, we have made things worse for some and better for no one. There is nothing good in this — no respect in which it is an improvement. The Levelling Down Objection shows that telic egalitarianism is committed to a conclusion that is not merely counterintuitive but genuinely morally repugnant: that a world can be improved by gratuitous harm to the well-off, provided only that the harm produces equality.

ii. the priority view avoids levelling down

Priority View — absolute level, not comparative; no levelling down implicationThe Priority View avoids this problem entirely. On the Priority View, benefiting people matters more the worse off they are — but this is because of their absolute level, not their comparative position. If Ann suffered misfortune and became as badly off as Ben, this would be a change for the worse. Nothing is gained by Ann’s misfortune: no benefit accrues to Ben, the priority-weighted value of outcomes does not improve, and the Priority View correctly identifies this as unambiguously bad. The egalitarian must say there is at least one respect in which this levelling down is good; the prioritarian need say no such thing. This is a decisive advantage. Nagel’s intuition that we should move to the city is better explained by the Priority View than by egalitarianism, because the Priority View can generate that conclusion without the Levelling Down implication.

iii. the altitude analogy: why comparisons are irrelevant

Altitude analogy — absolute level, not relativity, does the moral workMy altitude analogy is designed to make vivid what the Priority View claims. People at high altitude find it harder to breathe, but the difficulty is not caused by their being higher than others at lower altitude. They would find it equally hard to breathe even if no one were at lower altitude. Similarly, on the Priority View, benefits to the worse off matter more because of where those people are in absolute terms — because their lives are harder, their opportunities more constrained, their welfare more precarious — not because of where they are in relation to the better-off. The comparison with the better-off is evidence of the absolute level, but it is not the ground of the moral claim. This means that if Ann and Ben were the only people in the world, with the same absolute levels, we would have equally strong reason to benefit Ben — even in the complete absence of inequality. Nagel’s egalitarian view cannot say this. I believe he should be able to say it, and that his intuitions, properly understood, support the Priority View.

iv. nagel is really a prioritarian

Nagel as prioritarian — his intuitions track absolute level, not relative positionNagel says it is more urgent to benefit the disabled child. I ask: would it be equally urgent to benefit the disabled child even if he had no sibling who was better off? I believe — and I believe Nagel believes, if he examines his own intuitions — that the answer is yes. The disabled child would be at the same absolute level whether or not he had an able-bodied sibling; and the urgency of benefiting him arises from that absolute level, not from the comparison. If this is right, then Nagel is not really an egalitarian. He is using the language of equality while reasoning in terms of the Priority View. My task is not to change his verdict but to correct his explanation of it.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Levelling Down Objection is decisive against telic egalitarianism
1.Telic egalitarianism: it is in itself bad if some are worse off than others. If true, the removal of inequality must be in at least one respect a change for the better, regardless of how it is achieved.
2.If the better-off suffer misfortune that equalises their welfare with the worse-off, the inequality is removed; telic egalitarianism must say this is in one way good.
3.But this is morally repugnant: blinding the sighted to produce equality is not good in any respect. When some are made worse off and no one benefits, there is no moral improvement. The Levelling Down Objection reveals that telic egalitarianism is committed to an absurdity.
∴Telic egalitarianism is false; the Levelling Down Objection is decisive. No view that treats equality as intrinsically valuable can avoid committing to the absurdity that gratuitous harm to the well-off is in some respect an improvement.
Argument II — The Priority View avoids Levelling Down while preserving pro-worse-off intuitions
1.On the Priority View, benefiting people matters more the worse off they are, because of their absolute level. Nothing good happens when the better-off are harmed without benefit to the worse-off: no priority-weighted improvement is registered.
2.The Priority View therefore generates no Levelling Down implication: it correctly identifies gratuitous equalisation as unambiguously bad (the priority-weighted sum of welfare is reduced, and nothing is gained).
3.Yet the Priority View still recommends moving to the city in Nagel’s case: the priority-weighted gain to the disabled child exceeds the priority-weighted gain to the healthy child, because gains from lower levels have greater moral weight.
∴The Priority View generates the correct verdict in Nagel’s case without the Levelling Down implication; it is superior to telic egalitarianism because it captures the intuition that benefits to the worse-off matter more while avoiding the absurd commitment to levelling down.
Argument III — The altitude analogy: absolute level, not comparative position, grounds priority
1.People at higher altitudes find it hard to breathe regardless of whether others are at lower altitudes. Their difficulty is caused by their absolute altitude, not by any comparison with others.
2.Similarly, benefits to the worse-off matter more because of their absolute level — the hardship, constraint, and precariousness of their lives — not because of their comparative position. The comparison is evidence of the level, not the ground of the reason.
3.This means that if there were no better-off people to compare with, we would have equally strong reason to benefit the worse-off. The Priority View is a non-comparative view; the egalitarian reason is comparative and therefore loses its ground in isolated populations.
∴The altitude analogy correctly identifies what does the moral work in our concern for the worse-off: their absolute level, not their relative position. The Priority View is therefore better grounded than egalitarianism, which makes the reason comparative and therefore contingent on the existence of the better-off.
Argument IV — Nagel is really a prioritarian: his intuitions track absolute levels
1.Nagel says it is more urgent to benefit the disabled child; he characterises this as an egalitarian conviction, grounded in the comparative fact that the disabled child is worse off than his sibling.
2.But if the disabled child had no sibling, Nagel would — I submit — have the same conviction that it is urgent to benefit him. The urgency arises from the child’s absolute level, not from the existence of a better-off sibling.
3.If the urgency persists in the absence of inequality, then it cannot be grounded in inequality. Nagel’s intuitions, properly interrogated, support the Priority View, not egalitarianism.
∴Nagel is a prioritarian who uses the language of equality; the Priority View correctly re-describes his intuitions as non-comparative concern for absolute level, without distorting the verdict or the strength of the moral reason.

I want to respond to Otsuka and Voorhoeve’s one-child objection directly.

Otsuka and Voorhoeve claim that the Priority View fails because it applies the same moral calculus to intrapersonal trade-offs and interpersonal trade-offs, thereby ignoring the separateness of persons. In the One-Child Case, they say, moving to the suburb uniquely maximises the child’s expected welfare; the prioritarian who requires the city is therefore acting against the only person’s expected interest for impersonal reasons, which is absurd. This is a serious objection and I want to give it its due. But I do not believe it is decisive.

Parfit’s reply. Consider which option is better for the child at risk of disability, from the perspective of what we owe to her. If there is a one-in-two chance that moving to the city would be better for her — because she turns out to be disabled and the city provides the treatment she needs — and if she would in that case be at a very low absolute level of welfare, there is strong reason to choose the city grounded entirely in concern for what is at stake in her life. This concern is prioritarian, not impersonal.
i. priority applies to prospects: hybrid prioritarianism

Hybrid prioritarianism — priority-weighted prospects, not just outcomesMy response is to endorse what I call hybrid prioritarianism: the view that we should care about the priority-weighted well-being both of outcomes people actually receive and of the prospects they face — the probability-weighted set of possible outcomes they might receive. Just as non-prioritarians who are egalitarians rightly care about equality of outcomes and equality of chances, prioritarians can and should care about people’s prospects weighted by priority as well as their actual outcomes weighted by priority. Applied to the One-Child Case: there is a one-in-two chance that the child will be at a very low level of welfare and will benefit from the city. The expected priority-weighted welfare of the city exceeds that of the suburb. The prioritarian therefore recommends the city, not for impersonal reasons but because of what is at stake in the child’s own life.

ii. the prudential-justification argument proves too much

Prudential justification argument overextended — many correct choices cannot be prudentially justified ex postOtsuka and Voorhoeve argue that one can offer a prudential justification for moving to the suburb to the child no matter how things turn out: the suburb maximised her expected welfare. This is true, but it does not show that the city is wrong. The availability of a prudential ex post justification for one option does not entail that the other option is impermissible. Many correct moral choices cannot be prudentially justified to each affected person after the fact: a fair lottery may result in one person winning and another losing, yet both faced the same fair procedure. The question is not whether we can justify the outcome to the child after the fact, but whether we have strong moral reason to choose the city given what is at stake in her life. And we do: there is a non-trivial chance she will be badly off, and the city would prevent this.

iii. egalitarianism faces its own difficulties under risk

Egalitarian difficulties under risk — ex ante vs ex post equality; inconsistencyOtsuka and Voorhoeve suggest that egalitarianism handles the one-child case by simply not applying its principles, because there is no inequality between separate persons in the one-child case. But this move creates its own problem: under risk, it is not always clear whether the relevant equality is ex ante (equality of prospects) or ex post (equality of outcomes). If two people face a fair coin toss that will make one of them better off and leave the other as they are, is there more equality in the prospect (both face the same chance) or the outcome (one will be better off than the other)? The egalitarian faces a genuine dilemma here that the Priority View does not: it must choose between ex ante and ex post equality, and each choice generates counterintuitive results in some cases. The priority view, which applies its framework uniformly, has a structural advantage in clarity and consistency.

iv. the separateness of persons cuts both ways

Separateness cuts both ways — egalitarianism also ignores individual boundariesOtsuka and Voorhoeve say the Priority View fails to respect the separateness of persons because it applies the same calculus to intrapersonal and interpersonal cases. But note that telic egalitarianism faces a parallel difficulty from a different direction: it is prepared, under some conditions, to accept that the better-off should be disadvantaged even when this benefits no one, in order to produce equality. This is, in a sense, the ultimate failure to respect the separateness of persons: it treats individuals as mere vessels of an impersonal value (equality) rather than as ends in themselves whose welfare matters. The Priority View, by contrast, is grounded entirely in what is at stake for particular persons at particular levels of welfare. It is the more person-centred, not the less person-centred, of the two views.

I want to suggest that both sides of this debate have missed the real source of our intuitions in Nagel’s case.

Both Nagel and Parfit agree that we should move to the city. Both agree that the reason has something to do with the disabled child’s worse situation. They disagree about whether that reason is comparative (egalitarian) or non-comparative (prioritarian). I want to propose a third diagnosis: the reason is neither equality nor priority but compassion — a direct, non-comparative concern for those who are badly off, which generates what I call the Compassion Principle, and which, when developed, points toward sufficientarianism rather than either of the views my interlocutors have defended.

“The basis of our special concern for the worse off, in cases like Nagel’s, is not that they are worse off than others, nor that well-being has diminishing marginal moral value. It is simply that they are badly off — that their lives fall below a level at which a reasonable person would find them good. This is a direct response to suffering, not a response to inequality or to a mathematical property of well-being curves.”
i. what is driving the intuition in nagel’s case

Compassion — not equality or priority; direct response to suffering below thresholdConsider what is actually generating the strong intuition that we should move to the city. Is it the inequality between the two children? Or is it the fact that the disabled child will have a very hard life? I submit that it is overwhelmingly the latter. We are moved by the prospect of a child growing up with a serious disability that will profoundly limit his life without treatment. If Ben’s disability were mild — if even without treatment he would live a flourishing life — the intuition that we must move to the city would be substantially weaker, even if the inequality between Ann and Ben were exactly the same. What is driving the intuition is not the gap between Ann and Ben but the badness of Ben’s situation in absolute terms. And this is not Parfit’s priority either: it is not that Ben’s welfare has diminishing marginal moral value in some abstract mathematical sense. It is that Ben is badly off, and we have compassion for those who are badly off.

ii. sufficientarianism: absolute priority below the threshold

Sufficientism — absolute priority below threshold; indifference above itThe principle that I derive from this diagnosis is sufficientarianism: we should give absolute priority to benefiting those whose lives fall below a threshold of sufficiently good life, over benefits to those who are above that threshold. Below the threshold, we have compelling reason to improve someone’s life regardless of the benefits foregone by those above the threshold. Above the threshold, the special urgency of the compassion principle fades: we have no more reason to help a millionaire reach a higher income than to help a billionaire do so. In Nagel’s case, Ben is below the threshold (or would be without treatment) and Ann is above it (even without the move to the suburb). The sufficientarian verdict is: move to the city, and move there decisively — not because of equality, not because of priority-weighted welfare, but because Ben’s situation falls below what we can reasonably regard as a sufficiently good life.

iii. against the priority view: the repugnant implication

Against Priority — millionaires and billionaires; priority has no thresholdThe Priority View has an implication that sufficientarianism avoids. Since the Priority View holds that benefits to the worse-off always matter more than benefits to the better-off, by virtue of the concavity of the transformation function, it follows that we should always give some extra weight to benefiting a moderately poor person over a rich one, and to benefiting a rich person over a very rich one, and so on indefinitely. There is no level above which the special concern for the worse-off ceases. This means the Priority View must give some extra weight to benefiting a millionaire over a billionaire. But this seems wrong: the difference in well-being between millionaires and billionaires is morally irrelevant. Above a sufficient level, equality and priority are simply beside the point. Sufficientarianism correctly identifies the threshold above which these concerns fade, in a way that the Priority View, with its smooth transformation function, cannot.

iv. against egalitarianism: levelling down and the Beverly Hills case

Against egalitarianism — Levelling Down; Beverly Hills case; equality lacks traction above thresholdEgalitarianism shares the Priority View’s problem of lacking a threshold. The egalitarian must say there is some moral reason to reduce the inequality between millionaires and billionaires, which seems implausible. But egalitarianism has the additional problem of Levelling Down: if we could equalise the welfare of two millionaires by slightly reducing the richer one, egalitarianism must regard this as in one respect an improvement. But it is not. I agree with Parfit on this point: levelling down is not good in any respect. Temkin’s response — that the Slogan is questionable — does not help here, because even if we grant that fairness is a genuine moral value independent of welfare, the fairness between millionaires and billionaires is simply not a pressing moral concern. Sufficientarianism correctly focuses our moral attention below the threshold of sufficiency, where genuine suffering and deprivation give grounds for compassion and priority, and neither egalitarianism nor prioritarianism can do this consistently.
