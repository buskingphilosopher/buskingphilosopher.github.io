Mr President,

I want to begin with the empirical case, because I have spent my career insisting that economics is an empirical science, not a collection of ideological positions. The historical record — documented in exhaustive detail by Anna Schwartz and myself in A Monetary History of the United States — is unambiguous: every sustained inflation in recorded history has been preceded and accompanied by an abnormally rapid expansion of the money supply; and every sustained contraction in the money supply has been followed by deflation or depression. This is not a theoretical deduction from assumptions. It is a finding that survives investigation across countries, across centuries, and across radically different economic institutions.

"Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon in the sense that it is and can be produced only by a more rapid increase in the quantity of money than in output. The cure for inflation — and it is a painful cure — is to slow the rate of increase of the money supply. There are no other cures. There are only disguises."
i. the quantity theory — from Fisher to Friedman

Quantity theory — MV=PQ; long-run money neutrality; short-run non-neutrality; velocity stabilityThe quantity theory of money — summarised in the exchange equation MV = PQ — is one of the oldest propositions in economics and one of the most durable. If the velocity of money V is stable, or at least predictable, then changes in the money supply M must be reflected, in the long run, in changes in either the price level P or real output Q. Since real output Q is determined in the long run by real factors — capital accumulation, technology, population — money growth beyond the growth rate of real output must eventually be reflected entirely in price level increases. This is the foundation of the proposition that inflation is a monetary phenomenon. I want to be precise about the relevant time horizon: in the short run, monetary expansion does affect real output, through mechanisms of price stickiness and expectational lags that Keynesians are right to emphasise. But in the long run, money is neutral: it determines only the price level, not real quantities. The stagflation of the 1970s — high inflation combined with high unemployment — was the decisive empirical refutation of the idea that there is a permanently exploitable trade-off between inflation and unemployment.

ii. the natural rate hypothesis — the long-run vertical Phillips curve

Natural rate — NAIRU; inflation-unemployment trade-off illusory long-run; expectations-augmented Phillips curveThe Keynesian tradition, as it existed in the 1960s, implied a stable long-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment — that policymakers could purchase lower unemployment at the cost of permanently higher inflation. Edmund Phelps and I independently demonstrated in 1968 that this trade-off is illusory in the long run. Workers and firms form expectations about inflation; once they expect a given inflation rate, they incorporate it into wage and price negotiations, eliminating the real effects of anticipated monetary expansion. The long-run Phillips curve is vertical at the natural rate of unemployment: the rate consistent with labour market equilibrium given existing search frictions, skill mismatches, and institutional rigidities. Attempts to drive unemployment below the natural rate through monetary expansion generate ever-accelerating inflation, not a stable elevated price level. The 1970s stagflation vindicated this prediction exactly: attempts to exploit the short-run trade-off generated accelerating inflation while leaving unemployment at or above its natural rate.

iii. the Volcker disinflation — the empirical test

Volcker — 1979-82 monetary tightening; M1 targeting; inflation broken; real recession cost; confirmationThe most important empirical test of monetarism was the Volcker disinflation of 1979 to 1982. Paul Volcker, appointed Federal Reserve Chairman by Jimmy Carter and retained by Ronald Reagan, implemented a deliberate and sustained reduction in money supply growth — money supply targeting, as I had recommended — and produced the most rapid disinflation in American peacetime history. US consumer price inflation fell from 14.8% in 1980 to 3.2% by 1983. The cost was a severe recession — unemployment peaked at 10.8% in late 1982 — which confirmed the short-run output costs of disinflation that my theory predicted. But the disinflation was achieved. The pain was real and I do not minimise it. But it demonstrated conclusively that a sufficiently determined monetary authority can break a sustained inflation, and that the mechanism of disinflation is precisely the monetary tightening my framework predicts. No rival theory offers as satisfactory an account of the Volcker episode.

iv. rules over discretion — Friedman's k-percent rule

k-percent rule — constant money growth; credibility; political business cycle; central bank independenceMy policy conclusion from the quantity theory is not merely that central banks should tighten when inflation appears. It is that they should commit, constitutionally if possible, to a fixed rule of money supply growth equal to the long-run growth rate of real output — approximately 3-4% per annum in the United States. This k-percent rule eliminates the political business cycle, removes discretionary authority from central bankers who will always be subject to political pressure to inflate, and provides the private sector with a stable nominal anchor for long-run planning. The absence of such a rule is the primary explanation for the inflation of the 1970s: the Fed accommodated the political preference of successive administrations for low unemployment, generating money growth far in excess of real output growth, and the inevitable result was the worst peacetime inflation in American history.

v. against the opposition — cost-push, greedflation, and supply-side excuses

Cost-push rebuttal — price controls fail; supply shocks cannot sustain inflation without monetary accommodation; greedflation requires purchasing powerThe opposition will argue, as the post-Keynesians always do in inflationary episodes, that the cause is cost-push: oil prices, supply chain disruptions, corporate pricing power. This argument has a distinguished history of being wrong. Cost-push shocks cannot sustain a general price level increase without monetary accommodation. A supply shock that raises oil prices raises energy costs and, if the money supply is held constant, must divert purchasing power away from other goods — causing their prices to fall. The result is a change in relative prices, not a sustained rise in the general price level. Sustained general price inflation requires more money chasing the same goods; if the money supply is not expanding to accommodate the higher prices, buyers cannot afford the higher prices and sellers cannot sustain them. The 2021-22 inflation was caused by an approximately $5 trillion fiscal expansion financed by money creation — not by corporate greed or supply chain disruption, though these contributed to its sectoral distribution.

Mr President,

I want to propose a refinement — and in some respects a correction — of the monetarist framework that Professor Friedman has defended. I agree with the monetarists that inflation is not principally about corporate greed or supply chains. But I disagree with their diagnosis of the mechanism. The fundamental source of inflation is not the money supply; it is the government's fiscal position — its outstanding debt and the credibility of its commitment to generate the future surpluses required to service that debt. The Fiscal Theory of the Price Level — developed by Christopher Sims, myself, and others — provides the most complete account of modern inflation in a world where financial innovation has made money demand unstable and central banks set interest rates rather than money supply targets.

"The price level — or equivalently, the exchange rate between dollars and goods — is determined by the government's balance sheet, just as a stock price is determined by a company's value. When people lose confidence that the government will generate the future surpluses to service its debt, they spend bonds rather than saving them, bidding up prices. The Fed can change the path of inflation, but it cannot determine the price level independently of fiscal policy. Monetary policy is not enough."
i. the government valuation equation

Government valuation equation — P = debt / PV(surpluses); fiscal backing of currency; stock analogyThe fiscal theory's central equation is the government's intertemporal budget constraint: the real value of nominal government debt equals the present discounted value of future primary surpluses — the excess of tax revenue over expenditure, excluding interest payments. This is not merely an accounting identity; it is an equilibrium condition that determines the price level. The price level P adjusts until the real value of the government's nominal debt — V/P, where V is the nominal face value — equals what people expect the government to repay in real terms. This is structurally identical to the Gordon Growth Model for stock valuation: the stock price equals the present value of future dividends. Currency is, in effect, an equity claim on the government's future surpluses. When those surpluses are credibly expected to be inadequate to service outstanding debt, the currency depreciates — the price level rises — until the real value of debt is reduced to match what people expect to be repaid.

ii. why monetary policy alone cannot control inflation

Fiscal dominance — interest rate hikes increase deficit if FTPL holds; monetary policy impotent without fiscal backingThe monetarist prescription — tighten monetary policy when inflation appears — is incomplete and potentially counterproductive in a world of fiscal dominance. When the central bank raises interest rates to fight inflation, it increases the government's interest expense on its outstanding floating-rate debt. If the government does not credibly offset this higher interest cost with future primary surpluses — either by raising taxes or cutting spending — the higher interest payments simply add to the stock of outstanding debt without any corresponding increase in the expected surplus stream. On FTPL logic, this increases the fiscal imbalance rather than reducing it, and may be inflationary rather than disinflationary. Thomas Sargent and Neil Wallace's "Unpleasant Monetarist Arithmetic" (1981) demonstrated this formally: under fiscal dominance, raising the interest rate today forces the central bank to print more money later to cover the higher interest bill, producing more inflation in the long run.

iii. the 2021-22 inflation as a fiscal shock

2021-22 fiscal shock — helicopter money; $5 trillion spending; no surplus offset; inflation as fiscal debt revaluationThe 2021-22 US inflation is the most important test of the fiscal theory in decades, and the evidence strongly favours a fiscal interpretation. The United States spent approximately $5 trillion in pandemic relief — direct transfers to households and businesses — largely financed by debt monetisation by the Federal Reserve. Unlike previous episodes of quantitative easing (2008-2015), this spending was directed at households' disposable income rather than bank reserves. People received cheques and spent them. The crucial fiscal dimension is that there was no credible commitment to offset this spending with future surpluses: the Biden administration followed the relief spending with additional stimulus in early 2021, signalling that fiscal policy would not return to sustainability. On FTPL, this fiscal signal — not the money printing per se — is what drove the sustained inflation. The Fed's subsequent rate hikes reduced but did not eliminate the inflation, consistent with FTPL's prediction that monetary policy can shape inflation's path but not determine its level without fiscal backing.

iv. Sargent's four great inflations — the fiscal theory of hyperinflation

Sargent's four inflations — ended by fiscal reform not monetary restriction; credibility mechanismThomas Sargent's analysis of the endings of the four great European hyperinflations — Germany, Austria, Hungary, and Poland in the 1920s — is the most compelling historical evidence for the fiscal theory. Each hyperinflation was ended not by a deliberate monetary contraction — reducing the money supply — but by a change in fiscal regime: the establishment of an independent central bank with constitutional prohibition on deficit financing, combined with a credible plan for fiscal sustainability. In Germany's case, the hyperinflation ended almost instantaneously — not gradually as monetary contraction theory would predict — when the Rentenmark was introduced and the fiscal situation was credibly reformed. The money supply continued to grow for some time after the inflation ended. The mechanism was entirely fiscal: once people believed the government would not continue to use seigniorage to finance its deficit, inflation expectations collapsed immediately.

v. disagreement with Friedman — and with the opposition

FTPL vs monetarism — velocity unstable; M1 abandoned; fiscal coordination essential; both agree cost-push insufficientI want to be clear about where I agree and disagree with my proposition colleague and with the opposition. I agree with Friedman that sustained inflation is not primarily about corporate pricing power or supply chains — these can create relative price changes but not sustained general inflation without a fiscal or monetary amplifier. Where I depart from monetarism is on the mechanism: the quantity theory's velocity stability assumption has broken down since financial innovation in the 1980s made money demand unpredictable. The Fed abandoned M1 targeting in 1982 precisely because the relationship between money supply and inflation had become unreliable. In a world where central banks set interest rates, the quantity theory loses its operational purchase. The fiscal theory provides the correct framework for this world: what determines the price level is not the money supply but the credibility of the government's intertemporal budget constraint.

Mr President,

I find myself in the unusual position of opposing a motion that my Austrian colleagues and I helped to inspire — for it was Ludwig von Mises and myself who first articulated the mechanisms by which monetary expansion generates economic distortion. But I want to argue that the motion, as framed, embeds a fundamental conceptual error that even the monetarist tradition has not corrected: the error of treating inflation as primarily a phenomenon of the general price level, to be addressed by aggregate monetary policy. The Austrian analysis of inflation is radically disaggregated: what matters is not that prices on average have risen, but that specific prices have risen in ways that falsify the information function of the price system and generate systematic malinvestment in the capital structure of the economy.

"The very concept of a 'general price level' is a statistical abstraction that conceals more than it reveals. The price system is a dispersed mechanism for coordinating information that no single mind can possess. When credit expansion injects new money into the economy through the loan market, it does not raise all prices simultaneously and proportionally. It changes relative prices — distorting the signals on which entrepreneurs depend — and it is in the resulting malinvestment, not in the statistical price index, that the real harm of inflation resides."
i. the price system as a knowledge coordination mechanism

Hayekian price system — dispersed knowledge; prices as signals; credit expansion distorts signalsIn my 1945 essay "The Use of Knowledge in Society," I argued that the price system is the only known mechanism for coordinating the dispersed, tacit, and locally specific knowledge that is distributed across millions of economic actors. No central planner — and no central bank — can possess this knowledge; it exists only in the decisions of individuals responding to price signals. This insight applies with particular force to monetary analysis. When the central bank expands credit, it lowers the interest rate below its market-clearing level. This false signal — an artificially low interest rate — tells entrepreneurs that there are more savings available to fund long-term investment than actually exist. Entrepreneurs respond by initiating more capital-intensive projects than the economy's real saving can sustain. The resulting malinvestment is not a peripheral side effect of inflation; it is its primary economic consequence — more important and more damaging than the averaging effect on the general price index that monetarists focus upon.

ii. the Austrian business cycle theory — Mises and the falsification of monetary calculation

ABCT — artificial credit expansion; below-market interest rate; capital elongation; malinvestment; inevitable bustLudwig von Mises's business cycle theory — the most rigorous formulation of the Austrian analysis — identifies the mechanism of inflation-induced distortion with precision. When banks create credit in excess of real savings and lend it at an interest rate below the natural rate, entrepreneurs engage in "monetary calculation" — the process of comparing expected returns on investment projects with the cost of capital — that systematically overestimates the profitability of capital-intensive, time-consuming production processes. Projects that appear profitable at an artificially suppressed interest rate would not be initiated at the natural rate. These projects represent malinvestment: not merely wasteful spending but a distortion of the production structure in the direction of more roundabout methods that the economy cannot support with its actual level of saving. When the artificial credit expansion must eventually be curtailed — when the rate of credit creation that was sustaining the boom slows or reverses — the malinvestments are revealed. The bust is not a failure of the market; it is the necessary and painful process of liquidating the distortions that the inflationary boom created.

iii. the general price index — a conceptual error

CPI critique — index number problem; aggregation conceals relative price distortions; Fisher's illusionThe monetarist tradition, inherited from Irving Fisher's quantity theory, takes the general price level — measured by the CPI, the GDP deflator, or some other index — as its primary object of analysis. I regard this focus as a category error of the first order. An average of prices across heterogeneous goods hides precisely the information that matters for understanding the economic consequences of monetary expansion: the changes in relative prices that determine the allocation of resources across sectors and time periods. Asset price inflation — the kind of monetary expansion that inflates equity and property prices while leaving consumer price indices relatively stable — is entirely invisible to the monetarist framework unless consumer prices happen to rise. The 2000s US housing bubble — a textbook Austrian malinvestment boom driven by artificially low interest rates — produced little measured CPI inflation while generating the largest capital misallocation in peacetime history. The monetarist framework, focused on the general price level, provided no warning.

iv. free banking and the abolition of central banking

Free banking — competitive currency; no lender of last resort; money creation limited by market discipline; gold standardMy policy conclusions differ sharply from both the monetarist k-percent rule and the FTPL's implicit acceptance of central banking as an institutional given. The root cause of inflation is not bad central banking — it is central banking itself. A central bank with monopoly authority over currency creation has no market mechanism to discipline its money creation decisions. It is inevitably subject to political pressure to inflate, to the time-inconsistency problem that makes credible commitments difficult, and to the epistemic limitations that afflict all central planning. The correct institutional remedy is competitive free banking, in which multiple private issuers of currency compete for customers and are disciplined by the convertibility constraint: over-issuing banks face note redemption by competitor banks, automatically contracting the money supply. This was the mechanism that maintained relative price stability in the Scottish free banking era of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, and it requires no central bank discretion, no k-percent rule, and no fiscal theory of price level determination.

v. against both the proposition speakers

Against Friedman and Cochrane — Austrians agree on harm, disagree on mechanism and remedy; relative prices primacyI want to be direct about my disagreements with both proposition speakers. With Friedman, I agree that monetary expansion is the primary cause of sustained inflation in the statistical sense. But his quantity theory treats inflation as a proportional increase in all prices, whereas the Austrian theory insists on the essential role of the injection point — where new money enters the economy — and the consequent distortion of relative prices. With Cochrane's fiscal theory, I note that it correctly identifies the government's fiscal position as a driver of inflation but, like monetarism, focuses on the general price level and uses the government's own fiscal apparatus as the remedy. The Austrian prescription is more radical: eliminate the institutional conditions that make systematic monetary expansion possible — central banking and fiat money — rather than trying to manage the system through better rules or fiscal coordination.

Mr President,

The motion asserts that inflation is fundamentally a monetary phenomenon with a single appropriate remedy. I want to challenge both halves of this claim — the causal diagnosis and the therapeutic conclusion — using the evidence of the 2021-23 inflation episode specifically, which the proposition treats as confirmation of its framework but which I believe reveals its decisive limitations. My argument, developed with Evan Wasner and drawing on the post-Keynesian tradition from Kalecki through Gardiner Means to contemporary conflict theory, is that the 2021-23 inflation was primarily a sellers' inflation: a microeconomic phenomenon in which firms with market power used sector-wide cost shocks as implicit coordination devices for price hikes that protected and expanded profit margins. The macroeconomic remedy — interest rate increases — was the wrong medicine for this disease, and the patients who bore its costs were not those who set the prices.

"The dominant view holds that inflation is always macroeconomic in origin and must always be tackled with macroeconomic tightening. But if the inflation is predominantly microeconomic — arising from the pricing decisions of concentrated upstream sectors that function as implicit coordination devices for price hikes across the economy — then monetary tightening attacks the wrong target. It raises the cost of credit for firms that were not part of the price-setting dynamic, and it suppresses the wages of workers who had no pricing power in the first place."
i. sellers' inflation — the microeconomic mechanism

Sellers' inflation — market power; implicit coordination; cost-shock as permission; four phasesMy research with Evan Wasner identifies a four-stage mechanism for the 2021-23 inflation. In the impulse stage, major cost shocks hit upstream, systemically significant sectors: commodity markets disrupted by pandemic supply chains, energy markets shocked by the Ukraine war, shipping disrupted by port bottlenecks. These shocks were real and large. In the propagation stage, downstream sectors used these upstream cost shocks as implicit coordination devices: when all competitors face the same cost increase, each can raise prices to protect margins without losing market share to competitors who have not raised prices. In the amplification stage — documented in my analysis of over 130,000 earnings call transcripts — firms with temporary monopoly positions or market power used the permissive pricing environment to expand margins beyond cost recovery. Finally, in the continuation stage, this dynamic became self-reinforcing: workers sought wage increases to compensate for higher consumer prices, threatening to extend the inflationary episode beyond the initial cost shock.

ii. the Kaleckian foundation — mark-up pricing and income distribution

Kalecki — mark-up pricing; income distribution; conflict inflation; inflation as distributional struggleMichal Kalecki's theory of pricing — developed in the 1930s and 1940s and largely neglected by the mainstream — provides the microeconomic foundation for sellers' inflation. In oligopolistic markets, firms set prices as a mark-up over unit prime costs: price = (1 + μ) × (unit labour costs + unit material costs), where μ is the mark-up reflecting market power. Changes in the mark-up — shifts in the degree to which firms can expand their profit share — drive inflation independently of aggregate demand conditions. This is the conflict theory of inflation: inflation is the outcome of a distributional struggle between firms seeking to expand profit shares and workers seeking to maintain real wages. The resulting inflation is not a consequence of "too much money chasing too few goods" but of competing income claims that cannot simultaneously be satisfied at existing prices. The money supply accommodates this distributional struggle — it is endogenous to it — not its cause.

iii. the earnings call evidence

Earnings calls — explicit coordination language; "pricing power"; margins above cost recovery; documented at scaleThe empirical evidence for sellers' inflation is not merely theoretical. My analysis of over 130,000 earnings calls of publicly listed US companies documents a systematic pattern: corporate executives expressed positive sentiments about economy-wide cost shocks — specifically because widespread cost shocks, unlike idiosyncratic cost shocks affecting only their firm, create a permissive environment for price increases. In normal times, price-making firms fear losing market share if they raise prices unilaterally. But when a sector-wide cost shock can be invoked as justification, competitors face the same pressure and are expected to raise prices similarly. This implicit coordination — not explicit price-fixing, which would be illegal — is documented in the language of earnings calls from 2021 onwards. Executives speak openly of "pricing power," of "taking price," of using cost inflation as an opportunity. This is not a sudden outbreak of corporate greed — it is a structural feature of pricing in concentrated markets that the pandemic and Ukraine shocks activated.

iv. the wrong medicine — interest rate hikes and their distributional consequences
