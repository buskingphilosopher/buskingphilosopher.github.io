Mr President,

The first question to ask of any political philosophy is not whether it is theoretically coherent but whether it works. By that standard, the opposition is in serious trouble — because anarchism has demonstrably, repeatedly, and in remarkably diverse contexts, worked. I am not here to describe a utopia. I am here to describe history.

On revolutionary Catalonia and Aragon

Empirical record: anarchist SpainIn Catalonia and Aragon between 1936 and 1939, anarchist workers and peasants collectively managed farms, factories, hospitals, schools, and transport systems without a state. Agricultural yields increased. Industrial output was reorganised on workers' control. Health and literacy services were extended to populations that had never received them. This was not a brief utopian experiment before the terror arrived — it was a functioning society, snuffed out not by its own contradictions but by the military force of Franco, with the enthusiastic cooperation of Soviet Communists who found anarchist success ideologically inconvenient.

On the Zapatistas of Chiapas

Contemporary stateless governanceSince 1994, the Zapatista communities of Chiapas have governed millions of people across hundreds of communities through assemblies, rotating councils, and structures explicitly designed to prevent the accumulation of power — the caracoles operating on the principle of mandar obedeciendo, to govern by obeying the community. Healthcare outcomes have improved dramatically relative to surrounding Mexican state communities. Women's participation in governance has expanded structurally. This is a functioning polity, operating for over thirty years, under conditions of hostile Mexican state pressure.

On Maroon communities and Zomia

Long-run stateless societiesThe Maroon communities of Jamaica and Suriname governed themselves for generations through consensual councils with no standing coercive apparatus. James C. Scott's work on Zomia documents how millions of people across centuries deliberately structured their societies, agriculture, kinship systems, and settlements to avoid state incorporation. The absence of the state was not a failure to develop. It was a choice — populations that had experienced state extraction, corvée labour, and conscription systematically reorganised to evade those structures, and sustained complex, peaceable communities for centuries as a result.

On mutual aid as the infrastructure of survival

Mutual aid as structural anarchismEvery major disaster of the modern period — from the San Francisco earthquake of 1906 to Hurricane Katrina to the COVID-19 pandemic — has been characterised by the same pattern. Official state and market responses are slow, hierarchical, and often actively harmful. Mutual aid networks and self-organised communities fill the gap immediately, effectively, and equitably. Human beings, absent coercive hierarchy, tend toward cooperation. The state does not produce this tendency. It suppresses it, then takes credit for the residue.

On the objection from complexity

Anarchism and complex coordinationThe opposition will say these examples are too small or too exceptional to generalise. This objection inverts the burden of proof. The state is not the default condition of human social life — it is the exception, a recent and often externally imposed institution. Non-hierarchical coordination has produced agriculture, trade, medicine, education, and justice. The hierarchy is not the solution. It is, too often, the problem it claims to solve.

Mr President,

I want to do something different from my colleague Gelderloos: I want to dismantle the ideological scaffolding on which the opposition's entire case rests. Because the case against anarchism does not primarily rest on evidence. It rests on a story — a story about human nature, prehistory, and the origins of political authority. And that story, when examined against the archaeological and anthropological record, simply collapses.

On what archaeology actually shows

The prehistory myth dismantledThe opposition's case ultimately relies on the Hobbesian picture of pre-state human life as anarchic in the pejorative sense — nasty, brutish, disordered. When David Wengrow and I compiled the evidence in The Dawn of Everything, drawing on decades of archaeology and anthropology, what we found was extraordinary: the record does not show a uniform trajectory toward hierarchy. It shows astonishing political diversity. Göbekli Tepe in Anatolia — monumental construction involving thousands of people, dating to 9,600 BCE — shows no evidence of a ruling class, no palaces, no differentiated royal burials. The builders coordinated at scale without a state. The assumption that complexity necessitates hierarchy is not a discovery. It is a projection.

On the Indus Valley and Mesoamerican republics

Prehistoric cities without hierarchyThe Indus Valley civilisation sustained cities of 40,000 people — Mohenjo-daro, Harappa — for centuries with no evidence of palaces, royal burials, or centralised coercive authority. No monuments to rulers, no mass graves of sacrificed subjects. The material record looks egalitarian. Tlaxcala in Mesoamerica was a functioning republic with council governance long before Spanish contact — its structure so impressed Cortés's allies that they described it as resembling Venice. These are not anomalies requiring special explanation. They are evidence that complex urban life has been achieved, repeatedly, without the hierarchical structures the opposition treats as preconditions of it.

On seasonal variation in governance

Deliberate institutional flexibilityOne of the most striking findings in The Dawn of Everything concerns what Wengrow and I call seasonal variation in governance: societies that were hierarchically organised during one part of the year — for communal hunts requiring coordination — and radically egalitarian during another, with explicit institutional mechanisms for switching between modes. The Kwakwaka'wakw and the Sioux both show evidence of this structural flexibility. This demonstrates not merely that non-hierarchical governance is possible, but that human beings have deliberately constructed it, maintained it, and switched into and out of it as circumstances required. Non-hierarchy was not default helplessness. It was a designed option.

On the playful diversity of human political creativity

Human political experimentationThe deeper point is about human political creativity. The standard story of political development — forager bands, then chiefdoms, then states, then modern democracies — is not a description of history. It is a retrospective imposition on a record far stranger and more varied than any such linear schema can capture. Human beings have experimented with monarchy, theocracy, council governance, rotating authority, deliberate statelessness, seasonal hierarchy, and forms that have no modern name. To say that the state is the necessary destination of political development is to mistake one outcome of many experiments for the only possible outcome.

On the real significance of the examples the opposition dismisses

Scale and archaeological evidenceThe opposition will say that prehistoric non-hierarchical societies were small — not comparable to the modern problem. But Mohenjo-daro had 40,000 inhabitants. The broader Indus network connected millions. Göbekli Tepe required the mobilisation of thousands for its construction. These are not village-scale experiments. They are civilisational achievements that postdate the industrial revolution's scale argument by eight millennia. The claim that non-hierarchical coordination is inherently limited in scale is empirically false, and The Dawn of Everything is the evidence.

Mr President,

My colleagues have examined the archaeological and anthropological record. I want to examine the philosophical mythology — what I call the prehistoric myth in modern political philosophy — through which the state and private property are represented as arising from legitimate consent rather than from conquest and enclosure. This mythology is the ideological foundation of the opposition's case. It is also false.

On the propertarian story and its function

The propertarian myth exposedThe propertarian story, traceable from Locke through Nozick, holds that private property and the state arose from a neutral baseline in which resources were unowned, and that those who first mixed their labour with unowned resources came to own them legitimately. The state then arose to protect these prior entitlements. This story serves a specific ideological function: it naturalises existing property relations and makes the dispossession of stateless peoples appear not as conquest but as the arrival of legitimate order. But as I document in Prehistoric Myths in Modern Political Philosophy, the baseline was never neutral. Pre-state peoples had resource access systems, governance structures, and sustainable relationships to land and commons that the enclosing state destroyed.

On the Hobbesian prehistory — what Hobbes did not know

The Hobbesian myth dismantled empiricallyWhen Hobbes wrote that pre-state life was solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short, he had no access to the prehistoric record. He was stipulating, not describing. We now have that record — and what it shows does not support the Hobbesian picture. Co-authored with Michael McQuade, my survey of the archaeological and anthropological evidence finds no support for uniform pre-state violence. Skeletal trauma data — our best proxy for interpersonal violence across deep history — shows no consistent elevation in pre-state populations. Many show evidence of egalitarianism, deliberate institutional design to prevent hierarchy, and sustained coordination. Hobbes was wrong because he was guessing. The anarchists are right because they have looked.

On freedom as exit: the basic income in the wild

Freedom as exit availabilityI draw a distinction that is philosophically crucial: genuine freedom is not merely the absence of formal prohibition but the presence of realistic exit options from conditions of domination. Pre-state peoples possessed what I call basic income in the wild — access to resources sufficient for subsistence without having to submit to an employer, a landlord, or a lord. This constituted real freedom as the capacity to leave. The state and private property together, across the history of enclosure and colonisation, systematically eliminated this exit option. They made submission economically compulsory. The history of the state is, in part, a history of the closing of exits.

On consent and the fiction of the social contract

The social contract was never consented toThe Hobbesian and Lockean traditions represent the state as the object of voluntary social contract — rational agents, seeing the advantages of coordination under a sovereign, consent to its authority. But no actual state arose this way. States arose through conquest, colonisation, enslavement, and the forcible absorption of populations who had not consented and who often actively resisted. To say that persons living under states today implicitly consent by remaining in them is to redefine consent as the absence of successful exit — at the very moment that the state has eliminated the exits. The circular logic of this argument would validate any sufficiently comprehensive domination as consent.

On what anarchism actually diagnoses

Anarchism as diagnosis of dominationAnarchism does not merely propose an alternative to the state. It diagnoses the state as the historical mechanism through which genuine freedom — freedom as the realistic capacity to live without submitting to domination — was eliminated. The enclosure of the commons, the criminalisation of vagrancy, the transformation of free foragers into dependent wage labourers, the privatisation of land — these were not the natural workings of economic development. They were specific political acts, carried out by states, to destroy the structural conditions under which non-submission was possible. Anarchism proposes restoring those conditions. The opposition proposes calling their elimination progress.

Mr President,

I confess that the motion before us fills me with something approaching theological alarm. We are asked to deliberate — in a parliamentary chamber, before formal authority, using written rules of procedure — on whether authority is necessary. The very form of this gathering is a refutation of its proposition.

On the executioner and the foundation of social order

Sovereignty: the irreducible foundationEvery organised society rests on the same foundation, which most prefer not to examine: the figure of the executioner. Remove from the social order the individual who, at the command of legitimate authority, imposes physical consequence on those who violate its commands, and you have not created freedom — you have created a problem no assembly can solve. Ask: what does the Zapatista council do with the murderer who refuses to submit to its verdict? What does the anarchist commune do with the man who will not leave his neighbour's property? At that moment of genuine, willed defiance — either authority asserts itself with force, or it dissolves. There is no third option.

On the pride of abstract reason

The hubris of rational reconstructionThe proposition presents plans — rotating councils, consensus assemblies, federated communes. These are the constructions of a particular kind of European intellectual who believes that because she can describe an institution in theory, it can therefore exist in practice. The French Revolution provides the reductio ad absurdum. The men of 1789 set out to reconstruct society on the basis of reason. They produced the Terror — not because they were evil, but because abstract reason, when it dissolves the accumulated wisdom of centuries, invariably produces consequences it cannot anticipate and cannot control. The Spanish anarchists governed for three years on the momentum of pre-existing institutions before being consumed. This is not evidence that anarchism works. It is evidence of how long pre-existing social wisdom can sustain a community after it has discarded the structures that produced that wisdom.

On what tradition actually is

Tradition as encoded social wisdomHierarchy is not merely the product of conquest, as the proposition suggests. It is the product of accumulated social experiment conducted over millennia — the sediment of what has worked, retained in institutions, laws, customs, and habits. When the proposition dismisses existing authority as the residue of violence, it commits a fundamental error: it assumes that because an institution has been maintained by force, it has no other justification. But almost every valuable social institution has been maintained by force at some point. The question is not whether force has been used, but whether the institution that force protects embodies genuine social wisdom. Traditional hierarchy, for all its cruelties, has survived because it solves real coordination problems that no assembly has solved better at comparable scale.

On the sacred character of authority

Authority must be sacred, not merely chosenI will say something that no contemporary academic dares to say plainly: authority is not merely useful. It is sacred. A society in which all authority is provisional — chosen, revisable, always contestable — gives every defector a rational basis for defection, making stable coordination impossible. Stable authority requires a principle that commands not because it has been chosen but because it commands — prior to rational deliberation, not derived from it. Anarchism, by rejecting all unchosen authority, removes the only foundation on which stable social order can rest. The anarchists have not abolished authority. They have merely pretended to — and called the resulting informal leadership, which invariably emerges, something else.

On the proposition's examples — a re-reading

Anarchist examples are state-parasitic enclavesThe Zapatistas operate in a region formally governed by the Mexican state, from whose shadow they derive their de facto autonomy. The Maroons purchased their peace through treaties with surrounding colonial states. The Spanish collectives lasted three years before being consumed by the civil war that was always already their context. None is a self-sufficient anarchist order. They are all enclaves within or beside states, parasitic on state structures they officially reject, or brief interludes in larger state-organised conflicts. They demonstrate not that anarchism works but that, in the shelter of state-maintained conditions, small communities can enjoy a temporary local autonomy. This is not a political theory. It is a weather forecast.

Mr President,

My colleague de Maistre has made the counter-Enlightenment case from tradition and providence. I want to make a different kind of case — the sociological case, drawn not from theology but from the study of organisations. I want to show you that anarchism fails not because of some metaphysical deficiency but because of a sociological law that has proven, across the full range of human organisation, as reliable as gravity.

On the Iron Law of Oligarchy

The Iron Law of OligarchyWriting in 1911 after studying the German Social Democratic Party — the most democratic, most committed to egalitarian principles of any mass organisation then existing — I arrived at a conclusion I found personally devastating: who says organisation says oligarchy. Every organisation, regardless of its explicit commitments, regardless of its founding principles, regardless of the idealism of its members, develops a leadership stratum that acquires interests distinct from and superior to those of its members, and that systematically resists accountability to those members. The SPD had committed itself to democracy and equality more sincerely than any previous mass political movement. Within a generation, it had an entrenched bureaucratic leadership class making decisions opaque to ordinary members. Anarchism provides no immunity to this process. It provides a different vocabulary for describing the leaders who inevitably emerge.

On elite recapture in anarchist organisations

Elite recapture in the CNTThe historical record of anarchist movements confirms the Iron Law at every turn. The Spanish CNT, the most anarchist of trade union federations, developed by 1936 a cadre of experienced militants — the cenetistas — who possessed information, contacts, organisational skills, and rhetorical authority giving them de facto power over the ostensibly leaderless organisation. When the revolution came, it was these cenetistas who made the crucial decision about whether to enter the Republican government — a decision that was, by any anarchist theory, a betrayal of principle. It was made by the very organisation committed to that principle, under pressure of events, by its de facto leadership. Every anarchist organisation that has lasted long enough to matter has developed an informal leadership class. The informality makes it less accountable, not more.

On the problem of scale

The scale problem for anarchismEvery example the proposition has offered — Zapatistas, Maroons, Spanish collectives, disaster communities — operates at small scale, in conditions of high social cohesion, often under external threat that suppresses internal defection. The relevant question for assessing anarchism as a political theory is not whether it works in a village of two hundred people with strong shared identity and an external enemy to cohere against. It is whether it works for a complex industrial society of millions of people with diverse, conflicting interests, requiring coordination across anonymous social relations, producing and distributing goods through supply chains of extraordinary complexity. No anarchist example addresses this question.

On the role of specialisation and expertise

Expertise and the emergence of informal leadershipThere is a structural reason why the Iron Law operates even in communities explicitly committed to non-hierarchy: specialisation. Any complex coordinating activity requires persons with specialist knowledge — and specialist knowledge is power. The anarchist who understands the water system, the commune member who manages the grain stores, the Zapatista militant who has negotiated with the Mexican government — these persons have information advantages that translate directly into informal authority, whether they seek it or not. The Iron Law is not primarily a story about ambition. It is a story about how information asymmetry, which is unavoidable in any complex organisation, inevitably produces informal hierarchy. Renaming it does not dissolve it.

On the real lesson of anarchist history

The real lesson: informal hierarchy is worseThe real lesson of anarchist history is not that anarchism works but that informal hierarchy is more dangerous than formal hierarchy. When the CNT cenetistas made their catastrophic 1936 decision, there was no formal mechanism for accountability, no recognised procedure for challenge, no legitimate channel for dissent. The decision was made by people whose authority was nowhere acknowledged — and therefore nowhere challengeable. Formal hierarchy, for all its defects, at least locates authority in a visible structure that can be criticised, reformed, or replaced. Informal hierarchy is invisible, unchallengeable, and permanent. Anarchism does not abolish oligarchy. It drives it underground.

I am told my prehistory has been refuted. Professor Widerquist has informed me that skeletal trauma data does not uniformly support the Hobbesian picture of pre-state violence, and that the Indus Valley had cities without palaces. I am willing, for the purpose of this debate, to concede every archaeological objection the proposition wishes to press. None of it matters to my argument. Because my argument is not historical. It is structural.

On the structure of the problem, not its prehistory

The Hobbesian argument is structural, not historicalThe state of nature is not a claim about a period of time before the invention of government. It is a claim about an incentive structure that exists whenever and wherever individuals interact without a power to overawe them. The prisoner's dilemma is not a Hobbesian fantasy. It is a structural feature of situations where cooperation produces collective benefit but defection produces individual benefit — and where the individual cannot be certain that others will cooperate. This structure is present in modern societies as clearly as it is in any imagined prehistory. The proposition has refuted an argument I never made. The argument I make — that rational individuals have structural reasons to defect from cooperative arrangements absent credible enforcement — stands untouched by any archaeological evidence.

On what the proposition's examples demonstrate about enforcement

Anarchist societies use enforcement — they just rename itLet us look closely at what actually happens in the anarchist societies the proposition celebrates. The Zapatista communities do have enforcement mechanisms — community sanctions, ostracism, restrictions on access to communal resources. The Spanish collectives did have coercive authority — those who refused to participate in collective farming faced serious social and material consequences. The Maroon communities had councils with real disciplinary powers. The proposition celebrates these communities as stateless. But what they actually demonstrate is that any community large enough to require coordination develops enforcement mechanisms. The anarchists have not abolished enforcement. They have renamed it community self-regulation and hoped that no one examines it too carefully.

On the free rider problem and its universality

Free riding and the universality of enforcement problemsThe free rider problem does not disappear in anarchist communities. It is managed — through social pressure, through reputation effects, through exclusion from communal benefits. These mechanisms work well in small communities with stable membership, shared identity, and dense social networks where reputation effects are powerful. They work very poorly in large, anonymous, heterogeneous societies where reputation is diffuse and exit is easy. The proposition's answer to the free rider problem in a city of eight million is essentially: social pressure. The Leviathan's answer is: credible enforcement. The proposition has demonstrated that social pressure works in a village. This has never been in doubt.

On why small-scale success does not generalise

Why small-scale anarchism does not scaleThere is a precise reason why anarchist solutions that work at small scale fail at large scale, and it has nothing to do with human depravity. Small communities solve collective action problems through repeated interactions among a stable membership — everyone knows everyone, defection is visible, reputation is valuable, and social sanctions are devastating. Large anonymous societies have none of these properties. Interactions are one-shot or rare. Membership is fluid. Defection is often invisible. Reputation effects are diffuse. In these conditions, social pressure cannot substitute for credible enforcement, and credible enforcement requires something that looks very much like a state.

On the genuine concession I am willing to make

The Leviathan need not be unjustI am willing to concede this: the Leviathan I described in 1651 need not be the only possible form of sovereign authority, and the proposition is right that many states have used their monopoly on violence to serve the interests of the few rather than the many. The question of what form of authority is legitimate is distinct from the question of whether authority is necessary. My argument is for the latter, not for any particular form of the former. An anarchist who persuades me that the state should be democratically accountable, constitutionally constrained, and redistributively just has not persuaded me of anarchism. She has persuaded me of a theory of legitimate state authority — a position entirely compatible with my Leviathan, and entirely incompatible with the proposition's motion as stated.

Thank you.
