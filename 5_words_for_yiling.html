Judith Butler develops the idea that identity—especially gender—is not something one simply is, but something one does. In works like Gender Trouble, she argues that repeated acts, gestures, and behaviors constitute the illusion of a stable identity, so ‘performance’ is not superficial or secondary, but the very process through which social reality is produced and maintained over time.

Edward Said critiques how the ‘East’ has been constructed in Western thought, most forcefully in his work Orientalism, where he argues that the East is less a geographical reality than a conceptual invention shaped by power, imagination, and colonial interests. For Said, the East becomes a mirror against which the West defines itself, often through stereotypes that obscure the diversity and autonomy of the societies being described.

In Leviathan, Thomas Hobbes argues that without a strong authority to marshal or order society, human life would descend into chaos. Figures of command – whether generals, marshals, or sovereigns – embody the artificial power required to impose peace and coordinate collective action in a world otherwise prone to conflict. More recently, Elinor Ostrom has pushed back on this, arguing that top-down authority is not necessary. Instead, communities can self-organise to manage shared resources. For more grassroots challenges to the Hobbesian Leviathan, there is a fantastic book called Prehistoric Myths in Modern Political Philosophy by Winderquist and McCall.

Aldous Huxley famously explored altered states of consciousness in his novel The Doors of Perception, where he suggests that psychedelic experience can temporarily dissolve the ‘reducing valve’ of the brain, allowing a fuller apprehension of reality. Rather than mere illusion, such states may reveal aspects of perception normally filtered out, raising philosophical questions about the limits of ordinary consciousness and the nature of mind. This idea was seized upon by 70s hippie radicals and later inspired Fisher and Colquhoun to write a short zine called Acid Communism in 2018, in which they wrote “We must regain the optimism of that Seventies moment, just as we must carefully analyze all the machineries that capital deployed to convert confidence into dejection. Understanding how this process of consciousness-deflation worked is the first step to reversing it.”

Wassily Kandinsky reflected on color as a spiritual and psychological language, arguing in Concerning the Spiritual in Art that colors carry inner resonances. For Kandinsky, grey suggests stillness, neutrality, or even lifelessness, while yellow radiates outward with restless, aggressive energy. When combined, the tension between grey’s quiet inertia and yellow’s vibrant force can evoke a dynamic interplay between suppression and expression, a visual metaphor for conflicting inner states.
