Cut While Shaving

Charles Bukowski — What Matters Most Is How Well You Walk Through the Fire (1999)
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
Structure & Context
Form: Free verse; no stanza breaks — the lines run as continuous, uninterrupted thought Voice: Dialogic — a reported 'he' and a responding 'I'; the two may be the same person Rhythm: Deliberately prosaic — Bukowski resists lyric elevation to keep the tone level and worn Title: Deliberately mundane — the cut is both literal (shaving) and the poem's central metaphor for everyday wounding Theme: Epistemic resignation — the awareness that nothing is ever quite right, and that awareness changes nothing Context: Published the year before Bukowski's death; the poem reads as a late-career reckoning with the inadequacy of everything, including language itself

It's never quite right, he said, the way people look, the way the music sounds, the way the words are written. It's never quite right, he said, all the things we are taught, all the loves we chase, all the deaths we die, all the lives we live, they are never quite right, they are hardly close to right, these lives we live one after the other, piled there as history, the waste of the species, the crushing of the light and the way, it's not quite right, it's hardly right at all he said. don't I know it? I answered. I walked away from the mirror. it was morning, it was afternoon, it was night nothing changed it was locked in place. something flashed, something broke, something remained. I walked down the stairway and into it.
