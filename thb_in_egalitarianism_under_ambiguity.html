Mr President,

The question before this House is whether egalitarian concerns for the distribution of welfare retain independent moral force when we face not merely risk — where probabilities can be precisely assigned — but severe uncertainty, where they cannot. I want to argue that they do, and that recognising this has profound practical implications for how we evaluate policies in health care, climate change, and any domain where the future is genuinely ambiguous.

On the distinction between risk and severe uncertainty

Severe uncertainty: probabilities cannot be assigned — this is not a technical limitation but a morally significant conditionLet me begin with the distinction that structures the entire debate. Under risk, a decision-maker can assign precise, well-grounded probabilities to all relevant outcomes. Under severe uncertainty — what economists following Knight call ambiguity — they cannot. This is not merely a technical limitation that better information would dissolve; it is a genuine and common feature of many of the most important decisions we face. When a novel medical treatment is deployed at scale, when a climate policy is enacted whose long-run interactions with economic and ecological systems cannot be fully modelled, when an intervention targets a social problem whose causal structure is incompletely understood — decision-makers face severe uncertainty. A moral theory that addresses only the risk case, as if probabilities were always available, is radically incomplete as a guide to action in the real world.

On pluralist egalitarianism

Pluralist egalitarianism: we should improve prospects, raise total welfare, and reduce inequality in both prospects and final welfare — not just maximise expected aggregateThe background moral theory we defend is pluralist egalitarianism. On this view, one ought to improve people's prospects for well-being, raise total expected welfare, and reduce inequality — both inequality in the distribution of prospects across persons before the outcome is known, and inequality in the distribution of final welfare once outcomes are realised. These are genuinely distinct moral considerations: a decision that produces higher expected aggregate welfare may nonetheless be worse than one that produces lower expected aggregate welfare but a more equal distribution of both chances and outcomes. Crucially, pluralist egalitarianism gives weight to ex ante fairness — what people can reasonably expect — and to ex post equality — what people actually end up with. Neither of these is reducible to the other, and neither is reducible to aggregate welfare.

On extending egalitarianism from risk to severe uncertainty

Extension to uncertainty: equality-aversion and uncertainty-aversion are analogous moral attitudes that reinforce each other under severe uncertaintyOur central contribution is to show that pluralist egalitarianism can be coherently extended to the case of severe uncertainty, and that when it is, something interesting happens. Both egalitarianism and appropriate uncertainty-aversion are expressions of a morally cautious attitude toward the distribution of welfare. Egalitarianism says: do not allow the gap between the best-off and worst-off to widen without sufficient justification. Uncertainty-aversion says: be especially reluctant to impose the risk of severe disadvantage on those who are already vulnerable. When we analyse the cases carefully, we find that the aims of limiting inequality and limiting the burdens of severe uncertainty are frequently congruent — both point in the same direction — and when they conflict, the conflict is principled and tractable. The framework allows us to identify, with precision, when pursuing equality requires accepting some uncertainty-cost and vice versa.

On the conflict between equality-aversion and uncertainty-aversion

Conflict cases: sometimes reducing inequality requires exposing the worst-off to more uncertainty — the framework adjudicates this principled tensionLet me be concrete about the hard cases — where our two moral concerns pull apart. Suppose we must choose between a policy that gives the worst-off a certain but modest benefit, and one that exposes them to severe uncertainty but with the possibility of a much larger benefit and a better distribution of final outcomes. Uncertainty-aversion counsels the certain option; the aim of reducing final inequality may counsel the risky one. Our framework does not pretend this tension dissolves. It insists instead that both concerns must be represented in the evaluation, that neither lexically dominates the other, and that the right answer depends on the specific magnitudes involved. This is not a weakness of the framework — it is an honest acknowledgement of the genuine moral complexity of decisions under uncertainty, which no single-value approach can faithfully represent.

On the utilitarian Pareto argument against egalitarianism

Pareto for Prospects: if egalitarianism violates Pareto under risk, it must also violate it under uncertainty — but uncertainty-aversion shows this Pareto principle is not compellingThe most important anti-egalitarian argument we address is what we call Pareto for Prospects: if every person has a more valuable prospect under alternative A than under alternative B, then A is better. This principle, endorsed by many utilitarians, seems to show that equality concerns cannot override unanimous individual preference. But we demonstrate that if uncertainty-aversion is morally permissible — and we argue it is — then utilitarians must relinquish this argument. An uncertainty-averse agent may rationally prefer B even when A gives better expected prospects, because A exposes people to a more uncertain distribution of outcomes. If uncertainty-aversion can permissibly override Pareto-for-Prospects for the individual, the same structure applies socially. The utilitarian's favourite weapon against egalitarianism is blunted by the very framework that the utilitarian must accept for individual decision-making under uncertainty.

On practical implications for health and climate policy

Practical implications: novel treatments and climate policies involve severe uncertainty — egalitarianism under uncertainty gives distinctive, practically significant guidanceThe practical import of our framework is not merely theoretical. Consider the evaluation of a novel medical intervention: the expected benefit for the average patient may be positive, but the distribution of outcomes across patients with different backgrounds and vulnerabilities is genuinely uncertain. A utilitarian framework, maximising expected aggregate welfare, would approve the intervention if expectations are positive. Our framework asks the further question: is the distribution of uncertain outcomes across persons equitable? Are the worst-off bearing a disproportionate share of the uncertainty-burden? These questions have no purchase within a purely aggregative framework. They are the questions that egalitarian concern for the distribution of welfare — extended to uncertainty — makes audible. A complete evaluation of health or climate policy must hear them.

On why the opposition cannot simply appeal to expected utility

Against expected utility as the complete moral framework: severe uncertainty breaks expected utility theory's own foundations — morality cannot shelter behind a broken toolLet me close with a challenge to the opposition. The standard utilitarian response to all questions of distributive justice under uncertainty is: maximise expected aggregate welfare, computed by assigning subjective probabilities to outcomes and summing welfare-weighted by probability. But this response presupposes that precise probabilities are available — that the decision-maker faces risk, not severe uncertainty. Under genuine uncertainty, expected utility theory as a decision procedure is radically underdetermined: different probability assignments, each equally permissible given the evidence, yield different expected values. The opposition cannot appeal to a framework that is itself indeterminate at the very cases our debate concerns. If any moral framework is to govern decisions under severe uncertainty, it must be one that takes the absence of precise probabilities seriously as a morally significant condition — and that, we submit, is precisely what our egalitarian framework does.

I — Severe uncertainty is a morally significant condition, not a technical gap to be elided
1.Severe uncertainty — where precise probabilities cannot be assigned to outcomes — is a genuine and common feature of major policy decisions, not a temporary epistemic limitation.
2.Moral frameworks that are defined only for the risk case (where probabilities are available) are radically incomplete as guides to action under severe uncertainty.
3.A complete moral theory of distributive justice must address severe uncertainty directly, as a condition with its own distinctive normative structure.
∴The distinction between risk and severe uncertainty is not merely technical — it marks a morally significant condition that existing frameworks (including simple expected utility maximisation) fail to address; egalitarianism must be extended to cover it.
II — Pluralist egalitarianism: equality in prospects and in final welfare are independent moral demands
1.Pluralist egalitarianism holds that we ought to improve prospects, raise total welfare, and reduce inequality — both ex ante (in the distribution of prospects) and ex post (in the distribution of final welfare).
2.These three considerations are genuinely distinct: a policy may raise expected aggregate welfare while worsening ex ante or ex post inequality; each dimension independently matters.
3.A purely aggregative framework that collapses all these considerations into expected total welfare fails to represent the full moral structure of distributive choices.
∴Pluralist egalitarianism captures moral demands that pure aggregation cannot — the independent weight of equality in both prospects and outcomes means that distributive concerns cannot be dissolved into welfare maximisation.
III — Equality-aversion and uncertainty-aversion are structurally analogous and frequently congruent
1.Egalitarianism expresses aversion to inequality in the distribution of welfare across persons; uncertainty-aversion expresses aversion to the imposition of ambiguous outcomes on those who are vulnerable.
2.Both attitudes reflect a morally cautious concern for those who may end up worst off — they are structurally analogous and frequently point in the same direction.
3.The extension of egalitarianism to severe uncertainty is thus not an ad hoc modification but a natural development of the same underlying moral concern.
∴Egalitarianism and uncertainty-aversion reinforce each other as moral attitudes — their congruence is not coincidental but reflects a shared underlying concern for those most exposed to harm, supporting the extension of the framework to the severe uncertainty case.
IV — Conflict between equality and uncertainty-aversion is principled and tractable, not a reductio
1.Cases arise where reducing inequality requires exposing the worst-off to more uncertain outcomes, creating genuine tension between the two moral concerns.
2.This tension is principled — each concern has an independent moral basis — and its resolution depends on the specific magnitudes and distributions involved, not on lexical priority of one over the other.
3.A framework that represents both concerns and adjudicates their conflict is more adequate than one that suppresses either — moral complexity is not a defect to be eliminated but a feature to be captured.
∴The existence of conflict cases between equality-aversion and uncertainty-aversion is not a reductio of the framework — it is evidence of the framework's fidelity to the genuine complexity of distributive choices under uncertainty.
V — Uncertainty-aversion defeats the utilitarian Pareto-for-Prospects argument against egalitarianism
1.The utilitarian argument against egalitarianism under risk relies on Pareto for Prospects: if A gives every person a better prospect than B, A must be chosen over B.
2.But if uncertainty-aversion is morally permissible — as there are good reasons to accept — an agent may rationally prefer B over A when A imposes severe uncertainty even at the cost of better prospects.
3.The same structure applies at the social level: if individual uncertainty-aversion can permissibly override prospect-dominance, so can social uncertainty-aversion combined with egalitarian concern.
∴If uncertainty-aversion is permissible — a premise the utilitarian must accept for individual choice — then the utilitarian's Pareto-for-Prospects argument against egalitarianism fails; the opposition must relinquish this favourite weapon against distributive concern.
VI — Practical implications: egalitarianism under uncertainty gives distinctive guidance for health and climate policy
1.Novel medical treatments and climate policies are paradigm cases of severe uncertainty, where expected benefits are speculative and the distribution of outcomes across persons is unknown.
2.Expected-utility maximisation approves such policies if aggregate expectations are positive, but cannot ask whether the distribution of uncertain outcomes across persons is equitable.
3.Egalitarianism under severe uncertainty raises the question of equitable distribution of uncertainty-burdens — whether the worst-off bear a disproportionate share of ambiguous risk — which is both practically significant and invisible to aggregative frameworks.
∴Our framework gives practically distinctive guidance that expected utility maximisation cannot — it demands that the distribution of uncertainty-burdens across persons be evaluated, not merely the expected aggregate — making visible what pure aggregation renders invisible.
VII — Expected utility theory is itself indeterminate under severe uncertainty — it cannot serve as the opposition's safe harbour
1.Expected utility maximisation requires precise probability assignments to outcomes — it is well-defined only under risk, not under severe uncertainty.
2.Under genuine uncertainty, different probability assignments — each permissible given the evidence — yield different expected values and different recommendations; the framework is radically indeterminate.
3.The opposition cannot appeal to expected utility maximisation as the correct framework precisely at the cases where our debate is located — doing so would presuppose the resolution of the debate in its own favour.
∴Expected utility theory is not the neutral baseline against which our egalitarian framework is evaluated — it is itself inapplicable under severe uncertainty; any adequate moral framework must address the uncertainty case directly, as egalitarianism under severe uncertainty does.

Mr President,

My approach to this debate is somewhat different from that of my colleagues Rowe and Voorhoeve. I want to press the case for equality under uncertainty not primarily from the perspective of pluralist egalitarianism but from a prior question: the nature of fairness itself. I believe that fairness — the proper response to competing claims — generates requirements that are independent of the maximisation of aggregate welfare, and that these requirements have distinctive implications for how we should distribute chances under uncertainty.

On fairness as a distinct moral consideration

Fairness is irreducibly distinct from goodness: it is not merely a component of total welfare but a separate moral consideration with its own structureIn my 1991 book and the surrounding work, I distinguish two components of the moral evaluation of social choices: goodness and fairness. Goodness is, roughly, the aggregated welfare of the persons affected — the thing utilitarians seek to maximise. Fairness is something different and irreducibly distinct: it concerns how well a decision responds to the competing claims of the persons involved. These two components do not have a common currency. Fairness cannot be cashed out as a contribution to aggregate welfare — it requires equalising, not maximising. A perfectly efficient allocation that ignores one person's strong claim in favour of another's weaker claim is unfair, even if the aggregate welfare is higher. This distinction is not merely terminological. It marks a genuine structural difference in how morality works — and the opposition, in collapsing everything into aggregate welfare, systematically misrepresents this structure.

On claims and the role of lotteries

Claims and lotteries: when claims are equal, a fair lottery that gives each person an equal chance is morally required — not merely instrumentally usefulConsider the allocation of a scarce indivisible good — a kidney, a ventilator — to one of two persons with equally strong claims. Aggregate-welfare maximisation says: give it to whoever will benefit more. Fairness says something different: when claims are equal, the response to those claims should itself be equal. A lottery that gives each person an equal chance of receiving the good is not merely a practical device for breaking ties — it is a morally required response to the equality of claims. Each person's claim is given equal weight in the procedure. The lottery satisfies a moral demand that direct allocation to the higher-benefit recipient does not, even if it produces the same expected aggregate welfare. Uncertainty — the uncertainty of the lottery outcome — is not a defect in this arrangement; it is constitutive of its fairness.

On equality under uncertainty and the ex ante / ex post distinction

Ex ante vs ex post equality: fair distribution of chances (ex ante) is morally significant even when ex post outcomes are unequalThe distinction between ex ante and ex post perspectives is crucial here, and it cuts directly against the utilitarian position. Utilitarians who evaluate social choices by their ex post outcomes — by what actually happens — dismiss the fairness of the process that generated those outcomes as morally irrelevant. But I want to insist that ex ante fairness — the fair distribution of chances before outcomes are realised — is a genuine moral demand, not merely an expression of risk-aversion. When we give each person an equal chance, we treat each person as equally important in the decision, regardless of who happens to benefit. The ex post perspective, which sees only winners and losers, misses this dimension entirely. A theory of justice must represent both dimensions — and this requires exactly the kind of multi-criterion evaluation that the proposition endorses.

On the utilitarian position on equality under uncertainty

Utilitarian incoherence under uncertainty: the utilitarian cannot simultaneously endorse expected utility theory and deny the moral significance of the distribution of chancesThere is a structural incoherence in the utilitarian position on equality under uncertainty that I want to expose. The utilitarian values individuals' welfare and evaluates policies by expected aggregate welfare — the probability-weighted sum of welfare across persons and states. But this evaluation already incorporates probability-weighting at the individual level: it treats each person's welfare in each state as having weight proportional to the probability of that state. If the utilitarian accepts that probability-weighting is appropriate at the individual level, it is arbitrary to deny that the distribution of probabilities across persons matters morally. Why should the probability-weighting of a single person's welfare across states be morally significant, but the distribution of the resulting expected values across persons be morally irrelevant? The utilitarian has no consistent answer to this question.

On the structure of fairness under severe uncertainty

Severe uncertainty reinforces fairness demands: when probabilities are unknown, the case for giving each person's claim equal procedural weight is strengthenedRowe and Voorhoeve focus on the extension of egalitarianism to severe uncertainty. I want to reinforce that argument from the direction of fairness. When probabilities can be precisely assigned, a utilitarian can at least argue for the expected-welfare framework as a determinate guide to choice. Under severe uncertainty, even this determinacy is lost. The utilitarian who insists on expected-welfare maximisation must first decide which probability assignment to use — and under genuine uncertainty, no single assignment is privileged. In this context, the case for a fairness-based approach that focuses on the distribution of claims and the equitable treatment of competing interests — rather than on expected aggregate welfare — is reinforced. Fairness does not require probability assignments; it requires sensitivity to the claims of persons, which is precisely what the utilitarian approach systematically discounts.

On the levelling-down objection

Levelling-down: fairness does not require equalising by levelling down — a better distribution at lower total welfare is not always preferableI should confront the levelling-down objection honestly. Critics of egalitarianism — including Parfit — argue that if equality is intrinsically valuable, then levelling down — making the better-off worse without improving the worse-off — should be an improvement, which is a morally repugnant conclusion. I agree that levelling down is not an improvement: it makes some people worse without making anyone better. But this does not show that equality is morally irrelevant. It shows that fairness is one component of evaluation and that the badness of levelling down — the loss of welfare with no compensating gain — outweighs the fairness gain from equalisation. Both components matter; neither alone determines the verdict. This pluralist structure is precisely what the proposition defends: equality and aggregate welfare are both genuine moral considerations, and their relative weight must be assessed in context.

On Diamond's objection to expected utility aggregation

Diamond's objection: a utilitarian social welfare function that satisfies expected utility axioms cannot also give independent weight to the distribution of chances — fairness requires denying one of Harsanyi's axiomsLet me close with the technical argument that I believe is decisive against the utilitarian position. Peter Diamond showed in 1967 that a social welfare function cannot simultaneously satisfy all the axioms of expected utility theory and give independent moral weight to the distribution of chances across persons. Harsanyi's aggregation theorem, which the opposition will invoke, proves that if individual utility functions and the social welfare function all satisfy expected utility axioms, the social welfare function must be a weighted sum of individual utilities — pure utilitarianism. But Diamond's objection shows that this conclusion requires accepting that the distribution of chances across persons has no independent moral weight — it is invisible to the utilitarian social welfare function. If we accept, as I do, that the distribution of chances does matter morally — that fairness is a genuine consideration — then we must reject one of Harsanyi's axioms. The proposition's framework does this consistently; the opposition does not.

Mr President,

The proposition asks us to accept that egalitarian concerns for distribution should constrain social choice over and above the maximisation of expected aggregate welfare. I want to argue the contrary — that expected utility maximisation is not one approach among several competing moral frameworks but the uniquely rational basis for social choice under uncertainty, and that egalitarian constraints on top of it are either redundant or irrational.

On the aggregation theorem and its ethical significance

Aggregation theorem: if individual and social preferences satisfy expected utility axioms and a Pareto condition holds, the social welfare function must be a weighted sum of individual utilitiesMy 1955 aggregation theorem establishes a result of fundamental importance for this debate. If individual preferences satisfy the von Neumann–Morgenstern axioms of expected utility theory, if the social preference ordering also satisfies these axioms, and if a Pareto condition holds — that the social ordering is consistent with unanimous individual preference — then the social welfare function must take the form of a weighted sum of individual utilities. This is not an empirical claim about how people happen to behave; it is a structural result about what rationality requires of social choice. Any social welfare function that departs from this form — any framework that gives independent weight to distributional concerns over and above the welfare of individuals — must violate either the expected utility axioms or the Pareto condition. The proposition's egalitarian framework is one such departure. It must therefore either be irrational by the lights of expected utility theory, or paternalistic by the lights of Pareto.

On the impartial observer and the utilitarian conclusion

Impartial observer: a rational, impartial observer who gives equal weight to every person's interests under a veil of ignorance will maximise expected aggregate welfare, not pursue equalityMy impartial observer theorem provides an independent route to the same conclusion. Imagine an ethical decision-maker who is required to be impartial — to give equal weight to every person's interests. We can model this impartiality by imagining the decision-maker behind a veil of ignorance: uncertain about which person in the society they will turn out to be, they must choose principles that serve them equally well in any position. If this impartial observer is rational — if their choices satisfy expected utility axioms — and assigns equal probability to being each person, they will maximise expected aggregate welfare. This is not a contingent result but a consequence of rationality under impartiality. The proposition's egalitarianism, by contrast, gives extra weight to the worse-off beyond what their welfare warrants — it is not impartial but systematically biased toward certain outcomes. Rawls was right to use the veil of ignorance device; he was wrong to pair it with the irrational maximin rule rather than expected utility maximisation.

On Pareto and why egalitarianism violates it

Pareto violation: any egalitarian constraint that can override unanimous individual preference for an alternative is a paternalistic imposition on the persons it purports to benefitThe Pareto principle — that if every person prefers alternative A to alternative B, A should be chosen — is not a controversial utilitarian assumption; it is a minimal condition of respect for persons. To override unanimous individual preference in the name of equality is to treat the persons involved as means rather than ends — to say that what matters is not their welfare but some abstract property of the distribution of their welfare. The proposition claims that egalitarian constraints can override Pareto-for-Prospects. But Pareto-for-Prospects is not merely a technical condition — it says that if every person has a better prospect under one option, that option should be preferred. To reject this in the name of distributional equality is to say that the distribution matters more than the welfare of the persons distributed. This is not a more sophisticated concern for persons; it is a departure from concern for persons in favour of concern for patterns.

On the nature of severe uncertainty and why it does not rescue egalitarianism

Severe uncertainty does not rescue egalitarianism: subjective expected utility theory handles uncertainty via subjective probabilities — there is no special moral significance to the absence of objective probabilitiesThe proposition makes much of the distinction between risk and severe uncertainty, arguing that the latter creates a special domain where egalitarianism has distinctive purchase. I dispute the moral significance of this distinction. Subjective expected utility theory — the Savage framework — shows that a rational agent facing genuine uncertainty can represent their beliefs as subjective probability assignments and maximise expected utility with respect to these. The absence of objective probabilities does not create a vacuum in which expected utility theory fails; it creates a domain in which subjective probabilities must do the work. A rational social decision-maker facing severe uncertainty should therefore identify their best subjective probability assignments and maximise expected aggregate welfare accordingly. The proposition's egalitarian framework is not a response to the failure of expected utility — it is an unnecessary addition to a framework that already handles uncertainty.

On why maximin is irrational under severe uncertainty

Maximin is irrational: it focuses only on the worst possible outcome and ignores all other information — it would choose absurd options to avoid tiny probabilities of the worst caseThe proposition's framework, like Rawls's maximin before it, is in the grip of an excessive fear of the worst outcome. Maximin — and the uncertainty-aversion it embodies — says: choose the option whose worst possible outcome is best, regardless of the probability of that outcome or the magnitude of the foregone benefits from alternatives. This produces manifestly irrational recommendations. It would advise a decision-maker to accept a certain minor harm to avoid an infinitesimal probability of a catastrophic but slightly worse harm — even if the expected cost of doing so vastly exceeds the expected harm avoided. A framework that ignores probabilities — or gives insufficient weight to them — cannot be a sound basis for social choice. The proposition's uncertainty-aversion is a sophisticated version of this irrationality dressed in more careful language.

On Broome's fairness argument and the Diamond objection

Responding to Diamond: the Diamond objection shows a tension but the resolution is to accept that fairness of chances is captured within expected utility, not added as a separate constraintBroome invokes Diamond's 1967 objection: that a social welfare function satisfying expected utility axioms cannot give independent weight to the distribution of chances. I accept the formal point. But I reject the conclusion Broome draws from it. Diamond's result shows that accepting the expected utility axioms at the social level rules out independent weighting of the process by which outcomes are generated — it makes only outcomes, not procedures, morally relevant. I believe this is correct. The fairness of a lottery is not a separate moral good; it is an expression of our uncertainty about outcomes and our inability to discriminate between claimants. A lottery is fair precisely because we cannot identify who should receive the benefit on outcome-relevant grounds. Once the outcome is known, the lottery has served its purpose and the distribution of chances is morally spent. The proposition inverts this: it treats the process as having value independent of outcomes, which confuses moral psychology with moral philosophy.

On the utilitarian's honest acknowledgement of distributive concerns

Utilitarian accommodation of distribution: diminishing marginal utility already builds distributional concern into the utilitarian framework without adding egalitarian constraintsLet me close by acknowledging what is correct in the proposition's concern. A sophisticated utilitarian does not simply maximise the sum of raw welfare; they maximise the sum of utility, where utility is a concave function of welfare. Because of the declining marginal utility of income and welfare, the utilitarian already gives extra weight to improving the situation of the worse-off — not because of any egalitarian principle, but because a unit of welfare is worth more to someone who has less of it. This generates a built-in distributional sensitivity that tracks the egalitarian's concern in most cases. What the utilitarian cannot accept is the proposition's additional, independent egalitarian constraint that operates even when it conflicts with the welfare of the persons involved. That constraint adds nothing to the moral calculus that diminishing marginal utility does not already provide — and it adds the irrationality of Pareto violations on top.

I — Aggregation theorem: rational social choice under uncertainty must be utilitarian
1.If individual preference orderings satisfy the expected utility axioms, the social preference ordering satisfies the expected utility axioms, and a Pareto condition holds, the social welfare function must be a weighted sum of individual utilities.
2.Any social welfare function that gives independent weight to distributional concerns — as egalitarianism does — must violate either the expected utility axioms or the Pareto condition.
3.Both violations are serious: violating the expected utility axioms is irrational; violating Pareto is paternalistic — it overrides unanimous individual preference.
∴The aggregation theorem shows that any egalitarian constraint on social choice beyond weighted utility maximisation is either irrational or paternalistic — there is no principled middle ground between expected utility maximisation and violation of rationality or Pareto.
II — Impartial observer: rational impartiality under uncertainty yields expected utility maximisation, not egalitarianism
1.An impartial observer who assigns equal probability to being each person and maximises their own expected utility will choose the alternative that maximises expected aggregate welfare.
2.This result follows from rationality plus impartiality — it does not require any additional utilitarian premises; the impartial observer theorem is not an argument for utilitarianism but a derivation of it from more basic conditions.
3.Egalitarianism, by giving extra weight to the worse-off beyond what their welfare warrants, is not impartial — it is systematically biased toward certain distributional outcomes.
∴Rational impartiality under uncertainty yields expected utility maximisation — egalitarianism is not a more thorough concern for persons but a departure from impartiality in favour of a distributional preference that has no basis in persons' own interests.
III — Pareto violation: overriding unanimous preference for equality is paternalism, not concern for persons
1.Pareto for Prospects — if every person has a better prospect under A than B, prefer A — is a minimal condition of respect for persons' expressed preferences.
2.The proposition claims egalitarian concerns can override this principle — accepting a worse prospect for everyone in the name of distributional equality.
3.This prioritises abstract distributional properties over the actual welfare of the persons in the distribution — concern for patterns over concern for persons.
∴Egalitarian constraints that override Pareto are paternalistic — they substitute a preference for distributional equality for the actual preferences and welfare of the persons the theory purports to benefit.
IV — Severe uncertainty does not create a domain beyond expected utility: subjective probabilities fill the gap
1.Subjective expected utility theory (Savage) extends expected utility to genuine uncertainty by representing beliefs as subjective probability assignments, making expected utility maximisation well-defined even when objective probabilities are unavailable.
2.The absence of objective probabilities does not create a moral vacuum in which egalitarianism has special purchase — it creates a domain in which subjective probabilities must be used.
3.A rational social decision-maker facing severe uncertainty should identify defensible subjective probability assignments and maximise expected aggregate welfare — egalitarian constraints add irrationality to this process, not insight.
∴Severe uncertainty does not rescue egalitarianism — subjective expected utility theory provides a rational framework for decisions without objective probabilities; the proposition's egalitarian framework is an unnecessary and distorting addition to an already adequate decision procedure.
V — Maximin and uncertainty-aversion are irrational: they ignore probabilities and produce absurd recommendations
1.Maximin — choose the option whose worst outcome is best — ignores the probability of the worst outcome and the magnitude of benefits foregone from alternatives.
2.This can produce manifestly irrational recommendations: accepting a certain significant harm to avoid an infinitesimal probability of a marginally worse outcome.
3.The proposition's uncertainty-aversion is a sophisticated version of maximin — it systematically underweights the probability and magnitude of foregone benefits in favour of avoiding worst-case scenarios.
∴Uncertainty-aversion of the kind the proposition endorses is irrational — it ignores morally and practically relevant information about probabilities and magnitudes; a sound framework for social choice under uncertainty must be sensitive to all relevant decision-theoretic information.
VI — Diamond's objection is real but the resolution favours expected utility, not independent process-value
1.Diamond's result shows that a social welfare function satisfying expected utility axioms cannot give independent weight to the distribution of chances — process and outcome cannot both matter independently.
2.The correct resolution is to accept that only outcomes — not the processes generating them — have independent moral weight; the fairness of a lottery is an epistemic response to uncertainty, not a source of value independent of outcomes.
3.The proposition's conclusion — that Diamond's result requires rejecting expected utility axioms — presupposes that process-value is genuine; but this is precisely what is at issue, and the utilitarian denies it.
∴Diamond's objection does not vindicate the proposition — it presents a choice between accepting expected utility axioms (and denying independent process-value) or accepting independent process-value (and rejecting the axioms); the utilitarian consistently accepts the former.
VII — Diminishing marginal utility already builds in distributional sensitivity without egalitarian constraints
1.Sophisticated utilitarian maximisation uses concave utility functions, giving extra weight to improving the welfare of the worse-off through diminishing marginal utility — generating built-in distributional sensitivity.
2.This distributional sensitivity tracks egalitarian concern in most cases without requiring any additional constraint beyond welfare maximisation.
3.The proposition's egalitarian constraint adds nothing to the moral calculus that diminishing marginal utility does not already provide — except Pareto violations and irrational recommendations in cases where the constraint conflicts with unanimous individual preference.
∴The utilitarian's concave utility function already captures the core distributive concern motivating egalitarianism — additional egalitarian constraints are redundant in the standard cases and irrational in the cases where they deviate; they add error, not insight.

Mr President,

I want to approach this debate from a position that is neither the proposition's egalitarianism nor Harsanyi's utilitarianism. I believe both positions are mistaken about what fundamentally matters in distributive justice. The proposition is right that aggregate welfare maximisation is not the whole story — but wrong about what the rest of the story is. It is not equality that matters; it is priority.

On the levelling-down objection to telic egalitarianism

Levelling-down: if equality is intrinsically valuable, making the better-off worse without improving anyone is an improvement — this is morally repugnant and reveals a flaw in telic egalitarianismConsider the levelling-down objection. If equality is intrinsically valuable — if the world is made better by more equal distributions of welfare, regardless of the absolute levels involved — then a world in which everyone is equally badly off is, to that extent, better than a world in which some are well off and others badly off. This is repugnant. It means that if we could reduce the welfare of the better-off without doing anything for the worse-off, we would have made the world better in one respect — the respect tracked by equality. But this makes no sense. Making people worse off without making anyone better off makes the world worse in every morally relevant sense. If a principle implies that levelling down is an improvement, that principle is false. Telic egalitarianism — the view that equality is intrinsically good — fails this test.

On the distinction between telic and deontic egalitarianism

Telic vs deontic egalitarianism: inequality may be unjust without being intrinsically bad — the deontic view avoids levelling-down but at the cost of applying only when injustice is caused by agentsOne response to the levelling-down objection is to move from telic to deontic egalitarianism. On the telic view, inequality is intrinsically bad as a state of affairs. On the deontic view, inequality is not intrinsically bad but may be unjust when it results from the wrong actions of agents. Deontic egalitarianism avoids the levelling-down objection: it is not a claim about the impersonal value of equal distributions but about the obligations of agents. But this retreat has costs. Deontic egalitarianism applies only where inequalities are caused by wrongdoing — it has nothing to say about naturally occurring inequality, about unequal outcomes that result from no one's fault. And in the case of severe uncertainty, where the inequalities of outcome may be entirely the result of impersonal processes rather than agent-caused decisions, the deontic framework provides no grip. The proposition's framework straddles these views in ways that are not fully specified.

On the priority view as the superior alternative

Priority view: it is more urgent to benefit the worse-off, not because equality matters but because their gains matter more — this avoids levelling-down while preserving distributional concernThe priority view holds that benefits to the worse-off matter more — are given greater moral weight — not because this reduces inequality but because those who are worse off have stronger claims on our moral concern. This is fundamentally different from egalitarianism. The egalitarian cares about relativities — about how each person's level compares with others'. The prioritarian cares about absolutes — about how well off each person is — but gives extra weight to those at lower absolute levels. This distinction has a decisive practical consequence: the priority view does not suffer from the levelling-down objection. Making the better-off worse without improving the worse-off does not generate any moral improvement under prioritarianism — there is no gain in priority-weighted welfare from making anyone worse off. The priority view therefore captures the distributional concern that motivates egalitarianism without inheriting its most serious defect.

On priority under uncertainty

Priority under uncertainty: the priority view applies under uncertainty by weighting welfare gains by the absolute welfare level of recipients — this generates distributional concern without egalitarian commitmentsHow does the priority view apply under the severe uncertainty that is the subject of this debate? The extension is natural. Under uncertainty, we should give greater weight to welfare gains accruing to those who are worse off — where "worse off" is assessed at their absolute level, not relative to others. This generates a form of uncertainty-sensitivity that prioritises those with the most at stake: those who are already badly off and whose situation is worsened by uncertainty-exposure. This is distributional concern without egalitarian relativity. Moreover, the priority view under uncertainty does not face the internal tension that the proposition's framework faces between equality-aversion and uncertainty-aversion — the two aims are unified in the single principle that worse-off people's welfare gains matter more, whether those gains are under risk, uncertainty, or certainty.

On the separateness of persons and aggregation

Separateness of persons: both utilitarianism and egalitarianism fail to adequately represent the separateness of persons — the priority view does better by focusing on individual absolute welfare levelsThe deepest objection to simple expected utility maximisation — the one that motivates egalitarianism — is that it treats persons as mere locations of welfare to be summed, without adequately respecting their separateness. A gain to A can always in principle compensate for a loss to B, regardless of their respective welfare levels. This is a genuine defect: it fails to respect the fact that there is no person who experiences both A's gain and B's loss, and therefore no sense in which the sum of benefits outweighs the loss from A's perspective. But egalitarianism does not solve this problem — it replaces one form of welfare-aggregation with another, adding concern for comparative distributions rather than abandoning the aggregative framework. The priority view, by giving extra weight to benefits at lower absolute levels, better captures the separateness intuition: it takes seriously that those who are worse off have stronger independent claims, not merely that they occupy a disadvantaged position in a comparative distribution.

On equality or priority under the veil of ignorance

Veil of ignorance argument: behind the veil, the priority view and egalitarianism come apart — rational self-interest behind the veil supports priority, not equality as suchRawls's veil of ignorance has been invoked as a framework for thinking about what principles a rational person would choose if they did not know their position in the distribution. Harsanyi claims this framework supports expected utility maximisation. I want to point out that it supports the priority view more naturally than it supports egalitarianism. Behind the veil, what I fear is not that others will be better off than me — I do not know who will be better off — but that I will be badly off in absolute terms. The rational response to this fear is to choose principles that give extra weight to improving the situation of the worst-off, whatever that situation is. This is the priority view. The egalitarian principle — reduce the gap between better-off and worse-off — gives me something I do not care about from behind the veil: I do not care about the gap; I care about my absolute level. Priority reasoning captures what the veil of ignorance argument actually motivates.

On what egalitarianism under severe uncertainty gets right and wrong

Conclusion: Rowe and Voorhoeve are right that distributive concerns survive severe uncertainty, but they mis-identify the concern as equality rather than priorityLet me state clearly what I accept and what I reject in the proposition's position. I accept that expected utility maximisation is not the complete story — distributional concerns have independent moral weight that the utilitarian aggregation framework cannot capture. I accept that these concerns survive and indeed are reinforced by severe uncertainty. I accept that egalitarian and utilitarian frameworks diverge significantly in their practical recommendations, and that where they diverge, utilitarianism is often wrong. What I reject is the identification of the relevant distributive concern as equality. Rowe and Voorhoeve are right that something beyond aggregate welfare matters under uncertainty. But that something is priority — the extra weight we should give to benefits for those who are worse off in absolute terms — not equality, which cares about relativities and thereby generates the levelling-down objection. The correct framework for distributive justice under severe uncertainty is prioritarianism, not egalitarianism.

∴Telic egalitarianism fails the levelling-down test — if it implies that making people worse off without helping anyone is an improvement in any morally relevant sense, the principle generating that implication must be rejected.
