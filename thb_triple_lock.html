Mr President,

The Institute for Fiscal Studies has spent years making economic arguments that politicians find uncomfortable. I am accustomed to that. But I want to be clear at the outset that the case against the triple lock is not ideological hostility to pensioners, not a desire to cut public spending for its own sake, and not indifference to pensioner poverty. It is a straightforward economic analysis of a mechanism that is irrational, arbitrary, unsustainable, and — in the long run — self-defeating. Defending it on behalf of today's pensioners, without attention to what it does to tomorrow's workers and taxpayers, is not fairness. It is intergenerational favouritism dressed up as protection.

On what the triple lock actually does — and what it costs

£11bn/year premium: the triple lock costs £11bn annually more than earnings indexation — a large untargeted transfer with no defined endpointLet me begin with the numbers, because they are stark and they are frequently understated in public debate. The triple lock commits the government to uprate the state pension each year by the highest of earnings growth, consumer price inflation, or 2.5 percent. Since its introduction in 2011, this mechanism has cost around eleven billion pounds per year more than simple earnings indexation would have done. The new state pension is now worth approximately £204 per week — around eleven percent more than it would have been under either price or earnings indexation alone. And the OBR's fiscal sustainability analysis is unambiguous: under the triple lock, state pension spending is projected to rise from around 5 percent of GDP today to 7.8 percent by 2070. In a high-volatility macroeconomic scenario, it could reach 9.1 percent — close to a quarter of all tax revenues spent on a single benefit. These are not small numbers. They represent a structural commitment that compounds over time, with no defined destination, no target level of adequacy, and no mechanism for correcting itself when the pension overshoots any reasonable benchmark.

Irrational mechanism: the triple lock produces an arbitrary pension level with no principled target — it is a maximum function, not a policyLet me be direct about something that economists agree on but that rarely penetrates the political debate: the triple lock is not a coherent policy. It is a political formula dressed up as a commitment to fairness. The state pension should be set at a level that is adequate, sustainable, and maintains a reasonable relationship with earnings over time. What level should that be? The triple lock cannot tell you. It produces whatever level happens to emerge from the interaction of three unrelated variables — wage growth, inflation, and an arbitrary 2.5 percent floor — in any given year. The pension level in 2040 is unknowable, because we cannot predict which of the three components will dominate in each of the next fifteen years. The IFS Pensions Review has documented how this uncertainty makes planning — both for individuals trying to assess retirement income and for government trying to forecast public finances — extremely difficult. A mechanism that produces a random walk upward, with no correction and no target, is not fiscal policy. It is fiscal drift.

On the ratchet and the long-run trajectory

The ratchet: the triple lock always takes the maximum of three — over time this compounds upward with no mean-reversion mechanismThe triple lock has a structural feature that makes it fundamentally different from either price or earnings indexation: it ratchets upward. When you take the maximum of three variables year after year, the result over time is systematically higher than any one of them alone. If wages grow faster than inflation in some years, and inflation grows faster than wages in others, earnings indexation would produce a roughly stable real pension. The triple lock captures the upside of both without any downside correction. There is no year in which the pension falls in real terms, no year in which it falls relative to earnings — and when earnings growth falls below 2.5 percent and inflation is also below 2.5 percent, the pension rises in real and relative terms regardless of what is happening to the economy that funds it. The consequence, documented by the Work and Pensions Select Committee, is that the basic state pension has risen from about 16 percent of average full-time earnings when the triple lock was introduced to around 25 percent today — and without reform it will continue rising indefinitely. I am not aware of any serious policy analysis that has established that 35 or 40 percent of average earnings is the right target for the state pension. We are drifting toward those levels without having chosen them.

On the pension age trade-off — the hidden tax on younger workers

Pension age trade-off: maintaining the triple lock forces pension age increases — the cost falls on younger cohorts, especially the low-paid and unhealthyThe OBR has been explicit about this: if the triple lock is maintained, fiscal sustainability requires accelerating increases in the state pension age. The IFS has calculated that keeping the triple lock on current trajectories would require raising the state pension age to 69 by 2048 and 74 by the late 2060s. These are not abstract projections — they describe the retirement age facing today's thirty and forty-year-olds. Here is the distributional injustice that the triple lock's defenders consistently ignore. Raising the pension age is not a neutral way of controlling pension costs. It falls most heavily on those with the shortest healthy life expectancy — manual workers, those in poor health, those in deprived regions — who are least likely to reach an elevated pension age. A 74-year-old pension age is not an inconvenience for the knowledge worker at a comfortable desk; it is unachievable for the construction worker whose back has been destroyed by thirty years of physical labour. The triple lock, by making itself financially necessary to offset with pension age increases, is systematically transferring income from those who cannot work longer to those who can and will receive a generous inflation-beating pension.

On what should replace it — the smoothed earnings link

Smoothed earnings link: the IFS proposes a target-based system linking the pension to a share of median earnings — rational, sustainable, and adequateThe proposition is not simply destructive. There is a principled alternative. The IFS and the Pensions Policy Institute have both proposed variants of a smoothed earnings link — a system that sets a target level for the state pension as a defined share of median earnings, and then uprates toward that target at the rate of earnings growth. In years when inflation exceeds earnings, the inflation component protects real value. But there is no 2.5 percent floor, and there is no ratchet that permanently overshoots. This approach has three advantages over the triple lock. First, it defines a destination: we can debate whether 28 or 30 or 33 percent of median earnings is the right target, but at least we are having the right debate. Second, it is predictable: government, individuals, and pension planners can project the state pension forward with reasonable confidence. Third, it is fiscally controllable: state pension spending remains bounded as a share of GDP, rather than drifting upward without limit. Australia uses a version of this system. The UK should too.

On intergenerational fairness — who benefits and who pays

Intergenerational transfer: current pensioners receive a large untargeted subsidy; young workers face high taxes, unaffordable housing, and poor private pension prospectsThe United Kingdom's intergenerational budget imbalance is, on OBR analysis, approximately £7.6 trillion — future generations are inheriting net liabilities over five times annual GDP. People aged 61 to 79 today are projected to withdraw over £220,000 more from the system than they contributed. Today's infants face contributing thousands more than they will ever receive. In this context, the triple lock is not a neutral mechanism for protecting the vulnerable. It is a large, untargeted transfer from younger cohorts — who face stagnant real wages, unaffordable housing, high rents, and inadequate private pension provision — to older cohorts, including a significant proportion of affluent pensioners who do not need the extra income. The Winter Fuel Payment was at least cut from universal to means-tested. The triple lock remains universal, benefiting wealthy asset-owning pensioners as much as the genuinely poor. A pound of triple lock expenditure directed to a retired homeowner with a final salary pension and property wealth of half a million pounds is not poverty relief. It is a political bribe to the most reliable voting demographic in the country.

On the political economy — why no party will say it, and why someone must

Political economy: the triple lock is sustained by electoral geography, not economic logic — no party will reform it, but the fiscal consequences are accumulatingI want to end with a point about political economy that embarrasses all parties equally. The triple lock is not sustained by economic analysis. It is sustained by the fact that pensioners vote in disproportionate numbers, that they are geographically concentrated in marginal seats, and that every party has concluded that the electoral cost of proposing reform is higher than the fiscal cost of perpetuation. This is the definition of a policy that exists to serve politicians rather than beneficiaries. The IEA, the IFS, the OBR, the Intergenerational Foundation, and the Pensions Policy Institute have all, in various ways, concluded that the triple lock cannot be sustained permanently. Even the architects of the mechanism — as we will hear from my colleague on the opposition — have accepted that it was designed as temporary medicine, not permanent policy. The National Insurance Fund is projected to be exhausted in the mid-2040s. The OBR projects debt exceeding 270 percent of GDP in the absence of reform. The triple lock is not a protection for pensioners — it is a fiscal time bomb wrapped in a pledge card, and the person left holding it will be a future Chancellor explaining to the country why the state pension age must rise to 73.

II — The irrationality of the mechanism: the triple lock has no principled target — it is a maximum function producing a random walk upward
1.The triple lock uprates by the maximum of three unrelated variables — wage growth, CPI inflation, and 2.5% — producing a pension level that is the outcome of a statistical coincidence rather than a policy choice about adequacy.
2.The future pension level is genuinely unknowable: it depends on which variable dominates in each future year, making planning impossible for individuals and the public finances.
3.No serious policy analysis has defined a target share of earnings that the state pension should reach — the mechanism drifts toward arbitrary levels without political or analytical endorsement of those levels.
∴The triple lock is irrational as a policy instrument — it produces results that nobody chose, cannot be evaluated against any benchmark, and creates uncertainty for savers, planners, and the fiscal authorities that is entirely avoidable.
III — The ratchet: taking the maximum of three variables compounds upward with no mean-reversion — the pension rises as a share of earnings without limit
1.The maximum function ensures the pension captures upside from any of the three variables in any given year, with no compensating downside in years where all three are low.
2.The state pension has risen from ~16% of average full-time earnings when the triple lock was introduced to ~25% today; on current trajectories it will continue rising indefinitely without any political decision to stop.
3.There is no year in which the triple lock produces a real or relative decline — it is structurally biased upward, unlike earnings or price indexation which mean-revert around a stable share of national income.
∴The ratchet structure of the triple lock means it cannot be sustained indefinitely — it will either be reformed by choice or produce a fiscal crisis that forces reform under worse conditions; choosing the former now is straightforwardly better public policy.

2.Raising the state pension age is not distributionally neutral: healthy life expectancy varies by up to 20 years across UK regions; pension age rises fall most heavily on manual workers and those in deprived areas who are least likely to survive to an elevated age in good health.
3.The triple lock therefore generates a systematic distributional transfer — generous, universal uprating for current pensioners at the cost of later retirement for the least healthy and least advantaged future cohorts.
∴The pension age trade-off reveals the true distributional logic of the triple lock — it is not a pro-poor policy but a mechanism that transfers from poorer younger cohorts to all current pensioners, including the affluent, at a fiscal cost ultimately borne by those with the least.
V — The smoothed earnings link alternative: a target-based system that is rational, predictable, and fiscally controllable
1.A smoothed earnings link sets the state pension as a defined share of median earnings — say 30% — and uprates toward that target at the rate of earnings growth, with an inflation floor protecting real value in years of wage stagnation.
2.This defines a destination (a policy-chosen adequacy level), produces a predictable trajectory, and bounds state pension spending as a share of GDP — none of which the triple lock achieves.
3.Australia uses a variant of this system successfully; the IFS Pensions Review and the Pensions Policy Institute have both endorsed this approach as the rational replacement for the triple lock.
∴The smoothed earnings link is a technically and politically superior alternative — it achieves genuine adequacy at a definable level, protects real value, and gives governments, individuals, and fiscal authorities the predictability the triple lock denies them.

3.Younger cohorts facing stagnant real wages, unaffordable housing, inadequate private pension provision, and rising tax burdens bear the cost of a mechanism that transfers to a cohort that, on average, has substantially more wealth than they do.
∴The triple lock is intergenerationally unjust — a large, untargeted transfer from asset-poor younger cohorts to all pensioners including the wealthy; genuine concern for pensioner poverty would target expenditure on the genuinely poor, not provide universal above-inflation uprating to all.

3.Deferring reform exacerbates intergenerational injustice and increases the eventual adjustment cost; the optimal time to reform is before fiscal compulsion forces a disorderly change.
∴The political economy of the triple lock exemplifies the fiscal time-bomb problem: costs accumulate silently, no party has the courage to act, and the eventual reform under fiscal pressure will be cruder and more painful than a considered reform now.

Mr President,

I spent most of my career defending pensioners. I was appointed Pensions Minister specifically because of my long record of advocacy for older people's financial security. So when I say that the triple lock has fulfilled its purpose and now needs to be reformed, I am not speaking from ideological hostility to the elderly or from indifference to poverty. I am speaking from experience of what the policy was designed to achieve, what it has achieved, and what it is now doing that was never intended.

On what the triple lock was designed to do — and what it has achieved

Mission accomplished: the triple lock was a corrective mechanism designed to recover lost ground — it has done that, and continuing it is now overshooting the original objectiveThe triple lock was introduced in 2010 as a corrective mechanism. Margaret Thatcher broke the earnings link in 1980. For three decades thereafter, the state pension was indexed primarily to prices, which meant it fell steadily as a share of earnings. By 2010, the basic state pension was worth just under 16 percent of average full-time earnings — a historic low. Pensioner poverty was widespread. The triple lock was designed to address this: to recover the ground lost over three decades and to give pensioners a period of above-inflation, above-earnings growth in their pension income. I supported it for exactly this reason. But a corrective mechanism is only appropriate while the condition it is correcting persists. When Jeremy Hunt said in 2024 that thanks to the triple lock pensioners were now less likely to be in poverty than other age groups, he was confirming the mechanism had achieved its central purpose. The question is no longer whether pensioners need catch-up protection. The question is whether the mechanism designed for a specific corrective purpose should continue indefinitely after the correction has been made.

On the 2.5% floor — the most economically unjustifiable element

2.5% floor: the minimum guarantee has no economic rationale — it uprates pensions even in deflation, is arbitrary, and was never defended on principled groundsI have always been clear that the 2.5 percent minimum is the most indefensible element of the triple lock. It has no economic rationale whatsoever. It was included as a political sweetener — a marketing figure that sounded reassuring — not as the output of any analysis about what the state pension should be relative to earnings or prices. Consider what it implies in practice. If we entered a period of deflation — which is not a remote possibility in an economy subject to deflationary shocks — the triple lock would require the state pension to rise by 2.5 percent in real terms even as the general price level fell and as workers' wages declined in nominal terms. Pensioners' real living standards would improve not because they deserved more but because a political formula required it. Philip Booth of the IEA put it with characteristic precision: it is "crazy to have a system by which the pension rises by the greater of three different figures and it just leads to a totally arbitrary level of the state pension." The double lock — the higher of earnings and inflation — retains all of the genuine protection against both rising prices and falling relative living standards, while removing the arbitrary minimum that has no connection to any economic variable.

On the distributional reality — who benefits from the triple lock today

Distributional problem: the triple lock is universal — it delivers equal proportional increases to the wealthiest pensioners and the poorest, making it poor value as poverty reliefI want to press a point about distribution that the opposition frequently elides. When Age UK argues that the triple lock must be preserved to protect the poorest pensioners, they are making a category error. The triple lock is not targeted poverty relief. It is a universal proportional uprating that gives the same percentage increase to a pensioner in Kensington with a final salary pension, property wealth, and investment income as it gives to a pensioner in Barnsley relying entirely on the state pension. If the objective is to protect the genuinely poor elderly — those for whom the state pension is their primary or sole income — then spending the same resources on that objective through enhanced Pension Credit, targeted uprating for those receiving only the state pension, or better take-up of existing means-tested benefits would achieve it far more efficiently. We are spending the equivalent of eleven billion pounds more per year than earnings indexation would cost, and we are distributing a substantial portion of that money to people who do not need it, in order to avoid the political difficulty of distinguishing between pensioners who do and do not need enhanced protection.

On the political construct — and the consequences of constructing it

Political construct: the triple lock is maintained by political calculation, not economic analysis — and this has corrupted the pension policy debateI had the unique and uncomfortable experience of being a Pensions Minister who tried to reform the triple lock from within government, and was blocked. I proposed a simple change: replace the triple lock with a double lock — the higher of earnings and inflation — removing only the arbitrary 2.5 percent floor. This was a modest, technically defensible reform that would have saved billions while retaining all genuine protection against inflation and earnings erosion. David Cameron refused. Not because the reform was economically wrong — it is economically right — but because he had decided, for reasons that were entirely electoral, that the triple lock was a political promise that could not be unpicked without cost. That experience crystallised for me what the triple lock actually is: a political construct, a totemic policy that is easy for politicians to trumpet — "we will always protect pensioners" — but from a pure policy perspective keeping it forever makes no sense. The corruption of pension policy by electoral calculation has meant that we are now facing a fiscal trajectory that the entire economics establishment considers unsustainable, and yet no party in Parliament will say so clearly to voters.

On the alternative — what targeted protection looks like

Targeted alternative: genuine poverty protection for the elderly poor is better achieved through Pension Credit, improved take-up, and earnings indexation for the new state pensionLet me be concrete about what the proposition's alternative looks like in practice. The new state pension, introduced in 2016, should be indexed to earnings going forward — preserving its value relative to working incomes, which is the relevant benchmark for intergenerational fairness. For those on very low incomes — the genuinely poor elderly — Pension Credit should be uprated more generously, and crucially, its take-up should be dramatically improved: currently around a third of eligible pensioners do not claim it, representing billions of pounds of uncollected entitlement. The Winter Fuel Payment, stripped of its universal character, should be restored on a means-tested basis to those in genuine need. And we should be honest about the pension age: it should rise in line with healthy life expectancy — not because we want to punish older workers, but because the alternative is an ever-rising tax burden on younger workers who cannot afford it. This is not cruelty to the elderly. It is the only way to run a pension system that remains fair across generations and fiscally solvent over time.

On women, the gender pensions gap, and the limits of the triple lock as a solution

Gender pensions gap: the triple lock is a blunt instrument for addressing women's retirement poverty — targeted measures are both more effective and more efficientThe opposition will invoke the particular vulnerability of women in retirement — and they are right to do so. Women are disproportionately likely to have gaps in their National Insurance record, shorter working lives due to caring responsibilities, lower lifetime earnings, and inadequate private pension savings. Three times more women than men in single-pensioner households are primarily dependent on the state pension. This is a serious injustice that deserves a serious policy response. But the triple lock is a remarkably blunt instrument for addressing it. It gives the same proportional increase to the wealthy woman with a public sector pension and a late husband's survivor benefits as it gives to the woman who took twenty years out to care for children and parents and has nothing but the state pension. Targeted interventions — enhanced NI credits for carers, auto-enrolment reform to include the lowest-paid part-time workers, better survivor benefits — would do more for the genuinely vulnerable women the opposition is concerned about, at a fraction of the cost of blanket universal uprating.

On what honest politics requires — a final word

Honest politics: the triple lock is maintained by political cowardice — reform requires telling voters a truth they do not want to hearThe proposition asks something politically demanding: that we make policy based on evidence and long-term sustainability rather than on the preferences of the most reliable voters. Every serious economist and independent fiscal institution in the country has concluded that the triple lock, as currently constituted, cannot be sustained permanently. Every politician knows this. And yet every party has run on a platform of protecting it, because the electoral cost of honesty is immediate and the fiscal cost of continued delay is invisible — until suddenly it is not. The history of fiscal irresponsibility is littered with the corpses of policies that everyone knew were unsustainable but that nobody had the courage to reform until crisis forced their hand. The triple lock is heading in the same direction. The time to reform it is now, when reform can be gradual, principled, and fair — not in a decade's time, when a fiscal emergency forces a disorderly and much more painful adjustment. I joined the Conservatives because I believed in responsible public finance. I would urge the House to vote for the proposition not as an act of hostility to pensioners, but as an act of seriousness about the country's long-term fiscal future.

Mr President,

I am in the unusual position of being the person who designed the policy under attack, and of having to defend it to a House that includes several people whose economic credentials I respect enormously. I want to be honest upfront: I agree with much of the IFS analysis. The triple lock, as a permanent mechanism, is not the right long-run policy. I have said so publicly, and I am on record as supporting a smoothed earnings link as the eventual destination. But the question before this House is not whether to maintain the triple lock forever. The question is whether to abolish it now — before the adequacy case for its continuation has been met — and whether the proposed alternatives are ready and credible. On both questions, I believe the proposition has not made its case.

On the starting point — what the triple lock was correcting

Three decades of erosion: Thatcher broke the earnings link in 1980 — the state pension fell from 26% to 16% of earnings over thirty years; the triple lock was medicine for this diseaseThe proposition's case for abolition treats the current level of the state pension as though it were the result of excessive generosity. Let me put the historical record straight. When Margaret Thatcher broke the earnings link in 1980, the basic state pension was worth approximately 26 percent of average full-time earnings — an inheritance from the post-war settlement that represented a serious floor against poverty in old age. Over the three decades that followed, as the state pension was indexed only to prices, it fell steadily to around 16 percent of average earnings. Meanwhile, workers' real living standards rose substantially. The result was that pensioners — people who had worked and contributed all their lives and were now entirely dependent on fixed incomes — fell further and further behind the rest of society. Gordon Brown's 75p weekly increase, in the year the Millennium celebrations cost billions, became the symbol of how far policy had drifted from political seriousness about retirement income. The triple lock was not excessive generosity. It was medicine for a disease that had been accumulating for thirty years. And the proposition is now arguing that we should stop taking the medicine before we have recovered.

On the inadequacy of the UK state pension by international standards

International comparison: the UK state pension is among the lowest in the OECD as a share of earnings — the case for adequacy is still unfinishedThe proposition repeatedly invokes fiscal sustainability, which is a legitimate concern. But it glosses over a parallel question that is equally important: is the state pension adequate? The IFS Pensions Review confirms that the new state pension is now worth about 25 percent of mean full-time earnings. The OECD average replacement rate for state pensions is around 40 percent of earnings. France pays around 60 percent. Germany around 42 percent. The Netherlands around 80 percent. Even the United States, which is hardly a model of generous social provision, pays Social Security at a net replacement rate considerably higher than the UK's. Despite thirteen years of the triple lock, the UK state pension remains among the lowest as a proportion of earnings in the developed world. For the large share of UK pensioners who have inadequate or no private pension — those who were never auto-enrolled, who spent years in self-employment, caring roles, or low-paid part-time work — the state pension is it. It is their income in its entirety. Telling these people that the triple lock has done enough — when they are receiving one of the lowest state pensions in the developed world — is a position that requires substantial justification.

Earnings indexation is necessary but not sufficient: in periods of stagflation or wage stagnation, it provides no real protection — pensioners need an inflation floorI want to press a technical point about earnings indexation that the proposition underplays. The proposition recommends replacing the triple lock with a smoothed earnings link. I have, as I noted, expressed sympathy for this as an eventual destination. But a pure earnings link is not the right replacement now, for an important reason. In periods of stagflation — when nominal wages are growing slowly while prices are rising faster — earnings indexation leaves pensioners with a falling real standard of living. The 1970s and the post-2008 period both demonstrated that real wage growth can be sharply negative for extended periods. Pensioners on fixed incomes who cannot work more hours, switch to better-paid jobs, or move to cheaper areas are especially exposed to this risk. The triple lock's inflation floor is not an arbitrary add-on. It is a genuine protection against the specific risk that pensioners face, which is distinct from the risk facing workers: they cannot respond to falling real wages by working harder, negotiating a rise, or changing employer. The opposition's position is not "keep the triple lock forever" — it is "do not remove the inflation protection before you have designed a system that provides equivalent security."

On timing — why now is the wrong moment to reform

Timing argument: the UK pension system is in a period of transition — auto-enrolment has not yet matured, private pension adequacy is still insufficient for millions, and removing the triple lock before that matures risks genuine povertyThe proposition treats the decision to abolish the triple lock as though it exists in isolation from the broader pension landscape. It does not. The UK pension system is in a structural transition. Auto-enrolment, introduced in 2012, has brought millions of workers into private pension saving who were previously excluded. But auto-enrolment does not reach the lowest-paid, does not reach many self-employed workers, has minimum contribution rates that most financial analysts consider insufficient for an adequate retirement income, and will not produce mature accumulated pots for another two to three decades. The cohort of workers currently approaching retirement in their 50s and 60s spent their entire working lives without the benefit of auto-enrolment. For these workers — and for the women who took career breaks, and for those in self-employment — private pension savings are limited or non-existent. Removing the triple lock before this transition has produced adequate private pension pots for the generation currently approaching retirement is to cut the floor from under people who have no other safety net. The timing of reform matters enormously, and the proposition has not engaged with it.

On what "adequate" means — the target question the proposition has not answered

The adequacy question: the proposition proposes a smoothed earnings link without defining the target pension level — this is a policy void dressed as a planThe proposition proposes replacing the triple lock with a smoothed earnings link targeting a defined share of median earnings. I support this in principle. But I want to press the proposition on the number: what share? The IFS Pensions Review has done careful work on this. The current new state pension of around 25 percent of mean full-time earnings is below any serious estimate of what constitutes an adequate retirement income for those without significant private pension savings. The commission on minimum income standards suggests an adequate income for a retired person requires something closer to 35 to 40 percent of median earnings when private savings are limited. If the proposition's smoothed earnings link targets 25 percent — the current level — it simply freezes the current inadequacy in place. If it targets 30 or 35 percent, it implicitly accepts the argument that the state pension needs to continue rising as a share of earnings — and we should have a debate about that target, not pretend that switching from a triple lock to an earnings link is a neutral technical change that avoids difficult decisions about adequacy.

On the intergenerational fairness argument — and its limits

Intergenerational fairness cuts both ways: younger workers are also future pensioners — and cutting the state pension now creates a worse deal for them in retirementThe proposition invokes intergenerational fairness — the argument that current pensioners are taking more than their fair share at the expense of younger cohorts. This is a real issue and I do not dismiss it. But I want to press on who "younger workers" actually are. Many of them — those without adequate auto-enrolment coverage, those in insecure employment, those with caring responsibilities, those who can never afford to own property — are going to be entirely or substantially dependent on the state pension in their own retirement. When the proposition recommends cutting the generosity of state pension uprating in the name of protecting younger workers, it is doing so in a way that also cuts the state pension that those younger workers will themselves eventually receive. Intergenerational fairness is not straightforwardly served by reducing the state pension — it depends on whether the alternative uses the savings to benefit younger cohorts in ways they will actually experience, rather than simply reducing a liability that shows up on fiscal forecasts but translates into real poverty for those with no private pension backup.

On the opposition's conclusion — a smoothed transition, not immediate abolition

Transition, not abolition: maintain the triple lock until the state pension reaches a defined adequacy target, then switch to a smoothed earnings link — this is the responsible centre groundLet me state clearly where I think the responsible policy is. The triple lock should be maintained until the state pension reaches a defined adequacy target — something like 30 to 33 percent of median full-time earnings — and then replaced with a smoothed earnings link that maintains the pension at that level in real terms, with an inflation floor. This approach defines a destination. It gives pension savers, advisers, and the government a clear trajectory. It maintains the inflation protection that pensioners genuinely need. And it ends the open-ended ratchet at the point where adequate pension income has been secured, rather than before it. I would add: any reform should be legislated well in advance, with transitional provisions, and should be accompanied by the other structural reforms the pension system needs — better Pension Credit take-up, higher minimum auto-enrolment contributions, and pension age rises tied to healthy life expectancy rather than to crude age thresholds. The proposition offers abolition now. The opposition offers a responsible transition to a more sustainable system on a timetable that does not leave millions of the most vulnerable pensioners behind.

I am going to bring this debate back to its human dimension, because I think it risks being conducted entirely at the level of GDP percentages and OECD comparisons while losing sight of the actual people whose lives are determined by the policy we are discussing. At Age UK, we hear from those people every day. I want to make sure that their experience is represented in this House, alongside the economic arguments that have been made with great sophistication by those on both sides.

On the reality of pensioner poverty — not an abstraction

Real poverty: 2 million pensioners in poverty — the triple lock makes a measurable difference to the most vulnerable; abolition before adequacy risks pushing them deeperTwo million pensioners in the United Kingdom are living in poverty. Not near poverty. Not technically below a threshold that may be imperfectly defined. In poverty — unable to adequately heat their homes, struggling to afford regular meals, cutting back on medications because of the cost. A further three in ten pensioners — in our own recent polling — report financial difficulties that materially affect their daily lives. And this is after thirteen years of the triple lock. The proposition asks us to accept that the triple lock has done its job, that the mission is accomplished, that pensioners are no longer disproportionately poor. I want to put the question another way: if two million pensioners are in poverty after thirteen years of above-inflation uprating, what would the position have been without it? And — crucially — what would it be if we removed it now, before the structural reforms to Pension Credit and private pension provision that the proposition gestures toward but does not commit to have actually been delivered? Policy reform in practice rarely matches the clean alternatives that are drawn on whiteboards by economists. The risk of abolishing the triple lock before the replacement safety net is actually in place is not a remote one. It is a near-certainty, and the consequences would fall on the people least able to bear them.

On who actually depends on the state pension

Who depends: 1.2 million pensioner households wholly dependent on the state pension — women, low-paid workers, carers, the self-employed — are the specific people this debate is aboutThe proposition's case treats pensioners as a relatively homogeneous group of comfortable, asset-owning individuals who do not need the extra money that the triple lock delivers. I want to describe who actually depends on the state pension. Around 1.2 million pensioner households are wholly dependent on the state pension and benefits as their only sources of income. These are disproportionately women — particularly women who took years out of paid employment for caring responsibilities and have no or minimal occupational pension. They are people who spent careers in low-paid work with no access to good workplace pensions. They are former self-employed workers for whom private pension saving was financially unviable. They are people who reached retirement age with no savings, no property, and no one to depend on. For these people — a significant and vulnerable minority of the pensioner population — the difference between the triple lock's above-inflation uprating and simple earnings indexation is not an abstraction about OECD averages. It is the difference between a pension that keeps pace with the rising cost of food, energy, and housing, and one that falls behind it in any year where inflation exceeds wage growth.

On the Pension Credit take-up problem — why the proposition's alternative is not available

Pension Credit failure: one-third of eligible pensioners do not claim Pension Credit — a targeted alternative to the triple lock requires solving this first, and there is no credible plan to do soThe proposition offers us a clean alternative: replace the universal triple lock with enhanced, targeted Pension Credit for the genuinely poor. I want to examine this alternative carefully. Pension Credit is already available. Around a third of pensioners who are eligible for Pension Credit do not claim it — a take-up failure representing billions of pounds of uncollected benefit and genuine poverty among people who qualify for support but do not receive it. This is not a new problem. It has been identified by every government for twenty years. Outreach programmes, simplified application processes, cross-referencing of data — every intervention has been tried, to limited effect. The reasons for non-take-up are deeply structural: stigma, complexity, the assumption by many elderly people that they do not qualify, cognitive difficulties with form-filling, reluctance to disclose personal finances. The proposition says: replace universal uprating with targeted Pension Credit. But the targeted mechanism already exists and already fails to reach a third of those who need it. Abolishing the triple lock before the take-up problem is solved would push hundreds of thousands of people who should receive Pension Credit — but do not and will not, for documented structural reasons — into deepening poverty. That is not a theoretical risk. It is a predictable consequence of a reform that has not built the delivery mechanism it requires.

On energy costs and the specific cost pressures on older people

Energy and housing costs: older people on fixed incomes face specific, unavoidable cost pressures — the triple lock's inflation protection addresses the specific cost of living in old ageThe proposition makes much of the triple lock's cost and very little of the specific cost pressures that pensioners face and that justify above-average income protection. Older people in the UK spend a substantially higher proportion of their income on energy than working-age people — older homes, reduced mobility, and the health need for warmth mean that energy costs are not discretionary for most pensioners in the way they are for younger households. The CPI index used to measure inflation underweights housing costs for older people, who are more likely to own outright but face large maintenance costs, and underweights energy costs relative to the actual spending patterns of the very old. The triple lock's 2.5 percent floor, the element the proposition finds most objectionable, was specifically designed to ensure that in years where official inflation figures underrepresent the actual cost pressures on older people, the pension still rises. The proposition replaces this with a formula that will produce a smaller pension in any year where official CPI falls below 2.5 percent, regardless of whether the specific cost basket of the most vulnerable elderly has actually fallen. This is not a neutral technical change.

On the fiscal argument — reframed from the other direction

Fiscal reframing: the cost of the triple lock must be compared to the cost of inadequate pensions — social care, NHS costs, poverty interventions all rise if the state pension falls behindLet me reframe the fiscal argument the proposition deploys, because it considers only one side of the ledger. The proposition totals the cost of the triple lock and presents it as a pure expenditure burden. But pension adequacy has fiscal consequences too, which the proposition ignores. Inadequate pension income drives up demand for social care as people cannot afford to maintain their homes or manage their health. It drives up NHS costs as cold homes, poor nutrition, and untreated conditions produce higher rates of hospitalisation. It drives up Housing Benefit costs as older people on inadequate incomes cannot meet housing costs. It generates social care spending as poverty-driven isolation and poor mental health manifest in service demand. The IFS correctly identifies the cost of the triple lock premium over earnings indexation. It has been less assiduous in quantifying the fiscal consequences of inadequate state pension income — the downstream costs that arise when millions of older people on fixed incomes fall behind rising living costs. A full fiscal accounting would look at both sides of this equation.

Three times more women than men in single-pensioner households are primarily dependent on the state pension. Women's median pension wealth is dramatically lower than men's — the gender pensions gap means women reach retirement with an average of 35 percent less private pension savings than men. Ethnic minority pensioners are more likely to have worked in sectors without good workplace pensions and to have lower private savings. These groups cannot wait for the long-term structural reforms that the proposition proposes. They cannot hold on while auto-enrolment matures over the next twenty years. They are retired now, or approaching retirement now, and the question of whether their pension keeps pace with prices and earnings is a question about whether they eat and heat adequately in their seventies and eighties. I do not accept that we should tell these people to accept a smaller pension now, in exchange for a promise of better targeted benefits that have already failed to reach a third of those who qualify for them.

On the opposition's conclusion — hold the line, reform through adequacy targets

Age UK's position — and my own — is this: maintain the triple lock while working to address its genuine flaws through a managed transition. Legislate a defined adequacy target for the state pension — say 30 to 33 percent of median earnings — and commit to a smoothed earnings link with an inflation floor once that target is reached. Reform at the right time, with the right safety net in place, would have the opposition's support. Reform now, before the safety net exists, would have devastating consequences for people who have no other option.
