'The Poison Tree' by William Blake

Close Analysis

The following poem belongs to Blake's The Songs of Experience, and I've had it pinned on my bedroom wall for years.

The first thing you notice about this poem, as with any poem, is its structure on the page. Analytically, it consists of 4 quatrains of AABB rhyme scheme in trochaic tetrameter.

The first stanza sets up the theme of the entire poem. If you harbour hiden feelings without expressing them directly, they will build up and consume you. If you're "angry with" someone, tell your "wrath" and it will "end." When the speaker of this poem "told it not," his "wrath did grow." The rest of this poem documents how those unacknowledged feelings grew and eventually lead to murder.

A closer reading of this first stanza unconvered 3 things to me.

1. Anaphoric repetition of "I"
"I was angry… I told… I waterd… I sunned…"

The relentless first-person subject foregrounds the speaker's agency and self-absorption. The cultivated wrath becomes an expression of ego unchecked by community or compassion — the "I" is both gardener and executioner, implicating the speaker in the foe's death.

2. Friend / foe antithesis
"angry with my friend… angry with my foe"

The binary opposition in the opening quatrain functions as a moral fable's thesis statement. Communication resolves anger with the friend; suppression with the foe corrupts the self. Blake implies that the distinction between friend and foe is socially constructed — both begin with identical anger, and it is only secrecy that creates the monster.

3. Polyptoton: "wrath"
"I told my wrath, my wrath did end… my wrath did grow"

The triadic repetition of "wrath" within four lines traces a transformation from noun-of-confession to noun-of-cultivation. The word itself does not change, but its context shifts from release to nourishment — suggesting that the emotion is neutral and it is the speaker's choice of action that renders it deadly.

The rest of the poem sustains an allegorical conceit in which repressed anger is a tree tended with deliberate effort. The agricultural register ("waterd," "sunned," "grew," "bore") implies that hatred requires cultivation, that it is not natural but manufactured. This echoes Blake's critique of institutionalised repression elsewhere in The Songs of Experience, notably in the poem 'London' where harm is systemic and chosen, not inevitable.

The paradox of nurturing your anger ("waterd it in fears") and smiling deceitfully ("sunned it with smiles") collapses the distinction between care and cruelty. "Sunned" is particularly disturbing since sunlight, connoting warmth and life, is revealed to be the outward performance masking inward malice. The sibilance of "sunned… smiles… soft… deceitful" creates a hissing undercurrent of menace beneath the pleasant surface. The word "sunned" also breaks with the regular iambic tetrameter with the omission of an unstressed beat (DUM-dee-DUM-DUM-dee-DUM), breaks the reader's expectation of meter and awkwardly foregrounds the word, adding to the feeling of discomfort.

The proliferation of soft sibilant sounds across stanzas 2 and 4 ("sunned… smiles… soft… deceitful… stole… see") creates an aural impression of serpentine cunning. The hissing quality mirrors both the biblical serpent in Eden and the speaker's own duplicity. The sound enacts the deception the poem describes.

Linking stanzas 2 and 3 are the paired opposites of "Night & morning" and "both day and night". Together they signify all time, without respite, producing a meristic effect. The wrath never sleeps, its cultivation is total and unrelenting. This also reinforces the poem's cyclical, inescapable logic: there is no moment in which the poison is not growing.

The past simple tense verb "stole" in "And into my garden stole" is semantically ambiguous. It operates on two registers simultaneously: (i) to enter furtively (trespass) and (ii) to steal (theft). The foe is both thief and victim, guilty of covetousness yet manipulated into it. The ambiguity implicates Blake's broader critique of a society that criminalises desire while creating the conditions that produce it.

The choice of archaic diction with "beheld" in "And my foe beheld it shine" carries the weight of biblical and epic register. This implies his foe does not merely look at the apple but has a moment of revelation. It echoes the serpent offering Eve the forbidden fruit to "behold." The elevated diction ironically dignifies a scene of entrapment, underscoring the moral horror through aesthetic distance.

The Edenic constellation of "apple bright", "garden" and "tree" invokes the Fall of Man and its attendant themes of forbidden knowledge, temptation, and death. Crucially Blake inverts the myth: here it is not God but the speaker who plants the deadly tree, implicating human psychology — not divine punishment — as the source of evil. The foe plays Adam, while the speaker plays both serpent and God.

The "pole" (the North Star, a symbol of fixed moral orientation and navigation) is veiled by night, suggesting that the foe acts in a moral darkness and without guidance. But so too does the speaker. Both have lost the pole-star of honest communication. The image extends the poem's theological register, night as both literal cover and spiritual blindness.

The volta lands with chilling understatement. "Glad" is the poem's most disturbing word — an adjective of uncomplicated joy placed next to a corpse. The speaker's gladness implicates them as the murderer while highlighting the moral bankruptcy of repressed wrath taken to its logical conclusion. "Outstretched" connotes both a body fallen in death and a hand reaching for what it desired, alluding to the poem's theme that desire is itself the mechanism of destruction. Notice how (i) the word "outstretched" is itself the longest word in the poem, which consisting almost entirely of short, simple words, and (ii) the line is also the longest line in the poem. Both of these are deliberate, producing a visual outstretched effect in the reader over and above the literal descriptive meaning of the word itself.

The tight AABB couplet rhyme scheme (end / grow; fears / tears; smiles / wiles; night / bright…) encloses each idea in a sealed, self-sufficient unit — formally enacting the repression the poem describes. Nothing escapes; every thought is contained and locked. The rhyme of "wiles / smiles" is particularly pointed, acoustically binding deception to its pleasant performance.

The dominant trochaic metre ("I was ANGry WITH my FRIEND") gives the poem a relentless, marching inevitability — each line falls like a footstep toward the poem's fatal conclusion. Unlike the iambic "rising" metre that connotes aspiration, the falling trochee enacts entropy and decline, formally mirroring the moral deterioration the poem traces.

Another thing to notice is that the poem is saturated with possessives ("my friend," "my foe," "my wrath," "my tears," "my garden," "mine"). The speaker owns everything, from concrete possession such as the garden to abstract entities like emotions and relationships. This possessive logic mirrors Blake's critique of "charter'd" ownership in London. The compulsive marking of territory as property underlies both poems' diagnosis of social violence.

Finally, 'A Poison Tree' is the philosophical inversion of The Divine Image' in The Songs of Innocence. Where Innocence sees mercy and love as human virtues, Experience reveals that human beings cultivate cruelty with equal deliberateness. The poem functions as Blake's diagnosis of how institutions — church, monarchy, social convention — teach suppression of honest emotion, and how it is that suppression, and not passion, which produces destruction.
