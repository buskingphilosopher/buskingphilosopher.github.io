Mr President,

The COVID-19 pandemic forced governments to make the hardest category of decision in all of public policy: how to weigh deaths against livelihoods, how to distribute costs across populations that differ dramatically in vulnerability, income, and life expectancy, and how to act under conditions of radical empirical uncertainty. Governments largely answered these questions by following their epidemiologists — tracking R numbers, modelling hospital capacity, projecting deaths. We argue that this was a serious error of institutional design. Not because the epidemiology was wrong, but because epidemiology, however accurate, cannot answer the questions that pandemic policy actually requires answering. Only a framework that incorporates welfare economics — and specifically, a distributional Social Welfare Function — can do that work.

"The question 'how many deaths will this policy produce?' is an epidemiological question. The question 'is this policy worth doing?' is a welfare-economic and ethical question. These are not the same question. Conflating them — by treating the reduction of COVID deaths as an unqualified policy objective — encodes a specific and contestable normative framework without subjecting it to any scrutiny."
i. the four instruments — and why VSL fails

VSL vs SWF — distributional blindness of BCAIn our G20 policy brief and the subsequent Oxford volume chapter, we survey four instruments for pandemic policy evaluation: the Value of Statistical Life (VSL), Value of Statistical Life Years (VSLY), Quality-Adjusted Life Years (QALYs), and Social Welfare Analysis using a Social Welfare Function (SWF). VSL — the workhorse of regulatory cost-benefit analysis — converts a prevented death into a monetary equivalent by aggregating individuals' marginal willingness to pay for small mortality risk reductions, then summing across the population. Its distributional blindness is the decisive flaw. VSL aggregates willingness to pay, which is anchored to income. A high-income person's VSL is greater than a low-income person's. An analysis using VSL therefore systematically underweights harms to the poor relative to harms to the rich — precisely inverting the distributional sensitivity a pandemic demands, since low-income workers were disproportionately exposed to COVID-19 and disproportionately unable to work from home.

ii. why the SWF is superior — distributional sensitivity

SWF prioritarianism — inequality-aversion, worse-off weightingThe Social Welfare Function approach evaluates pandemic policy by computing the effect of each policy option on the full distribution of individual wellbeing across the population — health outcomes, income, employment, educational disruption, mental health — and aggregating those wellbeing changes using a function that is inequality-averse: improvements in the wellbeing of the worse-off count for more than equivalent improvements in the wellbeing of the better-off. This is not an ad hoc ethical addition to welfare economics. It is the natural expression of a prioritarian moral theory — the view that benefits to the worse-off deserve extra weight — which has strong theoretical foundations and intuitive appeal. Concretely: in the US context, we show that when lockdown costs fall disproportionately on low-income workers who cannot work remotely, benefit-cost analysis may endorse lockdowns that cost 30% of GDP, while an SWF analysis with plausible inequality aversion endorses only policies costing at most 10–15% of GDP. These are policy differences of enormous practical significance, produced by the choice of evaluative framework.

iii. the three policy types — suppression, control, uncontrolled spread

Three policy types — SWF evaluation of suppression vs. controlOur analysis of three policy types — suppression, control, and uncontrolled spread — illustrates precisely why distributional analysis matters. Suppression policies (hard lockdowns pursuing elimination or very low caseloads) produce the best health outcomes but the worst economic outcomes and concentrate their costs on workers in contact-intensive industries — retail, hospitality, care work — who are disproportionately low-income. Control policies (managed transmission with targeted interventions) distribute costs differently, often more evenly. Uncontrolled spread concentrates health costs on the elderly and those with comorbidities. An SWF that is inequality-averse will not automatically endorse the suppression policy simply because it produces fewer deaths; it will ask: who bears the deaths, who bears the economic costs, and how do those distributional patterns interact? The epidemiological model cannot answer this. The SWF can.

iv. the "follow the science" error — value judgements laundered as facts

Science-policy boundary — value judgements hidden in scientific framingThe political phrase "following the science" was not merely a communications convenience — it was an epistemically confused account of what pandemic decision-making requires. Science can tell governments that a given policy will likely produce X deaths and Y percentage points of GDP loss, within specified uncertainty bounds. It cannot tell governments whether X deaths and Y GDP loss is an acceptable trade-off, which requires a value judgement about the relative weight of life and livelihood, the relative claims of different groups, and the appropriate response to uncertainty. By presenting the public health objective as self-evidently paramount — as though a framework focused exclusively on minimising deaths needed no justification — governments smuggled in a specific ethical position as a scientific finding. The SWF approach is not less scientific than this. It is more honest: it surfaces the value choices that must be made rather than encoding them invisibly in the choice of objective.

v. severe uncertainty — the SWF under ambiguity

Decision under uncertainty — SWF with ambiguity-aversionA natural objection to SWF analysis in pandemic conditions is that the wellbeing impacts of different policies are themselves deeply uncertain — so the SWF cannot be computed. We acknowledge the severity of this uncertainty. But the objection applies equally to any evaluative framework, including benefit-cost analysis and pure epidemiological minimisation. The question is not whether uncertainty exists — it does — but how to reason under it. We argue for an ambiguity-averse SWF approach: one that gives additional weight to worst-case wellbeing distributions across the population in conditions of severe uncertainty. This is not ad hoc caution — it reflects the moral principle that when we do not know the full consequences of our actions, we should be especially attentive to the possibility that the worst-off will bear disproportionate risks. Uncertainty is an argument for distributional sensitivity, not against it.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to support my colleagues' case for welfare-function-grounded pandemic policy while adding a dimension they do not fully develop: the argument that the ethical framework used to evaluate pandemic policy must not only be technically sound but also publicly articulated, inclusively deliberated, and democratically accountable. The motion before us is correct — pandemic policy must be evaluated by social welfare analysis. But I want to argue that the manner in which welfare analysis enters policy is as important as its content — and that the UK's pandemic governance failed both tests simultaneously.

"The trade-offs involved in pandemic policy — how to weight lives against livelihoods, which groups to prioritise, how to distribute the costs of restriction — are not technical parameters to be estimated by scientists. They are fundamental value choices that belong to democratic communities. For those choices to be legitimate, the communities that will bear them must participate in making them."
i. two failures — not one

Two failures — evaluative incompleteness & democratic exclusionIn our 2021 Nature Medicine paper, co-authored with Norheim, Abi-Rached, Bright, Bærøe, Ferraz, and Gloppen, we identify two overlapping failures in COVID-19 governance. The first is the evaluative failure that Adler and Fleurbaey have documented: pandemic policy was not assessed against a comprehensive welfare measure sensitive to distribution. The second is a governance failure: the trade-offs that governments were making — between liberties, economic activity, mental health, educational development, and the control of infection — were not made through inclusive, transparent deliberation that exposed those trade-offs to public scrutiny and gave affected stakeholders a genuine voice. These failures are related. If you frame pandemic policy as "following the science," you implicitly represent the relevant value choices as technical rather than political — and thereby exclude the public from the deliberation that legitimises those choices.

ii. what comprehensive welfare evaluation requires

Comprehensive welfare — health + economic + social + relationalA comprehensive welfare evaluation of pandemic policy must assess impacts across all the dimensions of life that pandemic responses affected: mortality and morbidity from COVID-19 directly, but also economic welfare loss from restriction, mental health harms from isolation and grief, developmental harms to children from school closure, and the social and relational harms of protracted distancing. The UK's SAGE advisory process, as Bradley and Roussos document, focused primarily on epidemiological outcomes — hospitalisation rates, deaths, R numbers. Social and economic harms appeared in the analysis but were not integrated into a comprehensive welfare assessment that placed them on a common evaluative footing with health outcomes. This is not a failure of the scientists on SAGE — they were being asked epidemiological questions, and they answered them. It is a failure of the institutional design that directed them to those questions and no others.

iii. the inclusion argument — why deliberation is not optional

Inclusive deliberation — legitimacy, adherence, trustThere are three reinforcing arguments for inclusive decision-making in pandemic contexts, and each is independently sufficient. First: the quality argument. Decisions made with input from diverse stakeholders are better decisions — they incorporate information and perspectives that expert bodies alone cannot access, including local knowledge about the distributional consequences of interventions and the populations most affected by different policy choices. Second: the legitimacy argument. Decisions that profoundly restrict liberty and impose large costs require justification to those who bear them, and justification requires transparency about the values and trade-offs involved. Decisions made behind closed expert doors — even technically correct ones — lack the justificatory basis for demanding compliance. Third: the adherence argument. Populations that understand the reasons for restrictions comply with them better. "Follow the rules" works less well than "here is why these specific restrictions represent a fair response to a shared emergency."

iv. the lives-and-livelihoods framework — what balanced evaluation looks like

Lives & livelihoods — value of a statistical life, fairness, severityIn our Oxford volume chapter, we offer a structured framework for balancing lives and livelihoods that makes the relevant considerations explicit. It involves: a measure of health loss (ideally not QALYs but a richer wellbeing-sensitive equivalent); an assessment of economic and social loss on the same scale; a weighting principle that reflects both the magnitude of individual harms and their distribution across the population — giving extra weight to the worst-affected — and a principled approach to uncertainty that does not simply assume the worst and act accordingly, but incorporates the full range of plausible outcomes weighted by their probability and severity. This framework is not a replacement for scientific advice — it requires epidemiological inputs. But it integrates those inputs into an evaluative structure that can answer the question governments actually need answered: which policy is best, all things considered?

v. the fairness argument — which distributions are acceptable

Fairness — distribution of pandemic burden, equal considerationPandemic policy raises fairness questions that welfare analysis must address directly. Is it fair that elderly and immunocompromised people bear an overwhelming share of the mortality risk while younger people bear the bulk of the economic and social costs of restriction? Is it fair that low-income workers in contact-intensive industries face both elevated infection risk and greater economic insecurity? Is it fair that children bear long-term educational and developmental costs to protect adults? These are not questions to which there are simple utilitarian answers. They require a conception of fair distribution — one that acknowledges both the separateness of persons and the differential vulnerability of groups — that standard welfare economics does not automatically supply. A pandemic welfare framework must build in a concern for equal consideration of all affected persons, weighted by the severity and nature of what they stand to lose.

Mr President,

I want to make clear at the outset what my position is and what it is not. I agree with the proposition that pandemic policy must be evaluated against a comprehensive assessment of impacts — not just epidemiological outcomes but economic, social, and mental health consequences as well. I agree that "following the science" was an epistemically confused formulation that concealed value choices within the mantle of scientific authority. What I contest is the proposition's conclusion: that the Social Welfare Function provides the coherent basis for pandemic decision-making that they claim for it. Under conditions of severe uncertainty — and pandemic conditions are precisely those conditions — the SWF cannot be computed with any reliability, and the pretence that it can creates a different form of false confidence.

"My colleagues on the proposition have built a careful and sophisticated framework. My concern is not with its theoretical elegance but with its operational conditions. Social welfare analysis requires knowing, or being able to estimate, how policies affect individual wellbeing across multiple dimensions, and how to aggregate those estimates. In the first weeks of the COVID-19 pandemic, governments did not know these things. And acting as though they could compute an SWF in those conditions would have produced confident decisions on a shaky empirical foundation."
i. SAGE and the science-policy interface

SAGE — science-policy interface failure, not science failureIn our LSE Public Policy Review paper, Joe Roussos and I argue that UK pandemic policy suffered two specific and correctable deficiencies in how it related to science. First: policy was not based on a comprehensive assessment of policy impacts — the proposition is correct about this. But second: the particular form of scientific advice that SAGE offered — focusing on a "reasonable worst-case scenario" as a planning basis — was itself methodologically problematic in ways that the SWF framework does not automatically resolve. SAGE largely eschewed probabilistic prediction in favour of scenario planning: advising on plausible outcomes rather than assigning probabilities to them. The proposition's SWF approach requires expected wellbeing calculations — which require probabilities. If the scientists cannot or should not assign probabilities (because the uncertainty is too severe), the SWF calculation cannot be performed as specified.

ii. the reasonable worst-case scenario — its failure and what follows

RWCS — planning basis loses decision-relevant informationThe reasonable worst-case scenario (RWCS) methodology SAGE employed has a clear intuitive appeal: plan for the worst plausible outcome rather than the best or the average. But it has two decisive deficiencies. First, using a single scenario as the planning basis loses all the decision-relevant information contained in the full range of plausible scenarios — information about how different policies perform differently under different epidemiological assumptions, which is precisely the information needed to distinguish between them. Second, it does not provide a coherent basis for decision: knowing that a policy is the best response to the reasonable worst case does not tell you it is the best response to the plausible range of cases, especially if it performs badly in better scenarios. The RWCS approach is a heuristic for managing catastrophic risk, not a decision procedure. But neither is the SWF, without the probabilistic inputs it requires.

iii. what pandemic decisions actually required — a different framework

Severe uncertainty — confidence approach, not SWF maximisationThe right framework for decision-making under severe uncertainty is not SWF expected welfare maximisation — because that requires probabilities we did not have — and not the RWCS approach — because that loses decision-relevant information. In our work on model ensembles and decision-making under uncertainty, we propose what we call a "confidence approach": a framework that ranks options by considering how they perform across the full range of plausible scenarios, with extra weight given to options that perform acceptably across many scenarios rather than optimally in the expected case. This is not the same as maximin (choosing to maximise the minimum outcome), which can be too conservative. It is a systematic way of identifying robust policies — policies that are reasonably good under many plausible conditions — in the absence of reliable probability estimates.

iv. the value judgement problem persists in the SWF

SWF evaluative uncertainty — which SWF? Contestable parametersEven setting aside empirical uncertainty about wellbeing impacts, the SWF approach faces serious evaluative uncertainty. Which SWF should be used? The choice of inequality aversion parameter — how much extra weight to give to the worse-off — is not a technical choice that welfare economics can determine. Different plausible inequality aversion parameters produce dramatically different policy recommendations, as my colleagues on the proposition themselves demonstrate. The proposition implies that a SWF framework makes value choices explicit and therefore more amenable to democratic scrutiny. This is true in principle. But in practice, the technical complexity of SWF computation creates new expert dependence — governments will rely on welfare economists to specify the SWF parameters, and those choices will be no more democratically accountable than the choice of epidemiological model. Surfacing value choices in complex mathematical form is not the same as genuinely democratising those choices.

v. what the opposition is and is not

Positive proposal — comprehensive impacts + robust decision + deliberationMy opposition to the motion is not a defence of "following the science" in its pandemic incarnation, nor is it a defence of the RWCS approach. I accept that pandemic policy must be evaluated against comprehensive welfare impacts — not epidemiology alone. What I resist is the claim that the SWF is the appropriate operational framework for doing this under conditions of pandemic-level uncertainty. The appropriate framework is one that: (a) identifies a comprehensive set of policy-relevant impacts across health, economic, and social dimensions; (b) considers how policies perform across the full range of plausible scenarios rather than collapsing to expected values; (c) identifies robust options that perform acceptably across many conditions; and (d) makes the value choices involved in defining "acceptable performance" explicit and subject to democratic deliberation — in exactly the spirit of Voorhoeve and Norheim's inclusive decision-making argument, but without requiring that the deliberation resolve to a specific SWF parameter.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I occupy a peculiar position in this debate: I am an author of work on both sides of the motion as framed. I have argued, with Adler, Fleurbaey, and colleagues, that Social Welfare Analysis is superior to VSL-based benefit-cost analysis for pandemic evaluation. And I have argued separately, in my 2021 work on policy evaluation under severe uncertainty, that the standard SWF maximisation framework is itself inadequate to the conditions of deep uncertainty that pandemic policy actually involves — and that a more cautious, egalitarian approach is required. These positions are not contradictory. But they reveal a genuine tension within the proposition's own framework that I want to make explicit, because resolving it is not optional.

"The motion asks whether pandemic policy must be grounded in social welfare analysis. I say: yes, but the social welfare analysis must itself be grounded in an appropriate response to severe uncertainty — one that is genuinely cautious about the worst outcomes for the worst-off, and that does not mistake the formalism of welfare-economic optimisation for actual knowledge of consequences we do not have."
i. severe uncertainty is not just a technical challenge

Severe uncertainty — not computable but morally significantIn my 2021 paper "Policy Evaluation under Severe Uncertainty: A Cautious, Egalitarian Approach," I argue that pandemic conditions involve uncertainty so severe that standard expected utility maximisation — the formal basis of SWF computation — is not the appropriate decision framework. Severe uncertainty is uncertainty under which we cannot reliably assign probabilities to outcomes: not merely "we do not know the exact number" but "we do not know the distribution of possibilities." In early pandemic conditions, the distribution of outcomes under different policies was not merely imprecisely known — it was the subject of genuine expert disagreement, rapidly shifting evidence, and novel dynamics with no prior analogue. Acting as though an SWF can be computed in these conditions overstates what we can know, and the overstatement has moral consequences.

ii. the cautious egalitarian approach

Cautious egalitarianism — prioritise worst-off, avoid catastrophic outcomesMy "cautious, egalitarian approach" to policy evaluation under severe uncertainty incorporates two principles. First, caution: under severe uncertainty, we should give additional weight to avoiding the worst outcomes — outcomes in which large numbers of people experience severe harm — relative to their expected probability. This is not simple maximin reasoning, which I believe is too conservative. It is a structured additional weight on bad outcomes that reflects the moral seriousness of failing to protect people from catastrophic harm when we have acted on unreliable beliefs. Second, egalitarianism: the additional weight given to bad outcomes should be sensitive to who experiences them — worse outcomes for those who are already worse-off deserve even greater aversion than worse outcomes distributed equally. This is the intersection of caution and prioritarianism.

iii. what this means for the suppression-control choice

Cautious SWF — suppression vs. control under uncertaintyThe cautious egalitarian approach gives different policy recommendations than standard SWF maximisation in important cases. A standard SWF, given the best epidemiological estimates available at any time, would recommend the policy with the highest expected welfare across the population. A cautious egalitarian SWF would recommend the policy that: (a) avoids the worst-case outcomes for the worst-off with higher reliability, even at some cost to expected welfare; and (b) does not concentrate unrecoverable harms — deaths, permanent disability, irreversible economic ruin — on any identifiable group. This is not a small technical refinement. In early COVID-19 conditions, it systematically supported stronger initial suppression (to buy time for uncertainty reduction) over control policies that optimised expected welfare on the basis of models that turned out to be significantly wrong.

iv. the evaluation-deliberation connection

Evaluation-deliberation — cautious SWF requires democratic input on risk toleranceThe cautious egalitarian approach makes the connection to inclusive deliberation even more direct than the standard SWF does. The question "how cautious should we be in conditions of severe uncertainty?" is not one that welfare analysis alone can answer. It depends on the public's attitude to risk — specifically, to the risk of catastrophic outcomes for identifiable groups — which is itself a political value that democratic communities must express. An SWF that incorporates caution without consulting the public about their risk attitudes is no more democratically accountable than the RWCS approach. Inclusive deliberation is therefore not merely a complementary procedural virtue — it is an epistemic requirement of applying the cautious SWF correctly, because the public must supply the risk-aversion parameter that science cannot.

v. the limits of technocracy — even welfare-economic technocracy

Anti-technocracy — even SWF requires democratic legitimationI want to end by registering a concern about the motion even from within the proposition's position. The motion says pandemic policy "must be evaluated by social welfare analysis." The word "must" implies that welfare analysis, correctly applied, yields the right answer — that it is the authoritative evaluative framework. I am more modest. Welfare analysis, under conditions of severe uncertainty and appropriately cautious, can help governments identify which policies are robustly better across a range of plausible scenarios. It cannot determine what level of risk to catastrophic outcomes is acceptable — that is a democratic question. It cannot determine which conception of wellbeing to use — that is a philosophical question on which reasonable people disagree. And it cannot substitute for the open deliberation about trade-offs that is itself a component of legitimate pandemic governance. Welfare analysis is an indispensable tool. It is not a decision procedure that governments can deploy as a replacement for politics.

Thank you.

Drawing on: Voorhoeve (2021) "Policy Evaluation under Severe Uncertainty: A Cautious, Egalitarian Approach," in Routledge Handbook of Philosophy of Economics.
Argument I — Severe uncertainty makes standard SWF maximisation inappropriate
1.Severe uncertainty — under which probabilities cannot be reliably assigned to outcomes — is not merely quantitative imprecision; it characterised early pandemic conditions, where expert disagreement and novel dynamics precluded reliable probability estimation.
2.Standard SWF expected welfare maximisation requires probability assignments to compute expected wellbeing distributions; these were not reliably available in early pandemic conditions.
3.Operating as though the SWF can be computed in conditions where it cannot produces false precision — a different form of the overconfidence the proposition rightly criticises in "following the science."
∴Standard SWF maximisation is not appropriate under severe uncertainty; a modified framework sensitive to uncertainty structure is required.
Argument II — The cautious egalitarian approach: two principles under uncertainty
1.Caution: under severe uncertainty, additional weight should be given to avoiding worst-case outcomes — outcomes where many people experience catastrophic harm — beyond their expected probability.
2.Egalitarianism: the additional weight given to bad outcomes should be sensitive to who bears them — worse outcomes for the already worse-off deserve greater aversion (prioritarian caution).
3.Together these principles define a structured framework — cautious prioritarianism — that is more appropriate to severe uncertainty than either standard SWF maximisation or maximin.
∴Cautious egalitarianism — extra weight on bad outcomes for the worse-off, under severe uncertainty — is the appropriate modification of the SWF framework for pandemic conditions.
Argument III — Cautious SWF supports stronger initial suppression under early-pandemic uncertainty
1.A standard SWF in early pandemic conditions would recommend the policy with highest expected welfare given best-available models — which turned out to be significantly wrong.
2.A cautious SWF systematically prefers policies avoiding worst-case outcomes for the worst-off with higher reliability, even at expected welfare cost — which supported stronger initial suppression to buy time for uncertainty reduction.
3.This is not a small technical difference — it produces systematically different policy recommendations precisely in the conditions of early pandemic decision-making.
∴Cautious SWF analysis produces different — and more defensible — policy recommendations than standard SWF maximisation under early pandemic uncertainty; the distinction matters for real decisions.
Argument IV — Risk-aversion under uncertainty requires democratic input, not technical determination
1.The caution parameter — how much extra weight to give to catastrophic outcomes for the worst-off — is not technically determined by welfare analysis; it reflects the public's attitude to catastrophic risk under uncertainty.
2.An SWF that incorporates caution without democratic input about risk attitudes is no more democratically accountable than technocratic epidemiological planning.
3.Inclusive deliberation is therefore an epistemic requirement of correctly applying a cautious SWF — the public must supply the risk-aversion parameter that welfare analysis cannot determine from first principles.
∴Cautious SWF application requires democratic input to determine the risk-aversion parameter; inclusive deliberation and welfare analysis are not alternatives but jointly necessary components of legitimate pandemic governance.
Argument V — Welfare analysis is an indispensable tool, not a decision procedure
1.Welfare analysis, appropriately cautious, can identify policies that are robustly better across plausible scenarios — this is its genuine contribution.
2.It cannot determine acceptable risk levels (democratic), the correct wellbeing conception (philosophical), or substitute for open deliberation about trade-offs (political).
3.A motion saying pandemic policy "must be evaluated by social welfare analysis" implies welfare analysis is the authoritative decision procedure — this overstates its role and underestimates the legitimate role of politics and democratic deliberation.
∴Welfare analysis is a necessary but not sufficient component of legitimate pandemic governance; the motion's "must" overstates its authority, which is that of an indispensable analytical tool, not a replacement for democratic decision-making.
