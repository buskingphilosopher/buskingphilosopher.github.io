Mr President,

My result is a mathematical theorem. This is the first thing to establish clearly — because the opposition will attempt to argue that it rests on implausible behavioural assumptions, or that it targets only one version of expected utility theory, or that it is a clever paradox rather than a decisive refutation. Each of these moves fails. The theorem takes as its premise only that an expected-utility maximiser with a concave Bernoulli utility function over final wealth turns down a specified small-stakes lottery. It derives as its conclusion that such an agent must turn down large-stakes lotteries that no reasonable person would ever decline. The logic from premise to conclusion is valid. The conclusion is empirically absurd. Therefore the premise — the conjunction of expected utility of final wealth and concave utility — cannot simultaneously be both psychologically realistic and normatively adequate.

"The key insight is geometrically simple. If you turn down the 50-50 bet of losing $100 or gaining $110 at every wealth level, you have revealed that the utility gain from an extra $110 is always less than the utility loss from $100. This means your utility function must be steeply and persistently concave — and a function that is that persistently concave over small intervals must be astronomically concave over large ones. The curvature required to generate modest small-stakes aversion generates monstrous large-stakes aversion."
i. the theorem — stated precisely

The calibration theorem — concavity from small bets geometrically compounds; large-stakes implications are absurdThe result proceeds as follows. Suppose a person rejects the lottery {lose $100 with probability 0.5, gain $110 with probability 0.5} at every wealth level between $0 and $300,000. This is a very modest assumption: the person turns down a slightly favourable bet at every wealth level. What does this reveal about the Bernoulli utility function u? It reveals that u(w + 110) − u(w) < u(w) − u(w − 100) for every w in the range. That is: each $110 gain buys less utility than each $100 loss costs. This means the marginal utility of money must decline by at least a factor of (100/110) = 0.909 over each $210 interval. Now apply this over a large range. Starting from $290,000 of wealth:

Implication: An agent who rejects the above small bet at all wealth levels between $0 and $300,000 must also reject a 50-50 lottery of losing $2,000 against gaining any amount — even $1 billion, even an infinite sum — at wealth of $290,000.
This conclusion is not a theoretical curiosity. It means the concave utility function required to generate even very modest small-stakes risk aversion must be so steeply curved that over large stakes the agent would forgo essentially unlimited expected gain to avoid a $2,000 loss. Nobody actually behaves this way. The model generates an absurdity, and a model that generates absurdities is refuted.

ii. why the argument is purely mathematical

Mathematical proof — no empirical uncertainty; valid deduction; conclusion is empirically absurd; modus tollens refutes premiseI want to stress the logical structure here because my opponents will try to muddy it. The argument is a modus tollens. The premises are: (P1) the agent has a concave Bernoulli utility function over final wealth; (P2) the agent rejects the specified small bet at all wealth levels in the range. The conclusion C is that the agent would turn down the absurd large-stakes lottery. C is empirically false — nobody refuses $1 billion to avoid losing $2,000. By modus tollens, either P1 or P2 must be false. P2 is not unreasonable: many people do decline small unfavourable bets even when slightly positively expected. So P1 — the assumption of a concave utility function over final wealth — must be false or at minimum cannot serve as the explanation of small-stakes risk aversion. The theorem does not rely on any empirical data about how people actually behave. It relies on valid deduction. Its conclusion can be checked against our intuitions about rational large-stakes behaviour — and those intuitions unambiguously reject it.

iii. the marginal utility intuition — why the curvature compounds

Marginal utility compounding — diminishing marginal utility too slow for small stakes; curvature needed is geometrically explosiveThe intuition behind the mathematics is worth making explicit. The diminishing marginal utility of wealth is a real and important phenomenon at the scale of whole lifetimes and economies. A billionaire genuinely values an extra dollar less than a pauper does. This is sensible. But the magnitudes involved are wrong for explaining small-stakes risk aversion. A 50-50 bet of losing $100 versus gaining $110 is not a bet about whether you will be a billionaire or a pauper. It is a bet about fluctuations that are, relative to lifetime wealth, utterly negligible. The marginal utility of money should not change meaningfully over such tiny ranges of wealth. But if the risk aversion exhibited over small bets is to be explained by the concavity of a utility function over final wealth, then the utility function must be steeply concave over those tiny ranges — and a function steeply concave over tiny ranges is explosively concave over large ranges. The geometry does not admit of a moderate solution. Either the function is flat over small ranges (making small-stakes risk aversion inexplicable by curvature) or it is steep over small ranges (making large-stakes behaviour absurd).

iv. what does explain small-stakes risk aversion — loss aversion

Loss aversion — not diminishing marginal utility but reference-dependence and loss weighting; prospect theory's account is correctThe correct explanation of small-stakes risk aversion is not the concavity of a utility-over-wealth function. It is loss aversion: the asymmetric weighting of losses and gains relative to a reference point, as established by Kahneman and Tversky. When a person declines the $100 loss / $110 gain lottery, they are not expressing a judgement about the declining marginal utility of wealth across a tiny range. They are expressing a preference that treats the $100 loss as worth more than the $110 gain, because losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains. This is a local, reference-dependent phenomenon — it applies around the current reference point, and it does not compound across wealth levels in the way that utility function curvature must. Loss aversion generates plausible small-stakes risk aversion without generating absurd large-stakes implications, because it is not a property of the utility function's global curvature but a local, reference-dependent feature of how outcomes are evaluated.

v. Thaler's practical implications — the concavity assumption pervades applied economics

Applied economics — insurance, labour supply, asset pricing all use EU with concave utility; calibration refutes these applicationsRichard Thaler and I argued in our 2001 Journal of Economic Perspectives paper that the theorem's implications extend beyond the laboratory into applied economics. Much of applied economics treats agents as expected utility maximisers with concave utility over terminal wealth — this is the workhorse model of insurance demand, labour supply, portfolio choice, and asset pricing. The equity premium puzzle — the observation that stock returns exceed bond returns by far more than any reasonable risk aversion coefficient can explain — is one symptom of this underlying problem. The Rabin calibration theorem demonstrates that the model used in all these applications is inconsistent with itself: if the concavity coefficient is calibrated to explain small-stakes behaviour observed in experiments or surveys, it generates large-stakes predictions that are wildly wrong; if it is calibrated to explain large-stakes behaviour, it implies near-risk-neutrality for small stakes that contradicts observed small-stakes aversion. The model cannot do both jobs simultaneously.

Mr President,

Rabin's theorem targets expected utility of terminal wealth specifically. The opposition's most powerful move is to retreat to alternative theories — non-expected utility models, prospect theory, rank-dependent utility, cumulative prospect theory — and argue that these escape the calibration critique. Uzi Segal and I have demonstrated that this escape route is largely illusory. The calibration problem, properly generalised, applies to any decision theory that exhibits what we call first-order risk aversion at small stakes — and this includes virtually all the leading non-EU alternatives that have been proposed as replacements.

"Expected utility theory exhibits second-order risk aversion: an agent who is risk averse turns down bets that are unfavourable in expected value, but their indifference curve bends smoothly — they are willing to accept a slightly favourable bet at each wealth level. First-order risk aversion is different: the agent has a kink in their value function at the reference point, creating a discontinuity in risk tolerance. Prospect theory's loss aversion generates first-order risk aversion. And first-order risk aversion, we show, generates its own calibration problems that are just as severe as Rabin's."
i. first-order versus second-order risk aversion

First vs. second order risk aversion — smooth indifference curves vs. kinks; prospect theory exhibits first-order; calibration applies to bothThe distinction between first-order and second-order risk aversion, due to Segal and Spivak (1990), is crucial for understanding the scope of the calibration problem. Second-order risk aversion — the kind that EU theory generates from a smooth concave utility function — means that an agent's risk premium for a small actuarially fair bet is proportional to the variance of the bet: it goes to zero as the stake goes to zero. This implies near-risk-neutrality for very small stakes, which is a feature, not a bug: it means the EU model does not predict strong small-stakes aversion. First-order risk aversion arises when the agent's valuation of a gamble has a kink at the reference point — as in prospect theory's loss aversion or in rank-dependent utility. First-order risk aversion means the risk premium is proportional to the standard deviation, not the variance — remaining non-negligible even for infinitesimally small bets. This generates the plausible small-stakes aversion that Rabin says EU cannot explain. But it also generates its own calibration problems.

ii. the Safra-Segal extension — first-order risk aversion is also calibration-vulnerable

Safra-Segal theorem — first-order risk aversion implies calibration problems; non-EU theories not exempt; loss aversion amplified versionOur 2008 paper "Calibration Results for Non-Expected Utility Theories" establishes a calibration result for non-EU theories. The argument generalises Rabin's approach: instead of showing that the curvature of a utility function compounds geometrically, we show that any theory exhibiting first-order risk aversion at small stakes — meaning any theory with a kink at the reference point — will exhibit calibration problems when confronted with a sequence of independent small bets. Specifically: if an agent declines a small unfavourable bet in isolation, a theory with first-order risk aversion implies that they would decline a compound lottery offering a large expected gain with very high probability, as long as there is any probability of a moderate loss. The kink that makes small-stakes aversion plausible also makes large-stakes behaviour excessively cautious when the gamble can be decomposed into a sequence of small bets. The escape from Rabin's theorem through non-EU models is much narrower than the opposition suggests.

iii. the Cox-Sadiraj income utility escape — and why it fails

Cox-Sadiraj rebuttal — EU of income not wealth; Safra-Segal show income model also calibration-vulnerable; the escape is narrowThe opposition's strongest technical argument — developed by Cox and Sadiraj — is that Rabin's theorem targets EU of terminal wealth, and that EU of income rather than terminal wealth escapes the calibration problem. Rabin's argument requires that the concavity of the utility function be preserved across all wealth levels: each small bet compresses the marginal utility by a fixed factor. If the argument of the utility function is income rather than terminal wealth, the function resets at each decision and the compounding does not occur. Cox and Sadiraj 2006 establish this formally. But the escape is narrower than it appears for two reasons. First, Schechter 2007 shows that empirical estimates of the risk aversion coefficient using EU of consumption are still in the thousands — far above any plausible value — so the qualitative problem persists even if the formal calibration theorem does not apply. Second, our generalisation shows that the first-order risk aversion which motivates loss aversion as the alternative also generates calibration problems that income-utility models do not avoid.

iv. prospect theory's own calibration problem

Prospect theory calibration — Neilson and Stowe; loss aversion parameter calibrated to small stakes implies absurd large-stakes predictions in PTThe most direct demonstration that the retreat to prospect theory does not escape the calibration problem is Neilson and Stowe's result that loss aversion, if calibrated to explain small-stakes risk aversion, generates absurd large-stakes predictions within prospect theory itself. Specifically: a loss aversion parameter calibrated to explain why agents decline the Rabin-type small bets implies that the same agent would refuse any large bet with positive probability of loss, regardless of the expected gain — because loss aversion magnifies losses by a constant factor applied to every dollar of loss, which compounds over large loss amounts in a way analogous to Rabin's compounding of concavity. Prospect theory escapes one horn of the dilemma only to impale itself on another: the mechanism it offers to explain small-stakes aversion — loss aversion — generates its own calibration problem at large stakes.

v. the broader lesson — no standard theory escapes unscathed

General calibration problem — dual calibration critique; Cox-Sadiraj-Vogt-Dasgupta 2013; no theory escapes for realistic parameter rangesCox, Sadiraj, Vogt, and Dasgupta's 2013 paper "Is There a Plausible Theory for Risky Decisions? A Dual Calibration Critique" extends the analysis to a comprehensive survey of decision theories — EU of terminal wealth, EU of income, rank-dependent utility, prospect theory, cumulative prospect theory — and shows that all face calibration problems for realistic parameter ranges. The dual calibration critique applies both from the small-stakes direction (the Rabin direction: small-stakes aversion implies absurd large-stakes caution) and from the large-stakes direction (large-stakes aversion implies excessive caution at small stakes that contradicts observed near-risk-neutrality in small-stakes experiments). No standard theory provides a parameter range in which both small- and large-stakes risk behaviour is simultaneously plausible. The Rabin theorem is not an isolated problem for EU. It is the tip of a calibration iceberg that threatens every parametric theory of risk preference.

Mr President,

I want to establish a precise technical point and then draw its philosophical implications. The Rabin calibration theorem is a valid mathematical result — about one specific version of expected utility theory. That version takes the agent's utility function to be defined over terminal wealth: the total amount of money they possess after the gamble is resolved. The theorem shows that if utility is a function of terminal wealth, and if this function is concave, and if the agent declines a specified small bet at all wealth levels in a range, then the agent would also decline a specified large bet. This is valid. But expected utility theory is more general than this one version. There is a version — expected utility of income — in which the utility function is defined over the monetary payoff of the gamble itself, not over the agent's total terminal wealth. And Rabin's theorem does not apply to this version.

"The argument of the utility function matters enormously. A utility function defined over terminal wealth has to be simultaneously consistent with behaviour at all wealth levels — and Rabin's compounding exploits exactly this requirement. A utility function defined over income — over the gain or loss from the gamble itself — resets at each decision and does not accumulate the concavity across wealth levels. This is not a minor technical patch. It is a fundamental difference in what EU theory says about how agents evaluate gambles."
i. the terminal wealth assumption — the theorem's hidden premise

Terminal wealth assumption — EU(terminal wealth) vs EU(income); Rabin's theorem requires terminal wealth; income utility escapesRabin's theorem requires a specific premise that he states but whose significance he underestimates: the utility function must be defined over the agent's terminal wealth — the total amount of money the agent has after all transactions. This is why the compounding works: if u(w) is the utility of terminal wealth w, then declining a bet at every wealth level w in [0, $300,000] gives information about how steeply u declines over the entire range. The marginal utility at $290,000 is forced to be astronomically small relative to the marginal utility at $0. But if the utility function is defined over income — the payoff of the gamble itself, independently of initial wealth — then declining a bet with a $100 loss and $110 gain reveals that u(income=+$110) − u(income=0) < u(income=0) − u(income=−$100). This tells us about the local curvature of the income utility function around zero, but it does not tell us anything about the curvature of u at income = $2,000 or u at income = $1,000,000. The compounding does not occur because the function does not depend on wealth.

ii. EU of income — the formally correct version for small-stakes gambles

EU of income — empirically supported; lab experiments involve income not terminal wealth; partial asset integration; Wakker reviewThere are strong reasons to think that EU of income is the empirically correct specification for laboratory experiments and small-stakes field choices. When a subject in an experiment is offered a bet of losing $100 or gaining $110, they do not typically calculate their total terminal wealth including their savings, home equity, pension, and all other assets before deciding. They evaluate the bet's payoffs as income — as gains and losses from the status quo — independently of the vast majority of their wealth. This is what psychologists call narrow bracketing: the tendency to evaluate gambles in isolation rather than integrated with total wealth. Narrow bracketing is empirically robust: subjects in experiments do not exhibit asset integration, and their choices are not predictable from their wealth levels. This means the appropriate EU specification for explaining small-stakes behaviour is EU of income — and EU of income escapes Rabin's calibration critique entirely.

iii. the asset integration assumption is what is refuted

Asset integration — Rabin assumes it; it is empirically false; the refutation targets an implausible assumption not EU per seRabin's theorem assumes that an agent who declines a small bet exhibits the same risk preference as an agent who declines the same bet at every possible wealth level from $0 to $300,000 — that the agent's risk preference is invariant across all initial wealth levels, and that their utility function integrates gamble payoffs with total wealth before evaluation. This is the asset integration assumption. It is this assumption that generates the compounding. And asset integration is empirically false: the extensive literature on narrow bracketing, mental accounting, and reference-dependent choice consistently shows that agents do not integrate gamble payoffs with background wealth. They evaluate gambles locally. What Rabin's theorem actually demonstrates is not the failure of EU in general but the failure of the specific combination of EU and asset integration — which is, as Rubinstein and I have independently argued, an extremely specific and empirically dubious joint hypothesis.

iv. the income utility response to the equity premium puzzle

Equity premium puzzle — EU of income can address it without calibration problem; consumption-based CAPM vs income based; the macroeconomic application remains validThaler and Rabin argue that the calibration problem extends to applied economics, particularly the equity premium puzzle. But the equity premium puzzle concerns the relative returns on stocks and bonds over the long run — a large-stakes, long-horizon problem where asset integration is more plausible than in the laboratory context. The appropriate EU specification for this problem is EU of consumption or of terminal wealth — and here the calibration problem is a real concern, addressed by behavioural finance through loss aversion and myopic loss aversion. But this large-stakes macroeconomic problem is distinct from the small-stakes laboratory problem. Rabin treats them as refuting the same model. They are not: the EU of income model is appropriate for small-stakes behaviour; the EU of terminal wealth model is appropriate for large-stakes long-horizon decisions; and these are different specifications that need not be simultaneously evaluated against both types of evidence.

v. the dual calibration — EU of income faces its own problem, but differently

Dual calibration — Cox-Sadiraj show income utility also has calibration results on bounded domains; these are weaker and more parameter-sensitive than Rabin'sI want to be honest about the limits of my own response. Our 2006 paper shows that Rabin's theorem does not apply to EU of income on an unbounded domain. But on bounded domains — realistic ranges of income — concavity calibration results do apply to EU of income as well, though they are substantially weaker and more parameter-sensitive than Rabin's result for terminal wealth. And the qualitative problem — that implausible small-stakes aversion at zero income implies implausible large-stakes caution for very large income levels — persists in a milder form. What I claim is not that EU of income is perfectly calibration-consistent in all cases, but that the dramatic absurdities Rabin identifies — refusing $1 billion to avoid $2,000 — are artefacts of the terminal wealth assumption and do not arise for EU of income. The calibration result for income utility is considerably milder and more tractable.

Mr President,

I want to take a philosophically more fundamental position than my colleague Cox. I do not merely contest the scope of Rabin's theorem. I contest the inferential move from the theorem to the conclusion that EU theory has been "refuted." A mathematical theorem is not an empirical refutation. The argument from a mathematical theorem to a claim about what a theory "demonstrates" about the real world is a philosophical argument — and I believe this philosophical argument contains a crucial error about the relationship between formal theories and the phenomena they are used to study.

"When Rabin proves that a concave utility function that generates modest small-stakes aversion must also generate absurd large-stakes aversion, he has shown something interesting and important about the mathematics of concavity. What he has not shown is that expected utility theory, as an abstract mathematical framework, is refuted. He has shown that a specific psychological interpretation of the utility function — that concavity represents the declining marginal utility of wealth, and that this applies globally to all wealth levels — is false. But this interpretation is not built into EU theory. It is a psychologistic addition to a mathematical structure that does not require it."
i. the theory versus its interpretation

Theory vs. interpretation — EU is an abstract mathematical structure; concavity is a property of a representation, not a psychological commitmentExpected utility theory, as von Neumann and Morgenstern formulated it, is a representation theorem: if an agent's preferences satisfy certain axioms (completeness, transitivity, continuity, independence), they can be represented by a utility function u such that gambles are ranked by their expected utility. The utility function u is a representation of preferences — a convenient mathematical object. Its concavity is a mathematical property: it represents risk aversion in the sense that the agent prefers certainty to gambles with the same expected value. But the concavity of u does not have to be interpreted as the declining marginal utility of terminal wealth accumulation. It can be given many interpretations or no interpretation beyond its representational role. Rabin's theorem identifies a problem with one specific interpretation — that concavity reflects the globally stable, wealth-integrated marginal utility of money. This interpretation may be psychologically false. The theorem does not show that the mathematical structure is incoherent.

ii. the critical premise — risk aversion at all wealth levels

Critical premise — risk aversion at all wealth levels is psychologically implausible; it is not a requirement of EU theory; the theorem's force depends on itRabin's key premise is that the agent declines the small bet at every wealth level between $0 and $300,000. This is psychologically implausible. An agent who is destitute and an agent who is wealthy do not exhibit the same risk attitude toward a $100/$110 bet — the same absolute risk aversion applied at all wealth levels. Most real agents are approximately risk neutral over small stakes relative to their total wealth, and become more risk averse as the stakes become a significant fraction of their wealth. Rabin's "for all wealth levels" assumption is the engine of his theorem — without it, the geometric compounding does not occur. But this assumption is not a feature of EU theory. It is an additional empirical assumption about the constancy of risk attitudes across wealth levels — an assumption that is independently implausible and that most EU theorists would not endorse. The theorem is powered by an unrealistic assumption that EU theory does not require.

iii. EU as an abstract framework — multiple functional forms

Abstract framework — many utility functions consistent with EU; CARA, CRRA, quadratic; Rabin's problem specific to CARA-like functions; CRRA is near-risk-neutral at small stakesExpected utility theory does not mandate any particular functional form for the utility function. Different functional forms — constant absolute risk aversion (CARA), constant relative risk aversion (CRRA), quadratic, logarithmic — have different calibration properties. The Rabin calibration problem is most severe for functional forms that exhibit significant absolute risk aversion over small stakes — that is, for utility functions where the curvature does not decrease proportionally with wealth. CRRA utility functions, in contrast, exhibit near-risk-neutrality for small bets relative to total wealth: for an agent with $300,000 of wealth, a $100 bet is 0.03% of their wealth, and a CRRA function generates near-zero risk aversion for such a tiny fraction. If the agent is observed to exhibit significant risk aversion over this small bet, the CRRA model says they are making a mistake — not that the CRRA model is wrong. The Rabin theorem does not demonstrate that all utility functional forms generate the calibration problem: it demonstrates it for specific functional forms applied to specific assumptions.

iv. the right response — separate what the theorem establishes from what it does not

Correct lesson — EU is a formal theory; its application requires appropriate calibration; small-stakes lab data should not be used to calibrate large-stakes modelsThe correct lesson from the Rabin theorem is methodological, not theoretical. It is: do not use experimental evidence of small-stakes risk aversion to calibrate a utility function that will then be applied to large-stakes decisions. The theorem shows that the calibration will generate implausible large-stakes predictions. The appropriate response is to use different evidence for different applications — large-stakes field data for large-stakes applications — rather than to abandon EU as a framework. Rabin himself acknowledges in a footnote that the theorem's premise — constant risk aversion at all wealth levels — is unrealistic, and that the result would be weakened if this assumption were relaxed. This footnote contains the defeat of the most dramatic version of the claim. The calibration theorem demonstrates a constraint on how EU models should be calibrated. It does not demonstrate that EU should be abandoned.

v. the philosophical excess — "refutation" as too strong a conclusion

Philosophical excess — Rabin and Thaler claim "establishment of implausibility"; this is too strong; the theorem constrains model calibration, not the theory itselfI am struck by the language in which Rabin and Thaler present their conclusion. They write that they "establish the implausibility of expected utility theory" by showing that "absurd large-stakes risk aversion follows inherently from non-negligible modest-scale risk aversion." The word "inherently" is doing a great deal of philosophical work. The implication follows only given the terminal wealth specification and the universal wealth-level assumption — neither of which is "inherent" to EU theory. EU theory is a mathematical framework flexible enough to accommodate EU of income, partial asset integration, different functional forms, and different interpretations of what the utility function's argument represents. Rabin and Thaler have demonstrated that one specific implementation of EU theory — with strong and empirically implausible auxiliary assumptions — generates absurd predictions. This is important. But it is a refutation of those auxiliary assumptions, not of EU theory as such.

Thank you.

Argument I — EU is an abstract mathematical structure; the concavity interpretation is not built into it
1.EU is a representation theorem: preferences satisfying the axioms are representable by a utility function. The concavity of u represents risk aversion in a mathematical sense — it is not committed to a specific psychological interpretation.
2.Rabin's theorem identifies a problem with the interpretation that concavity reflects globally stable, wealth-integrated diminishing marginal utility. This interpretation is not entailed by EU theory.
3.Refuting a specific psychologistic interpretation of a mathematical structure is not the same as refuting the mathematical structure; Rabin's theorem demonstrates the falsity of the interpretation, not the incoherence of EU.
∴Rabin's theorem refutes a specific psychological interpretation of EU's concavity requirement; it does not refute EU as an abstract mathematical framework for representing preferences under risk.
Argument II — The "all wealth levels" premise is unrealistic and not required by EU theory
1.Rabin's theorem requires: the agent declines the small bet at every wealth level in [0, $300,000]. This is the engine of the compounding; without it, the geometric accumulation does not occur.
2.Constant absolute risk aversion at all wealth levels is psychologically implausible: agents' risk attitudes toward fixed absolute stakes change with wealth. EU theory does not require this assumption.
3.Rabin himself acknowledges the assumption's unrealism in a footnote, noting the result is weakened when it is relaxed. The theorem's most dramatic conclusions depend on this unrealistic premise.
∴The "all wealth levels" assumption is the theorem's critical engine and is independently implausible; relaxing it substantially weakens the calibration result — the theorem's force depends on an assumption EU theory does not require.
Argument III — Different functional forms escape the most severe calibration problems
1.CRRA utility functions exhibit near-risk-neutrality for small stakes relative to total wealth: a $100 bet is 0.03% of $300,000 wealth; CRRA generates near-zero risk aversion for such a fraction.
2.Observed significant small-stakes risk aversion with a CRRA model indicates the agent is making a mistake — not that the CRRA model is wrong; the model is consistent with different calibrations.
3.Rabin's severe calibration problem arises for specific functional forms (CARA-like, high absolute risk aversion at all wealth levels); EU theory accommodates many functional forms that do not exhibit this problem.
∴EU theory is not uniformly subject to Rabin's calibration; different functional forms have different calibration properties — the theorem identifies a problem for specific forms, not for EU as a framework.
Argument IV — The correct lesson is methodological: do not cross-calibrate between stakes domains
1.The theorem establishes a constraint on how EU models should be calibrated: do not use small-stakes experimental evidence to calibrate utility functions for large-stakes applications.
2.Using domain-appropriate calibration — large-stakes field data for large-stakes models, income utility for small-stakes experiments — preserves EU's applicability while avoiding the calibration problem.
3.This methodological lesson is compatible with EU remaining a valid framework; the theorem demonstrates a calibration constraint, not a theoretical incoherence.
∴The Rabin theorem is best read as a methodological constraint on EU model calibration across stakes domains — not a refutation of EU theory, which remains valid with appropriately domain-specific calibrations.
Argument V — "Inherently" is too strong; the absurdity follows from auxiliary assumptions, not from EU per se
1.Rabin-Thaler claim the absurdity "follows inherently" from EU. But it follows only given: terminal wealth specification + constant risk aversion at all wealth levels + a specific functional form range. None is "inherent" to EU.
2.EU theory is flexible enough to accommodate EU of income, CRRA utility, partial asset integration — none of which generate Rabin's most dramatic calibration results.
3.Refuting a conjunction (EU + auxiliary assumptions) by showing the conjunction implies absurdities does not establish which element of the conjunction is false; the theorem does not identify EU as the false element.
∴"Inherently" overstates the theorem's scope; the absurdity follows from auxiliary assumptions that EU theory does not require; the correct target of the refutation is those assumptions, not EU itself.
