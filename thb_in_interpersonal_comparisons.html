Mr President,

The question before this House seems, at first glance, to be a technical one in the philosophy of economics. It is not. It is a question about whether rational, moral reasoning about social welfare is possible at all. For if interpersonal comparisons of welfare are genuinely impossible — if we cannot say, even in principle, whether one person is better or worse off than another — then moral reasoning about distributive justice, about how to organise society, about who deserves more and who has received less, becomes incoherent. The stakes are therefore very high.

The problem: utility functions represent ordinal preferences, unique only up to positive monotone transformation; no common unit, no interpersonal measuring rod appears available
Harsanyi's first solution (1953 — Impartial Observer): a rational agent behind a veil of ignorance, assigning equal probability to occupying each person's position, maximises expected utility ↠ average utilitarianism
Harsanyi's second solution (1955 — Aggregation Theorem): if social preference and each individual's preference satisfy expected-utility axioms, and social preference respects individual unanimity, then social welfare = weighted sum of individual utilities
Key concept: extended sympathy — imagining oneself in another's position with that person's tastes and circumstances On what interpersonal comparisons actually require

Impartial observer theorem: extended sympathy grounds IUCsThe opposition will say that utility functions are merely ordinal and that there is no scientific basis for comparing them across persons. I agree that raw ordinal preference rankings do not generate interpersonal comparisons. But this is not the end of the argument. It is the beginning. What grounds interpersonal comparisons is not the technical properties of utility functions but a different operation: extended sympathy. When I ask myself "how would I evaluate being in person A's position, with A's tastes, A's circumstances, A's preferences?" I am performing a different comparison from the mere ranking of outcomes for a single self. I am asking about hypothetical situations in which my identity is different. And it is this operation of imaginative empathy, formalised through the device of the impartial observer, that grounds interpersonal welfare comparisons.

On the impartial observer theorem

Impartial observer: equal probability over persons yields average utilitarianismConsider an observer who must assess social arrangements from behind a veil of ignorance — who does not know which person she will be. She assigns equal probability to occupying each person's position. She has preferences over lotteries that satisfy the von Neumann–Morgenstern expected utility axioms. She forms ethical preferences over social arrangements by imagining herself successively in each person's shoes, with that person's tastes and objective circumstances. Under these conditions, it can be shown that her ethical preferences over social arrangements correspond to maximising the arithmetic mean of individual utilities. This is not a value judgement imported from outside the framework. It is derivable from rationality axioms alone, combined with the device of extended sympathy.

On the aggregation theorem

Aggregation theorem: Bayesian rationality + individual unanimity entails utilitarian SWFMy 1955 result provides an independent grounding. If each individual's preferences over lotteries satisfy the expected-utility axioms, and if the social welfare ordering satisfies the same axioms, and if whenever all individuals are indifferent between two alternatives the social ordering is also indifferent — what I call the Pareto indifference condition — then it follows that social welfare is a weighted sum of individual utility functions. The weights express the relative importance given to different persons' utilities. Interpersonal comparisons are implicitly required by the structure of rational social choice, even without the veil-of-ignorance device. The aggregation theorem shows that any rational social welfare function must make interpersonal comparisons.

On the objection from personal identity

Against the personal identity objection to extended sympathyThe opposition will argue that imaginative empathy cannot be fully coherent — that I cannot genuinely place myself in another's position with another's preferences, because if I had different preferences I would be a different person. This objection is philosophically interesting but practically overstated. We perform exercises of imaginative empathy constantly in ordinary moral life, in literature, in legal reasoning about reasonable persons, and in political philosophy. The impartial observer thought experiment does not require the metaphysically strong claim that I become another person. It requires only that I have preferences over hypothetical situations described in terms of another person's tastes and circumstances — and this is something we demonstrably do. The philosophical puzzles about personal identity at the margins do not undermine the core operation.

On the practical significance of grounding comparisons in rationality

Practical necessity: no welfare economics without IUCsI want to close by noting what is at stake in accepting the opposition's position. If interpersonal comparisons are genuinely impossible — not merely difficult, but impossible in principle — then welfare economics is reduced to the Pareto criterion alone: we can only rank social arrangements as better when someone gains and no one loses. But the most important social questions — how to tax, how to distribute healthcare, how to adjudicate between competing claims on scarce resources — all involve situations where gains for some come at costs to others. The Pareto criterion has nothing to say about them. Robbins's positivist squeamishness about interpersonal comparisons does not make these questions go away. It simply renders economics silent in the face of the questions that matter most.

The comparisons are not merely possible. They are unavoidable. Every act of social choice implicitly makes them. The question is whether we make them honestly and rigorously, or whether we pretend we are not making them at all.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to approach this question differently from Professor Harsanyi. He has argued that interpersonal comparisons are possible because of a specific formal operation — extended sympathy via the impartial observer. I want to make a less ambitious but more robust argument: that partial interpersonal comparisons are possible and are sufficient for the practical and moral purposes that drive our interest in welfare comparisons in the first place. The demand for full cardinal comparability — for a common utility unit that would allow us to sum welfare precisely across persons — is far more demanding than anything we actually need, and its impossibility should not distract us from the partial comparisons we can make and do make every day.

On partial comparability and why full comparability is not required

Partial comparability is sufficient for welfare analysisConsider the claim that, despite whatever utility Nero derived from it, the Great Fire of Rome made the world worse off overall. This claim involves an interpersonal comparison — that the misery of thousands of Romans fleeing their burning homes outweighed whatever pleasure the Emperor took in the spectacle. We do not need a precise cardinal measure of Nero's pleasure and the Romans' suffering to sustain this judgement. We need only partial comparability: the capacity to make some interpersonal welfare comparisons, in some cases, with sufficient confidence to support practical conclusions. Such partial comparability is not only possible but is exercised constantly in moral, legal, and political reasoning. The impossibilist position confuses the absence of a precise universal measure with the absence of any meaningful comparison.

On capabilities as a workable basis for interpersonal comparison

Capabilities and functionings provide a non-utility basis for comparisonThe search for interpersonal comparisons in the utility space is, I have argued, the wrong place to look. Utility — whether construed as hedonic experience or preference satisfaction — is problematic as a comparative measure precisely because it is subjective: a person who is very good at deriving pleasure from very little will register high utility despite severely impoverished circumstances. The comparison we need for justice is not a comparison of felt satisfaction but a comparison of the substantive freedoms people have to live the kinds of lives they have reason to value. The capabilities approach — focusing on what people are genuinely able to do and to be — provides a basis for interpersonal comparison that is both meaningful and less susceptible to the adaptive preference problem than utility comparisons.

On the intersection approach and incomplete but meaningful orderings

Intersection approach: incomplete orderings are workable for social choiceI have developed what I call the intersection approach to interpersonal comparisons: the idea that we can identify welfare orderings that are agreed upon across a range of different evaluative frameworks, without requiring convergence on a single complete cardinal ranking. If multiple reasonable conceptions of welfare all agree that person A is worse off than person B in situation S, that convergent judgement is meaningful and usable for policy, even if the different frameworks disagree about how much worse off. This intersection produces incomplete but non-trivial comparisons. Social choice theory can work with such incomplete orderings. The opposition's demand for completeness before accepting any comparison sets a standard that no theory meets and that no practical purpose requires.

On the Robbins challenge and the informational basis of social choice

Against Robbins: the informational basis of social choice must be expandedLionel Robbins argued that since interpersonal comparisons are value judgements rather than empirical facts, they have no place in scientific economics. But this argument proves too much. Virtually every evaluative judgement that enters policy analysis involves value commitments that go beyond pure logical implication from observable facts. The question is not whether we can keep evaluative judgements out of economics — we cannot — but how to make them carefully, explicitly, and in ways that are open to reasoned scrutiny. Expanding the informational basis of social choice beyond the purely ordinal preferences that Robbins permits is not a violation of scientific rigour. It is a recognition that restricting information to ordinal preference profiles produces impoverished and sometimes morally indefensible social choices.

On why the impossibilist position is itself value-laden

The impossibilist position is itself a value judgement, not a neutral scientific stanceI want to close by turning the opposition's argument against itself. Robbins and Hausman present the denial of interpersonal comparability as a position of scientific neutrality — as the refusal to smuggle value judgements into economics. But the restriction to Pareto-only welfare economics is itself a value judgement: it assigns zero weight to equality, to distributional concerns, to the relative welfare of the better and worse off. It is not value-neutral to adopt the Pareto criterion; it is value-laden in a particular way that strongly favours those who begin better off. The pretence of neutrality conceals a substantive ethical commitment. Acknowledging that interpersonal comparisons are possible and required is the more honest position.

We need not choose between full cardinal comparability and none at all. We can have enough comparability to do what justice requires, through capability comparisons, intersection methods, and partial orderings. That is the position this House should defend.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to make a focused argument about what is required for a workable public standard of welfare — the kind that can ground interpersonal comparisons for the purposes of justice in a pluralistic society. My argument draws on both welfare economics and the philosophy of social justice, and it leads to a conclusion that is more modest than Harsanyi's but more robust: a bounded public welfare standard that is preference-sensitive in a specific sense does make meaningful interpersonal comparisons possible.

On what a public standard of welfare must do

Requirements for a public welfare standard in a pluralistic societyFor a standard of welfare to serve as the basis for interpersonal comparisons in a pluralistic society, it must satisfy two requirements simultaneously. First, it must be preference-sensitive: it must, to the greatest reasonable extent, reflect what each person actually wants for herself and give these things the weight she herself assigns to them. Second, it must be publicly accessible: it must be something that people with different, reasonable conceptions of the good can nonetheless recognise as a standard that applies to them. These requirements are in tension, because people's preferences about their own welfare differ. But the tension is not intractable, and resolving it yields a specific, workable approach to interpersonal comparison.

On the bounded welfare function and preference change

Bounded welfare function: upper and lower bounds from cross-type preference agreementMy approach assigns welfare values to (state of the world, person, preference type) triples. A crucial feature is that this welfare function is bounded above and below in a specific way. The upper bound is determined by what a person with any reasonable preference type would regard as among the best possible situations for someone in that position. The lower bound is determined by what any reasonable preference type would regard as among the worst. Between these bounds, the welfare function tracks the degree of preference satisfaction relative to these anchors. The bounds do not depend on a single preference type and are therefore accessible across different conceptions of the good. This structure makes the welfare function publicly anchored in a way that single-preference-type accounts are not.

On preference change and why it reveals the limits of pure preference satisfaction

Preference change undermines pure preference satisfaction theory; welfare must be boundedI have argued that the fact that people can change their preferences about which preferences it is good for them to have reveals a deep problem for any attempt to ground welfare entirely in preference satisfaction. If welfare is just the degree to which current preferences are satisfied, then a person who successfully changes her preferences to fit worse circumstances — who adapts to deprivation, learns to want less — appears to be doing as well or better as her welfare increases after adaptation. But this result is clearly wrong. The welfare function must be anchored at upper and lower bounds that are not themselves preference-relative, so that adaptive preference change does not automatically improve welfare. These anchors provide the structure that makes interpersonal comparisons possible across persons with different preference types.

On the thought experiment that reveals the public character of welfare bounds

Imaginative thought experiment: what any preference type would recognise as best/worstHere is the thought experiment that reveals the bounds. Suppose you are asked to evaluate two situations: one in which you are a person in good health with secure material circumstances and good social relationships, and one in which you are a person suffering severe chronic pain, extreme poverty, and social isolation. Whatever your preference type — whatever specific things you value in life — you would, I submit, recognise the second situation as worse for the person in it. This cross-type agreement is not accidental. It reflects the fact that certain conditions — severe suffering, extreme deprivation, social exclusion — undermine the pursuit of any reasonable life-plan whatever it is. And certain conditions — health, material sufficiency, social connection — support the pursuit of most reasonable life-plans. These convergent judgements provide the public bounds that anchor interpersonal comparisons.

On why this approach is superior to both pure preference satisfaction and pure objective list theories

Bounded welfare standard superior to pure preference theory and pure objective listMy approach avoids the two standard failures. Pure preference satisfaction theory fails because it is too sensitive to adaptive preferences and preference change — and because it generates no intersubjective anchors for comparison, as Greaves will argue. Pure objective list theories fail because they impose a single conception of the good on persons who reasonably disagree about what constitutes a good life. The bounded preference-sensitive welfare standard lies between them: it is responsive to individual preference variation between the bounds but anchored at bounds that are accessible across diverse reasonable preference types. This structure makes meaningful interpersonal comparisons possible while respecting the pluralism that characterises just societies.

We do not need Harsanyi's strong machinery, nor Sen's capabilities alone. A bounded public welfare standard, anchored by what any reasonable preference type would recognise as the best and worst conditions for a human life, provides the structure we need.

Mr President,

I want to begin by insisting on a distinction that the proposition has already obscured: the distinction between the question of whether interpersonal comparisons are possible and the question of whether they are desirable. I am here to argue that they are not possible as empirical, scientific propositions. They may be made as value judgements — as ethical commitments about how different persons' welfare ought to be weighted against each other. But that is not the same thing. And conflating the two is, I will argue, both intellectually dishonest and practically dangerous.

On the ordinal nature of utility and the absence of a common measure

Utility is ordinal; there is no common interpersonal measuring rodThe fundamental point is straightforward. Utility, as economics uses the term, is a representation of ordinal preferences over outcomes. If person A prefers outcome X to outcome Y, we assign a higher utility number to X. The numbers have no significance beyond their ordering: we can apply any positive monotone transformation to A's utility function and preserve everything that is economically meaningful about it. Now suppose we wish to compare A's utility gain from some policy with B's utility loss. We are comparing numbers from two different ordinal scales that have been independently constructed. There is no procedure, no operation, no empirical observation that can tell us whether the numbers are comparable. The idea that A's utility of 7 bears any determinate relationship to B's utility of 5 is a category error.

On why interpersonal comparisons are value judgements, not scientific facts

IUCs are value judgements masquerading as factsWhen economists speak of comparing different persons' welfare, they are not making empirical claims that could, even in principle, be tested against observable evidence. They are making ethical commitments about whose preferences should count for more and in what proportion. These commitments may be reasonable and important. But they are not the kind of thing that a science of economics can establish. They require a political or moral decision about the relative weighting of persons' interests. To dress up this political decision in the technical language of utility comparisons is not to give it scientific legitimacy. It is to obscure its genuinely normative character behind a facade of mathematical rigour.

On the old utilitarian error and Pareto

The utilitarian error: diminishing marginal utility does not justify redistribution scientificallyThe old utilitarian economists — Pigou, Edgeworth — assumed that interpersonal comparisons were scientifically valid. They argued that because of diminishing marginal utility, transferring income from the rich to the poor would increase aggregate welfare. This argument is not merely questionable. It is invalid. The claim that a pound of income means more to a poor person than to a rich person is not a scientific claim about measurable magnitudes. It is a value judgement about how much weight to give to their respective utilities. We can accept this value judgement — many do — but we must acknowledge it for what it is. The Pareto criterion, which recommends improvements only when no one loses, is the only welfare criterion that does not require interpersonal comparisons and therefore the only one with a secure scientific foundation.

On the response to the proposition's practical arguments

The practical need for IUCs does not establish their possibilityThe proposition — and Professor Harsanyi in particular — argues that we must be able to make interpersonal comparisons because without them welfare economics cannot address the most important policy questions. This argument is entirely correct about the practical need. It is entirely invalid as an argument for the possibility of the comparisons. The fact that we need something does not establish that we have it. We may also wish for the ability to measure the temperature of justice or the weight of freedom. Our need for these measures does not bring them into existence. The honest response to the practical need is not to pretend that interpersonal utility comparisons are possible as empirical facts. It is to acknowledge that when we go beyond the Pareto criterion, we are making ethical choices that should be made openly and democratically, not hidden inside utility functions.

On the virtue of intellectual honesty about the limits of economic science

Intellectual honesty requires distinguishing science from ethics in welfare judgementsI close with a plea for intellectual honesty. The hard questions of distributive justice — how to tax, how to allocate healthcare, how to weigh the interests of different persons — are genuine moral and political questions. They require moral and political answers, arrived at through democratic deliberation. When economists smuggle value judgements about interpersonal welfare comparisons into ostensibly scientific analyses, they are not helping resolve these questions. They are pre-empting democratic deliberation by presenting one set of value commitments as if they were scientific findings. The explicit acknowledgment that interpersonal comparisons are value judgements, not scientific facts, is the condition under which honest moral and political reasoning about distribution can begin.

Economic science is limited. But its limits are honest. The proposition asks us to pretend those limits do not exist, in the service of conclusions it has already decided to reach.

Mr President,

I want to engage with the proposition's arguments at a philosophical level that Robbins did not fully reach. His argument is that interpersonal utility comparisons are value judgements rather than scientific facts. This is correct, and important. But it does not fully address the philosophical attempts by Harsanyi, Voorhoeve, and others to ground interpersonal comparisons in imaginative projection, extended preferences, or bounded welfare standards. I want to show that these attempts fail — not because they are ethically objectionable, but because they face deep conceptual difficulties that cannot be resolved by technical refinement.

On the fundamental problem: welfare theories are contested

No single theory of welfare can ground IUCs; welfare theories are deeply contestedThe central problem with any attempt to ground interpersonal welfare comparisons is this: welfare itself is a contested concept. The main candidates — hedonism (welfare as pleasurable experience), preference satisfaction (welfare as the satisfaction of one's preferences), and objective list theories (welfare as possession of specific goods) — make radically different claims about what welfare is. Any attempt to ground interpersonal comparisons must presuppose one of these theories or some combination of them. But each theory faces serious objections, and there is no philosophical consensus on which is correct. Interpersonal comparisons grounded in, say, hedonism will be systematically different from those grounded in preference satisfaction. There is no theory-neutral basis for the comparisons.

On why an absolute welfare scale conflates theories

Absolute welfare scale conflates preference satisfaction with mental-state theoriesSome have proposed grounding interpersonal comparisons in an absolute welfare scale — an anchored measure of welfare with a determinate zero point and common unit. I argued in my 1995 paper that this proposal faces a fatal difficulty. An absolute scale of the kind required would need to identify a zero point — a state of neither positive nor negative welfare — and a common unit of welfare change. But any such scale must implicitly commit to a theory of what welfare is. If welfare is preference satisfaction, the zero point is when no preferences are satisfied and none frustrated — a condition that is essentially incoherent, since all living persons have some preferences. If welfare is hedonic experience, the zero point is the neutral state between pleasure and pain. These zero points are different, and there is no principled way to select between them without already having resolved the dispute between welfare theories.

On the limits of imaginative empathy

Extended sympathy faces the problem of preference opacity across personsHarsanyi's extended sympathy and the impartial observer device require that I can imaginatively project myself into another person's position with that person's tastes and circumstances, and thereby compare how well off I would be in their position with how well off they are. But this projection faces a serious obstacle. The subjective character of welfare — how things feel from the inside for a person with genuinely different psychological constitution and life history — is not fully accessible to imaginative projection. I can imagine having your circumstances. I cannot straightforwardly imagine having your subjective experience of those circumstances as it actually is for you. The imaginative operation Harsanyi requires involves assumptions about cross-personal psychological continuity that are far stronger than mere circumstantial imagination.

On the adaptive preference problem for Voorhoeve's approach

Voorhoeve's bounded welfare standard cannot escape the theory-dependence problemVoorhoeve argues that bounds determined by what any reasonable preference type would recognise as best and worst conditions provide theory-neutral anchors. But what counts as a best or worst condition depends on what theory of welfare is operative. On a hedonist account, severe chronic pain clearly constitutes the worst conditions. On a preference satisfaction account, the worst conditions are those in which the person's most central preferences are frustrated. On an objective list account, the worst conditions are those in which the most basic objective goods are absent. These accounts will sometimes agree and sometimes disagree about which conditions are worst. When they agree, the agreement is either trivial or secretly depends on a substantive theory. When they disagree, there is no theory-neutral way to adjudicate.

On the practical consequences of impossibilism for welfare economics

Impossibilism forces welfare economics to be honest about its value commitmentsThe proposition argues that accepting impossibilism makes welfare economics useless for policy. I want to reverse this argument. The practical consequence of accepting impossibilism is not paralysis. It is honesty. When a policy analyst compares the welfare gains and losses of different persons, she is making a value commitment about how those welfare levels relate. If she acknowledges that this is a value commitment — that it rests on one theory of welfare rather than another, that reasonable people may disagree — she is practising welfare economics honestly. If she presents the comparison as if it were a scientific finding grounded in individuals' own utilities, she is practising it dishonestly. Impossibilism is not the death of welfare economics. It is the demand that welfare economists acknowledge what they are actually doing.

The impossibility of interpersonal comparisons is not a counsel of despair. It is a counsel of honesty. The proposition's various attempts to circumvent it do not succeed.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to target the most technically sophisticated attempt to ground interpersonal welfare comparisons within the preference-satisfaction framework: the extended preferences programme that traces back to Harsanyi and has been developed by a number of more recent philosophers. My argument, developed with Harvey Lederman, is that this programme faces a decisive trilemma from which it cannot escape. Whatever form the extended preferences account takes, it generates either inconsistency, massive incomparability, or deeply unattractive holism. If the proposition cannot defend the extended preferences programme, it has lost its most promising technical strategy for grounding IUCs within preference theory.

On what extended preferences are supposed to do

Extended preferences: the programme and its ambitionAn extended preference is, roughly, a preference over situations whose description includes not just outcomes but also the agents whose preferences those outcomes involve. When you have an extended preference, you are expressing a preference over packages of the form "being in situation S with the preferences of person P." The extended preference programme holds that such preferences, if they satisfy appropriate conditions, generate interpersonal welfare comparisons: if you prefer being in my situation with my preferences to being in your situation with your preferences, then this reveals that my situation is welfare-superior to yours. This is the formal version of Harsanyi's impartial observer and of imaginative empathy more generally.

On the first horn: inconsistency with ordinary preference-satisfaction theory

Horn 1: extended preferences inconsistent with ordinary preference-satisfaction theoryThe first horn of the trilemma. The extended preferences programme, if it is to generate welfare comparisons that cohere with ordinary preference satisfaction theory, must ensure that the welfare ordering it generates for each person agrees with that person's ordinary preferences about her own life. But this constraint — what we call "respect for individual preferences" — is not automatically satisfied by extended preference orderings. When the observer imagines being in another person's shoes with another person's preferences, the resulting welfare comparison may conflict with what that other person actually prefers about her own life. If the extended preferences programme overrides individuals' own preferences about what is good for them, it has abandoned the preference-satisfaction framework it was supposed to defend.

On the second horn: massive incomparability

Horn 2: extended preferences that respect individual preferences yield massive incomparabilitySuppose we insist that the welfare ordering generated by extended preferences must respect each person's own preferences about her life. Then we face the second horn. Different persons have radically different preferences about what makes for a good life. When an observer imagines successively occupying the position of a person who values achievement over pleasure and a person who values pleasure over achievement, the welfare comparisons she reaches by imagining herself in each person's shoes will be sensitive to which preferences she brings to the imaginative exercise. The comparisons will be systematically incomplete whenever the two persons' preference orderings over their respective situations diverge. In many real cases — cases of genuine value pluralism — this incomparability will be massive.

On the third horn: unattractive holism

Horn 3: avoiding incomparability requires unattractive holismTo avoid massive incomparability, the extended preferences theorist might adopt a holistic approach: defining welfare not on the basis of individual preferences over specific life-aspects but on the basis of some integrated, holistic assessment of whole lives or whole persons. This allows the observer to rank lives without being torn between conflicting preference types. But this holism is deeply unattractive for two reasons. First, it abandons the preference-satisfaction foundation by imposing a welfare ordering that is not grounded in individuals' own preferences. Second, it makes welfare attributions profoundly ungroundable: on what basis does the holistic assessment proceed, if not on the individual preferences it has purported to transcend? The holism is not a solution; it is an evasion.

On the implications for the proposition's case

The trilemma shows the extended preferences programme cannot ground IUCs in preference theoryThe trilemma shows that the extended preferences programme cannot consistently deliver rich interpersonal welfare comparisons within the preference-satisfaction framework. The three horns correspond to three different ways of developing the programme, and each fails. If the proposition wishes to ground interpersonal comparisons in preference theory — as Harsanyi, and to a lesser extent Voorhoeve, attempt to do — the extended preferences route is not available. They must either abandon preference theory, adopt a different theory of welfare that makes interpersonal comparisons more tractable but faces Hausman's objections, or accept that the comparisons they need cannot be generated from the resources available. The trilemma is not a technical obstacle to be overcome by further refinement. It is a structural feature of the relationship between preference satisfaction and interpersonal comparison.

The extended preferences programme was the most promising technical strategy for grounding interpersonal comparisons within preference theory. It fails. The proposition must reckon with this.

Thank you.
