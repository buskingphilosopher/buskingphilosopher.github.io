Motion before the House
"This House Believes the Iraq War was an Unjust War"
Proposition Speaking for the motion
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr President,

The just war tradition, refined over centuries from Aquinas to the present day, sets a demanding but coherent test for armed conflict. A war must be undertaken for a just cause, with right intention, by competent authority, as a last resort, and with reasonable prospect that good will outweigh harm. The Iraq war of 2003 failed, to a greater or lesser degree, on every single one of these counts. Taken together, those failures are not a collection of quibbles. They are a cumulative indictment.

Just Cause — a vanishing foundation

The argument from just causeThe declared basis for military action was the disarmament of Iraq's weapons of mass destruction. We now know, from the Iraq Survey Group, that Iraq had no militarily significant WMD stocks by the mid-1990s. The foundation of the just cause had, in the most literal sense, disappeared. It is true that the intelligence errors were genuine rather than fabricated — shared, indeed, by French, German and Russian agencies. But the just war tradition sets the evidential bar deliberately high, because the suffering caused by war is certain while the gains are not. The intelligence on which the case rested was, in the words of the Butler report, "sporadic and patchy." That is not a sufficient foundation for unleashing war.

Competent Authority — acting without the world

The argument from competent authorityThe British government rightly sought a second UN Security Council resolution explicitly authorising force. It failed to obtain one — not because of a veto by one recalcitrant power, but because there was no broad international consensus for action. A claim to act on behalf of the United Nations requires more than a legal argument about earlier resolutions; it requires evidence that military enforcement is the avowed wish of the international community. That evidence was absent.

Last Resort — inspectors cut short

The argument from last resortUNMOVIC inspectors had been in Iraq only since November 2002. By March 2003 they were beginning to report greater Iraqi cooperation. The condition of last resort requires that all reasonable alternatives be genuinely exhausted. Cutting short an inspection regime that was showing results does not meet that condition. The rush to war precluded the very process that might have resolved the question of WMD without a shot fired.

Proportion — a catastrophically miscalculated balance sheet

The argument from proportionalityThe most serious charge against those who planned the Iraq war is that they massively underestimated the harm military action would cause. No WMD were found. Over 100,000 civilians died — not at the hands of coalition forces alone, but as a direct consequence of the power vacuum the invasion created. The insurgency, the sectarian carnage, the collapse of civil order: none of this was adequately anticipated, because planners applied worst-case assessments to Saddam's capabilities and best-case assessments to the aftermath. That asymmetry of imagination was not a misfortune. It was a moral failure.

A government that could not have secured democratic support in 2003 for a war expected to cost 100,000 civilian lives had no business launching one on the assumption that it would not.

Conduct and just peace

The argument from just conduct and peaceThe just war tradition requires not only a just entry into war but a just prosecution and a just exit. The assault on Fallujah, the abuse at Abu Ghraib, the death of Baha Mousa in British custody — these were not isolated lapses but symptomatic of an occupation for which there were no robust plans to restore civil governance, maintain law and order, or achieve a stable and democratic peace. Noble intentions, as the tradition teaches, are not enough.

The verdict

The doubts over each condition did not stand in isolation. They reinforced one another. Weak just cause undermined the claim to competent authority. Lack of international consensus cast doubt on whether last resort had been reached. Uncertainty about the justice of the cause amplified concern that the balance of harm and good could not be struck. And the absence of adequate post-war planning ensured that even the limited goods achieved came at a price no honest pre-war accounting could have justified.

Noble aspirations are not enough to justify armed conflict. The Iraq war was not merely imperfect. It was, by the standards of the tradition our civilisation has developed precisely for moments like this, unjust.

Thank you.

Opposition Speaking against the motion
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I do not come before this House to defend every decision taken in the planning and prosecution of the Iraq war. The failures were real, and some were morally culpable. But the question before us is not whether the war was perfectly executed. It is whether the war was unjust. And that is a considerably harder case to make — one the proposition has not, I submit, succeeded in making.

Just cause — an atrocious regime

The argument from just causeIn the Christian just war tradition, just cause is found in the righting of a grave wrong done to neighbours — specifically, the mass murder of a state's own citizens. Saddam Hussein's regime killed at least 400,000 of its own people between 1988 and 2003: 100,000 in the Anfal campaign against the Kurds alone, many with chemical weapons. Human Rights Watch classified this as genocidal. That is not merely sufficient just cause — it is overwhelming just cause. The proposition has spoken of WMD as if that were the only conceivable basis for intervention. But a genocidal regime does not require the additional charge of WMD possession to furnish just cause for military action. WMD augmented the cause and gave distant nations an interest in it. It was not the cause's foundation.

Legality is not morality

The argument from law vs. moralityMuch of the case against the war rests on its alleged illegality. But legality and morality are not the same thing. UN Resolution 1441 was the product of political consensus achieved through deliberate ambiguity — some read it as authorising force, others did not. There is no international court with authority to settle the matter. More fundamentally: positive law is always subject to moral law. NATO's intervention in Kosovo was, strictly speaking, illegal — Russia would have vetoed Security Council authorisation. Yet most people now regard it as legitimate. If illegality were sufficient to establish injustice, those who plotted to assassinate Hitler would be criminals. They were heroes.

Civilian casualties — a false standard

The argument from civilian casualtiesThe proposition cites over 100,000 civilian deaths as proof of the war's injustice. But the liberation of Europe from Nazism — almost universally regarded as a just war — cost 70,000 French civilian lives and some 500,000 German ones through Allied bombing. More critically: the vast majority of Iraqi civilian deaths were directly caused not by coalition forces but by insurgents — Sunni, Shia and Al-Qaeda — who chose to send suicide bombers into marketplaces. An agent is not equally responsible for the reactions of other agents. The insurgents had a moral obligation not to target civilians. That they chose to violate it does not transfer their guilt to those who removed a genocidal dictator.

Last resort — containment was collapsing

The argument from last resortWas there a viable alternative to military action? Containment is the obvious candidate — but containment was failing. Since 1997, France, Russia and China had been pressing for relaxed sanctions in pursuit of oil contracts and debts owed. China was constructing a fibre-optic communications system for Iraqi air defences. The no-fly zones were straining US military capacity to breaking point. Security Council members were breaking sanctions before the invasion; there was no reason to expect greater resolve after. Dr David Kelly — no hawk, a former UN weapons inspector — concluded shortly before the invasion that military force was "regrettably the only way of finally and conclusively disarming Iraq." That is a serious judgement from a serious person.

Flawed execution, not unjust war

The argument from flawed execution vs. unjust warThe gravest errors of the war — the disbanding of the Iraqi army, the inadequate troop numbers for post-war security, the abuses at Abu Ghraib — were real and some were morally culpable. But consider: the war against Hitler was also morally vitiated. Roosevelt fabricated the Greer incident. Bomber Harris pursued vengeance. The Red Army raped an estimated two million German women. Allied forces shot German prisoners in cold blood during Normandy. Eastern Europe was delivered to Stalin. Most wars, even just ones, are morally flawed. The existence of grave errors does not, by itself, establish that a war was unjust overall.

Right intention — proven over time

The argument from right intentionRight intention is not merely declared at the outset; it is demonstrated by what follows. After the failures of the initial occupation, the coalition did not walk away. It compensated, at great cost, over six years. The surge under General Petraeus achieved considerable success. And the young professional Iraqis who visited Oxford in 2011 — people who had lived under Saddam and under the occupation — were asked directly: should the invasion have happened? Their spokesman answered without hesitation: "It was good that it happened. It could have been done better. And it isn't over." We would do well to weigh that testimony before delivering our verdict.

The verdict

The Iraq war was badly planned and imperfectly prosecuted. Some of its errors were morally culpable. None of that makes it unjust. Just cause existed — in the form of a genocidal regime that had murdered hundreds of thousands of its own citizens. The legal authority was disputed, not non-existent. The alternatives to force were exhausted in any realistic assessment. And right intention, however imperfectly expressed at the outset, proved itself through sustained commitment over years. This House should reject the motion.

Thank you.
