Poetry Analysis

Ars Poetica, lines 1–37

Horace (Quintus Horatius Flaccus) — c. 19–18 BCE
Translation: A. S. Kline (2005), Poetry in Translation Passage: On unity and harmony (AP 1–37)
Structure & Context
Form: Verse epistle — dactylic hexameter in Latin original Addressees: The Pisones — a father (likely Lucius Calpurnius Piso) and his two sons Argument: The opening 37 lines establish the poem's foundational principle: unity (unitas) above all Method: Satirical demonstration before prescription — the grotesque precedes the rule Genre: Ars — a technical treatise in verse, combining instruction with entertainment Structural role: AP 1–37 functions as the theoretical foundation for all prescriptions that follow
I — The Grotesque and the Principle of Unity (1–13)

1If a painter had chosen to set a human head 2On a horse's neck, covered a melding of limbs, 3Everywhere, with multi-coloured plumage, so 4That what was a lovely woman, at the top, 5Ended repulsively in the tail of a black fish: 6Asked to a viewing, could you stifle laughter, my friends? 7Believe me, a book would be like such a picture, 8Dear Pisos, if its idle fancies were so conceived 9That neither its head nor foot could be related 10To a unified form. 'But painters and poets 11Have always shared the right to dare anything.' 12I know it: I claim that licence, and grant it in turn: 13But not so the wild and tame should ever mate, 14Or snakes couple with birds, or lambs with tigers.

II — Purple Patches and Misplaced Ornament (14–23)

15Weighty openings and grand declarations often 16Have one or two purple patches tacked on, that gleam 17Far and wide, when Diana's grove and her altar, 18The winding stream hastening through lovely fields, 19Or the river Rhine, or the rainbow's being described. 20There's no place for them here. Perhaps you know how 21To draw a cypress tree: so what, if you've been given 22Money to paint a sailor plunging from a shipwreck 23In despair? It started out as a wine-jar: then why, 24As the wheel turns round does it end up a pitcher? 25In short let it be what you wish, but whole and natural.

III — The Catalogue of Failures (25–37)

26Most poets (dear sir, and you sons worthy of your sire), 27Are beguiled by accepted form. I try to be brief 28And become obscure: aiming at smoothness I fail 29In strength and spirit: claiming grandeur he's turgid: 30Too cautious, fearing the blast, he crawls on the ground: 31But the man who wants to distort something unnaturally 32Paints a dolphin among the trees, a boar in the waves. 33Avoiding faults leads to error, if art is lacking.

IV — The Bronze-Smith and the Crooked Nose (34–37)

34The humblest craftsman, down by Aemilius' School, 35Who moulds finger-nails in bronze, imitates wavy hair, 36Is unhappy with the result, because he's unable 37To create a whole. Now if I wished to cast something, 38I'd no more wish to be him, than live with a crooked 39Nose, though admired for my jet-black eyes and black hair.
