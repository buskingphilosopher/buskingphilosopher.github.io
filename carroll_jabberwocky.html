Jabberwocky

Lewis Carroll — Through the Looking-Glass, and What Alice Found There (1871)
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
Structure & Context
Form: Seven quatrains, ABAB rhyme, ballad metre — the metrical form of heroic narrative verse Frame: The first stanza returns verbatim as the last — the poem is enclosed by itself Syntax vs. lexicon: Nearly every content word (noun, verb, adjective) is invented; nearly every function word (articles, pronouns, conjunctions, verb endings) is standard English — the grammar is real even though the vocabulary is not Carroll's own gloss: In the same book, the character Humpty Dumpty explains several of the poem's words as deliberate blends of two existing words — what Carroll called 'portmanteau' words Words that entered English: 'galumphing' and 'chortled' are both Carroll's coinages from this poem and are now standard dictionary entries Narrative arc: A warning, an arming, a wait, a battle, a victory, a celebration — the complete structure of a quest narrative, told entirely in invented vocabulary

'Twas brillig, and the slithy toves Did gyre and gimble in the wabe: All mimsy were the borogoves, And the mome raths outgrabe. Beware the Jabberwock, my son! The jaws that bite, the claws that catch! Beware the Jubjub bird, and shun The frumious Bandersnatch!" He took his vorpal sword in hand: Long time the manxome foe he sought— So rested he by the Tumtum tree, And stood awhile in thought. And, as in uffish thought he stood, The Jabberwock, with eyes of flame, Came whiffling through the tulgey wood, And burbled as it came! One, two! One, two! And through and through The vorpal blade went snicker-snack! He left it dead, and with its head He went galumphing back. "And hast thou slain the Jabberwock? Come to my arms, my beamish boy! O frabjous day! Callooh! Callay!" He chortled in his joy. 'Twas brillig, and the slithy toves
Did gyre and gimble in the wabe:
All mimsy were the borogoves,
And the mome raths outgrabe.
