Mr Speaker,

I wish to begin by inviting every member of this House to participate in a thought experiment that I first presented in 1961, and which has lost none of its force in the decades since. It is not a philosophical abstraction. It is a direct probe of what rationality requires — and what it does not.

Before you are two urns. Urn I contains exactly 50 red balls and 50 black balls. Urn II contains 100 balls, each of which is either red or black, in an unknown proportion. You are offered four bets. Bet A: £100 if you draw red from Urn I. Bet B: £100 if you draw black from Urn I. Bet C: £100 if you draw red from Urn II. Bet D: £100 if you draw black from Urn II. Almost universally, people prefer A to C and B to D. They prefer the known urn. And they do so upon reflection. This is the Ellsberg Paradox.

Why the paradox violates Savage’s Sure-Thing Principle.

Savage’s framework requires that you hold a unique subjective probability for every event. If you prefer A to C, you are implicitly asserting that the probability of red from Urn I (0.5) is higher than the probability of red from Urn II — i.e. P(red|II) < 0.5, implying P(black|II) > 0.5. But if P(black|II) > 0.5, you should prefer D over B, since Urn II offers a better chance of black. Yet you prefer B. Your choices are therefore self-contradictory within the SEU framework.

But here is my central contention: they are not self-contradictory. They reflect a perfectly coherent response to a qualitatively different epistemic situation. The two urns do not present the same kind of uncertainty. Urn I presents risk — measurable, quantifiable probability. Urn II presents ambiguity — unmeasurable, unquantifiable uncertainty. These are not the same thing, and treating them identically is not a triumph of rationality but a suppression of relevant information.
i. the risk-ambiguity distinction is real and epistemically significant

Risk vs ambiguity — Knight's distinction; Urn I gives measurable risk, Urn II gives Knightian uncertainty; collapsing them discards relevant informationFrank Knight drew this distinction in 1921: risk is uncertainty that can be expressed as a numerical probability because the underlying generating process is known; ambiguity (or Knightian uncertainty) is uncertainty that cannot be so expressed because the generating process is unknown. Urn I is a case of pure risk: the generating mechanism is a 50/50 draw. Urn II is a case of pure ambiguity: the generating mechanism is unknown. These are different epistemic situations, and a rational agent should respond to them differently. The Savage framework, by insisting that the rational agent always assign a unique subjective probability to every event, forces the agent to treat these qualitatively different situations as identical. This is not the height of rationality. It is a dogma — the dogma that all uncertainty can always be represented numerically.

ii. competent decision-theorists endorse the paradox upon reflection

Expert endorsement — Savage himself initially showed Ellsberg preferences; reversal under pressure, not reflection; competent agents endorse ambiguity aversion on reviewWhen I first presented this paradox, one of the participants who initially showed Ellsberg preferences was Savage himself. He caught himself, recognised the violation, and corrected it — but only under the pressure of the theoretical framework he had already committed to. This is precisely my point. The correction was not motivated by reflection on the epistemic situation but by allegiance to a pre-committed theoretical framework. When subjects are asked, without the framework looming over them, whether they endorse their Ellsberg preferences on reflection, the great majority do. They can articulate exactly why they prefer the known urn: they know what they are dealing with in Urn I; they do not know what they are dealing with in Urn II; and in a one-shot decision, this difference matters. This is not confusion. It is epistemic sensitivity.

iii. ambiguity aversion is rational in one-shot, high-stakes decisions

One-shot decisions — large-number frequency reasoning doesn't apply; in high-stakes single decisions, hedging against the unknown worst case is rationalThe opposition will argue that in the long run, with many repetitions, the law of large numbers vindicates Bayesian reasoning: your subjective probability should converge to the true frequency. This is true in the long run. But the Ellsberg problem is a single decision, not a sequence of repeated gambles. When you bet once, on something that matters, against an adversary who may have chosen the proportion in Urn II strategically — or even just against nature, which has no obligation to be kind to your priors — there is a genuine argument for caution. The Savage framework is built on the assumption of repeated decisions where long-run frequency arguments apply. In genuine one-shot decision problems, this assumption fails, and ambiguity aversion is not merely understandable but rational.

iv. the paradox proves the empirical inadequacy of SEU as a descriptive theory and motivates revisionism as a normative theory

Descriptive failure motivates normative revision — if virtually everyone violates SEU on reflection, the theory's normative authority is weakened; rational alternatives are availableThe opposition will retreat to the normative high ground: SEU is not a descriptive theory of how people do behave but a normative theory of how they should behave. Very well. But normative authority cannot be completely divorced from considered judgments. If virtually every decision-maker, including highly sophisticated ones, endorses Ellsberg preferences upon reflection — if they can articulate reasons for them, reasons that survive scrutiny — then the claim that SEU is the uniquely correct normative theory requires a defence that goes beyond mere assertion. The alternative frameworks that Schmeidler and Gilboa will present provide that defence: they show that Ellsberg preferences can be accommodated within rigorous, axiomatised decision theories. The normative debate is genuinely open.

3.If equally rigorous alternatives exist, SEU’s claim to exclusive normative authority cannot rest on formal rigour alone but requires additional argument.
∴SEU is not the uniquely correct normative standard; equally rigorous alternatives exist, and the normative debate is genuinely open.

Mr Speaker,

The debate before this House turns on what we mean by rationality under uncertainty. I published my Treatise on Probability in 1921, four decades before Ellsberg’s thought experiment was formulated, and I believe the conceptual foundations for understanding the paradox are already present in that work. They have simply been ignored by the orthodoxy that followed.

The central concept I wish to bring to this debate is the weight of evidence. This is not the same as the magnitude of a probability. It is a second dimension of probabilistic reasoning that the Savage framework systematically collapses and discards. And once we see that it exists and that rationality demands sensitivity to it, the Ellsberg preferences cease to be paradoxical and become exactly what a thoughtful agent ought to feel.

“As the evidence increases, the weight of the argument increases, even though the probability of the conclusion may not be altered.” — Keynes, A Treatise on Probability, 1921
i. the two dimensions of probability: magnitude and weight

Two dimensions — magnitude is the probability estimate; weight is the evidential basis for that estimate; these are independent; Savage collapses them into one numberProbability has two distinct dimensions. Its magnitude is the numerical estimate itself: 0.5, 0.7, 0.3. Its weight is the quantity and quality of evidence on which that estimate is based. Consider two probability assessments of 0.5. The first is based on examining an urn that contains exactly 50 red and 50 black balls. The second is based on no information at all — I simply have no idea what the urn contains. Both assessments yield a probability of 0.5 by the Principle of Insufficient Reason. But these two assessments are not the same. The first is based on solid, direct evidence. The second is based on nothing. Any rational agent should feel differently about them, and any adequate theory of rational belief should represent that difference. The Savage framework, by reducing all beliefs to a single numerical probability, cannot represent it. The probability 0.5 in both cases is treated as identical. This is a fundamental impoverishment of rational belief.

ii. the urn problem as a demonstration of weight differences

Urn problem shows weight — Urn I has maximum evidential weight; Urn II has zero weight; same probability magnitude, different weight; rational agents should respond differentlyThe Ellsberg urn experiment is precisely a demonstration that evidential weight matters. Urn I has maximum evidential weight for the probability 0.5: I know the exact composition of the urn. My probability estimate of 0.5 for red is as well-grounded as any probability estimate could be. Urn II has zero evidential weight for any probability estimate: I have no information about its composition. My only basis for assigning any probability at all is the Principle of Insufficient Reason — the inference from total ignorance to equal probability. But this is not the same kind of probability as the one I assign to Urn I. It is a probability of zero weight: an estimate with no evidential backing. A rational agent who prefers Urn I is not making a logical error. She is responding to a real difference in the evidential situation — a difference that the probability magnitude alone fails to capture.

iii. the inadequacy of the principle of insufficient reason

Laplace's principle fails — assigning 50/50 to total ignorance doesn't represent ignorance; it represents a specific claim; genuine ignorance should yield a range of priors, not a point estimateThe Savage framework, and more broadly the Bayesian approach, relies on the Principle of Insufficient Reason (Laplace) when no prior information is available: in the absence of a reason to favour any outcome over another, assign equal probability to all outcomes. But this principle is not innocent. To assign a probability of 0.5 to red in Urn II is not to represent ignorance. It is to make a specific claim — that red is as likely as black — that is no more warranted by the evidence than the claim that red is 0.9 likely. The Principle of Insufficient Reason transforms ignorance into a specific probability estimate, and then treats that estimate as if it were as well-grounded as a probability derived from knowledge. This is a mistake. Genuine ignorance should yield not a point estimate but a range — a set of priors over which we are genuinely indifferent — and this is precisely what the Maxmin approach of Gilboa and Schmeidler formalises.

iv. weight of evidence and the normative justification for caution

Weight justifies caution — low-weight probabilities warrant conservative decisions; this is rational, not risk-averse; the weight dimension grounds a normative case for ambiguity aversionThe weight of evidence provides a normative justification for caution that is independent of risk aversion. If my probability estimate of 0.5 for red from Urn II is based on no evidence at all, then acting as though it were as reliable as the 0.5 from Urn I would be epistemically overconfident. Just as a scientist who has run one trial should have less confidence in her estimate than one who has run a thousand, a decision-maker whose probability estimate is based on zero evidence should have less confidence than one whose estimate is based on direct knowledge of the generating mechanism. Ambiguity aversion — the preference for the high-weight probability over the zero-weight one — is not a cognitive bias but an appropriate response to the epistemic asymmetry between the two urns. The Savage framework, by insisting on a unique prior, forces the agent to act as if there were no such asymmetry. This is not rationality. It is false precision.

2.Two probability estimates can have the same magnitude but radically different weights: a well-evidenced 0.5 and an evidentially empty 0.5 are not the same rational state.
3.The Savage framework reduces all beliefs to a single numerical magnitude, discarding the weight dimension and treating evidentially rich and evidentially empty estimates as identical.

Mr Speaker,

My colleagues have established the philosophical case for ambiguity aversion. My role is to demonstrate that this case rests on rigorous mathematical foundations — that Ellsberg preferences can be accommodated within a fully axiomatised, internally consistent decision theory that does not require any sacrifice of logical coherence.

The claim that SEU is the uniquely rigorous decision theory has always rested on two pillars: that alternative frameworks lack axiomatisation and that they generate logical contradictions. My Choquet Expected Utility theory, presented in 1989, demolishes both pillars. CEU is fully axiomatised. And it does not generate logical contradictions. The opposition has no remaining formal argument.

From additive probabilities to non-additive capacities: the CEU framework.

Standard expected utility integrates payoffs against an additive probability measure P, where P(A) + P(not-A) = 1 for all events A. This additivity is the source of the impossibility of representing Ellsberg preferences within SEU: if P(red|II) + P(black|II) = 1, and you act as if both are below 0.5, you have an inconsistency.

CEU replaces the additive probability measure with a capacity v — a function that is monotone (if A ⊇ B then v(A) ≥ v(B)) and normalised (v(∅) = 0, v(S) = 1) but need not be additive. For Urn II, we can set v(red) = v(black) = 0.4 and v(red or black) = 1, with v(red) + v(black) = 0.8 ≠ 1. Expected utility is then computed as a Choquet integral against this capacity. The result: Urn II bets are worth less than Urn I bets, correctly representing ambiguity aversion. No contradiction arises.
i. the axiomatisation of CEU

CEU is axiomatised — Schmeidler's 1989 axioms replace Independence with Comonotonic Independence; internally consistent; Ellsberg preferences satisfy the axiomsChoquet Expected Utility is derived from a set of axioms that closely parallel Savage’s own axioms, with one crucial modification. Savage’s Independence Axiom requires that preferences between acts be independent of payoffs in states where the acts agree. CEU replaces this with the Comonotonic Independence Axiom: Independence is required only for acts that are comonotonic — that is, that rank outcomes in the same order across all states. This weakening is motivated precisely by the insight that Ellsberg intuitions reflect: in ambiguous situations, acts that agree in some states but not others cannot be evaluated independently of each other. The axiomatisation is complete, rigorous, and internally consistent. Ellsberg preferences satisfy the CEU axioms. The formal objection to ambiguity aversion is closed.

ii. the Choquet integral and its properties

Choquet integral — correctly aggregates non-additive beliefs; reduces to standard expected utility when probability is additive; generalises SEU rather than contradicting itThe Choquet integral is the appropriate tool for integrating payoffs against non-additive capacities. For a discrete act that yields payoff x in state s, the Choquet integral computes expected utility by ordering states from worst to best and weighting each increment in payoff by the capacity of the event that ‘triggers’ that increment. This is a well-defined, mathematically rigorous procedure. When the capacity is additive — when it is a standard probability measure — the Choquet integral reduces exactly to standard expected utility. CEU therefore generalises SEU: SEU is the special case of CEU when the capacity happens to be additive. Far from contradicting the SEU framework, CEU contains it as a special case and extends it to handle ambiguity. The opposition is defending a special case against a more general theory.

iii. uncertainty-aversion as an axiom, not a bias

Uncertainty aversion axiom — CEU derives ambiguity aversion from an axiom of uncertainty aversion; preference for mixtures; this is a principled axiom not an ad hoc patchWithin the CEU framework, ambiguity aversion is characterised by the axiom of uncertainty aversion: for any two acts f and g between which the agent is indifferent, the agent weakly prefers any mixture λf + (1-λ)g to either act alone. This axiom captures the intuition that averaging over uncertain outcomes is preferable to committing to either extreme — a principle of diversification under uncertainty. It is a substantive axiom with clear normative motivation, not an ad hoc patch applied to a damaged theory. The capacity v that represents the beliefs of a CEU-maximiser with uncertainty aversion is convex: v(A) + v(B) ≤ v(A ∪ B) for all events A, B. This is the precise mathematical expression of ambiguity aversion within the CEU framework.

iv. the money-pump objection is defused

Money pump fails against CEU — Raiffa's dynamic money pump requires act-by-act separability; CEU agents evaluate sequences holistically; the pump doesn't workRaiffa will argue that ambiguity-averse agents can be subjected to a money pump: a series of sequential bets that extract money from them. But this objection crucially assumes that the agent evaluates each bet in a sequence independently, using the same non-additive capacity at each stage. CEU does not require this: a sophisticated CEU agent can evaluate the entire sequence of bets as a compound act and apply the Choquet integral to the compound. When the sequence is evaluated holistically, the money pump argument fails. The pump works only against a CEU agent who naively applies her local preferences to each sequential choice without recognising that her preferences must be applied to the whole sequence. A rational CEU agent does not make this mistake.

Mr Speaker,

I shall close the proposition’s case by presenting the Maxmin Expected Utility framework and arguing that it not only accommodates Ellsberg preferences but provides the correct normative account of rational choice under genuine uncertainty — what Frank Knight called ‘true uncertainty’ as distinct from measurable risk.

The MEU approach, which I developed with David Schmeidler in 1989, represents the beliefs of a rational agent who faces genuine ambiguity not as a single subjective probability distribution but as a set of probability distributions — the set of priors that the agent regards as epistemically plausible given her evidence. The agent then evaluates each act by the worst expected utility it yields across all priors in this set. She is not pessimistic; she is robust.

Maxmin Expected Utility: the formal structure.

Let C be a convex set of probability distributions (the agent’s ‘priors’). For each act f, MEU assigns value: V(f) = minP ∈ C EP[u(f)].

The agent maximises V(f) across available acts. For Urn I, C is the singleton {P: P(red) = 0.5}, and V equals standard expected utility. For Urn II, C contains all distributions over red and black — from all-red to all-black — and minP ∈ C EP[u(f)] assigns near-zero expected utility to any single-colour bet. The preference for Urn I over Urn II follows immediately, without any logical contradiction.
i. the axiomatisation of MEU and the set-of-priors representation

MEU axiomatised — Gilboa-Schmeidler axioms; Uncertainty Aversion plus Certainty Independence yields the maxmin representation; fully rigorousOur 1989 paper provides a complete axiomatisation of MEU. The key axioms are: Completeness, Transitivity, Continuity, Monotonicity (all shared with Savage), plus two distinctive axioms: Uncertainty Aversion (as in CEU) and Certainty Independence. Certainty Independence is a weakening of Savage’s Independence Axiom: it requires that independence hold only when one of the acts being mixed is a constant act (a certain payoff). These axioms jointly imply the maxmin representation: the agent’s preferences are represented by a utility function u and a convex set C of priors, with value function V(f) = minP ∈ C EP[u(f)]. This is a theorem, not an intuition. MEU is as rigorously grounded as SEU.

ii. forcing a unique prior is an artificial imposition of false precision

Unique prior is dogma — when evidence supports a range of priors, choosing one arbitrarily is not rational precision but false confidence; MEU is epistemically honest about the rangeSavage’s framework insists that a rational agent must have a unique subjective prior. But when the evidence genuinely supports a range of priors — as it does for Urn II, where every distribution from all-red to all-black is consistent with what the agent knows — why must she pick one? The requirement that she do so is not a deep truth about rationality. It is a technical convenience that simplifies the mathematics of the theory at the cost of misrepresenting the agent’s epistemic situation. Choosing a unique prior from a range of equally plausible priors is not rational precision; it is arbitrary precision — precision that goes beyond what the evidence warrants. MEU is epistemically honest: it represents the agent’s uncertainty by the full set of priors consistent with her evidence, and evaluates acts conservatively across that set.

iii. maxmin is robust, not pessimistic

Robustness not pessimism — MEU seeks decisions that perform well across all plausible scenarios; this is standard engineering and policy practice; it is rational caution, not irrational fearBinmore will characterise the maxmin agent as irrationally pessimistic: she acts as if nature is always adversarial, always choosing the worst prior. But this is a mischaracterisation. The maxmin agent is not pessimistic; she is robust. She seeks decisions that perform acceptably across all plausible scenarios, not just the most likely one. This is precisely the standard of rational decision-making in engineering, medicine, finance, and public policy. A bridge engineer does not design for the average wind load; she designs for the worst plausible wind load within a specified envelope. A central bank does not set monetary policy only for the most likely economic scenario; it considers the full range. Robustness is not a bias. It is a rational strategy when the cost of catastrophic failure is high and the generating mechanism is unknown.

iv. the normative force of multiple priors against the dogma of unique subjectivity

Against unique subjectivity — Bayesian personalism is a philosophical choice not a logical necessity; multiple priors is an equally valid and more epistemically honest representation of genuine ignoranceBayesian personalism — the view that a rational agent always has a unique, personal, subjective probability distribution — is not a logical necessity. It is a philosophical choice, grounded in considerations of mathematical elegance and Dutch Book coherence. These are genuine virtues. But they do not establish that unique subjectivity is the only rational response to uncertainty. MEU shows that an equally rigorous, axiomatised, internally consistent decision theory can represent rational belief as a set of priors. The Bayesian dogma that unique priors are required for rationality is precisely that: a dogma. It is not a theorem. And once we see that multiple priors are theoretically coherent, the insistence on unique priors requires an argument that the opposition has not provided.

The Sure-Thing Principle and the contradiction in Ellsberg preferences.

The Sure-Thing Principle: if you prefer act f to act g regardless of what happens in some event E, then you should prefer f to g full stop — the payoffs in E are irrelevant to the comparison.

Applied to Ellsberg: let E be the event ‘red is drawn.’ In Bet A vs Bet C, the payoffs if black is drawn are identical (£0). The choice turns entirely on what happens if red is drawn: A pays £100 from Urn I, C pays £100 from Urn II. The only thing that can rationally differentiate A from C is your probability for red from Urn II. If you think that probability is below 0.5, you prefer A. Fine. But then by the same reasoning you must think the probability of black from Urn II exceeds 0.5, so you should prefer D to B. The preference for B over D contradicts your preference for A over C. This is not intuition versus theory. It is a direct logical inconsistency.

My role is to deploy the pragmatic hammer. The money pump argument — or, in the probability context, the Dutch Book argument — is not merely a theoretical curiosity. It is the acid test of a normative decision theory. A decision framework that allows an agent to be systematically exploited by a clever bookmaker or trading counterparty is not a framework for rational choice. It is a framework for rational self-destruction.

The Dynamic Money Pump for Ellsberg preferences: a schematic.

Step 1: You hold a bet on red from Urn II (bet C). You are indifferent between C and bet D (black from Urn II) but prefer both to having nothing (since both have positive expected value under any distribution).

Step 2: I offer to swap your C for D plus £ε. Since you prefer the known urn to either, and D + £ε seems slightly better than D, you accept — paying £ε to move from C to D.

Step 3: I now offer to swap your D for C plus £ε. By symmetric reasoning, you accept again — paying another £ε.

Step 4: You are back where you started (holding C) having paid 2£ε. Repeat indefinitely. You are a money pump. Your non-additive preferences have made you systematically exploitable.
i. the dutch book argument generalised to ambiguity

Dutch Book — non-additive capacities allow a clever bookmaker to construct a set of bets that guarantee a loss; this is the decision-theory analogue of logical inconsistencyThe Dutch Book argument is the decision-theoretic analogue of logical consistency. A set of beliefs is probabilistically coherent if and only if no Dutch Book — no set of bets that guarantees a net loss regardless of the outcome — can be constructed against an agent who acts on those beliefs. De Finetti proved that this condition is equivalent to the additivity of probability. A non-additive capacity, by definition, violates additivity, and therefore a Dutch Book can be constructed against an agent who acts on it. The Ellsberg agent who assigns capacity 0.4 to both red from Urn II and black from Urn II (adding to 0.8 ≠ 1) can be sold bets on red and black simultaneously that cost her 0.4 + 0.4 = 0.8 per unit but guarantee a payoff of 1 per unit. She pays 0.8 to guarantee receiving 1 but appears to gain; in fact a clever bookmaker who understands her non-additive beliefs can arrange complementary bets that extract the 0.2 per unit surplus from her.

The full sequence is not presented to the agent at once; she evaluates each step as it comes. In any real-world decision environment — financial markets, insurance markets, contracting — decisions are made sequentially, not as whole-sequence compound acts. The Ellsberg agent in these environments is exploitable.

The Binmore-Stewart-Voorhoeve Experiment (2012): key design and findings.

The standard Ellsberg experiment asks subjects to choose between bets — a discrete binary choice that conceals important information about the strength of the preference. Binmore, Stewart, and Voorhoeve instead asked subjects to locate their precise indifference point: the exact probability p at which they would be indifferent between a bet on red from the known urn (Urn I) and a bet on red from the unknown urn (Urn II).

Under the maxmin (MEU) model, subjects should act as if P(red|II) = 0, so the indifference point should be far below 0.5 — subjects should require very low odds on the known urn before they would be willing to switch to the unknown urn. Under the Laplace Principle of Insufficient Reason (Bayesian prior), subjects should be indifferent at p = 0.5 exactly. The experimental finding: the Laplace Principle dramatically outperformed maxmin. The median indifference point was very close to 0.5 across the sample. Most subjects, when forced to reveal their true indifference points, were essentially applying Bayesian logic — treating the unknown probability as 50/50.
i. the ellsberg experiment reveals confusion, not ambiguity aversion

By eliciting indifference points rather than binary choices, our 2012 study removed these confounds. When subjects were asked to specify the exact probability of a known-urn bet that would make them indifferent to an unknown-urn bet, the majority placed this at or very near 0.5. They were not applying maxmin. They were not applying CEU with low capacities. They were applying the Bayesian prior. Ellsberg’s paradox, at the level of genuine measured preferences, is largely a mirage created by the binary-choice framing of the original experiment.

Casual, unguided reflection on why the known urn ‘feels safer’ is not philosophical reflection on the structure of rational choice.
