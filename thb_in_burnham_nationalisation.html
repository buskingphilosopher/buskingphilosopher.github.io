Arguments

Mr President,

I want to start with what has actually happened. In September 2023, the first buses in Greater Manchester went back under public control for the first time since 1986. We didn't promise this would happen. We delivered it, on time, on budget, against a sustained legal challenge from Stagecoach and Rotala. And the results speak for themselves.

On the evidence from Greater Manchester

Bee Network: ridership +14%, fares -15%, punctuality from 66% to 80%+, fleet renewedThis is not ideology. It is evidence. Bus ridership in Greater Manchester has increased by fourteen percent in the first two franchised areas since we took control. Punctuality has improved from around sixty-six percent to consistently above eighty percent. Average ticket prices have fallen by fifteen percent. We have gone from a bus fleet whose average age was 9.2 years to one that will be twenty-five percent zero-emission by 2025. Seventeen million more bus journeys were made in 2024 than in 2023. For forty years, we were told the market would deliver. Instead, it gave us a network that by 2022 was forty percent smaller than in the 1970s — fragmented, expensive, unreliable, and shrinking. One policy change, executed well, reversed that trajectory. That is not a pilot. That is proof of concept at scale.

1986 deregulation produced fragmentation, shrinkage, and fare hikes — the market failed comprehensively in buses outside LondonThe 1986 Transport Act deregulated buses outside London on the theory that competition would drive down fares and improve services. What it produced was the opposite. Operators cherry-picked profitable routes, abandoned unprofitable ones, ran duplicate services on the same busy corridor while cutting coverage everywhere else, and raised fares consistently above inflation. The contrast with London — which retained regulation throughout — is definitive. By 2019, London had more bus journeys per capita than any comparable European city. Manchester, Birmingham, and Leeds had seen their bus networks decline year on year for three decades. The experiment ran for forty years. The verdict is in. This is not ideology; it is empirical failure on a documented, measurable, undeniable scale.

On democratic accountability

Public ownership makes services accountable to elected representatives answerable to users, not shareholders answerable to marketsThere is something beyond economics here that matters. When your bus route is cancelled, who do you call? Under deregulation, the answer was: nobody. The bus company made the decision for commercial reasons and you had no standing to challenge it. Under franchising, the answer is: the Mayor, who is answerable to you at election time. Democratic accountability is not a soft benefit that can be separated from economic efficiency. It is the mechanism by which service failures get corrected, routes get extended to underserved areas, and the system responds to need rather than profit. The proposition is not just that public ownership delivers better economics — though it does. It is that transport is a public good and should be governed as one, by people who answer to the public.

On the national case — from Manchesterism to the country

Burnham's Manchesterism: integrated public transport, social housing, patient capital — a template for the national programme Labour abandonedWhat Greater Manchester has shown is that genuine public ownership — not regulation of private ownership — is both deliverable and popular. The Bee Network model, integrated with Metrolink, with capped fares and a tap-in-tap-out system, has demonstrated what a fully integrated public transport system looks like outside London. The same logic applies to water: Thames Water, with its dividend extractions and sewage discharges, is a direct product of the model where a private company takes the profits and the public bears the risk. It applies to energy. It applies to housing. The question for the national Labour programme is not whether this model works — Greater Manchester has answered that — but whether there is the political will to extend it.

On cost and fiscal rules

Nationalisation via long-term public investment bonds costs nothing the Treasury should fear — assets cover liabilities; the constraint is political, not fiscalI want to address the fiscal objection directly. The Treasury argument — that nationalisation "costs taxpayers" — is economically illiterate. When you take a productive asset into public ownership, you acquire an asset that generates income. The cost is offset by the income stream. Thames Water's infrastructure has a value; nationalising it means exchanging debt for an asset, not creating a hole in the public finances. What is required is long-term public investment financed by bonds at government rates — substantially cheaper than the private borrowing that water companies currently use. The fiscal rules as currently constructed are a political choice, not an economic law. Saying I would observe fiscal rules while also pursuing this programme is not a contradiction — it is a statement that the programme can be financed responsibly within a borrowing framework designed to fund public investment, not current spending.

On the politics — why this wins

Public ownership is overwhelmingly popular — the political obstacle is Labour's caution, not the public's appetitePolling consistently shows that nationalisation of water and rail commands support from majorities of Conservative voters as well as Labour and Liberal Democrat ones. This is not a sectional left-wing position. It is the common sense of a country that has watched forty years of privatised services deliver high prices, poor performance, and private profit extracted from public subsidy. The political case for this programme is not difficult to make. What requires explaining is not why we would take these services into public ownership, but why we would not. Stagecoach and Rotala fought us in court twice. They lost twice. The argument that capital will resist does not mean we should capitulate. It means we should win — and in Greater Manchester, we did.

Mr President,

I want to provide the economic foundations for what Burnham has argued politically, because the opposition will come armed with efficiency arguments and it is important to meet them precisely. The case for public ownership in network industries is not a case against markets or against private enterprise — it is a case grounded in market failure theory, the economics of natural monopoly, and thirty years of accumulated evidence about what happens when you try to regulate privately owned monopolies in the public interest.

On natural monopoly and the structural case

Natural monopoly: network industries have declining average costs over the relevant range — competition cannot work; the choice is between public and private monopolyTransport infrastructure, water pipes, electricity grids, and railways share a defining economic characteristic: they are natural monopolies. The fixed costs of the network are so large relative to the variable costs of an additional user that average costs decline over the entire relevant output range. It is economically impossible to have competing water pipe networks or competing railway tracks between the same cities — the infrastructure cost would be prohibitive. In these sectors, the question is never "market or monopoly." The question is "public monopoly or private monopoly, and if private, how regulated." The privatisation experiment chose private monopoly with regulation. Thirty years of evidence has now answered whether that choice works.

On value extraction vs value creation

Value extraction: privatised monopolies extract value from captive users and infrastructure rather than creating it — dividends come from public subsidy and deferred maintenance, not productivityThe core dysfunction of privatised network industries is not inefficiency in the narrow sense — it is value extraction. Privatised water companies in England paid out £72 billion in dividends between 1989 and 2022 while allowing infrastructure to deteriorate to the point where Thames Water's collapse became inevitable. These dividends were not generated by productivity improvements or innovation. They were generated by: charging captive users monopoly prices, taking on debt to pay dividends (leveraging the regulatory asset base), deferring maintenance and infrastructure investment, and extracting value from public subsidies intended for network improvement. This is not a market generating returns. This is a system designed to privatise profit and socialise loss. The theoretical case for private ownership rests on efficiency incentives. The empirical case rests on four decades of dividend extraction from infrastructure that the public ultimately has to bail out.

Regulation fails natural monopolies: information asymmetry means regulated companies always know more than regulators — the result is regulatory capture and the Ofwat outcomeThe standard response to natural monopoly is regulation: keep private ownership but control prices, investment, and standards through a regulator. This has now comprehensively failed in the UK water sector, and the reason is structural, not incidental. Regulation requires the regulator to have information about costs, investment needs, and operating conditions that the regulated company has every incentive to conceal or distort. Abbey Innes has documented this as the fundamental information asymmetry of regulated private monopoly — the regulator can only ever work with a subset of the data the operator controls. Ofwat set prices, monitored investment, and was supposed to protect the public interest. The outcome was systematic dividend extraction, deferred infrastructure, sewage discharges into rivers, and the effective insolvency of the sector's largest company. This was not regulatory failure — it was a structural demonstration that regulation cannot substitute for genuine public ownership of natural monopolies.

On patient public capital and long-term investment

Patient capital: public ownership can make long-horizon investments private capital cannot — infrastructure, climate transition, network integrationOne of the most important advantages of public ownership in network industries is the capacity to make long-horizon investments that private capital systematically underweights. Private operators discount future returns at the cost of private equity — typically ten to fifteen percent. Public entities borrow at government bond rates — currently substantially lower. This means that a public water company can justify infrastructure investment that would not meet a private company's hurdle rate, precisely because it does not face the same short-term return pressure. The same logic applies to the climate transition: electrifying a bus fleet, building charging infrastructure, integrating rail and bus networks — these investments have long payback periods and significant co-benefits that a private operator captures only partially. Public ownership is not an alternative to investment. For long-horizon infrastructure, it is the only ownership model that can make the investment decision rationally.

On co-production and network externalities

Network externalities: integrated transport has system-level benefits that no private operator can capture — only public coordination can internalise themTransport networks have a property that makes private provision particularly dysfunctional: positive externalities that no individual operator can capture. When a bus route extends the catchment area of a train station, the train operator benefits but the bus operator bears the cost. When an integrated ticketing system reduces the friction of multi-modal travel, ridership increases on every mode — but no operator has the incentive to build and fund the integrated system unless they capture all the modes' revenue. This co-production problem is not a regulatory failure — it is a structural market failure that can only be solved by a single authority owning or controlling the whole network. Burnham's Bee Network works precisely because TfGM controls fares, routes, timetables, and ticketing across buses, trams, and eventually rail — eliminating the fragmentation that kills multi-modal integration under private operation.

On what the counter-arguments actually show

The efficiency objection confuses competitive markets with regulated monopolies — the evidence favours public ownership precisely where competition is structurally impossibleThe opposition will argue that privatisation improved efficiency and that public ownership generates X-inefficiency. Both claims are true in the right context — and that context is competitive markets. The IEA's own summary of David Parker's privatisation research acknowledges: "the successes of privatisation are clearest where it replaced a government monopoly with a competitive market." Where it has meant replacing a public monopoly with a private one — water, rail, buses — the case is "far less clear." This is a concession from the pro-privatisation evidence base that public ownership critics rarely acknowledge. In sectors where competition is structurally impossible, the efficiency argument for privatisation collapses. The remaining argument is regulatory, and I have shown why regulation fails. In natural monopolies, the economic case for public ownership is not just defensible — it is the dominant evidence-based position.

Mr President,

I want to make an argument that cuts across the standard left-right framing of this debate. I agree with much of the proposition's diagnosis — privatised utilities have failed, the regulatory framework has produced perverse incentives, and the status quo is indefensible. Where I disagree is on the prescription. Changing who owns the assets does not, by itself, fix the underlying problems. The problems are in the incentive structure, the regulatory design, and the governance framework — not primarily in the ownership structure. Getting ownership wrong twice is not better than getting it right once.

On ownership as a second-order variable

Ownership is second-order: what matters is incentive design — publicly owned firms facing the wrong incentives perform as badly as privately owned onesThe international evidence on public versus private ownership is substantially more nuanced than either side of this debate acknowledges. The empirical literature — from Boardman and Vining, to Megginson and Netter, to the OECD comparative studies — consistently shows that ownership per se is a secondary variable. What primarily determines performance is: competitive pressure (or its absence), the quality of incentive structures, governance independence, and the alignment between the organisation's objectives and social welfare. Publicly owned firms facing political interference and soft budget constraints perform as poorly — sometimes more poorly — than privately owned ones facing the same conditions. Privately owned firms with strong independent regulation and genuine competitive pressure can outperform public alternatives. The proposition treats ownership as the key variable. The evidence says it is not.

On political interference and long-run capital allocation

Political interference: public ownership subjects long-run capital investment to short-term political cycles — the result is systematic underinvestment whenever fiscal pressure mountsThe proposition celebrates Greater Manchester's Bee Network. The buses are run by private operators under franchise — the local authority owns the contracts and sets the conditions, but does not own and operate the vehicles. This is a carefully chosen ownership structure, and it is not the same as nationalisation. The reason it is carefully chosen is that full public ownership of an operating bus company brings with it full responsibility for industrial relations, fleet procurement, fuel costs, driver recruitment, and operational management — all of which interact with political cycles in ways that have historically produced underinvestment. The British Railways experience before privatisation was not one of consistently maintained infrastructure funded at the right level. It was one where capital expenditure was cut whenever fiscal pressure arose, and restored under political pressure, resulting in a long-run pattern of boom-and-bust investment that privatisation was partly designed to cure. The proposition needs to explain how publicly owned utilities would be shielded from the same political dynamic.

On the regulatory reform alternative

Regulatory reform: stronger independent regulation with genuine long-run contracts and hard investment requirements can solve the incentive problem without the costs of nationalisationWhat went wrong in UK water was not that it was privately owned. What went wrong was that the regulatory contract — as I have argued in detail in my work on infrastructure regulation — was badly designed. Regulators set prices in five-year cycles, creating short-term optimisation incentives. Dividend payments were permitted as long as notional investment targets were hit, even when actual investment was inadequate. The cost of capital embedded in the regulatory model was too generous, allowing excess returns. These are regulatory design failures, not ownership failures. Strong independent regulation with: hard long-run investment requirements enforceable through bond covenants; dividend restrictions tied to real investment delivery; regulated asset base methodology that reflects true replacement cost; and genuine financial ring-fencing of infrastructure assets — can solve the incentive problem. This reform is harder to announce than nationalisation. It is also substantially easier to deliver.

On the costs of nationalisation that the proposition ignores

Nationalisation costs: compensation, transaction costs, loss of private capital, political risk premium — these are real and substantial even if not "taxpayer money" in the Treasury senseBurnham says nationalisation costs taxpayers nothing. This is not quite right. When you take assets into public ownership at market value, you acquire the income stream but also the liabilities, the operational responsibilities, and the governance costs. The compensation cost for the UK water industry has been estimated at between £90 billion and £200 billion at current asset valuations — to be financed by government bonds, adding to the debt stock. This is not "nothing." It is a large contingent liability that the government must service. You also lose the private sector's risk-bearing capacity — when things go wrong in a publicly owned utility, the public sector bears the full operating loss. The proposition says this is better than privatising profit. That is a genuine argument. But presenting the fiscal case for nationalisation as though it has no cost is not serious economics.

On the Bee Network's actual ownership structure

The Bee Network is franchising, not public ownership — private operators run the buses under contract; this is sophisticated contracting, not socialismI want to press on what the Bee Network actually is, because there is a systematic ambiguity in the proposition's language. The buses are operated by private companies under franchise contracts with TfGM. Arriva, First Bus, and Stagecoach continued operating many routes through the transition — they are now franchisees rather than deregulated operators. TfGM sets fares, timetables, and standards; operators bid for route packages and deliver services for a fee. This is a sophisticated contractual mechanism — and it may well be the right mechanism for bus services. But it is not nationalisation. It is not public ownership of the means of production. It is high-quality public commissioning of private provision. Calling it nationalisation misleads the public about what is being proposed and what is being delivered. Greater Manchester's buses are not nationalised. They are regulated.

On the limits of the Manchesterism analogy

Manchesterism doesn't scale to water and energy: buses are operationally manageable; natural resource monopolies at national scale require different solutionsGreater Manchester's bus franchising is a genuine achievement in local transport governance. But the analogy to nationalising the water industry, the electricity grid, or the rail network is weaker than the proposition suggests. Bus franchising is operationally manageable: fixed routes, standard vehicles, competitive tendering for packages. Water infrastructure is a national network of pipes, reservoirs, and treatment works requiring continuous capital investment measured in decades, complex environmental compliance, and climate adaptation on geological timescales. The governance challenge of a nationally or regionally owned water company is an order of magnitude more complex than franchising buses. Success at one level of complexity does not automatically extend to another. The proposition needs to be more precise about what it is actually proposing and at what scale — and it needs to engage seriously with the international evidence on publicly owned water companies, which is considerably more mixed than it implies.

On the ownership sleight of hand

The category error: Burnham conflates direct public ownership, franchising, and regulated private operation — using "nationalisation" to describe all three is deliberately misleadingBurnham uses "public ownership" to cover at least three fundamentally different arrangements. First, direct state ownership of assets — the state owns the infrastructure and employs the workers. Second, franchising — private operators run services under contract to a public authority, as in the Bee Network. Third, regulated private operation under public planning frameworks — private companies own and operate assets subject to price controls and investment requirements. These are not the same thing. Owning assets, controlling prices, operating services, and bearing financial risks are separate functions that can be combined in very different ways. Burnham routinely collapses these distinctions. That is not a sloppy imprecision. It is a deliberate political choice designed to leave the public believing he is proposing the first when he often means the second or third. The distinction matters enormously because the second and third have already been tried — in buses before franchising and in water respectively — and they failed.

On the Ofwat model as the template for failure

Burnham's regulated model IS the Ofwat model: setting prices, monitoring investment, public planning with private ownership — this is exactly what produced Thames WaterHere is the critical point that the proposition has not addressed: the water sector has operated under exactly the model that Burnham's programme most often describes — and it has done so for three decades. Ofwat set prices, monitored investment, and was supposed to protect the public interest. Companies retained private ownership but operated under public regulatory control. That is the template that Burnham holds up as an alternative to privatisation, even though it was this model that produced Thames Water, sewage discharges, and £72 billion in extracted dividends. When Burnham says he wants to bring water "under public control" and talks about price regulation and public planning frameworks, he is describing Ofwat. He is proposing, as the solution to the model that failed, the model that failed. This is not a break from privatisation's outcomes. It is their structural reproduction in progressive language.

On fiscal rules as the decisive internal contradiction

Fiscal rules are the programme's killer: Burnham accepted Reeves's rules, which were designed to prevent the public investment his programme requiresBurnham has explicitly stated that he would observe Rachel Reeves's fiscal rules. Let me be precise about what that means. Reeves's rules tightly limit government borrowing and define investment in a way that excludes the kind of long-term public capital commitment that genuine public ownership of infrastructure requires. These are not neutral accounting conventions. They are the product of a Treasury ideology that treats public investment as a cost rather than an asset, that constrains the public sector's ability to borrow and invest precisely where the case for public investment is strongest, and that embeds the neoliberal framework at the heart of fiscal policy. You cannot build a serious public investment programme inside these constraints. Accepting them as Burnham says he will means accepting the Treasury's definition of what is economically permissible — which is precisely the definition that blocked the investment in water, rail, and transport for forty years. This is not a break from neoliberalism. It is neoliberalism with a regional planning layer added, at best.

On bond market deference as neoliberal frame

Bond vigilante deference: Burnham treats bond markets as arbiters of policy — this accepts the neoliberal frame that constrains public investment below any economically rational levelWhen Burnham talks about fiscal rules and bond markets as constraints on his programme, he is making a choice about epistemology, not just about policy. He is accepting the premise that private bond markets — entities whose primary objective is preserving the real value of their holdings — have legitimate authority to set the boundaries of democratic public investment. This is not an economic fact. It is a political choice to treat bond markets as co-legislators. Modern monetary theory shows that a currency-issuing government does not face the financing constraints that a household or a non-issuing entity faces. The constraints on public spending are real resource constraints — are the workers, materials, and productive capacity available — not bond market reaction functions. By accepting bond market deference, Burnham has conceded the central terrain of the neoliberal settlement before a single debate has been held. His programme cannot deliver what he says it will within those constraints.

On what genuine public ownership actually requires

What genuine ownership requires: direct asset ownership, public employment, state investment not subject to commercial return requirements, and fiscal space outside neoliberal rulesI want to be clear about what genuine public ownership requires — because the proposition is not proposing it. Genuine public ownership means: the state owns the infrastructure, employs the workers, makes investment decisions on social return criteria not commercial ones, retains operating surpluses for reinvestment rather than distributing them as dividends, and can finance investment through government borrowing at government rates without reference to commercial return thresholds. This is what Labour did in 1945 with coal, steel, railways, and electricity. It required fiscal space — the willingness to borrow for investment at a level that Reeves's rules prohibit. Nationalisation without fiscal space is not nationalisation. It is a change in the name on the letterhead. Burnham proposes to rename Ofwat's regulatory model "public ownership" and then tell us he has broken with neoliberalism — while accepting the fiscal rules that make the investment his programme requires impossible to finance.

On what a genuine programme would look like

The genuine alternative: full public ownership, public employment, public investment financed outside fiscal rules, with profit-seeking not permitted in natural monopoliesWhat would a genuine programme look like? First: full public ownership of natural monopolies — not franchising, not regulated private operation, but direct state asset ownership. Second: public investment financed by government bonds not subject to the arbitrary fiscal rules that treat public borrowing for productive assets the same as borrowing for current spending — as Labour understood in 1945, nationalisation does not cost taxpayers anything if the assets are genuinely worth acquiring. Third: operating surpluses retained for reinvestment, not distributed as dividends or recycled into management bonuses. Fourth: accountability mechanisms that are democratic, not regulatory — elected representatives answerable to users, not technocratic regulators captured by operators. This is the programme Burnham should be proposing. Instead he is proposing something that his own evidence — the Bee Network — shows is franchising, not nationalisation, while accepting the fiscal constraints that make the genuine programme impossible to finance. Until he resolves those contradictions, his programme will not deliver what he promises.
