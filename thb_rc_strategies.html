Critical-Level UtilitarianismCharles Blackorby, Walter Bossert & David Donaldson — Population Issues in Social Choice Theory, Welfare Economics, and Ethics (2005)
Total utilitarianism sets the threshold below which an added life makes the population worse at zero welfare. This is the source of the Repugnant Conclusion: lives barely above zero still count as net positive contributions, so enough of them can outweigh any finite population of very high-welfare lives. Critical-Level Utilitarianism (CLU) replaces zero with a positive critical level α: an added life contributes (welfare − α) to total value. Lives with welfare only slightly above zero, and below α, now contribute *negative* value, blocking the Repugnant Conclusion outright while preserving an additive, totalist structure that respects the Pareto-style intuitions total utilitarianism gets right.

Variable Value TheoryThomas Hurka — "Value and Population Size" (1983)
Rather than a single fixed threshold, the marginal value of adding a life of given welfare should itself depend on existing population size: the same quality of life contributes more value when added to a small population than when added to an already-large one. This diminishing marginal value of additional lives, modelled via a function that discounts each life's contribution as population size n grows, ensures that the cumulative value of adding enormous numbers of barely-positive lives converges rather than growing without bound — blocking the Repugnant Conclusion through the mathematics of diminishing returns rather than through a fixed cutoff.

Person-Affecting RestrictionJan Narveson — "Utilitarianism and New Generations" (1967); Melinda Roberts — "Person-Based Consequentialism" (2003)
The Repugnant Conclusion's force depends on treating the moral value of bringing additional people into existence as commensurable with, and addable to, the value of improving existing people's lives — as if "more lives, each barely worth living" could literally outweigh "fewer lives, each excellent," on a single common scale. The person-affecting restriction denies this: an outcome can only be better or worse if it is better or worse *for someone* — for some actual or existing person. Since the additional barely-content people in the large population are not made worse off by not existing in the small-population alternative, and no one existing in both outcomes is worse off in the large population, total addable "welfare from creating new lives" is not the right currency, and the Repugnant Conclusion's comparison is not even well-formed on its own terms.

Rank-Discounted UtilitarianismGeir Asheim & Stéphane Zuber — "Escaping the Repugnant Conclusion: Rank-Discounted Utilitarianism" (2014)
Both critical-level and variable-value approaches purchase their avoidance of the Repugnant Conclusion at the cost of generating a different, equally troubling implication: the Sadistic Conclusion, where adding lives of negative or low welfare can be ranked better than adding lives of high welfare, simply because of where the threshold or discount falls. Rank-discounted utilitarianism avoids both problems simultaneously: order individuals by welfare rank from worst to best within each population, and discount each person's welfare contribution by a factor depending on their *rank position*, not on a fixed welfare threshold or on population size as such. This rank-dependent (not level-dependent) discounting is the mathematically distinct manoeuvre that escapes the joint trap the threshold-based approaches fall into.

Critical-Range UtilitarianismWlodek Rabinowicz & Johan Gustafsson — "Population Axiology and the Possibility of a Fourth Category of Value" and related work on critical-range views (2010s–)
Critical-Level Utilitarianism is correct in spirit but wrong in formalism: it replaces total utilitarianism's zero-threshold with a single sharp critical level α, which generates exactly the discontinuity problem Professor Broome presses. The fix is not to abandon the critical-level idea but to generalize a single point into a range [α₁, α₂] of genuine value-indeterminacy or "parity," within which whether an added life counts as a positive or negative contribution is neither determinately true nor determinately false. This preserves CLU's blocking of the Repugnant Conclusion's clear cases while replacing its false precision at the margin with an honest acknowledgment of indeterminacy.

I want to locate, with some precision, exactly where total utilitarianism generates the Repugnant Conclusion, because I think once the source is correctly identified, the fix becomes almost forced — not an ad hoc patch, but the natural correction to a single, isolable parameter.

i. the zero-threshold is the source of the problem

Zero threshold — under total utilitarianism, social value is the sum of individual welfare levels: V = Σ w_i. A life contributes positively to V whenever w_i > 0 — that is, whenever the life is worth living at all, however marginally. This means that ANY number of lives with welfare ε > 0, however small, sum to a contribution that grows without bound as the number of such lives increases — and for a sufficiently large number of them, this sum exceeds the total welfare of any fixed, finite population of high-welfare lives. The Repugnant Conclusion is not an incidental feature of total utilitarianism; it is the direct mathematical consequence of using zero as the threshold for "this life adds positive value"Total utilitarianism computes social value as the sum of individual welfare levels: V = Σ w_i. The threshold at which an additional life contributes positively to V is welfare level zero — any life with w_i > 0, however close to zero, adds a positive amount to the total. This is the entire source of the Repugnant Conclusion: for any fixed population of high-welfare lives, with some finite total welfare W*, we can construct a population of N lives each with welfare ε > 0, and for N > W*/ε, the sum N·ε exceeds W*. This is not a surprising emergent property of a complex system; it is an elementary fact about unbounded sums of positive terms. The Repugnant Conclusion follows from total utilitarianism's structure as directly as "the sum of an unbounded number of positive terms is unbounded" follows from arithmetic, because that is precisely what it is.

ii. the critical-level correction

CLU formula — replace the zero threshold with a positive critical level α > 0. Social value becomes V = Σ(w_i − α). A life now contributes positively to V only if w_i > α — that is, only if its welfare exceeds the critical level, not merely zero. Lives with 0 < w_i < α — "barely worth living" lives, worth living in the ordinary sense but below the critical level — now contribute NEGATIVE value to V. This single change blocks the Repugnant Conclusion directly: the large population of barely-positive lives, each contributing (w_i − α) < 0, no longer sums to something large and positive as N grows — it sums to something that becomes more and more NEGATIVE as N grows, the opposite of what the Repugnant Conclusion requiresCritical-Level Utilitarianism makes a single, surgical correction: replace the threshold of zero with a positive critical level α > 0, chosen to represent the welfare level at which a life is, in some considered sense, "good enough" to count as a positive addition to the population — not merely "above the level of suffering outweighing enjoyment," which is all zero represents, but good enough that adding such a life is itself a contribution to value, rather than merely not a detraction from it. Social value becomes V = Σ(w_i − α). Now a life contributes positively only if w_i > α. Crucially, lives with welfare between zero and α — lives that are, in the ordinary sense, worth living, since w_i > 0 — now contribute negative value to V, because w_i − α < 0 for such lives. This single change blocks the Repugnant Conclusion directly and completely: the large population of barely-positive-welfare lives that the Repugnant Conclusion requires, each contributing a negative amount (w_i − α) to V, sums to an increasingly negative total as N grows, not an increasingly positive one. No amount of such lives can outweigh a smaller, high-welfare population, because each one is, by the very metric used to compare populations, making things worse rather than better.

iii. why this is principled, not ad hoc

Principled, not ad hoc — the introduction of α is not merely "whatever number blocks the unwanted conclusion." CLU can be derived axiomatically: starting from a set of independently plausible conditions on a social welfare functional (anonymity, a Pareto-type condition, continuity, an independence/separability condition, and a condition ruling out total utilitarianism's zero-threshold as the only admissible choice), CLU with SOME positive critical level is the unique form of generalized utilitarianism satisfying the full axiom set. The specific VALUE of α is then a further question (analogous to specifying a discount rate), but the FORM of the solution — utilitarianism with a positive critical level — is forced by the axioms, not chosen post-hoc to avoid an unwanted conclusionThe crucial point, and the one that distinguishes this from a merely ad hoc patch, is that the introduction of a positive critical level is not selected merely because it blocks the unwanted conclusion. CLU can be derived axiomatically: begin with a set of independently motivated conditions on how a social welfare functional should aggregate welfare across variable populations — anonymity (no privileging of which individuals exist), a Pareto-type condition (if everyone is better off, the outcome is better), continuity, an independence or separability condition (the ranking of two populations that agree on some individuals shouldn't depend on the welfare of those shared individuals), and a non-degeneracy condition ruling out the implausible special case where the critical level must be exactly zero. Given this axiom set, generalized utilitarianism with some positive critical level is the *unique* functional form that satisfies all the conditions simultaneously. The specific numerical value of α remains a further question, analogous to specifying a particular discount rate once the form of discounted utilitarianism has been established — but the *form* of the solution, utilitarianism corrected by a positive critical level, is forced by the axioms themselves, not chosen after the fact to avoid the Repugnant Conclusion.

I want to propose a different diagnosis of what has gone wrong in total utilitarianism, and correspondingly a different kind of fix — one that does not require us to specify any fixed threshold at all, critical or otherwise, but instead makes the marginal value of a life sensitive to the size of the population it is being added to.

i. why a fixed threshold might itself be suspect

Fixed threshold worry — any approach (zero, as in total utilitarianism; or α, as in CLU) that fixes a single welfare level as the universal threshold for "positive contribution," applied identically regardless of how large the existing population already is, treats the marginal value of a life of given quality as INDEPENDENT of population size. But there is an independent intuitive pull toward thinking that adding a wonderful life to a small, sparsely populated world is a MORE valuable addition than adding a life of identical quality to an already enormous population — diversity, plenitude, and the marginal contribution of "another instance of value" plausibly diminish as instances accumulate, in a way no FIXED threshold (whether 0 or α) can represent, since a fixed threshold only asks "is w_i above the line," not "how much does adding THIS life, at THIS population size, actually add"Both total utilitarianism and Critical-Level Utilitarianism share a structural feature: the threshold for what counts as a positive contribution — zero, in one case; α, in the other — is fixed, applied identically regardless of how large the existing population already is. Both treat the marginal value of a life of given quality as independent of population size. I want to suggest there is an independent intuitive pull against this. Consider adding a single wonderful life to a small, sparsely populated world, versus adding a life of identical quality to a world already containing billions of similarly wonderful lives. There is a natural thought that the first addition matters more — that the marginal contribution of "another instance of a good life" diminishes as such instances accumulate, in something like the way the marginal utility of an additional unit of a good diminishes as one's holdings of that good increase. No fixed threshold, whether zero or α, can represent this, because a fixed threshold only ever asks "is this life's welfare above the line," never "how much does adding this life, at this population size, actually contribute."

ii. the variable value formalization

Variable value formula — let the contribution of the n-th life added (for a life of welfare w) be w · f(n), where f(n) is a positive, strictly decreasing function of n (the number of lives already in the population), with f(n) → 0 as n → ∞. Total value of a population of N lives is V = Σ(from n=1 to N) w_n · f(n). Because f(n) decreases, each successive life of a GIVEN quality contributes less to V than the previous one did. Crucially, the INFINITE sum Σ f(n) can be made to CONVERGE (e.g., choosing f(n) = 1/n² or similar), meaning that even adding infinitely many lives of some fixed small positive welfare ε contributes only a FINITE total amount of additional value — which can be bounded below whatever value is needed to ensure it never exceeds a smaller high-welfare population's totalFormalize this as follows: let the contribution of the n-th life added, with welfare w, be w·f(n), where f(n) is a positive, strictly decreasing function of the number of lives already in the population, with f(n) → 0 as n → ∞. Total population value is then V = Σ w_n·f(n), summed over all lives in the population. Because f(n) decreases, each successive life of a *given* quality contributes less to total value than an identically-good life would have contributed earlier. The crucial mathematical feature is this: by choosing f(n) to decay quickly enough — for instance, f(n) = 1/n² — the infinite series Σf(n) converges to a finite sum. This means that even adding infinitely many lives, each with some small fixed positive welfare ε, contributes only a *finite* total amount of additional value to V, however many such lives are added — a finite amount that can, by appropriate choice of f, be bounded below whatever total is needed to ensure that no number of barely-positive lives ever exceeds a smaller, high-welfare population's total value.

iii. avoiding the appearance of an arbitrary cutoff

No arbitrary cutoff — unlike CLU, which must specify a single sharp number α below which a life "counts negatively," variable value theory requires no sharp cutoff at all: EVERY life of positive welfare always contributes SOME positive amount to V (since w · f(n) > 0 whenever w > 0 and f(n) > 0), it's just that the amount shrinks continuously as population grows. This avoids a specific objection to CLU: that there is something strange about a sharp threshold α such that a life at welfare level (α − 0.001) counts as making things WORSE while a life at (α + 0.001), nearly identical in quality, counts as making things better. Variable value theory has no such sharp discontinuity in WHETHER a life counts as positive — only a smooth, continuous decline in HOW MUCH it countsThis construction avoids a specific objection that can be pressed against Critical-Level Utilitarianism: the strangeness of a sharp threshold α such that a life at welfare (α − 0.001) counts as making the population *worse*, while an essentially identical life at (α + 0.001) counts as making it *better* — a discontinuity in kind arising from an infinitesimal difference in degree, which strikes many as itself an implausible feature for a theory of value to have. Variable value theory has no such discontinuity. Every life of positive welfare, however small, always contributes *some* positive amount to V, since w·f(n) > 0 whenever both w > 0 and f(n) > 0; there is no level of welfare and no population size at which a positive-welfare life flips to making a negative contribution. What changes, continuously and without any sharp threshold, is only *how much* a life of given quality contributes, as a smooth function of population size. The Repugnant Conclusion is blocked not by declaring barely-positive lives bad, but by ensuring that, in aggregate, an unlimited number of them can never amount to more than a finite, boundable total — while preserving the intuitively important judgement that every worthwhile life is, taken on its own, a good thing.

I want to take a quite different approach from the two we have just heard, because I think both Critical-Level Utilitarianism and Variable Value Theory accept a presupposition of the Repugnant Conclusion that should instead be rejected at the outset: that the question "is population A better than population B" is always a well-formed question with a determinate answer, answerable by some single aggregating function, however cleverly adjusted.

i. better, but not better for anyone

Person-affecting restriction — the core claim: an outcome can be better or worse than another ONLY IF it is better or worse FOR SOMEONE — some person who exists in at least one of the outcomes being compared. The Repugnant Conclusion compares a small population A (existing, happy people) to a large population Z (many additional people, each barely content, who do not exist in A). The additional people in Z are not made WORSE OFF by their non-existence in A — non-existence is not a state a person is in and finds bad — and no one who exists in A is worse off in Z than in A (assuming, as in the standard construction, A's people are equally well off in Z too, or simply do not exist in Z). So the comparison "Z is better than A" cannot be cashed out as "better for someone" in the way the person-affecting restriction requires — the comparison the Repugnant Conclusion relies on is not well-formed under a person-affecting viewThe person-affecting restriction holds that an outcome can be better, or worse, than another only if it is better or worse *for someone* — for some person who actually exists, in at least one of the outcomes being compared. Now consider the standard construction of the Repugnant Conclusion: population A, a smaller group of very happy people, versus population Z, a vastly larger population consisting mostly of additional people, each living a life barely worth living, who do not exist in A at all. Are the additional people in Z made worse off by their non-existence in A? No — non-existence is not a deprivation that a non-existent person suffers; there is no one, in the A-outcome, who is failing to get something they need or want, because there is no such person in A to begin with. Is anyone who exists in A worse off in Z than they are in A? Not in the standard construction, where the original happy people's welfare is unaffected (or they too are simply absent from Z, replaced by the new population). So the judgement "Z is better than A," which the Repugnant Conclusion needs in order to be a repugnant *conclusion* rather than a merely descriptive fact about sums, cannot be cashed out as "better for someone" in the sense the person-affecting restriction requires. The comparison the Repugnant Conclusion relies on is not, on this view, even a well-formed evaluative comparison.

ii. roberts's refinement: variabilism

Variabilism — Roberts refines the simple person-affecting view (which can seem to imply we have NO reason to prefer creating happy people over creating miserable ones, since neither comparison is "better for" anyone determinate across both outcomes) by distinguishing FIXED comparisons (the same set of people exists in both outcomes — here, ordinary betterness-for applies straightforwardly) from VARIABLE comparisons (the populations differ in WHO exists). For variable comparisons, what matters is not a single aggregate "total value" but whether each outcome is good or bad FOR THE PEOPLE WHO EXIST IN THAT OUTCOME, evaluated outcome-by-outcome rather than via a cross-outcome subtraction — meaning "Z is better than A" is rejected not because Z is bad, but because the cross-population SUBTRACTION the Repugnant Conclusion performs (totalling up Z's people and comparing the total to A's total) is not the right operation to perform when the populations don't share their membersI want to note a refinement of this view, due to Melinda Roberts, that addresses an immediate worry: doesn't the simple person-affecting restriction imply we have no reason at all to prefer creating happy future people over creating miserable ones, since in neither case is the comparison "better for" some fixed person across both outcomes? Roberts's variabilism distinguishes *fixed* comparisons — where the same set of people exists in both outcomes being compared, so ordinary person-affecting evaluation applies straightforwardly (it is better for them if they are better off) — from *variable* comparisons, where the populations differ in who exists at all. For variable comparisons, the right question is not "what is the total value, summed across both outcomes, and which total is bigger" — that question presupposes the very aggregative, addable-across-outcomes structure that generates the Repugnant Conclusion. The right question is whether each outcome is good for the people who exist *in that outcome*, evaluated outcome-by-outcome. We can still say that creating a population of flourishing people is choice-worthy compared to creating a population of miserable people, because flourishing is good for the people in the first outcome and misery is bad for the people in the second — without ever needing to perform a cross-population subtraction that treats "more barely-content people" as a quantity that can be added up and compared against a smaller population's totally different total.

iii. dissolving, not answering, the repugnant conclusion

Dissolving vs answering — both CLU and Variable Value theory ACCEPT the framing of the Repugnant Conclusion — that there is a single aggregative function over populations, and the task is to design it so that it doesn't rank Z above A — and then engage in the technical work of designing such a function. The person-affecting approach instead denies that this is the right kind of question to be asking when comparing populations that don't share their members: it says the Repugnant Conclusion's comparison ("Z is better than A") was never well-formed in the way total utilitarianism (and its critical-level and variable-value cousins) assume, because all three of those theories share the assumption that population comparisons proceed via a single real-valued aggregate function applicable across any two populations whatsoever, including ones with no members in common — an assumption the person-affecting restriction rejects at the foundational level, rather than accepting and then constrainingThe contrast I want to draw with the two previous speakers is this. Both Critical-Level Utilitarianism and Variable Value Theory *accept* the framing that generates the Repugnant Conclusion — that there exists a single real-valued aggregate function applicable to any two populations whatsoever, including populations sharing no members, and the task is to design that function cleverly enough that it never ranks the repugnant large population above the appealing small one. They then do the considerable technical work of designing such a function: a critical level, or a population-size-discounting rule. The person-affecting approach denies the underlying framing itself: it says that "is Z better than A," asked as a question about a single aggregate quantity comparable across populations with entirely different memberships, was never a well-formed question to begin with, on a view that takes seriously the idea that betterness must always be betterness *for* someone. This is not answering the Repugnant Conclusion's question with a clever new formula. It is declining to accept that the question, posed in the way total utilitarianism poses it, is one a theory of value needs to answer in kind.

I want to address a problem that has been lurking in this debate since the first two speeches: both Critical-Level Utilitarianism and Variable Value Theory, in solving the Repugnant Conclusion, generate a different and arguably equally troubling implication. I want to show why this happens, and how a third, mathematically distinct kind of discounting avoids it.

i. the sadistic conclusion, diagnosed

Sadistic Conclusion — under CLU, adding a population of NEGATIVE-welfare lives can sometimes be ranked BETTER than adding a population of POSITIVE but below-critical-level welfare lives, because the negative-welfare addition, while bad, may produce a smaller total welfare DECREASE than adding many lives each contributing (w − α) < 0. More starkly: CLU implies that for some populations, it is better to add a smaller number of people with very negative welfare than a larger number of people with positive but sub-critical welfare, since the THRESHOLD-relative accounting can make the larger positive-but-low population's total deficit (relative to α) exceed the smaller negative population's actual deficit. This is the Sadistic Conclusion: adding lives of suffering ranked better than adding lives of (lesser, but still positive) welfareConsider what Critical-Level Utilitarianism implies once we ask about adding lives of negative welfare, not merely lives of low positive welfare. Under CLU, a life with welfare w contributes (w − α) to total value, regardless of whether w itself is positive or negative. This means a population of lives with substantial *negative* welfare can, under the right numerical configuration, generate a smaller total deficit than a larger population of lives with positive but sub-critical welfare — because the sub-critical positive lives are each still being penalised by the gap (w − α), and enough of them, even though individually each life is a life worth living, can produce a larger aggregate deficit than fewer lives of substantial suffering. This is the Sadistic Conclusion: for some pairs of populations, CLU ranks adding a number of people with *negative* welfare as better than adding a (larger) number of people with positive but sub-critical welfare. A theory that can prefer creating suffering to creating modest happiness has traded one repugnant implication for another.

ii. why variable value theory inherits a structurally similar problem

Variable value's version — variable value theory's discount factor f(n) depends on POPULATION SIZE, not on welfare RANK or LEVEL as such. This means the value of adding a given life depends on how many lives already exist, regardless of whether those existing lives are themselves high or low welfare. This generates a related problem: the same discount f(n) applies whether the n existing lives are flourishing or miserable, so variable value theory can be made to imply that the marginal value of adding a happy life is suppressed simply because many OTHER lives (of whatever quality, including very low quality) already exist — divorcing the discount from any connection to the welfare distribution it is meant to be sensitive to, and opening structurally similar "worse population ranked better" possibilities depending on parameter choicesVariable Value Theory's discount factor f(n) depends on population size — how many lives already exist — not on the welfare rank or level of those existing lives.
