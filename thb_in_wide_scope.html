Prime Minister — Wide Scope & Normative RequirementsJohn Broome — “Normative Requirements,” Ratio, 1999; “Wide or Narrow Scope?” Mind, 2007; Rationality Through Reasoning, 2013
Requirements of rationality have wide scope: they are requirements to make true a whole conditional, not requirements to make true its consequent; the instrumental principle is not “if you intend an end E, intend the necessary means M” but “rationality requires (if you intend E and believe M is necessary for E, intend M)”; the wide-scope formulation avoids the bootstrapping problem that afflicts narrow-scope accounts, and the detachment principle that would move from wide to narrow scope is not a requirement of rationality.

The main objection to wide-scope accounts is the symmetry objection: that wide scope permits satisfying a rational requirement by abandoning an end rather than adopting a means, treating these as equally rational options; but this objection conflates the content of a rational requirement (which is wide-scope) with basing permissions and prohibitions (which are narrow); wide-scopers can and should maintain independent basing constraints that explain why adopting means is typically the more rational response, without collapsing back into narrow scope.

Wide-scope reasons — reasons to make true the whole conditional — are the correct account of instrumental rationality; critics of wide scope who invoke the Facilitative Principle (that having a reason to Φ and believing that Ψ-ing is necessary for Φ-ing gives you a reason to Ψ) are making a logical error: the facilitative principle is neither trivially true nor derivable from wide-scope reasons alone; once this error is identified, the main arguments for narrow scope and detachment lose their force, and the wide-scope account stands as the most defensible account of rational requirement.

Logical Arguments

Mr Speaker,

The question before this House is deceptively simple to state and deeply difficult to answer: when rationality requires something of you, what exactly does it require? I shall argue that it requires you to make true a certain conditional — a wide-scope requirement — and that the move from this requirement to a narrower requirement to perform a specific action is a step that rationality does not license.

Let me begin with the instrumental principle, since it is the clearest case. There is a requirement of rationality that concerns means and ends. The narrow-scope version states it as:

(N) If you intend an end E, rationality requires you to intend the necessary means M.

The wide-scope version states it as:

(W) Rationality requires: (if you intend E and believe M is necessary for E, intend M).

These look similar. But the logical scope of the modal operator “rationality requires” differs crucially. In (N), the requirement is inside the conditional: it attaches to the consequent. In (W), the requirement is outside the conditional: it governs the whole. This difference has enormous consequences.

The scope distinction: a logical anatomy.

Let R = “rationality requires,” B(p) = “you believe p,” I(Φ) = “you intend to Φ.”

Narrow scope: B(M is necessary for E) & I(E) → R[I(M)]
Wide scope: R[B(M necessary for E) & I(E) → I(M)]

In the narrow-scope reading, once the antecedent conditions hold, you are under a freestanding rational requirement to intend M. The requirement has detached from its conditional context and lands on you as a direct obligation to act.

In the wide-scope reading, you are required to make the whole conditional true. You can satisfy this requirement in multiple ways: by intending M (satisfying the consequent), by abandoning E (dropping part of the antecedent), or by revising your belief that M is necessary (dropping another part). The requirement never detaches to place a freestanding demand on any particular attitude.
i. the bootstrapping catastrophe: why narrow scope must be wrong

Bootstrapping — narrow scope + rationality is normative → crazy beliefs give you reasons to do crazy things; Ava must intend to exterminate humanity; narrow scope generates this catastropheConsider Ava, who has formed the crazy belief that she ought to exterminate all human life. The narrow-scope enkratic principle (N) says: rationality requires that, if you believe you ought to Φ, you intend to Φ. Applied to Ava:

Premise 1: Ava believes she ought to exterminate human life.
Premise 2 (N): Rationality requires that if Ava believes she ought to Φ, she intends to Φ.
Conclusion: Rationality requires that Ava intend to exterminate human life.

Now if rationality is normative — if one ought to satisfy rational requirements — we reach: Ava ought to intend to exterminate human life. This is a reductio of narrow scope. The conclusion follows validly from premises that narrow-scopers must accept. The wide-scope formulation avoids this entirely: (W) requires Ava to make true the conditional ‘if I believe I ought to exterminate human life, I intend to exterminate human life.’ She can satisfy this wide-scope requirement by revising her crazy belief. No detachment to a freestanding obligation to exterminate occurs.

ii. what detachment requires and why it is not a requirement of rationality

Detachment is not licensed — moving from wide to narrow scope requires a further controversial principle; rationality does not include this principle; the move is not validThe opposition will argue that the wide-scope formulation is unsatisfying because it never tells you what to do. When you actually have the belief that you ought to Φ, you satisfy the wide-scope requirement only by Φ-ing or by revising the belief. But the wide-scope requirement does not specify which. Surely, says the opposition, rationality must give you a directive here. I deny that rationality does this. Rationality is a structural matter: it concerns the coherence of your attitudes. It does not generate freestanding demands on which particular attitude to have. To move from “rationality requires (if B then I)” to “given B, rationality requires I” is to invoke the detachment principle — and detachment is not a requirement of rationality. It requires the further claim that rationality licenses modus ponens on requirements. Broome’s view is precisely that this further claim is not part of rational requirement.

iii. the enkratic requirement: wide scope preserves the right content

Enkratic requirement — rationality requires: if you believe you ought to Φ, intend to Φ; wide scope correctly makes this a coherence requirement; narrow scope wrongly makes it a directive to actThe enkratic requirement is perhaps the central case. In my earlier formulation, it states:

(Enkratic-W) Rationality requires: (if you believe you ought to Φ, intend to Φ).

This is a coherence requirement: it demands that your intentions be aligned with your normative beliefs. An agent who believes she ought to pay back her debt but has no intention of doing so violates this requirement. The wide-scope formulation correctly captures this as a structural property of the agent’s attitude-set, not as a directive to take any particular action. The narrow-scope version:

(Enkratic-N) If you believe you ought to Φ, rationality requires you to intend to Φ.

...makes it a freestanding directive. Once you have the belief, rationality commands the intention. This is too strong: it implies that all your normative beliefs are action-guiding through rationality alone, which would mean that rationality is doing the work that reasons are supposed to do. Rationality should govern coherence, not substitute for substantive normative justification.

iv. reasoning through rationality: wide scope and the process of reasoning

Reasoning — wide scope correctly models reasoning as a process that can reach intention by traversing the conditional; the wide-scope requirement is what reasoning satisfies; narrow scope confuses the product with the requirementIn Rationality Through Reasoning I argue that reasoning is a process by which wide-scope requirements come to be satisfied. When you reason from your belief that you ought to Φ to the intention to Φ, you traverse the conditional that the wide-scope requirement governs. The output of successful reasoning is the intention — but the requirement that governed the reasoning was wide-scope throughout. The opposition confuses the product of reasoning (the intention, which is narrow-scope in content) with the requirement that the reasoning satisfies (which is wide-scope). There is no inconsistency in having wide-scope requirements be satisfied through reasoning processes that generate narrow-scope attitudes as their outputs. The detachment principle would make the output of the reasoning a direct requirement before the reasoning has occurred — which is precisely the bootstrapping that must be avoided.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The bootstrapping reductio: narrow scope + normative rationality → absurd obligations from crazy beliefs
1.Narrow-scope enkratic: if you believe you ought to Φ, rationality requires you to intend to Φ.
2.Ava believes she ought to exterminate humanity. By (N): rationality requires Ava to intend to exterminate humanity.
3.If rationality is normative (you ought to satisfy its requirements), then: Ava ought to intend to exterminate humanity. This is a reductio.
∴Narrow scope must be rejected; it generates absurd obligations from crazy beliefs via modus ponens once rationality is taken to be normative.
Argument II — Wide scope avoids bootstrapping by keeping the requirement outside the conditional
1.Wide-scope enkratic: rationality requires (if you believe you ought to Φ, intend to Φ). The requirement governs the whole conditional; no detachment is licensed.
2.Ava can satisfy this requirement by revising her crazy belief; no conclusion about what she ought to intend follows from the wide-scope requirement plus her having the belief.
3.Wide scope correctly locates rational requirement as a structural property of attitude-sets, not as a content-generating directive to act.
∴Wide scope avoids the bootstrapping reductio; rationality imposes coherence demands, not freestanding action directives.
Argument III — Detachment is not a requirement of rationality; it requires an independent and controversial principle
1.Moving from “R(if B then I)” to “given B, R(I)” requires the detachment principle: that rational requirements license modus ponens on their content.
2.The detachment principle is not a requirement of logic (modus ponens applies to propositions, not to deontic operators without bridging axioms) and is not a requirement of rationality.
3.Without detachment, wide-scope requirements do not yield narrow-scope directives; the instrumental and enkratic principles remain wide-scope.
∴Detachment requires an independent controversial principle that Broome denies; without it, wide-scope requirements do not yield the narrow-scope directives the opposition claims.
Argument IV — Reasoning traverses the conditional; the product (intention) is narrow-scope but the requirement is wide-scope throughout
1.Reasoning from a normative belief to an intention traverses the conditional that the wide-scope requirement governs; the output is a narrow-scope intention.
2.The requirement that governed the reasoning was wide-scope throughout; the narrow-scope intention is the product, not the requirement itself.
3.The detachment principle would wrongly make the intention a requirement before reasoning has occurred, generating bootstrapping from unexamined beliefs.
∴Wide scope and the process of reasoning are entirely compatible; reasoning generates narrow-scope intentions by satisfying wide-scope requirements, not by detaching them.

Mr Speaker,

My colleague Broome has established the wide-scope position and its central virtue: avoiding the bootstrapping catastrophe that afflicts narrow-scope accounts. My role is to address the opposition’s strongest objection: the symmetry problem. This objection holds that wide scope is not merely too permissive in one direction but structurally broken — that it treats giving up an end as rationally on a par with taking the means to it, and that this symmetry is unacceptable.

I shall argue that the symmetry objection conflates two distinct questions: the content of a rational requirement and the basing norms that govern how that requirement is to be satisfied. Wide scope correctly answers the first question while leaving the second to independent norms. Once the distinction is clear, the symmetry objection loses all force.

The symmetry objection stated.

You intend to run a marathon and believe that buying running shoes is necessary for running the marathon. The wide-scope instrumental requirement says: rationality requires (if you intend to run the marathon and believe shoes are necessary, intend to buy shoes).

You can satisfy this requirement by intending to buy shoes, or by abandoning your intention to run the marathon, or by revising your belief that shoes are necessary. Schroeder and Kolodny object: surely it is more rational to intend to buy shoes than to abandon the marathon intention. Wide scope treats these as equally rational ways of satisfying the requirement. This seems wrong.
i. the symmetry objection conflates requirement-content with basing norms

Basing distinction — content of wide-scope requirement is symmetric; basing norms are not; the objection misidentifies what wide scope claims; way separates these two questionsThe symmetry objection correctly observes that the wide-scope requirement is satisfied by any combination of attitudes that makes the conditional true. It incorrectly infers that wide scope therefore treats all satisfaction routes as equally rational. The wide-scope requirement specifies what coherence pattern the agent’s attitudes must exhibit. It does not specify which attitudes should be formed or revised to achieve that pattern. The latter question — which revision is the rational one — is answered by basing norms: norms about which attitudes it is rational to hold on the basis of which others. Basing norms are separate from, and not in tension with, wide-scope requirements. A wide-scope theorist can maintain both that the requirement is wide-scope (symmetric content) and that basing norms make it irrational to satisfy the requirement by abandoning one’s end without reason (asymmetric basing). The symmetry objection treats the first claim as excluding the second; it does not.

ii. basing permissions and prohibitions: the wide-scoper’s tools

Basing permissions and prohibitions — you are not permitted to base the abandonment of an end merely on the absence of an intention to take the means; this is a basing prohibition, not part of requirement content; wide scope uses this correctlyConsider the prohibition: you may not revise your intention to run the marathon on the basis of your failure to intend to buy shoes, when the failure to intend shoes is not itself grounded in anything about the marathon. This is a basing prohibition: certain attitude revisions are not permissible as responses to the wide-scope requirement when they are grounded only in other attitudes whose absence triggered the requirement. This prohibition does not contradict the wide-scope requirement. The requirement says: make the conditional true. The prohibition says: do not make it true by means of certain attitude revisions that are only permissible on independent grounds. Together, these two norms — the wide-scope requirement and the basing prohibition — explain exactly what the narrow-scoper wants to explain, without collapsing into narrow scope or licensing the bootstrapping catastrophe.

iii. the narrow-scoper cannot avoid symmetry either

Narrow scope has symmetry too — Schroeder's narrow-scope principle also permits satisfying the requirement by revising the antecedent belief; narrow scope cannot avoid symmetry without additional constraints; so the objection doesn't favour narrow over wideSchroeder and Kolodny press the symmetry objection against wide scope. But narrow scope has an analogous problem. The narrow-scope enkratic principle says: if you believe you ought to Φ, rationality requires you to intend to Φ. But what if your belief that you ought to Φ is based on false or weak grounds? Surely in that case rationality requires you to revise the belief, not to form the intention. The narrow-scoper must say: yes, and in that case you are also rationally required to revise the belief (by some further narrow-scope requirement). But then narrow scope also has multiple routes to satisfying or circumventing requirements — multiple ways of avoiding the irrational state. The symmetry the objector complains about is a feature of any account that allows multiple incoherent states to be resolved by revising any member of the incoherent set. Wide scope faces it no more than narrow scope.

iv. the positive case for asymmetric wide scope: reasons explain what revision is warranted

Reasons explain revision — when you have reasons to maintain E and no reasons to abandon it, you have reason to intend M; this is a reasons-based asymmetry, not a rationality-based one; the two-level view is correctThe correct account of why intending the means is typically more rational than abandoning the end is not that rationality itself mandates it — that would be narrow scope. The correct account is that you have reasons to maintain your ends that you do not have reasons to abandon. When you have no reason to abandon the marathon intention, rationality-plus-reasons delivers the verdict: intend the shoes. The wide-scope requirement operates at the structural level; reasons operate at the substantive level. Together, they explain the intuitively asymmetric result without the bootstrapping liability. The opposition collapses this two-level structure into a single normative demand, losing both the anti-bootstrapping virtue of wide scope and the substantive guidance of reasons.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The symmetry objection conflates requirement-content with basing norms; these are separate questions
1.The content of a rational requirement specifies what coherence pattern must obtain; it does not specify which attitudes should be formed or revised to achieve it.
2.Basing norms specify which attitude revisions are rational given the agent’s reasons; they are independent of requirement-content.
3.The symmetry objection treats requirement-content as if it were the only normative constraint; it ignores basing norms, which are available to the wide-scoper as separate constraints.
∴The symmetry objection misfires; wide-scopers have the resources to explain why certain satisfaction routes are more rational without abandoning wide scope.
Argument II — Basing prohibitions explain why abandoning ends without reason is irrational, consistent with wide scope
1.Basing prohibition: you may not revise your end-intention merely on the basis of your failure to intend the means, unless there are independent reasons for revising the end.
2.This prohibition does not contradict the wide-scope requirement; it specifies which satisfaction routes are permissible given your other attitudes and reasons.
3.The combination of wide-scope requirement and basing prohibition explains exactly the asymmetry the opposition wants, without narrow scope and without bootstrapping.
∴Wide scope plus basing prohibitions yields the right verdicts without narrow scope; the symmetry objection is dissolved by the two-level structure of requirements and basing norms.
Argument III — Narrow scope faces an analogous symmetry problem; the objection does not favour narrow over wide
1.Narrow-scope enkratic requires you to intend to Φ if you believe you ought to Φ. But if the belief is unwarranted, a further narrow-scope requirement may require revision of the belief; multiple routes remain.
2.The symmetry the objection identifies — multiple permissible routes to satisfying or escaping requirements — is a feature of any account that allows incoherent sets to be resolved by revising any member.
3.If both accounts face analogous symmetry considerations, the objection provides no differential reason to prefer narrow scope over wide scope.
∴The symmetry objection applies to narrow scope as much as to wide scope; it does not provide a reason to prefer narrow scope; wide scope retains its bootstrapping-avoidance advantage undiminished.
Argument IV — Reasons explain the asymmetry between taking means and abandoning ends; this is a two-level account, not a collapse into narrow scope
1.When you have reasons to maintain your end and none to abandon it, reasons plus wide-scope structural requirements jointly recommend taking the means.
2.This is a two-level account: structural rationality governs coherence; reasons govern which particular attitude is warranted; neither level alone generates the verdict.
3.The narrow-scoper collapses this to a single level, losing both the anti-bootstrapping virtue of wide scope and the explanatory role of reasons.
∴The two-level account is superior: reasons explain asymmetry of rational revision, wide scope governs structural coherence, and bootstrapping is avoided throughout.

Mr Speaker,

I want to contribute a more recent and in some ways more radical defence of the wide-scope position. My argument proceeds in two steps. First, I shall show that coherence — the satisfaction of wide-scope rational requirements — is genuinely normative, in the sense that there is something genuinely amiss with an incoherent agent, even if this normativity is not the normativity of reasons. Second, I shall show that narrow-scopers, when they try to capture the asymmetries they care about, are forced to appeal to constraints that are, at bottom, wide-scope.

The first step addresses Kolodny’s challenge: why be rational if rationality does not give you reasons? My answer: because the normativity of structural coherence is its own kind of normativity, not reducible to or derivable from the normativity of reasons. An incoherent agent is criticisable not because she has failed to respond to reasons but because her attitudes are in structural conflict.

The conflict between evidence-responsiveness and coherence.

Anna believes she ought to call her friend. She fails to intend to call. She is incoherent: her belief and intention are in structural conflict — she violates the enkratic wide-scope requirement. This is a genuine rational failing, but notice it is not necessarily a failure to respond to reasons. Anna might have excellent evidence that she ought to call; she might have excellent reasons to call; she might even recognise those reasons. The failing is specifically structural: her attitudes are in conflict with each other. This is the domain of structural rationality, which is governed by wide-scope requirements and is not the same domain as reasons-responsiveness.
i. the normativity of coherence is independent of the normativity of reasons

Coherence normativity — being incoherent is genuinely bad in its own right, not merely because it reflects badly on your reasons-responsiveness; wide-scope captures this; Kolodny's challenge is metKolodny argues that there are no reasons to satisfy structural requirements, and therefore that structural rationality has no normative authority. My response is to distinguish two kinds of normativity: the normativity of reasons (you ought to have the attitudes you have conclusive reasons to have) and the normativity of coherence (you ought not to have attitudes that are in structural conflict with each other). These are different normative domains. A detective who has excellent evidence that the butler did it but refuses to form the corresponding belief is criticisable under the normativity of reasons. An agent who believes she ought to Φ but fails to intend to Φ without revising the belief is criticisable under the normativity of coherence. The latter criticism is not captured by any reasons-based account, because no reasons are necessarily involved in the incoherence.

ii. narrow-scopers must appeal to wide-scope constraints after all

Narrow scope smuggles wide scope — to avoid bootstrapping, narrow-scopers must restrict when narrow-scope requirements apply; those restrictions are wide-scope constraints in disguiseHere is my central claim about the narrow-scope position: it cannot be maintained consistently without smuggling in wide-scope constraints. The narrow-scope enkratic principle says: if you believe you ought to Φ, rationality requires you to intend to Φ. But narrow-scopers recognise that this generates bootstrapping with crazy beliefs. To avoid it, they typically add a restriction: the narrow-scope requirement applies only when the belief is reasonable, or warranted, or formed on adequate grounds. But these restrictions are themselves wide-scope constraints in disguise: they say that the requirement as a whole — the full conditional — applies only under certain conditions. The narrow-scoper has not escaped wide scope; she has presupposed it as a condition on her narrow-scope claim.

iii. narrow-scoping for wide-scopers: the convergence result

Convergence — Worsnip shows wide-scopers can derive narrow-scope verdicts in specific cases without narrow-scope requirements; narrow scope is derivable from wide scope plus substantive facts; the opposition's view is not independentIn my 2015 paper I argue for a convergence result: wide-scopers can derive narrow-scope verdicts in specific cases without invoking narrow-scope requirements. Suppose you believe you ought to Φ, your belief is well-grounded, you have no reason to revise it, and there are no obstacles to intending to Φ. In this case, the wide-scope requirement plus these substantive facts jointly entail that the only rational way to satisfy the requirement is to intend to Φ. The narrow-scope conclusion is derivable without a narrow-scope premise. The opposition’s narrow-scope requirement is therefore not a further primitive but a derived result that wide-scopers can explain. When the narrow-scope conclusion does not hold — because the belief is unwarranted — the wide-scope account correctly withholds the narrow-scope conclusion. This is exactly what we want.

iv. the conflict of evidence and coherence: why structural rationality matters

Conflict of evidence and coherence — an agent can be coherent but respond badly to evidence, or respond well to evidence but be incoherent; these are genuinely different normative assessments; wide scope captures thisThe deepest reason to prefer wide scope over narrow scope is this: structural rationality and evidence-responsiveness are genuinely different normative domains, and conflating them generates confusion. An agent who has brilliant evidence that she ought to Φ and promptly intends to Φ may satisfy the narrow-scope enkratic requirement. But she satisfies it for the right reason only if she intends to Φ because she has that evidence. The wide-scope account, by keeping structural coherence separate from reasons-responsiveness, correctly captures the two independent dimensions of rational assessment. An agent can fail on one dimension while succeeding on the other. The narrow-scope collapse of these dimensions into a single requirements structure loses this fine-grained assessment. Wide scope is the more discriminating and therefore the more adequate account.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The normativity of coherence is independent of the normativity of reasons; Kolodny’s challenge does not succeed
1.There are two kinds of normativity: the normativity of reasons (ought to have attitudes one has conclusive reasons to have) and the normativity of coherence (ought not to have attitudes in structural conflict).
2.Structural incoherence — believing one ought to Φ while failing to intend to Φ — is genuinely criticisable even in the absence of any reasons-failure; the criticism is structural, not reasons-based.
3.Kolodny’s argument that there are no reasons to be structurally rational does not show that structural rationality has no normative authority; it shows that its authority is not derived from reasons.
∴The normativity of coherence is genuine and independent; wide-scope requirements have normative authority that is not undermined by the absence of reasons to comply with them.
Argument II — Narrow-scopers must appeal to wide-scope constraints to avoid bootstrapping; narrow scope is not independent of wide scope
1.To avoid bootstrapping with crazy beliefs, narrow-scopers must restrict the application of their requirements to cases where the antecedent belief is reasonable or well-grounded.
2.These restrictions are wide-scope constraints: they apply conditions to the whole requirement, not just to its consequent.
3.Narrow-scopers cannot maintain their position consistently without presupposing wide-scope constraints; their view is not a genuine alternative to wide scope.
∴Narrow scope smuggles wide scope in to avoid bootstrapping; the apparently opposed positions are not symmetric alternatives; wide scope is the more principled foundation.
Argument III — Narrow-scoping for wide-scopers: narrow-scope verdicts are derivable from wide scope plus substantive facts
1.When an agent has a well-grounded belief that she ought to Φ, no reasons to revise it, and no obstacles to Φ-ing, the only rational way to satisfy the wide-scope requirement is to intend to Φ.
2.This narrow-scope verdict is derivable from wide-scope requirements plus substantive facts; no narrow-scope primitive is needed.
3.When the belief is unwarranted, wide scope correctly withholds the narrow-scope verdict; this is precisely the anti-bootstrapping result we want.
∴Narrow-scope verdicts are derivable from wide scope; the opposition’s narrow-scope requirements are not primitives but derived results; wide scope is the more fundamental account.
Argument IV — Wide scope correctly separates structural coherence from evidence-responsiveness; narrow scope conflates them
1.Structural rationality (coherence) and evidence-responsiveness are genuinely different normative dimensions; an agent can fail on one while succeeding on the other.
2.Wide scope keeps these dimensions separate: structural requirements govern coherence independently of whether particular attitudes are warranted by reasons.
3.Narrow scope conflates these dimensions by making structural requirements conditional on the warrantedness of the antecedent belief; this loses the fine-grained two-dimensional assessment.
∴Wide scope is the more discriminating and adequate account; it correctly separates coherence from evidence-responsiveness and provides independent assessments on both dimensions.

I close the government’s case. My colleagues have established the wide-scope position and defended it against the symmetry and normativity objections. My role is to address the opposition’s claim that wide-scope rational requirements can nevertheless generate narrow-scope verdicts through the Facilitative Principle — the principle that a reason to Φ combined with the belief that Ψ-ing is necessary for Φ-ing generates a reason to Ψ. The opposition uses this principle to argue that wide-scope reasons entail narrow-scope reasons via the Facilitative Principle, so that the distinction between wide and narrow scope ultimately collapses.

I shall show that this argument rests on a logical error in how it applies the Facilitative Principle, and that once the error is identified, the opposition’s strategy fails. Wide-scope reasons do not entail narrow-scope reasons via any valid application of the Facilitative Principle.

The Facilitative Principle and the opposition’s strategy.

Facilitative Principle (FP): If you have a reason to Φ, and you believe that Ψ-ing is necessary for Φ-ing, then you have a reason to Ψ.

The opposition’s argument: (1) You have a wide-scope reason to make true: (if you believe you ought to Φ, intend to Φ). (2) You believe that intending to Φ is necessary for making that conditional true when you already believe you ought to Φ. (3) By FP: you have a (narrow-scope) reason to intend to Φ. (4) Therefore wide-scope reasons entail narrow-scope reasons, and the distinction collapses.

The error: premise (2) misidentifies what intending to Φ is necessary for. It is necessary for making true the consequent of the conditional, but that is not the same as being necessary for making true the conditional as a whole. The conditional can also be made true by revising the antecedent belief.
i. the facilitative principle is not violated by wide scope

FP doesn't apply — intending to Φ is not necessary for making the wide-scope conditional true; you can make it true by revising the belief; the opposition's application of FP rests on confusing the consequent with the whole conditionalThe opposition’s application of the Facilitative Principle to wide-scope reasons rests on the following inference: “you have reason to make true (if B, then I); you believe that I-ing is necessary for making it true; therefore you have reason to I.” But the premise is false: I-ing is not necessary for making the conditional true. The conditional (B → I) is made true by making I true (given B), but it is also made true by making B false. When you have a wide-scope reason to make true (B → I), and you can make it true either by forming I or by revising B, I-ing is not a necessary means to satisfying the reason. Therefore the Facilitative Principle does not apply, and no narrow-scope reason to I is generated. The opposition’s collapse argument fails at this step.

ii. wide-scope reasons and their proper object

Wide-scope reasons — the object of a wide-scope reason is the whole conditional; reasons do not transfer through the conditional to the consequent; this is the crucial logical pointThe proper object of a wide-scope reason is the whole conditional: the reason is to make true “(B → I).” Reasons transfer through necessary means by the Facilitative Principle only when the necessary means is truly necessary for the reason’s object. Since the reason’s object is a material conditional, and material conditionals can be made true by falsifying the antecedent, no single element of the consequent is a necessary means to satisfying the reason. The Facilitative Principle therefore generates no specific narrow-scope reason from a wide-scope reason. This is not a limitation of the wide-scope position; it is its strength. The wide-scope reason imposes a structural constraint without generating specific action directives — and this is precisely the right level of normative force for structural rationality.

iii. wide-scope reasons without detachment: the independence of rationality from reasons

Rationality independent of reasons — Broome argues rationality requirements are not reasons; there is no bridge principle; even if you have wide-scope reasons, detachment to narrow-scope action-guidance requires an independent controversial premiseThere is a deeper point underlying the opposition’s strategy. Broome believes that the wide-scope formulation rescues rationality from the bootstrapping problem while preserving its normative significance. I shall argue that it cannot do both. Either wide-scope requirements are normative — in which case bootstrapping returns in a different form — or they avoid bootstrapping only by becoming normatively idle, telling us nothing about what we have reason to do. The wide-scope manoeuvre does not solve the problem Broome thinks it solves; it merely changes which horn of the dilemma we are impaled on.

My argument has three steps. First, I show that wide-scope requirements, taken as normative, generate a structurally identical bootstrapping problem at the level of the conditional itself. Second, I show that if wide-scope requirements are not normative — if there is no sense in which we ought to satisfy them — then satisfying or failing to satisfy them is not the kind of thing we have any reason to care about, contrary to the entire motivation for the rationality debate. Third, I argue that the only stable position is to deny that rationality, in the structural sense Broome describes, generates reasons at all — which undermines the normative weight the wide-scope formulation was meant to preserve.

The dilemma for wide scope.

Horn 1 (Normative wide scope): Rationality requires (B → I), and you ought to satisfy this requirement. Horn 2 (Non-normative wide scope): Rationality requires (B → I), but you have no reason to satisfy this requirement. I want to press the symmetry objection in its sharpest form, because I believe Way and Worsnip have not actually answered it — they have redescribed it. The objection is not merely that wide scope “permits” satisfying a requirement by abandoning an end. The objection is that wide scope, by its very logical form, is constitutively incapable of explaining a basic asymmetry that any adequate theory of practical rationality must explain: the asymmetry between processes of reasoning that are recognisably rational and processes that, while logically resulting in coherence, are not rational at all.

Consider two agents. Agent One believes she ought to call her mother, and on this basis, comes to intend to call her mother. Agent Two believes she ought to call her mother, fails to form any intention to call, and resolves the resulting tension by abandoning the belief that she ought to call — not because she has acquired any new evidence, but simply because she finds it easier to give up the belief than to act on it. Both agents end up coherent. Wide scope treats both as satisfying the same requirement. But Agent One has reasoned well, and Agent Two has rationalised — in the pejorative sense. This is not a subtle point; it is the most basic distinction in the theory of practical reason, and wide scope cannot draw it.

Way and Worsnip say: the wide-scope requirement explains what coherence pattern is required; basing norms explain which route to coherence is rational.

I want to develop the positive case for narrow scope by addressing the question that the government has, I think, never adequately answered: in what sense, if any, ought we to be rational? My view is that rationality is genuinely normative — there is a sense in which we ought to comply with rational requirements — but that this normativity is grounded in our reasons, not independent of them. The transparency of rational requirements to reasons.

When an agent believes she ought to Φ and yet does not intend to Φ, what exactly has gone wrong? The wide-scope answer: her attitudes fail to satisfy a coherence pattern. The narrow-scope answer (suitably developed): if her belief that she ought to Φ is correct — if she really does have conclusive reason to Φ — then she has failed to respond to that reason by intending to Φ. If her belief is incorrect, then she has formed a false normative belief, and that is the failure (a failure of theoretical, not practical, reason).

The narrow-scope answer explains the badness of incoherence by reference to a more fundamental failure: failure to respond to reasons (practical or theoretical) that the agent in fact has. The wide-scope answer posits a free-floating coherence requirement whose badness is not explained by anything more fundamental.

2.It avoids bootstrapping: Ava’s unwarranted belief does not trigger the requirement; no conclusion follows about what she ought to intend.
3.It is reasons-tracking: when the antecedent is warranted, the requirement connects directly to the agent’s actual reasons, unlike wide scope’s free-floating conditional.
∴The warrant-restricted narrow-scope enkratic principle achieves everything wide scope achieves (no bootstrapping) while also achieving what wide scope cannot (direct connection to reasons); it is strictly superior.
Argument IV — Wide scope reduces rationality to a formal property detached from reasons, making “why be rational?” unanswerable in the relevant sense
1.Wide scope characterises rationality as formal coherence among attitudes, independent of whether those attitudes track the agent’s actual reasons.
2.Two agents with identical formal coherence can differ enormously in how well their attitudes track their reasons (one reason-responsive, one a rationalising monster).
3.If rationality is merely this formal property, “why be rational?” has no good answer; narrow scope (warrant-restricted) answers it by tying rationality to reasons.
∴Wide scope cannot answer “why be rational?” in the way that matters; narrow scope, by connecting rational requirements directly to an agent’s reasons via the warrant condition, can.

Mr Speaker,

I want to close the opposition’s case by stepping back from the all-or-nothing framing that has dominated this debate. Both sides have argued as if the choice were binary: either all rational requirements have wide scope, or all have narrow scope. I think this framing is itself a mistake, and that recognising this is the most important contribution the opposition can make.

My view, developed across several papers, is that different requirements of rationality have different logical structures, and that the instrumental principle in particular — the principle that has dominated this debate — supports narrow-scope detachment in a way that the enkratic principle does not. The government has over-generalised from the enkratic case, where bootstrapping is a genuine worry, to the instrumental case, where it is not.

Why the instrumental principle is different from the enkratic principle.

The enkratic principle’s antecedent is a normative belief: “you believe you ought to Φ.” Normative beliefs can be wildly mistaken — Ava’s belief that she ought to exterminate humanity is a normative belief that is about as wrong as a normative belief can be. This is what makes narrow-scope enkratic bootstrapping so vivid: the antecedent itself can already be crazy.

The instrumental principle’s antecedent is different: “you intend E, and you believe M is necessary for E.” Intending an end is not, in general, the kind of attitude that can be “crazy” in the way a normative belief can be. Intending to run a marathon, intending to buy groceries, intending to learn a language — these are not normative claims that could be wildly mistaken about the moral structure of the universe. They are plans. And believing that a particular means is necessary for a particular end is typically a matter of ordinary means-end cognition, not metaethical commitment.
i. the asymmetry between enkratic and instrumental antecedents

Different antecedents — enkratic antecedent (normative belief) can be crazy in a way that instrumental antecedent (intention + means-belief) generally cannot; bootstrapping worry doesn't transferThe bootstrapping worry trades on the fact that a normative belief can be radically mistaken while still being a belief the agent actually has — Ava’s belief is a paradigm case. But intentions are not beliefs about how things stand morally; they are plans for action, and the space of “crazy intentions” that could fuel an analogous bootstrapping argument for the instrumental principle is much more constrained. Could someone intend to exterminate humanity, believe that some action M is necessary for this, and thereby be narrow-scope-required to intend M? Yes — but in this case, the badness is already fully present in the antecedent: the agent already intends something monstrous. The instrumental principle does not manufacture new badness; it merely says that, given this already-monstrous intention, intending the necessary means is what coherence with that intention would involve. This is a very different situation from the enkratic case, where the normative belief alone (without any intention yet in place) generates an intention to do something monstrous.

ii. detachment for the instrumental principle is mostly benign

Benign detachment — in ordinary cases (intend to run marathon, believe shoes necessary), detaching to 'rationality requires intending to buy shoes' is exactly the action-guidance we want; the government's blanket rejection of detachment throws this out tooConsider the overwhelming majority of instances of the instrumental principle: an agent intends some ordinary, non-monstrous end (running a marathon, finishing a degree, cooking dinner) and believes some ordinary means is necessary. In these cases, detachment — “given that you intend the marathon and believe shoes are necessary, rationality requires you to intend to buy shoes” — is exactly the action-guiding conclusion we want from a theory of practical rationality. The government’s blanket rejection of detachment, motivated by the enkratic bootstrapping worry, throws out this overwhelming majority of benign cases along with the rare problematic ones. A theory that cannot tell us, in the ordinary case, that we are rationally required to buy the shoes — full stop, not merely required to satisfy some conditional that could equally be satisfied by abandoning the marathon — is not doing the job a theory of practical rationality is supposed to do.

iii. a unified treatment: detachability tracks the badness already present in the antecedent

Unified treatment — detachment is safe exactly when the antecedent doesn't itself encode something that could be 'crazy' in the relevant sense; this explains both the enkratic worry and the instrumental safety with one principleHere is the unifying generalisation. Detachment from a wide-scope requirement “rationality requires (A → C)” to a narrow-scope requirement “given A, rationality requires C” is safe exactly when A does not itself encode a normative error that the detachment would then propagate into a positive requirement. The enkratic antecedent (a normative belief) can encode exactly this kind of error, which is why enkratic detachment is dangerous. The instrumental antecedent (an intention plus a means-belief) typically does not encode a normative error of the relevant kind — intending to buy groceries is not a normative error, even if eating implies all sorts of further normative commitments about animal welfare or environmental impact that the agent hasn’t considered. This unifying generalisation explains both why the government is right to worry about enkratic detachment and why they are wrong to extend that worry to the instrumental principle.

iv. the upshot: a mixed view is correct, and it vindicates the spirit of detachment

Mixed view — some requirements (enkratic) are wide-scope or warrant-restricted narrow-scope; others (instrumental, in ordinary cases) support detachment; the government's blanket defence of wide scope is the over-generalisation, not the opposition's viewThe correct view of rational requirements is mixed: the enkratic requirement should indeed be handled carefully, perhaps along the lines Kiesewetter suggests (narrow scope restricted to warranted beliefs) or with the wide-scope formulation Broome favours — reasonable people can disagree about the enkratic case specifically. But the instrumental principle, in the vast majority of its applications, supports detachment to narrow-scope, action-guiding conclusions, and this is exactly what we want from a theory of practical reason. The government’s position — that all rational requirements have wide scope and detachment is never licensed — is the over-generalisation in this debate, not the opposition’s view. The motion before this House asks us to endorse wide scope and reject detachment simpliciter. Given that detachment is benign and action-guiding for the instrumental principle in ordinary cases, this motion is too strong, and the House should reject it.
