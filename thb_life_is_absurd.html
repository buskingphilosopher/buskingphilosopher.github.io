Mr President,

Most of us feel at some moments that life is absurd. This sense is reported so widely, and provokes such philosophical interest, that it deserves serious examination rather than either dismissal or theatrical despair. I want to argue three things: that the common arguments for the absurdity of human life are inadequate; that there is nevertheless a genuine and important sense in which life is absurd; and that this absurdity, properly understood, calls for neither the despair nor the defiance with which it is usually greeted, but for something more subtle — something like ironic acknowledgement.

On why the familiar arguments fail

Common arguments for absurdity fail: they depend on comparative standards that undermine themselvesIt is often said that life is absurd because nothing we do now will matter in a million years. But consider the principle on which this argument rests: that what matters must matter always, or at any rate eventually. This principle is self-undermining. If it is true that what matters must matter always, then the fact that in a million years nothing will matter also fails to matter — in a million years. And if it does not matter then that our present concerns will not matter in a million years, why should this fact make us doubt whether they matter now? The argument from temporal remoteness cancels itself. Similarly with spatial smallness. If our lives are absurd because we are tiny, then they would presumably be less absurd if we were larger. But why should size matter to significance? A universe full of large things engaged in ultimately pointless activity would be no less absurd for their size. The arguments from scale — temporal or spatial — confuse what is large with what matters, and that confusion is precisely the mistake they should be exposing.

On the genuine source of absurdity — the dual-perspective structure

True absurdity: the clash between our inescapable subjective engagement and our capacity to see that engagement as arbitrary from outsideThe true source of the sense of absurdity is not the smallness of human life in space and time but a peculiar discrepancy within human life itself. We are unique among animals, so far as we know, in being able to step outside our immediate engagement with the world and view ourselves from a vantage point outside that engagement. We can regard our lives with the kind of scrutiny that we ordinarily apply to other things: we can ask whether the projects that absorb us are really worth pursuing, whether the seriousness with which we pursue them is justified, whether the whole enterprise of living one's life is grounded in anything at all. And when we do this — when we take up what I have called the view from nowhere — we find that nothing compels the answer yes. No external justification presents itself. The projects, values, and commitments that from within seem pressing and important appear, from outside, as arbitrary impositions on our brief and insignificant sojourn in the cosmos. And yet — crucially — we cannot simply adopt the external view and remain there. We are dragged back into our lives. We wake up, we must decide what to do, we must live. The consciousness that our engagement with life might be groundless cannot remove the engagement itself. This is the absurdity: not that life is small, but that we are simultaneously fully engaged in it and fully capable of seeing that engagement as groundless.

On why absurdity is ineliminable — the analogy with scepticism

Absurdity is ineliminable: it mirrors epistemological scepticism — engaging at one level while doubting at another, without either dissolving the otherThe structure of the absurd is interestingly similar to the structure of epistemological scepticism. The sceptic can produce arguments that none of our beliefs about the external world are justified, because any grounds we might cite could be challenged further. And yet we cannot stop believing that there is a world around us. We cannot suspend belief merely because we have identified a gap in its justification. We engage with the world as believers while simultaneously being aware that this engagement may be groundless. The absurdity of life has the same structure. We cannot step outside our lives and choose not to take them seriously: even the person who believes nothing matters still gets out of bed, still prefers comfort to pain, still acts. The external perspective that reveals the groundlessness of our engagement cannot dissolve the engagement itself. This ineliminability is important: it means that the two responses commonly offered — Camus's defiant rebellion, which insists on living as though meaning could be wrested from meaninglessness, and the quietist resignation that counsels detachment — both miss the point. Neither can remove the clash. Defiance keeps the internal perspective while refusing the external. Resignation keeps the external while denying the internal. The absurd requires both at once.

On Camus and the failure of rebellion

Against Camus: rebellion is a form of bad faith that refuses the full implications of the view from outsideCamus recommended rebellion. Faced with the silence of the universe, faced with the gap between our demand for clarity and the world's refusal to provide it, we should defy: we should continue to live as though it mattered, should shake our fist at the uncaring universe, and should find our freedom precisely in refusing to be crushed by absurdity. This is a bracing position. But I think it is philosophically dishonest. Rebellion involves insisting, in the face of the objective view that our engagement is groundless, that it is not groundless — or at least acting as though it were not. This is to refuse the full implications of the external perspective. It is to occupy the internal perspective while merely gesturing at the external. The absurdity consists precisely in the fact that both perspectives are simultaneously valid and that neither can defeat the other. To rebel is to treat the internal perspective as though the external could be overcome by sheer will. But it cannot. The absurdity remains, whatever we decide to do about it. Rebellion does not dissolve the tension; it just performatively ignores the external side of it.

On ironic equanimity as the appropriate response

Ironic equanimity: holding both perspectives simultaneously with detachment rather than distress — the distinctively human response to an ineliminable conditionWhat response is left? I want to suggest something different from both rebellion and resignation: a kind of ironic detachment that does not pretend the absurdity can be dissolved. If there is no reason to take our lives seriously and no reason not to take them seriously — if the question cannot be answered from the outside — then there is no reason to be distressed by the absence of an answer. We can acknowledge the absurdity of our condition with the same equanimity we bring to other permanent features of our situation: the fact of death, the fact of ignorance, the fact that our subjective experience is necessarily limited. We do not collapse in despair at the fact of death; we acknowledge it and go on. The absurdity of life deserves a similar acknowledgement. It is, after all, one of the most distinctively human things about us. No other creature we know of can stand outside itself and see its own projects as arbitrary. This capacity — the capacity that generates the absurdity — is itself a mark of our peculiar kind of self-consciousness, the same capacity that grounds whatever richness and reflective depth human life has. To be distressed by absurdity is to miss what is valuable in the very feature of human life that generates it.

On sub specie aeternitatis — the view from outside and its proper limits

Sub specie aeternitatis: the external view is optional — we need not always occupy it, and its deliverances do not trump the internal viewThe external perspective that reveals absurdity — the view sub specie aeternitatis, the view from outside and above — is a perspective we can adopt but are not compelled to inhabit continuously. It is a possibility of our reflective nature, not its permanent condition. Most of the time, we engage with life from the inside: we pursue projects, form relationships, care about outcomes. The external view is available when we step back, but it is not the master perspective that adjudicates all the others. The deliverances of the view from nowhere do not automatically trump the deliverances of the view from here. To think they do is to privilege one perspective over another without justification. What I want to insist is not that the external view is wrong but that it is one view among several, and that the tension between it and the internal view is what generates the absurdity rather than resolving it. Nothing — no argument, no perspective, no attitude — resolves the tension. We live in it. And living in it well, with clear sight of its structure, is all that can reasonably be asked of us.

On absurdity as distinctively human — not a defect but a mark of our kind of consciousness

Absurdity is humanising: the capacity that generates it is the same capacity that makes human life distinctively rich — it is not a defect to be remediedI want to close with a point that the opposition will find uncomfortable: the absurdity of human life is not something that would be better eliminated. The capacity for self-transcendence — for stepping outside our immediate engagement and viewing it from a distance — is the same capacity that generates art, science, philosophy, moral reflection, and everything we most value in human civilisation. A creature without this capacity would not suffer the sense of absurdity — but it would also not have the depth of reflective engagement that makes human life what it is. The absurdity and the richness are the same thing, seen from different angles. To wish that human life were not absurd is to wish that we were incapable of the very reflectiveness that generates philosophy, literature, and the moral life. The appropriate response to absurdity is therefore not despair, not rebellion, not wishing it away, but acknowledging it as one of the most interesting and characteristic features of a consciousness like ours. We are the beings who can see their own lives as arbitrary — and that is one of the more remarkable things about us.

3.This ineliminability means that both common responses — Camusian rebellion (maintaining the internal while refusing the external) and quietist resignation (maintaining the external while denying the internal) — fail to resolve the clash; they merely suppress one of its two poles.
∴The absurdity of life is ineliminable — no attitude, decision, or philosophical position can dissolve the tension between the internal and external perspectives; the correct response must acknowledge both simultaneously.

∴Camusian rebellion is a form of philosophical bad faith — it occupies the internal perspective while merely gesturing at the external; it does not address the absurdity but rather turns away from one of its two constitutive poles.
V — Ironic equanimity: acknowledging both perspectives simultaneously without distress — the distinctively honest response
1.If neither the internal nor the external perspective can defeat the other, and if the tension between them is permanent, then there is no reason from outside to be distressed by the absence of justification that the external view reveals.
2.Ironic equanimity holds both perspectives simultaneously without collapsing into either — acknowledging the groundlessness seen from outside while continuing to engage from inside, without pretending this tension has been resolved.
3.This response is more honest than rebellion (which suppresses the external) and more engaged than resignation (which suppresses the internal) — it does justice to the full structure of absurdity.
∴Ironic equanimity is the philosophically correct response to absurdity — it holds both perspectives without distress, without bad faith, and without false resolution; it acknowledges what cannot be resolved and continues within it.

∴The external perspective does not defeat the internal — the absurdity is constituted by the equal validity of both; Kahane's and Wolf's claims that the external view can be contextualised or set aside miss the ineliminable force of the clash.

Mr President,

I join my colleague Nagel in his diagnosis: human life is genuinely absurd, and the responses that deny or evade this are philosophically dishonest. But I want to press the proposition's case further — to argue that Nagel's account of absurdity is, if anything, too mild. He ends with ironic equanimity, as though the absurd were an amusing peculiarity of the human condition, something to acknowledge with a wry smile and then set aside. I want to argue that it is considerably worse than this, and that the equanimity Nagel recommends is itself a product of the very psychological distortions that prevent us from seeing our situation clearly.

On the pessimist's diagnosis — the quality of human life honestly assessed

Pessimist diagnosis: human life contains far more suffering than we acknowledge — cognitive biases systematically distort our self-assessment upwardWe humans are extraordinarily bad at assessing the quality of our own lives. This is not a casual empirical claim; it is supported by a substantial body of psychological research. We have multiple biases that distort our assessment upward: a negativity bias that is somewhat offset by a hedonic adaptation that prevents us from fully appreciating how bad our bad experiences are in retrospect; a rose-tinted memory that colours the past more favourably than it was; an optimism bias that projects the future as better than it will be; a near-universal tendency to compare ourselves favourably with others even when the comparison is unwarranted. The result is that when people say their lives are going reasonably well — when they report life satisfaction scores above the midpoint — they are reporting not the actual quality of their lives but a systematically distorted upward estimate of it. The philosophical tradition of optimism about human life — from Aristotle's account of eudaimonia to Wolf's fitting fulfillment — depends on taking these self-reports at face value. I do not think we should. The absurdity of human life is considerably more distressing than the cheerful optimists allow.

On the asymmetry and what it reveals about existence

Asymmetry argument: the absence of pain is good even if not enjoyed; the absence of pleasure is not bad if not deprived — coming into existence is always a harmMy asymmetry argument begins with a simple observation about the value of absences. Consider the presence and absence of pain: the presence of pain is bad; the absence of pain is good, even when there is no one to enjoy the absence. Now consider pleasure: the presence of pleasure is good; but the absence of pleasure is only bad if there is someone who is deprived of it. This asymmetry, between the unconditional goodness of the absence of pain and the merely conditional badness of the absence of pleasure, generates a striking conclusion: never coming into existence is always better, or at worst equal to, coming into existence. For those who do not exist, there are no pains — which is good — but there are no pleasures either — which is not bad, since there is no one to be deprived. The net position for non-existence is never negative and often positive compared to existence. This is not nihilism: it is not the claim that nothing matters. It is the claim that bringing a being into a world of suffering is always a harm to that being, even if the being, once it exists, does not prefer non-existence. And this puts the absurdity of human life in a considerably starker light than Nagel's ironic equanimity acknowledges.

On Nagel's equanimity as a form of Pollyannaism

Equanimity is Pollyannaism: Nagel's ironic detachment is itself a cognitive defence mechanism — a way of not looking too hard at one's actual conditionNagel recommends ironic equanimity: an acknowledgement of absurdity accompanied by a kind of amused acceptance. He treats the external view — the view that our engagement is groundless — as a philosophical curiosity that deserves acknowledgement rather than distress. But I want to ask: why equanimity specifically? Why not distress? The honest answer, I think, is that equanimity is Nagel's preferred affect because it is the affect that allows one to continue functioning without taking the implications of the external view too seriously. It is a way of acknowledging the diagnosis without really accepting its full force. If one truly accepted the view from nowhere — truly accepted that one's engagement with life is groundless, that one's projects and values are arbitrary impositions that nothing endorses — then equanimity would be the psychologically implausible response. Distress, or at least a profound rethinking of how to spend one's time, would be more honest. Nagel's equanimity is, I submit, a sophisticated version of the optimism bias I described earlier: a way of absorbing the force of pessimism without fully surrendering to it.

On death and the incompleteness of Nagel's account

Death deepens absurdity: Nagel's account of mortality intensifies rather than resolves the absurdity — all investments lose their pointNagel has elsewhere written brilliantly about death — arguing that death is bad because it is a deprivation, because it forecloses the goods of continued existence. I want to press this into the debate about absurdity. If death is a deprivation — if it takes from us things that were worth having — then the absurdity of our condition is not merely that our engagement is groundless from an external perspective. It is that we build, invest, love, and strive, knowing that everything we build will be dismantled, that everyone we love will die or we will, and that the investment of a lifetime will leave no permanent trace. The external view sees the projects as groundless. The temporal view sees the investments as doomed. These are not the same thought, but they compound each other. A life that is both groundless and finite faces a kind of double absurdity that Nagel's account of ironic equanimity is not designed to address. Equanimity may be adequate to the groundlessness. It is less obviously adequate to the groundlessness combined with the knowledge that everything you care about will be destroyed.

On the comparison with Taylor's subjectivism — and where it goes wrong

Against Taylor: subjective engagement does not generate meaning — a Sisyphus who loves rolling rocks is not thereby living meaningfullyRichard Taylor argued that the absurdity of Sisyphus is dissolved if we imagine the gods implanting in him a desire to roll rocks — because then he will be doing exactly what he most wants to do, and this subjective engagement with his activity constitutes meaning. I find this deeply unconvincing. Sisyphus's love for rolling rocks, if it was implanted by the gods, is itself a kind of manipulation — it is not a response to something genuinely worth caring about, but a manufactured desire that happens to align with the activity available. More fundamentally: the idea that meaning is whatever you happen to care about most, regardless of whether that thing is worth caring about, collapses into a triviality. On Taylor's account, a being manipulated into loving its own degradation lives a meaningful life. The felt sense of engagement and fulfilment is not sufficient for meaning, because it can be entirely disconnected from anything that merits such engagement. This matters for our debate because the optimists who oppose the proposition often fall back on something like Taylor's subjectivism when their other arguments fail.

On the implications for how to live — what clarity about absurdity demands

Practical implications: honest assessment of absurdity and suffering demands more than equanimity — it demands rethinking procreation and possibly much elseWhat follows practically from an honest assessment of the human condition? I do not think the answer is suicide — Nagel is right that the external perspective does not compel any particular practical response, because the internal engagement persists regardless. But I do think it demands, at minimum, a radical rethinking of procreation. If coming into existence is always a harm — if we import into the world beings who will suffer, whose projects are groundless, whose lives will end in loss and dissolution — then we have no good reason to continue creating new lives, and strong reasons not to. This is not a conclusion for everyone to embrace immediately; it is a philosophical conclusion about the ethics of bringing people into existence. Nagel's equanimity is entirely focused on how to live once one exists. He says almost nothing about whether existence itself should be propagated. On this question — arguably the most practically important question to which the absurdity of life gives rise — his position is conspicuously silent.

On what Nagel gets right — and what the proposition adds to his account

Concession and extension: Nagel's diagnosis of absurdity is correct; the proposition adds that the absurdity is worse than he allows and that equanimity is an insufficient responseLet me be precise about what I accept and what I dispute. Nagel's account of the structure of absurdity is, I believe, substantially correct: it arises from the clash between our inescapable subjective engagement and our capacity to see that engagement as groundless from outside. His dismissal of the common arguments from cosmic scale is also correct. What I dispute is his conclusion that the correct response is ironic equanimity. This conclusion understates the gravity of the condition it diagnoses, relies on psychological defences that are themselves forms of the optimism bias he elsewhere identifies, and is silent on the most practically pressing implications of the view. The proposition's case is not that we should despair — despair would itself be a distortion, since the external view no more compels distress than it compels equanimity. The proposition's case is simply that we should look clearly at what our condition is, without the comfortable distortions of optimism, and see it for what it is: a condition more serious, more demanding, and less easily resolved than ironic detachment suggests.

Mr President,

Nagel's essay is one of the most beautifully written and genuinely interesting contributions to analytic philosophy of the twentieth century. I want to honour what is right in it before explaining why I think it is importantly wrong. What is right is the dismissal of the common arguments — from scale, from temporal remoteness — as inadequate. What is wrong is what Nagel puts in their place, and especially the response he recommends: ironic equanimity. My argument is that Nagel's account of absurdity depends on a particular picture of what meaning and significance would have to be — a picture I reject — and that once we replace it with a better picture, the absurdity largely dissolves.

On the view from nowhere as the wrong frame for meaning

Wrong frame: Nagel imports into the theory of meaning an unjustifiably demanding standard — the view from nowhere — that only makes sense given a particular metaethicsNagel's account of absurdity rests on a particular claim about what would be required for life to be genuinely meaningful: it would have to be endorsed not merely from within our projects and values, but from a standpoint entirely outside them — sub specie aeternitatis, in the view from nowhere. Only then would our seriousness be "justified" in the deep sense that would prevent absurdity. But I want to ask: why should this be the standard? Why should the meaningfulness of a life depend on its endorsability from a perspective that abstracts away from all human concerns and values? This standard, I submit, is borrowed from a particular metaethical picture in which genuine value must be value-from-nowhere — mind-independent, unconditional, grounded in something entirely outside the space of human projects. And this metaethical picture is highly contestable. If values can be genuine without being endorsed from nowhere — if the value of a loving relationship, or a scientific discovery, or a great work of art does not require cosmic grounding to be real — then Nagel's standard for what would prevent absurdity is too demanding, and the alleged absurdity that results from failing to meet it is an artefact of an unreasonably demanding metaethics.

On the fitting fulfillment view — meaning as subjective attraction meeting objective attractiveness

Fitting fulfillment: meaning arises when subjective engagement meets objectively worthy objects — neither purely subjective nor requiring cosmic groundingI want to offer a positive alternative to Nagel's picture. Meaning in life, I argue, arises when subjective attraction meets objective attractiveness — when a person is actively and lovingly engaged with projects, relationships, and activities that are genuinely worth caring about. This is not a purely subjective account: not just anything that a person cares about constitutes meaning, because caring deeply about something worthless does not make life meaningful. But it is also not the demanding account Nagel implicitly requires: meaningfulness does not require endorsement from nowhere. What is required is that the objects of engagement be genuinely worth caring about — that there be real value in what one cares about — and that one be genuinely engaged with them. Notice that this account allows meaning to be both real and grounded without being cosmically grounded. The value of friendship, of understanding, of creative achievement, of moral goodness does not depend for its reality on being endorsed from outside all human perspectives. It is real within the space of human concerns — and within that space, life can be genuinely meaningful.

On the Sisyphus case — why Taylor's subjectivism and Nagel's irony both miss the diagnosis

Sisyphus: neither pure desire (Taylor) nor ironic detachment (Nagel) correctly analyses the myth — meaningful activity requires both engagement and genuine worthThe myth of Sisyphus is the touchstone for this debate, and I want to show how my account handles it better than either Taylor's or Nagel's. Taylor says that Sisyphus with an implanted desire to roll rocks lives meaningfully — because he is doing what he wants. This is wrong: the manufactured desire is a response to nothing worth caring about, and manufactured satisfaction is not meaning. Nagel says that even if life is like Sisyphus's (in being groundlessly engaged), the correct response is irony. But this concedes too much: it treats the Sisyphean character of life as settled and asks only how to respond. My view resists the concession. Most human lives are not like Sisyphus's even without cosmic grounding — because most human lives involve engagement with things that are genuinely worth caring about: other people, genuine understanding, real beauty, moral achievement. These are not endorsable from nowhere — but they do not need to be. They are worth caring about within the human world, and engagement with them constitutes genuine meaning. The Sisyphean life is the life devoid of such engagement — and the response to that is not irony but the cultivation of genuine worthwhile engagement.

On why irony is a cold substitute for genuine engagement

Against ironic detachment: irony distances us from the very engagements that constitute meaning — it is a philosophical pose that undermines what it pretends to protectNagel's recommendation of ironic equanimity seems to me not merely philosophically wrong but practically damaging. Irony, in the relevant sense, is a form of detachment — a way of holding one's commitments and engagements at arm's length, acknowledging that they might not be ultimately justified, maintaining a kind of philosophical distance from one's own life. But genuine engagement — the loving, committed, wholehearted engagement that makes a life meaningful — is precisely what irony erodes. One cannot be wholeheartedly devoted to a person or a project while simultaneously maintaining ironic detachment from that devotion. The person who loves their child wholeheartedly does not simultaneously think, "but of course, from outside, this love is groundless." The scientist who is seized by a problem does not simultaneously maintain ironic distance from her curiosity. If Nagel is right that ironic equanimity is the appropriate response to life, then he has recommended an attitude that undermines the very engagements that, on my account, constitute its meaning. Irony is what you have when meaning is absent — it is not what you have instead of meaning.

On the distinction between meaning and happiness — and why absurdity misses both

Meaning is distinct from happiness and morality: Nagel conflates the absence of cosmic endorsement with meaninglessness, but these are differentThere is an important distinction that the proposition's account obscures: the distinction between meaning in life and happiness on the one hand, and between meaning and moral goodness on the other. Meaning is its own category. A life devoted to a great artistic project might be meaningful even if the artist is unhappy; a life of pleasant, comfortable selfishness might produce happiness without meaning. The proposition — following Nagel — conflates the absence of cosmic endorsement with meaninglessness. But these are different. It is possible that one's projects lack cosmic endorsement while being genuinely meaningful within the space of human values; and it is possible that one's life be cosmically endorsed (if cosmic endorsement makes sense) while being meaningless because it lacks genuine engagement with worthwhile things. Nagel's view that the external perspective reveals the groundlessness of our engagement — and that this constitutes absurdity — rests on the implicit assumption that groundlessness from nowhere is the same as meaninglessness. I deny this. Meaning has its own conditions, and they do not include cosmic grounding.

On Benatar's asymmetry — and where pessimism goes wrong

Against Benatar: the asymmetry argument depends on a contested axiology; life's genuine meaning is evidence against pessimism, not something to be explained awayBenatar's asymmetry argument is subtle, but I think it depends on a controversial axiology that should be resisted. The claim that the absence of pain is unconditionally good even if no one enjoys it is not obviously true — it presupposes that states of affairs can be good or bad independently of any subject who experiences them, which is a strong metaethical commitment that many reasonable philosophers reject. More importantly: the asymmetry argument, even if sound, establishes only that non-existence is never worse than existence — not that life is meaningless or that the absurdity Nagel diagnoses is as grave as Benatar claims. My fitting fulfillment view suggests that many lives are genuinely meaningful — that the people who live them are engaged with things genuinely worth caring about. The fact that such lives also involve suffering does not entail that they are not worth living. Benatar's pessimism asks us to accept a philosophical conclusion that is strongly counterintuitive and is derived from a contested asymmetry; the meaningful engagement of actual lives is at least as strong evidence against the conclusion as the asymmetry argument is for it.

On what the opposition concludes — the ordinary meaningful life

Ordinary meaningful life: most lives can be genuinely meaningful without cosmic grounding — the demand for cosmic significance is itself the source of the problem, not the condition of its solutionLet me state the opposition's positive conclusion. Most human lives — not extraordinary lives, not heroic lives, but ordinary human lives — contain genuine meaning: real friendship, real love, real understanding, real engagement with things genuinely worth caring about. The absurdity Nagel identifies is not a permanent inescapable condition of human life but an artefact of importing a too-demanding standard for what meaning would require. The demand for cosmic endorsement — the demand that our seriousness be justified from nowhere — is itself the problem. Once we drop that demand, and accept that meaning can be real without being cosmically grounded, the sense of absurdity largely dissolves. We are left not with ironic equanimity but with genuine engagement — not the detached awareness of the view from nowhere, but the wholehearted involvement that makes a life worth living. This is not naivety; it is the recognition that the demand for cosmic significance is itself a philosophical mistake, not a hard truth about the human condition.

My colleague Wolf has argued that Nagel's absurdity depends on an unreasonably demanding standard for meaning, and that once we replace it with a better account meaning becomes available without cosmic grounding. I want to approach the problem from a different direction, one that takes the question of cosmic significance more seriously than Wolf does, and argues that Nagel's worry — and the related worry about our cosmic insignificance — is more confused than either the proposition or my colleague on the opposition has recognised. I speak also for the recent work of Joshua Glasgow, whose 2024 book is the most thorough recent treatment of these questions.

On what cosmic significance actually means — separating the concept from the worry

Cosmic significance confused: the worry conflates metaethical concerns (are there objective values?) with a distinct relational concern (do we matter in the wider universe?)When people feel cosmically insignificant — when the vastness of the universe makes their lives seem pointless — what exactly are they worried about? Nagel speaks of our projects appearing arbitrary from the outside. Benatar invokes the scale of suffering relative to the scale of the cosmos. But it is important to separate two very different worries that are frequently confused. The first is a metaethical worry: there are no objective values, nothing genuinely matters, and we are therefore insignificant in the sense of being value-less. The second is a relational worry: there may well be objective values, but we have very little of them — we are cosmically minor contributors to whatever value the universe contains. These are entirely different concerns. The first is a form of nihilism: it denies that anything matters. The second is a form of modesty: it accepts that things matter but questions whether we contribute much to what matters. Most of the felt sense of cosmic insignificance, I argue, is the second worry, not the first. And the second worry has a very different structure from what the proposition supposes.

On whether we are actually cosmically insignificant — Kahane's surprising conclusion

Surprising conclusion: we might be of immense cosmic significance — intelligent observers may be vanishingly rare, making our existence cosmically centralHere is a surprising thought, and I think it is correct. Whether we are cosmically significant depends not just on our size relative to the universe but on what else is in the universe that matters. If the universe is otherwise empty of value — if we are the only beings who experience, reflect, create, and care — then we might be of immense cosmic significance, despite our small size. We might be, in a cosmic sense, everything that matters. The vastness of the universe does not automatically reduce our cosmic significance; it reduces our significance only if the rest of the universe is also full of things that matter. The probability that the universe contains other intelligent, conscious, reflective beings is empirically uncertain. The available evidence — the Fermi paradox, the apparent rarity of life, the extraordinary improbability of the evolutionary path to consciousness — does not rule out our being alone. And if we are alone, or nearly so, then the sense of cosmic insignificance is simply mistaken. The vast universe is not full of things mattering more than us — it may be full of nothing that matters at all, in which case we matter enormously by default. Nagel simply assumes the external perspective reveals our smallness. It may instead reveal our centrality.

On the significance impulse — why striving for cosmic importance is misguided

Glasgow's significance impulse: the quest for cosmic importance is both unrealistic and not in our interest — we cannot achieve it and we would not benefit from itJoshua Glasgow has argued, in his recent book, that the widespread human impulse to seek exceptional significance is doubly misguided. First, it is unrealistic: cosmic-grade importance — the kind of importance that would satisfy the demand Nagel implicitly makes — is simply not achievable by beings of our size, duration, and power. We cannot be that important. But second — and this is Glasgow's more striking claim — being cosmically significant would not even be good for us if we could achieve it. Being exceptionally important would not advance our well-being, our meaning in life, or any of our genuine interests. In fact, it would impose costs: greater pressure to make good choices, the burden of causal responsibility for more outcomes, the weight of being observed and evaluated by a wider world. Glasgow calls this the tyranny of value. The significance impulse — the widespread human desire to matter cosmically — is therefore not merely unrealistic but genuinely contrary to our interests. We should embrace our ordinariness rather than lamenting it.

On the sub specie aeternitatis perspective — why it is optional and why habitually occupying it is a mistake

The external view is optional: we need not occupy the view from nowhere, and habitually doing so is not wisdom but a distortion of the relationship between perspectivesThe proposition presents the external perspective — the view sub specie aeternitatis — as something we cannot avoid, something that is always available to undermine our internal engagement. I want to resist this framing. The external view is a perspective we can adopt, but there is no reason to think we must, or that doing so is epistemically mandatory, or that its deliverances reveal the truth about our situation in a way that the internal view does not. Consider an analogy. I can adopt the perspective of future generations and wonder whether my present concerns will matter to them. I can adopt the perspective of very different cultures and question whether my values have universal force. I can adopt the perspective of a scientist studying human behaviour and see my personal relationships as patterns of social reinforcement. None of these perspectives is wrong; each is illuminating. But none of them has epistemic priority over the first-person perspective from which I actually live. Nagel treats the external view as though it has a special authority — as though what is seen from nowhere is more real than what is seen from here. But this is a substantive and contestable epistemological claim, and there are strong reasons to reject it.

On the Fermi paradox and the empirical dimension of cosmic significance

Fermi paradox: the absence of detectable extraterrestrial intelligence is evidence that intelligent life is vanishingly rare — which makes us potentially cosmically centralI want to press an empirical point that is often ignored in this philosophical debate. Nagel writes as though the insignificance revealed by the external perspective is simply given — as though looking at the cosmos from outside makes our smallness obvious. But whether we are cosmically significant is an empirical question, and the empirical evidence is surprising. The Fermi paradox — the observation that the universe contains billions of potentially habitable planets, yet we have detected no signs of intelligent life beyond Earth — is evidence, if not conclusive, that intelligent life is extraordinarily rare. If conscious, rational, morally concerned beings are vanishingly rare in the universe, then we are not one small voice among billions of equally loud voices — we may be the only voice. The value of consciousness, understanding, love, and moral achievement, if it exists anywhere in the observable universe, may exist almost entirely in us. This does not prove cosmic significance. But it does show that the sense of cosmic insignificance — the feeling that the universe is vast and we are tiny and therefore unimportant — rests on an empirical assumption (that the rest of the universe is also full of important things) that is far from obviously true.

On the local significance that matters — community, history, and the human scale

Local significance: what matters is significance at the human scale — the people we affect, the history we shape, the communities we inhabit — not cosmic grade importanceEven if we accept that cosmic-grade significance is unattainable — even if Glasgow is right that the significance impulse is misguided — it does not follow that our lives lack significance altogether. There is a middle ground between cosmic centrality and complete insignificance. We matter to the people who love us. We shape communities that shape others. We contribute to histories that extend beyond our lifetimes. We produce knowledge, art, and moral insight that persist after we are gone. This local, human-scale significance is real — it satisfies any reasonable criterion for significance that is not artificially inflated to cosmic proportions. Nagel's account treats it as inadequate — as failing to satisfy the demands of the external view. But the demand of the external view is itself the mistake. The significant question is not whether we matter from nowhere but whether we matter to the beings and communities and projects that constitute our lives. And the answer to that question is very often yes.

On what the opposition concludes — embracing the ordinary over the cosmic

Embrace ordinariness: the correct response to cosmic insignificance is not Nagelian irony but liberation — freedom from the tyranny of value that exceptional significance would imposeLet me state the opposition's final conclusion on this side of the debate. The correct response to the question of our cosmic significance is not Nagel's ironic equanimity — which accepts the diagnosis of insignificance and counsels making peace with it — but a more positive attitude toward ordinary life. We are not cosmically insignificant in any well-defined sense; the worry is confused. If we cannot be cosmically significant, that is good news — cosmic significance would impose burdens we do not want. What we have — significance at the human scale, real value in our relationships, projects, and communities — is both real and sufficient. The demand for cosmic significance is itself a philosophical mistake, not an honest recognition of our predicament.
