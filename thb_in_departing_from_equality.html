The Cost-Based PrerogativeMichael Otsuka — “Prerogatives to Depart from Equality” (2010)
There is a genuine moral prerogative to give some priority to one's own projects and interests, grounded in the same considerations that ground agent-centered prerogatives generally: the unreasonable demandingness of requiring constant impartial sacrifice. But the prerogative is not unlimited. It is bounded by an asymmetry between the costs an agent may permissibly bear in declining to promote equality and the costs the agent may permissibly impose on others by that same choice. This bounded prerogative vindicates much of Cohen's critique of unjustified incentive-taking while still refuting his demand for an exceptionless egalitarian ethos.

Agent-Centered Prerogatives & the Basic StructureSamuel Scheffler — The Rejection of Consequentialism (1982/1994) & “Is the Basic Structure Basic?” (2005)
Any moral view that requires agents to treat their own projects, relationships, and commitments as having no more weight than the equivalent interests of strangers is unacceptably demanding — this is as true of egalitarianism as it is of consequentialism. The agent-centered prerogative permits agents to give some extra weight to their own concerns. Applied to distributive justice, this supports the Rawlsian view that principles of justice are primarily addressed to the basic structure of society; individual compliance with a just basic structure does not require the additional, separately-justified ethos that Cohen demands.

The Institutional Division of Moral LabourLiam Murphy — “Institutional Division of Moral Labour” (1998)
Cohen's "monism" — the view that the very same principles of distributive justice apply both to the design of institutions and to the personal conduct of individuals within them — should be rejected in favour of "dualism": principles of distributive justice are addressed to institutions, and individual conduct is governed by separate (though not unconstrained) principles. The Publicity ConditionAndrew Williams — “Incentives, Inequality, and Publicity” (1998)
Rawls's principles of justice must satisfy a publicity condition: they must be capable of forming the public, mutually acknowledged basis of social cooperation. Cohen's egalitarian ethos cannot satisfy this condition. Either the ethos is itself publicly avowed as part of the rules of the scheme — in which case it has simply become part of the basic structure, collapsing Cohen's distinction — or it operates as a private supplementary motivation that citizens are required to have but that cannot be built into the public terms of cooperation, which is precisely the kind of arrangement Rawlsian justice as fairness was designed to avoid.

Chair,

I want to start by taking Cohen's challenge as seriously as it deserves to be taken, because I think both sides of this debate have sometimes failed to do so. Cohen is right that there is something troubling about a society in which talented people demand higher pay as a condition of working hard, and in which this demand is then treated as an unalterable fact that justice must simply accommodate. But I do not think the correct response is the one Cohen draws from this observation — an exceptionless personal ethos of equality that would require individuals to dedicate themselves to maximising the position of the worst off whenever they are able to do so. I want to defend a prerogative that is real, that does genuine moral work, but that is considerably narrower than the prerogative the incentives argument actually requires.

i. the structure of agent-centered prerogatives

Agent-centered prerogative — the prerogative permits an agent to give extra weight to their own interests, but the literature on prerogatives (Kagan, Scheffler) distinguishes the cost an agent bears to themselves from the cost an agent imposes on others; the prerogative is much more secure for the former than the latterThe literature on agent-centered prerogatives, going back to Scheffler's original formulation, is primarily about a particular kind of case: cases in which an agent could produce a better impartial outcome only at considerable cost to themselves — their own projects, relationships, and life plans. The intuitive force of the prerogative in these cases is that it would be unreasonably demanding to require the agent to bear this cost. But Shelly Kagan, in his critical examination of prerogatives, drew an important distinction that the literature on distributive justice has not always carried over: there is a difference between an agent declining to bear a cost themselves, and an agent's choice imposing a cost on someone else. The prerogative not to sacrifice oneself for the impartial good is far more secure than any supposed prerogative to impose costs on others for one's own benefit. When we apply this to the incentives debate, the question becomes: is the inequality-generating choice a case of a talented person declining to bear a cost (continuing in a less demanding occupation, working fewer hours, retaining more leisure) or a case of a talented person extracting a transfer from others (demanding higher pay for the same effort, as a condition of contributing it at all)? These are different cases, and the prerogative speaks to them very differently.

ii. what the cost-based prerogative permits

What's permitted — the prerogative permits a talented person to choose a less demanding life, accepting lower output and lower personal income; it does not permit demanding higher pay for the same effort as a precondition of contributing it, because the latter is not a matter of the agent bearing a cost but of the agent imposing oneOn the cost-based account, the prerogative permits Cohen's "Jules" — the talented person who chooses a quieter life, more time with family, less remunerative but more fulfilling work — to make that choice, even though a more demanding, more productive life would have generated more resources for redistribution to the worst off. Jules is declining to bear a cost (the cost of a more demanding life) for himself, and the prerogative protects this. What the prerogative does not permit is the doctor or executive who says: "I will perform the same surgery, the same hours, the same effort — but only if you pay me significantly more than the egalitarian baseline, because otherwise I will simply withhold this effort." This is not a case of declining to bear a cost. It is a case of making the badly-off bear a cost — in the form of either reduced redistribution or induced inequality — as the price of the talented person's continued cooperation at the same level of effort they were already capable of and, ex hypothesi, willing to provide. This is precisely the case Cohen is right to find troubling. My account vindicates that judgement while still preserving a genuine prerogative.

iii. why this is not simply cohen's view

Not Cohen's view — Cohen's ethos, on its most natural reading, would require talented individuals to maximise their contribution to the worst off whenever the marginal cost to themselves is outweighed by the marginal benefit to others; the cost-based prerogative denies this, protecting the Jules-type choice that an unrestricted ethos would condemnIt might seem that I have simply restated Cohen's position. I have not. Cohen's ethos, on the most natural reading of "Rescuing Justice and Equality," requires that individuals be disposed to act, in their economic choices, in ways that would maximise the position of the worst off, constrained only by what Cohen calls the "personal prerogative" — which he leaves underspecified but treats as a relatively narrow exception. On this reading, Jules's choice of a quieter life is itself questionable: if Jules could, with moderate additional effort, generate substantial benefits for the worst off, an exceptionless or near-exceptionless egalitarian ethos would press hard against his choosing not to. My account inverts the relative weight: the prerogative not to bear costs oneself is robust and general — it covers the wide range of personal choices about how to live one's life, including choices that forgo opportunities to benefit others. What is narrow is the permission to impose costs on others for one's own benefit. Cohen's ethos has this backwards: it treats the failure to maximise contribution as the central case requiring justification, and treats incentive-demanding as a special case of the same phenomenon. I treat these as categorically different, and this is what licenses a "robust" prerogative without licensing exploitative incentive demands.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Kagan's distinction: declining to bear a cost oneself is categorically different from imposing a cost on others; the prerogative tracks this distinction
1.Agent-centered prerogatives are most securely grounded in cases where an agent declines to sacrifice their own interests for an impartially better outcome — the demandingness objection to consequentialism.
2.Kagan's distinction: declining to bear a cost oneself (agent-favouring) is different from a choice that imposes a cost on someone else (agent-imposing). The prerogative is far more secure for the former.
3.Incentive-demanding by the talented is an agent-imposing choice: it makes the badly-off bear a cost (via reduced redistribution or induced inequality) as the price of cooperation the talented person was already capable of providing.
∴The prerogative tracks Kagan's distinction: it robustly protects choices that decline personal sacrifice (agent-favouring) but does not extend to choices that impose costs on the badly-off as a condition of cooperation (agent-imposing).
Argument II — The prerogative protects Jules's choice of a quieter life but not the demand for incentive pay for unchanged effort
1.Jules choosing a less demanding, less remunerative life is declining to bear the cost of a more demanding life. This is agent-favouring and is protected by the prerogative.
2.A talented person demanding higher pay for the same effort, as a precondition of providing it, is not declining a cost to themselves — it is extracting a transfer, imposing the cost of reduced redistribution on the badly-off.
3.The cost-based prerogative draws this line precisely: agent-favouring choices are protected; agent-imposing demands are not, regardless of how they are dressed up in incentive-compatible institutional language.
∴The prerogative permits Jules-type choices but not incentive-extraction for unchanged effort; this vindicates Cohen's central intuition about the troubling case while preserving a genuine prerogative for the much wider class of personal life-choices.
Argument III — The cost-based account inverts Cohen's relative weighting: the general permission is to decline sacrifice; the narrow exception is incentive-extraction
1.Cohen's ethos treats failure to maximise contribution to the worst off as the case requiring justification, with the “personal prerogative” as a narrow, underspecified exception.
2.The cost-based account treats declining-to-maximise (agent-favouring choices, including Jules's) as the general case protected by a robust prerogative, and treats incentive-extraction (agent-imposing) as the narrow excluded case.
3.This inversion is what makes the prerogative “robust” without collapsing into licence for exploitative incentive demands: the scope of what is protected and what is excluded is determined by the agent-favouring/agent-imposing distinction, not by an underspecified residual category.
∴The cost-based prerogative is not a restatement of Cohen's view: it generally protects personal life-choices (including those an unrestricted ethos would press against) while excluding only the specific incentive-extraction cases Cohen is right to find troubling.

Chair,

I want to return to first principles, because I think the debate over Cohen's ethos has sometimes proceeded as though the question of demandingness, which I raised in a quite different context thirty years ago, has no bearing on distributive justice. It has every bearing. The argument I made against consequentialism applies, with the relevant modifications, against any view — egalitarian or otherwise — that requires agents to treat the promotion of an impartial value as having unconditional priority over their own projects and commitments.

“The deepest and most powerful intuitive basis for the agent-centered prerogative is the independence and importance of the personal point of view. Each person has reason to give special weight to his own projects and commitments, even when this means failing to maximise the impartial good.” — Scheffler, The Rejection of Consequentialism
i. the demandingness argument applies to egalitarianism too

Demandingness — Cohen's ethos would require individuals to treat the position of the worst-off as a constant, near-overriding consideration governing their economic choices; this is exactly the kind of demand the agent-centered prerogative was developed to resist, regardless of whether the impartial value in question is aggregate welfare or distributive equalityThe original argument against consequentialism was not an argument specifically against maximising aggregate welfare. It was an argument against any moral view that requires the promotion of an impartial value to take unconditional priority over the personal point of view — over an agent's own projects, relationships, and life plans. Cohen's egalitarian ethos has exactly this structure. It asks individuals to treat the position of the worst off as a standing consideration that governs their economic choices — their choice of career, their willingness to relocate, the hours they work, the pay they accept — such that any departure from what would maximise the position of the worst off requires justification. This is the demandingness objection in egalitarian dress. The fact that the impartial value at stake is distributive equality rather than aggregate welfare does not change the structure of the objection. If it was too demanding to require everyone to be a constant utility-maximiser for the collective good, it is too demanding to require everyone to be a constant equality-maximiser for the worst off.

ii. is the basic structure basic? the division of responsibility

Basic structure — Rawls's restriction of the principles of justice to the basic structure is not an arbitrary limitation but reflects a considered view about where the demands of justice are most appropriately addressed: to the rules of the game, not to the strategies players adopt within rules that are themselves justIn my paper "Is the Basic Structure Basic?" I defended, against Cohen, the Rawlsian view that the principles of justice are primarily addressed to the basic structure of society — its major political, economic, and social institutions — rather than directly to the conduct of individuals within a just basic structure. This is not an arbitrary restriction designed to let the talented off the hook. It reflects a considered view about the proper relationship between rules and conduct within rules. If the rules of an economic system are themselves just — if the basic structure satisfies the difference principle — then an individual's participation in that system, including their response to the incentives the system presents, does not itself require a separate, independently-justified ethos. The rules already embody the egalitarian constraint; individual choices within the rules are governed by ordinary considerations of personal life, constrained by the rules themselves (one cannot steal, defraud, or otherwise violate the rules) but not requiring the additional motivational layer Cohen demands.

iii. responding to the charge of complicity

Complicity — Cohen's deepest charge is that talented individuals who demand incentive pay are complicit in perpetuating an unjust level of inequality even within an otherwise just basic structure; Scheffler responds that this complicity charge proves too much, since it would apply to virtually all economic participation within any institutional systemCohen's deepest charge is not merely that the ethos would be desirable but that its absence renders the talented complicit in the inequality their incentive-demands sustain — that they cannot point to the justice of the basic structure to excuse choices that, but for their own attitudes, would not need to be accommodated by that structure in the first place. I want to note how far-reaching this complicity charge is, if accepted. Virtually every economically consequential choice an individual makes within a market economy — what career to pursue, what wage to accept, what prices to charge, what to buy — has some effect, however marginal, on the overall distribution of resources, and therefore on the position of the worst off relative to some alternative pattern of choices. If complicity in suboptimal distributions is sufficient to trigger the demands of an egalitarian ethos, then the ethos applies to essentially all economic conduct, not merely to the dramatic incentive-demanding cases that animate Cohen's examples. This is precisely the totalising demandingness that the agent-centered prerogative was developed to resist. The basic-structure restriction is not a way of carving out an exception for the talented; it is the only way of preventing the demands of distributive justice from becoming coextensive with the whole of practical life.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The demandingness objection to consequentialism applies, structurally unchanged, to Cohen's egalitarian ethos
1.The original prerogative argument targets any view requiring an impartial value to take unconditional priority over the personal point of view — not specifically aggregate welfare.
2.Cohen's ethos requires individuals to treat the position of the worst off as a standing consideration governing career, location, hours, and pay choices — an impartial value taking priority over personal life-plans.
3.The structure of the demandingness objection is unchanged by substituting “equality for the worst off” for “aggregate welfare” as the impartial value. If the objection succeeded against utilitarianism, it succeeds against the ethos.
∴The demandingness objection applies to Cohen's ethos with the same force it applied to consequentialism; an agent-centered prerogative against the ethos is at least as well-motivated as the prerogative against consequentialist demands.
Argument II — The basic structure restriction reflects the proper division between just rules and conduct within rules, not an arbitrary carve-out
1.If the basic structure is just — if it satisfies the difference principle — the egalitarian constraint is already embodied in the rules of the system.
2.Individual conduct within just rules is governed by the rules themselves (prohibiting theft, fraud, etc.) and by ordinary considerations of personal life, not by an additional independently-justified ethos duplicating the rules' function.
3.This division of labour between rules and conduct-within-rules is the standard way we think about justice in other institutional contexts (e.g., a fair game does not require players to additionally constrain their strategies by the game's fairness).
∴The basic structure restriction is principled, not ad hoc: it reflects the proper allocation of the demands of justice to institutional rules rather than duplicating those demands at the level of individual conduct within just rules.
Argument III — Cohen's complicity charge, if accepted, makes the ethos coextensive with all economically consequential conduct — the totalising result the prerogative exists to prevent
1.Cohen's complicity charge: talented individuals cannot appeal to the justice of the basic structure to excuse incentive-demanding choices that affect the position of the worst off.
2.But virtually every economically consequential choice (career, wages accepted, prices charged, purchases made) affects the position of the worst off relative to some alternative. If complicity triggers the ethos, the ethos applies to essentially all economic conduct.
3.This totalising scope is precisely the demandingness the agent-centered prerogative was developed to resist. The basic-structure restriction prevents the demands of justice from becoming coextensive with practical life as a whole.
∴Cohen's complicity charge generalises beyond the incentive-demanding cases that motivate it to virtually all economic conduct; the basic-structure restriction is necessary to prevent this totalising result, not a special exemption for the talented.

Chair,

I want to set out the structural choice that I think underlies this entire debate, because I believe Cohen's position depends on a specific answer to this structural question that he does not adequately defend. The question is: do the principles of distributive justice constitute a single set of principles that apply uniformly to the design of institutions and to the conduct of individuals — what I call monism — or do they constitute two distinct sets of principles, one for institutions and one (less demanding, differently grounded) for individuals — what I call dualism?

i. monism and its costs

Monism's costs — Cohen's monism requires that the same fundamental principle (e.g., maximise the position of the worst off, subject to a prerogative) governs both the design of the basic structure and the choices of every individual within it; this generates a compliance problem absent from dualist viewsCohen's view is monist: the fundamental principles of distributive justice — for him, broadly, principles requiring that inequalities be to the advantage of the worst off — apply both to the design of social institutions and, subject to a prerogative, to the choices of individuals operating within those institutions. I want to draw out a cost of monism that is distinct from the demandingness objection Scheffler has pressed: the compliance problem. A principle addressed to the basic structure can be satisfied by the basic structure regardless of whether any particular individual, considered separately, is disposed to comply with its underlying rationale — the structure can be designed so that self-interested behaviour within it produces just outcomes (this is, after all, one of the classical justifications for market mechanisms). A principle addressed to individuals, by contrast, can only be satisfied by widespread individual compliance. If the ethos is not widely shared — and Cohen does not claim it currently is — then an individual who adopts it bears real costs (in the form of forgone income, forgone bargaining position) without thereby making the overall distribution significantly more equal, because the overall pattern is determined by the aggregate of everyone's choices, most of whom are not following the ethos. Monism asks individuals to bear costs whose distributive payoff depends on a level of compliance the theory does not predict will obtain.

ii. the unworkability of comprehensive ethos-application

Unworkability — applying an egalitarian ethos to the billions of individual economic transactions that occur daily would require a level of constant moral calculation about distributive effects that is not merely demanding but practically impossible to implement coherentlySet aside compliance for a moment and consider the sheer practical structure of what the ethos demands. Every individual, in every economically consequential choice — what job to take, what wage to negotiate, what to charge for one's services, where to invest, what to buy — would need to assess the distributive implications of that choice relative to the available alternatives, and constrain their choice accordingly, subject only to the (on Cohen's account, narrow) personal prerogative. This is not merely demanding in the sense of requiring sacrifice; it is epistemically and practically unworkable. Individuals do not have, and could not feasibly acquire, the information needed to assess the marginal distributive effect of their economic choices relative to the relevant counterfactual baseline. The basic structure, by contrast, is precisely the kind of large-scale, designed system that can be evaluated and adjusted — through tax policy, transfer programmes, regulation — on the basis of aggregate distributive data that no individual could replicate in their personal decision-making. Dualism is not merely less demanding; it allocates the task of securing distributive justice to the level of social organisation that is actually capable of performing it.

iii. dualism is not laissez-faire

Not laissez-faire — dualism does not deny that individual conduct is subject to any moral constraints; it denies that individual conduct is subject to the specific principles of distributive justice that govern institutional design; individuals remain bound by ordinary moral duties, including duties not to subvert just institutionsI want to forestall a misreading of dualism as a laissez-faire view that places individual economic conduct entirely beyond moral assessment. This is not the view. Dualism holds that the specific principles of distributive justice — the principles that determine what counts as a just pattern of holdings across society — are addressed to the basic structure. It does not hold that individual conduct is morally unconstrained. Individuals remain subject to ordinary moral duties: duties not to defraud, not to exploit market power illegitimately, not to subvert the basic structure's ability to perform its distributive function (for instance, by evading taxes that are part of the very mechanism by which the difference principle is to be realised). What dualism denies is the additional, specifically distributive duty Cohen proposes — the duty to govern one's economic choices by their effect on the overall pattern of distribution, over and above compliance with the rules (including the tax rules) of a just basic structure. This is a substantive but bounded set of constraints, not the absence of constraint.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Monism generates a compliance problem: individual sacrifice under the ethos does not translate into more equal outcomes absent widespread compliance
1.A principle addressed to the basic structure can be satisfied regardless of individuals' underlying dispositions; a principle addressed to individuals can only be satisfied by widespread individual compliance.
2.Cohen does not claim the ethos is currently widely shared. An individual who adopts it bears real costs (forgone income, bargaining position) while the aggregate distribution is determined mainly by the choices of non-compliant others.
3.Monism therefore asks individuals to bear costs whose distributive payoff depends on a compliance condition the theory itself does not predict will be satisfied.
∴Monism's compliance problem means individual ethos-adoption, under realistic conditions of partial compliance, imposes costs on compliant individuals without proportionately advancing the egalitarian goal — an additional cost of monism beyond demandingness.
Argument II — Comprehensive ethos-application is epistemically and practically unworkable; the basic structure is the level of organisation capable of performing the distributive task
1.The ethos requires assessing the distributive implications, relative to counterfactual alternatives, of every economically consequential personal choice — an epistemic burden no individual can feasibly meet.
2.The basic structure can be evaluated and adjusted using aggregate distributive data (tax records, transfer programme outcomes) that is available at the institutional level but not replicable in individual decision-making.
3.Allocating the distributive task to the level of organisation that can actually perform it — the basic structure — is not merely less demanding than the ethos; it is the only level at which the task is performable at all.
∴Dualism allocates responsibility for distributive justice to the level of social organisation — the basic structure — that has the informational and coordinating capacity to discharge it; the ethos asks individuals to perform a task they structurally cannot perform.
Argument III — Dualism retains substantive moral constraints on individual conduct; it denies only the specific distributive duty Cohen proposes
1.Dualism holds that individuals are bound by ordinary moral duties: not to defraud, not to exploit market power illegitimately, not to subvert the basic structure's distributive function (e.g., through tax evasion).
2.What dualism denies is a further, specifically distributive duty: that individuals govern their economic choices by the choices' effect on the overall pattern of holdings, beyond compliance with the rules of a just basic structure.
3.This is a principled distinction between general moral constraints (retained) and the specific apparatus of distributive justice (addressed to institutions), not an absence of constraint on individual conduct.
∴Dualism is not laissez-faire: it retains substantial moral constraints on individual economic conduct while denying the specific, additional distributive duty that Cohen's monism requires.

Chair,

I want to press an objection to Cohen's ethos that is, I think, more fundamental than the demandingness objection or the division-of-labour objection, because it concerns whether the ethos could function as a principle of justice at all, given what Rawls — and Cohen, in other contexts — take principles of justice to require: that they be capable of forming the public basis of social cooperation among free and equal citizens.

“A conception of justice cannot be deduced from self-evident premises... Its justification is a matter of the mutual support of many considerations, of everything fitting together into one coherent view... it can be publicly recognised as the basis of social cooperation.” — Rawls, paraphrased by Williams in defence of the publicity condition
i. the publicity condition and its rawlsian basis

Publicity — Rawls's principles of justice must be ones that can be publicly known and acknowledged by citizens as the shared basis of their cooperative scheme; this is not an incidental feature but central to the idea of a "well-ordered society" and to the stability and legitimacy of the principles themselvesRawls's conception of a well-ordered society is one regulated by a public conception of justice: a conception that everyone accepts, and knows that everyone else accepts, and that is the publicly recognised basis of social cooperation. This publicity condition is not an afterthought; it is central to what makes a conception of justice fit to govern the basic structure of a society of free and equal citizens, as opposed to a modus vivendi that happens to be stable for contingent reasons. A principle that could not be publicly avowed — that depends for its operation on citizens not fully understanding or acknowledging it — cannot serve this role. Now consider Cohen's ethos. The ethos requires that individuals constrain their economic choices — their wage demands, career choices, and so on — according to the principle that they should not seek to benefit from inequalities that are not necessary to benefit the worst off. I want to ask: can this requirement be publicly avowed as part of the basis of social cooperation, in the way Rawls's two principles can?

ii. the dilemma: collapse or privacy

The dilemma — if the ethos is publicly avowed as a rule everyone must follow, it becomes part of the basic structure (a known, generally-applicable rule), collapsing Cohen's institutions/individuals distinction; if it is not publicly avowed in this way, it operates as a private supplementary motivation that the public conception of justice cannot itself specify or rely uponHere is the dilemma. Suppose the ethos is publicly avowed: everyone knows that everyone is supposed to constrain their wage demands and career choices according to the egalitarian principle. But a publicly known, generally applicable rule governing economic conduct, which everyone is expected to follow and which can be appealed to in assessing whether someone's conduct is justifiable, is not a "personal ethos" operating outside the basic structure — it is a social norm, and social norms of this kind are precisely the sort of thing that, on a Rawlsian view, get incorporated into the basic structure as part of the background culture and expectations against which institutions operate. In that case, Cohen's distinction between the basic structure and the ethos that supplements it has collapsed: the ethos has simply become part of the basic structure, confirming rather than refuting the basic-structure-restriction view. Alternatively, suppose the ethos is not publicly avowed in this way — it is a private motivation that some citizens have and others do not, not built into the shared, mutually acknowledged terms of cooperation. But then it cannot be a principle of justice in the Rawlsian sense at all: it is, at best, a personal virtue that some citizens display and others lack, not part of what citizens are entitled to expect of one another as a matter of justice. Either horn of this dilemma is fatal to Cohen's central claim that the ethos is a requirement of justice that operates as a necessary supplement to just institutions.

iii. the interpersonal test reconsidered

Interpersonal test — Cohen's interpersonal test asks whether a justification for inequality could be offered by the advantaged to the disadvantaged in good faith; but the test, properly applied, is a test of institutional justifications, and a just basic structure already passes it; the test cannot additionally be applied to private motivational states without presupposing the very ethos it's meant to establishCohen's interpersonal test asks whether the talented person could justify their incentive-demanding conduct to the worst-off, face to face, as something the worst-off could not reasonably reject. I want to suggest that this test, properly understood, is a test of the justifiability of institutional arrangements — of whether the rules of the scheme, including the incentive structures they create, are ones the worst-off could reasonably accept given the alternatives. A just basic structure, by hypothesis, passes this test: its rules, including whatever incentive structures it contains, are part of an arrangement that the worst-off could not reasonably reject, because the alternative arrangements would leave them worse off still. Cohen wants to apply the test a second time, to the private motivations of the talented individuals operating within this already-justified structure — asking whether their personal willingness to respond to the incentives the structure offers is itself something they could justify. But this second application presupposes that there is an independent standard — the ethos — against which private motivations are to be assessed, prior to and independent of the justified institutional rules. That is precisely what is in question. The interpersonal test cannot be used to establish the ethos without circularity, because the test's application to private motivation already assumes the ethos as the standard of assessment.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The publicity condition is central to Rawlsian justice: principles must be capable of forming the publicly acknowledged basis of cooperation
1.A well-ordered society is regulated by a public conception of justice: everyone accepts it, knows others accept it, and it is the publicly recognised basis of cooperation.
2.This publicity condition is constitutive of what makes a conception of justice fit to govern a society of free and equal citizens, distinguishing it from a merely contingent modus vivendi.
3.Any candidate principle of justice, including Cohen's ethos, must be assessed against whether it can satisfy this publicity condition.
∴The publicity condition is a legitimate and central test for any candidate principle of distributive justice; Cohen's ethos must be shown to satisfy it.
Argument II — The ethos faces a dilemma: public avowal collapses it into the basic structure; private operation disqualifies it as a principle of justice
1.If the ethos is publicly avowed as a generally applicable rule, it becomes a social norm incorporated into the background culture and basic structure — collapsing Cohen's distinction between institutions and the supplementary ethos.
2.If the ethos is not publicly avowed in this way, it is a private motivational state some citizens have and others lack — a personal virtue, not part of the shared terms of cooperation citizens are entitled to expect of one another as a matter of justice.
3.Either horn defeats Cohen's claim that the ethos is a requirement of justice operating as a necessary supplement to (and distinct from) just institutions.
∴The publicity dilemma is fatal to Cohen's central claim: the ethos either collapses into the basic structure (vindicating the basic-structure restriction) or fails to qualify as a principle of justice at all.
Argument III — The interpersonal test, properly applied, is a test of institutions; its second application to private motivation is circular
1.The interpersonal test asks whether a justification for inequality could be offered to the worst-off as something they could not reasonably reject. A just basic structure, by hypothesis, passes this test at the institutional level.
2.Cohen applies the test a second time to private motivations within the already-justified structure, asking whether individuals' willingness to respond to its incentives is itself justifiable.
3.This second application presupposes an independent standard (the ethos) for assessing private motivation, prior to and independent of the justified institutional rules — which is exactly what the test was supposed to establish. The application is circular.
∴The interpersonal test cannot be used to establish the ethos without circularity; its legitimate application is to institutional arrangements, which a just basic structure already satisfies.
The Egalitarian Ethos & the Incentives ArgumentG.A. Cohen — If You're an Egalitarian, How Come You're So Rich? (2000) & Rescuing Justice and Equality (2008)
A society regulated only by just institutions, whose members nevertheless feel free to extract whatever the institutions will permit through self-interested bargaining, is not a just society in the full sense — it is, at best, a society whose injustice is contained by institutional design rather than absent. I want to begin with the argument that I think the proposition has not adequately confronted: the incentives argument for inequality, examined on its own terms, undermines itself. This is not a claim that incentive-based inequality is merely regrettable. It is a claim that the standard justification for it — an justification that proceeds via the difference principle — is internally unstable in a way that points directly to the need for an ethos.

“If you're an egalitarian, how come you're so rich? ... The intelligent and effective pursuit of self-interest by people of unusual talent results in distributions that are unfair, even if those distributions also satisfy the difference principle, because the talented could have produced the same benefit to the worst off without the inequality, had they so chosen.” — Cohen, paraphrased
i. the incentives argument and its self-undermining structure

Self-undermining incentives argument — the difference principle permits inequality only if it benefits the worst off; the incentives argument says talented people need higher pay to be motivated to produce that benefit; but if the talented people affirm the difference principle, their own willingness to work hard should not depend on the incentive — the need for the incentive is generated by their own insufficient commitment to the principle that is supposed to justify itThe difference principle permits inequality when, and only when, the inequality works to the benefit of the worst off — typically because it is necessary to induce the talented to be maximally productive, with the surplus then available for redistribution. Now ask: why is the incentive necessary? The standard answer is that, absent the incentive, talented individuals would not produce at the higher level — they would withhold effort. But if these same individuals affirm the difference principle as the correct principle of justice for their society — if they are, as the proposition's framework requires, citizens who endorse a just basic structure — then their own willingness to produce at the higher level should not depend on receiving a larger share for themselves. The fact that it does depend on this is not a fixed feature of the world that the difference principle must accommodate; it is a fact about these particular individuals' priorities, which is itself assessable from the standpoint of the very principle they claim to affirm. A society in which the difference principle is satisfied only because talented people demand more than equality would give them, despite professing to endorse the difference principle, is a society whose distributive outcome rests on a performative contradiction: the principle is satisfied only because those subject to it do not, in their economic conduct, actually act on it.

ii. the jules example and the limits of the prerogative

Jules — Otsuka's "agent-favouring/agent-imposing" distinction does not track the cases that matter; many real incentive-demanding choices can be redescribed as "declining to bear the cost" of working harder for the same pay, exactly like Jules; the distinction collapses under redescriptionOtsuka offers a distinction between Jules, who chooses a quieter life and is protected by the prerogative, and the surgeon who demands higher pay for unchanged effort, who is not. I want to press on how stable this distinction is. Consider a surgeon who currently works fifty hours a week and is asked, by the institutions of a more strictly egalitarian society, to continue working fifty hours a week for a smaller share of the resulting surplus — a share closer to what an unskilled worker receives. The surgeon declines, and continues working fifty hours a week only if compensated at the higher rate; under the more egalitarian arrangement, the surgeon reduces their hours to, say, thirty. Is this surgeon Jules — declining to bear the cost of a more demanding life — or the incentive-extractor Otsuka wants to exclude? The case can be redescribed either way. From the surgeon's perspective, reducing hours in response to reduced pay is "declining to bear a cost" exactly as Jules does. From the perspective of the worst-off, the effect is identical to the incentive-extractor case: the same person, capable of the same output, produces less unless paid more. The agent-favouring/agent-imposing distinction does not track a difference in the underlying choice; it tracks only how the choice is described, and any incentive-demanding choice can be redescribed in agent-favouring terms by reframing the baseline.

iii. the personal is not insulated from the political

Personal/political — Rawls's own framework, in extending principles of justice to "background institutions" and acknowledging that the family and personal choices affect the realization of fair equality of opportunity over time, already implies that personal conduct cannot be cleanly insulated from the demands of justice; the basic structure restriction is in tension with Rawls's own broader commitmentsThe basic-structure restriction that Scheffler and Murphy defend is in tension with commitments that Rawls himself, and the broader liberal egalitarian tradition, have acknowledged elsewhere. Consider the family. Rawls came to acknowledge, in response to feminist critiques, that the internal life of the family — a paradigmatically "personal" domain — has profound effects on the realisation of fair equality of opportunity across generations, because it is within families that children's life prospects are shaped. If the internal arrangements of family life — how labour, encouragement, and resources are distributed between parents and among children — are relevant to whether fair equality of opportunity is realised, then the basic-structure restriction cannot mean that "personal" choices are simply outside the purview of justice. The restriction, taken seriously, would have to exempt family life along with economic incentive-taking, which even committed Rawlsians have not been willing to do. Once this exemption is denied for the family, the principled basis for granting it for economic conduct becomes very hard to identify.

I want to address what I take to be the most common objection to the egalitarian ethos — an objection that has not been explicitly stated by this proposition bench but that lurks behind several of their arguments, particularly Murphy's claim about the practical unworkability of ethos-governed conduct. The objection is that an egalitarian ethos, if widely adopted, would undermine the price signals and incentive responses on which an efficient market economy depends — that you cannot have both market efficiency and an egalitarian personal ethos. I want to show that this objection rests on a confusion.

i. separating the allocative and distributive functions of income

Allocative vs distributive — the function of differential wages in signalling where labour is needed (allocative function) is separable from the question of who ultimately retains the resulting income (distributive function); a society can operate the former via market prices while handling the latter via comprehensive redistribution motivated by an internalised ethosDifferential wages perform two distinct functions in a market economy. The allocative function: by paying more for scarce or highly productive labour, the market signals where labour should be directed, and individuals respond to these signals by acquiring skills, choosing occupations, and allocating their effort in ways that tend to be socially productive. The distributive function: the wages an individual receives constitute their income, determining their command over resources. These two functions are conceptually and institutionally separable. A society could operate a fully market-based wage system — with all the allocative signalling this involves — while subjecting the resulting income to comprehensive taxation and redistributing the proceeds according to an egalitarian formula. Individuals would still respond to wage differentials in choosing their occupations and effort levels (because doing so is part of how the economy as a whole produces more, which is socially valuable and which the ethos itself endorses), but their personal disposable income would not track those differentials. The objection that the ethos is incompatible with market efficiency conflates the allocative and distributive functions of wages, which a well-designed institutional scheme can separate.

ii. the role of the ethos in such a scheme

Role of ethos — in the scheme just described, the egalitarian ethos operates not as a constraint on individual market behaviour (which remains incentive-responsive) but as the citizens' shared commitment to the redistributive institutions that capture and redistribute market income; this is publicly avowable and does not face Williams's dilemmaWhere does the egalitarian ethos fit into this scheme? Not as a constraint on individuals' market behaviour — in my scheme, individuals respond to wage signals just as they would in an unregulated market, choosing occupations and effort levels accordingly. The ethos operates at a different level: it is citizens' shared commitment to maintaining and supporting the redistributive institutions — the comprehensive taxation and transfer system — that capture market-generated income and redistribute it according to egalitarian principles. This is a commitment to a set of institutions, fully publicly avowable, and one that citizens can affirm and act on collectively (through their political choices, their willingness to pay taxes without evasion, their support for redistributive policy) without it requiring any departure from incentive-responsive behaviour in their market conduct. I think this directly addresses Williams's publicity dilemma: the ethos, on this construction, is precisely the kind of thing that can be publicly avowed as part of the basis of cooperation — a shared commitment to redistributive institutions — without thereby collapsing into a rule governing market behaviour, because it is not a rule governing market behaviour at all.

iii. why this still vindicates cohen against the proposition

Vindicating Cohen — even on this market-preserving construction, an ethos-supported redistributive scheme achieves more equality than a scheme that relies on the political bargaining power of the talented to determine redistribution levels; the difference is precisely whether citizens' political and economic behaviour is informed by an egalitarian commitmentThe proposition might respond that my scheme simply collapses into ordinary redistributive taxation, which the basic structure can implement without any ethos at all — Murphy's view exactly. But this misses what the ethos contributes. A society's redistributive institutions are not fixed independently of citizens' attitudes; they are themselves the object of ongoing political contestation, and their generosity depends substantially on whether citizens — including talented, high-earning citizens — support or resist redistributive taxation. A society whose talented citizens are egalitarian-minded will tend, through ordinary democratic processes, toward more redistributive institutions than a society whose talented citizens resist redistribution as an infringement on their earnings. The ethos, on my construction, operates precisely at this level: not as a constraint on individual market behaviour, but as a disposition that shapes citizens' political behaviour regarding the institutions that determine the ultimate distribution. This is exactly the kind of personal commitment to equality that Cohen is calling for, located at exactly the level where it can do real distributive work without the practical problems Murphy identifies.

I want to focus on the structural premise that underlies Murphy's dualism and Scheffler's basic-structure restriction: the assumption that there is a stable, principled distinction between "the basic structure" — the object of distributive justice — and "individual conduct within the basic structure," which is governed by separate and less demanding considerations. I want to argue that this distinction, while it can be drawn for some purposes, cannot bear the weight the proposition places on it, because the basic structure is not independent of the individual conduct it is supposed to be distinguished from.

i. the basic structure is constituted by individual choices

Constitution — the basic structure (labour markets, family structures, patterns of social cooperation) is not a fixed background against which individual choices occur; it is itself the aggregate product of countless individual choices, continuously reproduced and altered by those choices; the rules/conduct distinction presupposes a separation that does not existWhat is the basic structure, concretely? It includes the labour market — but the labour market is not a freestanding entity; it is constituted by the aggregate of individuals' decisions about what work to seek, what wages to accept, what skills to acquire. It includes the structure of the family — but family structures are constituted by individuals' decisions about partnership, the division of domestic labour, and the allocation of resources and encouragement among children. It includes social norms about gender, race, and merit that shape who has access to which opportunities — but these norms are themselves constituted, reproduced, and gradually altered by the cumulative pattern of individual attitudes and choices. Murphy's dualism treats the basic structure as a fixed institutional background, designed independently of and prior to individual conduct, such that individual conduct can be assessed by reference to whether it complies with the rules of an already-given structure. But the structure is not given independently of conduct in this way. It is continuously constituted by the very conduct that dualism wants to locate "outside" it. The rules-of-the-game / strategies-within-the-game analogy that Scheffler and Murphy rely on breaks down when the players' strategies are themselves what determine, over time, what the rules are.

ii. against the totalising fear

Against totalising — Murphy and Scheffler fear that if individual conduct is subject to justice, the demands of justice become coextensive with all of practical life; but this fear assumes justice-assessment requires constant impartial calculation; a more plausible view is that justice provides a background constraint that is normally not salient, becoming relevant primarily where conduct has substantial and foreseeable distributive effectsScheffler and Murphy both warn that if individual conduct is subject to the demands of distributive justice, those demands become coextensive with the whole of practical life — every purchase, every career choice, every wage negotiation becomes a site of moral assessment requiring constant calculation of distributive effects. I think this fear is overstated, and it rests on a picture of what it would mean for justice to apply to personal conduct that no one actually needs to hold. Consider an analogy with other moral constraints. The fact that I am subject to a duty not to cause gratuitous harm to others does not mean that I must constantly calculate the harm-effects of my every action; the duty is a background constraint that is normally not salient and becomes relevant when my conduct has substantial and foreseeable harmful effects. Similarly, the demands of distributive justice on personal conduct need not require constant calculation; they become salient primarily in cases where an individual's conduct has substantial, foreseeable, and significant effects on the position of the worst off — precisely the kind of case the incentives debate is about. The vast majority of personal economic choices — what to have for dinner, which film to watch, which neighbourhood to live in within one's means — simply do not have this character, and the extension of justice to personal conduct does not implicate them.

iii. what follows for the incentives debate

Implications — once the basic structure / individual conduct distinction is seen as unstable, and the totalising fear is addressed by restricting the demands of justice to conduct with substantial distributive effects, the incentives-demanding choices of the talented fall squarely within what justice can assess, without this implying that justice governs all of practical lifePutting these two points together: the incentives-demanding choices that animate Cohen's critique — the choices of talented individuals about whether to work at full capacity only in exchange for above-egalitarian compensation — are precisely the kind of choice that (a) cannot be insulated from justice by appeal to a basic-structure/individual-conduct distinction that does not hold up, because these choices are part of what constitutes the labour-market institutions in question, and (b) fall within the restricted class of personal conduct that has substantial, foreseeable distributive effects, and so are appropriately subject to assessment by principles of distributive justice without this implying that justice governs the whole of practical life. The proposition's fear of totalising demandingness is addressed not by walling off all personal conduct from justice, but by recognising that justice's demands on personal conduct are themselves restricted to conduct of this kind — which includes, centrally, the incentive-demanding conduct of the talented, but does not include the vast range of everyday choices the proposition worries about.

I have listened to this debate with a certain sense of déjà vu, because I think both sides are conducting an extraordinarily sophisticated argument about a question that is, at bottom, the wrong question. The question this debate has been asking is: under what conditions, and to what extent, may individuals depart from a pattern of distribution that would otherwise track some egalitarian ideal — equality of outcome, equality adjusted for choice, or whatever the relevant ideal is — without thereby acting unjustly? I want to suggest that this question, however it is answered, is not the question a defensible theory of equality should be asking in the first place.

“The proper negative aim of egalitarian justice is not to eliminate the impact of brute luck from human affairs, but to end oppression... Citizens of a democratic state... need to stand in relations of equality to others.” — Anderson, “What Is the Point of Equality?”
i. the luck-egalitarian framework shared by both sides

Shared framework — both Cohen's ethos and the prerogative theorists' cost-based accounts operate within a luck-egalitarian framework that asks whether a given distributive outcome reflects choice (legitimate) or unchosen circumstance/preference (illegitimate, requiring correction); the entire apparatus of "costs," "prerogatives," and "incentive-extraction" is an attempt to sort cases within this frameworkNotice what both sides of this debate share. Cohen's ethos asks whether the talented person's demand for higher pay reflects a preference that the person should, as an egalitarian, have chosen not to act on — an illegitimate "expensive taste" for higher consumption that the worst-off should not have to subsidise. Otsuka's cost-based prerogative asks whether a given departure from equality reflects the agent declining to bear a cost (legitimate, a matter of the agent's own choices about their own life) or imposing a cost on others (illegitimate, since the badly-off should not bear costs arising from others' choices). Both frameworks are operating within what I have called luck egalitarianism: the view that distributive justice is fundamentally about ensuring that people's holdings track their choices rather than their luck, such that holdings resulting from "bad" choices (in Cohen's framework, choices reflecting insufficiently egalitarian preferences; in Otsuka's, choices that impose costs on others) are illegitimate, while holdings resulting from "good" choices (genuinely costly personal sacrifices, in Otsuka's framework) are legitimate. The entire elaborate apparatus of this debate — ethos, prerogative, cost-imposition, agent-favouring versus agent-imposing — is an attempt to draw this choice/luck line correctly.

ii. why this framework is the wrong one

Wrong framework — the luck-egalitarian project of sorting holdings into legitimate (choice-based) and illegitimate (luck-based or imposing costs) categories generates harsh and demeaning implications when applied to the badly-off, and is not what citizens fundamentally owe each other; what citizens owe each other is standing as equals, not a correct sorting of their holdings by the choice/luck criterionThe luck-egalitarian framework, applied consistently, generates implications that I and others have argued are deeply troubling when we consider not the talented but the badly-off. If the fundamental question is whether someone's disadvantage reflects their own choices or their luck, then a person whose poverty results, even in part, from choices that could be characterised as imprudent — choices about education, employment, family formation — becomes, to that extent, someone whose disadvantage the rest of us have less reason to address. This is not a hypothetical worry; it is the actual rhetorical structure of arguments used to limit welfare provision to the "deserving poor." My point is not that this debate's participants intend any such implication — they plainly do not. My point is that the underlying framework, which sorts people's situations into choice-based (their own responsibility) and luck-based (society's responsibility) categories, is not a sound basis for a theory of justice, regardless of which side of the incentives debate one takes within it. What citizens fundamentally owe one another is not a correct application of the choice/luck distinction to their respective holdings. It is standing as equals: relations free from domination, in which no one is positioned to demand subservience or deference from anyone else, and in which the terms of social cooperation do not consign anyone to an inferior status.

iii. what relational equality says about this debate

Relational verdict — relational equality does not require the granular policing of talented individuals' economic motivations that Cohen's ethos demands, nor does it require the elaborate cost-based taxonomy the prerogative theorists develop; what it requires is that economic arrangements not produce relations of dependence, exclusion, or status hierarchy, which is a question about the overall shape of social and economic life, not about the moral quality of any individual's wage demandsWhat does relational equality say about the incentives debate, then? It says that the question of whether a particular surgeon's wage demand reflects a legitimate cost or an illegitimate extraction is, in itself, not the right question to be asking. The right questions are: do the economic arrangements of this society produce relations of dependence, in which some citizens are positioned to dominate or exploit others? Do they produce a status hierarchy, in which some occupations or income levels confer a kind of social standing that allows their holders to treat others as inferiors? Do they exclude anyone from full participation in social and economic life on terms of equality? These are questions about the overall shape of a society's institutions and culture — about whether, for instance, vast income disparities translate into disparities of political influence, social respect, or access to basic goods that undermine equal citizenship — not questions about whether any individual's personal wage negotiation passes a test of motivational purity. A society could have considerable income inequality, including inequality sustained by something like incentive payments, and still be relationally egalitarian, if that inequality does not translate into the forms of social hierarchy and domination that relational equality is concerned with. Conversely, a society could achieve Cohen's ethos perfectly — every citizen motivated by exactly the right egalitarian disposition in their economic choices — and still fail at relational equality, if its institutions nonetheless produce hierarchies of status and dependence through other means. The entire debate this house has been having, while intellectually serious, has been conducted on a terrain that is, I believe, adjacent to but ultimately distinct from where the real questions of equality lie.
