Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I want to begin with a number, because the argument I am about to make is ultimately an argument about scale, and scale is best apprehended through numbers. The observable universe contains somewhere between 100 and 400 billion stars in our galaxy alone, and there are estimated to be two trillion galaxies. The amount of matter, energy, and — crucially — computronium available for the construction of minds, experiences, and civilisations is so vast as to make every event that has occurred in human history look, from a certain vantage point, like a rounding error. If Earth-originating intelligence has any value at all — any positive moral weight — then the question of whether that intelligence persists and expands through the cosmos, or extinguishes itself on a single small planet orbiting an unremarkable star, is the most important question that has ever been asked.

On the concept of existential risk

Existential risk: extinction forecloses all future value — the stakes are asymmetric and irreversibleI define an existential risk as one that threatens either the extinction of Earth-originating intelligent life, or the permanent and drastic curtailment of its potential. What makes existential risks categorically different from ordinary catastrophes — however terrible — is not just their severity but their irreversibility. A pandemic that kills a hundred million people is an unspeakable tragedy; but humanity recovers, rebuilds, and the potential of the future remains intact. An extinction event forecloses everything: every life that might have been lived, every discovery that might have been made, every moment of joy and understanding — gone permanently. The asymmetry between recoverable and unrecoverable catastrophes is what makes existential risk the most important category in ethics and policy. Against the loss of everything, ordinary trade-offs look different.

On astronomical waste and the stakes of inaction

Astronomical waste: every moment confined to Earth, the potential of a vast universe goes unrealised — the cost is staggeringIn my 2003 paper "Astronomical Waste," I argued that the universe contains resources sufficient to support, by conservative estimates, 10 to the 38th power person-years of experience — or vastly more, if we consider non-biological substrates. Every second that passes without this potential being realised represents a loss of value that dwarfs anything that has ever occurred on Earth. I am not claiming we have an obligation to colonise the cosmos at any cost to present people. I am claiming that existential risk reduction — including space colonisation as insurance against single-point-of-failure catastrophes on Earth — deserves to be weighted very heavily in our moral calculus, because the stakes on the downside are incomparably vast. The failure to take seriously the possibility of extinction, when the costs of modest precautionary action are so small relative to the potential losses, is not prudence. It is a failure of moral imagination on a cosmic scale.

On the single-planet bottleneck

Single-planet bottleneck: all intelligence on one world is a catastrophic single point of failureHere is the argument in its starkest form. Earth is a single planet, vulnerable to asteroid impact, supervolcanic eruption, pandemic, climate catastrophe, nuclear war, and engineered biological agents. The probability that any one of these will cause human extinction or permanent civilisational collapse in any given century is, by most careful estimates, somewhere between one and twenty percent. Over a thousand years, the compound probability of at least one such catastrophe is deeply alarming. The only structural solution is diversification. Spreading intelligence across multiple worlds, multiple star systems, makes its extinction incomparably less likely. A civilisation spanning multiple planets cannot be destroyed by any event affecting only one of them. The argument for space colonisation as existential insurance requires only that we place positive value on the continuation of intelligence and the preservation of choice.

On the value of potential people and the far future

Future generations: the potential people of the far future have enormous moral weight if we take consequentialism seriouslyThe opposition will press the claims of present people against hypothetical future ones. The people who might exist in a flourishing multi-planetary civilisation a thousand years hence are not yet real. But their potential reality — the probability-weighted expectation of their existence — has enormous moral weight on any consequentialist framework. If we act in ways that reduce the probability of their existence — by failing to establish the preconditions for long-run civilisational survival — we harm them. I am not claiming that present people's needs are unimportant. I am claiming that the moral calculus of decisions affecting the very long run must include the interests of the potentially vast numbers of future beings whose existence depends on those decisions. To ignore them entirely — because they do not yet exist — is temporal parochialism no more defensible than spatial parochialism about distant present strangers.

On the simulation argument and the rarity of intelligence

Simulation argument: under any horn of the trilemma, technological civilisation is rare and precious — worth protectingMy simulation hypothesis holds that one of three propositions must be true: virtually all civilisations go extinct before technological maturity; virtually all mature civilisations choose not to run ancestor simulations; or we are almost certainly in a simulation. I do not press the third horn here. Under the first two horns, the rarity and preciousness of technological civilisation is confirmed — making its survival an achievement worth protecting at great cost. If civilisations like ours typically go extinct, that is precisely the scenario making existential risk reduction most urgent. If mature civilisations have a distinctive character, then reaching maturity without extinction is a profound achievement. Either way, the argument for taking civilisational survival seriously is reinforced rather than weakened.

On proportionality and the ethics of cosmic ambition

Proportionality: the moral obligation is commensurate — small costs, enormous existential protectionI am not claiming space colonisation should be prioritised over all other human concerns. My argument is structural and proportionate: we should invest, at a level commensurate with the existential stakes, in developing the capacity to establish sustainable human presence beyond Earth. The costs — the fraction of global GDP required for a serious multi-decade space programme — are genuinely small relative to the potential gains. The argument does not require sacrificing present generations for future ones; it requires that we not neglect an insurance policy that costs relatively little and protects against a downside of literally incalculable proportions. This is not cosmic utopianism. It is the most basic form of rational risk management.

On the moral weight of intelligence itself

Intelligence has intrinsic value: its emergence from cosmic evolution is among the greatest achievements in the history of the universeLet me close with a philosophical point that underlies the entire argument. The emergence of intelligence — of beings capable of consciousness, understanding, moral reasoning, and wonder — out of the blind processes of cosmic evolution is, on almost any plausible axiology, an achievement of enormous positive significance. It represents the universe becoming aware of itself. The extinction of Earth-originating intelligence would not merely be a tragedy for the beings involved. It would be a loss of something that took billions of years to produce and might not be reproduced anywhere in the observable universe within any timeframe that matters. Preserving and expanding that intelligence — beginning with the modest step of establishing it on more than one world — is not merely an option. It is a moral imperative of the highest order.

Thank you.

3.Modest investment in existential risk reduction and space colonisation provides insurance against this loss at a fraction of the potential cost.
∴The moral calculus strongly favours investment in space colonisation — the potential downside of extinction against the cost of prevention makes inaction an ethically indefensible gamble.

3.Distributing intelligence across multiple worlds makes its total extinction incomparably less likely — no single event can then destroy everything.

3.The speculative possibility that intelligence is rare reinforces rather than weakens the imperative to preserve and expand it.

2.The existential protection this provides has value that is, in expectation, incomparably greater than the cost.
3.No significant trade-off with present consumption is required at the scale the argument demands — this is prudent insurance, not heroic sacrifice.
∴The proportionality argument shows the obligation to invest in space colonisation is not demanding — the insurance premium is small, the protection enormous; this is among the most cost-effective moral investments available.
VII — Intelligence has intrinsic value: its extinction would be a cosmic loss, not merely a human one
1.The emergence of consciousness and moral understanding from blind evolutionary processes represents an achievement of positive cosmic significance — the universe becoming aware of itself.
2.This achievement took billions of years to produce and may not be reproduced anywhere in the observable universe if Earth-originating intelligence is lost.
3.Preserving and expanding this intelligence — beginning with multi-planetary presence — is not merely a prudential concern but a moral imperative grounded in the intrinsic value of mind.
∴The intrinsic value of intelligence grounds the strongest version of the colonisation imperative — we are not merely preserving ourselves but preserving the only known site of consciousness and moral care in the cosmos.

Mr President,

I want to speak not from the mathematics of existential risk but from something harder to quantify — the long view of our species' history, the perspective that comes from understanding what Earth is and what it is not. We are a young species on a small world. We have been technological for an eyeblink. And yet we have, in that eyeblink, developed the capacity to destroy ourselves many times over, to alter the planet's climate, to reach — barely, tentatively — beyond our atmosphere. The question this House must answer is not whether space is appealing, or whether the science is interesting. The question is whether remaining on a single, fragile, contested world is a rational strategy for a species that wishes to persist.

On the pale blue dot

Pale Blue Dot: Earth's smallness and fragility, seen from outside, argues for both protecting and leaving itIn 1990, at my urging, the Voyager 1 spacecraft turned its camera toward home from a distance of about six billion kilometres. What it photographed was a point of light less than a pixel across, suspended in a sunbeam against the vast darkness of space. That is Earth. That is where every human being who has ever lived has lived — every king and peasant, every saint and sinner. Everything. On a mote of dust suspended in a sunbeam. What this image teaches us is not despair but urgency. The smallness of Earth means that everything we care about is concentrated in a single, deeply vulnerable location. Our planet has survived five mass extinctions we know of. It will be sterilised by the Sun in approximately five billion years. In the nearer term, it faces risks that our own activities have created and that we have not begun adequately to address. The cosmic perspective does not make Earth seem less precious. It makes the imperative to protect what Earth contains — and to give it a second home — feel more urgent than ever.

On the civilisational backup

Civilisational backup: if Earth is lost, everything is lost — a spacefaring civilisation has copies; this is the most basic prudential wisdomEvery librarian knows that a single copy of an irreplaceable text is a catastrophe waiting to happen. Every prudent organisation maintains backups of data it cannot afford to lose. Every species that has survived hundreds of millions of years has done so partly through geographic diversification — spreading its range so that no single environmental catastrophe can eliminate it entirely. We are the first species capable of consciously choosing civilisational diversification. We are the first species capable of deliberately creating a backup. And yet, uniquely among species with this capacity, we are considering whether to use it. The opposition will offer many reasons why space colonisation is complicated, expensive, ethically fraught, and potentially dangerous. All of these things may be true. They are also true of every great and necessary thing humanity has ever done. The question is not whether diversification is perfect. The question is whether the alternative — remaining forever on a single world, no backup, no margin of safety — is rational.

On the nature of the threats we face

Earth's vulnerability: the threats to Earth-based civilisation are real, diverse, and some are of our own makingLet me be concrete about what we face. Near-Earth asteroids of the kind that ended the reign of the non-avian dinosaurs exist in abundance; we have identified only a fraction, and our capacity to deflect them is in its infancy. Supervolcanic eruptions occur on geological timescales and could cause global cooling sufficient to collapse agriculture. We have built enough nuclear weapons to cause a nuclear winter killing most of the human population; we have not disarmed. We are actively destabilising the climate system that has supported civilisation for the past ten thousand years. We are developing biotechnologies that could, in the wrong hands or through accidents, produce pandemics of unprecedented lethality. I raise these threats because the honest scientific assessment of our situation leads directly to the conclusion that a one-planet species is a fragile species. Not probably doomed. But fragile in ways that can be addressed.

On the history of exploration and expansion

Exploration imperative: the history of life on Earth is a history of expansion — space is the next frontier, and this time the destination is emptyThe history of life on Earth is, in one way of reading it, a history of expansion into previously uninhabited environments. Life began in the oceans and colonised the land. It colonised the air. Homo sapiens spread from Africa to every continent on Earth within perhaps a hundred thousand years. We are, in our bones, an exploring and expanding species. The opposition will argue that this history should give us pause — that colonisation has often caused harm to existing inhabitants. I want to agree and draw a lesson. The lesson is not that expansion is always wrong. The lesson is that expansion should be undertaken thoughtfully, with respect for what exists — and that in the case of space, where there are no prior inhabitants to be displaced, the historical shadow of terrestrial colonialism does not fall in quite the same way. The ethical constraints are different when the destination is empty.

On the overview effect and our relationship with Earth

Overview effect: seeing Earth from space deepens commitment to it — colonisation makes us better stewards, not worseThere is an irony in the opposition's argument that I want to press. Every astronaut who has seen Earth from space has reported a profound shift in perspective — what the astronaut Ron White called the "overview effect." They describe Earth as unexpectedly small and fragile. They describe the borders and conflicts that seemed so important from the ground as invisible. They describe an overwhelming sense of the preciousness and uniqueness of Earth and of life. Far from making us indifferent to Earth, the experience of seeing it from outside tends to deepen our commitment to protecting it. The opposition imagines that investing in space means giving up on Earth. The experience of those who have actually left suggests the opposite: leaving Earth makes you understand what you are leaving, and why it must be preserved.

On the false dichotomy between Earth and space

False dichotomy: caring for Earth and colonising space are not in tension — they are the same impulse at different scalesThe opposition sets up a choice: spend resources on Earth's problems, or spend them on space. I reject this framing entirely. The scale of resources required to establish a sustainable human presence on Mars or the Moon is a fraction of what humanity spends on military budgets or fossil fuel subsidies. Moreover, the technologies developed in the pursuit of space colonisation — advances in energy, life support, closed-loop ecological systems, materials science — have direct applications on Earth. The opposition presents a false dichotomy between Earth and space. The reality is that becoming a spacefaring civilisation and solving our planetary problems are complementary projects, both expressions of the same commitment to human and civilisational flourishing. A species serious enough to go to space is serious enough to fix its home planet.

On the long perspective of cosmic time

Cosmic time: whether intelligence escapes its home planet is the most consequential question in the history of the universeLet me close where I began, with the long view. On the timescale of the cosmos, human civilisation has lasted an instant. The question of whether this civilisation remains on its home planet or extends itself into the wider universe is, from the perspective of cosmic time, the most consequential question that has been asked in the thirteen billion years since the Big Bang. We do not know whether intelligence has arisen elsewhere. We do not know whether, if it has, it has survived its own technological adolescence. We may be alone. We may be the only carriers of consciousness in the observable universe. If we are — and even if we are not — the question of whether we preserve and extend that consciousness, or allow it to be extinguished through shortsightedness and timidity, is one that future generations, if there are any, will judge us on with unmerciful clarity. I believe we are capable of the wisdom to choose survival. I hope we choose it.

Thank you.

Mr President,

The proposition has presented an argument that begins with numbers so large they dissolve ordinary moral reasoning, and concludes with an obligation so vast that it sweeps before it every concern about the real, present, and foreseeable consequences of the specific project it endorses. I want to argue that this is precisely what makes it dangerous. The existential risk argument for space colonisation sounds compelling in the abstract; but when you examine what space colonisation actually entails — what it does to the geopolitical structure of humanity, what weapons it places in orbit, what incentives it creates — the argument inverts. Space expansionism is not primarily an insurance policy against extinction. It is, in the medium term, one of the most significant accelerators of the very risks it claims to mitigate.

On the militarisation of space

Space militarisation: colonisation requires and accelerates weapons in space, increasing existential risk rather than reducing itThe proposition imagines space colonies as lifeboats — safe havens for humanity in the event of earthly catastrophe. But space colonies, in any realistic scenario, are not merely humanitarian refuges. They are geopolitical entities with interests, and they require enormous defensive and offensive capabilities. A spacefaring civilisation is one in which nuclear weapons, kinetic bombardment platforms, and directed energy weapons are distributed across cislunar space and beyond. The physics of orbital mechanics makes space-based weapons inherently destabilising: objects in orbit have potential energy that, redirected toward Earth, represents extraordinary destructive capacity. The proposition tells us space colonisation reduces existential risk. I want to invert this: the specific path to space colonisation currently being pursued — competitive, nationally and corporately fragmented, driven by the same geopolitical rivalry that has characterised terrestrial great-power competition — dramatically increases the risk of the very catastrophic wars it is supposed to help us survive.

On the logic of the escape hatch

Escape hatch: if elites can exit to space, they lose the incentive to solve Earth's problems — colonisation undermines earthly political accountabilityConsider who, in any realistic scenario, would actually occupy Bostrom's multi-planetary civilisation in its early phases. Not the world's poor. Not the most vulnerable populations. Not the people whose interests most need representation in decisions about existential risk. The first off-world colonies would be populated by the wealthy, the technologically capable, and the geopolitically powerful. Here is the moral hazard: if those with the greatest resources and capabilities to address Earth's existential problems — climate change, nuclear proliferation, pandemic preparedness — can instead exit to a space colony, they have dramatically reduced incentives to solve those problems. The escape hatch does not make Earth safer. It makes Earth more dangerous, by allowing those most capable of improving it to opt out of the political and moral obligations that would otherwise compel their engagement. Bostrom's insurance policy is one that the rich buy for themselves and that the poor are left to pay for.

On the nature of existential risks and their actual drivers

Risk misdiagnosis: actual existential risks are anthropogenic and require political solutions, not technological escapeI want to challenge the proposition's implicit assumption that existential risks are primarily natural — events that happen to us — and that technology, including space colonisation, is the response. The actual probability-weighted existential risks we face are overwhelmingly anthropogenic: nuclear war, engineered pandemics, and runaway climate change. These risks are not addressed by having a colony on Mars. A nuclear war that devastates Earth does not spare a Mars colony; cut off from its supply chain, it dies too. What mitigates anthropogenic existential risks is political — effective international governance, arms control, non-proliferation, and cooperative global institutions. Space colonisation diverts resources, attention, and political energy from these actual risk-reduction strategies into a technological fix that does not address the political problem.

On the planetary case for Earth-centrism

Earth-centrism: Earth is the irreplaceable civilisational commons — it requires our undivided attention, not hedgingThe proposition describes Earth as a fragile mote of dust from which we should be anxious to escape. I want to propose a different description. Earth is a four-billion-year-old living system — the only one we know of in the universe — of extraordinary complexity, resilience, and value. It is not merely where we happen to live; it is the civilisational commons that sustains every human institution, every ecosystem, every cultural achievement. The correct response to understanding Earth's fragility is not to diversify away from it — as if it were a bad investment to be hedged — but to invest everything we have in protecting and sustaining it. The proposition's logic treats Earth as replaceable — one node in a distributed network that can be lost without catastrophe. I reject this framing entirely. Earth is not a node. It is the irreplaceable matrix of everything we value.

On the geopolitics of the space frontier

Space geopolitics: the frontier reproduces and amplifies terrestrial competition in a more dangerous environmentPolitical scientists and historians of technology know that new frontiers do not transcend the political conflicts of the societies that enter them — they reproduce and amplify them. The American frontier did not produce harmony; it produced dispossession and conflict. The Arctic and Antarctic have been sites of national competition and strategic rivalry. Cyberspace has not produced the borderless global village its enthusiasts predicted; it has become a theatre of espionage and great-power competition. There is no reason to expect space to be different. The proposition imagines a future of cooperative, cosmopolitan multi-planetary civilisation. The actual trajectory of space development — driven by US-China competition and billionaire entrepreneurs accountable to no democratic constituency — is producing a competitive, fragmented space order that reproduces the worst features of terrestrial geopolitics in a far more dangerous environment.

On the asymmetry of catastrophic capability in space

Orbital bombardment: space colonisation gives actors the ability to threaten Earth with kinetic bombardment — a novel catastrophic capabilityI want to be specific about a physical threat the proposition has not adequately addressed. Objects in low Earth orbit carry kinetic energy that, when directed, can produce explosions equivalent to nuclear weapons. The more developed a space-based civilisation becomes — the more infrastructure, the more mass in orbit — the more capability exists for catastrophic directed attacks on Earth. A Mars colony that develops the capacity to redirect asteroids or mass-driver projectiles toward Earth has a first-strike capability against the entire terrestrial population. This is not science fiction; it is orbital mechanics. The proposition asks us to accept that this capability, in the hands of whatever political entities come to control space infrastructure, will be used only for good. History gives us no reason for that confidence. The off-world actor with kinetic bombardment capability is the most powerful political actor that has ever existed — a structural feature of space colonisation that the proposition has not begun to address.

On why Bostrom's argument proves too much

Proves too much: the astronomical waste argument licenses any action that increases survival probability — including authoritarian global governanceLet me end with the most fundamental objection. Bostrom's astronomical waste argument is a species of expected-value reasoning that, applied consistently, proves far too much. If the potential future value of a multi-planetary civilisation is so astronomically large that virtually any present cost is justified to secure it, then the argument licenses not merely space colonisation but any action, however destructive of present rights and welfare, that increases the probability of civilisational survival even slightly. It would justify permanent authoritarian global governance to prevent wars. It would justify compulsory re-engineering of human psychology. It would justify sacrificing the present generation entirely for the sake of the vastly larger number of potential future people. Bostrom is aware of this implication and has tried to limit it; but the limitations are ad hoc. Once you grant the framework, the conclusion is hard to contain. The argument, in short, is not safe. It is one of the most dangerous tools in the philosophical toolkit — a justification machine that can override every constraint liberal political philosophy has developed over centuries.

III — Risk misdiagnosis: anthropogenic existential risks require political solutions, not technological escape
1.Probability-weighted existential risks are overwhelmingly anthropogenic — nuclear war, engineered pandemics, climate change — not natural events addressable by off-world colonies.
2.A Mars colony does not survive a nuclear war that devastates Earth — it is cut off from its supply chain and dies; off-world presence does not mitigate anthropogenic catastrophe at realistic scales.
3.What reduces anthropogenic existential risks is political — effective international governance, arms control, cooperative institutions — and space colonisation diverts resources from these actual solutions.
∴Space colonisation is a misdiagnosed solution to misidentified risks — actual existential risks require political not technological responses, and the colonisation project diverts resources from real solutions.
IV — Earth is the irreplaceable civilisational commons, not a node to be hedged or abandoned
1.Earth is a four-billion-year-old living system of extraordinary complexity — the irreplaceable matrix of every human institution, ecosystem, and cultural achievement.
2.The proposition treats Earth as one node in a distributed network — replaceable, hedgeable — but this misrepresents its moral and civilisational status.
3.The correct response to Earth's fragility is intensive investment in protecting and sustaining it, not diversifying away from it as if it were a depreciating asset.
∴Earth-centrism is the correct civilisational priority — Earth is not a node to be hedged but the irreplaceable commons that sustains everything; the proposition's network logic systematically undervalues what is most precious.
V — Space geopolitics: the frontier reproduces and amplifies terrestrial conflict in a more dangerous environment
1.Historical evidence shows that new frontiers reproduce, not transcend, the political conflicts of the societies that enter them — the American frontier, Arctic, and cyberspace all follow this pattern.
2.The actual trajectory of space development — US-China competition and unaccountable billionaire entrepreneurs — is producing a competitive, fragmented space order.
3.Reproducing great-power competition in an environment with novel catastrophic capabilities is more dangerous, not less, than terrestrial competition.
∴The space frontier will amplify geopolitical conflict in a more dangerous environment — the proposition's cosmopolitan vision of cooperative multi-planetary civilisation has no basis in the actual trajectory of space development.
VI — Orbital bombardment: space colonisation creates catastrophic new first-strike capabilities against Earth
1.Objects in orbit carry kinetic energy equivalent to nuclear weapons — a developed space colony has catastrophic directed-energy or kinetic-bombardment capability against Earth.
2.An off-world actor with this capability is the most powerful political entity that has ever existed — a structural feature of space colonisation, not a contingent risk.
3.History gives us no basis for confidence that this capability will be used only for benign purposes by whatever political entities control space infrastructure.
∴Orbital bombardment capability is a structural consequence of space colonisation representing a novel catastrophic existential risk — the proposition has not addressed this and cannot do so without fundamentally revising its account of the colonisation project.
VII — Proves too much: the astronomical waste framework licenses authoritarian overrides of every liberal constraint
1.The astronomical waste argument treats potential future value as so vast that virtually any present sacrifice is justified to secure it — expected-value reasoning with enormous leverage.
2.Applied consistently, this licenses permanent authoritarian global governance, compulsory human re-engineering, and any action that increases survival probability — however destructive of present rights.
3.Bostrom's attempts to limit the argument are ad hoc — once the framework is granted, the conclusion is very difficult to contain within liberal political philosophy.
∴The astronomical waste framework is philosophically dangerous — it is a justification machine that proves too much; its application to space colonisation should make us suspicious of the argument form, not just its conclusions.

Mr President,

My colleague Deudney has argued against space colonisation primarily on geopolitical and strategic grounds. I want to press a different and in some ways deeper objection: that the conceptual architecture of the space colonisation argument — the very way it understands humanity, Earth, other worlds, and the future — is structured by assumptions that are not universal human values but the specific, historically contingent, and in important ways harmful values of a particular tradition. The proposition presents its case as if it were speaking for all of humanity. I want to ask: whose humanity? Whose future? Whose cosmos?

On the colonial logic embedded in space discourse

Colonial logic: space colonisation discourse reproduces the conceptual structure of terrestrial colonialism, with the same hierarchies and assumptionsThe language of space colonisation is not innocent. When Sagan speaks of the "exploration imperative" and the historical expansion of humanity into "uninhabited" environments, he is reproducing a specific colonial narrative — one in which expansion is natural, progress is linear, and uninhabited land is land whose existing inhabitants have simply not been recognised as inhabiting it. The Americas were not uninhabited when Europeans arrived; they were populated by peoples whose occupation was not recognised because their modes of presence did not conform to European categories. When we describe Mars or the Moon as "uninhabited," we are making a similar category claim. It may be correct — we do not yet know whether there is or has been microbial life on Mars. But the certainty with which the proposition dismisses this possibility, and the speed with which it moves from "probably uninhabited" to "ours to colonise," should give us pause. The conceptual template through which the proposition sees the cosmos is the colonial frontier template: empty land, heroic settlers, manifest destiny. This template has a history, and the history is not clean.

On who speaks for humanity in space

Representation problem: the actors driving space colonisation are unaccountable to most of humanity — this is a project of the wealthy fewThe proposition speaks of "humanity" colonising space — a collective subject whose welfare it claims to represent. But look at who is actually doing the colonising. SpaceX is a private corporation controlled by one of the world's wealthiest individuals, accountable to no democratic constituency on Earth and operating under no international treaty regime adequate to govern its activities. Blue Origin is another. The Chinese space programme is a geopolitical project of the Chinese Communist Party. The NASA programme reflects the interests and priorities of the United States government. None of these actors represents "humanity." None has been authorised by the world's poorest, most vulnerable, and most affected populations to make decisions about what kind of civilisational future to build. The proposition presents space colonisation as a universal human project. It is, in its current form, a project of concentrated wealth and power — with all the risks of exclusion, exploitation, and dispossession that concentrated wealth and power historically produce.

On the theology of the cosmic frontier

Cosmic theology: space colonisation discourse has a quasi-religious structure — salvation, chosen people, transcendence — that deserves critical examinationI want to press a point that sounds strange but is important. The discourse of space colonisation has a recognisable quasi-religious structure. There is a fallen world — Earth, damaged, fragile, morally compromised. There is a salvation narrative — escape to a new world, transcendence of earthly limitations. There is a chosen people — the technologically capable, the entrepreneurially visionary. There is a promised land — the cosmos, infinite and available. Bostrom's argument, with its vast future populations and astronomical welfare calculations, is a secular eschatology — a story about the end of earthly history and the beginning of a cosmic afterlife for the species. I say this because the theological structure of a belief system is evidence about its cultural origins and hidden assumptions. The space colonisation argument is the product of a specific intellectual tradition — one that values transcendence over embeddedness, expansion over stewardship, the infinite future over the finite present — and we should be aware of that fact when evaluating its claims to universal validity.

On terraforming and the ethics of planetary transformation

Terraforming ethics: transforming another world to suit human needs raises profound ethical questions the proposition ignoresThe proposition speaks of establishing human presence on other worlds. The medium-term version of this — the one that would actually provide meaningful existential insurance — requires terraforming: the wholesale transformation of a planet's atmosphere, geology, and chemistry to support human life. Let us assume Mars is lifeless. Even then, Mars is not merely an empty container waiting to be filled with human civilisation. It is a world with its own geological history, its own chemistry, its own form of existence as a physical system. The project of terraforming it raises profound questions about the value of non-human nature. Do we have the right to destroy what exists on another world in order to replicate ourselves there? The proposition assumes the answer is yes, without argument. This assumption reproduces the same anthropocentrism that has driven environmental destruction on Earth: the view that the non-human natural world has value only instrumentally, as a resource for human purposes.

On the overview effect and its limits

Overview effect critique: the effect is real but limited — it has not prevented environmental destruction; it cannot substitute for structural changeSagan invokes the overview effect as evidence that space colonisation makes us better environmental stewards. Consider the evidence carefully. The overview effect is a genuine psychological phenomenon: astronauts do report profound shifts in perspective. But we have been flying humans to space for sixty-five years, and in that same period we have accelerated climate change, driven unprecedented rates of species extinction, and degraded Earth's ecosystems at a rate with no precedent in human history. The overview effect is real and beautiful. But it has not, in practice, been sufficient to change the structural incentives and political economies that are driving environmental destruction. Sagan's argument proves too much — it implies that space colonisation would make us more responsible. The evidence from the actual practice of spaceflight does not support this inference at the civilisational scale.

On alternative cosmologies and the limits of the Baconian tradition

Alternative cosmologies: space colonisation embeds one tradition's values as universal, ignoring others — this is intellectual imperialismThe proposition speaks in the name of humanity, but it speaks in the conceptual vocabulary of one specific tradition — the Baconian tradition of scientific knowledge as mastery, of nature as resource, of expansion as progress. This is not the only way human beings have understood their relationship to Earth and cosmos. Indigenous cosmologies from across the world understand humanity as embedded in, responsible to, and in reciprocal relationship with the natural world — not as its sovereign master. These cosmologies embody real wisdom about what sustainable habitation looks like, wisdom that the Western scientific tradition has systematically undervalued. The project of space colonisation, as currently conceived, extracts from these alternative cosmologies only what is useful to it and ignores the rest. It does not ask whether the relationship to Earth and cosmos that it embodies is the right one. It simply assumes it is, and calls it "humanity's" view. This is an intellectual imperialism that deserves to be named.

On what a genuinely inclusive space ethics would require

Inclusive space ethics: legitimate space expansion requires democratic governance, equitable terms, and genuine cosmological pluralismI want to close by saying what I am not arguing. I am not arguing that all space exploration is wrong, or that we should never go to space, or that the risks Bostrom and Sagan identify are imaginary. I am arguing that the space colonisation project as currently constituted — driven by concentrated wealth, reproducing colonial logic, embedded in the theology of the cosmic frontier, operating outside adequate democratic and international governance — is not "humanity's" project. It is a particular powerful faction's project, presented in universal language. A genuinely legitimate space programme would require democratic deliberation at the global level about goals, governance, and terms of off-world expansion. It would require genuine engagement with the full diversity of human cosmologies and values. It would require that the benefits and risks of space colonisation be shared equitably. Until those conditions are met, the claim that humanity has a moral obligation to colonise space is not a universal moral argument. It is the ideology of a specific group, dressed in universal language — one of the oldest and most recognisable moves in the history of power.
