Mr President,

Aristotle gave us the formulation from which all subsequent discussion of truth has departed: "To say of what is that it is, and of what is not that it is not, is true." It is a formulation of enormous compression, and almost everything in the philosophy of truth since consists in unpacking what "what is" means, and what the relation between saying and what is amounts to. The correspondence theory of truth is the sustained attempt to take Aristotle seriously — to insist that truth is a genuine, substantive relation between a truth-bearer and the world, and that this relation cannot be dissolved into anything epistemic, pragmatic, or merely formal.

"The proposition 'The cat is on the mat' is true not because it coheres with other propositions, not because believing it is useful, not because the word 'true' is a redundant device of endorsement. It is true because there is a state of affairs — a cat, a mat, and a particular spatial relation between them — and that state of affairs obtains. The truth of the proposition consists in this obtaining. Remove the cat, and the proposition becomes false. This is the fundamental insight that no deflationary theory can dissolve without falsifying our pre-theoretical understanding of what truth is."
i. the two-component structure of correspondence

Two components: truthmakers & correspondence relationKirkham distinguishes two components that any correspondence theory must provide: an account of the truthmakers — the worldly items that make propositions true — and an account of the correspondence relation — what it is for a proposition to correspond to its truthmaker. On the first question, the candidates are facts (Russell's structured complexes of objects and properties), states of affairs (Armstrong's preferred terminology, with the metaphysical advantage of encompassing both obtaining and non-obtaining possibilities), and situations (Barwise and Perry's partialist alternative). On the second, the candidates are correlation (a one-to-one mapping between propositions and facts) and isomorphism (the structural mirroring that the early Russell and Wittgenstein's Tractatus proposed, in which the logical form of the proposition matches the ontological form of the fact). The isomorphism account is philosophically richer and more demanding: it explains not merely that true propositions are paired with facts but that they represent facts in virtue of sharing a structure with them.

ii. Armstrong's truthmaker theory — the ontological grounding

Truthmaker theory — Armstrong; necessitation; grounding; not internal but externalD. M. Armstrong's development of the correspondence theory through truthmaker theory provides the most rigorous contemporary metaphysical formulation. The truthmaker principle states that for every contingent truth there must be some entity in the world — a truthmaker — whose existence necessitates the truth of the proposition. Truthmakers are not merely correlated with truths; they necessitate them: if the truthmaker exists, the proposition cannot but be true. For atomic propositions — "Socrates is wise" — the truthmaker is a state of affairs: the state of affairs of Socrates's being wise. For existential propositions — "There is at least one philosopher" — the truthmaker is any existing philosopher. For negative propositions — "There are no unicorns" — the truthmaker is more controversial: Armstrong postulates totality facts that necessitate the absence. The theory requires that facts be genuine constituents of the world, neither reducible to sets of objects-and-properties nor to the objects and properties themselves taken separately.

iii. Austin's performative account — truth and conventional correspondence

Austin — statement vs. utterance; demonstrative & descriptive conventions; not isomorphismJ. L. Austin's correspondence theory differs importantly from both Russell's and Armstrong's. Austin argues that the statement "The cat is on the mat" is true when the state of affairs to which it refers, by the demonstrative conventions of the language, is of the type that the descriptive conventions of the language correlate with the sentence type used. This makes correspondence a double-conventional relation: conventional links between words and situations (demonstrative) and between sentence-types and situation-types (descriptive). Austin's account avoids the metaphysical commitment to structural isomorphism — it does not require that propositions mirror the logical form of facts — while retaining the genuine worldly element. Its weakness, as Strawson pressed in debate, is that it cannot explain how the same statement can be true when made on one occasion and false on another without making truth context-sensitive in ways that strain the concept.

iv. the funny-fact objection — and how correspondence survives it

Funny facts objection — negative & modal truths; Kirkham's taxonomy of responsesThe most serious objection to correspondence theories — what Kirkham calls the "funny facts" objection — is that many truths seem to resist the assignment of corresponding facts. What is the fact corresponding to "There are no unicorns"? A negative fact — the non-existence of unicorns — seems metaphysically suspicious. What is the fact corresponding to "If it had rained yesterday, the picnic would have been cancelled"? A counterfactual fact? An impossible fact? Kirkham surveys four responses. The first is to bite the bullet and admit negative facts and modal facts — Russell eventually did so. The second is to use Tarski's recursive apparatus to handle molecular truths through truth-functional composition from atomic truths that do have straightforward fact-correlates. The third is Armstrong's totality-fact approach. The fourth — the most radical — is to abandon the claim that every truth requires its own corresponding fact and instead hold only that every contingent atomic truth requires one. Each response has costs; none is decisively refuted. The existence of the problem does not show the theory is wrong — it shows that the metaphysics of facts is hard, which we already knew.

v. what the opposition cannot explain

Realism argument — deflationism cannot explain why true beliefs are useful; success argumentThe deepest argument for the correspondence theory is what Hilary Putnam called the "success argument" and what Kirkham discusses as the explanatory role of truth. Why is it that acting on true beliefs reliably produces successful outcomes? If "true" is merely a device of disquotation — if all there is to "Snow is white" being true is that snow is white — then the correlation between truth and success becomes miraculous. There is no explanation of why true beliefs guide action to successful outcomes unless true beliefs are beliefs that accurately represent how the world is. The deflationary account, which refuses to say anything substantive about what truth is, thereby refuses to explain what the realist can: that true belief constitutes a genuine cognitive achievement, a genuine grasp of how things are, which is why it serves as an appropriate guide to action. Deflationists have worked hard on this objection; in my judgment, none of their responses is adequate.

Argument I — Two-component structure: truthmakers and correspondence must both be specified
1.An adequate correspondence theory requires specifying both truthmakers (worldly items: facts, states of affairs, situations) and the correspondence relation (correlation or structural isomorphism).
2.Isomorphism — the structural mirroring of propositions and facts — is the philosophically richer account: it explains why propositions represent facts, not merely that they are paired with them.
3.Any alternative theory that refuses to specify either component has not explained truth — it has changed the subject.
∴The correspondence theory's two-component structure is a theoretical requirement, not a choice; the opposition must either supply an alternative account of both components or concede the substantive question is unanswered.
Argument II — Armstrong's truthmaker principle: necessitation, not mere correlation
1.The truthmaker principle: for every contingent truth there is an entity whose existence necessitates the truth — if the truthmaker exists, the proposition cannot be false.
2.Necessitation is stronger than correlation: it explains why the truth of a proposition depends on the world, not merely which truths are paired with which worldly items.
3.States of affairs — not sets of objects-and-properties, not the objects and properties separately — are the metaphysically appropriate truthmakers because they are unified in a way mere collections are not.
∴Armstrong's truthmaker theory provides the most rigorous contemporary metaphysical foundation for correspondence; truth is constituted by necessitating states of affairs, not by any epistemic or pragmatic relation.
Argument III — Austin's conventional correspondence: avoiding isomorphism's metaphysical overcommitment
1.Austin identifies two conventional links: demonstrative (between utterances and situations) and descriptive (between sentence-types and situation-types); truth obtains when the referred-to situation is of the correlated type.
2.This avoids the structural isomorphism commitment of early Russell and Tractatus Wittgenstein — it does not require that propositions mirror the logical form of facts.
3.The worldly element is preserved: truth still depends on how things stand in the world, not on coherence, utility, or disquotational convention.
∴Austin's account demonstrates that correspondence can be maintained without metaphysical isomorphism; the core insight — truth tracks the world — survives even as the specific metaphysics is lightened.
Argument IV — The funny-facts objection is survivable: four response strategies
1.Negative and modal truths resist simple fact-assignment: what fact corresponds to "There are no unicorns"?
2.Four survivable responses: (a) admit negative and totality facts; (b) handle molecular truths recursively through Tarski's apparatus from atomic facts; (c) use Armstrong's totality states of affairs; (d) restrict the truthmaker principle to contingent atomic truths only.
3.None is decisively refuted; the difficulty shows that the metaphysics of facts is hard — it does not show correspondence is false.
∴The funny-facts objection is a challenge for the metaphysics of facts, not a refutation of correspondence; the theory has available responses that the opposition has not decisively ruled out.
Argument V — The success argument: deflationism cannot explain why true beliefs guide action
1.Acting on true beliefs reliably produces successful outcomes — a correlation that demands explanation.
2.Deflationism — which says nothing substantive about what truth is — cannot explain this correlation; if true beliefs did not represent how things actually are, their correlation with success would be miraculous.
3.Correspondence explains it: true beliefs represent the world accurately, and accurate representation of how things are is a reliable guide to successful action in the world represented.
∴The success argument is a decisive advantage for correspondence over deflationism: only substantive truth explains why truth matters practically.

Mr President,

The semantic theory of truth, which I developed in the 1930s, is frequently claimed by the opposition as a deflationary victory: the truth predicate, they say, is exhausted by the T-schema instances I generate, and my definition is therefore a vindication of their view that truth requires no substantive metaphysical explanation. I want to resist this reading with some force. My definition of truth was not motivated by deflationary considerations. It was motivated by the need to provide a formally precise, materially adequate definition that would dissolve the liar paradox and ground semantic concepts within a rigorous scientific framework. Hartry Field's critique of my definition then shows what is missing from the semantic account when it is taken to be a complete theory rather than a formal achievement within a physicalist research programme.

"Convention T — the requirement that any adequate definition of truth entail, for every sentence S, the biconditional: S is true if and only if S — is a material adequacy condition, not a theory of truth. It tells us what any theory of truth must achieve; it does not tell us what truth is. The deflationist who reads Convention T as the whole story has confused a test for adequacy with an answer to the question."
i. the T-schema and Convention T — adequacy condition, not theory

Convention T — adequacy condition not definition; T-sentences as consequencesMy definition requires that any formally correct definition of truth for a language L must entail, for every sentence S of L, an instance of the schema: "S" is true ↔ S (with appropriate quotation and metalanguage/object-language distinctions). This is Convention T — a test that any candidate definition must pass. It is not itself a definition. My actual definition proceeds through the recursive definition of satisfaction: a sentence is true if and only if it is satisfied by all infinite sequences of objects. This recursive apparatus handles quantifiers, connectives, and predicates through clauses that together define satisfaction and thereby truth in terms of the formal relations between expressions and the entities they denote. The resulting definition is not a list of T-schema instances; it is a compositional semantic theory that generates those instances as theorems. The deflationist who reduces my theory to those instances has discarded the explanatory structure that generates them.

ii. satisfaction — the semantic core

Satisfaction — recursive definition; denotation; sequences; handles quantifiersThe concept of satisfaction is the semantic core of my theory. An open formula is satisfied by a sequence of objects when substituting those objects for the free variables yields a truth. The base clauses define satisfaction for atomic formulae: "x is wise" is satisfied by a sequence iff the object assigned to x by the sequence is wise. The recursive clauses handle complexity: a conjunction is satisfied iff both conjuncts are; an existential quantification is satisfied iff some sequence differing from the given sequence at most in the value of the bound variable satisfies the formula. The closed sentences — propositions with no free variables — are satisfied by all sequences or by none: if all, the sentence is true; if none, it is false. This apparatus grounds truth in the semantic relationship between expressions and the world — in what expressions denote and what objects satisfy open formulae. It is not disquotational; it is referential and denotational at its base.

iii. the object language / metalanguage distinction — dissolving the liar

Liar paradox — hierarchy of languages; undefinability theorem; formal rigourThe liar paradox — "This sentence is false" — generates contradiction within any language that contains its own truth predicate. My undefinability theorem demonstrates that no consistent formal language can contain its own truth predicate satisfying Convention T: a truth predicate for language L must be defined in a metalanguage ML that contains L but exceeds it in expressive power. This is not an arbitrary stipulation — it is a theorem. The regress of languages it generates is precisely what a rigorous formal semantics requires. Critics who object that natural language has no such hierarchy are correct that my definition does not directly apply to natural language: I was explicit that my definition applies to formalized languages. The application to natural language is an extension — an aspiration of the programme, not a delivered result.

iv. Field's critique — my definition is incomplete without a theory of primitive denotation

Field's critique — denotation not physicalized; list-like; science demands moreHartry Field's 1972 critique of my theory is the most philosophically important extension of the semantic approach. Field argues that my definition reduces the truth of complex sentences to the truth of atomic sentences and the denotation of names and the extension of predicates — but that I provided only a list-like specification of primitive denotation: "Paris" denotes Paris, "London" denotes London, and so on. This list establishes a correlation between names and objects, but it does not explain what the relation of denotation consists in. A genuine physicalist theory of truth — one adequate to the naturalistic demands of science — must explain what it is for a name to denote an object in terms of physical, causal, or informational relations between the name-using community and the object. Without this, my definition leaves the most fundamental semantic relation — reference — unexplained. Field's contribution is to identify that this explanation is the crucial remaining task: a causal theory of reference that grounds denotation physically is needed to complete what my formal apparatus began.

v. the semantic theory is substantive — against deflationary appropriation

Anti-deflationary — Tarski's theory is a correspondence theory; substantive semantic relationsKirkham argues, and I agree, that my semantic theory is most naturally read as a sophisticated version of the correspondence theory rather than as a deflationary theory. The base clauses of my recursive definition — the clauses that specify what predicates are satisfied by — are substantive semantic claims: "is wise" is satisfied by an object iff that object is wise. These are not disquotational tautologies; they are genuine semantic relations between a predicate and a property of objects. The recursion then compositionally extends these base-level semantic relations to complex sentences. The whole apparatus constitutes a substantive semantic theory that locates truth in the relationship between language and the world — which is precisely what the correspondence theory insists upon, even if my version avoids the talk of "facts" and "correspondence" that characterises more traditional versions.

1.Convention T specifies what any adequate definition of truth must entail — it is a test, not an answer.
2.Tarski's actual definition proceeds through recursive clauses defining satisfaction, generating T-schema instances as theorems rather than taking them as axioms.
3.The compositional semantic structure that generates those instances is the explanatory content of the theory; deflationism discards this structure while retaining only the instances it generates.
∴Deflationary readings of Tarski mistake the adequacy condition for the theory; the recursive semantic apparatus is substantive and not reducible to disquotational schemas.
Argument II — Satisfaction is the semantic core: a substantive relation between language and world
1.Satisfaction is defined recursively: base clauses specify when atomic formulae are satisfied by sequences; recursive clauses extend to complex formulae via connectives and quantifiers.
2.The base clauses are substantive semantic claims — they specify the extensions of predicates relative to the world — not disquotational tautologies.
3.Truth, defined as satisfaction by all sequences, is therefore grounded in substantive semantic relations between expressions and the objects that satisfy them.
∴The semantic theory locates truth in a genuine relationship between language and world via satisfaction; it is a substantive theory, not a formal tautology.
Argument III — The undefinability theorem dissolves the liar through the object/metalanguage hierarchy
1.The liar paradox arises in any language that contains its own truth predicate satisfying Convention T; Tarski's undefinability theorem proves no consistent such language exists.
2.The metalanguage hierarchy is therefore a theorem-mandated necessity, not an arbitrary stipulation; truth predicates must always belong to a richer metalanguage than the language they apply to.
3.The solution is formally rigorous and provides a principled reason why the liar generates contradiction — its expression requires a language that cannot self-apply truth.
∴The undefinability theorem provides the most rigorous available treatment of the liar paradox; the semantic theory uniquely combines formal adequacy with paradox-dissolution.
Argument IV — Field's critique: primitive denotation must be physically grounded to complete the theory
1.Tarski's definition reduces truth to primitive denotation (for names) and satisfaction (for predicates), but specifies these by list — "Paris" denotes Paris — not by identifying what the denotation relation consists in.
2.A list-like specification establishes correlation but not explanation; a physicalist theory demands that denotation be explicated in terms of causal-historical or informational relations.
3.Field's programme — grounding primitive denotation in physical-causal relations — is the necessary completion of the Tarskian framework, not an alternative to it.
∴Field's critique identifies the unfinished task of the semantic theory; the response is to complete it with a causal theory of reference, not to retreat to deflationism.

1.The base clauses of Tarski's recursive definition are substantive semantic claims about the extensions of predicates — they relate language to the world, not language to language.
2.The compositional recursion extends these base-level correspondences to complex sentences — it is a systematic theory of how language corresponds to the world, not a disquotational shorthand.
3.Kirkham argues Tarski's theory is best classified as a sophisticated correspondence theory; the deflationary appropriation is a misreading that discards the explanatory content to keep only the formal apparatus.
∴Tarski's semantic theory is a substantive theory of the correspondence kind; claiming it for deflationism is a category error that mistakes the apparatus's formality for metaphysical neutrality.

Mr President,

The proposition has given us a rich and carefully developed account of the correspondence theory and the semantic theory. What it has not done — what I submit it cannot do — is identify a genuine, substantive property that true sentences have and false sentences lack, over and above the trivial fact that true sentences are true. Frank Ramsey saw this clearly in 1927: to say that the proposition that Caesar was murdered is true is simply to say that Caesar was murdered. The word "true" adds nothing. This is not a counsel of despair about the concept of truth — it is a discovery about its logical role.

"The proposition's speakers have mistaken the importance of truth — its role in generalisation, its indispensability to logic and inquiry — for evidence that truth is a substantial property requiring a substantial theory. These are different things. A concept can be of enormous theoretical importance without naming a property that requires metaphysical analysis. 'True' is the most important word in our language precisely because it performs a logical function that nothing else can — not because it names a relation between sentences and facts."
i. Ramsey's redundancy theory — the original deflation

Ramsey — redundancy; "it is true that p" = "p"; no property addedRamsey's 1927 proposal is stark: "It is true that Caesar was murdered" means no more than "Caesar was murdered." The truth predicate is eliminable in any context where we know what proposition is being evaluated. When we say "The Pythagorean theorem is true," we mean only what we mean when we assert the Pythagorean theorem directly. Truth adds no content to our assertion; it is a logical redundancy. Kirkham notes that this cannot quite be the whole story — "Everything Goldbach says is true" cannot be reduced to a conjunction of what Goldbach says if we do not know what Goldbach says — which is why Ramsey acknowledged that the truth predicate serves an ineliminable generalising function. But this generalising function is a logical role, not evidence for a substantive property. The word "true" behaves like a quantifier over propositions, not like a predicate attributing a genuine relational property to them.

ii. Horwich's minimalism — the minimal theory

Horwich — minimal theory; all instances of T-schema; no further explanation neededPaul Horwich's minimalism refines the deflationary position with greater precision than Ramsey's. The minimal theory of truth consists of all instances of the equivalence schema: ⟨p⟩ is true ↔ p (where ⟨p⟩ is the proposition that p). These infinitely many instances constitute the entire theory of truth; nothing further need be said. Horwich argues that every theoretical role truth plays — in logic, in propositional attitude psychology, in semantic theory — can be explained by the minimal theory's instances without invoking any substantial property of correspondence. The proposition's appeal to the "success argument" — that deflationism cannot explain why true beliefs guide successful action — is met by Horwich's observation that the explanatory work is done by the conjunction of the instances: "She believed that there was a bus at noon, and there was a bus at noon, and so she caught it." The generalisation over all such instances does not require a substantial property; it requires only the minimal theory's quantificational resources.

iii. Grover's prosentential theory — "true" is not a predicate at all

Prosentential theory — Grover; "it is true" as prosentence not predicate; syntactic functionDorothy Grover's prosentential theory takes the deflationary insight in a different and more radical direction: "it is true" is not a predicate at all but a prosentence — a grammatical unit functioning like a pronoun but standing in for whole sentences rather than noun phrases. Just as "she" is a pro-form for a name — referring back to a previously introduced individual without re-using the name — "that is true" is a pro-form for a sentence, inheriting its content from the sentence it stands proxy for. "It is true that it is raining" is therefore not a subject-predicate sentence in which "is true" attributes a property to the proposition that it is raining; it is a prosentential expression whose content is exhausted by "it is raining." The appearance that "true" names a property is a grammatical illusion generated by the surface subject-predicate structure of English. This account explains why deflationary paraphrases always work: not because truth is a trivial property, but because "true" is not a property-attributing predicate in the first place.

iv. against correspondence — the problem of the correspondence relation

Correspondence relation — Frege's dilemma; identity or nothing; mysterious third entityFrege's dilemma for the correspondence theory is decisive. If a proposition truly corresponds to a fact, the proposition and the fact must share all the relevant properties — otherwise the correspondence is unexplained. But if they share all relevant properties, they are identical. And if identical, correspondence reduces to identity, which is not what correspondence theorists intend. Conversely, if they do not share all properties, correspondence is a mysterious external relation between entities of fundamentally different ontological categories — a proposition (abstract) and a fact (concrete or abstract?) — that no theory has adequately characterised. The notion of "correspondence" functions as a promissory note that is never redeemed. Armstrong's truthmakers, the proposition insists, necessitate the truths. But necessitation is itself a modal relation, and explaining modality is at least as difficult as explaining truth. The correspondence theory has not solved the problem of truth — it has relocated it into the problem of facts, and then into the problem of necessitation, and so on indefinitely.

v. the generalisation problem — why deflationism needs the T-schema

Generalisation problem — "all snowflakes are unique" problem; quantification over propositionsI acknowledge that the deflationary account faces a genuine challenge in what is called the generalisation problem: how does deflationism explain the truth of "Everything Einstein said is true" without either listing Einstein's statements or invoking a property that all true statements share? The prosentential theory and Horwich's minimal theory both address this through quantification over propositions — "For all p, if Einstein said that p, then p." This is propositional quantification, and it is genuinely non-trivial. But it is not the same as positing a substantial property of correspondence. The resources needed to explain generalisation are formal and logical — they require no facts, no states of affairs, no correspondence relation, and no necessitation. The challenge to the proposition is: can they explain the same logical behaviour of "true" without the enormous ontological surplus of facts, states of affairs, and mysterious correspondence relations that their account requires?

3.The correspondence theory relocates the problem of truth into the problem of facts and then into the problem of necessitation — it has not solved the problem but deferred it indefinitely.

2.This formalisation requires no facts, states of affairs, correspondence relations, or necessitation — only the logical resources of propositional quantification already available in the deflationary framework.
3.The proposition's ontological surplus — facts, states of affairs, necessitation — is therefore not required by the explanatory tasks that motivate the search for a theory of truth.
∴The generalisation problem is solved by propositional quantification; it is not evidence for a substantive property of truth and provides no support for correspondence theories.

Mr President,

I want to challenge the motion from a different direction than my deflationary colleagues. They accept that the proposition has correctly identified what truth would have to be — a relation to the mind-independent world — and argue that no such property exists. I want to argue something stronger: that the very notion of a mind-independent world to which our sentences could "correspond" is incoherent or unknowable, and that the appropriate response is not deflation but reconception. Truth must be understood in epistemic terms — as what we would believe under ideal conditions, as what coheres with our entire system of beliefs, as what survives the test of inquiry — or it is a concept that does no work for beings situated as we are.

"The correspondence theorist speaks of a mind-independent world to which our sentences correspond. But how do we access this mind-independent world except through our concepts, our language, our modes of inquiry? Any characterisation of the world that could serve as the target of correspondence is already a conceptualised, theory-laden characterisation. The notion of correspondence to a world entirely outside all conceptual schemes is not a thought we can make good. It is a picture that appears to say something while saying nothing."
i. Blanshard's coherence theory — truth as systematic consistency

Blanshard — coherence as systematic entailment; not mere consistency; degrees of truthBrand Blanshard's coherence theory, as Kirkham analyses it, goes beyond the simple claim that truth is consistency with other beliefs. Blanshard insists that the coherence relation required for truth is not mere logical compatibility but systematic entailment: a belief is true to the extent that it forms part of a system in which each element entails and is entailed by every other. This is an idealist vision of truth: the fully coherent system — the Absolute of idealist metaphysics — is fully true, and individual beliefs are true to the degree they approach this ideal. The most serious objection, pressed by Russell and Kirkham, is that many mutually coherent but false systems of beliefs are possible: a coherent detective novel coheres internally but is not thereby true. Blanshard's response is that coherence must be maximally comprehensive — incorporating all possible evidence — not merely internally consistent. This response generates a different problem: how do we know when our system is maximally comprehensive, and how does comprehensiveness differ from simply taking all truths into account, circularly?

ii. Peirce and James — the pragmatist conception

Peirce — truth as ideal end of inquiry; James — truth as useful belief; divergence between themThe pragmatist tradition offers two distinct and importantly divergent conceptions of truth. Peirce's formulation defines truth as the opinion that the community of inquirers would converge upon in the long run, under ideal conditions of investigation. This is not a subjectivist view — Peirce's ideal community of inquiry is sufficiently large, patient, and rigorous that its conclusions would be forced by the evidence — but it is genuinely epistemic: truth is the limit of inquiry, not a prior fact that inquiry is trying to track. William James's formulation is more radical and more problematic: truth is what it is "good for us to believe." James emphasises the cash value of true beliefs — their usefulness in navigating experience, organising expectation, and guiding action. Kirkham is rightly sceptical: James's formulation seems to allow that false but comforting beliefs might be true if they are sufficiently useful. Peirce's formulation is more defensible but faces the objection that we cannot know what the ideal limit of inquiry is, and that a definition of truth we cannot in principle verify gives us no practical guidance.

iii. Dummett's anti-realism — truth as warranted assertibility

Dummett — meaning as verification conditions; bivalence failure; recognition-transcendent truth incoherentMichael Dummett's anti-realism about truth is the most philosophically sophisticated challenge to the correspondence theory's metaphysical picture. Dummett argues that the meaning of a sentence is constituted by its verification conditions — the conditions under which a competent speaker would be warranted in asserting it. A theory of meaning that assigns verification-transcendent truth conditions — conditions whose satisfaction or non-satisfaction might outstrip all possible evidence — cannot serve as an adequate theory of meaning for natural language, because a speaker's grasp of such conditions could not be manifested in their linguistic behaviour. The principle of bivalence — every statement is either true or false — fails for statements whose truth value is, even in principle, unknowable. The anti-realist replaces truth with warranted assertibility as the central semantic concept, and replaces classical with intuitionistic logic, which does not validate bivalence or the law of excluded middle. This is not pragmatism: it is a systematic semantic theory with far-reaching logical consequences.

iv. the common ground — the unknowability of mind-independent truth

Epistemic access problem — all three positions share a rejection of mind-independent truth as accessibleDespite their differences, Blanshard, Peirce, James, and Dummett share a common ground that motivates the opposition to the motion: the rejection of truth as a relation to a world wholly outside all epistemic access. Any characterisation of the world that could serve as the target of correspondence is already a characterisation from within some conceptual scheme, some language, some theory. The "mind-independent world" invoked by the proposition is not a bare, unconceptualised reality — it is always already described, theorised, and conceptualised. Putnam's "brain in a vat" argument is a vivid version of this concern: the correspondence theorist cannot coherently point at the world from outside all conceptual schemes and say "that is what our sentences correspond to." The concept of truth as correspondence to the world-in-itself is not an analysable concept — it is a picture that generates the illusion of meaning while conveying none.

v. what epistemic theories explain that correspondence cannot

Epistemic advantage — truth guides inquiry; connection to warrant; normative role of truthThe proposition's Kirkham-derived account emphasises the explanatory power of correspondence. I want to point to an explanatory advantage on the opposition's side. The motion's "success argument" — that true beliefs guide successful action — explains success causally: accurate representation causes reliable action. But truth plays a normative role in human life that the causal story cannot capture: truth is what we ought to believe, what inquiry aims at, what distinguishes knowledge from mere true belief. An epistemic theory of truth integrates truth into the normative structure of inquiry: truth is what rationally directed inquiry tends toward, what survives the test of all possible evidence, what it is warranted to assert. This normative integration — truth as the telos of inquiry — is something the correspondence theory cannot provide, because the correspondence relation is not itself a normative concept. We care about truth not merely because accurate representations cause successful actions, but because there is something right about believing truly and something wrong about believing falsely — and this rightness is not causal but normative.

Argument I — Blanshard's coherence theory: truth as systematic entailment within a maximally comprehensive system
1.Blanshard's coherence is not mere consistency but systematic entailment: a belief is true to the degree it belongs to a system where each element entails and is entailed by all others.
2.The maximally comprehensive and mutually entailing system — the ideal limit of coherence — constitutes full truth; individual beliefs approach truth as they approach this ideal.
3.The "coherent fiction" objection (Russell's) is met by the comprehensiveness requirement: a coherent fiction is not maximally comprehensive — it excludes empirical evidence that would destroy its coherence.
∴Blanshard's coherence theory provides a non-trivial epistemic account of truth as the ideal limit of a process of systematic integration of evidence — not mere internal consistency.
Argument II — Peirce and James: truth as the limit of inquiry vs. truth as useful belief
1.Peirce: truth is the opinion the community of inquirers would converge upon at the ideal limit of investigation — an epistemic but objective concept that does not reduce to individual utility.
2.James: truth is what is good for us to believe — cash value in guiding experience — a more radical and more problematic formulation that risks making comfortable falsehoods true.
3.Peirce's formulation is the more defensible and provides a genuine alternative to correspondence: truth as the telos of rational inquiry rather than a pre-given relation to a mind-independent world.
∴Peirce's pragmatist conception defines truth functionally within the practice of inquiry; it is a coherent alternative to correspondence that does not require access to a mind-independent world.
Argument III — Dummett's anti-realism: verification-transcendent truth is semantically incoherent
1.Meaning is constituted by verification conditions — the conditions under which a speaker is warranted in asserting a sentence; a speaker's grasp of meaning must be manifestable in linguistic behaviour.
2.Verification-transcendent truth conditions — conditions whose satisfaction might outstrip all possible evidence — cannot be manifested in behaviour; a theory of meaning assigning them cannot be adequate.
3.Bivalence fails for statements with unknowable truth values; the semantic revision from classical to intuitionistic logic follows from the rejection of recognition-transcendent truth.
∴Dummett's anti-realism demonstrates that the correspondence theory's metaphysical picture — truth as a relation to a world that might outstrip all verification — is semantically incoherent; meaning cannot be constituted by unknowable conditions.
Argument IV — The epistemic access problem: any characterisation of the "mind-independent world" is conceptually mediated
1.Any characterisation of the world that could serve as the target of correspondence is already a characterisation from within a conceptual scheme, a language, or a theory — not a bare unconceptualised reality.
2.The notion of correspondence to the world-as-it-is-in-itself, prior to all conceptualisation, is not a coherent thought: we have no access to such a world and no way to characterise what correspondence to it would consist in.
3.Putnam's internal realism makes this precise: "true" cannot coherently mean correspondence to a world that is viewable from no possible perspective — reference itself is not theory-independent.
∴The correspondence theory's mind-independent world is not a coherent target for correspondence; any world we can characterise is already conceptually mediated, which undermines the realist picture the motion defends.
Argument V — Truth's normative role: epistemic theories explain what correspondence cannot
1.Truth plays a normative role in inquiry: truth is what we ought to believe, what distinguishes knowledge from lucky true belief, what it is right to assert — not merely a causal relation between accurate representations and successful actions.
2.The correspondence relation is not itself a normative concept; it cannot explain why there is something right about believing truly — it can only explain why true belief reliably produces successful action.
3.Epistemic theories — truth as the limit of ideal inquiry, as warranted assertibility — integrate truth into the normative structure of inquiry as its telos, which is the appropriate location for a concept that plays the normative role truth does.
∴Epistemic theories explain truth's normative role — why truth is what inquiry aims at, what it is right to believe — which correspondence theories, as causal-representational accounts, cannot; the normativity of truth is an advantage for the epistemic family of theories.
