The Individuation of Actions

Published May 09, 2026

It is sometimes said—often with a tone suggesting that something deeply puzzling has been uncovered—that when we act, we never do just one thing. I flip a switch, and thereby turn on a light, illuminate the room, alert the burglar, wake the baby, and perhaps even, in some more extended chain, cause a series of further events. The question then arises: what did I do? Did I do all of these things? Or only some? And if only some, which?

At first, this may appear to present us with a problem about the individuation of actions: how many actions are there here? But before we attempt to answer that, it may be worth asking a prior question—namely, what sorts of things we are doing when we say, in these various ways, what someone “did.”

Let us begin with a simple report: “He turned on the light.” This is, in most ordinary contexts, perfectly in order. Now consider: “He alerted the burglar.” This, too, may be true, though it is less likely to be said unless the presence of the burglar is relevant. And again: “He woke the baby.” Also true, perhaps, but again dependent on context.

Are these three different actions? Or three descriptions of one and the same action?

If we are inclined to say that they are three different actions, we may be pressed to say how many actions there are in total. Does every consequence of what I do count as another action of mine? This seems excessive. If the light I switch on contributes, by some distant chain, to an increase in energy consumption, which in turn affects the climate, which in turn affects someone’s crops, do I thereby perform all these actions?

On the other hand, if we say that there is only one action—the movement of my finger, perhaps—then the other descriptions begin to look curiously detachable. For it would be odd to insist that I did not turn on the light, but merely moved my finger.

The difficulty, then, seems to arise from the assumption that there must be a single answer to the question, “What did he do?”—as though we were being asked to identify one item from a list.

But this assumption is already questionable.

For consider how the question actually functions. If someone asks, “What did he do?” in a given situation, the appropriate answer depends on what is at issue. If we are concerned with electricity usage, “He turned on the light” may be the right answer. If we are concerned with why the baby is crying, “He woke the baby” may be the more relevant description. If we are concerned with a burglary, “He alerted the burglar” takes precedence.

In each case, we are not identifying a different action in some metaphysical inventory; we are selecting a description that serves a particular purpose.

This suggests that the various descriptions are not competing answers to a single question, but answers to different questions—questions that share a grammatical form but differ in their point.

We might say, somewhat loosely, that an action can be described under many aspects. But we should be careful not to reify these “aspects” into separate entities. The multiplicity lies in our descriptions, not necessarily in the action itself.

This becomes clearer if we consider cases where the descriptions come apart in more interesting ways.

Suppose I say, “He turned on the light, but he didn’t mean to wake the baby.” This is perfectly intelligible. But if we were to say, “He woke the baby, but he didn’t wake the baby,” we would be talking nonsense. The difference lies in the fact that “turning on the light” can be something one does intentionally, while “waking the baby” may be an unintended consequence.

So intention attaches differently to different descriptions.

This gives us a clue as to why we might be tempted to distinguish between “the action itself” and its “consequences.” But even here, the line is not as sharp as we might like.

For in some contexts, we do treat what might elsewhere be called a consequence as the action itself. If someone asks, “Who woke the baby?” the answer “He did” is entirely in order, even if waking the baby was not his intention.

So again, what counts as “the action” depends on the context in which the question is asked.

We may now be in a position to see why the original puzzle arises. It arises when we take the various true descriptions of what someone did and suppose that they must correspond to distinct items—distinct actions—over and above the act itself. We then ask how many such items there are, and how they are related.

But this is to impose a kind of bookkeeping requirement that our ordinary practices do not observe.

In ordinary life, we do not need to decide, once and for all, whether “turning on the light” and “waking the baby” are the same action or different actions. We simply use whichever description is appropriate to the occasion, and we are rarely in doubt about which that is.

This does not mean that anything goes. There are limits. It would be odd to describe my switching on the light as “causing a hurricane,” even if, in some remote causal chain, it contributes infinitesimally to such an event. Our practices include a sense of what counts as a relevant or salient consequence.

But these limits are not drawn by a single principle that can be stated in advance. They are part of a more general sensitivity to context, purpose, and significance.

If we are still dissatisfied, it may be because we are looking for a theory that will tell us, in all cases, what the “real” action is. But the lesson of our examination is that there may be no such thing to be found.

What there is, instead, is a range of ways of describing what someone did, each tied to different concerns—intention, outcome, responsibility, explanation—and each perfectly at home in its proper setting.

The philosophical problem, then, does not so much admit of a solution as it dissolves once we give up the assumption that there must be a single, context-independent answer to the question, “What did he do?”
