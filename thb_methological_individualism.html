Mr President,

The motion asks whether social phenomena must be explained by reference to individuals alone. We want to argue that this formulation conceals a crucial distinction that, once made, dissolves the apparent conflict between methodological individualism and holism. Our position is neither a capitulation to holism nor a defence of pure individualism. It is a reconciliation grounded in the philosophy of science — specifically in the concepts of supervenience and multiple realizability that have transformed the philosophy of mind over the past four decades and that apply, we argue, with equal force to the social sciences.

The Key Distinction (List & Spiekermann, APSR 2013) Supervenience individualism: Social facts supervene on individual facts — there can be no social difference without some individual-level difference. This is almost certainly true and both sides should accept it.
Explanatory individualism: Social phenomena must always be explained by citing individual-level facts. This does not follow from supervenience and is sometimes false — when social regularities are robust across multiple individual-level realizations, only a higher-level description captures the salient explanatory property.
i. supervenience without reduction

Supervenience without reduction — the core reconciliationWe accept supervenience individualism: social facts are fully determined by individual facts; there can be no possible world that differs socially while being identical at the individual level. This concedes the individualist’s most important ontological claim. But supervenience does not entail reducibility. The classic case is mental properties: every mental fact supervenes on physical facts — there is no mental difference without a physical difference — yet mental properties are not thereby eliminable in favour of purely physical descriptions. The reason is multiple realizability: a single mental property type can be realized by many different physical configurations. The same lesson applies to social science. A social regularity — say, that groups with competing interests tend to fail at collective coordination — can be realized by indefinitely many different configurations of individual beliefs, preferences, and decision procedures. The social regularity captures something that no individual-level description captures, because the individual-level descriptions vary while the social pattern remains.

ii. when non-reductionistic explanation is mandated

Multiple realizability mandates higher-level explanationThe key criterion we propose is this: non-reductionistic explanation is mandated when social regularities are robust across changes in their individual-level realization. Consider our example from the paper: the Copenhagen climate summit (2009) failed because the participating states lacked a common interest. This is a higher-level social explanation. It captures something genuine: even if we had specified the detailed beliefs and preferences of every negotiator, we would not have identified the explanatorily salient property unless we described it as a social property — the absence of a shared collective interest. Why? Because the same outcome would have resulted from any of a large number of different individual-level configurations, all of which realize the same higher-level property of non-shared interest. The higher-level property is causally potent, not merely a redescription of the individual facts.

iii. the analogy with philosophy of mind

Mind analogy — Fodor’s special sciences argument applies to social scienceJerry Fodor’s 1974 argument for the special sciences applies directly here. Fodor argued that the regularities captured by psychology, economics, and other special sciences cannot be translated without residue into the language of physics, even if mental and economic properties supervene on physical ones. The reason: a psychological law like “beliefs and desires cause actions” holds across wildly different physical realizations — different brain architectures, different neural implementations — and the property that makes the law work is the psychological one, not the physical one. The same structure obtains in social science. A sociological law like “status competition increases inequality” may hold across enormously varied individual-level realizations. Trying to replace it with a list of individual-level mechanisms loses the lawlike generalization, which is precisely what makes it explanatory.

iv. what this means for the motion

Conclusion — MI is sometimes correct, sometimes incorrect; robustness is the criterionOur reconciliation means: the motion is partly right and partly wrong. Where social phenomena are not robust — where the individual-level details do all the explanatory work — individualistic explanation is correct and holistic explanation adds nothing. Game theory, rational choice analysis of individual decisions, and micro-level causal tracing are often the right tools. But where social regularities are robust to changes in their individual-level realization — where the higher-level property is multiply realizable and causally stable — non-reductionistic explanation is not merely permissible but required. The motion, as stated, claims MI is always necessary. That claim is false, and it is false for principled theoretical reasons that the individualist cannot simply dismiss as bad philosophy of science.

Argument I — Supervenience without reduction: the ontological concession does not entail explanatory reduction
1.Supervenience individualism: social facts supervene on individual facts; no social difference without individual difference. This is almost certainly true.
2.Supervenience does not entail reducibility: mental properties supervene on physical ones without being reducible to physical descriptions (multiple realizability).
3.The same structure applies to social science: social properties supervene on individual facts without being eliminable in favour of individual-level descriptions.
∴Accepting supervenience individualism is compatible with rejecting explanatory individualism; the individualist’s ontological thesis does not establish the methodological thesis the motion requires.
Argument II — Multiple realizability mandates higher-level explanation
1.A social regularity is multiply realizable when it holds across many different individual-level configurations; the higher-level property is what all these configurations share.
2.The Copenhagen summit failure is explained by the absence of shared collective interest — a higher-level property realized by many possible individual preference configurations.
3.No individual-level description captures the explanatorily salient property without describing it at the social level; the social property does explanatory work the individual-level description cannot.
∴When social regularities are robust across multiple individual-level realizations, higher-level explanation is not a mere redescription but is explanatorily mandated; pure individualism is insufficient.
Argument III — Fodor’s special sciences: social laws cannot be translated without residue into individual-level laws
1.Special-science laws (psychology, sociology, economics) hold across different lower-level realizations; translating them into lower-level language breaks the generalization.
2.A sociological law like “status competition increases inequality” holds across varied individual configurations; the lawlike generality is at the social level.
3.Replacing it with a list of individual mechanisms loses the law; what is explanatory is precisely the social-level regularity.
∴Social laws are genuine generalizations at the social level; methodological individualism that requires reduction to individual-level descriptions loses the explanatory content that makes social science possible.
Argument IV — The robustness criterion: a principled dividing line
1.Non-reductionistic explanation is mandated when social regularities are robust to changes in individual-level realization; individual explanation suffices when they are not.
2.This criterion is principled, not ad hoc: it follows from the logic of causal explanation and multiple realizability in the philosophy of science.
3.The motion claims MI is always necessary; the robustness criterion shows it is sometimes insufficient and sometimes superfluous.
∴The robustness criterion provides a principled reconciliation: individualism is correct when individual-level variation explains social variation; holism is correct when it does not. The motion’s “must” is too strong.

Mr President,

I want to defend functional explanation in social science — and specifically in historical materialism — against the charge that it is scientifically illegitimate because it lacks individual-level microfoundations. Jon Elster, my most persistent and formidable critic, argues that functional explanations without specified mechanisms are not explanations at all; they are placeholders awaiting the real, individual-level explanatory story. I want to argue that this charge fundamentally misunderstands the logic of functional explanation, and that functional or consequence explanations are a legitimate and indispensable tool for social science.

“The central claims of historical materialism are functional explanations. Capitalism persists because it develops the productive forces. The superstructure stabilizes the base. These are not mere descriptions: they identify something that is explained by its propensity to produce a certain effect — and the Darwinian parallel shows that this form of explanation can be scientifically respectable without specifying the mechanism.”
i. what a functional explanation is

Functional explanation — consequence explanation in social scienceA functional explanation is a consequence explanation: something X is explained by its propensity to produce effect Y. In my Karl Marx’s Theory of History I argued that the central claims of historical materialism have this form. Capitalism persists not because of the intentions of capitalists or the preferences of workers but because it develops productive forces — because it has the propensity to produce that beneficial consequence. The legal superstructure has the form it has because it stabilises the economic base. These are functional explanations: the character of the explanandum is determined by its effect on something else. Crucially, such explanations do not require specification of the mechanism by which the effect produces the persistence. They require only a “consequence law”: a general pattern to the effect that items of type X persist when and because they have effect Y.

ii. the darwinian analogy and the legitimacy of consequence laws

Darwinian analogy — natural selection legitimises consequence explanation without mechanismThe Darwinian theory of natural selection is the clearest example of a scientifically legitimate consequence explanation that preceded detailed knowledge of its mechanism. Darwin explained the adaptation of organisms to their environments by the propensity of better-adapted traits to spread through populations — without knowing anything about genetics, molecular biology, or the specific mechanisms of inheritance. The explanation was legitimate, powerful, and generative of research before the mechanism was understood. The same structure is available in social science. We can explain the persistence of social institutions by their functional consequences — their tendency to stabilise certain social relations, develop certain productive capacities, or solve coordination problems — without fully specifying the micro-level mechanisms by which these effects sustain the institutions.

iii. against elster: microfoundations are not always available

Against Elster — microfoundations often unavailable; functional explanation is the best we haveElster insists that functional explanations without microfoundations are not genuine explanations. I have two responses. First, in the introduction to the 2000 edition of my book, I acknowledged that “a micro-analysis is always desirable and always in principle possible, even if it is not always possible to achieve one in practice.” Functional explanations are sometimes the best available — the best science of the case — even if not the ideal final explanation. Elster’s demand for microfoundations before functional explanation is permitted would have required us to reject Darwinian evolution until genetics was developed. That would have been a scientific and methodological error. Second, the demand for microfoundations assumes that individual-level rational-choice explanation is always available in principle. But many social phenomena involve emergent properties, path-dependence, and structural constraints that rational-choice models cannot easily capture without imputing unrealistic informational and computational capacities to individuals.

iv. what distinguishes me from list & spiekermann

Cohen vs. List — functional explanation vs. multiple realizability; different defences of holismList and Spiekermann defend higher-level social explanation through the multiple realizability argument: social properties are causally potent because they are realized by many individual configurations. My defence of functional explanation operates differently: I argue that even before we know the individual-level mechanism, the functional explanation has genuine explanatory force through the consequence law. These are complementary strategies. List and Spiekermann show that higher-level properties are irreducible even when mechanisms are fully specified; my functional explanation defence shows that legitimate explanation does not always require specifying mechanisms at all. Together, they mount a two-level defence of social-level explanation against the individualist’s methodological demands.

Argument I — Functional explanation: consequence laws explain without specifying mechanisms
1.A functional/consequence explanation explains X by its propensity to produce effect Y; the character of the explanandum is determined by its beneficial consequences for something else.
2.Such explanations require a consequence law: items of type X persist when and because they have effect Y; this law need not specify the mechanism of persistence.
3.Historical materialism offers explanations of this form: capitalism persists because it develops productive forces; the superstructure has its form because it stabilizes the base.
∴Functional explanation is a legitimate explanatory form in social science that does not require individual-level microfoundations; consequence laws provide genuine explanatory power.
Argument II — The Darwinian analogy: legitimate science can precede mechanism specification
1.Darwin explained adaptation by the propensity of better-adapted traits to spread — a consequence explanation — without knowing the mechanism of inheritance (genetics).
2.This was scientifically legitimate, generative of research, and indispensable even before the mechanism was known; demanding mechanism-specification before explanation was permitted would have been a methodological error.
3.The same structure is available in social science: functional explanations can be legitimate and productive before their individual-level mechanisms are specified.
∴The Darwinian case shows that consequence/functional explanation is scientifically legitimate without prior mechanism specification; Elster’s demand for microfoundations before explanation applies an anachronistic methodological standard.
Argument III — Against Elster: microfoundations are often unavailable and not always necessary
1.Elster demands individual-level rational-choice microfoundations before functional explanations are legitimate; but many social phenomena involve emergent properties and structural constraints that rational-choice models cannot easily capture.
2.Functional explanations represent the best available science when mechanisms cannot be fully specified; rejecting them leaves no explanation at all rather than a better one.
3.The requirement that micro-analysis is “in principle possible” does not make it currently available; functional explanation is appropriate when it is the best available tool.
∴Elster’s demand for microfoundations would have invalidated Darwinian evolution and would eliminate the best available social explanations; it sets an unachievable standard that defeats the scientific purpose of explanation.
Argument IV — Cohen vs. List: complementary defences of social-level explanation
1.List and Spiekermann defend higher-level explanation by showing that social properties are causally potent because multiply realizable; this works when mechanisms are known but individual-level descriptions fail to generalize.
2.Cohen defends functional explanation by showing that consequence laws are legitimate even when mechanisms are unknown; this works when mechanisms are not fully specified.
3.Together these mount a two-level defence: social explanation is legitimate both when mechanisms are known (multiple realizability) and when they are not (consequence laws).
∴The Cohen and List/Spiekermann defences are complementary; together they show that social-level explanation is indispensable across a wide range of social scientific contexts.

Mr President,

My colleagues have argued against methodological individualism in the philosophy of social science. I want to argue against it in moral philosophy — specifically, against what I call normative individualism: the thesis that moral duties can always be fully specified at the level of individual agents, and that apparent attributions of duty to groups are either reducible to individual duties or are merely rhetorical. In my book Group Duties I argue that this is false: some groups are genuine duty-bearers, and the duties they bear are not merely the sum of their members’ individual duties.

Collins’s Tripartite Model Combinations: Collections with no shared goals or procedures. Cannot bear duties; their purported duties reduce to separate individual duties to “I-reason” (act as best you can independently).
Coalitions: Collections sharing goals but lacking decision-making procedures. Cannot bear duties; their purported duties become members’ several duties to “we-reason” (act on the assumption others will cooperate).
Collectives: Groups with shared goals and decision-making procedures. These can bear genuine duties, irreducible to any individual member’s duties.
i. groups can have duties individuals cannot

Group duties irreducible — collectives have capacities no member hasThe fundamental reason that collectives can be genuine duty-bearers is that they have capacities no individual member has. A government has the capacity to pass legislation, fund public services, and deploy institutional authority at a scale no individual minister or official possesses. The duty to provide universal healthcare, for example, cannot be assigned to any individual member of the government — no minister can discharge it unilaterally — nor is it simply the aggregate of each member’s individual duties. It is a duty of the collective institution as such, bearing on it in virtue of the institution’s decision-making procedures and institutional powers. Normative individualism — which insists that all genuine duties must attach to individuals — cannot accommodate this. It will either deny that the government has any duty at all, which is absurd, or try to parcel out the duty as individual duties, which distorts its structure.

ii. the normative independence of group duties

Normative independence — group duty can exist when no individual has corresponding dutyThe most striking feature of genuine group duties is that they can exist when no individual member has a corresponding individual duty. Consider a collective institution with the capacity to prevent a serious harm, where no individual member has the causal power to prevent that harm on their own. The collective has a duty to prevent the harm. But which individual member has a duty to prevent it? None — because no individual can. The duty attaches to the collective in virtue of the collective’s distinctive causal and institutional powers. Normative individualism must either deny this duty or manufacture spurious individual duties — duties for individuals to “do their part,” “support the collective,” or “promote collective action.” These are derivative member duties that flow from the collective duty; they do not constitute it or replace it. The collective duty is explanatorily prior.

iii. what distinguishes me from list and cohen

Collins vs. List/Cohen — normative not explanatory holismList and Spiekermann argue against explanatory methodological individualism: sometimes social explanation must proceed at the social level. Cohen argues against explanatory individualism in the particular domain of historical materialism: functional explanation is legitimate even without individual-level mechanisms. My argument is different: I argue against normative individualism — the claim that all genuine moral duties must attach to individuals. This is a claim in ethics, not in philosophy of explanation. The target is not the individualist’s view that social science should cite individual agents, but the view that moral responsibility, obligation, and duty are essentially individual-level phenomena. That view is false, and it is false because collectives have capacities, decision-making procedures, and institutional powers that generate genuine duties irreducible to any individual’s duties.

iv. the political stakes: targeting demands correctly

Political stakes — correct attribution of duty matters for accountabilityThe stakes of this debate are not merely philosophical. If normative individualism is correct, political demands for structural change — demands that governments reform healthcare, that corporations reduce carbon emissions, that states compensate for historical injustice — are either confused or must be reformulated as demands on individuals. This reformulation distorts the demands. The call for systemic reform is not simply a call for individual ministers to behave differently; it is a call for an institution with a decision-making procedure to exercise its collective capacity in a particular way. Getting this right matters for accountability: if we attribute the duty incorrectly — to individuals rather than to the collective — we hold the wrong entities responsible and design the wrong remedies. Collins’s tripartite model is a tool for targeting political demands at the right entities, in the right way, for the right reasons.

Thank you.

Argument I — Collectives have capacities no individual has: group duties are irreducible
1.Collectives (groups with shared goals and decision-making procedures) have causal and institutional capacities that no individual member possesses.
2.Duties can attach to an entity in virtue of its capacities; where the relevant capacity belongs only to the collective, the duty attaches to the collective, not to any individual member.
3.The duty to provide universal healthcare cannot be assigned to any individual minister (who cannot discharge it unilaterally) or to the aggregate of members; it belongs to the government as a collective.
∴Collectives can bear genuine duties irreducible to individual duties, because they have distinctive capacities that generate obligations at the collective level that no individual-level description can capture.
Argument II — Group duties can exist when no individual has a corresponding duty
1.A collective may have the capacity to prevent a harm that no individual member can prevent alone; the collective therefore has a duty where no individual has a corresponding duty.
2.Normative individualism must either deny this duty (absurd) or manufacture spurious individual duties (“do your part”) that are derivative of, not constitutive of, the collective duty.
3.The collective duty is explanatorily prior to member duties; member duties flow from the collective duty, not the reverse.
∴Normative individualism cannot account for cases where collective duties exist without corresponding individual duties; the collective duty is ontologically and normatively primitive in these cases.
Argument III — The tripartite model: precision in attributing duties to the right entities
1.Combinations (no shared goals/procedures) cannot bear duties; their purported duties reduce to individual “I-reasoning.” Coalitions (shared goals, no procedures) cannot bear duties; their purported duties become several “we-reasoning” duties.
2.Only collectives (shared goals with decision-making procedures) can bear genuine group duties; these are irreducible and generate member duties derivatively.
3.The tripartite model provides precision that pure normative individualism and pure holism both lack; it identifies exactly which groups can be genuine duty-bearers and which cannot.
∴Collins’s tripartite model shows that some but not all groups bear genuine duties; this is more precise than either individualism (no groups bear duties) or unrestricted holism (all groups bear duties).
Argument IV — The political stakes: correct attribution matters for accountability
1.Political demands for structural reform target collectives (governments, corporations, states); reformulating them as demands on individuals distorts their content and misdirects accountability.
2.Normative individualism that forces such reformulation either trivialises the demands or generates an implausibly diffuse set of individual duties that no one can be held accountable for.
3.Correct attribution of duty — to collectives when collective capacities are at issue — is a prerequisite for effective political accountability and institutional design.
∴The political stakes of normative individualism are concrete: misdirecting obligations to individuals when they belong to collectives weakens accountability and distorts institutional reform demands.

Mr President,

I want to defend methodological individualism not as a philosophical dogma but as a scientific discipline — a requirement that social science specify the mechanisms by which macro-level outcomes are produced through the actions, beliefs, and interactions of individual agents. The opposition to MI typically rests on the claim that higher-level explanations are doing genuine causal work. I want to argue that this claim, when examined carefully, either reduces to the individualist programme or involves what I call a “black box” explanation that is not genuine explanation at all.

“If the social sciences are to make progress, they must study the elementary units of social life: individual actions and their unintended consequences. The only viable alternative to the analytical method is the use of verbal theories, which all too often are mere metaphors that fall apart when subjected to analytic scrutiny. Methodological individualism is not an ideological commitment but a scientific standard.”
i. the black box objection to holistic explanation

Black box — holistic explanation without mechanism is not explanationList and Spiekermann say that the Copenhagen summit failed because of the absence of shared collective interest. This is a redescription, not an explanation. What actually produced the outcome was the behaviour of individual negotiators with specific beliefs about their states’ interests, specific incentive structures, specific information about what others would accept. The “social property” of absent collective interest does not do causal work independently of these individual-level facts; it supervenes on them, as List and Spiekermann themselves concede. To say that the absence of collective interest explains the outcome without specifying the individual-level mechanisms by which that absence was realized is to leave a black box at the centre of the explanation. Black-box explanations are not scientific explanations; they are promissory notes for the real explanation that individualism would provide.

ii. against cohen on functional explanation

Against Cohen — functional explanation requires an independent mechanism theoryCohen’s functional explanations of historical materialism are the most sophisticated challenge I have faced, and I want to give them their due. The Darwinian analogy is initially compelling: Darwin explained adaptation without knowing the mechanism of inheritance. But the analogy fails at a crucial point. Darwin’s explanation was legitimate because we had — independently, before genetics was understood — a theory of the selection mechanism: differential reproduction. We knew that better-adapted organisms reproduced more; this was an independently validated causal story that grounded the functional explanation. Cohen’s historical materialism lacks the analogue. We do not have an independently validated theory of why capitalist production relations persist when and because they develop productive forces — a mechanism of selection operating on social institutions analogous to differential reproduction. Without it, Cohen’s consequence law is an ungrounded correlation, not an explanation.

iii. the multiple realizability argument misfires

Multiple realizability — doesn’t show higher-level properties are causally potentList and Spiekermann invoke multiple realizability to argue that social properties can be causally potent even if they supervene on individual properties. But this argument, as applied to social science, proves too much or too little. It proves too much if it implies that any multiply realizable property is causally potent: the property of “being in a situation where some individual takes an umbrella” is multiply realizable, but we would not say the situation-property is a cause over and above the individual’s action. It proves too little if it only shows that social descriptions can capture generalizations — because capturing a generalization is not the same as identifying a cause. What makes a higher-level property causally potent rather than merely a useful redescription is precisely that there is a mechanism at the individual level by which it operates. Without specifying that mechanism, the multiple-realizability argument is just a defense of useful description, not of genuine higher-level causation.

iv. rational choice as the correct method

Rational choice — the individualist method that delivers genuine explanationsThe alternative I defend is rational choice theory as the methodological framework for social science. By modelling individual agents with preferences, beliefs, and decision procedures, and studying how their strategic interactions produce outcomes, rational choice gives us the mechanism that holistic explanations promise but cannot deliver. Game theory in particular allows us to explain collective action failures, institutional stability, the emergence of norms, and the dynamics of negotiation without invoking irreducible social properties. The Copenhagen summit failure is explained by the game-theoretic structure of the negotiation: the participants were in a game with a non-cooperative equilibrium given the distribution of beliefs and incentives. This is a genuine causal explanation, not a redescription. Holistic sociology, by contrast, identifies patterns and calls them causes; rational choice identifies the mechanisms by which those patterns are produced.

2.Cohen’s historical materialism lacks the analogue: there is no independently validated theory of why social institutions that serve productive development tend to persist.

Mr President,

The case for methodological individualism is both a scientific and a political one. Popper and I arrived at it from different but converging directions: Popper from the logic of scientific explanation and the demand for testability; I from the theory of the price mechanism and the analysis of how market coordination produces order through individual actions that no mind designed or intended. I want to argue that holism — the view that social wholes have explanatory primacy over individuals — is not merely a methodological error but, in its typical forms, a form of intellectual self-deception that systematically misidentifies the sources of social order and the conditions of social knowledge.

i. popper: social institutions are logical constructions from actions

Popper — social institutions are logical constructs; methodological collectivism is untestablePopper’s case for MI in The Poverty of Historicism and The Open Society proceeds from two arguments. First, a logical argument: social entities like “the State,” “the Nation,” and “the Market” are not physical objects or natural kinds; they are institutional arrangements constituted by the behaviours, beliefs, and expectations of individuals. To reify them — to treat them as having properties, interests, or causal powers independent of the individuals who constitute them — is a category error. Second, a methodological argument: holistic theories about social wholes are typically untestable in Popper’s sense, because they invoke entities whose properties cannot be independently specified and cannot be falsified by empirical observation of individual behaviour. MI requires that social explanations cite observable individual actions and their consequences; holism invokes social wholes whose properties are not independently accessible.

ii. hayek: dispersed knowledge and the epistemic case for individualism

Hayek — dispersed knowledge; no mind can hold the information distributed across individualsMy own contribution to MI is different from Popper’s: it comes from the analysis of knowledge. The fundamental fact about social order — about markets, prices, and coordination — is that the knowledge required to produce it is dispersed across millions of individuals, none of whom possesses more than a tiny fraction of the relevant information. The price system works precisely because it aggregates and communicates this dispersed knowledge without any central mind grasping it as a whole. The holist who invokes social properties — “collective interests,” “social structures,” “institutional logics” — is implicitly presupposing that there is some perspective from which these properties are visible and causally operative. But the fundamental insight of individualism is that there is no such perspective. Social phenomena are the unintended consequences of individual actions, not the products of any social mind or social causation operating independently of individuals.

iii. holism and the road to serfdom

Political danger — holism legitimises collective planning and suppression of individual libertyThere is also a political argument that I do not think should be entirely separated from the methodological one. Holism typically generates the conclusion that social wholes have interests, rationality, or purposes of their own that can be invoked to override individual interests and rights. This is the form of reasoning that has historically been used to justify central planning, collectivism, and the suppression of individual liberty in the name of social goods. If social wholes are genuine causal agents with genuine interests, it becomes plausible to say that those interests should be served even at the expense of individuals. MI, by contrast, insists that there are only individual interests, individual choices, and their unintended aggregate consequences. Social goods, if they exist, must be understood as goods for individuals — not as goods of a social entity distinct from its members.

iv. the list/spiekermann reconciliation — a false peace

Against List/Spiekermann — supervenience is all that is conceded; the reconciliation understates MIList and Spiekermann offer a reconciliation: supervenience individualism is true, but higher-level explanations are sometimes mandated. I accept supervenience individualism; of course social facts supervene on individual facts. But the claim that higher-level social explanations are “mandated” when social regularities are robust across individual-level realizations assumes that social regularities are genuine laws rather than descriptions of patterns produced by individual behaviour. On the Hayekian view, social regularities are always the unintended product of individual actions; what makes them robust is not that a social property is causally operating independently but that the individual-level incentive structures and information conditions that produce them are stable. The robustness is individualistic in its ultimate explanation even if the description is social. The reconciliation understates the demands of MI.

Argument I — Popper: social institutions are logical constructs from individual actions
1.Social entities (the State, the Market, the Nation) are not natural kinds or physical objects but institutional arrangements constituted by individual behaviours, beliefs, and expectations.
2.Treating them as having independent properties and causal powers is a category error: reification of logical constructs into natural objects.
3.Holistic theories invoking social wholes are typically untestable: the properties of the social wholes cannot be independently specified or falsified by empirical observation of individual behaviour.
∴Methodological collectivism rests on a category error (reification) and generates untestable theories; MI’s requirement that social explanation cite observable individual actions is both logically and methodologically correct.
Argument II — Hayek: dispersed knowledge means no social property can be causally operative
1.The knowledge required for social coordination is dispersed across millions of individuals; no mind and no social entity grasps it as a whole.
2.Holism invokes social properties (“collective interests,” “structural logics”) that presuppose a perspective from which they are visible and causally operative; the epistemic case for MI shows this perspective does not exist.
3.Social phenomena are unintended consequences of individual actions; they are not produced by social causation operating independently of individuals.
∴Hayek’s analysis of dispersed knowledge provides an epistemic foundation for MI: there is no vantage point from which social properties are visible as causal forces independent of individuals.
Argument III — The political danger: holism legitimises overriding individual interests
1.Holism typically attributes interests, rationality, or purposes to social wholes; this makes it plausible to override individual interests in the name of social goods.
2.This is the reasoning historically used to justify central planning, collectivism, and suppression of liberty; it flows from the ontological mistake of treating social wholes as genuine agents.
3.MI insists that social goods, if they exist, are goods for individuals; social entities have no interests independently of the individuals who constitute them.
∴Holism’s attribution of autonomous interests to social wholes has a political logic that runs toward collectivism; MI’s insistence that only individuals have interests protects against this error.
Argument IV — Against List/Spiekermann: robustness is individually grounded; the reconciliation understates MI
1.List and Spiekermann say social regularities are mandated as higher-level explanations when they are robust across individual-level realizations; but robustness is itself individualistic in its ultimate explanation.
2.What makes a social regularity robust is that the individual-level incentive structures and information conditions that produce it are stable; the robustness is a feature of the individual-level substrate.
3.The reconciliation allows social descriptions to be useful but does not establish that social properties are causally operative independently of individual-level facts; the individual-level explanation remains ultimate.
∴List and Spiekermann’s reconciliation underestimates MI: robustness is itself explained individually; social descriptions capture patterns without identifying causes independent of individual-level processes.

Mr President,

I occupy an unusual position in this debate. With List in our joint book Group Agency I have argued that groups can be genuine agents — can hold beliefs, form intentions, and act — in ways that cannot be reduced to any simple aggregate of their members’ mental states. In this sense I am against pure MI. But I also have reservations about Collins’s account of group duties and about the inference from group agency to group moral responsibility that some holist conclusions require. My position is: groups can be agents, but this does not straightforwardly license the full range of moral attributions that holists want to make.

“A group can display the marks of genuine agency — functional beliefs, intentions, and a capacity for reason-responsive action — without being a substance, without having interests independent of its members, and without being the kind of entity that can be held morally responsible in the way an individual person can. Group agency is real but thin.”
i. group agents are not group substances

Group agents not substances — group agency without group ontologyIn Group Agency List and I argue that a group is a genuine agent if it has a representational system (beliefs about the world), a motivational system (goals), and a capacity for reason-responsive action. These conditions can be met by a group through its decision-making procedures without any member holding the group’s beliefs or intentions individually. A government can believe that interest rates should rise even if no individual minister holds that precise belief; the group’s belief is constituted by the procedure that aggregates individual positions. But crucially: this does not make the group a substance with independent interests or a mind separate from its members. Group agency is a functional achievement of coordinated individual actions, not an ontological entity over and above those individuals. Hayek’s worry about reification is addressed: I am not reifying the group; I am describing a functional organization of individual contributions that displays agentive properties.

ii. against collins: group agency does not automatically generate group duties

Against Collins — agency necessary but not sufficient for moral duty-bearingCollins argues that collectives can be genuine duty-bearers because they have decision-making procedures and institutional capacities. I want to press a distinction she elides: the distinction between having a capacity and having a moral obligation to exercise it. A group agent may have the capacity to prevent harm — a government may be able to provide universal healthcare — without thereby having a moral duty to do so that is irreducible to the duties of its members. The transition from “the group can do X” to “the group ought to do X” requires a moral argument that Collins’s account does not fully supply. Furthermore, moral responsibility requires not only agency but also the right kind of accountability relationship — the capacity for guilt, shame, and reformed behaviour in response to moral criticism. It is not clear that corporate groups display these in the way that genuine moral duty-bearing requires.

iii. virtual control and the individual responsibility residue

Virtual control — group agency is accountable through individual membersEven granting group agency, I think the most defensible account of group moral responsibility runs through what I call virtual control: the idea that group agents are responsible for their actions in virtue of the fact that individual members have — or should have — the capacity to prevent the group from acting wrongly. A group acts wrongly when it could have been redirected by the exercise of individual members’ influence within the group’s procedures. Responsibility, on this view, is distributed: the group is responsible as a whole, but this collective responsibility is grounded in the individuals who have — or failed to exercise — virtual control. This is not a reduction of group responsibility to individual responsibility; it is an account of how group responsibility is anchored in individual-level accountability.

iv. where i agree with elster and where i differ

Pettit vs. Elster — group agency real but consistent with individualist methodologyElster insists that only individual-level rational choice explanation is scientifically legitimate. I disagree with this: groups can display properties — group beliefs, group intentions — that are not simply the aggregates of their members’ mental states, and these group-level properties can figure in genuine explanations. In this I am with List and Spiekermann. But I agree with Elster that the inference from group agency to group moral substance is too fast. The fact that groups display agentive properties does not make them substances with interests independent of their members, does not automatically generate irreducible duties, and does not eliminate the need to ground accountability in individual members’ capacity to influence group behaviour. Group agency is real; group moral substance is not.

Thank you.

Argument I — Group agents are not group substances: functional agency without ontological reification
1.A group is a genuine agent if it has representational and motivational states and a capacity for reason-responsive action constituted through its decision-making procedures.
2.These functional properties can be present without the group being a substance with independent interests; group agency is a functional achievement of individual coordination, not an ontological entity.
3.Acknowledging group agency addresses the explanatory requirements of holism without conceding the ontological reification that Hayek and Popper correctly resist.
∴Group agency is real as a functional property of coordinated individual action; it is not a substance over and above individuals, so accepting group agency is compatible with resisting strong holist ontology.
Argument II — Against Collins: capacity does not automatically generate duty
1.Collins argues that collectives with decision-making procedures and institutional capacities can be genuine duty-bearers; the capacity grounds the duty.
2.But having a capacity to do X does not automatically generate a moral obligation to do X; the transition requires an independent moral argument that Collins does not fully supply.
3.Moral duty-bearing also requires a robust accountability relation — the capacity for guilt, shame, and reformed behaviour — which corporate groups may not display in the relevant sense.
∴Collins’s inference from group capacity to group duty is too fast; the capacity establishes possibility, not obligation, and moral duty-bearing requires accountability conditions that may not be satisfied by all collectives.
Argument III — Virtual control: group responsibility anchored in individual accountability
1.Virtual control: a group agent is responsible for its actions in virtue of the fact that individual members have (or should have) the capacity to prevent the group from acting wrongly through the group’s procedures.
2.This grounds collective responsibility without reducing it to individual responsibility: the group is responsible as a whole, but this is anchored in individual members’ accountability for exercising or failing to exercise virtual control.
3.This account avoids both the holist’s free-floating group responsibility and the individualist’s denial of collective responsibility.
∴Virtual control provides a principled middle position: genuine collective responsibility that is nonetheless anchored in individual members’ accountability, avoiding both holism and pure individualism.
Argument IV — Group agency is real; group moral substance is not
1.Groups can display genuine agentive properties (beliefs, intentions, reason-responsiveness) not reducible to individual aggregates; Elster’s strict MI is wrong to deny this.
2.But group agency does not make groups substances with independent interests; the inference from group agency to group moral substance and irreducible duty is too fast.
3.Accountability must remain anchored in individuals through virtual control; without this anchor, collective responsibility becomes untethered from any mechanism of reform or sanction.
∴Pettit’s position: group agency is real and explanatorily significant; group moral substance is not. This is a principled middle position that respects both the holists’ insights about group properties and the individualists’ concerns about accountability.
