Mr President,

The question before this house is whether rational agents should be modelled as maximisers of expected utility over a single, well-defined, subjective probability distribution. I want to begin by being precise about what this claim actually asserts, because it is frequently misunderstood by the opposition and, I am afraid, sometimes by its own defenders. The claim is not merely a description of how people do behave. It is a normative claim about what rationality requires — about what coherent, non-self-defeating behaviour under uncertainty looks like when properly formalised. My purpose is to show that this normative claim is correct, that the stochastic framework it generates is the right mathematical language for uncertainty, and that the apparent counterexamples the opposition will raise are misdirected.

On what a random variable actually is

Random variable: a function from states of the world to outcomes, not a mysterious entity with intrinsic randomnessLet me begin with the concept of a random variable, because it is the foundation on which everything else rests and it is persistently misunderstood. A random variable is not a thing that is randomly varying in some metaphysically deep sense. It is a function — a mathematical mapping from states of the world to real-number outcomes. If I toss a fair coin, the random variable X might be defined as: X = 1 if the coin lands heads, X = 0 if it lands tails. What makes X a random variable is not that it is intrinsically random — it is that its realised value depends on which state of the world obtains, and we do not yet know which state that is. The probability distribution of X — P(X = 1) = 0.5, P(X = 0) = 0.5 — is our representation of our uncertainty about which state will obtain. This is the key insight: probability is not a property of the world but a property of our epistemic relationship to the world. It is our degree of belief in each possible state. And this is why the stochastic framework is the right framework for reasoning under uncertainty: it is precisely the mathematics of uncertain epistemic states.

On subjective probability and its axiomatic foundations

Subjective probability: coherence axioms — non-negativity, normalisation, countable additivity — uniquely determine a probability measure from rational preferencesThe crucial question is whether a rational agent must have a single, precise probability distribution over all uncertain states, or whether — as the opposition will argue following Knight and Keynes — genuine uncertainty resists probabilistic representation. My answer, which I developed in The Foundations of Statistics, is that a rational agent — one whose preferences over acts satisfy a small number of compelling coherence axioms — must behave as if she has a unique subjective probability distribution over states and a utility function over outcomes, and as if she is maximising the expected value of utility with respect to that distribution. The axioms are: ordering (preferences are complete and transitive), continuity (there is no lexicographic preference), independence (the sure-thing principle: if you prefer act A to act B when state S obtains, and you are indifferent between A and B when state S does not obtain, then you prefer A to B overall), and a few technical conditions. From these axioms, the representation theorem follows: there exist unique probability P and utility u such that act A is preferred to act B if and only if the expected utility of A under P exceeds the expected utility of B under P. The stochastic framework — probability distributions, expected values — is not an external imposition on the theory of choice; it is what rationality itself demands.

On the sure-thing principle and its power

Sure-thing principle: if you prefer A to B regardless of the outcome in one state, this preference should not reverse when that state's probability changesThe most important of my axioms, and the most contested, is what I called the sure-thing principle. Let me state it carefully with an example. Suppose you are choosing between two investments. In the event that the market rises (state S₁), investment A yields £10,000 and investment B yields £8,000, so you prefer A. In the event that the market falls (state S₂), investment A yields £3,000 and investment B yields £3,000 — they are identical. The sure-thing principle says: you should prefer A to B overall, regardless of what probability you assign to S₁ and S₂ rising or falling. The reasoning is direct: A is at least as good as B in every possible state, and strictly better in some state. Any agent who violates this principle can be shown to hold incoherent preferences — preferences that can be exploited to make her accept a series of bets that guarantee her a loss, regardless of which state obtains. This is the Dutch book argument, and it shows that violating the sure-thing principle is not merely irrational in some soft philosophical sense: it is self-defeating in a precise, operational, monetary sense. The stochastic framework is what you must adopt if you wish to avoid being made a fool of by a clever bookmaker.

On the distinction between risk and uncertainty — and why it collapses under scrutiny

Risk vs uncertainty: Knight's distinction collapses — all uncertainty can be represented by a subjective probability distribution for a rational agentThe opposition's star witness will be Frank Knight, who famously distinguished between risk — situations with known probability distributions — and uncertainty — situations where no such distribution is available. I want to argue that this distinction, while intuitive, collapses under rigorous scrutiny. The key question is: what determines whether a probability distribution is "available"? For a frequentist, a probability exists only for repeatable experiments where stable limiting frequencies can be observed. On this view, Knight's uncertainty includes most of the important decisions we face: what will the economy look like in ten years? Will this new drug work? Will this person default on their loan? These are not repeatable experiments with known frequencies. But this just shows that frequentism is the wrong interpretation of probability for decision theory. For a subjectivist — and I am a subjectivist — any coherent degree of belief qualifies as a probability. The decision-maker who faces a genuinely novel situation still has degrees of belief about outcomes — she has some sense of what is more or less likely, even if she cannot point to a historical frequency. My representation theorem shows that any agent whose preferences satisfy the coherence axioms behaves as if she has a unique probability. Knight's "uncertainty" is just risk with an imprecisely specified prior — and the right response to that imprecision is to specify the prior more carefully, not to abandon the probabilistic framework.

On expected utility and why it is the right aggregation rule

Expected utility: EU = Σ p(s) · u(outcome in state s) — the unique aggregation rule consistent with the sure-thing principleLet me explain precisely what expected utility maximisation requires and why it is the right rule. If I have a utility function u over outcomes and a probability distribution P over states, the expected utility of an act A is the sum — or integral, in continuous cases — of u(A(s)) · P(s) over all states s. Expected utility is therefore a probability-weighted average of the utilities of all possible outcomes. The intuition is that this is exactly what the sure-thing principle demands: since I treat each state as independent, and since my valuation of an act in each state is given by u, the natural way to aggregate across states is by probability-weighting. Von Neumann and Morgenstern showed in 1944 that any preference relation over lotteries satisfying their four axioms — completeness, transitivity, continuity, and the independence axiom — has a unique expected utility representation. The uniqueness is up to positive affine transformation: the utility function is unique up to choice of origin and scale, exactly as temperature scales are. This mathematical precision — the exact characterisation of the freedom and the constraints on the utility function — is one of the great achievements of twentieth-century economics.

On probability distributions — discrete versus continuous — and what they represent

Probability distributions: the PMF for discrete variables and the PDF for continuous variables; the CDF unifies both; moments capture key featuresI want to take a moment to explain the mathematical objects we are discussing, because the opposition's critiques often rest on confusing them. A probability distribution is a complete specification of the probabilities associated with each possible value — or range of values — of a random variable. For a discrete random variable — one taking a countable set of values — the distribution is given by a probability mass function: a function f(x) = P(X = x) that assigns a non-negative probability to each value, with all probabilities summing to one. For a continuous random variable — one taking values in an interval — the distribution is given by a probability density function: a non-negative function f(x) such that the probability that X falls in any interval [a, b] is the integral of f from a to b. The cumulative distribution function F(x) = P(X ≤ x) is defined for both discrete and continuous cases and is the most general representation of a distribution. From the distribution, we can derive moments: the mean (expected value), the variance (expected squared deviation from the mean, measuring spread), the skewness (measuring asymmetry), and the kurtosis (measuring the weight of the tails). All of these are properties of the probability distribution — of our representation of uncertainty — not mysterious features of the world itself.

On the Ellsberg paradox and what it actually shows

Ellsberg paradox: violations of SEU by subjects reflect heuristic biases, not a correct alternative theory; the normative force of SEU is unaffectedDaniel Ellsberg famously presented subjects with the following choice. There is an urn containing 30 red balls and 60 balls that are each either black or yellow, in unknown proportions. You are asked to choose between: Bet A (win £100 if red is drawn) and Bet B (win £100 if black is drawn). Most people prefer A. You are then asked to choose between: Bet C (win £100 if red or yellow) and Bet D (win £100 if black or yellow). Most people prefer D. But these preferences are inconsistent with any expected utility representation. If P(red) = 1/3 and you prefer A to B, you must believe P(black) < 1/3. But then P(yellow) > 1/3, which means P(red or yellow) > P(black or yellow), so you should prefer C to D, not D to C. The Ellsberg paradox shows that people systematically violate expected utility when facing ambiguity about probabilities. But I contend this shows that people are making an error — specifically, the error of treating ambiguity about the probability as a reason to shift preferences in a way that cannot be coherently represented. The normative force of my representation theorem is unaffected by descriptive violations. People also violate transitivity, fail to update on evidence correctly, and are susceptible to framing effects — none of this shows that transitivity or Bayesian updating are the wrong norms. The opposition must show that there is a coherent alternative to expected utility that does not violate some equally compelling rationality requirement. They have not done so, and I believe they cannot.

Mr President,

Savage has established the philosophical and axiomatic foundations of the stochastic framework. I want to bring us from the abstract to the concrete and show how these foundations cash out in the most powerful and beautiful body of mathematical theory in the history of economics: the continuous-time stochastic calculus of financial markets. My argument is that Brownian motion — the stochastic process at the heart of modern finance — is not merely a convenient approximation but the mathematically correct model for the uncertainty faced by agents in liquid financial markets, and that the machinery built upon it — Itô's calculus, the Black-Scholes-Merton model, the theory of stochastic differential equations — represents the correct application of the stochastic framework to economic decision-making.

On the random walk and its continuous-time limit

Random walk: discrete steps with equal probability of up/down; in the limit as steps become infinitesimal, converges to Brownian motionLet me build Brownian motion from first principles, because understanding its construction is crucial to understanding what it represents. Consider a particle that at each time step moves either up by Δx with probability 1/2 or down by Δx with probability 1/2. This is the symmetric random walk. The position after n steps is a random variable X_n = Δx · (U₁ + U₂ + ... + Uₙ), where each Uᵢ is +1 or -1 with equal probability. By the Central Limit Theorem — one of the most important results in probability theory — as n grows large with the right scaling, the distribution of X_n converges to a normal distribution. Specifically, if we let the time step Δt → 0 and the step size Δx = σ√Δt for some constant σ, then as we take increasingly fine steps over a fixed time interval [0, T], the random walk converges in distribution to a continuous-time process: standard Brownian motion, also called a Wiener process. This is the mathematical construction, and it tells us precisely what Brownian motion is: it is the limit of symmetric random walks as the time step becomes infinitesimally small. It is the stochastic process that emerges when uncertainty accumulates continuously through time.

On the key properties of Brownian motion

Brownian motion W(t): starts at 0; independent increments; increments normally distributed with mean 0 and variance t; continuous but nowhere differentiable pathsStandard Brownian motion W(t) has four defining properties that make it the canonical stochastic process for finance and economics. First, W(0) = 0 — it starts at zero. Second, it has independent increments: for any times 0 ≤ s < t, the increment W(t) − W(s) is independent of the history W(u) for all u ≤ s. This property captures the idea that the future evolution of the process is not predictable from its past — the memory-less property of markets in their simplest form. Third, the increments are normally distributed: W(t) − W(s) ~ N(0, t−s), meaning the increment over any time interval has mean zero and variance equal to the length of the interval. The variance growing linearly with time is characteristic of diffusion processes. Fourth, the paths of W(t) are almost surely continuous — no sudden jumps — but they are also almost surely nowhere differentiable. This last property is crucial and surprising: a Brownian motion path is continuous at every point but has no slope at any point. It is infinitely jagged at every scale. This mathematical fact has profound implications for how we do calculus with these processes.

On Itô's lemma and stochastic calculus

Itô's lemma: the chain rule for stochastic processes; dF = (∂F/∂t + μ∂F/∂X + ½σ²∂²F/∂X²)dt + σ∂F/∂X dW — the extra ½σ²∂²F/∂X² term arises from the quadratic variation of Brownian motionBecause Brownian motion paths are nowhere differentiable, ordinary calculus cannot be applied to them. We need a new calculus — stochastic calculus — developed by the Japanese mathematician Kiyoshi Itô in the 1940s. The key result is Itô's lemma, which is the stochastic analogue of the chain rule. Suppose X(t) is a stochastic process satisfying a stochastic differential equation: dX = μ dt + σ dW, where μ is the drift (the deterministic trend), σ is the volatility (the scale of the random fluctuations), and dW is the Brownian motion increment. If F(X, t) is a smooth function of X and t, then by ordinary calculus, dF = (∂F/∂t) dt + (∂F/∂X) dX. But with a stochastic process, there is an extra term: dF = (∂F/∂t + μ·∂F/∂X + ½σ²·∂²F/∂X²) dt + σ·∂F/∂X dW. The extra term ½σ²·∂²F/∂X² arises from the fact that Brownian motion has non-zero quadratic variation — the squares of its increments accumulate to a finite quantity, unlike ordinary smooth functions. This is the mathematical signature of genuine stochastic uncertainty, and it fundamentally changes how the dynamics of functions of stochastic processes work. In finance, this term is why option pricing is a second-order partial differential equation problem, not a first-order one.

On geometric Brownian motion and the log-normal distribution of asset prices

Geometric Brownian motion: dS = μS dt + σS dW; by Itô's lemma, log(S) follows arithmetic Brownian motion; returns are normally distributed; prices are log-normally distributedThe standard model for asset prices in continuous time is geometric Brownian motion (GBM): dS = μS dt + σS dW, where S is the asset price, μ is the expected return (drift), and σ is the volatility. This says that the instantaneous change in price dS is proportional to the current price S — a percentage-return model, which is natural since a £1 move in a £10 stock means something very different from a £1 move in a £1000 stock. By applying Itô's lemma to F = log(S), we find: d(log S) = (μ − ½σ²) dt + σ dW. This is arithmetic Brownian motion with drift — a process whose increments are normally distributed. This means that log returns — the natural logarithm of price relatives — are normally distributed: log(S(t)/S(0)) ~ N((μ − ½σ²)t, σ²t). Consequently, the price level S(t) has a log-normal distribution: the logarithm of a log-normal random variable is normal. The log-normal distribution is the correct distributional assumption for asset prices given the GBM model, and it has several practically important properties: prices are always positive (since e^x > 0 for all x), the distribution is right-skewed (large gains are possible but large proportional losses are bounded by zero), and the volatility parameter σ has a clear interpretation as the standard deviation of log returns per unit time.

On the Black-Scholes-Merton model and dynamic hedging

Black-Scholes-Merton: under GBM, a continuously rebalanced hedge portfolio eliminates all risk; the option price satisfies a PDE; the formula C = S·N(d₁) − K·e^{−rT}·N(d₂)The crowning achievement of the stochastic framework in finance is the Black-Scholes-Merton (BSM) option pricing model of 1973. A European call option gives its holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy an asset at price K (the strike price) at time T (the expiration). The value of this option at expiry is max(S(T) − K, 0): positive if the price ends above the strike, zero otherwise. The question is: what is the fair price of this option today? Black, Scholes, and Merton showed that under the GBM model, you can create a portfolio consisting of the option and a continuously adjusted position in the underlying asset that is perfectly risk-free — all the randomness cancels out. This portfolio must therefore earn the risk-free rate r. Setting up and solving the resulting partial differential equation using Itô's lemma gives the celebrated Black-Scholes formula: C = S·N(d₁) − K·e^{−rT}·N(d₂), where N(·) is the cumulative normal distribution function, d₁ = (log(S/K) + (r + ½σ²)T) / (σ√T), and d₂ = d₁ − σ√T. This formula is remarkable for what it does not require: it does not depend on the expected return μ of the asset, only on its volatility σ and the risk-free rate r. The stochastic framework here is doing real work: the GBM model, Itô's calculus, and the no-arbitrage argument together determine a unique, preference-free price for the option.

On stochastic differential equations and the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process

Stochastic differential equations: dX = μ(X,t)dt + σ(X,t)dW; the O-U process models mean-reversion; interest rates, volatility, and commodity prices follow O-U dynamicsBeyond GBM, the broader theory of stochastic differential equations (SDEs) gives us a rich palette of stochastic processes for modelling different aspects of economic and financial uncertainty. A general SDE takes the form dX = μ(X, t) dt + σ(X, t) dW, where the drift μ and diffusion coefficient σ may themselves depend on X and t. One of the most important examples is the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (O-U) process: dX = θ(m − X) dt + σ dW, where m is the long-run mean and θ is the speed of mean-reversion. The O-U process is the continuous-time analogue of a first-order autoregressive process (AR(1)) in discrete time. It has the crucial property that the drift pulls X back toward m whenever X deviates from it — mean-reverting behaviour that is appropriate for modelling interest rates (the Vasicek model), commodity prices (which revert to marginal cost), and volatility (which is clustered but mean-reverting). The key point for our purposes is that the stochastic framework — SDEs driven by Brownian motion — is not limited to the GBM model; it encompasses a wide family of processes with different properties, making it a genuinely flexible and powerful framework for modelling economic dynamics under uncertainty.

On the Central Limit Theorem and why the normal distribution is not merely an assumption

CLT: the sum of n i.i.d. random variables with mean μ and variance σ² converges to N(nμ, nσ²) as n→∞ — normality emerges from aggregation, not assumptionA common objection to the stochastic framework in finance is that it assumes normally distributed returns, and that this assumption is violated in practice — which the opposition, particularly Taleb, will press forcefully. I want to defuse this objection at its source by explaining why normality arises and when it is and is not appropriate. The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that if X₁, X₂, ..., Xₙ are independent, identically distributed random variables with mean μ and finite variance σ², then the standardised sum (X₁ + ... + Xₙ − nμ) / (σ√n) converges in distribution to the standard normal N(0, 1) as n → ∞. The CLT is the reason that aggregate quantities — the sum of many small, independent shocks — are approximately normally distributed, regardless of the distribution of the individual shocks. This is not an assumption; it is a theorem. The reason asset returns are approximately normal at moderate timescales is that each return is the aggregate of thousands of small, partially independent price movements over the period — exactly the conditions the CLT requires. The breakdown of normality that Taleb observes at very short timescales (where individual large trades dominate) and at distributional extremes (the tails) is not a refutation of the CLT — it is precisely what the CLT predicts: normality is an approximation that improves as the number of aggregated components grows. The appropriate response is to model fat tails explicitly, using distributions with infinite variance, not to abandon the stochastic framework.

Savage has given us the philosophical foundations of the stochastic framework and Samuelson has shown us its mathematical machinery. I want to show how this framework delivers its most striking empirical prediction: the Efficient Market Hypothesis. My argument is that the stochastic structure of asset prices is not merely a convenient mathematical model but a deep consequence of how information is processed by rational agents in competitive markets — and that the empirical evidence, properly interpreted, overwhelmingly supports this view.

On what efficient markets actually claim

EMH: prices are sufficient statistics for available information; three forms — weak, semi-strong, strong — correspond to different information setsThe Efficient Market Hypothesis, as I have defined it, states that security prices fully reflect all available information. This is not a single claim but a family of claims distinguished by what counts as "available." In the weak form, prices reflect all information contained in past prices — so technical analysis, which attempts to predict future prices from past price patterns, should yield no abnormal profits. In the semi-strong form, prices reflect all publicly available information — so fundamental analysis based on public information about earnings, dividends, and macroeconomic conditions should also yield no abnormal profits above what is fair compensation for risk. In the strong form, prices reflect all information, including private information — even insiders cannot earn abnormal profits by trading on information not yet in prices. The weak and semi-strong forms have been extensively tested and the evidence is, for the most part, highly supportive. The strong form is the most controversial, but even here the evidence is largely consistent with efficiency. The key insight is that efficiency is a property of the information processing done by the market as a whole — a stochastic equilibrium outcome — not a property of any individual agent's sophistication.

On the random walk model and its stochastic content

Random walk: price changes are i.i.d.; E[P(t+1)|P(t), P(t-1),...] = P(t); this is the stochastic content of weak-form efficiencyThe stochastic content of weak-form efficiency is the random walk model. A stochastic process P(t) is a random walk if its increments ΔP(t) = P(t) − P(t−1) are independently and identically distributed — i.i.d. — with mean zero. The random walk property says: the best forecast of tomorrow's price is today's price; price changes are unpredictable from their own history. In formal terms: E[P(t+1) | P(t), P(t−1), ..., P(0)] = P(t). This is mathematically a martingale — a stochastic process whose expected future value, given the past, equals its current value. The martingale property is the precise stochastic formulation of the intuition that in an efficient market, there are no free lunches — no strategy based on past prices can generate positive expected returns above the risk-free rate. The random walk hypothesis implies that price changes follow a white noise process: they are serially uncorrelated, their autocovariances are zero at all lags, and the spectral density is flat — no frequency in the historical record is predictable. Extensive testing of stock return autocorrelations has consistently found near-zero values, strongly supporting the random walk hypothesis.

On why rational agents produce the random walk

Rational arbitrage: if prices were predictable, arbitrageurs would exploit the pattern until it disappeared; competition drives prices to the efficient equilibriumThe random walk is not merely an empirical observation; it has a compelling theoretical explanation grounded in rational, utility-maximising behaviour. If tomorrow's price were systematically predictable from today's — if, say, prices always rose on Mondays — then rational, profit-seeking traders would buy on Sundays and sell on Mondays, driving Sunday prices up and Monday prices down until the pattern disappeared. Any systematic, exploitable pattern in prices will be arbitraged away by profit-seeking agents. Competition among rational agents is the mechanism that enforces efficiency. The stochastic residual — the unpredictable component of price changes — represents the genuine uncertainty that remains after all predictable patterns have been exploited. This residual is the true stochastic noise, the realisation of the probability distribution of fundamentals that could not have been predicted in advance. It is not a deficiency of the model; it is the model's prediction of what must remain after rational agents have done their work.

On stochastic dominance and portfolio theory

Stochastic dominance: first-order (distribution A shifts all probability rightward vs B) and second-order (A has the same mean but less spread than B) — conditions under which all expected utility maximisers prefer one lottery over anotherWithin the stochastic framework, a particularly powerful tool for comparing risky investments without specifying the exact utility function is the concept of stochastic dominance. Investment A first-order stochastically dominates investment B if F_A(x) ≤ F_B(x) for all x — where F denotes the cumulative distribution function — with strict inequality for some x. This means: for every possible threshold x, the probability that A yields at most x is no greater than the probability that B yields at most x. In other words, A's distribution is shifted to the right of B's. The critical result is: A first-order stochastically dominates B if and only if every expected utility maximiser with a non-decreasing utility function (i.e., every agent who prefers more to less) prefers A to B, regardless of the specific shape of their utility function. Second-order stochastic dominance is weaker: A second-order dominates B if ∫₋∞ˣ F_A(t) dt ≤ ∫₋∞ˣ F_B(t) dt for all x — A has less probability mass in the lower tail. A second-order dominates B if and only if every risk-averse expected utility maximiser prefers A to B. These dominance concepts are extremely powerful because they allow investment comparisons without knowing the precise utility function — they identify investments that are unambiguously better for broad classes of rational investors.

On the equity premium and the link between stochastic risk and expected return

Risk premium: E[R] = r_f + β·(E[R_M]−r_f); the CAPM shows that only systematic stochastic risk — covariance with the market — commands a premium; idiosyncratic risk is diversifiableA core prediction of the stochastic expected utility framework is that investors must be compensated for bearing risk — specifically, for the stochastic variation in their portfolio returns. But not all risk commands a premium: only undiversifiable, systematic risk does. The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), which follows from mean-variance portfolio theory and the expected utility framework, shows that the expected return on any asset equals the risk-free rate plus a risk premium proportional to the asset's beta — its covariance with the overall market portfolio divided by the market's variance: E[R] = r_f + β·(E[R_M] − r_f). Beta measures the systematic stochastic exposure of the asset — how much of the market's random variation it amplifies. An asset with β = 2 moves, on average, twice as much as the market in both directions; it commands twice the market risk premium. An asset with β = 0 has no systematic exposure; its expected return equals the risk-free rate. The CAPM tells us that stochastic variability per se is not what matters for expected returns — only the covariance structure of returns with the market matters. This is because investors can eliminate idiosyncratic (asset-specific) risk by diversifying; they cannot eliminate market-wide risk. The logic is clean: the market prices only the risk you are forced to bear.

On the joint hypothesis problem and the interpretation of anomalies

Joint hypothesis problem: testing EMH requires a model of equilibrium returns; apparent anomalies may reflect mis-specified risk premia, not inefficiencyAny test of market efficiency is a joint test of two hypotheses: that markets are efficient and that the model used to measure "normal" returns is correct. This is the joint hypothesis problem, and it has a crucial implication: any apparent anomaly — any pattern of returns that seems to violate efficiency — could reflect either genuine inefficiency (prices not fully incorporating information) or a mis-specified model of normal returns (risk factors not properly measured). Consider the value premium — the observation that stocks with high book-to-market ratios earn higher average returns. This is often cited as an anomaly that refutes the CAPM-based form of the EMH. But the correct interpretation may be that high book-to-market stocks are simply riskier in ways the CAPM does not fully capture — they tend to be financially distressed companies whose returns are highly correlated with bad economic times, exactly the states in which consumption-based utility is high. The Fama-French three-factor model adds value and size factors to the CAPM, capturing these additional risk dimensions. The apparent anomaly disappears once risk is properly measured. This is the systematic lesson: most supposed violations of the EMH vanish once we use a richer stochastic model of risk that better captures the relevant dimensions of uncertainty.

On conditional versus unconditional distributions and time-varying volatility

GARCH: conditional variance σ²(t) = ω + α·ε²(t-1) + β·σ²(t-1); volatility clustering is not a violation of efficiency but a feature of the correct stochastic model of the conditional distributionA common empirical observation is that financial returns exhibit volatility clustering: periods of high volatility tend to be followed by periods of high volatility, and vice versa. This is sometimes presented as a violation of the i.i.d. random walk model and therefore of the efficient markets framework. But it is no such thing. Efficiency requires that price changes are unforecastable — that expected returns are unpredictable. It does not require that the variance of returns is constant over time. The correct stochastic model for time-varying volatility is the GARCH family, pioneered by Engle and Bollerslev. In the GARCH(1,1) model, the conditional variance evolves as: σ²(t) = ω + α·ε²(t−1) + β·σ²(t−1), where ε(t) = σ(t)·z(t) with z(t) i.i.d. N(0,1). Here, σ²(t) is the variance of this period's return conditional on the past, and it depends on last period's squared shock and last period's variance. The unconditional distribution of returns under GARCH has fatter tails than the normal — precisely what Taleb and Mandelbrot observe — but this is a property of the stochastic model, not a refutation of it. GARCH shows that the stochastic framework, properly extended, can accommodate empirical facts about volatility clustering and fat tails that the simplest GBM model misses.

Mr President,

The proposition has presented us with an elegant, mathematically coherent framework and argued that it is the correct — indeed the only — rational approach to uncertainty in economic life. I want to challenge this at its foundations. The stochastic framework, however mathematically beautiful, rests on a conceptual confusion between two fundamentally different situations: risk, where probabilities can be meaningfully assigned, and genuine uncertainty, where they cannot. Most of the important decisions in economic life belong to the second category. And treating genuine uncertainty as if it were merely imprecisely specified risk — as Savage recommends — does not resolve the distinction; it papers over it.

On the fundamental distinction between risk and uncertainty

Risk vs uncertainty: risk has a known probability distribution; uncertainty does not — the distinction is not about precision but about whether a probability exists at allThe distinction I want to draw is not merely one of precision. It is not that in risky situations we know the probability exactly while in uncertain situations we know it only approximately. The distinction is more radical: in genuinely uncertain situations, there is no probability distribution to be known, even approximately, because the situation is sui generis — it has never occurred before and is not a member of a well-defined reference class of repeatable events. Consider the question that every entrepreneur must answer: will this new business succeed? This is not a question like "will this fair coin land heads?" The coin flip is a member of a well-defined repeatable experiment with a known, stable frequency. The new business is a unique event. It is not one of a large number of similar businesses; its chances of success depend on a specific configuration of technology, consumer preferences, competitive conditions, managerial talent, and luck that has never existed in precisely this form before. What probability does a rational entrepreneur assign to its success? Any number she assigns is not a frequency — it is a guess, a hunch, a hope. It is not a probability in any rigorous sense. Savage's response — that any coherent degree of belief counts as a probability — is to change the subject. It is to say: call your guess a probability. But calling it a probability does not make it tractable in the way that a genuine frequency-based probability is tractable, and it does not give the decision-maker the precision and stability that the stochastic framework's machinery requires.

On the nature of entrepreneurial profit — it exists because of genuine uncertainty

Entrepreneurial profit: Knight's theory — pure profit is the return to bearing genuine uncertainty; if all uncertainty were risk, competition would eliminate profit above the risk premiumMy deeper point — the one that motivates the distinction in my 1921 book — is that genuine uncertainty is not merely an epistemic inconvenience. It is the source of entrepreneurial profit in a competitive economy. Here is the argument. If all uncertainty were risk — if every economic event had a well-defined probability distribution — then insurance markets would price every risk at its actuarially fair value. Every entrepreneur who faced a risky venture could insure against adverse outcomes at a fair price. Competition would drive the expected return from any venture down to the risk-adjusted rate of return that compensates for bearing risk. There would be no pure profit — no return above what is required to compensate for bearing the quantifiable risk. But we observe persistent entrepreneurial profit in real economies. Where does it come from? It comes from the fact that some entrepreneurs are better than others at navigating genuine uncertainty — situations where no probability distribution exists and where no insurance is possible. The entrepreneur who builds a successful new business does not merely bear a quantifiable risk; she makes correct judgments under genuine uncertainty — judgments that cannot be expressed as probability-weighted expectations because there is no probability to weight with. Pure profit is the reward for correct judgment under irreducible uncertainty. The stochastic framework cannot account for this: if all uncertainty were risk, pure profit would be impossible.

On Keynes's weight of evidence and the inadequacy of a single probability

Weight of evidence: Keynes — the weight of the probability is distinct from its magnitude; a probability based on little evidence is less reliable than one based on much, even if numerically identicalKeynes, writing in the same year as me, made a complementary observation about the inadequacy of representing all uncertainty by a single probability. He introduced the concept of the weight of evidence: the total amount of evidence on which a probability judgment is based. Two probability judgments can have the same numerical value — both equal to 0.5 — but very different weights. The probability that a fair coin lands heads is 0.5, and it is based on rich statistical evidence about coins and physical symmetry. The probability that a novel medical treatment works might also be assessed at 0.5, but based on one small, preliminary trial. Both are "0.5" on Savage's view — coherent degrees of belief — but they are very different epistemic states. The one based on thin evidence is much more vulnerable to revision, much less reliable as a basis for action, and warrants much greater caution. Savage's framework has no way of representing this difference: all that matters is the number, not the evidence base on which it rests. A decision theory for bounded, real agents must capture the weight of evidence, not just its direction.

On the animal spirits of investment and the limits of probabilistic forecasting

Animal spirits: Keynes — most long-run investment decisions are driven by conventions and animal spirits, not probability calculations, because the future is genuinely unknowableIn my General Theory, Keynes took the point about genuine uncertainty further into the theory of investment and macroeconomics. The decision to invest in a long-lived capital project — to build a factory, to lay a railway, to develop a new drug — requires a forecast of returns over decades. No probability distribution over such distant, complex futures is available. The most honest statement is: we do not know. What actually drives investment in practice is what Keynes called animal spirits — the spontaneous urge to action rather than inaction, sustained by conventional expectations about the future. Investors follow conventions — they assume the present situation will continue, they follow what others are doing, they respond to sentiment — not because they are irrational but because they have no better basis for their decisions. When conventions shift — when confidence collapses or surges — investment can change catastrophically and quickly, not because new information has arrived but because the fragile conventional expectations that were sustaining investment have broken down. The stochastic framework cannot model this: it assumes stable probability distributions over outcomes, but Keynesian genuine uncertainty means that the distribution is itself unstable and convention-dependent.

On the difference between first-order and second-order uncertainty

Second-order uncertainty: uncertainty about the probability distribution itself; model uncertainty is irreducibly distinct from the first-order uncertainty within a fixed modelThe proposition acknowledges that the stochastic framework can be extended — GARCH for time-varying volatility, jump-diffusion for fat tails, and so on. But this extension only addresses first-order uncertainty — uncertainty about outcomes within a given model. It does not address second-order uncertainty — uncertainty about which model is correct. A bank that uses a GARCH model to measure its risk has a precise probability distribution over tomorrow's returns conditional on the GARCH model being the right model. But how confident should it be in the GARCH model itself? This is a question no stochastic model can answer from within the stochastic framework, because it requires taking a probability distribution over probability models — a second-order distribution. And any second-order distribution faces the same problem at the third order, and so on. The stochastic framework generates an infinite regress of model uncertainty that it cannot resolve. The correct response is not to go further up the hierarchy but to acknowledge that genuine uncertainty exists and to use decision rules that are robust to it — exactly what the opposition recommends and what the proposition's framework cannot accommodate.

On the coordination failures generated by uniform stochastic modelling

Coordination failure: when all agents use the same stochastic model, they create correlated exposures; diversification fails precisely when it is most neededThere is a further, practical objection to the stochastic framework that the proposition has not addressed: the problem of coordination failure when all agents use the same models. The efficient markets framework and the GBM model are not merely theories observed from outside the market — they are used by the agents who make up the market. When every major bank, every hedge fund, and every asset manager uses Black-Scholes to price options and VaR (Value at Risk) to measure risk, they create correlated exposures that are not in any individual's model. Each agent's stochastic model tells her that her risk is well-diversified. But all the models are making the same distributional assumptions, and all the agents are holding similar positions. When a large shock arrives — one that the shared stochastic model assigns low probability — all agents rebalance in the same direction simultaneously. The diversification that the model promised does not materialise, because diversification is only possible when agents hold different beliefs and take offsetting positions. Uniform stochastic modelling destroys the diversity of opinion that makes diversification possible.

On the practical implications of genuine uncertainty for economic decision-making

Practical implications: maximin, precautionary principles, and robustness as decision rules under genuine uncertainty — not expected utilityIf genuine uncertainty is irreducible — if Knight's distinction holds — what should decision-makers do? The answer is not to pretend that they have a probability distribution and to maximise expected utility. It is to use decision rules that are robust to uncertainty about the distribution. Several such rules have been developed. Maximin — choosing the action with the best worst-case outcome — is appropriate when the stakes are very high and the potential for catastrophic outcomes is significant. The precautionary principle — avoiding actions whose potential consequences are severe and irreversible, even when the probability of those consequences cannot be quantified — is a similar heuristic for genuine uncertainty. Minimax regret — choosing the action that minimises the worst-case regret you might feel for having made the wrong choice — is another. None of these requires a probability distribution over states. All are more appropriate than expected utility maximisation when the stochastic framework is inapplicable. The proposition has given us a powerful theory for the narrow class of situations where genuine risk prevails. For the much larger class of situations where genuine uncertainty prevails — entrepreneurial decisions, long-run investment, novel policy, technological development — the stochastic framework is the wrong tool, and insisting on its use is not rigour but scientism.

On the structure of prospect theory

Prospect theory: value is defined over gains and losses relative to a reference point, not absolute wealth; the value function is S-shaped — concave for gains, convex for losses, steeper for losses (loss aversion)Amos Tversky and I proposed Prospect Theory in 1979 as a descriptive replacement for expected utility theory. The theory has four key components, each of which represents a systematic departure from the stochastic expected utility framework. First, outcomes are evaluated as gains and losses relative to a reference point — typically the status quo — not as absolute wealth levels. The reference point is a random variable that depends on context and can shift. Second, the value function v(x) is S-shaped: it is concave in the domain of gains (diminishing sensitivity to gains), convex in the domain of losses (diminishing sensitivity to losses), and steeper for losses than for gains. The asymmetry between losses and gains is loss aversion: empirically, losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. This means that a symmetric bet — win £100 or lose £100 with equal probability — feels bad to most people; the pain of losing £100 outweighs the pleasure of gaining £100. Third, probabilities are not used at face value but are transformed by a probability weighting function w(p): small probabilities are overweighted (which is why people buy lottery tickets and insurance), and large probabilities are underweighted. Fourth, the overall value of a prospect is computed not as Σ p(x)·u(x) but as Σ w(p(x))·v(x−reference point). This is mathematically different from expected utility in every dimension.

On the probability weighting function and its implications

Probability weighting: w(p) is an inverse S-shaped transformation of probability; w(0)=0, w(1)=1, but w overweights small p and underweights large p — this explains the coexistence of gambling and insuranceThe probability weighting function w(p) is one of the most important and counterintuitive features of Prospect Theory. The function is not the identity — w(p) ≠ p — but a distorted version of probability that is inverse-S-shaped: it is higher than the 45-degree line for small probabilities (overweighting) and lower than the 45-degree line for large probabilities (underweighting). The crossover point is typically around p = 0.3–0.4. This distortion has dramatic implications. Consider a lottery that pays £1,000,000 with probability 0.001. The expected value is £1,000 — not much. But many people pay more than £1,000 for such a ticket, because they overweight the tiny probability of winning. Now consider insurance against a loss of £1,000,000 with probability 0.001. The actuarially fair premium is again £1,000. Many people pay more than this for insurance, because — again — they overweight the small probability of the bad outcome. The stochastic expected utility framework, with its linear probability weighting, cannot simultaneously explain why people buy both lotteries and insurance: a risk-averse expected utility maximiser should buy insurance but not lottery tickets; a risk-seeking one should buy lottery tickets but not insurance. Only probability weighting — overweighting small probabilities in both tails — can explain the coexistence of gambling and insurance-buying in the same agent. This is a fundamental prediction of Prospect Theory that expected utility theory cannot make.

On the certainty effect and the Allais paradox

Allais paradox: systematic violation of the independence axiom; people overweight outcomes that are certain relative to probable ones — the certainty effectMaurice Allais demonstrated in 1953 what is now called the Allais paradox, a systematic violation of the independence axiom — the cornerstone of expected utility theory. Consider two choice problems. Problem 1: choose between A (£1M for certain) and B (£5M with probability 0.1, £1M with probability 0.89, £0 with probability 0.01). Most people choose A. Problem 2: choose between C (£1M with probability 0.11, £0 with probability 0.89) and D (£5M with probability 0.1, £0 with probability 0.9). Most people choose D. These choices are inconsistent with expected utility. If you prefer A to B, then u(1M) > 0.1·u(5M) + 0.89·u(1M) + 0.01·u(0), which simplifies to 0.11·u(1M) > 0.1·u(5M) + 0.01·u(0). But this is exactly the condition for preferring C to D in Problem 2 — yet people prefer D to C. The inconsistency arises from the certainty effect: people give extra weight to outcomes that are certain, compared to outcomes that are merely probable. The certain £1M in Problem 1 is more attractive than its expected utility weight would justify. This is not a random error; it is a systematic feature of how people process probability, and it is correctly captured by Prospect Theory's probability weighting but not by expected utility.

On the framing effect and reference dependence

Framing: the same objective probability distribution presented differently generates different choices; expected utility theory predicts invariance, but subjects reliably switchOne of the most robust and disturbing findings in the psychology of decision-making is the framing effect: the same objective decision problem, presented in different frames, generates systematically different choices. In a classic experiment, we asked subjects to choose between two medical programmes to combat a disease expected to kill 600 people. In the positive frame: Programme A saves 200 people for certain; Programme B saves 600 with probability 1/3 and nobody with probability 2/3. Most people chose A. In the negative frame: Programme C leads to 400 deaths for certain; Programme D leads to no deaths with probability 1/3 and 600 deaths with probability 2/3. Most people chose D. But A and C are objectively identical — both result in 200 survivors and 400 deaths — and B and D are objectively identical. The stochastic expected utility framework predicts invariance: since the probability distributions over outcomes are the same in both frames, the choice should be the same. But subjects reliably switch from the risk-averse choice (A) to the risk-seeking choice (D) when the frame shifts from gains to losses. This shows that what matters to decision-makers is not the objective probability distribution but how the outcomes are described relative to a reference point — exactly what Prospect Theory's reference dependence captures and expected utility theory cannot.

On heuristics, biases, and System 1 versus System 2 thinking

System 1 vs System 2: fast, automatic, heuristic-based vs slow, deliberate, rule-based — most real decisions are made by System 1, which systematically violates expected utilityThe deeper explanation for why agents systematically violate expected utility comes from the psychology of thinking itself. In my later work with Thaler and others, I have developed the distinction between System 1 and System 2 thinking. System 2 is the slow, deliberate, analytically sophisticated process that could in principle apply the rules of expected utility theory. System 1 is the fast, automatic, heuristic-based process that makes most real decisions — including most financial decisions — under time pressure, cognitive load, and emotional activation. System 1 uses rules of thumb — availability (judging probability by how easily examples come to mind), representativeness (judging probability by resemblance to a prototype), and anchoring (adjusting insufficiently from an initial anchor value). These heuristics are fast and often adequate, but they systematically violate the axioms of the stochastic expected utility framework. The availability heuristic, for instance, leads to overweighting of salient, recently-experienced events — dramatic plane crashes are overweighted relative to their actual frequency; slow-burning risks like radon gas are underweighted. These are not random errors; they are directional biases that are predictable from the cognitive architecture of the human decision-maker and that produce systematic, correctable departures from the stochastic framework's predictions.

On mental accounting and the disposition effect in financial markets

Disposition effect: investors hold losing stocks too long and sell winners too early — a direct consequence of loss aversion and the S-shaped value function, not of rational expected utilityThe financial markets implications of Prospect Theory are not merely theoretical. The disposition effect — the well-documented tendency of investors to sell winning stocks too quickly and hold losing stocks too long — is a direct prediction of the theory and a clear violation of expected utility maximisation. Consider an investor holding a stock that has risen in value. Under Prospect Theory, the gain brings her into the concave region of the value function, where additional gains generate diminishing marginal value. She is now risk-averse in the gains domain — she prefers to lock in the gain rather than risk it for potentially larger gains. The rational response under expected utility would be to continue holding if the stock's future prospects still exceed the risk-adjusted hurdle rate. But the Prospect Theory agent locks in gains prematurely. Conversely, a stock that has fallen puts the investor in the convex region of the value function — the domain of losses, where she is risk-seeking. She now prefers to gamble on recovery rather than crystallise the loss. The rational response under expected utility would be to cut losses if the stock's fundamentals have deteriorated. But the Prospect Theory agent holds on, gambling on recovery. This asymmetry — selling winners too early, holding losers too long — has been documented in every major stock market in the world. It is not a random noise; it is a systematic consequence of the S-shaped value function and loss aversion, and it creates exploitable price patterns that the efficient market hypothesis predicts cannot exist.

On why Prospect Theory matters for market prices, not just individual behaviour

Market-level effects: when many agents follow Prospect Theory, aggregate prices reflect the biases; momentum, reversals, and volatility smiles are Prospect Theory predictions that EMH cannot explainThe proposition will argue that even if individual agents are biased, rational arbitrageurs will correct prices and restore efficiency. I want to challenge this argument directly. First, there are limits to arbitrage — the costs and risks of maintaining a position against irrational pricing can exceed the profits of exploiting the mispricing, particularly when the position must be held for an uncertain and potentially long period. Second, and more importantly, when the biases are systematic and correlated across agents — as they are under Prospect Theory — rational agents are a minority and cannot move prices sufficiently to eliminate the biases. The empirical consequences are real and large. The momentum effect — stocks that have risen recently continue to rise — is consistent with under-reaction to new information, which Prospect Theory (with its reluctance to realise losses) predicts. The reversal effect — long-term winners become long-term losers — is consistent with over-reaction driven by availability bias. The volatility smile in options markets — implied volatility is higher for out-of-the-money options than GBM predicts — is consistent with Prospect Theory's probability overweighting of extreme outcomes. These are not coincidences; they are structural features of markets populated by Prospect Theory agents, and they require us to revise the stochastic expected utility framework at the market level, not merely at the individual level.

On Mediocristan versus Extremistan

Mediocristan vs Extremistan: in Mediocristan (thin tails) the sample mean is stable; in Extremistan (fat tails / power laws) a single observation can dominate the entire sample — financial returns are ExtremistanThe fundamental mistake of the stochastic framework as applied to finance is to assume that financial variables live in what I call Mediocristan — a domain governed by distributions with thin tails, like the normal — when they actually live in Extremistan — a domain governed by distributions with fat tails, often power laws, where extreme events are far more frequent than any Gaussian model predicts. In Mediocristan, no single observation can dominate the sample. If you measure the heights of one million people, the tallest person in the sample will be perhaps three standard deviations above the mean — unremarkable. Adding one more person cannot change the average significantly. The normal distribution and its kin live in Mediocristan. In Extremistan, a single observation can dominate. If you measure the wealth of one million people, the richest person may have more wealth than the other 999,999 combined. Adding Bill Gates to the sample changes the average dramatically. Wealth, income, book sales, city populations, financial returns, and market losses all live in Extremistan. The correct distributional model for these quantities is not the normal distribution but a power-law distribution: P(X > x) ~ x^{−α} for some tail exponent α, with no finite variance when α ≤ 2. The GBM model assumes normality — it is a Mediocristan model applied to Extremistan data. The consequences are catastrophic.

On power laws and the tail exponent

Power law: P(X > x) = C·x^{−α}; the tail exponent α determines the existence of moments — if α ≤ 2, variance is infinite; if α ≤ 1, mean is infinite; Pareto distributions in wealth and income have α ≈ 1.5A power-law distribution is one where the probability of observing a value greater than x decays as a power of x: P(X > x) = C · x^{−α}, for some constant C and tail exponent α. The tail exponent α determines which moments of the distribution are finite. If α > 2, the variance is finite and the Central Limit Theorem applies — the sum of n such variables is approximately normal for large n. If α ≤ 2, the variance is infinite — the CLT in its classical form does not apply, and the sum of n such variables does not converge to a normal distribution but to a Lévy-stable distribution with fat tails. If α ≤ 1, even the mean is infinite. Mandelbrot showed in the 1960s that cotton prices follow a power law with α close to 1.7 — a distribution with finite mean but infinite variance. The S&P 500 daily returns have been estimated to have a tail exponent of approximately α ≈ 3, which gives a finite variance — but the distribution still has far more probability in the extreme tails than the normal distribution, generating the "fat tails" that every risk manager observes but that Black-Scholes denies. The difference between α = 3 and the normal distribution (which behaves like α = ∞) is not quantitative but qualitative: the extreme events that the normal model assigns probability 10^{−20} actually occur with probability 10^{−5} under a power law with α = 3. In finance, this difference is the difference between safety and catastrophe.

On the ludic fallacy — confusing the map with the territory

Ludic fallacy: assuming that the structured probability model used in games of chance (dice, roulette) applies to the unstructured complexity of real economic systemsThe deepest epistemological error of the stochastic framework in finance is what I call the ludic fallacy: the confusion of a model of uncertainty with uncertainty itself. In a casino, the stochastic framework is exact: the probability of a fair die showing six is precisely 1/6, calculable from physical symmetry, stable across rolls, and not subject to the intervention of new, unforeseen random variables. The casino is a closed, controlled system in which all sources of randomness are known and quantifiable. Financial markets are not closed systems. They are open, reflexive, social systems in which the variables that matter — investor psychology, political events, technological changes, regulatory interventions, geopolitical shocks — are not enumerable in advance, are not governed by stable statistical laws, and do not respect the stationarity assumptions on which all stochastic modelling depends. The financial modeller who takes her Monte Carlo simulations seriously — who treats the output of a GBM simulation as a genuine representation of the probability distribution of future prices — is making the same mistake as the gambler who assumes that his probability calculations remain valid at a poker table populated by players who can observe and react to his behaviour. The system is not a casino; it is a complex adaptive environment in which the act of modelling changes what is being modelled.

On the problem of stationarity and structural breaks

Non-stationarity: financial time series have structural breaks — regime changes that invalidate parameters estimated from historical data; the distribution of returns is not stable over timeAll stochastic models for financial time series — GBM, GARCH, jump-diffusion — share a common assumption that the proposition's speakers have not addressed: stationarity. A time series is stationary if its statistical properties — mean, variance, autocorrelation — are constant over time. Stationarity is what allows us to estimate model parameters from historical data and use them to forecast the future. Without it, the future distribution of returns may be completely different from the past distribution, and all statistical inference from historical data is invalid as a guide to the future. Financial time series are notoriously non-stationary. They exhibit structural breaks — sudden, permanent changes in the statistical regime — driven by regulatory changes, technological shifts, changes in investor composition, macroeconomic transitions, and political events. The volatility of the S&P 500 in 2008 was an order of magnitude higher than in 2006. The correlation structure between assets changed completely during the crisis. The GARCH model estimated on pre-crisis data gave completely wrong predictions for crisis volatility. This is not a failure of GARCH's specific functional form — it is a fundamental feature of the data-generating process: it is non-stationary in ways that no finite-parameter stochastic model can capture, because the regime changes are driven by factors outside the model.

On silent evidence and the problem of sample selection

Silent evidence / survivorship bias: we observe only the surviving institutions; the graveyards of failed banks and strategies are not in the dataset — estimates of distribution tails are systematically biased downwardThere is a particularly dangerous form of bias that infects all empirical estimation of the tails of financial distributions: silent evidence, which statisticians call survivorship bias. When we estimate the distribution of financial returns from historical data, we observe only the returns of institutions, strategies, and markets that survived. The banks that failed, the funds that collapsed, the markets that shut down — they are not in the data. But their exits were often driven by precisely the extreme tail events whose probability we are trying to estimate. The true probability of a catastrophic loss for a financial institution is not well-estimated from the historical experience of surviving institutions, because the institutions most exposed to catastrophic loss — those that were destroyed by it — have removed themselves from the sample. The result is that all historical estimates of tail probabilities are biased downward: we systematically underestimate the frequency of extreme events because the most extreme events are those least likely to leave survivors to report them. This is not a problem that more sophisticated stochastic models can solve — it is an epistemological limitation on our ability to estimate the very quantities the stochastic framework requires.

Consider a bank that uses value at risk (VaR) to limit its risk: the VaR model tells it to stay within a maximum daily loss of £100M at 99% confidence under the GBM model. On the epistemics of forecasting under power laws

Under a power law with α = 3, P(return < −10%) ≈ (10/3)^{−3} ≈ 0.003 — it happens about once every few years. This is the difference between "impossible" and "regular occurrence." Now consider the sensitivity of these probability estimates to the model parameters. Under the normal distribution, halving the volatility reduces the tail probability by a factor of 10^{20} — the estimate is exquisitely sensitive to σ. Under the power law, halving the scale parameter reduces the tail probability by a factor of 8 — manageable. This extreme sensitivity of tail probabilities to small parameter errors makes the stochastic framework's probability estimates for extreme events essentially meaningless as a basis for risk management. The proposition has given us a model that is precisely wrong rather than approximately right — and in finance, being precisely wrong about tail probabilities is how institutions go bankrupt.

Mr President,

Knight has argued that genuine uncertainty cannot be represented by probability at all. Taleb has argued that the specific probability models used in finance are catastrophically mis-specified. I want to offer a more nuanced and, I believe, more philosophically precise position. I do not deny that the stochastic framework is valuable — it is indispensable for much of decision theory and economics. What I deny is the claim that a rational agent must have a single, precise, sharp probability distribution over all uncertain states, and that departures from this ideal represent irrationality rather than appropriate sensitivity to the limits of one's evidence. My view is that severe uncertainty — uncertainty that resists precise probabilistic representation — is a genuine and common feature of economic life, and that the appropriate decision-theoretic response is a richer framework: imprecise probability, or sets of probability distributions, rather than a single sharp prior.

On the distinction between risk, uncertainty, and severe uncertainty

Three tiers: risk (precise probability known), uncertainty (probability assignable but imprecisely), severe uncertainty (no tractable probability — act and state spaces may be unknown); Bradley's taxonomyIn my work on decision theory, I have found it useful to distinguish three tiers of epistemic situation. The first tier is risk: the agent knows the probability distribution over outcomes precisely. A fair die, a well-calibrated coin, a well-understood actuarial table — these are situations of genuine risk. The stochastic framework is the correct tool here, and Savage's expected utility theory applies. The second tier is uncertainty: the agent can assign probabilities to outcomes, but those assignments are imprecise — she can say "I believe the probability is somewhere between 0.2 and 0.4" but cannot pin it down further without pretending to a precision her evidence does not support. This is the domain of imprecise probability, and it characterises most economic decisions. The third tier, which I call severe uncertainty, is the most challenging: here the agent is not only uncertain about probabilities but may be uncertain about the state space itself — she does not know what outcomes are possible, what factors are relevant, or even how to frame the decision. This is Knightian uncertainty in its most radical form, and it requires decision rules that are robust to radical incompleteness of information. Savage's framework applies cleanly only to the first tier; the proposition is wrong to present it as a universal theory of rational decision-making.

On the Ellsberg paradox as a rational response to imprecise probability

Ellsberg paradox reinterpreted: subjects are not being irrational — they are rationally responding to the fact that their probability over black balls is genuinely imprecise, and hedging against this imprecisionThe proposition's Savage presented the Ellsberg paradox as evidence of human irrationality — a violation of the sure-thing principle that shows agents making errors. I want to offer a different interpretation: Ellsberg subjects are being rational. They are exhibiting a coherent response to genuine imprecision in their probability over the composition of the urn. Recall the setup: 30 red balls, 60 balls of unknown composition between black and yellow. The agent knows P(red) = 1/3 precisely. She does not know P(black): her probability over black is genuinely imprecise — she might reasonably assign any value between 0 and 2/3. The Ellsberg subjects who prefer A (red) over B (black) are expressing a preference for the bet with known probability over the bet with unknown probability — a preference called ambiguity aversion. And the preference for D over C reverses because in the second problem, choosing the colour-inclusive options hedges away the ambiguity about the black-yellow composition. This is not irrational; it is the rational response to imprecise probability: prefer bets on events whose probabilities are known over bets on events whose probabilities are genuinely imprecise. The appropriate decision theory for this situation is not SEU with a single sharp prior but a multiple-priors model — one in which the agent holds a set of probability distributions and evaluates acts by their expected utility under the worst-case distribution in the set.

On multiple priors and maximin expected utility

Maximin Expected Utility (MMEU): Gilboa-Schmeidler — under ambiguity, rational agents maximise the minimum expected utility over a set of priors; ambiguity aversion is axiomatisedThe formal framework that correctly models the Ellsberg-type preferences is the Maximin Expected Utility (MMEU) model, developed by Gilboa and Schmeidler in 1989. Instead of a single probability distribution P, the agent has a closed convex set of probability distributions C — a set of priors. An act A is evaluated by its minimum expected utility over all distributions in C: V(A) = min_{P ∈ C} E_P[u(A)]. The agent is pessimistic about which distribution in C actually governs the world, and acts to maximise her expected utility under this pessimistic assumption. This is a generalisation of Savage's SEU: when C is a singleton — when there is only one probability in the set — MMEU reduces to SEU. But when C is non-trivial — when the agent genuinely cannot pin down a precise probability — MMEU gives different, and more appropriate, recommendations. Crucially, Gilboa and Schmeidler showed that MMEU has a clean axiomatic foundation: it follows from Savage's axioms minus the independence axiom, plus an uncertainty aversion axiom. It is not an ad hoc response to the Ellsberg paradox but a well-grounded decision theory that rational agents with imprecise beliefs should follow.

On Bayesian updating and its limits under severe uncertainty

Jeffrey conditioning vs Savage conditioning: when new evidence shifts the probability of the evidence event itself, standard Bayesian conditioning is inapplicable; Jeffrey conditioning generalises it; under severe uncertainty even Jeffrey conditioning may be inapplicableThe stochastic framework's model of learning is Bayes' theorem: posterior probability P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / P(E), where H is a hypothesis, E is the evidence, and P(H) is the prior. Bayesian conditioning is the unique coherent updating rule when evidence is certain and the prior is precise. But real learning is often neither. First, evidence is often uncertain: we do not directly observe a definitive event E but rather experience a change in our probability over events — we become more confident that it rained, without being certain of it. Richard Jeffrey (my doctoral supervisor) showed that in this case, standard Bayesian conditioning must be replaced by Jeffrey conditioning: P′(H) = P(H|E)·P′(E) + P(H|¬E)·P′(¬E), where P′(E) is the new probability of E after the uncertain evidence. This is a genuine generalisation. Second, and more fundamentally, under severe uncertainty the prior P(H) itself may be unknown — the agent may be in the situation where she does not have a well-defined probability over the hypothesis space to update. In this case, neither Bayes nor Jeffrey conditioning applies, and the agent must use robust updating rules — expanding or contracting her set of priors in response to evidence — rather than the stochastic framework's conditioning. This is not irrationality; it is the appropriate response to a genuine epistemic situation that the stochastic framework cannot represent.

On imprecise utility and normative uncertainty

Imprecise utility: as well as imprecise probability, a rational agent may have imprecise preferences — a partial order rather than a complete order over outcomes; SEU requires complete preferences, which is often unrealisticThe standard stochastic expected utility framework requires not only precise probabilities but also a complete utility function — a utility u(x) defined for every possible outcome x, such that for any two outcomes x and y, either u(x) ≥ u(y) or u(y) ≥ u(x). But completeness of preferences is often empirically implausible and arguably normatively questionable. Consider a choice between two job offers: one that maximises income and another that maximises creative fulfilment. A genuinely undecided person between these — not indifferent, in the technical sense of being willing to accept a small sweetener to tip either way, but genuinely unable to compare them — has incomplete preferences. SEU's completeness axiom rules this out, requiring the agent to have a precise tradeoff between income and fulfilment. But this may be a case where the preferences are genuinely incomparable rather than indifferent. The framework of imprecise utility — where u(x) is a set [u_low(x), u_high(x)] rather than a precise number — accommodates this incomparability while retaining enough structure for rational decision-making in most cases. An agent with imprecise utilities and imprecise probabilities can use the maximin expected imprecise utility rule: choose the act that maximises the minimum expected utility over all combinations of utility functions and probability distributions in her sets. This is a genuinely richer theory than SEU, and it is the correct theory for bounded, cognitively limited agents operating under severe uncertainty.

On the relationship between stochastic models and economic models

Economic models under uncertainty: agents' stochastic beliefs are inputs to equilibrium models; if agents have imprecise beliefs, equilibrium prices reflect this imprecision — not a precise distribution but a rangeThe implications of imprecise probability extend from individual decision-making to market equilibrium. In standard asset pricing theory, equilibrium prices are determined by the interaction of agents' probability beliefs and risk preferences. If agents have precise subjective probabilities, as Savage requires, then equilibrium prices aggregate these into a single market-clearing price. But if agents have imprecise probabilities — sets of priors — then equilibrium prices may not be uniquely determined: there may be a range of prices consistent with each agent holding their most pessimistic prior and no agent having an incentive to deviate. This has the important implication that bid-ask spreads in financial markets may reflect genuine ambiguity about the asset's value — not just transaction costs or information asymmetry, but the incommensurability of agents' imprecise probability sets. The evidence for ambiguity premia — higher expected returns required to induce agents to hold ambiguous assets, above what is explained by risk premia alone — is consistent with this view. Ambiguity aversion generates a demand for holding the market portfolio (known composition, known probability of tracking the market) over individual stocks (unknown composition, genuinely ambiguous future earnings), which is one reason for the empirically observed home bias and under-diversification of individual investors.

On the practical upshot — how decision theory with a human face differs from classical SEU

Decision Theory with a Human Face: real bounded agents need a theory that accommodates imprecise probability, incomplete preferences, and learning under uncertainty — not a theory that demands impossible precisionThe title of my book — Decision Theory with a Human Face — is meant to signal what is at stake. The classical Savage framework is a beautiful mathematical theory of an idealised rational agent — one with precise, consistent, complete preferences, unlimited computational capacity, and access to all relevant information. It is a useful theoretical benchmark. But it is not a guide for the rest of us. Real decision-makers — consumers, investors, managers, policymakers — are bounded. They do not have precise probabilities over all contingencies; they do not have complete preference orderings over all possible outcomes; they are subject to cognitive limitations that make full Bayesian updating computationally infeasible. A decision theory with a human face must accommodate these features without abandoning the aspiration of rationality. Imprecise probability gives us the right level of commitment: agents should be committed to whatever probability information they have, while remaining appropriately non-committal about what they do not know. The sure-thing principle should be respected when it applies, but not extended, through forced precision, to situations where genuine ambiguity makes it inapplicable. This is not a counsel of irrationality. It is a counsel of epistemic humility — the recognition that the stochastic framework is a tool, not a description of reality, and that using it wisely requires knowing its limits.
