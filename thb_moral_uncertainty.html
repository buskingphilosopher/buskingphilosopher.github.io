Mr President,

Moral uncertainty — not knowing with confidence which ethical theory is correct — is the normal condition for any thoughtful person who has seriously engaged with ethics. This is not a deficiency to be apologised for; it is the natural epistemic state of someone who has understood how hard the questions are and how much careful argument has been devoted to each competing answer without decisive resolution. The question before us is not whether to act under moral uncertainty — we have no choice about that — but how to act well when we do not know which moral theory is true.

On the failure of My Favourite Theory

My Favourite Theory: acting on one's best-guess theory ignores genuine uncertainty and is rationally indefensibleThe simplest response to moral uncertainty is what I call My Favourite Theory: identify the moral theory you find most credible and act as if it were certainly true. This is the approach most people take implicitly, and it is rationally indefensible. Suppose I assign sixty percent credence to consequentialism and forty percent to a deontological theory that holds a certain action to be absolutely prohibited. My Favourite Theory tells me to act consequentialistically — to treat my forty percent credence in the deontological constraint as if it were zero. But this is precisely what a rational agent does not do under empirical uncertainty: she does not ignore a forty percent probability that an action will cause catastrophic harm simply because she assigns a sixty percent probability to a theory on which it is permissible. Moral credences should be treated with the same epistemic seriousness as empirical credences. To ignore them is not decisiveness — it is a form of irrationality.

On the demandingness of moral uncertainty

Moral uncertainty is demanding: it can require acting against one's best-guess theory to hedge against catastrophic wrongdoingTaking moral uncertainty seriously is, in important respects, more demanding than acting on a single theory. Consider a case in which consequentialism — my most credible theory — recommends an action that deontology classifies as the gravest possible wrong: the deliberate killing of an innocent person to produce marginally better aggregate outcomes. Even if I assign only moderate credence to the deontological theory, the gravity of what that theory condemns — the wrongness is not merely significant but categorical and profound — should give me powerful reason to refrain. Moral uncertainty functions as a kind of moral risk-management: it demands that we give extra weight to the possibility of catastrophic moral error, even where the probability of that error is not dominant. The asymmetry between catastrophic and ordinary wrongs under uncertainty is a central feature of any adequate account of how to act morally under uncertainty.

On moral intuitions as evidence

Moral intuitions are evidence: they constrain theory selection and ground credence assignmentOn what basis should we assign credences to competing moral theories? The answer, I believe, involves treating moral intuitions as evidence — data points that any adequate moral theory must accommodate. When a theory produces a conclusion that strikes virtually everyone as monstrous, this widespread intuitive reaction is not mere squeamishness — it is evidence that the theory has gone wrong somewhere. The history of moral philosophy is in large part a history of theories being revised in response to their counterintuitive implications. Credence in a theory should be partly determined by how well it accommodates our most robust intuitions, how elegantly it systematises our considered judgments, and how it fares in wide reflective equilibrium. The proposition does not claim that intuitions are infallible; only that they are evidential, and that a theory of action under moral uncertainty must account for the evidential weight they carry.

On intertheoretic comparisons and their tractability

Intertheoretic comparisons: despite the absence of a common currency, rough comparisons across theories are tractableThe most serious objection to maximising expected moral value is the problem of intertheoretic comparisons: if different theories use incommensurable value currencies, how can we aggregate across them? I want to argue that this objection, though real, is less devastating than the opposition supposes. We routinely make rough comparisons across incommensurable scales — we compare the importance of health against the importance of friendship, of artistic achievement against economic productivity. These comparisons are imprecise and contested, but they are not nothing. We can identify cases where the stakes under one theory are so much higher than under another that the comparison is relatively uncontroversial. Moreover, the absence of precise intertheoretic comparisons does not force us back to My Favourite Theory — it should make us more cautious, more willing to hedge, more attentive to moral risk. Imprecision is not the same as impossibility.

On moral progress as evidence that uncertainty is navigable

Moral progress: the historical track record shows that moral uncertainty has been navigated successfully; this vindicates the approachMoral progress — the recognition that slavery was wrong, that women are full moral persons, that animals have morally significant interests — required acting under moral uncertainty. The abolitionists were not certain that the dominant moral views of their time were wrong: they had credences, they made arguments, they acted on those credences against the confident assertions of those who were sure the prevailing view was correct. The history of moral progress is a history of minority credences prevailing over confident majorities — which shows both that moral uncertainty is navigable and that the method of taking all credences seriously rather than simply acting on the majority view is the right one. The opposition's scepticism about intertheoretic comparison and rational navigation of moral uncertainty would, if consistently applied, have paralysed moral reform whenever the reformers lacked certainty.

On the practical upshot — robustness to moral uncertainty

Robustness: actions that are good across multiple theories are especially recommended; universal dominance is the gold standardThe practical upshot of taking moral uncertainty seriously is a preference for actions that are robustly good — good across a wide range of credible ethical theories. If an action is recommended by both consequentialism and deontology, its moral status is unusually secure: it dominates under both frameworks. Conversely, actions that are condemned by every credible theory — whatever their first-order justification — are especially suspect. The proposition recommends what might be called a robustness heuristic: under moral uncertainty, give extra weight to actions that are defensible across multiple frameworks, and be especially wary of actions that are condemned by multiple frameworks even if permitted by your favourite. This is not moral paralysis; it is moral wisdom.

On the alternative — moral dogmatism and its dangers

Moral dogmatism: acting as if one's theory is certainly correct risks catastrophic moral error; uncertainty is the honest positionThe alternative to navigating moral uncertainty is moral dogmatism — acting as if one's preferred theory is certainly correct and the alternatives certainly wrong. The history of moral catastrophe is substantially a history of moral dogmatism: of people who were certain their theory was right and acted accordingly without regard to the possibility of error. The person who tortures a prisoner to extract information, certain that the consequences justify it; the person who maintains rigid rules regardless of consequences, certain that the deontological constraint overrides all cost — both exhibit a confidence in their moral theory that their actual epistemic situation does not warrant. Taking moral uncertainty seriously is not scepticism about ethics; it is epistemic honesty about one's actual epistemic situation. The opposition's view that we should not attempt to systematically navigate moral uncertainty is, in practice, an invitation to proceed as if our favourite theory were certainly true — which is precisely the dogmatism that has done so much harm.

Mr President,

Ord has established why My Favourite Theory is irrational and why taking moral uncertainty seriously is the right approach. I want to go further and develop a positive account: how, precisely, should a rational agent act under moral uncertainty? My answer, developed with Ord and Bykvist in our book Moral Uncertainty, is that the right decision procedure is Maximise Expected Choiceworthiness — MEC — and I want to explain why this is correct and how the objections to it can be answered.

On Maximise Expected Choiceworthiness

MEC: the right act is that which maximises expected choiceworthiness, weighted by credences in theoriesMaximise Expected Choiceworthiness holds that the right act under moral uncertainty is the one that maximises the sum, across all credible moral theories, of each theory's credence multiplied by the choiceworthiness that theory assigns to the act. This is the direct analogue of maximising expected utility under empirical uncertainty — and it has the same justification. Just as a rational empirical agent maximises expected utility by weighting outcomes by their probability, a rational moral agent under moral uncertainty should weight the moral value of outcomes by the credence she assigns to the theory that assigns that value. The formal structure is impeccable. The philosophical challenge is supplying the inputs — particularly intertheoretic comparisons of choiceworthiness — and I will explain how that challenge can be met.

On the Parliamentary Model

Parliamentary Model: theories are like MPs whose votes are weighted by credence; compromise is the rational outcomeAs a heuristic complement to MEC, consider the Parliamentary Model of moral uncertainty. Imagine a parliament in which each credible moral theory is represented by a bloc of MPs whose size is proportional to your credence in that theory. The parliament votes on which action to take. The outcome is a weighted compromise — theories with higher credence have more votes, but minority theories are not simply ignored. This model captures several important features of rational action under moral uncertainty: it takes all credible theories seriously, it produces compromise rather than winner-takes-all outcomes, and it naturally represents the aggregation of moral evidence. The Parliamentary Model is not the complete story — it needs to be supplemented by an account of how MPs within each bloc bargain and trade — but it is a powerful heuristic that helps illustrate what MEC recommends in practice.

On intertheoretic value comparisons

Intertheoretic comparisons: normalisation by variance or by degree of confidence provides tractable comparison pointsThe central technical challenge for MEC is the problem of intertheoretic comparisons: how do we compare the choiceworthiness assigned to an action by, say, a utilitarian theory with the choiceworthiness assigned by a contractualist theory? These theories do not share a common currency. I want to suggest that this challenge, though real, can be addressed in several ways. One approach is normalisation by variance: we set the zero point of each theory's choiceworthiness scale at its mean and then normalise the variance to be equal across theories. This gives us a common currency without assuming any deeper metaphysical commensurability. A second approach attends to the degree of confidence each theory has in its own claims: a theory that regards the wrongness of an action as settled and fundamental should be weighted more heavily in the aggregation than one that regards its verdict as tentative.

On variance voting and fanaticism

Variance voting: a decision procedure that avoids fanaticism while preserving sensitivity to stakesA particular challenge for MEC is fanaticism: the worry that a theory assigning astronomically large values to some outcomes will dominate the expected choiceworthiness calculation even at vanishingly low credence. If I assign even a tiny credence to a theory that says some action has infinite choiceworthiness, MEC seems to recommend that action regardless of what all other theories say — this is fanaticism. I address this problem through what I call variance voting: rather than aggregating raw choiceworthiness, we aggregate the relative rank of actions within each theory's ordering. This preserves sensitivity to the stakes — a theory that regards the difference between options as vast will have its rankings reflect this — while avoiding the fanaticism that arises from unbounded raw values. Variance voting is a refinement of MEC that handles this pathological case without abandoning the core insight.

On the objection from moral fixed points

Moral fixed points: some prohibitions are so certain that they should function as constraints, not as inputs to aggregationThe opposition will press the objection that some moral prohibitions are so certain — torturing children for amusement, enslaving whole peoples — that they should function as inviolable constraints, not as inputs to a choiceworthiness calculation that could in principle be outweighed by sufficiently strong competing considerations. I want to take this objection seriously. My response is that MEC can accommodate it: where our credence in the wrongness of an action approaches certainty — approaches but never quite reaches one — the expected choiceworthiness calculation will assign effectively infinite disvalue to that action, making it effectively impermissible regardless of what other theories say. The fixed-point intuition is captured by MEC at the limit, without requiring us to treat any moral claim as absolutely certain in a way that is inconsistent with our actual epistemic situation.

On the action-guidingness of MEC

MEC is action-guiding: it gives practical verdicts in real cases, unlike the alternativesThe opposition may charge MEC with being practically useless — too abstract, too dependent on contested intertheoretic comparisons, to provide real guidance. But compare it to the alternatives. My Favourite Theory is action-guiding but rationally indefensible. The opposition's preferred approaches — following rules that are robust across theories, or simply following your considered judgment — are either indeterminate in hard cases or simply restate the problem without solving it. MEC, by contrast, gives a principled procedure: assign credences to theories, identify how each theory ranks the available options, apply normalisation, aggregate by credence. This procedure will often confirm what we already believe, but in contested cases — cases where theories diverge sharply — it provides a determinate answer rather than a shrug.

On moral uncertainty and supererogation

Supererogation under uncertainty: MEC illuminates the interaction between demandingness and uncertainty in moral theoryA final point. Moral uncertainty interacts in interesting ways with the question of supererogation — actions that are good but beyond what is required. If I am uncertain between a demanding theory that requires significant self-sacrifice and a less demanding theory that does not, MEC recommends a weighted position: somewhat self-sacrificing, but not as demanding as the most demanding theory. This has the appealing implication that moral uncertainty about demandingness yields moderate demands — a conclusion that seems independently plausible. It also shows that MEC is not a blunt instrument that simply averages out to mediocrity: it is sensitive to the structure of the theories being aggregated and can generate nuanced verdicts about the full range of moral questions, including those about how much we are required to do for others.

Mr President,

I want to offer a more foundational perspective on the question before us. My colleagues have argued that moral uncertainty should be managed by a specific decision procedure — MEC. I want to argue for something prior: that the very framing of ethics as a domain in which uncertainty is appropriate, and in which evidence can bear on credences, is correct — and that this Bayesian approach to metaethics not only supports the proposition's view but explains why the opposition's scepticism is itself misguided.

On treating ethics empirically

Empirical ethics: moral questions should be approached with the same epistemic methods as empirical ones — evidence, updating, credencesThe central insight I want to defend is that moral inquiry should be treated empirically rather than dogmatically. What does this mean? It means that we approach moral questions with the same tools we bring to empirical questions: we form hypotheses, we look for evidence, we update our credences in light of argument and counterexample, and we hold our conclusions with appropriate uncertainty proportional to the strength of the evidence. The alternative — treating one's favourite moral theory as an axiom from which conclusions are deduced with certainty — is a form of dogmatism that is inconsistent with the actual epistemic situation of any reflective moral reasoner. Moral philosophy has been making progress for two and a half millennia without converging on a single theory. This is powerful evidence that moral questions are genuinely hard, that no theory has yet been conclusively established, and that appropriate uncertainty is warranted.

On moral intuitions as moral evidence

Moral intuitions as evidence: intuitions are not merely psychological but carry evidential weight about moral truthOn an empirical approach to ethics, moral intuitions play the role of observational data. Just as empirical intuitions — perceptual experiences, instrument readings — provide evidence about the physical world, moral intuitions — considered judgments about cases, reactions to thought experiments — provide evidence about the moral world. They are not infallible: just as perceptual experiences can be illusory, moral intuitions can be biased, self-serving, or parochial. But this shows only that intuitions should be weighed, not that they should be dismissed. The method of wide reflective equilibrium — moving between principles and particular judgments, revising both in search of coherence — is the appropriate evidential method for ethics. And crucially, this method is precisely the kind of Bayesian updating under evidence that the empirical approach recommends.

On the normative significance of intertheoretic convergence

Convergence: where multiple theories agree, confidence is higher; divergence marks genuine moral uncertaintyAn important corollary of the empirical approach is the significance of convergence across moral theories. Where multiple independent ethical frameworks agree on a verdict — where both consequentialism and deontology condemn an action, or both recommend one — this convergence is evidential: it raises our credence that the verdict is correct, much as independent empirical measurements that agree raise our credence in a hypothesis. Conversely, where frameworks sharply diverge — where consequentialism strongly recommends what deontology prohibits — this divergence marks a region of genuine uncertainty where we should hedge. The empirical approach thus provides a principled basis for the robustness heuristic that Ord has recommended: convergence raises credences, divergence counsels caution.

On the metaethical grounding of MEC

Metaethical grounding: MEC is supported by moral realism and moral non-realism alike, because it tracks epistemic rather than metaphysical uncertaintyOne might worry that MEC depends on a particular metaethical position — that it requires moral realism, or some form of cognitivism, to make sense. I want to argue that this worry is misplaced. MEC tracks epistemic uncertainty — uncertainty about which theory is correct — rather than metaphysical uncertainty about what moral facts exist. Even a moral anti-realist who thinks there are no mind-independent moral facts can accept that she faces practical uncertainty about which normative framework to act on, and that the expected-value structure of MEC is the right response to that practical uncertainty. The metaethical grounding of moral uncertainty and MEC is therefore more secure than the opposition supposes: it is not committed to any controversial metaethical view.

On the objection from moral knowledge and tollensing the ponens

Tollensing the ponens: when an argument's conclusion is morally monstrous, this is evidence against one of the premisesThe opposition — Enoch in particular — will argue that moral knowledge sometimes warrants simply rejecting an argument's conclusion rather than accepting it, regardless of the apparent validity of the argument. If a valid argument from plausible premises leads to the conclusion that we should harvest one person's organs to save five, the right response, on this view, is to deny one of the premises rather than accept the conclusion. This is what philosophers call tollensing the ponens. I want to concede that this is sometimes the right response — but argue that it is precisely what the empirical approach recommends. On the empirical approach, the manifest wrongness of the conclusion is evidence against one of the premises. The question is: how strong is this evidence? Strong enough to outweigh the plausibility of the premises? This is an evidential question, and answering it requires exactly the kind of credence-weighting and expected-value reasoning that the proposition defends.

On the population ethics problem and the importance of theory aggregation

Population ethics: the difficulty of choosing between Totalism and Averagism illustrates why theory aggregation is essentialThe domain of population ethics provides a particularly vivid illustration of why theory aggregation under moral uncertainty is essential. Consider the choice between Totalism — which holds that we should maximise total welfare — and Averagism — which holds that we should maximise average welfare. These theories diverge sharply in their practical implications: Totalism recommends creating vast numbers of lives worth living even at the cost of lower average welfare (generating the Repugnant Conclusion), while Averagism has its own deeply counterintuitive implications. Neither theory is clearly correct; serious philosophers assign non-trivial credence to both. In such a case, MEC recommends a position that hedges between them — taking into account the stakes under each theory and the credence assigned to each. This seems far more defensible than simply acting on whichever theory one finds marginally more plausible.

On the role of moral uncertainty in practical ethics and effective altruism

Practical ethics: MEC under moral uncertainty reshapes how we should give, act, and prioritise in practical contextsThe propositions we have been defending are not merely academic. They have profound practical implications for how we should act in the world — particularly for how we should allocate our resources and efforts to do good. If we are uncertain between a theory that assigns great moral weight to reducing suffering in the near term and a theory that assigns great moral weight to improving the long-run trajectory of civilisation, MEC recommends a hedged portfolio of actions — investing in both near-term and long-run causes, calibrated by our credences. This is in fact the practical approach endorsed by the effective altruism movement, which I have helped to develop theoretically. Moral uncertainty, far from being a counsel of paralysis, is an engine of practical clarity: it tells us to diversify our moral investments, take all credible theories seriously, and be especially attentive to actions that are good across multiple frameworks.

Mr President,

The proposition has offered us a seductive picture: moral uncertainty is like empirical uncertainty, and the rational response to both is expected-value maximisation. I want to resist this picture at every level. The analogy between moral and empirical uncertainty is weaker than it appears; the project of intertheoretic comparison is not merely difficult but conceptually confused; and the practical upshot of the proposition's view is not wisdom but a new and particularly dangerous form of moral complacency.

On the disanalogy between moral and empirical uncertainty

Disanalogy: moral theories are not probability distributions over possible worlds; the formal analogy to empirical uncertainty is misleadingThe proposition's central move is to treat moral theories as analogous to empirical hypotheses and moral credences as analogous to empirical probabilities. But this analogy breaks down at a crucial point. When I assign probability to an empirical hypothesis, I am distributing credence over possible states of the world — there is a fact of the matter about which state obtains, and my uncertainty is about that fact. But when I assign credence to a moral theory, what am I doing? Moral theories are not descriptions of possible moral worlds that might or might not obtain. They are normative systems that make claims about what we ought to do. There is no underlying "moral fact of the matter" that is independent of all theories and to which all theories are attempts at approximation — at least not in any way that is uncontroversial. The formal analogy between moral and empirical uncertainty is therefore either question-begging or vacuous: it either assumes moral realism or reduces to practical uncertainty that needs no talk of credences at all.

On the impossibility of intertheoretic comparisons

Intertheoretic comparisons are impossible in principle: different theories operate with incommensurable evaluative frameworksMacAskill has proposed normalisation by variance as a technical solution to the problem of intertheoretic comparison. But normalisation by variance does not solve the problem — it stipulates a solution. Why should the variance of utilitarian choiceworthiness be set equal to the variance of deontological permissibility? There is no principled answer: the choice of normalisation method is itself a substantive normative decision that cannot be made from a standpoint outside any theory. Different normative frameworks do not merely use different units of measurement for the same underlying currency — they use categorically different evaluative concepts that cannot be translated into each other without remainder. The difference between consequentialist goodness and deontological rightness is not a scaling factor; it is a difference in kind. Intertheoretic comparisons are not merely difficult but conceptually ill-formed.

On the problem of moral deference and its limits

Moral deference: deferring to a moral calculus rather than exercising one's own judgment undermines moral agencyThere is a deeper problem with the proposition's approach that concerns the nature of moral agency itself. The proposition recommends, in effect, that we defer to the output of a formal decision procedure — MEC — rather than exercising our own moral judgment directly. But moral agency is not like financial agency, where deferring to an algorithm may be perfectly rational. To be a moral agent is to be responsible for one's moral judgments, to own them, to stand behind them. The person who acts badly because MEC told her to has not thereby exonerated herself — she has simply added an extra layer of mechanism between herself and her action. Moral responsibility requires direct engagement with the moral landscape, not the interposition of a formal procedure. The proposition's MEC is not a substitute for moral judgment but a way of evading it — dressed up in the language of rationality.

On tollensing the ponens — and when arguments should be rejected

Tollensing the ponens: when an argument leads to a clearly monstrous conclusion, the right response is to reject the argument, not to accept the conclusionGreaves has tried to appropriate the practice of tollensing the ponens for the proposition's view, arguing that it is simply a case of Bayesian updating. I want to resist this appropriation. When I confront an argument for a monstrous conclusion — harvest one person's organs to save five, or maximise expected welfare by sacrificing the individual for the aggregate — and I judge the conclusion to be clearly wrong, this judgment is not merely a data point to be weighed against the premises. It is a piece of moral knowledge — knowledge that is direct, non-inferential, and foundational in a way that the probabilistic framework cannot capture. Some moral claims are known with a confidence that cannot be coherently traded off against the premises of any argument. Greaves's "evidential weighing" of the monstrous conclusion dissolves this knowledge into a credence that can in principle be outweighed — and thereby loses what is most important about it.

On moral knowledge and the limits of uncertainty

Moral knowledge: some moral claims are known with certainty; treating them as mere credences misrepresents our epistemic situationThe proposition has claimed that moral uncertainty is the "normal condition" for any careful moral reasoner. I want to challenge this claim. There are moral claims I know — not merely believe with high credence, but know, in the same sense in which I know that I exist. I know that torturing children for amusement is wrong. I know that gratuitous cruelty is wrong. I know that basic human dignity demands respect. These are not items of uncertain belief that might be traded off against competing theoretical considerations; they are pieces of moral knowledge that serve as constraints on any acceptable theory. The proposition's framework, which treats all moral claims as subject to uncertainty and all uncertainties as tradeable, is not an honest description of our moral epistemic situation — it is a theoretically-motivated distortion of it.

On the fanaticism problem as a symptom of deeper failure

Fanaticism: the fanaticism problem is not a technical glitch but a symptom of the fundamental incoherence of the MEC approachMacAskill has acknowledged the fanaticism problem — the tendency for MEC to be dominated by theories assigning astronomical values — and proposed variance voting as a fix. But the need for this fix reveals something important. The fanaticism problem is not a technical glitch; it is a symptom of a fundamental incoherence in the MEC approach. The problem arises because MEC treats choiceworthiness values as if they were probability-weighted utilities — but moral theories do not assign real-valued choiceworthiness to outcomes in the way that utility functions assign real-valued utilities to outcomes. When we force theories into this mould, we import into moral reasoning a structure — the structure of cardinal, interpersonally comparable, unboundedly scalable value — that simply does not belong to most moral frameworks. Variance voting is a patch on a procedure that should not have been formulated in the first place.

On why the right response to moral uncertainty is moral seriousness, not a calculus

Moral seriousness: the right response to moral uncertainty is direct moral engagement — judgment, sensitivity, wisdom — not a formal procedureThe right response to moral uncertainty is not a formal decision procedure but moral seriousness: the cultivation of moral sensitivity, the willingness to think carefully about each situation, the development of practical wisdom — phronesis, in Aristotle's sense — that enables one to navigate moral complexity without reducing it to a calculation. The morally serious person under uncertainty does not ask "what does MEC recommend?" — she asks "what does this situation require of me?" and brings to bear all her moral knowledge, her sensitivity to the particular features of the case, and her judgment. This is not a failure to systematise; it is a recognition that moral judgment is irreducibly personal and contextual in a way that formal procedures cannot capture. The proposition has confused the difficulty of moral uncertainty with the need for a calculus. The right response is wisdom, not arithmetic.

Mr President,

Enoch has pressed the philosophical objections to the proposition's framework. I want to add a different dimension to the opposition's case: one drawn from the relationship between moral uncertainty, moral responsibility, and the conditions under which agents can be excused for wrongdoing. My claim is that moral uncertainty, properly understood, is primarily a story about excuses and the limits of culpability — not a story about how to act — and that the proposition's attempt to use it as a guide to action inverts its proper role.

On moral ignorance and culpability

Moral ignorance and culpability: moral uncertainty can excuse wrongdoing; this is its primary normative roleThe primary philosophical significance of moral ignorance — not knowing the moral truth — lies in its relation to culpability. When an agent acts wrongly because she genuinely did not know, and could not reasonably have known, that her action was wrong, she is typically excused from the full moral blame that would otherwise attach to her action. This is a familiar and important point about moral responsibility: ignorance is an excuse. What is less often recognised is that this use of moral uncertainty — as a condition that limits culpability — is very different from, and in tension with, the proposition's use of it — as a guide to action. The proposition assumes that knowing one is uncertain is sufficient to generate a positive obligation to aggregate credences and maximise expected choiceworthiness. But uncertainty can generate obligations only if there is a clear procedure for discharging them — and the proposition has not shown that MEC is that procedure.

On the limits of the evidential analogy

The evidential analogy fails: moral theories are not evidence about a moral reality independent of all theoriesGreaves has argued for an empirical approach to ethics in which moral intuitions and theories serve as evidence about moral truth. I want to press the question: evidence about what? In the empirical case, evidence is evidence about a mind-independent reality that our theories are attempting to describe. In the moral case, what plays the role of this mind-independent reality? If it is robust moral intuitions, then the claim is circular: we are using intuitions as evidence for theories and theories as evidence for intuitions, with no independent check. If it is some form of moral Platonism — mind-independent moral facts that our intuitions and theories attempt to track — then we are committed to a highly controversial metaethical position that many reject. The evidential analogy either presupposes moral realism or collapses into coherentism, and neither option is comfortable for the proposition.

On the regress problem for moral uncertainty

Regress problem: uncertainty about how to act under uncertainty generates a regress that MEC cannot resolveHere is a problem that the proposition has not adequately addressed. Suppose I accept that I should act under moral uncertainty by maximising expected choiceworthiness. But now I face a second-order question: am I certain that MEC is the right decision procedure for acting under moral uncertainty? Surely I should have some credence that the opposition's preferred procedures — following the most credible theory, deferring to robust intuitions, applying Ross's prima facie duties — are correct. And so I face uncertainty about how to act under uncertainty, which seems to require a third-order procedure for aggregating my second-order credences, and so on indefinitely. The proposition has not explained why the regress stops at the first-order application of MEC rather than continuing indefinitely. And if the regress does not stop, the proposition's approach generates not a determinate recommendation but an infinite regress of aggregations.

On the scope of rational moral obligation

Scope of obligation: MEC generates implausibly demanding obligations; moral uncertainty does not obligate us to perform arbitrarily complex aggregationsEven granting that MEC is the right procedure, the question arises: does moral uncertainty generate an obligation to apply it? The proposition seems to assume that it does — that recognising one's uncertainty about moral theories obligates one to engage in the complex procedure of assigning credences, normalising choiceworthiness scales, and aggregating by expected value. But this is itself a highly demanding obligation. Most people, most of the time, lack the philosophical sophistication to assign well-calibrated credences to ethical theories, to identify the relevant theories in the first place, or to perform anything like the normalisation that MEC requires. If MEC is the procedure one must follow, then moral uncertainty generates an obligation of philosophical competence that the vast majority of moral agents cannot satisfy — which seems an implausible implication of a theory of how ordinary people should act.

On moral testimony and the problem of deference

Moral testimony: deferring to moral experts or to formal procedures is problematic in ways that deferring to empirical experts is notThe proposition's approach has an uncomfortable implication about moral testimony and deference. If moral uncertainty is like empirical uncertainty and should be managed by the same methods, then it seems that I should defer to moral experts — people who have assigned well-calibrated credences and have done the MEC calculation — in the same way that I defer to medical or scientific experts about empirical matters. But this seems wrong. There is something deficient about the person who acts well only because a moral expert told her to, or because an algorithm recommended it, in a way that there is nothing deficient about the person who takes medicine because a doctor recommended it. Moral virtue requires that one have the right dispositions, that one be moved by the right considerations, not merely that one produce the right outputs. The proposition's framework is blind to this dimension of moral evaluation.

On the conditions for legitimate moral uncertainty

Legitimate uncertainty: not all moral uncertainty is epistemically respectable; some uncertainty reflects confused thinking, not genuine moral difficultyThere is a further problem about what counts as legitimate moral uncertainty. The proposition seems to assume that any sincerely-held credence in a moral theory is epistemically respectable and should be incorporated into the MEC calculation. But this is too permissive. A person who assigns significant credence to the view that women are not full moral persons, or that members of certain racial groups have lesser moral status, has not thereby identified a genuine area of moral uncertainty that should be weighted in the aggregation. Their credence reflects confusion, bias, or motivated reasoning, not genuine philosophical difficulty. But how do we distinguish legitimate moral uncertainty from confused pseudo-uncertainty? The proposition has no answer to this question — and without an answer, MEC is liable to be corrupted by including indefensible credences in the aggregation.

On moral uncertainty as a resource for practical wisdom

Practical wisdom: the right use of moral uncertainty is to foster humility and careful attention, not to generate a calculusThe right use of the insight that we face moral uncertainty is not to generate a formal decision procedure but to cultivate practical wisdom — humility about the limits of one's moral knowledge, attentiveness to features of situations that might reveal one's initial judgments to be mistaken, openness to revising one's views in light of argument and evidence. This is the Socratic insight: it is not knowledge of the right procedure that makes a person morally good, but the recognition of one's own ignorance combined with the willingness to engage seriously with moral questions. Moral uncertainty, on this view, is a spur to moral seriousness rather than a technical problem requiring a technical solution. The proposition has given us an answer to the wrong question — it has told us how to maximise expected choiceworthiness when what we needed was a reminder of how to be good.

I want to speak from an earlier tradition than the one the proposition invokes — a tradition that takes moral pluralism seriously from the start, rather than arriving at pluralism as a problem to be managed. My claim is that moral uncertainty, properly understood, points not toward MEC but toward a pluralistic intuitionism that takes the moral landscape to be irreducibly complex and cannot be systematised without falsification.

On prima facie duties and the structure of moral reality

Prima facie duties: the moral landscape consists of multiple irreducible duties; no single principle unifies themThe moral landscape, as I see it, consists of a plurality of prima facie duties — duties of fidelity, of reparation, of gratitude, of non-maleficence, of beneficence, of justice, and of self-improvement. These duties are not derived from any single principle; they are irreducibly plural and are known directly through moral intuition. Each prima facie duty represents a genuine moral consideration that, in ordinary circumstances, gives us reason to act in a certain way. When prima facie duties conflict — when keeping a promise would prevent me from helping someone in need — we face genuine moral difficulty, not a mathematical optimisation problem. The right response is to judge, carefully and with sensitivity to the particular features of the case, which duty is strongest in this situation. This is what I call the judgment of the actual duty — the duty we have all things considered — and it is irreducibly a matter of wisdom, not calculation.

On why moral reality resists systematisation

Moral reality resists systematisation: the attempt to reduce the moral landscape to a single principle or procedure falsifies itBoth the proposition and the single-theory approaches it criticises share a common assumption: that moral reality can and should be systematised — reduced to a single principle or a unified procedure. I reject this assumption. The moral landscape is genuinely complex, and its complexity is not a deficiency waiting to be resolved by a better theory. It is a feature of moral reality itself. The facts about what we owe to each other — to our friends and families, to strangers, to our past benefactors, to those we have wronged — are irreducibly plural. Any theory that claims to derive all these obligations from a single master principle has already falsified the data it is attempting to explain. The propositions' MEC is not an improvement on single-theory approaches; it is a more sophisticated version of the same category error.

On the relationship between prima facie duties and moral uncertainty

Prima facie duties and uncertainty: the conflict between duties is not uncertainty about which theory is true but recognition of genuine moral complexityHow does my account relate to the question of moral uncertainty? The proposition treats uncertainty as arising from our ignorance of which moral theory is correct. But on my view, the difficulty of moral judgment does not arise primarily from theoretical uncertainty — from not knowing whether consequentialism or deontology is true. It arises from the recognition that the moral landscape is genuinely complex and that our prima facie duties can conflict in ways that resist systematic resolution. When I face a conflict between the duty to keep a promise and the duty to prevent harm, I am not uncertain about which moral theory is correct — I am confronting the genuine complexity of a situation in which real moral considerations point in different directions. This is a different kind of moral difficulty from theoretical uncertainty, and it requires a different kind of response — judgment, not aggregation.

On the role of moral intuition in adjudicating conflicts

Moral intuition: in cases of conflict between prima facie duties, intuition is the ultimate arbiter — it cannot be replaced by a procedureWhen prima facie duties conflict, how do we determine which has priority? My answer is that we rely on moral intuition — not the raw, unreflective intuition of the person who has not thought carefully about ethics, but the considered, cultivated intuition of the morally serious person who has attended carefully to the features of the situation and brought to bear all her moral knowledge and sensitivity. This reliance on intuition is not a counsel of arbitrariness; it is a recognition that moral judgment in particular cases cannot be reduced to the application of a general procedure. The intuition of the morally serious person encodes a vast amount of moral knowledge that cannot be fully articulated in a theory — including knowledge about how to weigh competing considerations in context. MEC cannot capture this knowledge; it can only produce verdicts that may or may not coincide with the verdicts of the morally wise.

On the objection that intuitionism cannot resolve conflicts

Against the objection: the conflict-resolution problem for intuitionism is less severe than for MEC; intuitionism is honest about what it cannot doThe proposition will object that intuitionism cannot resolve conflicts between prima facie duties in hard cases — that it simply says "use your judgment" without providing a procedure. I accept that this is true, and I want to turn it into a point in favour of intuitionism. It is true that intuitionism cannot resolve all hard cases algorithmically. But neither can MEC, once we recognise that the intertheoretic comparisons on which it depends are conceptually ill-formed. What intuitionism offers over MEC is honesty about what moral philosophy can and cannot deliver. It does not pretend to a precision — a mathematical determination of the right action — that the subject matter does not support. And it does not import into moral reasoning a structure — the expected-value structure — that presupposes a commensurability of moral considerations that is very probably false. Intuitionism is honest about the limits of systematisation; MEC is not.

On the convergence of pluralism and particularism

Pluralism and particularism: Ross's pluralism converges with moral particularism in recognising that context determines the weight of moral considerationsMy pluralistic intuitionism converges with a more recent tradition in moral philosophy — moral particularism, as developed by Jonathan Dancy — in a key insight: the weight of a moral consideration can vary across contexts. The fact that an action would cause pain is normally a reason against it; but in a case of surgery, or of necessary punishment, it may not be. The fact that someone stands in a relationship of friendship to me is normally a reason to give them preferential treatment; but in a case of institutional fairness, it may not be. This context-sensitivity of moral reasons is something that MEC, with its fixed choiceworthiness functions for each theory, cannot accommodate. The proposition must treat each theory as assigning a fixed value to each outcome regardless of context — which falsifies the structure of the moral considerations those theories are attempting to capture.

On what moral wisdom actually looks like

Moral wisdom: the practically wise person navigates moral complexity through cultivated sensitivity, not through maximising a procedureThe practically wise person — the person of good character who has cultivated her moral sensibilities over a lifetime of attention to moral considerations — does not navigate moral complexity by assigning credences to theories and maximising expected choiceworthiness. She does it by attending to the morally relevant features of each situation, drawing on her accumulated moral experience, and forming a judgment that reflects her sensitivity to the full range of considerations at play. This is the Aristotelian picture of practical wisdom, and it is correct. It is correct not because it is a tidy theory but because it accurately describes what morally excellent people actually do, and because the moral judgments of such people are our best guide to moral truth. MEC is a theory for philosophers who have not yet understood that the goal of moral philosophy is not to systematise but to become wise.

2.The plurality and irreducibility of moral obligations is a feature of moral reality, not a deficiency — any theory deriving all obligations from a single master principle has falsified the data.
3.MEC is a more sophisticated version of the same category error as single-theory approaches — it aggregates across theories rather than selecting one, but retains the same false assumption of systematisability.
∴MEC shares the category error of single-theory approaches — both assume moral reality can be systematised, when the correct view is that it is irreducibly plural in ways no procedure can capture.
III — Conflict between prima facie duties is not theoretical uncertainty; it requires judgment not aggregationclick for image
1.The difficulty of moral judgment arises from genuine complexity in the moral landscape — conflicting duties — not primarily from ignorance about which theory is true.
2.Confronting a conflict between the duty to keep a promise and the duty to prevent harm is not theoretical uncertainty — it is recognition of genuine moral complexity.
3.This kind of moral difficulty requires a judgment about which duty is strongest in this case — a response the proposition's MEC cannot deliver because it addresses the wrong kind of difficulty.
∴The proposition's framework addresses the wrong kind of moral difficulty — MEC targets theoretical uncertainty about which single theory is correct, while the genuine difficulty is the practical conflict between irreducibly plural moral considerations.
IV — Moral intuition is the ultimate arbiter of duty-conflicts; no procedure can replace itclick for image
1.In cases of conflict between prima facie duties, the considered intuition of the morally serious person is the ultimate arbiter — encoding moral knowledge that cannot be fully articulated in a theory.
2.This reliance on intuition is not arbitrariness but a recognition that moral judgment in particular cases cannot be reduced to the application of a general procedure.
3.MEC cannot capture this context-sensitive, accumulated moral knowledge — it can only produce verdicts that may coincide with or diverge from the verdicts of the morally wise.
∴Moral intuition in the hands of the morally serious person is the correct guide in hard cases — no procedure, including MEC, can replace or improve upon it because the relevant knowledge is irreducibly particular and context-sensitive.
V — Intuitionism is honest about its limits; MEC is not honest about its dependence on impossible comparisonsclick for image
1.Intuitionism cannot resolve all hard cases algorithmically — it honestly acknowledges this limitation and recommends judgment rather than pretending to false precision.
2.MEC cannot resolve hard cases either, once intertheoretic comparisons are seen to be conceptually ill-formed — but it pretends to precision through normalisation that is arbitrary.
3.Honesty about what moral philosophy can deliver favours intuitionism — it does not import a false structure of commensurability into a domain where commensurability does not obtain.
∴Intuitionism is more honest than MEC about the limits of moral systematisation — it does not pretend to a precision the subject matter does not support, while MEC imports a false structure of commensurability and pretends that normalisation solves what it merely stipulates.
VI — Particularism: the weight of moral considerations varies by context; MEC's fixed choiceworthiness functions cannot accommodate thisclick for image
1.The weight of a moral consideration can vary across contexts — causing pain is normally a reason against acting but not in the case of surgery; friendship is normally a reason for preferential treatment but not in cases of institutional fairness.
2.MEC requires each theory to assign a fixed choiceworthiness function to outcomes regardless of context — it cannot accommodate this context-sensitivity.
3.This inflexibility means MEC falsifies the structure of the moral considerations it is attempting to aggregate — it is not merely imprecise but structurally wrong.
∴MEC cannot accommodate the context-sensitivity of moral reasons that both Rossian pluralism and Dancy's particularism correctly identify — it is structurally committed to fixed choiceworthiness functions that falsify the moral landscape.
VII — Moral wisdom navigates complexity through cultivated sensitivity, not expected-value maximisationclick for image
1.The practically wise person navigates moral complexity by attending to morally relevant features of each situation, drawing on accumulated moral experience, and forming a judgment sensitive to the full range of considerations.
2.This is what morally excellent people actually do — and the moral judgments of such people are our best guide to moral truth.
3.MEC is a theory for philosophers who have not understood that the goal of moral philosophy is to become wise, not to systematise — it provides a procedure where wisdom is what is needed.
∴Moral wisdom — Aristotelian phronesis — is the correct response to moral complexity; MEC mistakes the goal of moral philosophy for systematisation rather than the cultivation of wisdom, and its verdicts will be worse than those of the practically wise person it is meant to assist.
