Mr President,

The question before this House is not really about whether we should value life. Every government policy that affects mortality implicitly values life. The question is whether we should do so honestly and systematically, or whether we should pretend the trade-offs don't exist and thereby make them worse. The Value of Statistical Life framework represents the honest answer.

VSL definition: the marginal rate of substitution between mortality risk and money — what individuals are willing to pay for a small risk reduction, scaled to a full life saved
Empirical basis: compensating wage differentials in labour markets; stated-preference surveys
Current US regulatory VSL: approximately $11–12 million (2024)
Alternative: the status quo ante — arbitrary, unaccountable, inconsistent valuations across agencies On the principle of consumer sovereignty

Consumer sovereignty and revealed preferencesThe fundamental principle of a free society is that individuals are the best judges of their own welfare. When a worker accepts a wage premium to work in a dangerous occupation — mining, construction, commercial fishing — she is revealing something real and important about how she values the risk she is taking on. She is not deceived. She is not coerced. She is making a trade-off between her safety and her income that reflects her own preferences, her own circumstances, and her own assessment of the risk. The VSL is simply the aggregation of these revealed trade-offs, scaled appropriately. To reject the VSL is to say that this worker's own valuation of her own risk is inadmissible in public policy. That is paternalism of the most thoroughgoing kind.

On the alternative to VSL

The alternative to VSL is arbitrary inconsistencyThe opposition will criticise the VSL for being insufficiently egalitarian, for being biased toward the preferences of the wealthy, for treating human life as a commodity. But what is the alternative? Before systematic VSL analysis, different government agencies assigned wildly different implicit values to human life in their regulatory decisions — from less than a million dollars to hundreds of millions — with no coherent basis for the difference. The result was not greater protection of human life. It was the arbitrary over-regulation of some risks and systematic under-regulation of others. The VSL at least imposes consistency and accountability. The opposition offers high-minded objections without a workable substitute.

On the anti-paternalism argument

Anti-paternalism: the state should not override individual risk preferencesWhen government agencies substitute their own valuations of human life for the valuations of the individuals whose lives are at stake, they are acting paternalistically. They are saying: we know better than you how much your risk reduction is worth to you. But the whole point of a free society is that the state does not make these substitutions without compelling reason. The VSL framework respects individual autonomy by grounding the value of risk reduction in what individuals themselves reveal they are willing to pay. The alternative frameworks — social welfare functions, prioritarian weighting, philosophical constructs about the separateness of persons — are all ways of substituting the preferences of academic economists and philosophers for the preferences of the actual people whose lives and risks are in question.

On the income variation objection

Income variation is not a defect but an honest featureThe opposition will note that VSL varies with income — that richer individuals have a higher willingness to pay for risk reduction. They will present this as a defect, as evidence of bias toward the wealthy. But this variation is not a defect of the VSL framework. It is an honest reflection of reality. A richer person has more resources to spend on safety; she will spend more on it. To insist on equalising VSL across income levels is to impose a philosophical preference for equality that the individuals themselves have not expressed. The equalised VSL is not more egalitarian; it is simply dishonest about what people's actual preferences are.

On VSL as the foundation of rational regulation

VSL as foundation for consistent, rational regulationThe free market in risk is not perfect. Workers face information asymmetries; they may not know the true risks they face. Regulation to correct these asymmetries is legitimate. But the regulation that is warranted is the regulation that reflects what informed individuals would choose for themselves. The VSL is our best estimate of that. A regulation that costs more per life saved than the VSL is a regulation that imposes costs on individuals that they themselves would not choose to bear. A government that ignores this calculation is not protecting its citizens. It is overriding their preferences in ways they would reject if they understood what was being done.

The VSL is not a perfect framework. It is the most honest, the most consistent, and the most respectful of human agency that we have. The opposition's alternatives are not more human. They are more paternalistic.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I have spent my career developing and refining the empirical methods for estimating the value of statistical life from labour-market data. I want to make the case that the VSL framework is not merely a convenient simplification. It is the most rigorous, most empirically grounded, and most policy-relevant measure of how individuals actually value reductions in mortality risk that we possess.

On compensating wage differentials and revealed preferences

Compensating wage differentials: the empirical foundationThe empirical foundation of VSL is the theory of compensating wage differentials, pioneered by Adam Smith in the Wealth of Nations and formalised by modern labour economics. Workers in dangerous occupations command wage premiums over equally skilled workers in safer occupations. These premiums are not random. They reflect the marginal rate at which workers are willing to trade income for safety — the precise trade-off that defines VSL. Using large-panel datasets linking individual workers to industry-level fatality risk data, my research and that of colleagues has produced robust estimates of VSL in the range of seven to twelve million dollars in current US regulatory use. These estimates are replicated across datasets, across methods, and across countries with consistent results.

On the robustness of the labour-market method

Robustness of the labour-market VSL methodCritics of the labour-market method argue that workers may not have accurate information about the risks they face, that labour markets are not perfectly competitive, and that selection effects bias the estimates. These concerns are real, and they have generated a large and rigorous literature examining each. The results of this examination are not what the critics hoped. Controlling for selection, information quality, and market structure produces VSL estimates that are remarkably stable. Furthermore, stated-preference studies using contingent valuation — survey methods that directly elicit willingness to pay for risk reduction — produce estimates broadly consistent with the revealed-preference labour-market approach. The convergence of methods supports the robustness of the underlying magnitude.

On VSL and the regulation of occupational safety

VSL in regulatory practice: OSHA, EPA, and the settlement of cost-benefit disputesThe VSL was not adopted by US regulatory agencies because economists imposed it on reluctant policymakers. It was adopted because it resolved a genuine practical problem. In the early 1980s, a dispute between the Occupational Safety and Health Administration and the Office of Management and Budget over the appropriate cost to assign to the prevention of a workplace fatality was preventing safety regulations from moving forward. The VSL, derived from labour-market data, provided a principled basis for resolution. The regulatory framework that emerged — now used by OSHA, the EPA, the Department of Transportation, and HHS — has saved many lives precisely because it provided a consistent basis for prioritising life-saving investments across the entire regulatory portfolio.

On the income elasticity of VSL

Income elasticity of VSL and its policy implicationsMy research establishes that the income elasticity of VSL is approximately 0.5 to 0.6: a one percent increase in income produces roughly a half to six-tenths of a percent increase in VSL. This finding has important policy implications. It means that as incomes grow over time, the VSL should be updated upward — and regulatory agencies that fail to do so will systematically underinvest in safety. It also means that using a single VSL across all income groups is a defensible approximation: the income elasticity is less than one, so higher-income individuals' higher VSL does not swamp the picture. The framework is robust to the income variation the opposition treats as damning.

On why the SWF alternatives are not ready for policy use

SWF alternatives lack empirical tractability for policyProfessor Adler's social welfare function framework is intellectually elegant. But it requires interpersonally comparable utility measurements that we do not have and cannot get. To implement a prioritarian SWF in practice, we need to know each affected individual's lifetime utility level, apply a concave transformation, and aggregate. None of these steps is operationally tractable for the regulatory impact analyses that agencies must complete within months. The VSL, by contrast, is estimable from existing data, updated regularly, and applied consistently. The relevant comparison is not VSL against an ideal SWF implemented by omniscient regulators. It is VSL against the kind of prioritarian SWF that real regulators can actually use. There is, at present, no such thing.

The VSL framework is not perfect. But it is empirically grounded, practically tractable, and demonstrably better than the alternatives at guiding life-saving regulation. No alternative framework has yet demonstrated the capacity to replace it.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I have served as Administrator of the White House Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs — the office that oversees all major federal regulation. I have applied the VSL framework to hundreds of regulations affecting millions of lives. I want to make the case for it not as an abstract philosophical position but as a practical matter of governance: the VSL humanises the regulatory state. Its alternatives would not.

On what VSL actually does in the regulatory state

VSL's practical function: preventing both over- and under-regulationThe VSL framework serves two functions simultaneously. First, it prevents under-regulation: when a regulation will save lives at a cost below the VSL threshold, the analysis supports moving forward. Second, it prevents over-regulation: when a regulation costs far more per life saved than the VSL, it is using resources that could save more lives elsewhere. Both functions are essential. A government that does not value life quantitatively will spend far too little preventing deaths from air pollution and highway safety while spending far too much on low-probability catastrophic risks that terrify but rarely kill. The VSL framework is not indifference to human life. It is a tool for ensuring that every dollar of regulatory expenditure saves as many lives as possible.

On VSL and human dignity

VSL as consistent with human dignityThe opposition will argue that putting a dollar figure on life is an affront to human dignity — that some things should not be priced. I take this objection seriously. But consider the alternative. A government that refuses to trade off lives against money is not thereby treating lives as priceless. It is treating them as worthless. Every dollar spent on a regulation that fails the VSL threshold is a dollar not spent on regulations that could prevent more deaths. To pretend that the money-mortality trade-off doesn't exist is not to honour human dignity. It is to guarantee worse outcomes while preserving the comfort of moral self-congratulation.

On VSL and the problem of dread risks

VSL and the correction of dread-risk biasOne of the most important functions of the VSL framework in the regulatory process is its resistance to what I call dread-risk bias. People are systematically more frightened of certain risks — nuclear power, toxic chemicals, aeroplane crashes — than of statistically larger risks that are more familiar. Regulators who respond to public fear rather than statistical risk will systematically over-regulate dread risks and under-regulate mundane risks that kill more people. The VSL framework, by converting risk reductions to a common monetary denominator, imposes a discipline on this tendency. It forces regulators to compare: are we spending our safety budget on the risks that actually kill people, or on the risks that frighten them?

On how VSL can be improved while remaining VSL

VSL improvements: behavioural adjustments, context-sensitivityI do not defend the VSL framework as currently implemented without qualification. I have argued that VSL estimates should be sensitive to context — that people value reductions in cancer risks differently from reductions in acute accident risks, that chronic illness has a different VSL from sudden death, that risks imposed involuntarily may have a higher VSL than voluntarily accepted risks. These refinements are possible within the VSL framework. They do not require abandoning it. The framework is not a single number. It is a methodology for eliciting and applying individual preferences for risk reduction, and that methodology can accommodate greater sophistication without losing its empirical grounding.

On the comparison with alternatives in practice

VSL vs SWF in practice: VSL wins on tractability and accountabilityProfessor Adler's prioritarian SWF is a genuine intellectual advance on utilitarian cost-benefit analysis. It correctly identifies that we should give extra weight to the worse-off. But when I ask how a regulatory agency is supposed to measure each affected individual's lifetime utility level, apply a strictly concave transformation function, and aggregate the results to compare two possible regulations, the answer is: it cannot. Not within the time constraints of regulatory review, not with the data available to agencies, not in a form that is publicly comprehensible and politically accountable. The VSL is comprehensible. It can be explained to Congress, to the public, and to affected industries. That transparency is itself a democratic value the opposition's alternatives sacrifice.

The VSL is not a cold number. It is a commitment to honesty about the trade-offs that government always makes, whether it acknowledges them or not. Humanising the regulatory state means facing those trade-offs clearly.

Mr President,

Cost-benefit analysis using the Value of Statistical Life has been the dominant methodology for US regulatory policy for four decades. It is required by executive order. It is used by every major regulatory agency. And it is wrong — not wrong because it attempts to value life, but wrong because it values life in the wrong way, using the wrong measure, with a systematic bias that disadvantages the poor and ignores distribution. The social welfare function framework I defend is not an impractical academic abstraction. It is a rigorous correction to a deeply flawed methodology.

On the core defect of VSL: willingness-to-pay is biased toward the wealthy

VSL's wealth bias: WTP reflects budget constraints, not well-beingThe VSL framework values mortality risk reduction using willingness-to-pay — how much individuals are willing to spend on small risk reductions. But willingness to pay is not a measure of well-being. It is a measure of how much money you can afford to spend on a good while maintaining your current consumption level. A rich person has a higher willingness to pay for the same risk reduction not because the risk reduction is worth more to her well-being, but because she has more money to spend. The VSL framework therefore systematically assigns higher value to risk reductions enjoyed by the wealthy than to identical risk reductions enjoyed by the poor. This is not distributional neutrality. It is bias toward the rich encoded into regulatory methodology.

On the social welfare function as the correct alternative

Prioritarian SWF: well-being measures and priority for the worse-offThe social welfare function framework I have developed corrects this bias. Instead of aggregating willingness-to-pay, it aggregates individual well-being — a measure of how well-off each person actually is, not how much money she can spend. And it uses a prioritarian specification: improvements in well-being at lower absolute levels of well-being count for more than identical improvements at higher levels. This means that a risk reduction enjoyed by a poorer, worse-off person is assigned greater social weight than the same risk reduction enjoyed by a richer, better-off person. This is not an arbitrary political preference. It is the morally correct reflection of the diminishing marginal social value of well-being as well-being increases.

On the VSL framework's failure under uncertainty

VSL fails to distinguish ex ante and ex post valuation properlyVSL values risk reductions ex ante — before the uncertain outcome is resolved. This can generate deeply problematic recommendations. Consider a policy that provides a small risk reduction to a large number of people versus a policy that provides a much larger risk reduction to a small number of people who face a much higher baseline risk. VSL-based cost-benefit analysis aggregates across individuals using willingness-to-pay weights, and may recommend the broadly distributed small reduction even when the concentrated large reduction would save more lives and be preferred by every reasonable measure of equity. Prioritarian SWF analysis, applied ex post, correctly weights the outcomes by the well-being levels of those actually affected, and can recommend the equity-sensitive option.

On the tractability objection

SWF is more tractable than opponents claimProfessor Viscusi argues that prioritarian SWF analysis is not operationally tractable for regulatory agencies. This objection is weaker than it appears. The SWF framework does not require precise measurement of individual lifetime utility. It requires interpersonally comparable well-being indices — measures that translate policy outcomes into comparable assessments of individual welfare. Such indices are already in use in health economics: quality-adjusted life years (QALYs), for example, are a widely used approximation. The conceptual gap between current practice and a prioritarian SWF is much smaller than the proposition implies. The barrier is not tractability but political will to move beyond the comfortable inertia of VSL.

On how prioritarian SWF changes policy recommendations

Prioritarian SWF vs VSL: different policy recommendations on age and incomeMy research with James Hammitt and others has shown that prioritarian SWF recommendations for life-saving policy differ systematically from VSL-based recommendations in morally important ways. The prioritarian SWF correctly gives priority to the young over the old for life-saving interventions — not because the young are more valuable as persons, but because they have more lifetime well-being still ahead and are therefore at a lower lifetime well-being level at the point of intervention. And crucially, the prioritarian SWF gives equal social value to risk reductions for the rich and the poor — correcting the systematic wealth bias of VSL. These are not minor technical differences. They are morally fundamental.

The VSL framework has served as a useful first approximation. For a methodology that requires executive order to be taken seriously by regulators, it is past time for a second approximation that is actually morally defensible.

Mr President,

I want to push the critique of VSL in a different direction from Professor Adler. His concern is distributional: VSL systematically privileges the preferences of the wealthy. My concern is philosophical: VSL aggregates across persons in a way that violates the separateness of persons and fails to take seriously what each individual is owed. I draw on contractualist moral theory — on the idea that principles governing our collective life must be justifiable to each person affected by them — to argue that VSL cannot be so justified.

On the separateness of persons and aggregation

The separateness of persons and the aggregation problemVSL aggregates willingness-to-pay across thousands or millions of individuals to calculate the total benefit of a risk-reducing regulation. The implicit assumption is that it is appropriate to sum individual risk preferences in this way — that small risk reductions for many people can compensate for the failure to provide larger risk reductions for a smaller number of people who face higher baseline risks. But this aggregation ignores the separateness of persons. The person who faces a 1-in-10 risk of death is not comforted by the knowledge that a thousand people faced 1-in-10,000 risks that were reduced. Each person's claim on our attention is individual, not fungible. A framework that routinely recommends policies that serve many people marginally over a few people desperately is not morally neutral. It has taken a controversial aggregative position.

On ex ante versus ex post valuation

Ex ante vs ex post: VSL conflates what matters morallyVSL policy analysis is conducted ex ante: it values risk reductions before the uncertain outcomes are resolved, and aggregates ex ante willingness-to-pay across all affected individuals. But moral evaluation of outcomes should often be conducted ex post: we should ask what outcomes actually befall actual persons and evaluate their moral weight at that level. The ex ante approach conflates the probability of a bad outcome with the magnitude of the bad outcome if it occurs. A policy that prevents certain deaths for a small number of high-risk individuals may be morally superior to a policy that marginally reduces risk for a large number of low-risk individuals, even if the VSL calculation favours the latter. The ex ante aggregation systematically obscures this.

On what individuals are owed — the claims approach

The contractualist claims approach vs VSL aggregationI have argued, drawing on T.M. Scanlon's contractualism, that moral evaluation of risk policy should focus on the strength of the claims of each individual affected, not on the aggregate sum of willingness-to-pay. A person who faces a high baseline risk of death has a strong individual claim to protection that cannot be overridden simply because many people with weaker claims — lower baseline risks — sum to a larger aggregate willingness-to-pay. The VSL framework, by aggregating without limit, allows very large numbers of small claims to override the urgent claims of a few. This is morally problematic: the strong claim of a person facing certain death should not be outweighed by the summed marginal preferences of the many facing tiny risks.

On VSL and identified versus statistical lives

Identified vs statistical lives: VSL ignores the moral differenceThere is a well-documented moral asymmetry between how people respond to identified lives — the child trapped in a well, the named victim of a disaster — and statistical lives — the anonymous expected deaths prevented by an air-quality regulation. VSL analysis treats statistical lives as if this asymmetry were a cognitive bias to be corrected rather than a moral signal to be attended to. But I argue that the concern for identified lives reflects a genuine moral insight: that individuals have claims on us that are not exhausted by their expected-value contributions to aggregate welfare. A framework that systematically suppresses this concern in the name of efficiency is not morally neutral. It has adopted a controversial utilitarian position without acknowledging it.

On the universal health coverage case

Universal health coverage and VSL's limits: who gets priority?My work with the WHO on making fair choices toward universal health coverage demonstrates why the VSL framework is inadequate for the most important life-and-death allocation decisions. When resources are scarce and must be allocated across populations, VSL recommends allocating to those with the highest willingness-to-pay — in practice, the richest and those with the longest remaining healthy life expectancy. A fair framework must ask instead: who has the strongest claim? Who is worst off? Who has received the fewest benefits from the health system so far? These questions cannot be answered by aggregating willingness-to-pay. They require the kind of moral reasoning about individual claims and fairness that VSL is structurally incapable of performing.

VSL is not wrong to attempt to value risk reductions. It is wrong to value them by aggregating individual preferences in a way that ignores the strength of individual claims and the separateness of persons.

Argument I — VSL aggregation violates the separateness of personsclick for image
1.VSL aggregates willingness-to-pay across individuals, allowing small risk reductions for many to outweigh larger risk reductions for a few who face higher baseline risks.
2.This aggregation ignores the separateness of persons: the person facing a high baseline risk is not compensated by others' willingness-to-pay for smaller reductions.
3.A framework that routinely recommends serving many marginally over serving a few urgently has adopted a controversial aggregative position without acknowledging it.
∴VSL's unconstrained aggregation violates the separateness of persons; the framework's apparent neutrality conceals a contested utilitarian commitment.
Argument II — Ex ante VSL conflates probability with the moral weight of actual outcomesclick for image
1.VSL analysis is conducted ex ante, aggregating willingness-to-pay for risk reductions before uncertain outcomes are resolved.
2.This conflates the probability of a bad outcome with its moral weight if it occurs; ex post evaluation of what actually befalls actual persons is morally important but suppressed by ex ante aggregation.
3.A policy preventing certain deaths for high-risk individuals may be morally superior to one marginally reducing risk for many, even when VSL analysis favours the latter.
∴VSL's ex ante framework systematically obscures morally important ex post considerations about what actually happens to actual persons.
Argument III — Individual claims cannot be overridden by aggregate WTPclick for image
1.Contractualist moral theory holds that principles must be justifiable to each affected person; a person facing high baseline risk has a strong individual claim that aggregative WTP cannot override.
2.VSL allows very large numbers of small claims (low baseline risk, low WTP) to sum to a value that overrides the urgent claim of a person facing very high baseline risk.
3.This is morally unacceptable: the urgent claim of a person at great risk should not be outweighed by the aggregated marginal preferences of the many facing tiny risks.
∴VSL's unconstrained aggregation allows urgent individual claims to be overridden by summed marginal preferences; contractualist fairness requires a claims-based alternative.

Mr President,

My colleagues have argued that VSL produces the wrong numbers — that it is distributionally biased and philosophically aggregative in ways that violate individual claims. These are powerful arguments. I want to make a different kind of argument: that the VSL framework is not merely technically deficient but expresses an impoverished moral vision of what it means to value human life, and that this impoverishment has costs that no technical correction can repair.

On the commodification of mortality risk

Commodification corrupts the goods it pricesWhen we assign a dollar figure to mortality risk reduction — when we say that a statistical life is worth $11 million — we are treating the value of human life as if it were the same kind of thing as the value of a car or a refrigerator: a value determined by what people are willing to pay in a market. But some things are corrupted when they are subjected to market valuation. The value of human life is not the price individuals are willing to pay to avoid small probabilities of death. It is something categorically different: a dignity, a moral status, an inviolability that market pricing cannot capture and that market pricing, when applied, tends to crowd out. The language of VSL shifts our moral vocabulary from the sacred to the instrumental, and this shift has costs that Professor Friedman's framework is incapable of registering.

On the limits of willingness-to-pay as a moral measure

WTP reflects constraints, not values; poor people's preferences are distorted by scarcityThe proposition presents willingness-to-pay as a neutral, preference-respecting measure. It is not. When a low-income worker accepts a dangerous job because she needs the income and has no better option, her revealed willingness-to-accept dangerous conditions does not reflect her genuine valuation of safety. It reflects the constraints of poverty. The VSL framework mistakes adaptive preferences — preferences deformed by unjust background conditions — for authentic preferences. A miner who accepts rock-fall risk because her only other option is unemployment is not thereby revealing that she values her life at some particular dollar amount. She is revealing the desperation of her circumstances. To build a framework for valuing human life on these preferences is to launder injustice into regulatory methodology.

On what VSL crowds out

Market norms crowd out moral norms in the VSL frameworkThere are two ways of valuing goods. One is through markets, prices, and willingness-to-pay. Another is through the moral, civic, and religious frameworks that treat certain goods as beyond price — as things that should not be traded off against money at all. When market pricing is applied to goods that were previously governed by non-market norms, something important changes. The non-market norms tend to erode. The good tends to be treated as though it were the kind of thing that can be bought and sold. I am not arguing that mortality risk can never be traded off against resources. The trade-offs are real and unavoidable. I am arguing that the VSL framework, by formalising and operationalising these trade-offs in the language of willingness-to-pay, entrenches a market vocabulary for human life that crowds out the vocabulary of dignity, solidarity, and inviolability that should also inform public deliberation about how to protect people from death.

On the democratic legitimacy of VSL decisions

VSL removes life-and-death decisions from democratic deliberationThe VSL framework presents itself as a tool for rational, technocratic governance. But the question of how much we as a society should spend to prevent deaths is not a technical question with a correct answer derivable from revealed preferences. It is a deeply contested moral and political question. When regulatory agencies use VSL to resolve this question, they are substituting a particular economic methodology — grounded in individual willingness-to-pay derived from labour markets — for the democratic deliberation that such questions require. They are presenting a philosophical commitment dressed as a technical procedure. The appropriate forum for deciding how to value human life in public policy is democratic debate about the kind of society we want to be, not optimisation over hedonic wage regression coefficients.

On the alternative to VSL as a mode of public reasoning

Democratic deliberation over markets in mortality riskI am not arguing for paralysis — for refusing to make difficult life-and-death trade-offs. I am arguing for a different mode of reasoning about those trade-offs: one that is explicit about the moral values at stake, that invites public deliberation rather than suppressing it behind technical language, and that resists the reduction of human dignity to a willingness-to-pay figure. The regulatory state can and should make cost-benefit comparisons. But it should do so in a mode that acknowledges the moral gravity of what it is doing, that invites democratic accountability for its value choices, and that does not present contested philosophical commitments as though they were merely technical parameters. The VSL framework does precisely the opposite.

The question is not whether we can put a price on human life. In practice, we always do. The question is whether we should formalise and institutionalise this pricing in a framework that crowds out the moral vocabulary we need to think clearly about what we are doing.

Thank you.
