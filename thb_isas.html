Proposition (ISA as Shelter) • Johnson · Engen/Gale · Sturrock · Kay  —  Opposition (ISA as Correct Theory) • Mirrlees · Thaler · Poterba · Haldane
The Distributional CritiquePaul Johnson — Director, Institute for Fiscal Studies
The ISA's tax relief is "upside down": its value rises with the marginal tax rate and the size of the balance an individual can already afford to lock away. The annual allowance is nominally available to everyone, but only those with substantial uncommitted savings — disproportionately older, wealthier households — can use much of it. The result is a multi-billion-pound annual fiscal cost that flows overwhelmingly to people who would have saved regardless, while contributing little to the financial resilience of the median household.

The Asset-Reshuffling CritiqueEric Engen & William Gale — the "no new saving" school (US tax-advantaged saving literature)
The central question for any tax-advantaged savings vehicle is not how much flows into it, but how much of that flow represents genuinely new saving versus a relabelling of existing assets. "Bed and ISA" — moving an existing taxable holding into an ISA wrapper — is, by construction, not new saving. Under standard lifecycle and permanent-income models, a rational unconstrained household's total saving should be invariant to which account it is held in. The empirical evidence from the equivalent US debate over IRAs and 401(k)s suggests most contributions by higher-income households are reshuffled, not new, and that genuinely liquidity-constrained households — the ones for whom new saving would matter most — are precisely the ones who cannot use the allowance.

Cumulative Allowances & the "ISA Millionaire"David Sturrock — IFS, wealth and inheritance research
The ISA's annual "use it or lose it" allowance looks egalitarian in any single year, but its effect compounds over decades into something starkly unequal. Households that have been able to max out the allowance every year since 1999 — or its predecessor PEPs since 1987 — have built tax-free portfolios worth hundreds of thousands or even millions of pounds, every pound of growth and income on which is permanently exempt. The Additional Permitted Subscription on the death of a spouse extends this shelter across a couple's lifetime and into the next generation's hands. This is not a savings policy; it is a mechanism for the tax-free compounding of wealth that already exists.

The Cash ISA Is a Redundant, Behaviourally-Exploited ProductJohn Kay — Financial Times columnist; Kay Review of UK Equity Markets
Since the introduction of the Personal Savings Allowance in 2016, the overwhelming majority of basic-rate taxpayers can earn up to £1,000 of savings interest a year — and higher-rate taxpayers up to £500 — entirely tax-free in an ordinary account. For most realistic balances, a Cash ISA now confers no tax advantage whatsoever over a normal easy-access account. Yet tens of billions of pounds remain parked in Cash ISAs, frequently earning rates below the best non-ISA accounts, sustained purely by brand loyalty and the psychological framing of "ISA" as the responsible choice. The wrapper has become a tool for extracting a "loyalty penalty" from savers who believe, wrongly, that they are obtaining a tax benefit.

Chair,

I want to start with a simple question that I think gets obscured by the way ISAs are usually discussed. The policy is presented — and was originally sold — as a savings incentive for ordinary people: a simple, accessible wrapper that helps the typical household build a financial cushion. I want to ask: given the actual structure of the allowance, who is actually in a position to benefit from it, and by how much?

An allowance that is formally universal but only usable by those with spare resources well above what most households hold is not, in any meaningful sense, a universal benefit. It is a benefit for the constrained few who happen to already have the assets the allowance is designed to shelter.
i. the structure of the relief is "upside down"

Upside down — the value of ISA tax relief to an individual depends on (a) their marginal rate of tax on the income/gains the wrapper shelters, and (b) the size of the balance they can afford to lock inside it; both of these increase with income and wealth, so the relief is regressive by constructionThe value of any tax exemption depends on two things: the rate of tax that would otherwise apply, and the size of the base being exempted. On both dimensions, the ISA's relief scales up with income and wealth. A higher-rate or additional-rate taxpayer sheltering dividend and capital gains income that would otherwise be taxed at 33.75% or 39.35%, or at 20%/24% capital gains rates, receives a far larger benefit per pound sheltered than a basic-rate taxpayer whose dividend and CGT allowances might cover most of their actual liability anyway. And the size of the balance that can be sheltered is determined entirely by how much someone can afford to set aside — the £20,000 annual allowance is, for the median household, an entirely theoretical ceiling, while for a household with a substantial investment portfolio it is the binding constraint on how quickly they can move assets into permanent tax exemption. A relief whose value rises with the marginal tax rate and with the saver's existing wealth is regressive by construction, regardless of its formally universal availability.

ii. the fiscal cost and its opportunity cost

Fiscal cost — the revenue forgone through ISA tax relief runs into the billions annually; this is revenue that could fund more targeted interventions; the question is not whether ISAs do any good but whether this is the best use of several billion pounds of forgone revenue each yearThe Exchequer forgoes several billion pounds in income tax, dividend tax, and capital gains tax revenue every year as a direct result of ISA holdings. This is not a cost-free policy; it is a substantial annual transfer, financed by all taxpayers, concentrated on those who hold ISA wealth. The relevant comparison is not "ISAs versus nothing" — it is "ISAs versus what else several billion pounds a year could achieve." A targeted savings-matching scheme for low-income households, of the kind piloted in Help to Save, costs a fraction of this for a much more progressive distribution of benefit, reaching households who are currently unable to build even a modest emergency fund. When a policy's stated goal is to encourage saving among ordinary households, but its actual fiscal incidence is concentrated among households who were already saving substantial sums, the policy's design has become disconnected from its stated rationale.

iii. the "everyone can use it" defence fails on the data

Take-up data — the proportion of eligible adults who use anything close to the full annual allowance is small and concentrated in higher income deciles; most ISA holders, particularly Cash ISA holders, hold balances far below the level at which the allowance becomes binding, meaning the marginal relief accrues to a small group with large balancesThe defence offered for the ISA's universality is that everyone is, in principle, free to contribute up to the annual limit. But the relevant question for assessing distributional incidence is not who is formally permitted to contribute, but who actually does, and to what extent. The data on ISA subscriptions show a heavily skewed pattern: a small proportion of account holders contribute amounts close to the annual limit, and these "maximisers" account for a disproportionate share of total ISA wealth and of the associated tax relief. The median ISA balance is modest; the relief associated with it is correspondingly modest. The relief associated with the small number of large, maximised portfolios is what drives the bulk of the fiscal cost. A policy whose benefits are this concentrated cannot be defended by appeal to its formal universality — the same could be said of almost any tax relief targeted, in form, at "everyone."

I yield the floor.

Argument I — ISA relief is regressive by construction: its value scales with marginal tax rate and with the saver's existing wealth
1.The value of a tax exemption depends on the marginal rate that would otherwise apply and the size of the base exempted. Both of these increase with income and wealth.
2.Higher-rate and additional-rate taxpayers sheltering larger balances receive proportionately greater relief per pound than basic-rate taxpayers with modest balances who may face little or no liability outside the wrapper anyway (given dividend and CGT allowances).
3.A relief structured this way is regressive in its incidence regardless of its formally universal eligibility; formal availability to all is not the same as benefit accruing to all in proportion to need.
∴ISA tax relief is regressive by construction: its value rises with the marginal tax rate and the size of the sheltered balance, both of which correlate with income and existing wealth.
Argument II — The multi-billion-pound annual fiscal cost is poorly targeted relative to alternative uses of the same revenue
1.ISA relief represents several billion pounds of forgone tax revenue annually — a real fiscal cost financed by all taxpayers.
2.Targeted alternatives (e.g., savings-matching for low-income households) cost a fraction of this for a more progressive distribution, reaching households currently unable to build basic financial resilience.
3.A policy whose stated goal is encouraging saving among ordinary households, but whose fiscal incidence is concentrated among those already saving substantial sums, has become disconnected from its rationale.
∴The opportunity cost of ISA relief — measured against better-targeted alternatives — is a central part of the case that the policy no longer serves its stated purpose.

Chair,

I want to take this debate to the question that, in our view, should be prior to any distributional analysis: does the ISA actually increase the total amount that British households save, or does it simply change which account their existing savings sit in? This is the question that animated the equivalent debate over IRAs and 401(k)s, and we think the lessons of that debate transfer directly.

i. "bed and ISA" is definitionally not new saving

Bed and ISA — the common practice of selling an existing taxable holding and immediately repurchasing the same or similar asset within an ISA wrapper is, by construction, a transfer of existing wealth into a tax-advantaged account, not an act of saving; yet it counts toward ISA subscription statistics exactly as if it were new saving"Bed and ISA" is among the most common transactions that financial advisers recommend, and it is worth being precise about what it is: an investor sells an asset they already hold outside an ISA, realising any gain (often using their annual CGT allowance to do so tax-efficiently), and immediately repurchases the same or an equivalent asset inside an ISA wrapper. The investor's total wealth is unchanged. Their consumption is unchanged. Nothing about their saving behaviour has changed. What has changed is the tax treatment of the future income and gains on an asset they already owned. Yet this transaction counts, in the headline statistics on ISA subscriptions, exactly as if £20,000 of new saving had occurred. We do not have precise figures on what fraction of subscriptions represent this kind of reshuffling versus genuinely new contributions from current income, but the practice is sufficiently well-established — sufficiently central to mainstream financial advice — that it must represent a substantial share of the headline numbers, particularly among the larger contributions that, as Professor Johnson has shown, dominate the aggregate.

ii. the fungibility problem

Fungibility — under the standard lifecycle/permanent-income model, a household's optimal consumption-saving path is determined by lifetime resources and preferences, not by the labels on the accounts those resources sit in; a rational unconstrained household's response to a new tax-advantaged account is simply to relabel existing assets, leaving total saving unchangedThe theoretical foundation for scepticism here is the lifecycle/permanent-income hypothesis, which is the standard framework for thinking about household saving decisions. On this framework, a household's consumption and saving decisions in any period are determined by an assessment of lifetime resources relative to lifetime consumption needs — not by which account a given pound of wealth happens to be held in. Money is fungible: a pound in an ISA, a pound in a taxable brokerage account, and a pound of home equity are, for the purposes of this optimisation, the same pound. If a household was already saving optimally given its lifetime resources, the introduction of a new tax-advantaged account does not change the optimal level of saving — it only changes which account the existing optimal saving is held in, since holding it in the tax-advantaged account is strictly better (lower tax, same liquidity within the wrapper's rules) and any rational household will therefore relabel. The prediction of the standard model is that ISA introduction should produce a one-time portfolio reallocation, not a sustained increase in the saving rate.

iii. the households who could benefit can't use it

Liquidity constraints — for the fungibility argument to fail and genuinely new saving to occur, the household must be liquidity-constrained — unable to simply relabel existing assets because they have none, or unwilling/unable to access funds locked elsewhere; but liquidity-constrained households are precisely those without spare income to direct into a new savings vehicle in the first placeThe fungibility argument has a well-known escape route: it applies only to households that are not liquidity-constrained — households that have either existing assets to relabel, or access to credit that lets them maintain consumption while directing current income into the new account. For a genuinely liquidity-constrained household — one with no spare assets and no spare borrowing capacity — the introduction of a tax-advantaged account could in principle induce new saving, because there is nothing to relabel. But this is precisely the population for whom the ISA allowance is least relevant in practice: a household with no spare assets and no spare borrowing capacity also, by definition, has no spare income to direct into a £20,000-a-year savings vehicle. The households for whom the fungibility critique does not apply are the households least able to use the product; the households who can use the product are, almost definitionally, the households for whom the fungibility critique applies most strongly. This is not a coincidence of implementation; it follows from the basic structure of who has spare resources to allocate.

We yield the floor.

Argument I — "Bed and ISA" transactions are counted as new saving in subscription statistics despite representing no change in wealth, consumption, or saving behaviour
1."Bed and ISA": selling an existing taxable holding and repurchasing the same/equivalent asset inside an ISA. Total wealth and consumption are unchanged; only the tax treatment of future returns changes.
2.This transaction counts in headline ISA subscription statistics identically to a genuinely new contribution from current income.
3.Given how central "Bed and ISA" is to mainstream financial advice, particularly for larger portfolios, it likely represents a substantial share of the headline subscription figures — especially the large contributions that dominate the aggregate.
∴Headline ISA subscription figures overstate genuinely new saving because they include "Bed and ISA" reshuffling, which is definitionally not new saving but is recorded identically to it.
Argument II — Under the standard lifecycle/permanent-income model, fungibility implies ISA introduction produces portfolio reallocation, not increased saving
1.The lifecycle/permanent-income model: optimal saving is determined by lifetime resources and consumption needs, independent of which account wealth is held in. Money is fungible across accounts.
2.If a household was saving optimally before a new tax-advantaged account is introduced, the rational response is to relabel existing assets into the new account (strictly better tax treatment, same underlying wealth) rather than to change the saving rate.
3.The model's prediction is therefore a one-time reallocation of existing wealth into the tax-advantaged wrapper, not a sustained increase in the aggregate saving rate.
∴The standard model of household saving predicts that ISA introduction primarily reallocates existing wealth rather than increasing the aggregate saving rate, absent some departure from the model's assumptions.
Argument III — The households for whom fungibility fails (liquidity-constrained) are precisely those who cannot use the ISA allowance
1.The fungibility critique applies only to households with assets to relabel or credit access to smooth consumption. Liquidity-constrained households — with neither — could in principle generate genuinely new saving via a tax-advantaged account.
2.But a liquidity-constrained household, by definition, also lacks spare current income to direct into a £20,000-a-year savings vehicle.
3.The population for whom the fungibility critique does not apply (liquidity-constrained households) is therefore largely the same population that cannot use the product; the population that can use the product is the population for whom fungibility applies most strongly.
∴The structure of who has spare resources to use the ISA allowance ensures that the product is least likely to generate new saving precisely where new saving would matter most, and most likely to generate reshuffling precisely where reshuffling is most feasible.

I want to shift the focus from the flow — the annual allowance — to the stock: the accumulated wealth that decades of ISA and PEP allowances have produced, and what that stock implies for wealth inequality between and within generations.

i. the arithmetic of compounding tax-free allowances

Compounding — an individual who has used the maximum allowance every year since PEPs began in 1987, and ISAs from 1999, investing in growth assets, would by now hold a portfolio worth a substantial multiple of the cumulative contributions, with every pound of that growth permanently tax-free; this is the source of "ISA millionaire" press storiesConsider what happens to someone who, since 1999 (and before that, through Personal Equity Plans from 1987), has consistently used their full ISA/PEP allowance and invested it in a diversified portfolio of equities. The annual allowance has varied over time but has generally been a meaningful sum — in recent years £20,000. Compounded over more than two decades at typical long-run equity returns, the cumulative contributions alone would amount to several hundred thousand pounds; with growth, a portfolio well into seven figures is entirely plausible for someone who started early and contributed consistently. Every pound of dividend income and capital gain on that portfolio — built up over decades — is permanently exempt from tax, for as long as the money remains in the wrapper, which in practice can be indefinitely. This is the origin of the "ISA millionaire" stories that periodically appear in the financial press. It is not a marginal curiosity; it is the predictable arithmetic outcome of a policy that has now been running, in one form or another, for nearly four decades.

ii. the additional permitted subscription and intergenerational transfer

APS — on the death of an ISA holder, their spouse or civil partner receives an Additional Permitted Subscription equal to the value of the deceased's ISA, allowing that value to be moved into the survivor's own ISA wrapper without counting against the survivor's annual allowance, preserving the tax-free status of the inherited wealthThe shelter does not end with the original holder. On the death of an ISA holder, their surviving spouse or civil partner is entitled to an Additional Permitted Subscription — an extra ISA allowance equal to the value of the deceased's ISA at the date of death, on top of the survivor's own annual allowance. This allows the full value of the inherited ISA to be moved into the survivor's wrapper and to retain its tax-free status, rather than being subject to ordinary tax treatment as inherited assets generally would be once outside a wrapper. For a couple who have both maximised their allowances over decades, this mechanism allows a combined, tax-sheltered portfolio to pass intact from one spouse to the other, continuing to compound tax-free. While the ISA itself does not escape inheritance tax on death to a non-spouse, the spousal mechanism means that for many couples the shelter effectively operates across two lifetimes before IHT becomes relevant at all — by which point the embedded gains, accumulated entirely tax-free for decades, can be very substantial.

iii. the design that looks fair in any one year is unfair across decades

Annual fairness vs lifetime unfairness — the "use it or lose it" annual allowance, with no carry-forward (unlike pension annual allowance carry-forward), looks egalitarian because everyone faces the same cap each year; but the cumulative effect over a lifetime depends entirely on how many years someone has had access to a meaningful allowance and how much spare capital they had each year to use it, which is highly correlated with age and existing wealthThe ISA's defenders often point to the egalitarian appearance of the annual cap: everyone, rich or poor, faces the same £20,000 ceiling each year, and unlike the pension annual allowance, there is no carry-forward of unused allowance from previous years — a design that, on its face, prevents the wealthy from "banking" unused allowances. But this annual symmetry conceals a profound lifetime asymmetry. The total tax-free capacity an individual has accumulated depends on how many years they have had access to a meaningful allowance — which depends on their age — and on how much spare capital they had available to use that allowance in each of those years — which depends on their wealth. A 60-year-old who has had access to an ISA or PEP allowance for their entire working life, and who has had the means to use it fully in most of those years, has accumulated a tax-free shelter that a 25-year-old, facing the same £20,000 annual cap today but with a fraction of the spare capital and only a few years of access, cannot possibly replicate — even if the younger person eventually earns just as much, because the tax-free compounding of the older saver's decades-old contributions cannot be undone or shared. A policy that treats everyone identically in any given year can still produce, and in this case has produced, dramatically unequal lifetime and intergenerational outcomes.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Decades of compounded allowances produce seven-figure tax-free portfolios for early, consistent users — the "ISA millionaire" outcome is the predictable arithmetic of the policy's history
1.An individual maximising PEP/ISA allowances since 1987/1999 and investing in equities would, given typical long-run returns, accumulate cumulative contributions of several hundred thousand pounds, growing to a seven-figure portfolio with compounding.
2.Every pound of dividend income and capital gain on this portfolio is permanently tax-exempt for as long as it remains in the wrapper — which can be indefinite.
3.This is not an edge case but the predictable outcome of nearly four decades of a policy structured this way, for anyone with the means to participate consistently from early on.
∴"ISA millionaire" outcomes are the structural, predictable result of compounding tax-free allowances over decades — not an unintended side effect but the arithmetic consequence of the policy's design and history.
Argument II — The Additional Permitted Subscription extends the shelter across a couple's combined lifetimes, deferring the point at which inherited wealth faces ordinary tax treatment
1.On an ISA holder's death, their spouse receives an Additional Permitted Subscription equal to the deceased's ISA value, allowing that value to retain tax-free status within the survivor's wrapper.
2.For couples who have both maximised allowances over decades, this allows a combined tax-sheltered portfolio to pass intact between spouses, continuing to compound tax-free.
3.While IHT applies on transfers to non-spouses, the spousal mechanism means the shelter can operate across two lifetimes before this becomes relevant, by which point embedded gains are very substantial.
∴The Additional Permitted Subscription extends the tax-free compounding of ISA wealth across a couple's combined lifetimes, deepening the intergenerational wealth advantage the policy confers on long-term, high-balance holders.

1.The annual cap applies identically to everyone each year, with no carry-forward — an apparently egalitarian design.
2.But total accumulated tax-free capacity depends on years of access (correlated with age) and spare capital available to use the allowance each year (correlated with existing wealth).

I want to bring this debate down from the level of portfolios in the hundreds of thousands of pounds to the level of the ordinary saver with a few thousand pounds in a building society account — because it is at this level that the ISA brand does its most quietly damaging work, and where the gap between the product's reputation and its actual function is widest.

i. the personal savings allowance made cash ISAs redundant for most people

PSA redundancy — since 2016, basic-rate taxpayers can earn up to £1,000 of savings interest tax-free in any account, and higher-rate taxpayers up to £500; given prevailing interest rates, this covers the interest on tens of thousands of pounds of savings for most people, meaning a Cash ISA provides no additional tax benefit over an ordinary savings account for the great majority of saversThe Personal Savings Allowance, introduced in 2016, lets a basic-rate taxpayer earn up to £1,000 of interest from savings, in any account, completely free of tax; for higher-rate taxpayers the figure is £500. Do the arithmetic at any realistic interest rate and this allowance covers the interest income on a very substantial sum of money — for a basic-rate taxpayer, tens of thousands of pounds at typical rates. The overwhelming majority of people with cash savings have balances well within the range where the PSA already renders their interest income tax-free, with or without an ISA wrapper. For these savers — which is to say, for most people with a Cash ISA — the "ISA" label confers literally no additional tax benefit over an ordinary savings account paying the same rate. The product's entire value proposition, for this population, has quietly evaporated, while the marketing and the consumer behaviour built around it have not caught up.

ii. the loyalty penalty

Loyalty penalty — providers price Cash ISAs knowing that ISA savers exhibit lower switching rates than savers in ordinary accounts, due in part to the perceived complexity of ISA transfer rules; this allows providers to offer below-best-buy rates on ISA products while maintaining higher rates on equivalent non-ISA accounts aimed at more price-sensitive saversIf the ISA wrapper conferred no benefit and no cost, it would simply be an irrelevance. But it is not cost-free, because of how providers price it. Savings providers are well aware that money held in Cash ISAs is "stickier" — less likely to be moved — than money in ordinary easy-access accounts, in part because of a perception (often inaccurate, but persistent) that moving money out of an ISA risks losing its tax-advantaged status, and in part because of the simple psychological framing of an ISA as a long-term, "set and forget" product. This stickiness allows providers to offer Cash ISA rates below those available on their best easy-access accounts, extracting a "loyalty penalty" from savers who believe — for the reasons just discussed, often wrongly — that they are obtaining a tax benefit that compensates for the lower headline rate. The ISA brand, in this segment of the market, functions less as a tax-advantaged savings vehicle and more as a mechanism that channels unsophisticated savers into lower-yielding products, under cover of a tax rationale that, for most of them, no longer applies.

iii. the two-tier system: simplicity for the unsophisticated, asset location for the sophisticated

Asset location — sophisticated investors and wealth managers use the ISA wrapper as part of "asset location" strategies: placing the most tax-inefficient assets (high-yield bonds, REITs, actively-managed funds generating frequent taxable distributions) inside the ISA, while holding tax-efficient assets (low-turnover equity index funds benefiting from the CGT allowance) outside it — extracting far more value per pound of allowance than an unsophisticated saver who simply holds whatever they already hadThe final point I want to make concerns who actually extracts the most value from the ISA wrapper among those who do understand its mechanics. A sophisticated investor, or their wealth manager, does not simply put "some investments" into an ISA. They practise what is called asset location: they identify which of their holdings generate the most tax-inefficient income — high-yield bonds, real estate investment trusts, actively-managed funds with high turnover and frequent taxable distributions — and place those specifically inside the ISA wrapper, while holding low-turnover equity index funds, which already benefit from favourable capital gains treatment and the annual CGT allowance, outside it. This strategy extracts substantially more tax saving per pound of ISA allowance than simply placing whatever assets one happens to hold into the wrapper indiscriminately — which is what an unsophisticated saver, including most Cash ISA holders, effectively does. The ISA wrapper, then, operates as a genuinely valuable tax-planning tool for those with the financial sophistication to optimise its use, and as a largely symbolic, occasionally costly, brand-loyalty product for everyone else. This two-tier reality sits uncomfortably with the policy's presentation as a simple, universal savings incentive.

The Expenditure-Tax Case: TEE Is the Efficient BenchmarkSir James Mirrlees — Mirrlees Review, Tax by Design (IFS, 2011)
The premise underlying the "tax shelter" framing — that the ordinary taxation of savings income is the neutral baseline, and the ISA is a deviation from it — gets the economics backwards. Taxing the normal return to saving taxes the same income twice: once when it is earned and saved, and again as it grows. This double taxation distorts the choice between consuming now and consuming later, taxing patience itself. A tax-exempt-exempt treatment of savings — taxed when earned, not taxed on growth, not taxed on withdrawal — removes this distortion. The ISA is not a loophole in an otherwise coherent system; it is the one part of the UK's savings taxation that approximates the efficient treatment recommended by mainstream public economics.

Mental Accounting & the "Use It or Lose It" DeadlineRichard Thaler — Misbehaving; "Save More Tomorrow" (with Benartzi, 2004)
The fungibility argument against ISAs is impeccable economics and poor psychology. Decades of behavioural research show that people do not treat money as fungible across mental accounts: money labelled "savings," and especially money inside a ring-fenced, deadline-driven wrapper like an ISA, is treated differently from money in a current account, even though in principle it is the same money. The ISA's annual deadline creates exactly the kind of salient, recurring commitment moment that "Save More Tomorrow"-style interventions exploit to increase saving. The asset-reshuffling critique assumes a frictionless rational agent who, on the evidence, does not exist in large enough numbers to be the right model for this policy.

The asset-reshuffling hypothesis is testable, and when tested in the equivalent US debate over IRAs and 401(k)s, the evidence did not support the strong reshuffling claim. Closing the Equity Gap: Simplification as the Real BenefitAndy Haldane — former Chief Economist, Bank of England; Chief Executive, RSA
The debate so far has focused entirely on the tax relief itself, treating the ISA as nothing but a fiscal transfer. This misses what may be the wrapper's most important effect: the elimination of the administrative burden of tracking and reporting capital gains and dividend income for tax purposes. This burden, more than the headline tax rates, is what has historically deterred ordinary households from holding equities directly — contributing to the UK's persistent "equity gap," in which household wealth is overwhelmingly held in cash and property, earning poor real returns over the long run. By removing this burden, the ISA wrapper has been a precondition for the growth of low-cost retail investment platforms that have genuinely broadened access to capital markets. The Cash ISA redundancy point is well taken — but it is an argument for reforming Cash ISAs, not for abandoning the wrapper concept that has done so much to expand equity ownership.

Chair,

I want to address the framing of this entire motion, because I think it contains a hidden assumption that, once made explicit, is very hard to defend. The motion calls the ISA a "tax shelter" — a phrase that presupposes there is some other, normal treatment of savings income from which the ISA represents a sheltered deviation. I want to ask: what is that normal treatment, and is it one that a well-designed tax system would actually want?

A tax system that takes a pound in tax when it is earned, and then takes further tax from that same pound's return for as long as it remains saved, is not treating saving neutrally relative to consumption. It is taxing the decision to defer consumption — taxing patience — on top of taxing the income itself.
i. the double taxation of saving under an income tax

Double taxation — under a comprehensive income tax, a pound of earnings is taxed once when earned; if saved rather than spent, the return on that saving (interest, dividends, capital gains) is taxed again; a pound that is spent immediately is taxed only once, while a pound that is saved and earns a return is taxed on both the original earning and the return — this differential treatment is a tax on saving relative to consumption, i.e. a tax on deferring consumptionConsider two taxpayers, each earning the same pound of income, taxed identically on that income when it is received. The first spends their pound immediately. The second saves it, and over the following years it earns a return through interest, dividends, or capital appreciation. Under a comprehensive income tax — one that taxes all income, including the return to saving — the second taxpayer pays additional tax on that return, on top of the tax already paid on the original pound when it was earned. The first taxpayer's pound was taxed once. The second taxpayer's pound is taxed once on the principal and again, repeatedly, on the return. The only difference between these two taxpayers is that the second one chose to defer consumption. A tax system that imposes additional tax specifically as a consequence of this choice is, in effect, taxing the choice to defer consumption — taxing patience itself. This is the foundational point of the expenditure-tax literature, going back to Kaldor and before, and it is the starting point for the analysis my colleagues and I conducted in the Mirrlees Review: an income tax that includes the normal return to saving in its base is not a neutral baseline. It is itself a distortionary choice.

ii. TEE as the efficient treatment of the "normal return"

TEE and the normal return — Tax-Exempt-Exempt treatment (taxed on the way in, no tax on growth or withdrawal — exactly the ISA's structure) removes the double taxation of the normal (risk-free, time-value-of-money) component of returns to saving; this is mathematically related to Exempt-Exempt-Taxed (pension-style) treatment, both of which avoid taxing the normal return twice, unlike ordinary taxation of savings incomeThe ISA's structure — tax paid on income when earned (out of which the contribution is made), no tax on the growth of the investment within the wrapper, no tax on withdrawal — is what is called Tax-Exempt-Exempt, or TEE, treatment. This treatment removes the second layer of taxation identified above for what we call the "normal return": roughly, the risk-free, time-value-of-money component of the return to saving, the compensation simply for waiting. TEE treatment is, for this component of returns, broadly equivalent to Exempt-Exempt-Taxed (EET) treatment — the structure used for pensions, where contributions are made from pre-tax income, growth is untaxed, and withdrawals are taxed — under reasonable assumptions about constant tax rates over time. Both TEE and EET avoid double-taxing the normal return; what an ordinary taxable account does, by contrast, is tax this normal return on top of the tax already paid on the income that was saved. The Mirrlees Review's recommendation was not that all savings income should escape taxation in some unprincipled give-away to the wealthy; it was that the specific component of returns that represents pure compensation for deferring consumption should not be taxed twice, and that TEE or EET treatment achieves this. The ISA is one of the very few features of the UK tax system that gets this right.

iii. what should actually be taxed: excess returns

Rate of Return Allowance — the Mirrlees Review proposed that returns above the "normal" rate — excess returns reflecting risk premia, skill, or rents — have a stronger case for taxation than the normal return itself; a Rate of Return Allowance would exempt the normal return (whatever account it's held in) while taxing returns above it; on this view the ISA's flat exemption of ALL returns, including very large excess returns on successful investments, is too generous, but the solution is to extend RRA-style thinking, not to retreat to taxing the normal return everywhereNone of this means that every pound of return, however large, should escape tax merely because it sits inside an ISA. The Mirrlees Review drew a distinction between the normal return — which should not be taxed, for the reasons just given — and excess returns: returns above the normal rate, which might reflect a risk premium that has paid off, exceptional investment skill, or in some cases pure rent. There is a much stronger case, on efficiency and equity grounds, for taxing these excess returns, because taxing them does not distort the basic saving-versus-consumption choice in the way taxing the normal return does. We proposed a Rate of Return Allowance precisely to operationalise this distinction: an allowance, applied to savings wherever held, that would exempt a normal return but leave excess returns taxable. If the concern animating this motion is that very large gains — the kind that produce "ISA millionaires" — escape tax entirely, the right response is to extend something like an RRA-based approach to distinguish normal from excess returns within the ISA, not to abandon TEE treatment of the normal return and reintroduce the double taxation that TEE was designed to remove. The critique, properly understood, is an argument for refining the ISA's design at the margin of very large balances, not for the proposition that the ISA's basic structure constitutes a "shelter" from some otherwise-coherent system.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Ordinary taxation of savings income double-taxes the same pound: once as income, again as the return to deferred consumption
1.Two taxpayers earn an identical pound, taxed identically on receipt. One spends it immediately (taxed once); the other saves it and earns a return, which is then taxed again under a comprehensive income tax.
2.The only difference between the two taxpayers is the choice to defer consumption. The additional tax falls specifically on this choice.
3.A tax system that imposes extra tax as a consequence of deferring consumption is taxing patience — distorting the intertemporal consumption choice, which is not a "neutral baseline" but itself a distortionary feature.
∴The premise that ordinary taxation of savings income is the neutral baseline from which the ISA deviates is false; ordinary taxation of the normal return to saving is itself the distortionary departure from neutrality.
Argument II — TEE treatment (the ISA's structure) removes the double taxation of the normal return, and is broadly equivalent to EET (pension) treatment in this respect
1.TEE (tax-exempt-exempt): taxed on the way in, untaxed growth, untaxed withdrawal — the ISA's structure. This removes the second layer of tax on the normal return identified above.
2.EET (exempt-exempt-taxed) — the pension structure — achieves the same removal of double taxation of the normal return via a different sequencing, under reasonable assumptions about constant tax rates.
3.Ordinary taxable accounts, by contrast, tax the normal return on top of tax already paid on the income saved — the genuinely anomalous treatment.
∴TEE treatment, as embodied in the ISA, is not an exception to a coherent system but an instance of the efficient treatment of the normal return to saving recommended by mainstream public economics; ordinary taxable accounts are the anomaly.
Argument III — The "ISA millionaire" problem is a problem of excess returns escaping tax, properly addressed by extending Rate-of-Return-Allowance thinking, not by abandoning TEE treatment of the normal return
1.The Mirrlees Review distinguishes the normal return (should not be taxed, per Argument I/II) from excess returns — risk premia, skill, or rent — for which the efficiency case for taxation is much stronger.
2.A Rate of Return Allowance operationalises this: exempting a normal return wherever savings are held, while leaving excess returns taxable.
3.The "ISA millionaire" cases involve very large excess returns escaping tax entirely under the ISA's flat exemption — a genuine design gap, but one addressed by extending RRA-style differentiation, not by reintroducing double taxation of the normal return for everyone.
∴Sturrock's "ISA millionaire" critique identifies a real design gap concerning excess returns on very large balances, but the appropriate response is to refine the ISA's treatment of excess returns at the margin, not to characterise its basic TEE structure as a "shelter" from a baseline that is itself badly designed.

I want to take direct issue with the theoretical foundation of the asset-reshuffling critique — not because the maths is wrong, but because the maths describes a creature that does not exist in the numbers required for the argument to carry the weight placed on it. The fungibility argument is a description of how a perfectly rational, frictionless, lifetime-optimising agent would behave. Decades of empirical work in behavioural economics — including work that earned a Nobel Prize for exactly this point — tells us that most people are not that agent.

i. mental accounting and the non-fungibility of money

Mental accounting — people categorise money into separate "mental accounts" (e.g., "rent money," "holiday fund," "savings," "ISA") and treat transfers between these accounts as costly or impermissible, even when, to a perfectly rational agent, money is fungible and account labels are meaningless; this is one of the most robust findings in behavioural economicsThe concept of mental accounting describes a finding that has been replicated across dozens of studies in many contexts: people do not treat a pound as a pound regardless of where it sits. They mentally categorise their money into separate accounts — "rent money," "the holiday fund," "savings for emergencies," "my ISA" — and they apply different decision rules to spending from, or transferring between, these accounts, even when, viewed from the standpoint of the lifecycle model, the accounts are entirely fungible and the categorisation is arbitrary. Money in an account labelled "ISA" is, for most people, treated as categorically different from money in a current account — not because it is legally harder to access (a Stocks and Shares ISA can typically be accessed, with the loss of the wrapper on withdrawal and re-deposit, fairly readily), but because the label itself triggers a different mental category, with a correspondingly different propensity to spend it. The fungibility argument against the ISA assumes away the very phenomenon that determines, for most actual households, whether money gets spent or saved.

ii. "save more tomorrow" and the annual deadline

Save More Tomorrow — Thaler and Benartzi's intervention had employees commit, in advance, to directing future pay rises into retirement saving; the key insight was that pre-commitment at a salient future date dramatically increased participation and saving rates compared to asking people to cut current consumption immediately; the ISA's annual "use it or lose it" deadline functions as a recurring version of exactly this kind of salient commitment momentIn the "Save More Tomorrow" programme that Shlomo Benartzi and I designed, the key innovation was not a new financial product but a change in timing and framing: instead of asking employees to reduce their take-home pay today in order to save more, we asked them to commit, now, to directing a portion of their future pay rises into retirement saving. Because the sacrifice was framed as happening in the future, and because the commitment was made at a moment when it was salient and easy to act on, participation and saving rates rose substantially — far more than equivalent programmes that simply asked people to save more starting immediately. The ISA's annual allowance, resetting every 6th of April with no carry-forward, creates a recurring version of exactly this kind of salient moment: a deadline, widely publicised by the financial services industry every spring, that prompts people to think about their savings in a way they otherwise would not. The asset-reshuffling critique treats this deadline as irrelevant, because a frictionless optimiser would not need a deadline to do what is already optimal. But the deadline is precisely what gets a great many non-optimising savers to act at all.

iii. ring-fencing changes future behaviour even for "reshuffled" assets

Ring-fencing — even when "Bed and ISA" moves an existing asset into the wrapper rather than representing new saving at the moment of the transaction, the act of moving the asset into a labelled, ring-fenced "savings" account changes the mental-accounting category the asset belongs to going forward, making the saver less likely to casually liquidate it for near-term consumption than if it had remained in an undifferentiated brokerage accountEven granting that "Bed and ISA" represents, at the moment of the transaction, a reallocation rather than new saving, the mental-accounting perspective suggests this reallocation is not without consequence for future behaviour. An asset sitting in an undifferentiated taxable brokerage account is, for many savers, mentally filed alongside other "available" resources — more likely to be tapped for a near-term want, a kitchen renovation, a car. The same asset, once moved into an account labelled "ISA," is recategorised into the "long-term savings" mental account — the account that, as the literature on mental accounting consistently finds, people are most reluctant to draw down. The "Bed and ISA" transaction may not be new saving in the instant it occurs, but by moving the asset into a mental category associated with greater protection against future dissaving, it can plausibly increase the probability that the asset remains saved over the following years — an effect the static, point-in-time framing of the reshuffling critique does not capture.

I yield the floor.

1.Mental accounting is a robust, widely replicated finding: people categorise money into separate mental accounts and apply different rules to each, even when the accounts are economically fungible.
2.Money labelled "ISA" is mentally categorised differently from money in a current account, affecting the propensity to spend it, independent of any legal accessibility differences.
3.The fungibility argument against ISAs assumes away mental accounting — the very phenomenon that, for most households, determines whether money is spent or saved.
∴The reshuffling critique's foundation — fungibility — is empirically false for the population whose behaviour determines the policy's aggregate effect; account labels matter to real saving decisions even though they "shouldn't" on the textbook model.

I want to bring some empirical discipline to a debate that has, so far on both sides, proceeded largely from theoretical first principles — the lifecycle model predicts reshuffling; mental accounting predicts genuine effects. These are both plausible stories. The question is which one the data support, and to what extent. This is not a new question; it was fought out, with considerable rigour, in the US literature on IRAs and 401(k)s, and I think the lessons of that debate are directly relevant here.

i. the empirical test and what it found

Empirical test — comparing households that contribute to tax-advantaged accounts with otherwise-similar households that do not, controlling for income, age, and other observable saving determinants, found that contributors' total wealth (inside and outside the tax-advantaged account combined) was higher than non-contributors', not merely reallocated — consistent with at least a substantial share of contributions representing genuinely new savingThe empirical strategy is conceptually straightforward, even if execution is demanding: compare households that contribute to a tax-advantaged account with otherwise-similar households — matched on income, age, and other observable determinants of saving behaviour — that do not contribute, and ask whether the contributors' total wealth, inside and outside the tax-advantaged account combined, is higher. If reshuffling were the whole story, contributors' total wealth should be no higher than non-contributors'; the tax-advantaged account would simply hold a larger share of an unchanged total. What the research my colleagues and I conducted on IRAs found was that contributors' total wealth was higher than non-contributors', by an amount that represented a substantial fraction — though not all — of their contributions. This is inconsistent with the strong reshuffling hypothesis, under which the coefficient should be close to zero. It is consistent with a substantial share of contributions representing genuinely new saving.

ii. why "rule of thumb" saving matters more than optimisation

Rule of thumb saving — the lifecycle model assumes households solve a complex intertemporal optimisation problem and would respond to a new tax-advantaged account by recalculating their optimal allocation across all accounts; but a large body of evidence suggests most households instead follow simple heuristics — "save what's automatically deducted," "save what's left over," "max out whatever account is in front of me" — and for these households, the introduction or expansion of a salient savings vehicle changes behaviour directly, because there is no recalculation step to undo the effectWhy might this be the case, given the theoretical force of the fungibility argument? The answer, I think, lies in recognising that the lifecycle model's prediction depends on households actually performing the optimisation the model describes — continuously reassessing their total wealth, their lifetime consumption plan, and the allocation of their portfolio across all accounts in light of any new account that becomes available, then adjusting accordingly. A large body of evidence on household financial behaviour suggests that most households do not do this. They follow simple heuristics: save whatever is automatically deducted from a paycheque, save what is "left over" at the end of the month, or — relevant here — direct available funds toward whatever savings vehicle is currently salient and has remaining capacity, such as an ISA allowance that has not yet been used this tax year. For a household following a heuristic of this kind, the existence of the ISA, and its annual reset, directly determines a portion of their saving behaviour, because there is no subsequent optimisation step that would undo the effect by adjusting saving elsewhere. The lifecycle model's prediction of full offset requires an optimisation process that, for most households, simply does not occur.

iii. conceding ground, and where it matters

Concession with limits — the "new saving" finding is an average effect across the population of contributors, and averages conceal heterogeneity; reshuffling is real and is concentrated among wealthier, more financially sophisticated households — exactly the population Sturrock and Engen/Gale describe — while genuinely new saving is concentrated among middle-income households without large pre-existing portfolios to reshuffle; this implies a case for better targeting, not for the proposition's conclusion that the instrument as a whole is merely a shelterI want to be careful not to overstate the case, because I think there is a genuine synthesis available here that the motion's framing obscures. The "new saving" finding is an average effect, and averages conceal substantial heterogeneity. For households with large pre-existing taxable portfolios — precisely the households Mr Sturrock and my colleagues on the other side describe — reshuffling is very likely the dominant effect; these households have assets to relabel and the financial sophistication to do so. For middle-income households without large pre-existing portfolios — households for whom an ISA contribution typically does come from current income rather than reallocated assets, because there is little to reallocate — the evidence points toward a substantial new-saving effect. Both halves of this picture can be true simultaneously, and I believe both are. The conclusion I draw is different from the proposition's, however: if reshuffling dominates among large-balance, sophisticated households, while new saving dominates among middle-income households, the policy response is to address the design features that allow large-balance accumulation — precisely the kind of refinement Sir James has discussed — while preserving and indeed better targeting the features that generate new saving among the middle of the distribution. The evidence supports reform of the instrument's upper reaches, not the characterisation of the instrument as a whole as merely a shelter.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Empirical comparison of contributors and matched non-contributors found higher total wealth among contributors, inconsistent with the strong reshuffling hypothesis
1.If reshuffling were the whole story, contributors to tax-advantaged accounts should have no higher total wealth (inside plus outside the account) than otherwise-similar non-contributors.
2.Empirical research comparing matched contributors and non-contributors found contributors' total wealth was higher by a substantial fraction of their contributions.
3.This finding is inconsistent with the strong reshuffling hypothesis (which predicts no difference) and consistent with a substantial share of contributions representing genuinely new saving.
∴The empirical evidence from the directly comparable US literature does not support the strong reshuffling hypothesis; a substantial share of tax-advantaged account contributions represents genuinely new saving.

Chair,

I want to close this debate by drawing attention to an effect of the ISA that has barely featured in the discussion so far, because it does not show up in distributional tables of tax relief or in studies of saving rates — but which I think may be the policy's most consequential legacy: its role in addressing what is sometimes called the UK's "equity gap."

i. the equity gap and its costs

Equity gap — UK household wealth has historically been concentrated in cash deposits and housing, with direct and indirect equity holdings much lower than in countries like the US; cash and low-yield deposits lose real value to inflation over long horizons, meaning the "safe" choice is, over a working lifetime, often the choice that erodes wealth most — a problem of household portfolio composition that compounds over decades, just as Sturrock describes ISA tax advantages compoundingUK households have long held a smaller share of their financial wealth in equities, directly or via funds, than households in comparable economies, with the bulk instead held in cash deposits and concentrated in housing. Over short horizons cash feels safe; over the long horizons relevant to retirement saving, cash that fails to outpace inflation is not safe at all — it is a slow, certain erosion of real purchasing power. A household that holds its long-term savings in cash for forty years, even without ever losing a nominal pound, can lose a very substantial share of its real value. This is a problem of portfolio composition that compounds over decades in exactly the way Mr Sturrock describes ISA tax advantages compounding — except in this case what compounds is a loss, not a gain, and it affects far more households than will ever come close to the ISA allowance.

ii. tax simplification as the binding constraint on participation

Tax simplification — holding equities or funds outside a wrapper requires tracking acquisition costs, calculating capital gains on each disposal, declaring dividend income, and potentially filing self-assessment returns — an administrative burden that, for many ordinary savers, is a more binding deterrent to equity investment than the headline tax rates themselves; the ISA wrapper removes this burden entirely for everything held within itWhy have UK households historically avoided direct equity holding, even when the expected returns over long horizons are well understood to exceed cash? Headline tax rates on dividends and capital gains are part of the story, but I think the administrative burden of compliance has historically been at least as significant a deterrent, particularly for households without an accountant or a financial adviser. Holding shares or funds outside a tax-advantaged wrapper means tracking the acquisition cost of every purchase (often complicated by reinvested dividends, stock splits, and partial disposals), calculating a capital gain or loss on every sale, declaring dividend income, and potentially needing to file a self-assessment return for the first time purely because of these holdings. For someone investing modest sums, this compliance burden can feel disproportionate to the amounts involved, and is itself a powerful deterrent to participation — independent of whatever the tax liability would actually be. The ISA wrapper removes this burden entirely: nothing held within it needs to be reported, regardless of how it performs. This administrative simplification, I would argue, has done more to make equity investing accessible to ordinary households than the headline tax exemption itself.

iii. the platform revolution and its preconditions

Platform revolution — the growth of low-cost, direct-to-consumer investment platforms, which have dramatically lowered the cost of market participation for retail investors, has been built substantially around the ISA wrapper as the default account type; the simplicity the wrapper provides is part of what made these platforms viable as mass-market consumer products rather than products requiring professional tax adviceThe growth, over the past two decades, of low-cost, direct-to-consumer investment platforms has dramatically reduced the cost of market participation for ordinary households — what once required a stockbroker and substantial minimum investments can now be done, for very modest sums, through an app. The Stocks and Shares ISA has been the default account type around which much of this platform growth has been built, precisely because it allows these platforms to offer a product that genuinely requires no tax expertise from the end user — a precondition for a mass-market consumer product in a way that a taxable brokerage account, with its attendant reporting obligations, is not. I want to acknowledge directly the force of Mr Kay's point about Cash ISAs: for the reasons he gives, the case for the Cash ISA specifically is now very weak for most savers, and reform — perhaps consolidating Cash ISA allowances into incentives to hold Stocks and Shares ISAs, where the long-run case for the wrapper is strongest — would be welcome. But this is an argument about how to allocate the wrapper's benefits between cash and investments, not an argument against the wrapper's role in making investment participation administratively feasible for the ordinary household in the first place — which I would suggest is the ISA's most significant, if least discussed, legacy.
