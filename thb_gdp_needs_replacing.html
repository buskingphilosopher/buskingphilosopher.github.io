Chair,

Let me begin with a question that sounds simple but is in fact devastating for the status quo: what is an economy for? Not how does it work, not how large is it — but what is it for? For most of the twentieth century, the answer embedded in our measurement systems was: for growth. GDP measures the rate at which an economy grows. It was designed, by Simon Kuznets, for precisely that purpose. And Kuznets himself warned, in 1934, that the welfare of a nation can scarcely be inferred from a measurement of national income. We ignored him.

“The goal is to meet the needs of all within the means of the planet. In other words, to ensure that no one falls short on life’s essentials while ensuring that collectively we do not overshoot our pressure on Earth’s life-supporting systems.” — Kate Raworth
i. the growth addiction and its costs

Growth addiction — GDP growth is now ecologically incompatible with planetary boundaries; the metric's framing as a policy target is the problem, not just its incompletenessThe problem with GDP is not merely that it misses some things. The problem is that it is used as a policy target, and that using it as a target has produced a specific, identifiable pathology: the subordination of every other value — ecological stability, social cohesion, wellbeing, equality — to the imperative of growth. When governments face a trade-off between growth and any other value, they have been systematically trained by their measurement systems to choose growth. The results are visible: we have nine planetary boundaries, of which we have now breached six. We have Easterlin paradoxes showing that beyond a threshold, more GDP produces essentially no more life satisfaction. We have the fastest acceleration of inequality in recorded history coinciding with the fastest acceleration of GDP in recorded history. The metric is not neutral. It shapes what governments optimise for. And what they are optimising for is no longer compatible with a habitable planet.

ii. what the doughnut replaces the growth target with

Doughnut space — the target is a just and safe space between social foundation (12 dimensions of human rights) and ecological ceiling (9 planetary boundaries); not a score but a locationThe Doughnut does not give you a score. This is deliberate and, I want to argue, a feature rather than a bug. The obsession with a single score — the instinct to rank countries — is itself a GDP-style pathology. The Doughnut gives you a location. The social foundation is defined by twelve dimensions drawn from the Sustainable Development Goals: food, water, health, education, income, housing, gender equality, social equity, political voice, peace and justice, networks, and energy. The ecological ceiling is defined by the nine planetary boundaries: climate change, ocean acidification, chemical pollution, nitrogen and phosphorus loading, freshwater use, land conversion, biodiversity loss, air pollution, and ozone depletion. The goal of policy is to be in the doughnut — above the social foundation, below the ecological ceiling. No country is currently there. This tells you something important that GDP cannot: that the entire global development model is pointed in the wrong direction.

iii. the measurement systems of the opposition are captured by the growth paradigm

Paradigm capture — supplementary metrics that retain GDP as a component or comparator accept its framing; genuine replacement requires a new conceptual framework, not additional columnsThe opposition will tell us that we should supplement GDP rather than replace it — that a dashboard, or an adjusted HDI, or the Stiglitz commission’s recommendations, can give us what we need without abandoning the familiarity and comparability of national accounts. I want to press hard on this. Every supplementary metric that retains GDP as a component — or that defines itself in relation to GDP — inherits GDP’s framing. It tells the political world: growth is still the main thing; here are some side constraints. That framing is the problem. The history of supplementary metrics is a history of being ignored: the HDI has been published since 1990, and in the intervening thirty-five years, GDP growth has remained the dominant policy target of virtually every government on earth. The reason is that supplementation does not change the paradigm. Only replacement does.

iv. the amsterdam precedent and implementation

Amsterdam — the city of Amsterdam formally adopted Doughnut Economics as its recovery framework in 2020; city-level implementation shows the framework is operational, not merely conceptualThe opposition will say the Doughnut is beautiful but impractical — a picture, not a number. Amsterdam disagrees. In 2020, the city of Amsterdam became the first in the world to formally adopt Doughnut Economics as its post-pandemic recovery framework. The city mapped its social foundation across every dimension of the SDGs, mapped its ecological ceiling across every planetary boundary, and used the resulting portrait to design policy. Subsequent work has been carried out by cities including Copenhagen, Brussels, and Dunedin. The framework is not a number, and that is precisely why it can capture what a number cannot: the multi-dimensional reality of a city or country’s position in the world. The question is not whether the Doughnut can be operationalised. It already has been. The question is whether we have the political courage to let it displace GDP as the frame within which policy is made.

∴The Doughnut framework is operationally viable, as demonstrated by Amsterdam and subsequent city-level adoptions; the objection from impracticality fails; the remaining question is political will, not methodological capacity.

Bhutan is a small, landlocked Himalayan kingdom with a GDP per capita that places it firmly in the lower-middle-income band. By every GDP-derived ranking, it is a developing country with much catching up to do. And yet Bhutan has produced something that no high-income country has produced: a coherent, constitutionally embedded framework for governing in the direction of human happiness rather than economic output. I want to argue that this is not a curiosity from a small Buddhist kingdom; it is a model that contains within it a fundamental truth about what government is for.

i. the four pillars and nine domains

GNH structure — four pillars (sustainable development, cultural preservation, environmental conservation, good governance) operationalised across nine domains; constitutionally mandated, not advisoryThe GNH framework rests on four pillars: sustainable and equitable socio-economic development; preservation and promotion of cultural values; conservation of the natural environment; and establishment of good governance. These pillars are operationalised across nine domains: living standards, health, education, governance, ecological diversity and resilience, time use, psychological wellbeing, cultural resilience and promotion, and community vitality. What distinguishes GNH from other frameworks in this debate is that it is constitutionally mandated. Article 9 of Bhutan’s 2008 constitution states that the state shall strive to promote those conditions that will enable the pursuit of Gross National Happiness. This is not a research project or an advisory index. It is the constitutional goal of the state.

ii. the policy screening tool

Policy screening — all major policy proposals in Bhutan are screened through the GNH index before adoption; this operationalises the framework as a governing instrument rather than a post-hoc measurementGNH is not merely a measurement exercise. It functions as a policy screening tool. The Centre for Bhutan Studies and GNH Research administers a GNH survey every five years, covering a nationally representative sample, measuring all nine domains. The GNH Commission uses the resulting index to screen major policy proposals: policies that score below a threshold on GNH are not adopted. This is the critical practical difference from supplementary metrics. A government using GDP plus a dashboard of wellbeing indicators still faces the same political economy: growth-promoting policies have a powerful, concentrated constituency; wellbeing-promoting policies do not. The policy screening tool changes the incentive structure: it imposes a formal institutional obligation to account for non-economic consequences before adoption.

iii. the sufficiency threshold and its political implications

Sufficiency not maximisation — GNH does not seek to maximise happiness but to bring all citizens above a sufficiency threshold across all nine domains; this reorients policy from marginal growth to universal adequacyGNH does not seek to maximise happiness in the utilitarian sense — to produce the greatest aggregate score. It seeks to bring the largest possible number of citizens above a threshold of sufficiency across all nine domains. This is a philosophically distinct aim: not maximisation but universalisation. Its policy implications are profound. Under a maximisation framework, it is rational to concentrate resources on populations that yield the highest marginal return to wellbeing. Under a sufficiency framework, the priority is the most deprived. Every resource allocation decision is anchored not in the question “where does investment produce the highest return?” but in the question “who falls furthest below the threshold?” This reorientation — from marginal growth to universal adequacy — is what a replacement for GDP must achieve. GNH achieves it.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — GNH is constitutionally mandated and operationally implemented, not merely advisory
1.GNH is embedded in Article 9 of Bhutan’s 2008 constitution as the state’s governing objective. It is not a research index or an advisory dashboard but a constitutional mandate.
2.The framework is operationalised across nine measurable domains administered through a five-yearly national survey. The resulting index is used to screen policy proposals before adoption.
3.Constitutional mandating and policy screening institutionalise multi-dimensional wellbeing as the primary policy objective; this is the organisational infrastructure that a replacement for GDP requires.
∴GNH demonstrates that a multi-dimensional wellbeing framework can be constitutionally embedded and operationally implemented as a governing instrument; it is not a concept but a functioning alternative governance model.
Argument II — The policy screening tool changes the institutional incentive structure in ways that supplementary metrics cannot
1.Supplementary metrics produce information alongside GDP but do not change the institutional incentive structure: growth-promoting policies still have concentrated political constituencies; wellbeing policies do not.
2.GNH’s policy screening tool imposes a formal institutional requirement to account for non-economic consequences. Policies failing the GNH threshold are not adopted, regardless of their GDP impact.
3.Changing the institutional incentive structure — not merely the available information — is what is required to shift policy away from the growth imperative. Screening tools achieve this; dashboards do not.
∴The GNH policy screening tool achieves what supplementary dashboards cannot: it changes the institutional incentive structure of policy-making, creating a formal obligation to prioritise multi-dimensional wellbeing over growth.
Argument III — GNH’s sufficiency orientation reorients policy from marginal growth to universal adequacy
1.GNH aims not at maximising aggregate happiness but at bringing all citizens above a sufficiency threshold across all nine domains. This is universalisation, not optimisation.
2.Under a sufficiency framework, priority allocation goes to the most deprived rather than to the population yielding the highest marginal wellbeing return. This is a structurally different allocation logic from GDP growth.
3.Universal adequacy, not aggregate maximisation, is the correct goal for a national progress framework; GNH’s sufficiency orientation embeds this goal in policy.
∴GNH’s sufficiency threshold orientation reorients policy from marginal growth toward universal adequacy; this is the correct goal for a national progress framework and constitutes a genuine philosophical advance over GDP.

I want to focus on what I think is the most fundamental question this debate needs to answer: what is the relationship between economic activity, human wellbeing, and the ecological systems that make both possible? GDP answers this question by ignoring it. The Happy Planet Index answers it directly, and the answer it gives is one that every government in this room should find deeply uncomfortable.

The HPI formula. HPI = (Wellbeing × Life Expectancy) / Ecological Footprint per capita. It measures the efficiency with which a country converts planetary resources into long and happy lives. The global average has barely moved since measurement began. The highest scorers are consistently Costa Rica, Mexico, and Colombia — not the United States, Germany, or the United Kingdom.
i. the efficiency imperative

Efficiency ratio — HPI reveals that high GDP nations are ecologically inefficient at producing wellbeing; the question a metric must answer is lives per unit of planetary resource, not activity per yearThe HPI reveals something that no other metric makes visible: the ecological efficiency of wellbeing production. A country that produces high wellbeing and long lives while consuming little of the planet’s resources is doing something right, at a fundamental level, that a country consuming vastly more while producing only marginally better outcomes is not. The United States in recent HPI data scores roughly 20 — a middling result despite having one of the highest GDPs per capita on earth. Costa Rica scores over 60, with a life expectancy comparable to the US and substantially higher subjective wellbeing, while consuming a fraction of the ecological resources. This is not a curiosity. It is a diagnosis. High GDP is associated with high footprint. The relationship between high GDP and high wellbeing is much weaker. The country that has cracked the problem is not the richest; it is the most efficient.

ii. against the wellbeing-without-ecology metrics

Ecology must be in the denominator — frameworks that add wellbeing dimensions without placing ecological cost in the denominator remain blind to the fundamental trade-off; sustainability must be structural, not additiveMany frameworks in this debate — the HDI, the Social Progress Index, GNH — add ecological or environmental dimensions alongside social and economic ones. I want to press a specific methodological objection to this approach. When ecology is one dimension among many, it can be offset by other dimensions. A country that scores highly on health, education, and income but badly on environmental performance can still achieve an acceptable overall score. The ecological ceiling is breached but the headline number looks fine. The HPI avoids this by placing ecological footprint in the denominator: it cannot be offset. A country that consumes three planets’ worth of resources cannot compensate for this by having good schools. The ecological constraint must be structural and inescapable, not additive and offsettable.

iii. the policy implication: degrowth or efficiency

Policy clarity — HPI creates unambiguous policy pressure: improve wellbeing, extend healthy life expectancy, and reduce footprint; these targets are achievable without GDP growth and incompatible with footprint expansionThe HPI creates unambiguous policy targets that GDP cannot: raise wellbeing, raise life expectancy, reduce per capita ecological footprint. These are not vague aspirations; they are specific, measurable goals that direct investment in healthcare, social infrastructure, clean energy, and the redesign of consumption patterns. Crucially, they do not require GDP growth. Costa Rica’s performance was not achieved through rapid industrialisation; it was achieved through investment in universal healthcare, education, and the conservation of natural ecosystems that provide services without generating GDP. A government optimising for HPI has a policy agenda that is both ambitious and ecologically coherent. A government optimising for GDP has an agenda that is internally coherent but ecologically catastrophic.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — HPI reveals that high-GDP nations are ecologically inefficient at producing wellbeing; efficiency is the correct policy question
1.HPI measures wellbeing achieved per unit of ecological footprint. This is the efficiency of wellbeing production — the fundamental question for a world of finite ecological resources.
2.High-GDP nations score poorly on HPI: the US scores ~20; Costa Rica scores ~60 with comparable or better wellbeing outcomes and a fraction of the footprint. High GDP predicts high footprint more reliably than it predicts high wellbeing.
3.The correct policy question is not “how much economic activity occurred?” but “how efficiently are we converting planetary resources into long and happy lives?” Only HPI directly answers this question.
∴HPI reveals an efficiency gap between high-GDP and high-HPI nations that GDP cannot; optimising for HPI rather than GDP would redirect policy toward the correct objective: maximum wellbeing per unit of ecological cost.
Argument II — Ecology must be in the denominator, not as an additive dimension; offsetting ecological failure with social performance is methodologically indefensible
1.Frameworks that include ecological performance as one dimension among many allow ecological failure to be offset by social and economic performance. A country can breach the ecological ceiling and still score acceptably.
2.The ecological constraint is inescapable in physical reality: no amount of good education or health outcomes allows a country to consume more than one planet’s worth of resources indefinitely. A metric that allows offset misrepresents this constraint.
3.Placing ecological footprint in the denominator, as HPI does, makes the constraint inescapable in the metric itself: it cannot be offset, ensuring that the ecological ceiling is structural rather than advisory.
∴Ecology must be structurally inescapable in any adequate metric of national progress; placing it in the denominator as HPI does is methodologically superior to including it as an additive dimension subject to offsetting.
Argument III — HPI creates unambiguous policy targets achievable without GDP growth; GDP creates targets incompatible with ecological survival
1.HPI creates three unambiguous policy targets: raise subjective wellbeing, raise healthy life expectancy, reduce per capita footprint. All three are achievable through investment in healthcare, social infrastructure, and clean energy without requiring GDP growth.
2.Costa Rica’s high HPI performance was achieved through universal healthcare and education investment and ecosystem conservation — activities that generate high wellbeing at low footprint, not high GDP.
3.A government optimising for HPI has a coherent policy agenda compatible with planetary boundaries; a government optimising for GDP has an agenda whose internal logic is ecologically catastrophic.
∴HPI’s policy targets are ambitious, measurable, ecologically coherent, and achievable without growth; GDP’s implicit policy target — growth — is ecologically incompatible with long-run human welfare.

The Social Progress Index was built to answer a specific and underappreciated question: what does economic development actually buy in terms of human outcomes? The answer, when you look at the data carefully, is: less than you think, distributed far more unequally than you would expect, and with enormous variation between countries at similar income levels. The SPI exists to make that variation visible and to make governments accountable for it.

i. the weak and variable gdp-social progress relationship

GDP-SPI correlation — the relationship between GDP per capita and SPI score is real but logarithmic and highly variable; countries at similar GDP levels differ enormously in social outcomes; this variation is what policy must explainThere is a positive correlation between GDP per capita and SPI score. We acknowledge this. But three features of the relationship are critical. First, it is logarithmic: the relationship is strong at low income levels and nearly flat at high income levels. Above roughly $15,000 GDP per capita, additional income produces rapidly diminishing social progress returns. Second, the variance around the trend line is enormous. Countries at broadly similar income levels — the United States and Denmark; Brazil and Costa Rica; South Africa and Botswana — differ by ten, fifteen, twenty SPI points. This variance is not noise; it is the signal. It is the consequence of policy choices about how income is distributed, what it is spent on, and what institutions are built. Third, GDP per capita cannot explain this variance, because GDP per capita is the same in the comparator pairs. Only non-economic factors can explain it. And those are exactly what the SPI measures.

ii. the three-tier architecture and what it reveals

Three-tier structure — Basic Human Needs, Foundations of Wellbeing, and Opportunity are three distinct social progress dimensions with different income elasticities and different policy leversThe SPI structures its fifty indicators across three dimensions with importantly different properties. Basic Human Needs — nutrition, water, shelter, safety — has the highest income elasticity: at low income levels, more income reliably buys more basic need satisfaction. Foundations of Wellbeing — health, education, information access, environmental quality — has a lower income elasticity and higher policy variance: what governments choose to spend money on, and how institutions function, matters enormously. Opportunity — personal rights, freedom, inclusiveness, access to advanced education — has the lowest income elasticity and the highest variance: rich countries differ as much from each other as middle-income countries on opportunity dimensions. A metric that averages across all three tiers, or that subsumes them all into a single score, loses the policy-relevant information in the differences between them.

iii. deliberate exclusion of economic data

Exclusion of economic data — SPI deliberately excludes GDP and economic indicators to force independent measurement of outcomes; this makes social progress separately accountable, not just a downstream of incomeThe SPI deliberately excludes all economic data. This is not an oversight; it is a methodological commitment. If economic variables are included in a social progress index, you cannot tell whether high social performance is the consequence of high income or of policy choices made independently of income. By measuring only outcomes — the actual state of people’s health, education, rights, and environment — and excluding the economic inputs, the SPI makes social outcomes separately accountable. A government cannot say: we have high income, therefore our social outcomes should be high. The SPI asks directly: are they? And for many high-income countries — the United States most notoriously, where SPI rank falls well below GDP rank — the answer is no. That gap is the most important policy finding the metric produces.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The GDP-SPI relationship is logarithmic and highly variable; the variance is the policy signal GDP suppresses
1.The correlation between GDP per capita and SPI is real but logarithmic: strong at low income, flat above ~$15,000. Additional GDP growth produces diminishing social returns in wealthy nations.
2.Countries at similar GDP levels differ enormously in SPI scores: US vs Denmark; Brazil vs Costa Rica. This variance reflects policy choices about distribution, public spending, and institutions — exactly what GDP cannot capture.
3.The variance around the GDP-SPI trend line is the policy signal: it shows what countries can achieve at a given income level. GDP suppresses this signal by treating all income as equally social-progress-producing.
∴The variance in social outcomes at similar income levels — visible in SPI but invisible in GDP — is the primary policy-relevant information; GDP suppresses it; SPI exposes it.
Argument II — The three-tier architecture reveals dimension-specific income elasticities and policy levers that aggregate metrics obscure
1.Basic Human Needs, Foundations of Wellbeing, and Opportunity have structurally different income elasticities: high for basic needs, lower for foundations, near-zero for opportunity dimensions at high income.
2.Aggregating across all three tiers loses dimension-specific policy information: what raises basic needs scores (income growth) is different from what raises opportunity scores (institutional reform, political rights).
3.Policy requires dimension-specific information; an aggregate score that mixes dimensions with different elasticities provides a misleading picture of what interventions are needed and where.
∴The three-tier SPI architecture provides dimension-specific policy information that both GDP and aggregate alternatives lose; this granularity is necessary for effective policy design.

1.Including economic variables in a social progress index prevents attribution: you cannot tell whether high social scores reflect high income or good policy. Exclusion of economic data forces independent measurement of outcomes.
2.The US scores 25th on SPI while ranking in the top 10 by GDP per capita. This gap — visible only when social outcomes are measured independently of income — is the most important finding the metric produces.
3.Independent accountability for social outcomes is only achievable with economic data excluded; any metric that includes GDP as a component or correlate inherits the attribution problem.
∴The SPI’s deliberate exclusion of economic data is methodologically essential: it makes social outcomes independently accountable, exposes the gap between income and achievement, and creates genuine policy pressure that income-inclusive metrics cannot.

I want to make an argument that is, in a sense, the most conservative one on this side of the debate: that even on its own terms — as a measure of economic prosperity — GDP is wrong. Not merely incomplete, not merely silent on things that matter, but actively mistaken as an accounting exercise. It treats the liquidation of natural capital as income. It counts the costs of crime, illness, and environmental cleanup as positive contributions to welfare. It ignores the entire non-market economy of unpaid care and domestic labour. The Genuine Progress Indicator and the Inclusive Wealth Index correct these errors within the framework of national accounting. What they reveal when they do so is arresting.

i. gdp’s accounting errors

Accounting errors — GDP treats defensive expenditures, environmental degradation, and natural capital depletion as positive; this is not a measurement gap but a false entry in the national accountsGDP counts the costs of an oil spill positively: the cleanup activity raises GDP. It counts the costs of car accidents positively: the medical treatment, the car repairs, the litigation all raise GDP. It counts the depletion of a fishery as zero: fish extracted from the sea enter GDP as income but the declining stock of future fish enters nowhere. These are not measurement gaps — omissions of things GDP does not try to measure. They are positive misstatements: entries in the national accounts that record costs as gains. The Genuine Progress Indicator was designed specifically to correct these misstatements. It begins with personal consumption expenditure — the same starting point as GDP — and then adds non-market contributions (the value of household work, volunteer activity, the services of consumer durables) and subtracts social and environmental costs (crime, family breakdown, income inequality, loss of leisure, pollution, depletion of non-renewable resources, long-term environmental damage). The result, across virtually every country where GPI has been calculated, diverges sharply from GDP after the mid-1970s. GDP has continued to rise; GPI has stagnated or declined.

ii. the inclusive wealth index and intergenerational accounting

Inclusive Wealth — IWI measures the total capital stock: produced, human, and natural; many nations show positive GDP growth alongside declining IWI, meaning they are consuming their capital baseThe Inclusive Wealth Index takes the argument a step further by asking the question that national income accounts have always structurally avoided: is a country’s total wealth growing or shrinking? A farmer who sells off their topsoil has a high income year but a depleted farm. GDP records only the income; IWI records the depletion. The IWI measures three capital stocks: produced capital (machinery, infrastructure, buildings), human capital (the education and health of the population), and natural capital (forests, fisheries, mineral deposits, ecosystem services). The 2018 Inclusive Wealth Report, covering 140 countries, found that while virtually all countries showed positive GDP per capita growth between 1992 and 2014, a significant number showed declining inclusive wealth per capita — meaning they were consuming their capital base to generate current income. Russia, Nigeria, Venezuela, and South Africa all showed positive GDP growth and declining IWI. This is not development. It is liquidation dressed as growth.

iii. genuine savings and the world bank’s own data

Genuine Savings — the World Bank's own ANS metric shows many resource-dependent nations with negative genuine savings rates; they are getting poorer while GDP risesPerhaps the most powerful evidence for replacing GDP comes from an institution that has historically been among its strongest champions: the World Bank. The Bank’s own Adjusted Net Savings metric — also called Genuine Savings — calculates a country’s true savings rate by taking gross national savings, adding education expenditure, and subtracting the depletion of energy resources, mineral resources, and forests, alongside damage from carbon emissions and particulate pollution. For a large number of resource-dependent developing economies, the resulting Genuine Savings rate is negative. These countries are not merely failing to save; they are running down their capital stocks faster than they are building them up. GDP reports positive growth. Genuine Savings reports negative real accumulation. Both cannot be right as measures of whether a country is getting richer or poorer. The World Bank’s own data condemns the GDP-centric development model it has historically promoted.

2.The GPI starts from personal consumption, adds non-market value (household work, volunteer activity), and subtracts social and environmental costs (crime, inequality, pollution, resource depletion). This corrects the false entries.

Chair,

Mahbub ul Haq designed the Human Development Index in 1990 with an explicit and deliberately modest ambition: to produce a single number that was at least as simple as GDP but measured something closer to what development is actually for. His collaborator Amartya Sen was initially sceptical — he preferred a richer multi-dimensional framework — but came to accept that the political impact of a simple, comparable, headline number was worth the inevitable simplification. Thirty-five years later, I think both of them were right about something and wrong about something, and the tension between their positions is exactly the tension at the heart of this debate.

“We need a measure of the same level of vulgarity as GNP — just one number — but a better one.” — Mahbub ul Haq
i. the capabilities approach and what HDI captures

Capabilities approach — HDI operationalises Sen's capabilities framework: a long healthy life, access to knowledge, and a decent material standard of living are co-equal dimensions of human flourishing, none reducible to moneyThe HDI operationalises Amartya Sen’s capabilities approach: the insight that human development is about expanding the freedoms that people have to live lives they have reason to value. Three dimensions were chosen as the most fundamental: a long and healthy life (measured by life expectancy at birth); access to knowledge (measured by expected and mean years of schooling); and a decent material standard of living (measured by GNI per capita, log-transformed to reflect diminishing returns). These are genuinely co-equal: no dimension can be substituted for another. A country with high income but low life expectancy is not developing its citizens; a country with high life expectancy but very low income is not providing the material conditions for flourishing either. The geometric mean used to combine them ensures that extreme shortfall in any dimension pulls the score down, regardless of performance on others.

ii. the IHDI and the inequality correction

IHDI — applies Atkinson inequality measure to each dimension separately, then takes the geometric mean; a country with high mean HDI but extreme inequality suffers a substantial penalty; this makes distribution structurally visibleThe Inequality-adjusted HDI addresses the most important limitation of the HDI: that country averages can mask extreme internal inequality. A country where life expectancy and education are very high for a wealthy minority but very low for a large poor majority will score well on HDI despite most of its population falling far short of flourishing. The IHDI corrects this by applying the Atkinson inequality measure to each dimension separately, before the geometric mean is taken. The Atkinson measure has an explicit inequality-aversion parameter; by setting it to one, the IHDI weights the poorest person’s outcomes as heavily as the richest. The loss of HDI points from inequality penalisation is substantial for highly unequal societies — the US loses around fifteen percent of its HDI score; Brazil loses over thirty percent. This penalty is not decorative; it directly reflects the distributional failure that GDP and even HDI conceal.

iii. why abandoning gdp entirely is a mistake

GDP retention — GNI per capita (log) remains in HDI as the material dimension because material resources are genuinely necessary for capability expansion; removing it produces frameworks blind to resource constraintsI want to argue against the more radical members of the proposition. The HDI retains a log-transformed income dimension — GNI per capita — not because income is the point, but because material resources are genuinely necessary for capability expansion. A country that scores highly on health and education while having very low income will struggle to maintain those achievements: the infrastructure, the teachers, the medical equipment, the institutional capacity — all require resources. Frameworks that exclude economic variables entirely, like the Social Progress Index, lose this constraint. They can produce rankings that suggest countries are making social progress while their fiscal positions are unsustainable and their service delivery is on borrowed time. The goal is not to replace economic measurement with social measurement; it is to ensure that economic measurement is properly contextualised by and answerable to human development outcomes.

iv. global coverage and comparability

Global coverage — HDI covers 193 countries with consistent methodology since 1990; no alternative framework approaches this coverage or longitudinal consistency; comparability is not a luxury but a requirement of international policyAny replacement for GDP must be calculable for all countries, comparable across countries, and comparable across time. The HDI currently covers 193 countries with consistent methodology and a time series extending back to 1990. No framework on the proposition’s side approaches this. GNH is implemented in one country. The Happy Planet Index has significant data gaps for many low-income countries. The Doughnut has been mapped comprehensively for a handful of cities. The Social Progress Index covers 170 countries but has a shorter time series. International development policy — aid allocation, debt relief, development finance, comparative policy learning — requires a framework that can be applied universally and compared longitudinally. This is not a bureaucratic nicety; it is a precondition for evidence-based international policy.

The proposition wants to replace one number with another number — or with one picture, or one location, or one efficiency ratio. i. the weighting problem is not solved by any proposed alternative

Weighting problem — any composite index must assign weights to dimensions; these weights embed value judgements; no authoritative body can legitimately impose these on diverse societies; the BLI makes this pluralism explicitEvery composite index in this debate faces the same fundamental problem: how to weight its dimensions. GNH weights nine domains. The SPI weights fifty indicators. The HDI weights health, education, and income equally in log-units. The HPI weights wellbeing, life expectancy, and ecological footprint in a particular ratio. In each case, a body of researchers or a government commission has decided that one dimension is worth so many units of another. These are value judgements. They are not derivable from data. And there is no reason why a Norwegian and a Congolese, a farmer and a software engineer, a Buddhist monk and a New York investment banker should agree on them. The OECD Better Life Index makes this explicit: it allows any citizen, anywhere, to assign their own weights to eleven dimensions — housing, income, jobs, community, education, environment, civic engagement, health, life satisfaction, safety, work-life balance — and see how their country performs on their own terms. The framework does not pretend to have solved the weighting problem. It gives it back to the people it belongs to.

Empirical grounding — the 11 BLI dimensions are derived from empirical wellbeing research, not arbitrary; they reflect the domains most consistently associated with life satisfaction across cultures and income levelsThe eleven dimensions of the Better Life Index are not arbitrary. The dimensions represent the areas of life most consistently associated with life satisfaction across different cultures and income levels: adequate shelter, meaningful work, social connections, health, safety, political participation, environmental quality. The framework is empirically grounded at the level of dimension selection even as it is pluralist at the level of dimension weighting. This combination — evidence-based dimensionality, citizen-driven weighting — is methodologically superior to both GDP (one dimension, no citizen input) and to alternatives that impose specific weights (multiple dimensions, no citizen input).

Nordic countries dominate citizen-weighted assessments across most weighting schemes, because their performance is strong and consistent across dimensions. Australia and New Zealand perform well above their GDP rank. This divergence between income rank and citizen-weighted wellbeing rank is not a curiosity; it is the most direct possible evidence that GDP is measuring something different from what citizens say they value. The Stiglitz-Sen-Fitoussi Commission was convened by the French government in 2008 to answer a specific question: is GDP a good measure of economic performance and social progress, and if not, what should be done? Within that domain, it does its job with impressive methodological rigour: the System of National Accounts, maintained jointly by the UN, World Bank, IMF, OECD, and Eurostat, ensures that GDP is calculated on consistent principles across nearly all countries, allowing genuine international comparison and longitudinal tracking. Market output matters: it correlates with the resources available for public spending, it correlates with employment, it tracks the business cycle in ways that are essential for macroeconomic management. Criticising GDP for not measuring wellbeing or sustainability is like criticising a thermometer for not measuring blood pressure. First, GDP itself: market output, measured as now, without modification. Second, household material living standards: how income, consumption, and wealth are distributed across households, corrected for household size and including non-market production. This immediately addresses the point that GDP per capita is an average that conceals radical inequality; median household consumption is a far better measure of typical material living standards. Third, quality of life: a multi-dimensional assessment including health, education, personal activities, political voice, social connections, environment, and insecurity. I want to play the role of the methodological sceptic in this debate, because I think it is a role that is systematically underplayed. I am not defending GDP as a complete measure of human welfare. Nobody serious has defended that claim since Kuznets himself warned against it in 1934. What I am defending is something more modest and more important: the claim that the alternatives proposed are, in most cases, methodologically inferior to GDP on exactly the dimensions that their advocates claim to improve upon, and that the enthusiasm for replacing a carefully constructed statistic with a less carefully constructed one should be tempered by attention to what we lose.

i. the measurement properties gdp possesses that alternatives lack

Methodological superiority — GDP has well-defined scope, consistent methodology (SNA), international comparability, verifiability, and a 70-year longitudinal record; most alternatives lack several of these properties simultaneouslyGDP has five properties that any replacement must match: a well-defined scope (market output in a defined territory and period); a consistent international methodology (the System of National Accounts, maintained by five international bodies and implemented in all major economies); verifiability (the accounts can be audited against tax records, survey data, and trade data); international comparability (the same definition applied in 193 countries); and longitudinal consistency (comparable series back to the 1940s for most developed economies and back further with Maddison Project reconstructions). Now examine the alternatives. The Doughnut: operationalised in a handful of cities, no consistent global dataset. GNH: implemented in one country, not calculable for 192 others. HPI: significant data gaps for low-income countries where subjective wellbeing surveys are sparse. GPI: heavy dependence on shadow-pricing conventions for environmental damage, where estimates vary by an order of magnitude between methodologies. IWI: natural capital valuation requires extensive assumptions about ecosystem service pricing that are fiercely contested. Each alternative trades some of GDP’s measurement properties for others. None improves on all five simultaneously.

ii. the shadow-pricing problem

Shadow pricing — GPI, Green GDP, IWI, and ANS all require monetary valuation of environmental damage; these valuations are methodologically contested, politically manipulable, and vary by orders of magnitudeThe accounting-correction alternatives — GPI, Green GDP, Inclusive Wealth, Genuine Savings — all require placing monetary values on non-market goods: the value of an ecosystem service, the cost of a tonne of carbon dioxide, the depletion cost of a barrel of oil, the value of a year of schooling. These valuations are not straightforward. The social cost of carbon is estimated by different reputable teams at figures ranging from $50 to over $400 per tonne — an eightfold range. The value of a biodiversity unit depends on the hedonic pricing methodology applied and the baseline chosen. The value of human capital depends on whether you use wage returns or an intrinsic capabilities approach. These are not minor methodological quibbles; they are uncertainties that dwarf many of the adjustments being made to GDP. A metric that corrects GDP’s $100 billion undercount of environmental damage with a $500 billion environmental valuation that has an uncertainty range of $400 billion has not improved on GDP’s accuracy; it has replaced a knowable error with an unknowable one.

iii. the subjectivity of wellbeing measurement

Subjective wellbeing — self-reported life satisfaction is a valid but methodologically limited instrument; it is sensitive to survey framing, question order, cultural response bias, and adaptation effects that make cross-country comparison unreliableThe wellbeing-based alternatives — GNH, the World Happiness Report, the HPI’s wellbeing component — rely heavily on self-reported life satisfaction. I want to note some methodological concerns that their advocates often understate. Life satisfaction responses are sensitive to question framing: how a question is asked changes the answer systematically. They are sensitive to question order: what precedes the life satisfaction question biases the response. They are subject to cultural response bias: societies differ in their norms about expressing satisfaction, and these norms are correlated with the very variables being measured. They are subject to adaptation: people adapt to chronic conditions (disability, poverty) in ways that make their reported satisfaction a poor guide to objective welfare. And they are subject to focalism: when asked to evaluate their lives, respondents focus on salient features of their current situation rather than integrating across their whole life. None of these concerns makes subjective wellbeing worthless. But they make cross-country comparison of subjective wellbeing scores significantly less reliable than GDP comparisons, and this methodological limitation must be acknowledged before wellbeing metrics are elevated to primary policy status.

iv. the political manipulation risk

Manipulation risk — subjective and multi-dimensional metrics are more susceptible to political gaming than GDP; Goodhart's Law applies; the more a metric is targeted the less useful it becomesGoodhart’s Law states that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. This applies to GDP, which is why GDP has been gamed. But it applies with greater force to its proposed replacements, for a simple reason: GDP is harder to manipulate than wellbeing indices because it is grounded in auditable financial transactions. A government that wants to improve its GNH score can commission flattering survey questions, frame the survey in prosperous periods, and emphasise cultural pride. A government that wants to improve its SPI ranking can select comparison countries strategically. A government that wants to improve its HPI score can adjust its footprint accounting conventions. GDP is not immune to manipulation — there are well-documented instances of statistical adjustment in politically sensitive periods — but the auditable transaction trail provides an external check that subjective and multi-dimensional indices largely lack.

Argument IV — Subjective and multi-dimensional metrics are more susceptible to political manipulation than GDP; Goodhart’s Law applies with greater force to alternatives
1.Goodhart’s Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. GDP has been gamed; but its grounding in auditable financial transactions provides an external check on manipulation.
2.Wellbeing indices and multi-dimensional dashboards lack this external check: survey framing, survey timing, country selection, and shadow-pricing conventions can all be adjusted by governments to improve scores without genuine improvement in outcomes.
3.The political manipulation risk is higher for the proposed alternatives than for GDP, because they are less grounded in auditable transactions and more dependent on methodological choices that governments can influence.
∴Proposed alternatives to GDP are more susceptible to political manipulation than GDP itself; their lack of auditable transaction grounding and dependence on contested methodological choices creates greater manipulation risk, not less.
