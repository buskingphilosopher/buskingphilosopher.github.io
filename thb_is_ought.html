Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I speak for two voices today — Hume, who first drew the blade, and myself, who sharpened it to its keenest edge. The question before this House is whether a valid argument can carry us, by pure logical force, from descriptions of how the world is to prescriptions of how it ought to be. My answer is that it cannot, and that those who believe otherwise have committed either a logical error or a smuggling operation. The ought-statement in their conclusion was already hidden in their premises; what they have proved is not a derivation but a tautology.

On Hume’s original observation and its radical force

Hume’s guillotine: the sudden switch from is to ought in moral arguments requires justification that has never been givenLet me begin with Hume’s own words, from the Treatise of Human Nature, Book III, Part I, Section I. After surveying the moral systems of his day, Hume observes that every author proceeds for some time in the ordinary way of reasoning, establishing propositions about what is — about God, about human nature, about social arrangements — and then suddenly, imperceptibly, the copula changes. Instead of “is” and “is not,” he begins to speak of “ought” and “ought not.” This change, Hume says, is insensible but of the last consequence: a new relation or affirmation is being expressed, and it needs to be explained and justified, since this new relation cannot be deduced from the preceding ones. Hume does not say the gap cannot be bridged. He says it has never been bridged with the explanation that is required. The burden of proof lies with those who claim the bridge can be built, and in nearly three hundred years of moral philosophy, the burden has not been discharged. The opposition tonight will offer their favoured bridges — institutional facts, natural goodness, teleological naturalism. I intend to show that each of them either conceals a normative premise or trades on an equivocation in the word “ought.”

On emotivism and the logical status of moral utterances

Emotivism: moral utterances are not propositions but expressions of attitude; ‘Stealing is wrong’ means ‘Stealing — boo!’ and cannot be the conclusion of a factual argumentMy own contribution, in Language, Truth and Logic, was to draw the sharpest possible conclusion from Hume’s observation. If moral utterances cannot be derived from factual ones, this is because moral utterances are not truth-apt propositions at all. When I say “Stealing is wrong,” I am not reporting a fact about the world — I am expressing an attitude of disapproval toward stealing. The sentence “Stealing is wrong” has the same descriptive content as “Stealing,” with the addition of an emotive tone that communicates my disapproval. It is not genuinely a proposition that can be true or false; it is what I have called a pseudo-proposition. This is not a paradox or an attack on morality — it is a clarification of what moral language actually does. It guides behaviour by expressing and evoking attitudes, not by describing moral facts. And this explains why moral utterances cannot be derived from factual premises: because derivation is a logical relation between propositions, and emotive utterances are not propositions. They are outside the domain of deductive inference altogether. The gap between is and ought is not a gap within logic; it is a gap between logic and something else entirely — between the domain of fact and the domain of attitude.

On Stevenson’s refinement — the two components of moral terms

Stevenson: moral terms have both descriptive meaning (which can change) and emotive meaning (which is relatively fixed); the emotive component cannot be derived from the descriptiveMy colleague Stevenson developed emotivism with greater care and precision in Ethics and Language. He showed that moral terms have two separable components: descriptive meaning and emotive meaning. The descriptive meaning of “good” — the factual criteria for its application — varies between speakers and over time; different people apply “good” to different things. But the emotive meaning — the positive attitude expressed by the word — is relatively fixed and constant across uses. This is an important refinement because it explains how moral disagreements can be genuine: two people can share the same factual beliefs but differ in their attitudes, and this difference in attitude is what the moral disagreement consists in. It cannot be resolved by facts alone, because the disagreement is not about facts. Crucially, Stevenson’s analysis reveals why Searle’s and Foot’s attempts to derive ought from is must fail: even if you give them every factual premise they want, the emotive component of the ought-conclusion — the attitude it expresses — cannot be generated by factual premises alone. You cannot argue someone into having an attitude they do not have, using only facts. You can change their factual beliefs and thereby indirectly affect their attitudes — but that is not a logical derivation.

On the logical grammar of ‘ought’

Logical grammar of ought: deontic operators (obligatory, permitted, forbidden) form a logical system irreducible to alethic or epistemic modalities; Ross’s theorem shows ought cannot follow from isThere is a formal logical point that reinforces the emotivist position. Deontic logic — the logic of obligation, permission, and prohibition — is a distinct modal system that cannot be reduced to alethic modality (the logic of necessity and possibility) or to any non-normative system. The axioms of deontic logic include: if something is obligatory, it is permitted (O implies P); if the conjunction of A and B is obligatory, then A is obligatory and B is obligatory separately; and so on. The crucial point is that in any sound deontic logic, deontic operators cannot be derived from non-deontic premises. This is sometimes called Ross’s theorem or the deontic version of Hume’s law. No set of factual premises, however large or well-chosen, will logically entail a deontic conclusion unless the argument form itself is deontic — unless it already contains a deontic premise. The opposition cannot escape this by adding premises: every premise they add that has genuine deontic force is itself an ought-statement whose normative status stands in need of explanation. And every premise they add that has no deontic force cannot contribute to a deontic conclusion. The logical gap is formal, not merely informal, and it cannot be closed by ingenuity in the selection of premises.

On the verification principle and its consequences for ethics

Verification principle: a statement is cognitively meaningful only if it is analytic or empirically verifiable; moral statements are neither, so they are not truth-apt; this grounds emotivism in the theory of meaningThe deeper ground for my position lies in the verification principle, which I developed as part of the logical positivist programme. A statement is genuinely factual — genuinely truth-apt — if and only if it is either analytically true by virtue of the meanings of its terms, or empirically verifiable by possible observations. Moral statements are neither. “Stealing is wrong” is not true by definition — there is no contradiction in saying “Stealing is wrong but I do not condemn it.” And it is not empirically verifiable: no observation of acts of stealing, however extensive, could verify that stealing is wrong rather than merely unpopular or socially disapproved. Therefore moral statements are not truth-apt. They are expressions of attitude masquerading as propositions. This explains why they cannot be derived from factual premises: derivation presupposes that what is derived is a proposition, and moral utterances are not propositions. The is-ought gap is a consequence of the emotivist theory of meaning. The opposition, to close the gap, must do one of two things: either show that moral statements are empirically verifiable (which they are not), or show that there is a third category of meaningful statement beyond the analytic and the empirical (which would require abandoning the verification principle entirely). I acknowledge that the verification principle faces difficulties of its own — most notably, that it appears to be neither analytic nor empirically verifiable, and is thus self-refuting by its own standard. But the core insight it captures — that moral language does not describe facts — survives the collapse of its over-rigid formulation.

On why the gap matters practically

Practical significance: the guillotine protects against ideological naturalism — the move from ‘nature ordains this’ to ‘we ought to do this’ that has underwritten much historical oppressionLet me say something about why this logical point has practical importance that transcends the seminar room. Hume’s guillotine is not merely a constraint on formal inference — it is a bulwark against a pervasive and dangerous form of argument: the inference from what is natural, or what has always been done, or what is socially functional, to what we ought to do. This inference has been used to justify the subordination of women (“it is natural for women to be caregivers; therefore women ought to be caregivers”), the persecution of homosexuals (“same-sex attraction is contrary to nature; therefore it ought to be suppressed”), and the defence of existing social hierarchies (“some people are naturally inferior; therefore they ought to occupy inferior positions”). In every case, the move from a claim about what is natural or actual to a claim about what ought to be is exactly the move Hume’s guillotine forbids. By maintaining the logical independence of the ought-domain from the is-domain, we preserve our ability to criticise what is from a standpoint that is not merely derived from what is. That critical standpoint is not factual — it is attitudinal — but its protection from factual arguments is precisely what makes moral reform possible.

On the strength and weakness of emotivism’s legacy

Legacy of emotivism: even if the strong verification-principle version is rejected, the core separation of fact and value survives; non-cognitivism in softer forms (expressivism, quasi-realism) retains the guillotineI am aware that emotivism in my strong, verificationist form is no longer fashionable, and that even I, in later reflection, softened some of its formulations. But the core insight — that there is a fundamental logical gap between descriptive and normative discourse — survives the collapse of the strong verification principle. In the softer, more sophisticated forms of non-cognitivism that have followed — Simon Blackburn’s quasi-realism, Allan Gibbard’s norm-expressivism — the guillotine is retained in modified form: moral utterances express non-cognitive states (attitudes, commitments to norms) that cannot be derived from purely factual premises. The guillotine survives its own sharpening. What the opposition tonight must show — and what I submit they cannot show — is not merely that some arguments cross from is to ought without being obviously fallacious, but that they do so without a hidden normative premise, without equivocating on the normative force of key terms, and without trading on conventions whose own normative standing is left unexplained. That is a very high bar. The guillotine has never been legitimately cleared.

Mr President,

I approach the question from a very different direction from my colleague Ayer. He denies that moral judgements are truth-apt propositions at all. I do not. I believe that ‘good’ stands for a genuine property of things — an irreducible, non-natural property — and that moral judgements are true or false. But precisely because this property is irreducible and non-natural, it cannot be derived from or identified with any natural property, however complex. The attempt to define ‘good’ in natural terms is what I call the naturalistic fallacy, and it is the error that underlies every apparent derivation of ought from is. Ayer and I agree that the derivation fails. We disagree about why.

On the indefinability of good

Indefinability of good: good is a simple, unanalysable property; any attempt to define it introduces a different concept; definitions of good are like attempts to define yellow in terms of wavelengthsConsider how we come to know the meaning of the word ‘yellow.’ We cannot define yellow by reference to other colours, or by reference to physical wavelengths, without making a mistake — the mistake of thinking that the property of being yellow is the same as the property of reflecting light of such-and-such a frequency. These are related properties, not identical ones. A person blind from birth who learns the physics of yellow light does not thereby come to know what yellow looks like. Similarly, ‘good’ is a simple, unanalysable property. It is not the same as pleasantness, or desirability, or evolutionary fitness, or conformity to God’s commands, or any other natural or supernatural property that philosophers have proposed as its definition. All such definitions commit what I call the naturalistic fallacy: they attempt to define a simple property by reference to something else, thereby changing the subject. ‘Good’ cannot be defined because it is a foundational concept in ethics — other ethical concepts are defined partly in terms of it, but it itself resists further analysis. This does not make it mysterious or unknowable: it is directly apprehended in moral intuition, just as yellow is directly apprehended in vision.

On the open question argument

Open question argument: for any natural property N, it is always an open question whether things that have N are good; this shows good is not identical with NThe open question argument is the main weapon in my armoury, and I want to deploy it carefully. Suppose a philosopher proposes that ‘good’ means ‘what we desire to desire’ — a definition offered by Mill and others in the utilitarian tradition. If this definition were correct, then the sentence “Is what we desire to desire actually good?” would be equivalent to asking “Is what we desire to desire, what we desire to desire?” — a trivial, closed, analytic question. But it is not trivial. It is a genuinely open question — a question any sincere, thoughtful person could meaningfully ask without confusing themselves or exhibiting a linguistic error. The very openness of this question shows that ‘good’ and ‘what we desire to desire’ do not mean the same thing: they refer to different properties, even if those properties are empirically correlated. And this argument generalises: for any natural property N whatsoever, the question “Is N actually good?” is always an open question. Therefore ‘good’ cannot be identical with any natural property. It is a non-natural property.

On naturalism’s many forms and why they all fail

Forms of naturalism: hedonism (good = pleasure), evolutionism (good = evolved fitness), social naturalism (good = socially approved), theological voluntarism (good = commanded by God) — the open question defeats allThe naturalistic fallacy takes many forms, and the opposition tonight will deploy several of them. Foot argues that good is grounded in natural facts about human flourishing — in what is naturally beneficial or harmful to members of our species. Anscombe argues that moral terms derive their force from facts about human needs and the concept of a human being. MacIntyre argues that the is-ought gap arises from the abandonment of Aristotelian teleology and can be closed by recovering it. But each of these views commits the same error in different dress. For Foot: “Is what promotes human flourishing actually good?” is an open question. For Anscombe: “Is what human beings need actually good for them in the moral sense?” is an open question. For MacIntyre: “Does the Aristotelian telos, even if we could identify it, give us genuine moral reasons?” is an open question. The open question argument is a master argument that defeats any naturalist definition of good, whatever its specific content. The naturalness of the property invoked is irrelevant: even if every human being were constitutionally disposed to approve of an action, the question “But is it actually good?” would remain meaningful.

On non-naturalism and the apprehension of goodness

Non-naturalism: good is a real, mind-independent, non-natural property apprehended by moral intuition; it is not derived from natural facts but is known directlyMy positive view is that ‘good’ stands for a real property — one that exists independently of our attitudes or preferences — but that this property is not natural. It is not observable by the five senses; it is not measurable or reducible to physical or biological properties. It is apprehended directly in moral intuition. When we contemplate a generous act and judge it good, we are perceiving a genuine feature of that act, not merely projecting an attitude onto it. This is why moral disagreements are genuine disagreements about a matter of fact, not mere conflicts of attitude — which is where I differ from Ayer. Two people who disagree about whether an act is good are disagreeing about whether it has a real property, not merely expressing different emotional reactions. And this is why moral intuitions can be correct or mistaken, refined or corrupted — we are attempting to perceive something real, and we can do so more or less accurately. The faculty of moral intuition is fallible but genuine. Where Ayer sees the guillotine as showing that ought-statements are not truth-apt, I see it as showing that they are truth-apt but not derivable from is-statements, because the properties they describe are not natural properties.

On supervenience and the is-ought relationship

Supervenience: moral properties supervene on natural ones — two situations identical in all natural respects must be identical in moral respects; but supervenience is not identity and does not enable derivationI want to acknowledge one important concession that does not undermine my position. It seems clearly right that moral properties supervene on natural ones: two situations that are exactly alike in every natural respect cannot differ in their moral properties. If an act is wrong in situation A, and situation B is exactly like A in all non-moral respects, then the act in situation B is also wrong. This supervenience relation holds — I do not deny it. But supervenience is not identity. The fact that moral properties covary with natural ones does not mean they are identical to them, any more than the fact that mental properties covary with physical ones means they are identical to them. Supervenience is compatible with the open question: it tells us that when the natural facts change, the moral facts change too, but it does not tell us which natural change is required to produce which moral change, and it does not enable us to read off the moral facts from the natural ones by any rule. The naturalistic fallacy — conflating supervenience with identity or derivability — is still a fallacy even when supervenience is granted.

On the contrast with Ayer and why realism is a stronger position

Realism vs emotivism: Moore’s non-naturalism explains why moral disagreements are genuine and why moral intuitions can be correct or mistaken — things Ayer’s emotivism cannot explainI want to press the case that my non-naturalist realism is a stronger position than Ayer’s emotivism. Ayer holds that moral utterances are not truth-apt — they express attitudes rather than report facts. But this seems to get the phenomenology of moral disagreement wrong. When you tell me that an act of gratuitous cruelty is permissible and I tell you it is wrong, we are not merely reporting different emotional reactions — we are contradicting each other. One of us is right and the other is wrong. Ayer’s view makes this contradiction impossible: two expressions of attitude cannot contradict each other any more than “Ouch!” and “Hurray!” can contradict each other. Stevenson attempted to account for this by analysing moral disagreement as disagreement in attitude, which can be rational if the attitudes are based on different factual beliefs. But this analysis leaves unresolved cases of moral disagreement that persists after all factual disagreements have been resolved — which is precisely the kind of disagreement that most interests us. My non-naturalism preserves the truth-aptness of moral discourse while maintaining the is-ought gap: we can be right or wrong about moral facts, even though those facts are not derivable from natural facts. This is, I submit, the correct analysis.

On the response to Foot’s challenge from natural goodness

Against Foot: the open question defeats natural goodness; even if natural flourishing facts are granted, the question “But are they morally good?” remains openFoot will argue that her account of natural goodness — grounded in facts about the characteristic functioning of organisms of a species — provides a route around the open question argument. She will say that just as a defective wolf is one that lacks traits characteristic of the species, a defective human being is one that lacks the virtues characteristic of humanity, and that this deficiency is not merely descriptive but genuinely evaluative. I want to press the open question against her directly. Even granting everything Foot says about characteristic human functioning — even granting that honesty, courage, and justice are characteristic of human flourishing — the question remains: “Is what is characteristic of human flourishing actually morally good?” This is not a trivial question. A Nietzschean can coherently hold that what is characteristic of human flourishing is precisely what the higher human being ought to transcend. The open question survives Foot’s naturalism, and the guillotine holds.

Mr President,

My colleagues have approached the problem from the theories of meaning and the metaphysics of moral properties. I approach it from the logic of moral language and the structure of moral argument. My claim is that the is-ought gap is not merely real but is actually the key to explaining how moral reasoning works as a rational enterprise. It is precisely because moral terms are prescriptive — not purely descriptive — that moral argument has the structure it has. And it is precisely because Searle’s derivation from promising conceals a normative premise that it fails, as I showed directly in my response to him in “The Promising Game.”

On the logical structure of moral terms

Prescriptivism: moral terms like ‘good’ and ‘ought’ have both descriptive meaning (criteria of application) and prescriptive meaning (commitment to guide action); the prescriptive component cannot be derived from purely descriptive premisesThe key to understanding the is-ought gap lies in the logical structure of moral terms. In The Language of Morals, I argued that moral terms like ‘good,’ ‘right,’ and ‘ought’ have a dual structure. They have descriptive meaning: when I call an action good, I am committing myself to certain factual criteria — perhaps that it produces happiness, respects persons, or manifests virtue. The descriptive meaning can vary between speakers and is what allows factual evidence to be relevant to moral conclusions. But they also have prescriptive meaning: in calling an action good, I am expressing a commitment to perform it, to commend it to others, to guide behaviour in its direction. This prescriptive meaning cannot be derived from purely descriptive premises, because it involves a commitment to action that goes beyond any description of how things are. The is-ought gap is the gap between descriptive and prescriptive meaning — between what is and what is to be done. It is real, logically compelling, and absolutely fundamental to the structure of moral reasoning.

On universalisability and moral argument

Universalisability: moral prescriptions must be universalisable — if you prescribe an action in situation S, you are committed to prescribing it in any situation that is relevantly similar; this gives moral argument its rational structureThe is-ought gap does not mean that moral reasoning is arbitrary or irrational. Quite the contrary: it is the universalisability of moral prescriptions that gives moral argument its rational structure. When I make a moral judgement — when I say “You ought to keep your promise in this situation” — I am committed, on pain of inconsistency, to making the same judgement in any situation that is relevantly similar. If I refuse to universalise — if I say “I ought to keep my promise but Jones, in the same situation, need not keep his” — I must point to a relevant difference between my situation and Jones’s that justifies the different prescription. If I cannot, I am inconsistent. Universalisability is a logical feature of the prescriptive component of moral terms, not a separate moral principle. It is what makes moral argument genuine argument rather than mere expression of preference. And it is what explains why moral discourse is not merely emotive but genuinely rational — while still maintaining the is-ought gap.

On the promising game and the refutation of Searle

The Promising Game: Searle’s derivation rests on the institutional rule “one ought to keep one’s promises” which is itself a normative premise, not a factual one; the derivation is valid only by smuggling in this premiseLet me now address Searle’s famous argument directly, since I have published a direct response to it. Searle claims to derive an ought-statement from pure is-statements by the following route: (1) Jones uttered the words “I promise to pay you five dollars.” (2) Jones made a promise to pay Smith five dollars. (3) Jones placed himself under an obligation to pay Smith five dollars. (4) Jones is under an obligation to pay Smith five dollars. (5) Jones ought to pay Smith five dollars. The argument appears to move from the factual premise (1) to the normative conclusion (5) by passing through the institutional facts of promising. But where does the normative force come from? It comes from the institution of promising, which includes the rule: those who make promises are thereby obligated to keep them. This rule is not a factual claim; it is a normative claim about the moral force of promising. Searle’s argument depends on the premise “One ought to keep one’s promises” — or more precisely, on the premise that the institution of promising carries genuine moral obligating force — and this premise is itself an ought-claim, not an is-claim. The derivation is not from pure is to ought; it is from an institutional ought (embedded in the practice) to an individual ought. The guillotine is not crossed; it is hidden inside the institution.

On the distinction between institutional and moral ought

Institutional vs moral ought: ‘ought’ in Searle’s conclusion means ‘is required by the institution of promising’ not ‘is morally required’; the latter cannot be derived without a further normative premiseThere is a further and deeper problem with Searle’s argument that my direct response to him in ‘The Promising Game’ identified. Even if we grant that the institution of promising generates an institutional obligation — the sort of obligation that exists within and relative to a social practice — this institutional obligation is not the same as a full-blooded moral obligation. Institutional obligations are conditional on the legitimacy of the institution: they bind you only if the institution has genuine moral authority. The statement “Jones ought to pay Smith” in Searle’s conclusion means, at most, “Jones is required to pay Smith by the norms of the institution of promising.” To derive “Jones morally ought to pay Smith” requires the further premise: “The institution of promising has genuine moral authority; its requirements create genuine moral obligations.” This is an unambiguously normative premise. And Searle has not derived it from factual premises; he has simply assumed it. The distinction between institutional and moral ought is the crack through which the guillotine’s blade falls.

On the limits of thick ethical concepts

Thick ethical concepts: terms like ‘cruel,’ ‘courageous,’ and ‘treacherous’ combine descriptive and evaluative elements; they appear to bridge is and ought but only because they already contain a normative commitmentThe opposition will also appeal to what Bernard Williams called ‘thick’ ethical concepts — terms like ‘cruel,’ ‘courageous,’ ‘treacherous,’ and ‘honest’ — which combine a descriptive component (describing a pattern of behaviour) with an evaluative component (commending or condemning it). The argument is: from the purely factual premise “What Jones did was cruel,” we can derive “Jones acted wrongly,” thus bridging is and ought without a separate normative premise. But this argument merely relocates the problem. The thick concept ‘cruel’ already contains an evaluative component — the negative prescriptive commitment that cruelty is to be avoided. The premise “What Jones did was cruel” is not a pure is-statement; it is already partly an ought-statement, or at least a statement with built-in prescriptive implications. The fact that it looks like a description does not make it one in the relevant logical sense. Once we recognise this — once we acknowledge that thick ethical concepts combine description and evaluation — the appearance of an is-to-ought derivation dissolves. We are not moving from is to ought; we are moving from a disguised ought to an explicit one.

On the rationality of moral argument and why the gap enables, not hinders, it

The gap enables rationality: because moral prescriptions must be universalised, factual argument can rationally challenge moral views by exposing their universalisation consequences; this is more rational, not less, than a naturalist derivationI want to press a point that gets lost in discussions of the guillotine: maintaining the is-ought gap is not an obstacle to rational moral argument — it is the precondition of it. Here is why. If moral conclusions could be derived from factual premises alone, then moral disagreement would be resolvable by factual enquiry: we would simply investigate the facts and read off the moral conclusion. But in reality, moral disagreement often persists after all factual disagreements have been resolved. Two people can agree on every fact about an abortion and still disagree about its morality. The is-ought gap explains this: the normative commitment — the value placed on foetal life, or on women’s autonomy, or on both — is not itself a factual matter, and cannot be resolved by factual enquiry. However, the universalisability requirement ensures that these normative commitments are subject to rational challenge. If you hold that abortion is wrong because it destroys a human life, I can challenge you by asking whether you are committed to the same judgement about all cases of destroying human life — war, capital punishment, letting die. If you cannot consistently universalise your prescription, you face a rational challenge to revise it. This is rational moral argument, and it operates precisely through the is-ought gap, not despite it.

On freedom of the will and moral responsibility

Freedom and the ought: ‘ought implies can’ — prescriptive force requires genuine freedom of action; if human behaviour were fully determined by natural causes, no prescriptive conclusion would follow from any natural premiseThere is a final argument for the guillotine that I want to draw from the connection between prescriptive force and freedom. Ought implies can: for a prescriptive statement to have genuine practical force — for “You ought to do X” to be a genuine prescription rather than an idle noise — it must be possible for the agent to do X. A prescription addressed to a stone is not a prescription; it is an absurdity. Now, if human actions were fully determined by natural causes — if human beings were nothing more than natural objects whose behaviour follows from prior natural states — then no prescriptive statement addressed to them would have genuine practical force. “You ought to have acted otherwise” would be like “You ought to have fallen upward.” The fact that prescriptive language makes sense — that we are genuine agents to whom prescriptions can be addressed — shows that human beings are not simply natural objects, not fully determined by prior natural causes. And this shows that the domain of the prescriptive is not the domain of the natural: the ought-domain presupposes a kind of freedom that the is-domain cannot supply. The guillotine cuts here too.

Mr President,

I want to begin by questioning the very terms in which this debate has been framed. The motion assumes that ‘ought’ in its moral use is a perfectly coherent concept whose logical relations to factual claims are the central problem of ethics. I believe this assumption is wrong. The moral ‘ought’ — the sense of ought that implies obligation, duty, and the special kind of moral necessity invoked by Hume and his followers — is not a coherent concept for those of us who lack the theological framework that originally gave it its sense. And if the concept is incoherent, the question of whether you can derive it from factual premises is not a deep metaphysical question but a pseudo-problem. My aim in Modern Moral Philosophy was to clear the ground by exposing this incoherence and pointing toward the Aristotelian concepts of virtue, human flourishing, and practical necessity that should replace the bankrupt vocabulary of obligation and ought.

On the incoherence of the moral ought

The moral ought is a survival of divine-law ethics: in a secular context it is incoherent; we use it without the framework that gives it senseThe term ‘ought’ in its specifically moral use — the ‘moral ought’ as opposed to the practical ought of “you ought to take an umbrella if you want to stay dry” — carries with it the notion of a special, overriding kind of necessity: a necessity that is not merely hypothetical but categorical, not merely prudential but moral. This notion — of an action being morally obligatory regardless of what the agent wants — makes sense within the framework of divine-law ethics. If there is a God who commands certain actions and forbids others, and if breaking His commands carries consequences of the deepest kind, then there is a real sense in which certain actions are categorically obligatory: they are commanded by a law that no human authority can override. But most of us, in the modern secular world, have abandoned that framework while retaining its vocabulary. We speak of moral obligations, of actions being categorically required or forbidden, of moral necessity that overrides prudential calculation — but without the divine-law framework that gave these notions their content. The result is that our moral vocabulary is a set of verbal survivals from a dead system of thought. The moral ‘ought’ is like the word ‘sin’ used by someone who has rejected the concept of God: the word is there, but nothing in the user’s world makes it apt.

On why this dissolves the is-ought problem

If the moral ought is incoherent, the is-ought problem is a pseudo-problem: the question “can we derive an ought from an is?” has no coherent answer because ‘ought’ has no coherent senseIf I am right that the moral ‘ought’ is an incoherent survival, then Hume’s guillotine, as a thesis about the impossibility of deriving moral oughts from factual ises, is not so much wrong as it is a theorem about a pseudo-concept. You cannot derive X from any set of premises if X has no coherent content. The is-ought problem is a problem about the logical relations of a concept — the moral ought — that we should be discarding rather than analysing. The proposition is defending the guillotine as if it were a deep metaphysical truth. I am saying it is a truth about an incoherent concept, and that the right response is not to puzzle over the logic of the moral ought but to replace it with concepts that do real work: virtue, human flourishing, practical necessity, natural goodness. When we ask “Is honesty a human virtue?” or “Does courage enable a human being to flourish?” we are asking questions that can be answered by attending to facts about human nature, human life, and what human beings genuinely need — without invoking the incoherent moral ought at all. The is-ought gap arises within the framework of modern moral philosophy; abandon the framework and the gap closes.

On brute facts and the grammar of ‘wanting’

Brute facts: some facts about what people want, need, and lack are not purely descriptive; they carry normative weight by virtue of their place in the grammar of human life; the is-ought gap does not run through these factsI introduced the concept of brute facts — facts that are brute relative to a context of description — to illuminate how factual language already carries normative weight in certain registers. Consider a shop assistant who sells me groceries and I hand him a piece of paper. Whether this constitutes payment depends on whether certain institutional and normative structures are in place. But below the level of institutions, there are facts about what human beings need, about what harms and what helps them, that are not simply neutral descriptions. When I say that a person is hungry, I am not merely describing a physiological state; I am already in the vicinity of the claim that there is something that would address this — food — and something from which the person suffers — the lack of it. The grammar of wanting, needing, suffering, and flourishing already has normative weight built into it, not by convention or institution, but by the place these concepts occupy in a form of life. This is why Foot can say that the viper who lacks venom is a defective viper without committing the naturalistic fallacy: the concept of deficiency is already normatively laden in a way that does not require deriving an ought from an is.

On the Aristotelian alternative and practical necessity

Practical necessity: the Aristotelian ‘must’ is grounded in facts about what a creature of a certain kind needs to flourish; it is not a moral ought but a necessity internal to a form of lifeThe alternative I propose draws on Aristotle’s concept of practical necessity — what a creature must do given what it is and what it needs. A wolf must hunt; a bee must gather pollen; a human being must develop practical wisdom, build relationships, and live in community. These are not merely prudential recommendations that happen to align with our desires; they are necessities internal to the kind of creature we are. The grammar of ‘must’ here is not the categorical moral must of the divine-law tradition — it is what I call the Aristotelian necessity, the necessity of what a creature needs in order to be a good specimen of its kind and to live a characteristically good life for that kind of creature. This concept is not derived from a prior ought; it arises from reflection on what we are. And it bridges the is-ought gap without either invoking a divine legislator or committing Moore’s naturalistic fallacy, because the normative weight of ‘must’ here is not placed on top of a neutral description of nature but is already internal to the concepts by which we describe the life of a creature with purposes.

On the harm done by the wrong moral philosophy

The harm of modern moral philosophy: by preserving the incoherent vocabulary of obligation while abandoning its framework, it has produced a moral philosophy unable to explain why we should be moral at allModern moral philosophy — the tradition running from Hume through Kant through the utilitarians to Ayer and Hare — has done real harm by preserving the incoherent vocabulary of obligation and moral necessity while abandoning the theological framework that gave them sense. The result is a moral philosophy that cannot answer the most fundamental question: why should I be moral? Ayer’s answer is that moral utterances express attitudes — but then the question becomes “Why should I have these attitudes rather than others?” and Ayer has no answer. Hare’s answer is that universalisability imposes rational constraints on prescriptions — but the rational constraints are constraints on consistency, and consistency alone does not tell us what values to have. Kant’s answer is that the categorical imperative is a requirement of pure practical reason — but as I argued in “Modern Moral Philosophy,” the notion of a categorical imperative without God makes no more sense than legislation without a legislator. The entire tradition is a sustained attempt to derive something like the moral ought from resources that are inadequate for the purpose. The solution is not to find a better derivation but to give up the concept of the moral ought and return to the more tractable concepts of virtue, human need, and practical wisdom that Aristotle bequeathed to us.

On the connection between intention and action

Intentional action: Anscombe’s account of intention — the ‘why?’ question in practical reasoning already contains a normative structure; description of intentional action is not purely descriptiveMy work on intention — in the book of that name — provides another route around the is-ought gap. Intentional actions are not simply events in the natural world; they are described under descriptions that the agent would acknowledge as correct answers to the question “Why are you doing this?” The question “Why?” asked of an action is not a causal question but a teleological one: it asks for the purpose or end for which the action is done. And the answer — “I am going to the shop because I want bread” — is not a purely factual statement but a statement with a normative structure: wanting bread is a reason for going to the shop, and this ‘reason-for’ relation is not a causal or factual relation but a practical one. The grammar of practical reasoning — of means and ends, of reasons for action — is not a purely descriptive grammar; it is already a normative grammar, one in which facts about wants and needs carry justificatory weight. This is not deriving an ought from a pure is; it is recognising that some of the concepts we use to describe human action are already normatively laden, and that the is-ought gap does not run through all the joints of reality.

On Hare’s prescriptivism and its failure

Against Hare: universal prescriptivism cannot explain why anyone should adopt any particular set of prescriptions; the universalisability requirement is too weak to generate moral contentLet me say a direct word about Hare, whose prescriptivism I find unsatisfying in precisely the way I have described. Hare maintains the is-ought gap and argues that moral terms prescribe rather than describe. The universalisability requirement is supposed to provide rational constraints on prescriptions. But Hare admits that someone who, after full reflection on the universalisation consequences of their prescriptions, is willing to prescribe that all members of a certain race be treated as inferior, is not making a logical error — they are making a moral one. And Hare’s framework cannot distinguish these two kinds of error: it can say only that the racist’s prescriptions are consistent, and consistency is not enough to make them moral. The framework has too little content to generate real moral conclusions. What we need — and what Aristotle provides — is not a logic of prescriptions but an account of what prescriptions are warranted by the kind of creatures we are and the kind of life that is good for us. That account is descriptive in a rich sense that makes the is-ought gap inapplicable to it.

Anscombe has argued that we should dissolve the is-ought problem by abandoning the incoherent moral ought altogether. My approach is more direct: I want to show that the is-ought gap, even understood on the proposition’s own terms, can be crossed — that there is a legitimate route from facts about living things to evaluative conclusions about what is good or defective in them, and that human moral goodness is a species of this more general natural goodness. The key is to attend carefully to what kind of evaluative language is already available within a purely biological or naturalistic framework, and to show that this language is genuinely evaluative, not merely descriptive.

On natural goodness and the life-form standard

Natural goodness: for any living thing, there is a life-form standard for its kind; an individual is a good specimen of its kind iff it has the traits characteristic of a flourishing member of that species; this is genuinely evaluativeMy central claim is that goodness and deficiency in living things — in plants, animals, and human beings — is assessed relative to a life-form standard: the pattern of development, capacities, and activity that is characteristic of a flourishing member of the species. A wolf that cannot run well is a defective wolf; not because running well is valued by us, but because running well is what wolves characteristically do and need in order to function as wolves. The evaluation “this is a defective wolf” is genuinely evaluative — not merely a mismatch between our preferences and the wolf’s traits — but it is grounded entirely in facts about what wolves characteristically are and do. No normative premise beyond the biological facts is needed to make this evaluation. This is a form of evaluation already available within a naturalistic framework, before we invoke any specifically moral concepts. And the crucial move in my argument is that human virtue and vice are species of this same natural goodness and deficiency: a human being who lacks the virtues of justice, courage, and practical wisdom is defective as a human being in precisely the way that a wolf without proper running capacity is defective as a wolf — evaluated against the life-form standard of our species.

On the two kinds of natural goodness — individual and species-level

Individual vs species goodness: natural goodness is assessed at species level, not individual level; what is good for the individual qua member of its species may differ from what is good for it as this individual; virtue is good at the species levelThere is an important distinction I need to make to prevent misunderstanding of my position. When I say that a good human being is one who has the virtues characteristic of human flourishing, I am not saying that whatever makes this individual feel good is good for her. The life-form standard is a species-level standard, not an individual preference standard. Just as a social bee that refuses to forage and contribute to the hive is a defective bee — not because it personally feels bad about this but because foraging is what members of its species characteristically do and what the species-level life form requires — so a human being who is unjust, cowardly, or dishonest is a defective human being, evaluated against the characteristic life form of the species. This species-level evaluation is not a disguised report of individual preference or social attitude; it is a claim about the relationship between individual traits and the characteristic life form of the kind. It is genuinely evaluative and it is grounded in natural facts about what human beings characteristically are.

On the route from biological facts to moral conclusions

Goodness, on my view, is not a simple property; it is a complex relational property: the property of having the traits characteristic of a flourishing specimen of one’s kind. On practical rationality and the will

Honesty is not merely a disposition to tell the truth; it is a disposition to tell the truth because telling the truth matters, because people deserve accurate information, because trust is essential to human relationships. The virtuous person acts for these reasons, and these reasons are facts about human life and human relationships — not mere expressions of preference. The connection between factual reasons and normative conclusions is therefore direct: the facts about human needs, social relationships, and the conditions for human cooperation provide reasons for action, and these reasons are not merely instrumentally related to independently specified desires but are partly constitutive of what it means to act well as a human being. The is-ought gap does not run between facts about human needs and reasons and conclusions about how to act, because the facts about human needs are already practical facts, already in the domain of reasons.

On why natural goodness is not a form of naturalism in Moore’s sense

This concept has its home in biology and in the study of living things, but it is not reducible to non-evaluative description. It is a genuinely normative concept that arises within nature rather than being imposed on it from outside. This is why my account does not commit Moore’s naturalistic fallacy: I am not identifying a non-natural property (moral goodness) with a natural one (biological fitness). I am arguing that moral goodness is a specification of a concept — natural goodness — that is already normatively laden in its biological applications. On the distinction between brute and institutional facts

Brute vs institutional facts: brute facts obtain independently of human institutions (the rock weighs 10kg); institutional facts obtain only in virtue of human practices and constitutive rules (Jones scored a goal, Jones made a promise)The key to my argument is the distinction between brute facts and institutional facts. A brute fact is one that obtains independently of human institutions, conventions, and practices: the rock weighs ten kilograms; the temperature is fifteen degrees; water is composed of hydrogen and oxygen molecules. These facts would be true even if human beings had never existed. An institutional fact, by contrast, obtains only in virtue of human practices, conventions, and constitutive rules: Jones scored a goal, Smith is the President, the piece of paper is a twenty-pound note. These facts presuppose a background of institutions — the game of football, the practice of government, the monetary system — without which they would not exist. The constitutive rules of institutions are rules of the form “X counts as Y in context C”: certain sounds count as promising in the context of human speech acts; certain movements count as scoring in the context of football. Institutional facts are generated by these constitutive rules operating on brute facts. And my claim is that some institutional facts have inherent normative implications: they generate obligations, rights, and duties simply by virtue of the institutional practices they instantiate.

On the five-step derivation

The derivation: (1) Jones said “I promise to pay you $5” → (2) Jones made a promise → (3) Jones placed himself under obligation → (4) Jones is under obligation → (5) Jones ought to pay; each step is entailed by the previous given institutional factsHere is my derivation. Premise (1): Jones uttered the words “I hereby promise to pay you, Smith, five dollars.” This is a brute fact — a fact about the sounds Jones produced. Step (2): Jones made a promise to pay Smith five dollars. This follows from (1) given the constitutive rules of the practice of promising: uttering those words in appropriate circumstances counts as making a promise. Step (3): Jones placed himself under (undertook) an obligation to pay Smith five dollars. This follows from (2) because creating an obligation is part of what promising is: it is of the very nature of a promise that by making it you place yourself under an obligation. This is not a separate normative claim added from outside; it is an analytic feature of the concept of a promise. Step (4): Jones is under an obligation to pay Smith five dollars. This follows from (3) — having placed himself under an obligation, he is under it. Step (5): Jones ought to pay Smith five dollars. This follows from (4) — being under an obligation to do X entails that one ought to do X, ceteris paribus. Each step is entailed by the previous one given the institutional and conceptual facts in play. No normative premise has been added from outside the argument.

On Hume’s own passage and its misreading

Hume’s passage misread: in “Hume on Is and Ought” (1959), MacIntyre argues Hume is making a methodological point, not a logical claim about the impossibility of deriving ought from is; he is calling for better argument, not proclaiming an eternal logical gapIn my 1959 paper “Hume on Is and Ought” in the Philosophical Review, I argued that the famous passage has been systematically misread. He is making a methodological point: be careful here; make your transitions explicit; do not assume the move from fact to value goes through without argument. This is not a logical thesis about the impossibility of derivation; it is a call for clarity and rigour in moral argument. The strong reading — that Hume is asserting a universal logical impossibility — was superimposed on him by later philosophers, particularly Moore and Ayer, who had their own reasons for wanting a sharp fact-value distinction. But the strong reading is not what Hume’s text says. And the misreading has had consequences: by taking the guillotine as a logical truth rather than a methodological warning, subsequent moral philosophy has painted itself into a corner from which it cannot escape without either collapsing into non-cognitivism or abandoning the fact-value distinction entirely.

Within this framework, moral precepts are not derived from a description of human nature in a value-neutral way; they are derived from a description of human nature in relation to its telos. The Enlightenment philosophers — Hume among them — rejected Aristotelian teleology as unscientific, as incompatible with the mechanistic world-view of Newtonian physics. On the objection that teleology is unscientific

This is a thoroughly naturalistic account of function and purpose that grounds teleological explanation in the causal history of natural selection, without invoking Aristotle’s vitalistic metaphysics or supernatural final causes. The heart has the function of pumping blood not because there is some spooky teleological force in nature, but because hearts were selected for in populations of organisms whose survival depended on blood being pumped. This rehabilitated teleology is available to ground the kind of claims about human nature and human function that the Aristotelian framework requires. The objection from science applies to Aristotle’s specific metaphysics, not to teleological explanation as such.

On narrative and the unity of a human life

Narrative unity: a human life has the form of a narrative; virtues are the qualities that enable us to sustain the narrative of a good life; the is-ought gap does not run through narrative descriptions of a human lifeI want to add a further dimension to the Aristotelian case — one that I develop in After Virtue through the concept of narrative. A human life, to be intelligible as a human life rather than a mere sequence of events, must have a narrative form: a beginning, a middle, and an end in which earlier stages bear on later ones, in which the agent pursues purposes across time and can give an account of why she does what she does in terms of a life story that makes sense. The virtues are the qualities that enable us to sustain the pursuit of good that defines the narrative of a human life: courage enables us to face the difficulties that obstruct our purposes; justice enables us to pursue our good without wronging others; honesty enables us to maintain the trust without which social cooperation is impossible. These are not merely descriptions of what virtuous people do; they are specifications of what it takes to live a recognisably human life with integrity, purpose, and coherence. And the move from the factual description of a human life to the normative conclusion about what virtues it requires is not a logical leap but an elaboration of what is already implicit in the concept of a life that has narrative form.

On what the debate reveals about modernity

What the debate reveals: the interminability of modern moral debate — including this one — is evidence that we are working with fragments of a moral vocabulary whose original context has been lost; recovering Aristotle is not nostalgia but necessityI want to close by pointing to what this debate itself reveals. The proposition’s three speakers disagree with each other about why the guillotine holds: Ayer says moral utterances are not truth-apt; Moore says moral properties are non-natural; Hare says moral terms are prescriptive. They each have a different theory of why the gap cannot be crossed. And the opposition’s speakers disagree about how to cross it: Anscombe says we should abandon the moral ought; Foot says we should ground it in natural goodness; Searle says promising is a direct derivation. This interminability — this inability of serious, intelligent philosophers to agree on basic questions — is precisely what I described as the symptom of moral fragmentation in After Virtue. We are working with fragments of moral vocabulary — pieces of an Aristotelian framework, of a divine-law framework, of a Kantian framework — that have been deracinated from the traditions and practices that originally gave them sense. The is-ought debate is not a timeless logical puzzle; it is the philosophical expression of a culture that has lost its moral inheritance. The way forward is not to resolve the debate on the proposition’s terms but to recover the tradition — specifically the Aristotelian tradition — within which the transition from human nature to human good was intelligible before the Enlightenment’s catastrophic intervention.
