JL Partners / The Telegraph — "Divided Britain"

Attitudinal Survey, March 31st – April 7th 2026

Published April 15, 2026

Why this poll matters more than any voting intention figure published this week

Every poll we have examined this week has answered the same question: who would people vote for if there were an election tomorrow? JL Partners have asked a different question entirely: what do people actually think, and does what they think map onto how they vote?

This distinction matters enormously. Voting intention tells you the output. Attitudinal data tells you the inputs — the values, fears, and cultural orientations that produce voting decisions. Understanding the inputs is what allows genuine prediction and analysis, rather than merely description of the current state. A party could use the other polls published this week to know they are trailing. They would use this poll to understand why, and what it would take to change.

The population caveat must be stated clearly upfront: JL Partners sampled adults in areas holding local elections in May 2026, not all GB adults. This makes the data directly relevant to the May electoral context but means the national headline figures are not strictly representative of the GB public as a whole. Areas with May 2026 local elections skew somewhat toward England outside London, which may marginally inflate the salience of certain cultural attitudes relative to a fully national sample. Readers should bear this in mind throughout.

The political divide that the voting intention polls cannot show you

The most analytically powerful feature of this dataset is the 2024 GE vote crossbreak applied to every attitudinal question. Across eleven substantive questions, a consistent pattern emerges that can be summarised as follows: Reform 2024 voters and Green 2024 voters sit at opposite poles of almost every cultural and values question tested, with Conservative 2024 voters closer to Reform on most dimensions and Labour 2024 voters closer to Green, while the electorate as a whole sits between them in a position that in many cases barely resembles either pole.

The immigration question is where the divide is most extreme. Asked whether they no longer recognise their country because of the scale of immigration in recent years, 85.5% of Reform 2024 voters agree (57.0% strongly). Among Green 2024 voters, 27.8% agree. The 57.7-point gap between Reform and Green voters on this question is the largest partisan gap in the entire dataset and larger than comparable measures from any attitudinal survey published this year. What is analytically interesting is where the other groups sit: Conservative 2024 voters at 61.4% are closer to Reform than to the national average of 51.0%. Labour 2024 voters at 39.3% are below the national average. 2024 non-voters at 51.6% are almost exactly at the national average, which tells you something about their political character: they are not a distinctive values group in the way that partisan voters are.

The "politics is broken" question is the one where partisan differences flatten most dramatically. Overall 64.2% agree that politics is broken and no one knows how to fix Britain. Among Reform voters: 76.4%. Among Green voters: 66.0%. Among Labour voters: 60.3%. Among Conservatives: 65.3%. This is the most cross-partisan finding in the dataset, and it is politically significant. A shared perception that the system is broken is the precondition for anti-system voting of all kinds — whether that anti-system vote goes to Reform, the Greens, or anywhere else depends on cultural orientation, not on the underlying grievance. The grievance is shared. The political direction it produces is determined by other values.

The diversity question is the cultural axis that most cleanly separates the electorate. Asked whether they embrace diversity and think it makes Britain a better place, 71.6% of Green 2024 voters strongly or tend to agree, against 22.7% of Reform 2024 voters. The distance between these two groups — 48.9 points — is almost as large as the immigration gap and operates in the opposite direction. This is not surprising but it is worth stating with precision: Reform voters are not merely more concerned about immigration than Green voters — they hold a fundamentally different orientation toward cultural diversity as a value. These are not positions on a spectrum that can be easily bridged by policy adjustment. They represent different worldviews.

The triple lock question produces the most striking inter-party anomaly. Offered a forced choice between "young people deserve more support — the triple lock should be reformed" and "older people deserve the protection the triple lock provides", 79.3% of Reform voters and 83.2% of Conservative voters chose the latter. The Reform figure is particularly striking because Nigel Farage's party has positioned itself in part as a vehicle for economic discontent among working-age voters squeezed by stagnation and rising costs. Yet on this question, Reform voters defend the pensioner priority more firmly than Labour voters (64.4%) or even the national average (65.8%). This is the clearest illustration in this dataset of the gap between Reform's stated economic positioning and their voters' revealed preferences. Reform is an older party — that demographic reality shapes their policy priorities, regardless of what their rhetoric says about left-behind working people.

Institutional trust: who trusts what, and what it means

The institutional trust section of this poll is the section most likely to be underreported and is, analytically, the most important for understanding long-term political dynamics.

The pattern across all institutions tested is consistent: the NHS is trusted broadly and across party lines; everything else is contested along identifiable partisan and class lines; and Reform 2024 voters are the most institutionally alienated group in the sample.

On the NHS "on my side" question, the net score (% saying on my side minus % saying not on my side) is +63 overall. Among Reform voters: +41. Among Green voters: +75. Even Reform voters — who have the lowest NHS trust in the sample — are substantially net positive. The NHS remains the one institution that transcends the partisan divide, and the finding that Reform voters give it a net +41 rather than a negative score is analytically significant for any party considering how to campaign on health.

The police trust figure is more revealing. The overall net is +41, but the partisan gap is the second-largest of any institution: Reform voters net +13, Conservative voters net +64, Green voters net +43, Labour voters net +63. Reform voters trust the police less than any other partisan group — including Labour voters, who historically have been more critical of police authority. This finding contradicts the assumption that Reform's law-and-order rhetoric reflects a corresponding trust in the existing law enforcement apparatus. Reform voters feel unsafe (63.7% say they sometimes feel unsafe from local crime — the highest of any group) and distrustful of the police simultaneously. This is the political psychology of a group that believes the existing institutions are failing to protect them — a posture that feeds anti-system sentiment rather than conservative institutional deference.

The Westminster government net scores are uniformly negative across all groups — the least surprising finding in the dataset, given that Labour governs and no major party's voters currently report satisfaction. But the distribution is instructive: Labour 2024 voters are net -7.5 on Westminster government (barely net negative, broadly consistent with soft governing-party loyalty); Conservative 2024 voters are net -21.7 (the opposition's voters disliking the government, as expected); Reform 2024 voters are net -54.0 (the most government-hostile electorate by a significant margin); Green 2024 voters are net -22.0 (more anti-government than Conservative voters, reflecting the progressive critique of Labour's performance).

The housing system produces the most uniformly negative scores of any institution tested. The overall net is -34, and no partisan group is net positive. Reform voters at -62 are most hostile. Green voters at -49 are second most hostile. Labour voters at -23 are the least hostile, but even they are substantially net negative. The housing system is the one area where all parts of the electorate — right-populist, progressive, centrist, non-voting — share a profound sense of institutional failure. This ought to be one of the most politically salient findings from any poll published this year. The next party to credibly claim ownership of a housing solution will be picking up votes from every part of the electorate simultaneously.

The BBC data contains one finding that demands specific attention. The net score for "BBC is on my side" among Reform 2024 voters is -32.4. This is the second-largest negative institutional net for Reform after the Westminster government. The BBC is more institutionally distrusted by Reform voters than the benefits system (-28.3), the tax system (-30.7), or the local council (-11.2). The political consequences of this are not trivial: if Reform voters regard the principal broadcast news organisation as adversarially positioned against them, any BBC coverage of Reform or its policies is filtered through a frame of institutional hostility. This is not a media literacy problem — it is a political psychology problem, and it shapes how political information reaches and is processed by a population of nearly half a million 2024 Reform voters.

The EU re-entry question: a trap Labour has not thought through

The question asking voters how a Labour pledge to rejoin the EU would affect their vote is the most strategically significant question in this dataset, and it illustrates precisely why attitudinal polling is more useful than voting intention polling for political strategy.

The headline figures look encouraging for Labour: 26.0% say they would be more likely to vote Labour if the party pledged to rejoin the EU, against 14.9% who say less likely — a net positive of 11.1 points. Among Labour 2024 voters themselves, 48.9% say more likely, 14.4% less likely — a strongly net positive response that suggests EU rejoining would consolidate Labour's existing base.

But the strategic question is not what it does to Labour's existing voters. It is what it does to the voters Labour needs to win back. Among 2024 Conservatives: 12.5% more likely, 18.0% less likely — net -5.5. Among Reform 2024 voters: 5.6% more likely, 20.5% less likely — net -14.9. Among 2024 non-voters: 20.5% more likely, 14.6% less likely — net +5.9.

The architecture of these numbers matters for seat-level analysis. If Labour is trying to win back votes from Conservative-held marginals in England — which is the arithmetic they need for a majority — an EU rejoining pledge loses them more Conservative-leaning switchers than it gains. If they are trying to turn out the non-voter bloc, it has a modest positive effect. If they are targeting the Greens, the 35.6% "more likely" figure suggests it would help retain progressive voters who might otherwise drift leftward.

The honest strategic read is: EU rejoining is a pledge that helps Labour in the seats they already hold and in fights with the Greens, and hurts them in the swing seats they need to regain from the Conservatives. A party that is already struggling to win back swing voters in the English shires would be making a structural electoral error by leading on EU re-entry, regardless of what their aggregate polling says.

National identity and social division: the "Divided Britain" headline findings

On the title question of this poll — is Britain more divided now than it was ten years ago? — 77.0% of respondents say yes (45.4% much more divided, 31.6% somewhat more divided). This is an overwhelming consensus on the fact of division, even if the causes and remedies are deeply contested.

The party crossbreak on this question is analytically interesting for what it reveals about the psychology of division. Reform 2024 voters lead on "much more divided" at 65.9%, which is consistent with their broader sense of institutional alienation. But Green voters are more likely than Conservative or Labour voters to say "somewhat more divided" (41.3%), suggesting that Green voters perceive division but locate it in a less apocalyptic register than Reform voters.

The common-ground questions — how much do you have in common with people in London, rural England, Scotland, Wales — reveal a geography of political alienation that maps closely onto the Reform vote. Reform 2024 voters are the group most likely to say they have "nothing at all in common" with people in London (34.7%) and least likely to feel common ground with Scotland (13.4% "nothing at all"). This is the data behind the intuition that Reform's electoral base is defined not only by what it opposes (immigration, the BBC, the Westminster government) but by what it feels excluded from — a sense that the country's economic, cultural, and political centres of gravity are oriented away from the communities that form Reform's heartland.

The pride questions tell a complementary story. Reform voters are the least proud of the United Kingdom as a whole (only 47.8% "very" or "fairly" proud, against 54.9% overall — and specifically 18.9% are "not at all proud", the highest of any group). This is a remarkable finding about a party whose brand is explicitly nationalist and patriotic. Reform voters are prouder of England specifically than of the United Kingdom, and their dissatisfaction with the UK as an entity is substantially higher than their dissatisfaction with England. This suggests Reform's nationalism is English rather than British in character — a finding with implications for constitutional politics as well as electoral strategy.

Five findings that should define how we understand this electorate

First: the politics-is-broken consensus is real and cross-partisan. 64.2% of the electorate agrees that politics is broken and no one knows how to fix Britain. This grievance is shared across Reform (76.4%), Green (66.0%), Conservative (65.3%) and Labour (60.3%) voters. The appropriate policy response differs radically between these groups, but the underlying diagnosis of institutional failure is held in common. Any political strategy that assumes the disaffected are a fringe phenomenon is working from the wrong premise.

Second: Reform voters are institutionally alienated in a way that is qualitatively different from Conservative discontent. Conservative voters are dissatisfied with the current government but broadly trust established institutions — police net +64, NHS net +64, local councils net +14. Reform voters distrust all of these. The -32 BBC score, -54 Westminster government score, -62 housing system score, and +13 police score represent a coherent psychology of institutional abandonment — the sense that no established institution serves or represents people like them. This is not Thatcherite scepticism of the state. It is something closer to withdrawal of consent from the social contract.

Third: the housing system is the one domain where genuine cross-partisan unity of grievance exists. Every partisan group is net negative on housing — -62 for Reform, -49 for Green, -27 for Conservative, -23 for Labour. Cost of living tops most issue-priority polls. Housing is the structural background to cost-of-living anxiety. The party that resolves it gains across the board. The party that fails gets punished across the board. At present, no party has claimed credible ownership of a solution.

Fourth: the EU question is a trap for Labour. The aggregate "more likely" figure looks encouraging. The segmented analysis by the voters Labour needs to win — Conservative-leaners in English marginals — shows a net negative. Labour cannot afford to lose more ground to the Conservatives on cultural issues while simultaneously fighting the Greens on progressive ones. EU rejoining is a policy that solves one half of that problem and worsens the other.

Fifth: Reform's populism is older and more defensive than its rhetoric suggests. The triple lock finding — 79.3% of Reform voters prioritise pensioner protection over support for young people — is the data beneath the rhetoric. Reform talks about left-behind working people. Their voters think like a constituency that has accumulated assets and is protecting them. The tension between these two things is the most important unresolved strategic question in British right-populist politics, and it will only become more pressing as the party approaches an actual general election with the prospect of governing.

-----------------------------

Analysis based on JL Partners polling for The Telegraph. Fieldwork 31 March – 7 April 2026. N=4,909. Population: adults aged 18+ in areas holding May 2026 local elections. Margin of error: 1.4% on headline figures; approximately 4.6% on Reform subgroup (N=492); approximately 7.0% on Green subgroup (N=235).
