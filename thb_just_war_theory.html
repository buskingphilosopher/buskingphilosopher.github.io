Mr President,

Let me begin by saying what traditional just war theory actually is, because the opposition will caricature it. It is not a set of quaint medieval prohibitions handed down unchanged from Aquinas. It is a living tradition of moral reasoning about the hardest questions humanity faces: when is it permissible to kill, and under what constraints must killing be conducted?

Jus ad bellum: just cause · right intention · proportionality · last resort · legitimate authority · reasonable chance of success
Jus in bello: discrimination (non-combatant immunity) · proportionality · military necessity
Moral equality of combatants: soldiers on both sides of a just/unjust war fight under equal moral constraints
The legalist paradigm: aggression against state sovereignty is the paradigm crime; defence is the paradigm justification On the legalist paradigm and state sovereignty

Argument from the legalist paradigmThe foundation of traditional just war theory is what I have called the legalist paradigm: the analogy between states and persons in international relations. Just as individuals have rights to life and liberty that others may not violate, states possess rights of territorial integrity and political sovereignty. Aggression against a state — the use of force to violate these rights — is the international analogue of murder and robbery. It is the paradigm crime in international affairs. This framework is not an abstraction. It is the moral structure that underlies the international order, the United Nations Charter, and the shared intuitions of virtually every political community on earth about when war is justified.

On the moral equality of combatants

Argument from moral equality of combatantsThe most important practical principle of just war theory is the moral equality of combatants. Soldiers who fight in accordance with the rules of war — who do not target civilians, who do not use forbidden weapons, who observe the laws of armed conflict — are morally permitted to kill enemy combatants, regardless of whether the war they are fighting is just or unjust. This is not a confusion. It is a moral recognition that soldiers are not responsible for the political decisions of their governments, that they typically fight under coercion and with incomplete information, and that holding them individually liable for the justice of their cause would make the conduct of war — which is already terrible enough — completely impossible to regulate. The moral equality of combatants is what makes international humanitarian law workable.

On non-combatant immunity

Argument from non-combatant immunityNon-combatant immunity is the cornerstone of jus in bello. Civilians — those who are not participating in the prosecution of war — may not be directly targeted. This prohibition is not derived from an abstract philosophical theory about individual liability. It is derived from the distinction, morally essential and practically crucial, between those who are engaged in threatening force and those who are not. The civilian who has no say in whether her country goes to war, who farms or nurses or teaches while others fight, has done nothing that could justify her being targeted. Traditional just war theory captures this moral truth simply and robustly. The revisionist attempt to ground immunity in individual liability threatens to erode it by introducing endless qualifications.

On the emergency ethic and supreme emergency

Argument from supreme emergencyTraditional just war theory also provides — through the doctrine of supreme emergency — a morally serious account of the limits of its own constraints. When a community faces a threat of utter destruction, when the enemy threatens to impose a tyranny that would eliminate the very conditions of political life, the normal prohibitions may temporarily give way. This is not a loophole or a licence for brutality. It is an honest acknowledgment that our moral commitments can sometimes conflict and that in genuinely extreme circumstances, ordinary rules may be overridden. The critics of traditional JWT offer no better account of this tragic moral territory.

On why revisionism is dangerous in practice

Argument from practical necessityThe revisionist challenge to traditional JWT — led by my opponents today — may be philosophically sophisticated, but it is practically disastrous. If soldiers must individually assess the justice of their cause before killing, military discipline becomes impossible. If liability tracks individual moral responsibility in the fine-grained way revisionists demand, no combatant can ever be confident she fights permissibly. The rules of war have achieved something remarkable: they have created a shared framework that all parties to a conflict can operate within. To dismantle that framework in favour of individually calibrated moral calculations is not moral progress. It is a recipe for moral anarchy on the battlefield.

The traditional framework stands because it is morally serious about what war is — organised violence between political communities — and practically honest about what norms can actually govern it.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to make a different case from Professor Walzer — not the case from institutional necessity, but the case from moral theory. I accept that revisionism has correctly identified some problems with the traditional account. What I deny is that those problems require us to abandon the framework. They require us to refine it.

On the distinction between jus ad bellum and jus in bello

Argument from independence of jus in belloTraditional just war theory's most important structural feature is the independence of jus ad bellum from jus in bello. Whether a war is just — whether it was legitimately initiated — is a separate moral question from how it must be conducted. This separation is not an arbitrary convention. It reflects the genuine moral complexity of armed conflict. A soldier who fights in an unjust war is not necessarily an unjust combatant. She may fight under orders she cannot reasonably defy, with information she could not have access to, in a political system that gives her no meaningful say. The independence of jus in bello preserves moral assessment of conduct even when the war's initiation is wrong.

On liability and non-combatant immunity

Argument from liability and civilian protectionThe revisionist account, as developed by Professor McMahan, holds that liability to be killed in war tracks individual moral responsibility for an unjust threat. I have sympathy with the underlying thought. But this account generates deeply counter-intuitive results for non-combatant immunity. If immunity is grounded in non-liability derived from individual responsibility, then civilians who contribute substantially to an unjust war effort — munitions workers, propagandists, those who voted for an aggressive government — might in principle be targetable. This is a reductio of the revisionist view. Traditional JWT's clean prohibition on targeting civilians does not depend on these invidious distinctions.

On self-defence and the right to kill in war

Argument from self-defence and defensive killingMy own work has focused on the ethics of self-defence as the foundation for the permission to kill in war. What I have argued — and what traditional JWT captures, if imperfectly — is that the right to kill an attacker derives from the threat they pose, not merely from their moral culpability. A culpably innocent aggressor — someone who poses a deadly threat through no moral fault of their own — can still be defensively killed. This is precisely what the traditional framework assumes when it permits soldiers to fight enemy combatants regardless of those combatants' personal guilt. The traditional rule gets the right answer even if for slightly imprecise reasons.

On the combatant/non-combatant distinction in practice

Argument from the practical combatant distinctionModern warfare has complicated the traditional combatant/non-combatant line — with embedded civilians, dual-use infrastructure, and contractors. But these complications are arguments for refining the traditional framework, not replacing it. The central moral insight — that those who pose an immediate armed threat are in a different moral category from those who do not — is sound. The complications at the margins do not defeat the principle at the core. And the traditional framework provides clearer guidance in the typical case than any revisionist alternative so far proposed.

On the burdens revisionism places on combatants

Argument from epistemic burdenRevisionist just war theory — at its most demanding — requires each soldier to assess, before each act of lethal force, whether her cause is just, whether her target is liable, and whether the harm is proportionate in ways that track individual moral responsibility. These are not calculations any soldier in the field can actually perform. A theory of warfare that sets epistemic demands that no actual combatant can meet is not a theory that can guide action. Traditional JWT's more tractable rules — do not target civilians, respect combatant status, observe proportionality at the operational level — are action-guiding in a way revisionist theory simply cannot match.

Revisionism diagnoses real problems. Traditional JWT, properly understood, has always had the resources to address them. The framework stands.

Thank you.

Mr President,

The traditional theory of just war, as articulated by Professor Walzer, rests on what I have argued is a fundamental moral error: the doctrine of the moral equality of combatants. My opposition today is not to the idea that war can be regulated by moral norms. It is to the particular norms that the traditional theory proposes, and to the philosophical foundations on which they rest.

On the moral equality of combatants — the central mistake

Argument against moral equality of combatantsThe doctrine of the moral equality of combatants holds that soldiers on both sides of a war — just and unjust alike — are morally permitted to kill enemy combatants. This doctrine is false. It is false for the same reason that it would be false to say that an aggressor and his victim are equally permitted to kill each other. The unjust combatant — the soldier fighting in an aggressive or otherwise unjust war — lacks the moral justification that the just combatant possesses. He is threatening to kill people who have no valid reason to be killed. He is, in the most fundamental sense, doing wrong. The mere fact that he acts under orders, or acts with subjective good faith, does not transform his objectively wrongful threatening into a morally permitted act.

On liability to defensive killing

Argument from individual moral liabilityThe correct account of the permission to kill in war is grounded in individual moral liability. A person becomes liable to be killed in a defensive context when, and because, they are morally responsible for an unjust threat to another. The unjust combatant who fights in an aggressive war is responsible for an unjust threat to the soldiers and civilians on the other side. He is therefore liable to be killed in defence against that threat. The just combatant who fights a defensive war is not responsible for any unjust threat. He is therefore not liable to be killed. This asymmetry of liability is the moral truth that the traditional theory obscures with its false equality.

On the response from coercion and ignorance

Argument that coercion and ignorance affect culpability, not liabilityThe proposition will say: but soldiers fight under coercion; they lack information about the justice of their cause. I accept this. But notice what it shows. Coercion and ignorance are excusing conditions — they reduce or eliminate the culpability of the unjust combatant. They do not establish his moral permission to kill. A driver who runs someone over through no fault of her own is not culpable. But the victim is still permitted to leap out of the way. The unjust combatant who fights through genuine ignorance may not be blameworthy. But just combatants are still permitted to resist him, and he remains in a moral situation fundamentally different from theirs.

On the practical objection — Walzer's response

Argument that moral truth and practical guidance can divergeProfessor Walzer argues that moral equality of combatants is necessary for the practical regulation of warfare — that international humanitarian law cannot function on any other basis. But this confuses the question of moral truth with the question of what rules we should publicly promulgate. I accept that soldiers in the field cannot reliably assess the justice of their cause. I accept that publicly endorsing a rule that allows individual liability assessments would be chaotic. But the fact that we should publicly promulgate rules different from the underlying moral truth does not mean the underlying moral truth is the equality doctrine. The laws of war can be justified instrumentally, as the best rules for practice, without claiming that they track moral permission accurately.

On non-combatant immunity — the revisionist account

Argument from revisionist non-combatant immunityThe traditional account of non-combatant immunity rests on the simple category of civilian status. My account rests on individual liability. Those who bear no moral responsibility for an unjust threat are not liable to be killed. This is a more principled account of immunity because it explains why civilians are immune — they have done nothing to make themselves liable — rather than merely stipulating the category. And it correctly identifies that some apparent non-combatants — those who directly and knowingly contribute to unjust lethal threats — may in principle forfeit some of their immunity. The traditional theory cannot account for this without abandoning its own foundations.

Traditional just war theory is morally comfortable. It tells soldiers they can fight without examining their consciences too closely. But comfort is not truth. The moral truth is harder, and we should face it.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to push the critique of traditional just war theory further than Professor McMahan — not only challenging the moral equality of combatants, but challenging the entire state-centric framework within which traditional JWT operates. Just war theory, on the traditional view, is fundamentally about the rights and wrongs of states. I argue it should be fundamentally about the rights and wrongs of individuals.

On the cosmopolitan foundation of just war theory

Argument from cosmopolitan foundationsTraditional just war theory, in Walzer's legalist paradigm, assigns states the same rights against interference that individuals have against assault. But this analogy is defective. States are not persons. They do not independently hold rights. Whatever rights and interests attach to political communities derive ultimately from the rights and interests of the individual human beings who compose them. A just war theory that takes states as its fundamental units, rather than persons, has inverted the proper order of moral priority. The moral significance of borders, sovereignty, and political communities must be grounded in, and constrained by, the individual rights of citizens and non-citizens alike.

On subsistence wars and resource conflicts

Argument from subsistence rights and resource warsOne of my central contributions to the just war debate has been to ask: can individuals or communities wage war to secure the resources they need for subsistence — for bare survival? Traditional just war theory has no good answer to this question. Its just causes are almost entirely cast in terms of political sovereignty and territorial integrity. But if individuals have fundamental rights to the resources they need to survive, and if those resources are unjustly withheld or controlled by others, then war in defence of subsistence — a war fought not for territory but for food, water, or the economic conditions of life — may be justified. The traditional framework, by privileging state interests over individual welfare, cannot account for this possibility.

On the state-centric account of legitimate authority

Argument against state-centric legitimate authorityTraditional JWT requires that war be authorised by a legitimate political authority — typically, the state. This requirement embeds a fundamentally statist assumption: that only states can wage just wars. But why should this be so? If the moral foundations of just war are individual rights — as cosmopolitanism holds — then the requirement of state authority is a contingent institutional rule, not a deep moral truth. Non-state actors who fight to defend individual rights against state oppression or predation may satisfy all the substantive conditions of just war — just cause, proportionality, discrimination — without possessing state authority. To deny them the moral permission to fight is to subordinate individual rights to institutional form.

On the moral status of economic and structural violence

Argument from structural and economic violenceTraditional just war theory restricts its attention to direct, kinetic violence — the use of armed force. But this restriction ignores the ways in which economic coercion, unjust trade regimes, and structural inequalities between wealthy and poor states can constitute grave threats to individual welfare — threats as serious, for those who face them, as military attack. A just war framework that is silent on these forms of structural violence, while loudly proclaiming the impermissibility of border crossing, has got its priorities wrong. Individuals whose survival is threatened by economic exploitation have claims that deserve moral recognition within a theory of just conflict.

On the rights of irregular combatants and resistance fighters

Argument from irregular combatants and resistanceTraditional just war theory has significant difficulty accommodating resistance fighters, partisans, and non-state armed groups. Its framework of legitimate authority was developed with symmetric interstate war in mind. When colonial populations resist occupation, when ethnic minorities resist genocide, when non-state communities fight against predatory states, traditional JWT has very little to say about whether and how they may fight. A cosmopolitan revisionist framework — which grounds the permission to fight in the defence of individual rights, not in state authorisation — can explain why resistance fighters may have just cause and moral permission even without state backing. This is not a marginal case. It is the central political reality of most armed conflicts since 1945.

Traditional just war theory was built for a world of sovereign states in symmetric conflict. That world is gone. The theory must be rebuilt from the ground up, on individual rights, if it is to do justice to the conflicts of our time.

Thank you.
