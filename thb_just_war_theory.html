Mr President,

Let me be precise about what traditional just war theory is, because the opposition will caricature it. It is not a relic of scholastic theology. It is the moral framework that underlies international humanitarian law, that has restrained the worst excesses of armed conflict for centuries, and that provides the only politically realisable account of when violence between communities is permitted.

Jus ad bellum: just cause · right intention · proportionality · last resort · legitimate authority · reasonable prospect of success
Jus in bello: discrimination (non-combatant immunity) · proportionality · military necessity
Moral equality of combatants: soldiers on both sides fight under identical in bello constraints
Legalist paradigm: aggression against state sovereignty is the paradigm wrong; defence is the paradigm justification On the legalist paradigm

Legalist paradigm and state sovereigntyThe foundational insight of traditional just war theory is what I call the legalist paradigm: states possess rights of territorial integrity and political sovereignty analogous to the rights of persons to life and liberty. Aggression against a state is the international analogue of assault and murder — the paradigm crime in international affairs, and defence against it is the paradigm justification for war. This is not mere legal positivism. It reflects the deep moral truth that political communities are the primary vehicles through which human beings live collective lives of meaning, build institutions, and realise shared goods. To dissolve state rights entirely into individual rights, as the revisionists do, is to mistake the derivative for the fundamental.

On the moral equality of combatants

Moral equality of combatantsThe moral equality of combatants is the most practically indispensable principle in the tradition. Soldiers who fight in accordance with the laws of war are morally permitted to kill enemy combatants, regardless of whether their war is just or unjust. This is not a philosophical error. Soldiers fight under coercion with radically incomplete information about the justice of their cause. Holding each soldier individually liable for the justice of his war would make regulation of armed conflict entirely impossible. McMahan's revisionism is philosophically elegant and operationally catastrophic. The laws of armed conflict have achieved something remarkable: a shared framework within which all parties can operate. Do not dismantle it.

On non-combatant immunity

Non-combatant immunityNon-combatant immunity is the cornerstone of jus in bello. The civilian population may not be directly targeted. This prohibition does not rest on the revisionist's liability calculus. It rests on the morally fundamental distinction between those engaged in the armed prosecution of war and those who are not. The farmer, the nurse, the schoolteacher — they have done nothing that could justify being made the object of lethal force. The revisionist attempt to ground immunity in individual liability introduces precisely the kind of qualification that has historically been exploited to target civilians. A simple, robust categorical rule is both more honest and more protective.

On supreme emergency

Supreme emergency doctrineTraditional just war theory is not naive about moral tragedy. The doctrine of supreme emergency acknowledges that when a political community faces genuinely existential threat — when the enemy threatens to eliminate the conditions of political life itself — ordinary moral constraints may be temporarily overridden. I am not proud of this doctrine. It acknowledges real moral cost. But it is honest about the tragic choices that real warfare imposes, where the opposition's fine-grained liability calculus is merely evasive.

On why revisionism fails in practice

Against revisionism in practiceRevisionism demands that each soldier, before each lethal act, assess the justice of her cause, the individual liability of her target, and the proportionality of harm in terms of individual moral responsibility. These calculations cannot be made in the field, under fire, with the information available to combatants. A theory of war that cannot guide combatants in the conditions of actual warfare is not a theory of war at all. It is moral philosophy produced for seminar rooms. Traditional just war theory is action-guiding because it is honest about what war is and what soldiers can know.

The framework stands. It has endured because it is morally serious, practically workable, and honest about the horror it attempts to regulate.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I approach this question not as a philosopher but as someone who has had to make decisions about the use of force in the real world. Philosophy is indispensable for clarifying principles. But principles that cannot be translated into the rough materials of international life are not principles; they are aspirations. Traditional just war theory has the great virtue of being grounded in the world as it actually is.

On the Westphalian order as moral achievement

Westphalian order and legitimate international systemThe international order established at Westphalia in 1648 was not merely a legal convention. It was a hard-won moral achievement — the recognition that the wars of religion, which had devastated Europe for a generation, could not be resolved by one party imposing its values on another. State sovereignty and non-interference were not power-politics cynicism. They were the institutional expression of the insight that a world of diverse communities requires shared rules of coexistence, not a single universal moral standard imposed by whoever happens to be strong enough to enforce it. Traditional just war theory, with its insistence on legitimate authority and the primacy of state sovereignty, embodies this insight.

On the limits of idealist intervention

Against idealist humanitarian interventionThe great danger of revisionist just war theory — and of its political cousin, liberal interventionism — is that it provides sophisticated moral cover for the exercise of power by the strong against the weak under the banner of universal values. When powerful states claim the right to intervene because their philosophers have calculated that individual rights are being violated, they are almost invariably advancing national interests dressed in moral language. The history of humanitarian intervention is not a history of neutral moral enforcement. It is a history of great-power politics conducted with a good conscience. Traditional just war theory, by insisting on strict criteria for legitimate authority and just cause, provides a check on this tendency.

On stability as a moral value

Stability and order as moral valuesOrder and stability are not merely prudential values. They are moral values. The absence of international order is itself a grave moral evil. Any system of world order, to be sustainable, must be accepted as legitimate by the major parties to it. Traditional just war theory, by building on the existing framework of state sovereignty and the laws of armed conflict, works with the grain of international legitimacy rather than against it. Revisionist theory, which condemns the moral equality of combatants and the existing laws of war as mere useful fictions, works against this legitimacy without offering a realistic alternative.

On moral realism in statecraft

Moral realism and achievable normsThe statesman who takes decisions about war and peace cannot operate on the assumption that moral principles will be universally observed. She must ask: given that the other side may not share my values, given that my capacity to enforce moral norms is limited, given that the costs of getting this wrong will be borne by real people — what rules of engagement can actually be sustained? Traditional just war theory has been refined over centuries precisely because it grapples with this question. Its rules are not ideal; they are achievable. They represent what can actually be demanded of parties to an armed conflict. This is not moral compromise. It is moral realism.

On the dangers of moral perfectionism in war

Against moral perfectionism in warThe revisionist programme is a form of moral perfectionism. It demands that we assess war by the standards of ideal moral theory and hold combatants liable by the standards of individual moral responsibility. But perfectionism in war is not merely impractical. It is dangerous. It tends to license the conclusion that ordinary constraints may be set aside when the cause is pure enough and the philosophical argument sound enough. History should make us deeply suspicious of those who use sophisticated moral reasoning to justify setting aside the rules that protect the innocent.

International order is a fragile achievement. The framework that sustains it is not perfect. But it has kept the most destructive impulses of states partially in check. Let us not dismantle it in the name of philosophical rigour.

Mr President,

I occupy an unusual position in this debate. I have spent much of my career demonstrating that the revisionist critique of Walzer's philosophical foundations is substantially correct. McMahan is right that the moral equality of combatants cannot be grounded in a simple status-based rule. Fabre is right that individual rights must ultimately ground our account of when war is justified. Frowe is right that self-defence theory needs to be rigorously applied. And yet I defend the broad conclusions of traditional just war theory, because I think the revisionist inference from true philosophical premises to radical practical conclusions does not follow.

On vindicating non-combatant immunity from first principles

Sparing Civilians: Moral Distinction from first principlesIn my book Sparing Civilians, I defended what I call the Moral Distinction: in war, with rare exceptions, killing non-combatants is worse than killing combatants. I did not ground this on simple status. I grounded it in a convergence of independent moral considerations: necessity — killing combatants is typically more directly connected to winning the war; the opportunistic versus eliminative nature of killing — targeting civilians treats them as obstacles rather than threats; and vulnerability — civilians lack the means of resistance that combatants possess. These independent grounds converge on a robust moral distinction. Traditional JWT gets the right answer, even if the revisionists correctly identify that Walzer's own grounds are insufficient.

On why reductionism fails

Against reductionism; war has its own moral logicThe revisionist programme is built on reductionism: the view that the ethics of killing in war reduces entirely to interpersonal self-defence and defence of others. I have argued that reductionism fails. The scale of war, the institutional context, the associative bonds between soldiers and those they defend, and the political goods at stake make war a distinctive moral domain that cannot be captured by aggregating interpersonal defensive permissions. Non-reductionism — the view that war has its own moral logic — is the correct framework, and it vindicates something much closer to traditional JWT than revisionism allows.

On a modicum of statism

Defending a modicum of statismI have argued for what I call a modicum of statism: not the full Walzerian legalist paradigm, but the recognition that states — and especially democratic states — play a morally significant role in authorising the resort to war that non-state actors typically cannot replicate. Democratic states are better able to secure the genuine authorisation of their citizens for collective violence, and this authorisation matters morally. Revisionism treats state authority as merely instrumentally valuable; I argue it has a deeper moral significance that the traditional framework correctly, if imperfectly, recognises.

On the revisionist critique and its limits

Revisionism exposes foundations but cannot justify its conclusionsThe revisionists have correctly exposed that Walzer's philosophical foundations are insufficient. But the radical conclusions they draw — that unjust combatants are not morally permitted to kill, that liability tracks individual moral responsibility in McMahan's fine-grained way — do not follow from their true premises. The inference from "Walzer's argument fails" to "the conclusions Walzer defends fail" is invalid. We can accept the revisionist critique of the foundations while rejecting the revisionist alternative to the conclusions, and rebuilding those foundations more rigorously.

On the revisionist methodology

Ideal theory misapplied to non-ideal warMost fundamentally, revisionism is an exercise in ideal moral theory applied to a radically non-ideal domain. War is characterised by massive uncertainty, radical inequality of information, institutional constraints on individual choice, and enormous scale. A moral theory calibrated to the fine-grained individual liability assessments of ideal deliberative agents cannot govern war as it is actually fought. Traditional just war theory's rules are calibrated to actual conditions. That is not a philosophical defect. It is the right methodological approach to the ethics of a non-ideal domain.

Revisionism is philosophically rigorous and practically useless. Traditional just war theory — properly re-founded — is the framework we actually need.

Mr President,

Traditional just war theory rests on a foundational moral error: the doctrine of the moral equality of combatants. Everything else in the traditional framework — the clean in bello rules, the categorical prohibition on civilian targeting, the comfortable separation of jus ad bellum from jus in bello — is built on this mistake. My task is to expose that error and show that the correct alternative, however uncomfortable, is more morally honest.

On the moral equality of combatants — the central falsehood

Moral equality of combatants is falseThe doctrine of the moral equality of combatants holds that soldiers on both sides of any war — just and unjust alike — are equally permitted to kill enemy combatants, provided they comply with in bello rules. This doctrine is false. It is false for the same reason it would be false to say that a mugger and his victim are equally permitted to attack each other. The unjust combatant — the soldier fighting in a war of aggression — is threatening to kill people who have no valid reason to be killed. He is, in the most fundamental sense, doing wrong. The mere fact that he acts under orders, or acts in good faith, does not transform his objectively wrongful killing into a morally permitted act. Good faith is an excusing condition, not a justifying one.

On liability and the asymmetry of permission

Liability tracks individual moral responsibilityThe correct account of the permission to kill in war is grounded in individual moral liability. A person becomes liable to defensive killing when they are morally responsible for posing an unjust threat to another. Unjust combatants are responsible for unjust threats to just combatants and civilians; they are therefore liable to be killed in defence against those threats. Just combatants defending against unjust aggression are not responsible for unjust threats; they are not liable to be killed. This asymmetry of liability is the moral truth that the traditional doctrine of equality systematically obscures.

On the coercion and ignorance objection

Coercion reduces culpability, not liabilityMy opponents will say that unjust combatants fight under coercion, with incomplete information. I accept this. But notice what it shows. Coercion and ignorance are excusing conditions: they reduce or eliminate culpability. They do not establish that what is done is not wrong, nor that it is morally permitted. A driver who injures a pedestrian through no fault of her own is blameless. The pedestrian may still leap aside. Unjust combatants who fight through genuine ignorance may lack culpability while remaining liable to be killed by those they threaten. The traditional framework conflates culpability with liability, and thereby reaches a false conclusion.

On Lazar's non-reductionism

Against non-reductionismProfessor Lazar argues that war has a distinctive moral logic not captured by reductionism. But non-reductionism, as Lazar defends it, risks becoming a licence for moral exceptionalism in war — for treating the killing of unjust combatants as morally different from any other unjustified killing without adequate explanation. The burden of proof is on the non-reductionist to explain what feature of war could make it permissible for unjust combatants to kill those who are mounting justified resistance to their aggression. That explanation has not been provided.

On the practical objection

Moral truth vs. practical rules can divergeProfessor Kissinger argues that revisionism is practically disastrous, that soldiers cannot perform the liability assessments I require. I accept this as a point about institutional design. But it is not a point about moral truth. The moral truth is asymmetric: unjust combatants do not have permission to kill just ones. The fact that we should, for practical reasons, promulgate rules that do not require individual soldiers to assess this truth does not show that the truth is the equality doctrine. Rules of war can be instrumentally justified as the best available approximation without being morally accurate.

The moral truth about war is harder than traditional theory acknowledges. We should face it rather than construct comfortable fictions to avoid it.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to push further than Professor McMahan. Not only is the moral equality of combatants false, not only is the state-centric account of liability mistaken — the entire framework within which traditional just war theory operates is misconceived. Traditional JWT is built for states. The world contains billions of individuals. A moral theory of armed conflict adequate to those individuals must be built from individual rights up, not from state sovereignty down.

On the cosmopolitan foundation

Cosmopolitan individual-rights foundationThe legalist paradigm treats states as if they were persons with rights analogous to individual rights. But states are not persons. They are institutions. Whatever moral significance political communities possess derives ultimately from the rights and interests of the individual human beings who compose and interact with them. A theory of armed conflict that takes state sovereignty as foundational has inverted the proper order of moral priority. The correct foundation is individual human rights: rights to life, bodily integrity, the resources necessary for a minimally decent existence. These rights are held by persons, not by states, and they hold across borders without regard to citizenship.

On subsistence wars and the limits of just cause

Subsistence rights and resource warsTraditional JWT restricts just cause almost entirely to the defence of territorial sovereignty and political integrity. But if individuals hold fundamental rights to the resources necessary for subsistence — food, water, the economic conditions of minimally decent life — then war in defence of those resources may be justified when they are unjustly withheld. I have argued for the possibility of subsistence wars: armed conflicts waged not to defend borders but to secure the individual rights of populations denied basic resources. Traditional JWT has no account of this possibility. Its state-centric conception of just cause is too narrow to capture the full range of morally significant grounds for conflict.

On legitimate authority and non-state actors

Non-state actors and legitimate authorityTraditional JWT insists that only legitimate political authority — paradigmatically, the state — may wage just war. This requirement has no adequate justification from the standpoint of individual rights. If the moral foundations of just war are individual rights, then the requirement of state authority is a contingent institutional rule, not a deep moral constraint. Non-state actors who fight in defence of individual rights against state oppression may satisfy all the substantive conditions of just war — just cause, proportionality, discrimination — without possessing state authorisation. Colonial liberation movements, minorities resisting genocide, populations defending subsistence rights — these actors are not morally precluded from waging just war merely because they lack a state behind them.

On the moral status of civilian contribution to war

Civilian contribution and conditional immunityTraditional JWT grants categorical immunity to all civilians. I argue this is too broad. Individuals who knowingly and significantly contribute to an unjust war effort — weapons designers who create systems used only for targeting civilians, propagandists who sustain public support for aggressive war — bear some moral responsibility for the unjust threats that result. This does not mean they may be directly targeted without further constraint. But it does mean their immunity is conditional rather than categorical, grounded in their lack of contribution to unjust threats rather than in civilian status alone.

On the Westphalian order and its victims

The Westphalian order and its excludedProfessor Kissinger presents the Westphalian order as a moral achievement. From the perspective of those outside the club of great powers, it was a system for the partition of the world between European imperial states, each sovereign over its territory and its colonial possessions alike. The principle of non-interference Kissinger celebrates was routinely applied to protect European states from each other while denying its protections to colonised peoples. A just war theory built on this order inherits its exclusions. The cosmopolitan alternative, built on individual rights, provides the framework that actually protects those the Westphalian order left unprotected.

Traditional just war theory was designed by and for the powerful states of the European order. The individuals it leaves outside deserve a moral framework that sees them too.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to make a focused argument about the moral foundations of the permission to kill in war, because this is where traditional just war theory is most fundamentally mistaken. My work on defensive ethics — on when and why it is permissible to kill in self-defence and the defence of others — leads me to conclusions that are irreconcilable with the traditional framework, and I want to explain precisely why.

On the permission to kill in war and self-defence theory

Self-defence as foundation of permissible killing in warThe moral permission to kill — in war as in ordinary life — must be grounded in something. The most plausible ground is defensive necessity: I may kill you insofar as it is necessary to avert an unjust lethal threat you pose to me or to others I am defending. When I apply this self-defence framework rigorously to war, the traditional picture dissolves. The unjust combatant who is advancing an unjust cause poses an unjust threat. The just combatant resisting him does not. Their moral situation is asymmetric, and the permission to kill reflects that asymmetry. Traditional JWT, by granting both sides equal permission, has suppressed this asymmetry with a legal fiction.

On the combatant permission and its limits

Status cannot ground the combatant permissionTraditional JWT grants combatants a permission to kill enemy combatants simply in virtue of their combatant status, provided they comply with in bello rules. I have argued that this status-based permission has no adequate moral grounding. The moral permission to kill is determined by what the agent is doing — whether she is posing an unjust threat — not by what she is. Combatant status is a proxy for the relevant doing at best; it cannot confer a permission to kill in cases where the doing itself is impermissible. The unjust combatant's status does not make her killing of just combatants permissible.

On proportionality and non-combatant lives

Rights-based proportionality is more protectiveTraditional JWT's account of proportionality in bello compares military advantage against collateral civilian harm, asking whether the advantage is proportionate to the harm. But this framing already concedes too much: it treats civilian deaths as costs to be weighed against military gains, rather than as violations of individual rights that stand as near-absolute constraints. A rights-based approach to proportionality — one that takes seriously each non-combatant's right not to be instrumentally harmed — generates significantly tighter constraints on collateral damage than traditional JWT provides.

On individual versus class-based immunity

Immunity is individual, not class-basedTraditional JWT treats non-combatant immunity as a categorical rule applying to a class of persons defined by non-participation in hostilities. I argue that immunity must be assessed at the individual level. A person's immunity from lethal targeting is determined by her particular moral situation — what she has done, what threat she poses, what responsibility she bears — not by membership of a class. Individual-level analysis is not a licence for targeting civilians; it is a more rigorous account of why and to what degree each individual is protected, and it generates a more principled basis for hard cases at the margins of the combatant/non-combatant distinction.

On what traditional JWT cannot explain

Traditional JWT's explanatory failureThe deepest problem with traditional just war theory is not its practical rules — which, as Professor Lazar argues, often produce the right answers — but its inability to explain why those rules hold. Why may combatants be killed and non-combatants not? Traditional JWT answers: because they are combatants or not. But that is a label, not an explanation. A genuinely adequate theory must explain what feature of the combatant's situation makes her liable to be killed, and what feature of the non-combatant's situation protects her. Only a revisionist theory, grounded in individual liability and defensive ethics, can provide that explanation. And it is when rules face hard cases — irregular combatants, human shields, dual-use targets — that the underlying theory determines the answer.

We can endorse most of the practical conclusions of traditional JWT while rejecting its theoretical foundations. And rejecting those foundations matters, because the theory determines the answers in the hard cases that actually arise.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleagues have addressed the moral equality of combatants, the cosmopolitan critique, and the foundations of the defensive permission to kill. I want to focus on a criterion that has received less critical attention: the requirement of legitimate authority. Traditional just war theory insists that only legitimate political authorities — paradigmatically, states — may wage just war. I want to argue that this criterion is both more important and more defective than the traditional account recognises.

On the legitimate authority criterion and its standard interpretation

Standard interpretation of legitimate authorityThe traditional account treats legitimate authority as a necessary condition for the just resort to war: only a sovereign state, or something sufficiently analogous, may permissibly initiate armed conflict. This criterion is standardly interpreted as a threshold condition for jus ad bellum, having no bearing on how war is conducted. I want to challenge both aspects of this interpretation. The legitimate authority criterion plays a more fundamental role than the threshold account acknowledges, and its significance extends beyond the decision to go to war into the conduct of war itself.

On authority and conduct in war

Authority criterion extends to in bello conductI have argued for a revised interpretation of the authority criterion: that legitimate authority is relevant not only to the just resort to war but to the permissibility of particular acts within war. The question of who authorises a specific act of lethal force — what chain of command, what institutional mandate, what democratic or legal sanction lies behind it — affects the moral status of that act. This has significant implications for targeted killings, drone strikes, and operations by private military contractors who lack the authorisation of any legitimate political authority. Traditional JWT, confining authority to the ad bellum decision, misses this dimension entirely.

On irregular belligerency and the authority challenge

Irregular combatants and the authority criterionContemporary armed conflict is dominated by irregular belligerency: non-state actors, insurgencies, resistance movements, and hybrid organisations that are neither clearly state forces nor clearly civilian. Traditional JWT was built for symmetric interstate war, and its account of legitimate authority — requiring something like sovereign political authority — has no adequate answer to the question of whether and when irregular combatants may permissibly fight. My revised account — grounding authority in the genuine mandate of the community on whose behalf a belligerent claims to fight — can assess irregular combatants in a principled way that traditional JWT cannot.

On non-state resistance and genuine authorisation

Non-state resistance and genuine mandateConsider partisan resistance fighters, independence movements, or communities defending themselves against state predation. Traditional JWT, by insisting on sovereign state authority, struggles to grant these actors the permission to fight that they clearly have on any adequate moral account. A revised account that grounds authority in genuine community mandate — in something like the authentic representation of a group's shared interests in the use of force — can accommodate these actors, while preserving the insight that organised, accountable force is morally different from individual or uncoordinated violence.

On the inconsistency of the authority requirement

Traditional JWT's authority requirement inconsistently appliedTraditional JWT's state-authority requirement has been applied inconsistently. It denied moral recognition to colonial liberation struggles while granting the full moral framework of just war to colonial powers with no greater claim to legitimate authority over the territories they controlled. A criterion that functions primarily to privilege the armed forces of established states over those who challenge them — however legitimate their grievances — is a conservative political principle dressed in moral language. The revisionist critique of the authority criterion, and my own revision of it, attempt to replace this conservatism with a more principled account.

Traditional just war theory offers a framework built for a world of sovereign states fighting each other. That world never fully existed, and it certainly does not describe the armed conflicts of our time.

Thank you.
