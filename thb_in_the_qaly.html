Mr President,

I will concede, at the outset, more than the opposition expects. The QALY is an imperfect instrument. Its utility weights are elicited by imperfect methods from imperfect respondents. It combines years and quality by a multiplication that involves contestable assumptions. Its use in any threshold-based decision rule involves moral choices that cannot themselves be resolved by health economics alone. All of this I freely grant.

And then I ask the opposition a single question: compared to what? Because this debate is not between the QALY and some superior instrument of perfect clarity. It is between the QALY and the alternatives that actually exist — political allocation, disease-specific advocacy, clinician discretion, historical precedent, pharmaceutical lobbying, and the unchecked market.

"All resource allocation decisions in healthcare involve implicit judgements about whose health matters, by how much, and at what cost. The QALY makes those judgements explicit, transparent, and challengeable. The alternative is not to avoid those judgements — it is to make them in secret, driven by whoever shouts loudest."
i. the inescapability of comparison

Opportunity cost & transparencyThe NHS spends over £180 billion annually. That sum is not unlimited. Every pound spent on one treatment is a pound not spent on another. Some principle of comparison is logically unavoidable. Without a structured measure like the QALY, allocation reverts to mechanisms demonstrably worse on any serious metric of fairness: politically salient diseases attract resources disproportionate to their burden; rare diseases with powerful advocacy groups crowd out more cost-effective interventions; pharmaceutical marketing shapes clinical practice for commercial rather than patient benefit. Against this reality, the QALY is not merely defensible — it is a significant moral advance.

ii. the fair innings argument

Fair innings — intergenerational equityThe opposition argues the QALY discriminates against the elderly. I want to turn this on its head. Is it fair that a 70-year-old and a 7-year-old receive equal priority when each gains the same QALYs? Most people's intuition is that the child's claim is stronger — not because the elderly person's life has less intrinsic value, but because the child has had less of a life. This is what I have called the "fair innings" argument. There is a morally relevant sense in which a person who has lived to 70 has had more of what life has to offer than one who dies at 7. Priority weighting reflecting this is not ageism — it is intergenerational fairness. Norman Daniels, using Rawlsian methodology, reaches an identical conclusion from behind the veil of ignorance.

iii. transparency and democratic accountability

Transparency & accountabilityThe QALY's most underrated virtue is precisely its transparency. When NICE uses a cost-per-QALY threshold to refuse a drug, the decision is visible, documented, and challengeable. Clinicians, patients, and researchers can scrutinise the evidence, contest the utility weights, challenge the modelling assumptions, and — through the formal appeals process — have the decision reversed. This has happened repeatedly: end-of-life weightings, orphan disease exceptions, and severity adjustments have all been incorporated into NICE methodology in response to reasoned critique. Contrast this with clinical discretion, which is invisible and unchallengeable.

iv. the disability objection — a distinction the opposition elides

Measurement vs. allocation — disabilityThe disability discrimination objection conflates two distinct QALY uses. First: evaluating cost-effectiveness of a treatment for a given condition. Second: prioritising between patients. The discrimination concern arises primarily in the second use. But the first — which constitutes the vast majority of NICE's work — does not implicate discrimination at all. Whether a new drug produces 0.8 more QALYs than standard care for rheumatoid arthritis patients is a question purely about incremental effectiveness within a defined group. The disability paradox critique is largely irrelevant here. NICE's 2022 severity modifier, end-of-life weighting, and distributional cost-effectiveness analysis address the second use. The framework is responsive; the opposition attacks its worst version.

v. the US counter-example

Comparative systems — US without QALYsThe US Inflation Reduction Act legislatively prohibits QALY-based Medicare evaluation. The result is not better healthcare for disabled patients. It is higher drug prices, no systematic negotiating mechanism, and spending twice the UK's per capita with materially worse population health outcomes. Spending is concentrated in the final months of life for high-income patients; preventive care for the poor is chronically underfunded. The QALY-free world the opposition implicitly defends already exists — and it is called the American healthcare system. That is not a recommendation.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleague Professor Williams has defended the QALY on its own terms. I want to go further: to argue that the QALY's most serious imperfections point not toward its abandonment but toward its natural successor — the Wellbeing-Adjusted Life Year, or WELLBY — and that the WELLBY answers, with considerable elegance, the most serious philosophical objections the opposition has raised.

"The QALY asks: how healthy was this year? The WELLBY asks: how good was this year? Those are not the same question. And the difference between them is not technical — it is a difference about what we fundamentally owe each other as members of a political community."
i. what the wellby is

WELLBY definition & structureThe WELLBY, formalised by Layard, De Neve, O'Donnell and colleagues in a 2020 BMJ paper, is a one-point change in life satisfaction on a 0–10 Likert scale, sustained for one year, for one individual. Where the QALY multiplies life-years by a utility weight derived from population health-state preferences, the WELLBY captures individuals' own global assessments of how their lives are going — integrating health, employment, relationships, housing, autonomy, meaning, and social connection in a single self-reported number. HM Treasury has assigned a value of £13,000 per WELLBY (2019 prices), anchored by pegging to the QALY: since full health corresponds to approximately 8/10 on the life satisfaction scale, and indifference between life and death to approximately 1/10, one WELLBY = £70,000 ÷ 7 ≈ £10,000.

ii. the wellby dissolves the disability paradox

Disability paradox — WELLBY uses lived not imagined experienceThe opposition's most telling argument is that QALY utility weights are derived from general population surveys that systematically underestimate disabled people's actual quality of life. Under the WELLBY framework, this concern dissolves almost entirely. When a person with paraplegia rates their life satisfaction at 6.5/10, that self-report — not a non-disabled person's imagination of life at 6.5 — is the input to the measure. Albrecht and Devlieger's disability paradox research, far from being an argument against measurement, is the empirical motivation for precisely the kind of self-reported subjective wellbeing measure the WELLBY employs.

iii. the cross-sectoral advantage the qaly cannot provide

Cross-sectoral comparison — healthcare vs. housing, employmentThe QALY is structurally unable to compare a cancer drug with an employment programme, a surgical innovation with a housing intervention, or a pharmaceutical treatment with a community mental health service. The WELLBY provides the common currency that allows a government to ask — rigorously — whether £100m produces more wellbeing spent on antidepressants or job creation, hip replacements or loneliness reduction. The LSE's analysis of the British Household Panel Survey shows that the largest determinants of population life satisfaction are not medical: they are unemployment, relationship breakdown, mental illness, and physical pain. A policy framework that can only measure healthcare benefits will systematically underinvest in the welfare determinants that matter most.

iv. the empirical foundation is now robust

Cardinality — empirical tractabilityThe cardinality concern — whether a move from 6 to 7 means the same as a move from 8 to 9 — is empirically tractable rather than merely assumed. OECD guidelines note that treating life satisfaction as cardinal in regression analysis produces results consistent with non-parametric approaches. The Happier Lives Institute's review concludes that interpersonal cardinal comparisons are supported by the linguistic and psychological structure of the data. Large longitudinal datasets — BHPS, German SOEP, Australian HILDA — allow identification of causal wellbeing impacts with rigour comparable to clinical trial evidence. The WELLBY's measurement problems are correctable through improving methodology — not through abandoning the framework.

Thank you.

∴The WELLBY is a defensible, institutionally grounded successor to the QALY that retains its advantages while addressing its most serious philosophical limitations.

2.The WELLBY provides a common welfare currency enabling comparison across housing, employment, mental health, and clinical care.

Mr President,

The proposition's defence of the QALY is a pragmatist one: not that it is philosophically sound, but that it is pragmatically necessary. I want to challenge both the pragmatism and the philosophy, because the QALY is not merely imperfect — it is built, at its foundation, on a measurement of human welfare that is systematically and demonstrably wrong about what welfare is.

"The QALY does not merely measure health. It embeds a specific, contested philosophical claim: that life with disability or chronic illness is worth less than life without. That claim is not a health-scientific finding. It is a value judgement — and one that the empirical evidence about disabled people's actual wellbeing substantially contradicts."
i. the disability paradox — a fundamental measurement failure

Disability paradox — imagined vs. lived welfareThe QALY's quality weight is derived from general population surveys, not from the reported wellbeing of people actually living in the health states being valued. Albrecht and Devlieger coined the term "disability paradox" in 1999 to describe a well-documented phenomenon: people who have not experienced a disability consistently rate its quality of life significantly lower than people who actually live with it. The EQ-5D anchors disability as a reduced-value state made by people who systematically overpredict their misery — in ways that decades of affective forecasting research document. Using these values to make public policy decisions about who gets healthcare is not a measurement imprecision. It is a structural bias embedded in the framework's foundations.

ii. laundered value judgements

Hidden philosophical premises — laundered as scienceThe QALY presents itself as a neutral scientific instrument. It is neither. When NICE uses a cost-per-QALY threshold, it implicitly endorses: (a) a utilitarian aggregation procedure treating health as summable across individuals; (b) a theory of wellbeing equating preference satisfaction with welfare; (c) a commensurability claim — that health states at 0.5 utility are exactly twice as good as those at 0.25 — that philosophers of wellbeing regard as deeply problematic; and (d) a discount rate for future health states embedding contested time-preference assumptions. None of these philosophical commitments is self-evidently correct. They are encoded so deeply in the QALY's technical apparatus that they escape democratic scrutiny entirely — this is not transparency, it is the laundering of contested value choices through the appearance of scientific neutrality.

iii. the CRPD incompatibility

CRPD — international human rights obligationsThe UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, ratified by the UK in 2009, requires that healthcare be provided to disabled persons on an equal basis with others. The CRPD Committee has interpreted this to prohibit differential valuation of disabled people's lives in resource allocation. The CRPD embodies the social model of disability: reduced quality of life associated with disability is not intrinsic to the condition but is produced by inaccessible environments, inadequate support, and social stigma. Using QALY weights reflecting socially-produced disadvantage to determine healthcare access compounds that disadvantage by treating its effects as a fixed, legitimate constraint on entitlement. The motion asks if the QALY is "acceptable" for public policy in a state that has ratified obligations to provide equal healthcare access for disabled people. It is not.

iv. palliative care — the measure's acknowledged failure

Palliative care — structural incommensurabilityExpert consensus — the QALY-time study (2020) — is that the QALY "in its current shape" is unsuitable for palliative care. Palliative care does not extend life, by design, and frequently cannot improve EQ-5D utility scores. Its value lies in dignity, pain management, family support, and the quality of dying — dimensions that standard QALY instruments are not designed to measure. Similarly, mental illness accounts for approximately 23% of the UK disease burden, yet QALY measurement maps poorly onto the relational, functional, and existential dimensions of mental health recovery. An "acceptable measure" that expert consensus finds structurally inadequate for all dying patients and partially inadequate for a quarter of the disease burden is not an acceptable measure.

Mr President,

My contribution to this debate begins not with health economics but with what I believe is the most fundamental error embedded in QALY-based reasoning: the aggregation of benefits across persons as though persons were not separate, as though a sufficient mass of smaller benefits could always justify failing to prevent a great harm to a single individual. I want to argue that this error is not peripheral to the QALY framework — it is its structural core.

"It is not enough that the sum of benefits is large. What matters is that each person's situation is considered on its own terms. There is something deeply wrong with a framework that would deny a life-saving treatment to a severely ill patient because the same resources could produce more aggregate QALY gains distributed across many people with minor conditions."
i. the separateness of persons

Separateness of persons — Parfitian objection to aggregationThe utilitarian tradition — within which QALY maximisation sits — treats the social welfare function as the sum of individual utilities. But this sum treats persons as mere receptacles for utility, as though what matters is the total amount of good in the world rather than who receives it. I argued in Reasons and Persons that this is the deepest error of utilitarianism: it fails to take seriously the fact that persons are distinct — that my suffering cannot be compensated by your joy. A QALY lost by a dying patient is not the same kind of thing as the accumulation of small quality-of-life improvements across a thousand relatively healthy people, even if the arithmetic is equal. The separateness of persons demands a framework that takes seriously the claims of each individual, not merely the aggregate across all individuals.

ii. the aggregation problem in healthcare

Aggregation problem — small benefits to many vs. urgent needs of fewConsider two policies. Policy A prevents mild, temporary discomfort for one million people. Policy B prevents death for 100 severely ill patients. If the QALY sums favour Policy A, QALY maximisation selects it. But this result strikes virtually everyone as morally monstrous — and Scanlon's contractualism explains why: no individual who would benefit from Policy B could reasonably be asked to accept the priority given to Policy A's minor benefits. The separateness of persons means that the strength of individual claims matters, not merely the aggregate. Frances Kamm's detailed case analysis confirms that moral intuitions consistently resist this aggregation: there is a threshold below which the smallness of a benefit removes it from relevance in competition with a serious claim, regardless of the number of beneficiaries.

iii. the non-identity between QALY gains

QALY non-identity — QALYs are not fungible across personsQALY maximisation treats all QALYs as fungible — a QALY gained by one person is exactly equivalent to a QALY gained by another. But this fungibility assumption is deeply problematic. Consider: a QALY gained by restoring full health to a 30-year-old who was severely ill is not the same kind of thing as a QALY gained by marginally improving the quality of life of ten people with mild chronic conditions, even if the arithmetic is identical. The first represents the restoration of a person's capacity to live a full life; the second represents marginal enhancements to already tolerable lives. These are morally non-equivalent, and a framework that treats them as equivalent has already committed the error of treating persons as receptacles for a fungible commodity rather than as beings with distinct lives and projects.

iv. the orphan disease problem

Orphan diseases — systematic failure for small populationsQALY-based frameworks systematically fail patients with rare diseases — not because those diseases are less important, but because the QALY denominator (total population QALY gain) is small for small patient populations. Huntington's disease, spinal muscular atrophy, Pompe disease — treatments for these conditions routinely fail cost-per-QALY assessments. When countries fund these treatments anyway — as they do — they are implicitly acknowledging that the framework fails to capture something morally crucial about the urgency and severity of individual health needs that cannot be dissolved into aggregate arithmetic. The QALY's failure here is not incidental; it is the aggregation error expressed in its most visible form.

Mr President,

My colleagues have attacked the QALY from welfare philosophy and from the separateness of persons. I want to attack it from a different direction: from the philosophy of value itself, and specifically from the question of whether health states are genuinely commensurable on a single cardinal scale — which is what the QALY requires, and which I believe cannot be adequately defended.

"The QALY assumes that dying at 45 in perfect health and living to 90 at 0.5 utility are exactly equivalent — both worth 45 QALYs. This commensurability claim is not a measurement finding. It is a philosophical assumption of the most ambitious kind, and it is one that I do not believe can be sustained."
i. the incommensurability of health states

Incommensurability — health states resist cardinal scalingThe QALY requires that health states be placed on a cardinal scale from 0 (death) to 1 (perfect health) such that arithmetic operations — multiplication, addition, comparison — are meaningful. But health states are not like heights or temperatures that can be measured on a natural scale. They are complex combinations of pain, function, cognition, emotion, and social participation that resist reduction to a single number. The commensurability assumption embedded in the QALY — that a life at 0.6 utility for ten years (6 QALYs) is exactly equivalent to a life at 1.0 utility for six years (6 QALYs) — is a philosophical claim of the most ambitious kind. There is no reason to believe it is true, and considerable reason to believe it cannot even be made precise.

ii. the utility measurement problem

Measurement problem — utility elicitation is philosophically flawedThe two principal methods for eliciting health-state utility weights — time trade-off (TTO) and standard gamble (SG) — measure different things and give systematically different results. TTO asks how many years of full health you would trade for a given period in a specified health state. SG asks what probability of death you would accept for a chance of full health. These different framings capture different psychological responses: TTO measures aversion to duration in poor health; SG measures risk attitudes. Neither is a neutral measurement of welfare. The systematic divergence between TTO and SG values for identical health states reveals that neither is measuring a single underlying welfare quantity — they are measuring different cognitive responses to different decision frames. The QALY's claim to be measuring "health-related quality of life" is thus not vindicated by either method.

iii. the problem of zero — death as a fixed point

Death as zero — the anchoring assumption failsThe QALY anchors its scale at 0 = death, 1 = perfect health. But death is not a health state — it is the absence of any state. Anchoring at death requires treating the end of a person's existence as equivalent in scale terms to a specific welfare level, which is philosophically incoherent. Furthermore, some health states are rated below zero by respondents — worse than death. The QALY must accommodate these "states worse than death" by extending its scale below zero, but there is no principled basis for how far that extension should go or what it means to say that continued existence is worse than non-existence on a welfare scale. The zero anchor is philosophically arbitrary, and without it the cardinal scale collapses.

iv. what the WELLBY inherits

WELLBY inherits cardinality problem — at greater scaleThe proposition's second speaker offers the WELLBY as a resolution. But the WELLBY makes the commensurability assumption even more ambitiously: not merely that health states are commensurable on a cardinal scale, but that the entirety of a life's quality — its meaning, relationships, achievements, and suffering — can be captured by a number between zero and ten comparable across individuals, cultures, and life circumstances. The cardinality concern that afflicts the QALY afflicts the WELLBY at greater intensity. Scale norming — the documented phenomenon whereby individuals adjust what each number on the scale means in response to their circumstances, reference groups, and cultural context — means that a 7/10 from one person is not the same welfare quantity as a 7/10 from another. The WELLBY's solution to the QALY's problems introduces its own, graver version of the same fundamental difficulty.

Mr President,

I approach this debate from the perspective of fairness economics and the capabilities approach — a tradition that insists welfare measurement must be sensitive not only to what people experience but to what people are able to do and to be, and to the fairness of the process by which health resources are allocated. Both the QALY and the WELLBY, I will argue, fail this test — though in different and instructive ways.

"A measure of health for public policy must ask not only how much welfare is produced, but for whom, under what conditions, and with what implications for the distribution of capabilities across persons. Neither the QALY nor the WELLBY asks these questions. Both are, in this fundamental sense, measures of the wrong thing."
i. what the capabilities approach requires

Capabilities approach — Sen and NussbaumSen and Nussbaum argue that human welfare cannot be adequately captured by any single metric — whether utility, preference satisfaction, resources, or life satisfaction — because what matters for a good life is a plurality of capabilities: mobility, cognitive engagement, social participation, emotional expression, play, and control over one's political and material environment. A person who is provided with adequate caloric intake but denied mobility, education, and social participation is not doing well in any sense that respects what it means to be human. The QALY reduces health to a single utility dimension; the WELLBY reduces it to a single life satisfaction score. Both are, from the capabilities perspective, measures of the wrong thing. They can tell you how much welfare has been produced on their narrow conception; they cannot tell you whether a rich human life is being enabled.

ii. the political economy of wellbeing maximisation

Political economy — wellbeing maximisation risks autonomyA policy framework that explicitly targets the maximisation of aggregate wellbeing — whether in QALYs or WELLBYs — creates a structural pressure toward interventions that are most effective at raising the measured metric, regardless of whether those interventions enhance autonomy, meaningful agency, or genuine human flourishing. Mill's "better Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied" captures the intuition: a life of engaged critical reflection may score lower on life satisfaction than unreflective comfort, but that is not a reason to prefer the latter for purposes of social welfare. A government targeting WELLBY maximisation will be structurally drawn toward nudging citizens toward the conventional life paths — marriage, home ownership, religious practice — that correlate with higher self-reported satisfaction, regardless of autonomous choice. This is not welfare. It is paternalism dressed in empirical language.

iii. the distributional adaptation problem — WELLBY's specific failure

Adaptation — WELLBY's reverse disability paradoxThe WELLBY's use of individuals' own life satisfaction reports, while better than the QALY's population utility weights for disability purposes, introduces a different and equally serious problem: it makes adaptation — the psychological mechanism by which people adjust their expectations and aspirations to their circumstances — determinative of policy priority. A person with a degenerative neurological condition who has adapted impressively and reports 7/10 life satisfaction receives less policy attention than their untreated clinical need would warrant. Someone with treatable severe depression, reporting 2/10, receives high priority. This is appropriate in one sense — treating severe depression is very important. But the WELLBY framework cannot distinguish between: (a) a low score reflecting genuine welfare deprivation treatable by healthcare; and (b) a low score reflecting circumstances (loneliness, poverty, meaninglessness) not remediable by medicine. It elides the distinction between health problems and life problems in a way that QALY-based health evaluation, for all its faults, deliberately avoids.

iv. fairness requires more than welfare maximisation

Fairness — distribution matters beyond welfare totalsMy work on fair social choice theory argues that any adequate framework for health policy must be sensitive not merely to the total welfare produced but to the fairness of its distribution. Two policies with identical expected QALY or WELLBY totals may differ enormously in their distributional implications — one concentrating benefits among the already advantaged, one spreading them across the most deprived. A genuinely fair framework must incorporate ex ante fairness — giving each person a fair chance — and not merely ex post efficiency — maximising the total. Both the QALY and the WELLBY, as typically deployed, maximise totals. They are insensitive to the fairness of distribution in exactly the way that Rawlsian and egalitarian theories of justice demand sensitivity. Severity weighting and distributional CEA are patches that partially address this. They do not resolve the framework's foundational indifference to fairness.

Argument I — The capabilities approach: QALYs and WELLBYs measure the wrong thing
1.Human welfare requires a plurality of capabilities — mobility, cognition, social participation, emotional expression, political agency — that cannot be captured by any single metric (Sen, Nussbaum).
2.The QALY reduces welfare to a single health-utility dimension; the WELLBY reduces it to a single life satisfaction score — both are systematically incomplete.
3.A measure that systematically omits dimensions of human welfare essential to a good life is not an acceptable measure of welfare for public policy.
∴Both the QALY and the WELLBY measure incomplete conceptions of welfare; from a capabilities perspective, both measure the wrong thing.
Argument II — Political economy of maximisation: autonomy and paternalism
1.A framework targeting aggregate wellbeing maximisation creates structural pressure toward interventions most effective at raising the metric, regardless of their effect on autonomy or genuine flourishing.
2.WELLBY-maximising policy will favour conventional life paths (marriage, religious practice, homeownership) that correlate with higher life satisfaction scores over autonomous choices that may score lower.
3.Mill's "Socrates dissatisfied vs. fool satisfied" principle establishes that welfare maximisation on a satisfaction metric is not sufficient for respecting human dignity and autonomy.
∴Wellbeing maximisation — whether in QALYs or WELLBYs — risks reducing policy to paternalistic nudging toward conventional satisfaction, incompatible with respect for autonomous human agency.
Argument III — The WELLBY's adaptation problem reverses the disability paradox
1.The WELLBY uses adapted patient preferences — those who have adjusted psychologically to their condition report higher satisfaction, receiving lower policy priority.
2.The WELLBY cannot distinguish low satisfaction reflecting treatable health deprivation from low satisfaction reflecting social circumstances (poverty, loneliness) not remediable by medicine.
3.This elision of health problems and life problems creates a different but equally serious allocation distortion to the QALY's disability paradox.
∴The WELLBY resolves the QALY's disability paradox by introducing its own adaptation problem; both instruments distort healthcare allocation in different but comparably serious ways.
Argument IV — Fairness requires distributional sensitivity beyond welfare totals
1.Two policies identical in expected QALY or WELLBY totals may differ enormously in distributional justice — one concentrating benefits among the advantaged, one spreading them to the deprived.
2.Rawlsian and egalitarian justice require ex ante fairness — each person gets a fair chance — not merely ex post efficiency in total welfare production.
3.Both the QALY and the WELLBY, as typically deployed, maximise totals and are structurally insensitive to this distributive dimension.
∴Both the QALY and the WELLBY are inadequate frameworks for fair health policy because they maximise totals while remaining insensitive to the fairness of how those totals are distributed.
