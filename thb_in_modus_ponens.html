The Original Counterexample — Election 1980Vann McGee — MIT • Journal of Philosophy, 1985
Modus ponens — from ‘If A then B’ and ‘A’ to ‘B’ — is not a universally valid inference rule for natural language indicative conditionals. The 1980 US presidential election provides a clear counterexample: both premises of the argument are highly credible given what we knew at the time, yet the conclusion is flatly unacceptable. The argument is not enthymematic; the premises are not false; the terms are not equivocal. Modus ponens simply fails for embedded indicative conditionals when the inference is made under conditions of uncertainty rather than certainty.

The deeper explanation of McGee’s result is that indicative conditionals are not truth-apt propositions but expressions of conditional probability. Under Adams’s thesis, the probability of ‘If A then B’ equals P(B|A). Validity is then probability-preservation: an inference is valid iff it is impossible for the premises to be highly probable while the conclusion is improbable. The argument I presented in 1985 is simple. Its simplicity is part of its force. I want to restate it with full precision and then address the standard defences, which I believe fail.

The setting is the eve of the 1980 US presidential election. The candidates are Ronald Reagan (Republican), Jimmy Carter (Democrat), and John Anderson (Independent, formerly Republican). Polls show Reagan decisively ahead, Carter in second, Anderson a distant third. Against this background, the following argument appears valid:

The 1980 Election Argument — formal structure
P1: If a Republican wins the election, then if it’s not Reagan who wins, it will be Anderson.
P1 is highly credible: if a Republican wins, the only Republicans running are Reagan and Anderson; so if a Republican wins and it is not Reagan, the winner must be Anderson. This is not merely probable — it follows from the structure of the race.
P2: A Republican will win the election.
P2 is highly credible: Reagan is decisively ahead in all polls. A Republican winning is the most probable outcome by a wide margin.
C (by Modus Ponens): If it’s not Reagan who wins, it will be Anderson.
C is clearly unacceptable. If Reagan somehow loses, the winner will almost certainly be Carter — who is in second place. Anderson is a distant third. The conclusion says, in effect, that if the front-runner loses, the third-place candidate wins rather than the second-place candidate. This is exactly backwards from what we should believe.
The argument form is A → (B → C), A ⊢ B → C. This is a direct application of modus ponens. The premises are credible. The conclusion is not. Therefore modus ponens fails for indicative conditionals.
i. why the counterexample is genuine: three tests

Three tests — the premises are genuinely credible; the conclusion is genuinely unacceptable; no equivocation; no hidden premise; the argument has the right logical form; MP failsThree tests determine whether this is a genuine counterexample to modus ponens rather than a disguised fallacy. First: are the premises genuinely credible? Yes. P1 is practically a logical truth given the structure of the race — it follows from the fact that only two Republicans are running. P2 is empirically well-supported: Reagan is ahead in every poll. Second: is the conclusion genuinely unacceptable? Yes. Conditional on Reagan losing, Carter — not Anderson — would win. We should have high confidence in “If Reagan doesn’t win, Carter will” and low confidence in “If Reagan doesn’t win, Anderson will.” Third: is the argument actually of the form A → (B → C), A ⊢ B → C? Yes. P1 has the form “If [a Republican wins], then [if [it’s not Reagan], [Anderson wins]]” — a conditional whose consequent is itself a conditional. P2 is the antecedent of P1. The conclusion is the consequent of P1. All three tests pass. This is a genuine counterexample.

ii. the import-export principle and its role

Import-Export — A → (B → C) is equivalent to (A ∧ B) → C; if this holds, P1 is equivalent to “If a Republican wins and it’s not Reagan, Anderson will win” — which is true; and C is equivalent to P1’s consequent — which we have independent reason to rejectA crucial structural feature of my argument is that it exploits the Import-Export principle: the logical equivalence of A → (B → C) and (A & B) → C. Under Import-Export, P1 is logically equivalent to “If a Republican wins and it’s not Reagan, Anderson will win.” This is clearly true: in a race with only two Republicans, a Republican winner who is not Reagan must be Anderson. Import-Export gives us the true reading of P1 while the conclusion — “If it’s not Reagan, Anderson will win” — remains clearly false. The reason the argument fails is that applying modus ponens to P1 in its nested form produces a conclusion that is not equivalent to any part of the true claim we used to establish P1. The inference draws on P1 in a way that severs the conditional from the evidential context that makes P1 plausible.

iii. why the “false premise” response fails

False premise response — the main opposition strategy says P2 is not known to be true; but McGee’s original argument is epistemic not alethic; we need only assert the premises with high credenceThe most common opposition response is to deny that P2 is really asserted as true — merely as highly probable. From the fact that P2 is highly probable, modus ponens cannot be applied, because MP requires the premises to be true, not just probable. I reject this response for two reasons. First: my argument is explicitly framed as an epistemic one, about what it is reasonable to believe. I never claimed the premises were known with certainty; I claimed they were highly credible — more credible than the conclusion. Modus ponens, if it is a rule of good reasoning rather than a rule of pure logic, must apply to credible premises, not only to certain ones. An inference rule that only validates from certainty is not a rule of useful reasoning. Second: even granting the epistemic version of the argument, the pattern remains: high credence in both premises combined with low credence in the conclusion is a clear violation of the probabilistic coherence that good reasoning requires.

iv. additional examples and why the failure is systematic

Additional examples — the election case is not isolated; similar cases arise in medicine, law, and everyday reasoning; the failure of MP for nested indicatives under uncertainty is systematicThe election case is not an isolated curiosity. Consider: “If the patient does not improve, then if the first antibiotic does not work, the second will.” “The patient will not improve.” Conclusion: “If the first antibiotic does not work, the second will.” But suppose we know that neither antibiotic is reliable and the patient’s condition is serious enough that improvement is unlikely regardless. The premises are credible; the conclusion is not. Or consider: “If there is a verdict, then if it is not guilty, it will be not proven.” (In a jurisdiction with three available verdicts: guilty, not guilty, not proven.) “There will be a verdict.” Conclusion: “If it is not guilty, it will be not proven.” But we might have strong reason to expect acquittal. The failure pattern is systematic: whenever the antecedent of the embedded conditional corresponds to an unlikely but possible case, modus ponens yields a conclusion that ignores the evidential probabilities of the alternatives.

The three tests pass. The failure is systematic. Modus ponens yields false conclusions from true premises. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Three tests confirm the election argument is a genuine counterexample to MP
1.Test 1 (Credible premises): P1 is near-analytically true given the race structure; P2 is empirically well-supported by decisive polling. Both premises are genuinely credible.
2.Test 2 (Unacceptable conclusion): Conditional on Reagan losing, Carter wins — not Anderson. We should be confident in “If not Reagan, then Carter” and reject “If not Reagan, then Anderson.” The conclusion is genuinely unacceptable.
3.Test 3 (Correct logical form): The argument is A → (B → C), A ⊢ B → C. This is modus ponens applied to a conditional whose consequent is itself a conditional. The form is correct.
∴All three tests confirm this is a genuine counterexample: credible premises, unacceptable conclusion, correct logical form. Modus ponens fails for indicative conditionals when nested under uncertainty.
Argument II — Import-Export explains why P1 is true while C is false: the context shift between premise and conclusion is the mechanism of failure
1.Import-Export: A → (B → C) is equivalent to (A & B) → C. P1 under IE reads: “If a Republican wins and it’s not Reagan, Anderson wins.” This is true — it follows from the race structure.
2.The conclusion C — “If it’s not Reagan, Anderson wins” — is not equivalent to (A & B) → C; it is B → C outright, with no restriction to Republican-win scenarios. Under this reading, it is false: if Reagan loses, we must consider all candidates including Carter.
3.Applying MP strips away the “if a Republican wins” restriction that made P1 plausible, generating a conclusion that ranges over all scenarios including Carter-wins scenarios that P1 carefully excluded.
∴MP fails by context-stripping: it detaches the conclusion from the evidential restriction that made the premise credible; the nested conditional in P1 implicitly restricts evaluation to Republican-win scenarios; MP removes this restriction and generates a false conclusion.
Argument III — The “false premise” response fails: MP must apply to credible premises or it is not a rule of reasoning
1.Opposition: P2 is not known to be true; MP only applies to premises known to be true; therefore MP is not violated. But this makes MP useless as a reasoning rule: we almost never know premises with certainty.
2.McGee’s argument is explicitly epistemic: about what it is reasonable to believe. If MP is a rule of good epistemic reasoning, it must apply to highly credible premises. A rule that only validates from certainty is not a rule of practical reasoning.
3.Even on the probabilistic reformulation: high P(P1) and high P(P2) should entail high P(C) if MP is probability-preserving. But P(C) is low. Therefore MP fails probabilistically as well as alethically.
∴The false-premise response saves the alethic version of MP at the cost of making it useless for practical reasoning; the epistemic version of MP fails too; the counterexample stands against any formulation of MP that claims to govern real-world inference.
Argument IV — The failure is systematic across multiple domains; the election case is not an isolated curiosity
1.Medical case: “If the patient doesn’t improve, then if antibiotic A fails, antibiotic B will work.” / “The patient won’t improve.” C: “If A fails, B will work.” False if neither is reliable. Same structure, same failure.
2.Legal case (three-verdict jurisdiction): “If there’s a verdict, then if not guilty, it will be not proven.” / “There will be a verdict.” C: “If not guilty, it will be not proven.” False if acquittal is expected. Same structure, same failure.
3.The pattern is systematic: MP fails whenever the consequent of the outer conditional is itself a conditional whose antecedent corresponds to an unlikely case, and applying MP strips away the evidential context that made the outer conditional credible.
∴The failure of MP is systematic across medical, legal, and political reasoning; it is not an artefact of the election scenario but a structural feature of nested indicative conditionals under uncertainty; the counterexample is robust.

McGee’s election case shows that modus ponens fails for a specific class of nested indicative conditionals. Our 2010 paper in the Journal of Philosophy shows that the same failure pattern arises independently, from a completely different direction, in the deontic domain. This convergence is significant: it shows that the problem is not a quirk of the election example, or of nested conditionals specifically, but a deep and pervasive feature of how ‘if’ interacts with information-sensitive operators. The opposition cannot explain away two independent failures of modus ponens by the same story.

The Miners Paradox — Kolodny and MacFarlane 2010.

Ten miners are trapped either in shaft A or shaft B. We don’t know which. Flood waters are rising. We have enough sandbags to block one shaft but not both. If we block the wrong shaft, the miners in the other shaft drown. If we block neither shaft, both shafts flood partially, and one or two miners die but not all ten.

Against this background, the following argument appears valid:

P1: If the miners are in shaft A, we ought to block shaft A.
P2: If the miners are in shaft B, we ought to block shaft B.
P3: Either the miners are in shaft A or they are in shaft B.
C: Either we ought to block shaft A or we ought to block shaft B.

The conclusion is wrong. We should block neither shaft — the expected-value calculation favours neither blocking and saving 9-10 miners, over blocking and risking losing all ten. The argument has the same structure as McGee’s: individually plausible premises, collectively unacceptable conclusion, valid logical form. The failure extends beyond nested conditionals to conditional obligations.
i. why the miners paradox independently confirms MP failure

Miners paradox — independently confirms MP failure in deontic domain; same logical structure as McGee; cannot be explained by features specific to nested conditionalsThe Miners Paradox is not a case of nested conditionals; it involves simple indicative conditionals paired with deontic modals. Yet it produces the same failure pattern: the premises are individually plausible, and the conclusion follows by what looks like a valid inference, but the conclusion is clearly wrong. This independence matters. The opposition’s responses to McGee typically target features specific to nested conditionals — the equivocation diagnosis (Lowe), the restrictor view (Kratzer), or the material-conditional escape (Sinnott-Armstrong). None of these diagnoses applies to the Miners Paradox, which does not involve nested conditionals at all. The recurrence of the failure across two structurally similar but formally different cases cannot be explained by any diagnosis targeted at nesting alone.

ii. the information-sensitivity of indicative conditionals

Information-sensitivity — indicative conditionals are evaluated relative to information states not possible worlds; this is why they resist classical truth-table treatment and why MP failsThe deeper explanation of both McGee’s case and the Miners Paradox is that indicative conditionals are not truth-apt propositions evaluated at possible worlds but are evaluated relative to an information state — a set of worlds compatible with what is currently known or assumed. When you assert “If a Republican wins, then if it’s not Reagan, it’s Anderson,” you are evaluating this conditional relative to an information state in which Reagan’s winning is still an open possibility. The nested conditional is true because, restricting to worlds where a Republican wins, Reagan wins in most of them. But when modus ponens detaches the consequent, it does so relative to an information state where the antecedent is known — and this changes the evaluation context. The information-sensitivity of the conditional is what makes MP fail: the semantic evaluation context shifts between premise and conclusion.

iii. the semantics that makes MP fail

Semantics — Kolodny and MacFarlane’s semantics evaluates sentences relative to world and information state; conditionals are evaluated at a pair (w,i); the information state changes between premise and conclusion when P2 is learnedIn our semantics, sentences are evaluated relative to a world-information state pair (w, i). Conditional “If A then B” is true at (w, i) roughly if B is true at (w′, i′) for all (w′, i′) accessible from (w, i) where A holds. The crucial point: when you apply modus ponens, you learn P2 (that a Republican wins), which updates i to i′ — an information state where Republican victory is certain. But the conclusion “If it’s not Reagan, Anderson wins” must now be evaluated at (w, i′), not (w, i). At (w, i′), where Republican victory is known, Carter is still a possible winner if Reagan somehow loses (since worlds where Democrats win are not excluded by mere Republican-victory certainty without further constraint). The update from i to i′ changes which worlds are salient for evaluating the conclusion, making it false even though both premises were true at (w, i).

iv. rejecting MP is compatible with vindicating most uses

Compatibility claim — rejecting general validity of MP is compatible with vindicating ordinary uses; MP is locally valid in many contexts; rejecting universal validity is a surgical move not a catastropheThe opposition will say: abandoning modus ponens is too high a price to pay. Logic would fall apart. We respond: rejecting the universal validity of modus ponens is compatible with vindicating the vast majority of its ordinary uses. MP fails only when (a) the conditional is an indicative, (b) it is applied under conditions of uncertainty where the antecedent is not known, and (c) the consequent of the conditional is itself sensitive to the information state in ways that change when the antecedent is learned. In the vast majority of everyday and mathematical inferences, none of these conditions obtain. The casualty is modus ponens as an absolutely universal formal rule; the practical inferential guidance it provides is largely preserved. The surgery is precise.

The Miners Paradox independently confirms the failure. The information-sensitivity of conditionals explains it. MP must go. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Miners Paradox independently confirms MP failure in the deontic domain without nested conditionals
1.The Miners Paradox: P1 (if miners in A, block A), P2 (if miners in B, block B), P3 (miners in A or B) ⊢ C (block A or block B). Valid inference to a wrong conclusion. No nested conditionals.
2.The opposition’s diagnoses of McGee target nesting-specific features: equivocation (Lowe), restrictor semantics (Kratzer), material conditional escape (Sinnott-Armstrong). None applies to the Miners Paradox.
3.Two structurally similar but formally distinct cases cannot be explained by any nesting-specific diagnosis. The failure is structural, not an artefact of nesting.
∴The Miners Paradox independently confirms MP failure; since it involves no nested conditionals, nesting-specific diagnoses cannot explain it; the failure must be attributed to a deeper feature of indicative conditionals in general.
Argument II — Indicative conditionals are information-sensitive; they are evaluated at world-information state pairs; this explains why MP fails
1.Indicative conditionals are evaluated relative to information states (sets of epistemically accessible worlds), not just at possible worlds. “If A then B” at (w, i) is true roughly iff B holds at all (w′, i′) accessible from (w, i) where A holds.
2.Learning the antecedent P2 updates the information state i to i′. The conclusion must then be evaluated at (w, i′), where contexts may differ from (w, i) in which worlds are epistemically accessible.
3.This context shift means P1 can be true at (w, i) while C is false at (w, i′): the context update that brings P2 into certainty changes the epistemic landscape in ways that falsify C even though both premises were true before the update.
∴Information-sensitivity is the mechanism of MP failure: applying P2 updates the information state, and the conclusion is evaluated in a different context from the premise; this explains both McGee’s case and the Miners Paradox with a unified semantic account.
Argument III — The wide-scope response to the Miners Paradox fails: it cannot preserve both the standard semantics and commonsense obligations
1.Wide-scope response: read P1 as “We ought to: (if miners in A, block A)” instead of “If miners in A, we ought to block A.” Under wide-scope, the conditional falls inside the ought, breaking the structure MP requires.
2.But the wide-scope reading does not capture commonsense obligation. Our obligations under certainty should be: if we discover miners are in A, we ought to block A. This is the narrow-scope, conditional-obligation reading, and it is correct. Wide-scope distorts the obligation.
3.Wide-scope cannot handle cases where we do discover which shaft: if we learn miners are in A, the narrow-scope P1 immediately gives us “we ought to block A.” Wide-scope gives no such upshot. The correct semantics requires narrow-scope, which makes MP generate the wrong conclusion.
∴The wide-scope escape fails: it preserves MP only by distorting the semantics of conditional obligations; the commonsensically correct reading is narrow-scope, which keeps MP but generates the wrong conclusion; the paradox persists under the correct reading.
Argument IV — Rejecting universal MP is compatible with vindicating ordinary uses; the surgery is precise
1.MP fails only when: (a) the conditional is indicative, (b) applied under uncertainty where the antecedent is not known, and (c) the consequent is information-sensitive in ways that change under antecedent-learning. These conditions are not met in the vast majority of everyday inferences.
2.For mathematical proofs, logical tautologies, and cases where premises are known with certainty, MP is fully preserved. The practical inferential guidance of MP is largely intact.
3.The price of universal MP is accepting conclusions we should reject. The price of restricted MP is losing a universal formal rule. Given the evidence, restricted MP is the better trade.
∴Rejecting universal MP is a precise, principled response that preserves the vast majority of ordinary inferential practice; the opposition’s warning that logic collapses without universal MP is false; the surgery is targeted and the patient survives.

I want to press the case that the standard defences of modus ponens all fail, and that this failure is not accidental but systematic. In my 1993 paper “MPP, RIP” I surveyed the responses to McGee then available and found them collectively inadequate. I want to update that assessment in light of more recent defences and argue that they too fail, while the counterexamples continue to multiply.

The standard defences can be grouped into four families. The material-conditional defence says: read the conditionals as material conditionals; then the argument is truth-functionally valid and the conclusion is true. The false-premise defence says: P2 is not really believed to be true but only probably true; MP only applies to certain premises. The equivocation defence says: the phrase “a Republican” shifts meaning between premises; the argument commits a fallacy of equivocation. The restrictor defence says: ‘if’ is not a connective but a restrictor; MP simply does not apply to the logical form of these sentences. I will address each in turn.

i. the material conditional defence: a pyrrhic victory

Material conditional defence — reading ‘if’ as material conditional makes the argument truth-functionally valid; but this forces the claim that “if not Reagan, Anderson” is true just because Reagan wins; a Pyrrhic victoryIf we read “if...then” as the material conditional ³, then the conclusion “If not Reagan, Anderson” is evaluated as &lnot;not-Reagan ∨ Anderson, which is true just in case Reagan wins (since &lnot;&lnot;Reagan = Reagan wins). Reagan wins. So the conclusion is (vacuously) true under the material conditional reading. Modus ponens is saved. But at what cost? The cost is that every conditional whose antecedent is false is trivially true. “If Reagan doesn’t win, Anderson will win” is true simply because Reagan wins — regardless of any relationship between Reagan’s losing and Anderson’s winning. This is not what we mean by the conditional. It is not what makes the conclusion unintuitive. The conclusion is unintuitive because we are asking what would happen if Reagan lost — and the material conditional reading ignores this question entirely. Saving MP by retreating to the material conditional is a Pyrrhic victory: the inference rule is preserved but the semantics has been drained of its substance.

ii. the false-premise defence: too restrictive

False-premise defence — saying P2 is not known-true makes MP inapplicable; but this makes MP useless for ordinary reasoning which always proceeds from uncertain premisesSinnott-Armstrong, Moor, and Fogelin argue that P2 (“A Republican will win”) is not asserted as true but as highly probable, and that modus ponens requires true premises, not merely probable ones. But this defence purchases MP’s survival at too high a price. If MP only applies to premises that are actually known to be true, it becomes useless for the vast majority of practical reasoning, which proceeds from probable rather than certain premises. Every piece of everyday reasoning — planning, deliberating, explaining, predicting — involves premises that are credible rather than certain. A logic rule that only validates from certainty is an idle formalism, not a guide to reasoning. Moreover, the epistemic version of the counterexample remains: we have high credence in both premises and low credence in the conclusion, which violates the probabilistic coherence that rational reasoning requires.

iii. the equivocation defence: too clever

Equivocation defence — Lowe argues “a Republican” shifts from specific to non-specific reading; but this diagnosis cannot explain the parallel cases and Lycan shows it depends on a contested semantics for indefinite descriptionsLowe argues that “a Republican” in P1 is read specifically — picking out a particular candidate, namely Reagan — while in P2 it is read non-specifically — picking out whichever Republican wins. Under the specific reading of P1, P1 says roughly “If Reagan wins, then if it’s not Reagan, Anderson wins” — which is trivially true since the consequent of the nested conditional has a false antecedent. The equivocation defuses the counterexample by making P1 read as non-trivially true only under the non-specific reading, which requires a different P2 to generate the argument. But this diagnosis is contested for three reasons. First: the specific/non-specific distinction for indefinite descriptions is itself philosophically contentious. Second: the diagnosis does not carry over to parallel cases that do not involve indefinite descriptions — the antibiotic case and three-verdict case use different constructions. Third: the “specific” reading of “a Republican” in P1 makes P1 trivially true, which removes its epistemic force and makes the argument uninteresting. The equivocation defence solves McGee by making his argument trivially valid rather than invalidly valid — a strange kind of victory.

iv. why the restrictor defence does not vindicate MP

Restrictor defence — Kratzer says ‘if’ is not a connective so MP doesn’t apply; but even under the restrictor view, inferences that look like MP can fail; the problem is displaced not solvedKratzer’s restrictor view says ‘if’ is not a propositional connective but a device for restricting modal operators. On this view, there is no propositional operator “if...then” in P1; there is only a modal operator with a restricted domain. Since MP is a rule about propositional connectives, it simply does not apply to the logical form of P1 — and so it cannot be violated. But as Schulz (2018) argues in the Journal of Philosophical Logic, even under the restrictor view, inferences that are surface-form instances of modus ponens can go wrong. And more importantly: the restrictor view displaces the problem rather than solving it. The natural language inference that McGee is discussing — from “If Republicans win, then if not Reagan, Anderson” plus “Republicans will win” to “If not Reagan, Anderson” — is a valid-seeming inference that produces a false conclusion. Whether or not this is technically modus ponens under Kratzer’s logical form, it is an inference that goes wrong in exactly the way McGee describes. The semantic pathology exists even if the technical label “modus ponens failure” is debatable.

Each defence fails. The pathology remains. MPP, RIP. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The material conditional defence is a Pyrrhic victory: it saves MP by draining the conditional of its substance
1.Material conditional reading: “If not Reagan, Anderson” = &lnot;(not-Reagan) ∨ Anderson = Reagan-wins ∨ Anderson-wins. Since Reagan wins, this is true. MP is saved.
2.Cost: the conclusion is true only vacuously, because Reagan wins. It has nothing to do with any relationship between Reagan’s losing and Anderson’s winning. Every conditional with a false antecedent becomes trivially true.
3.This is not what the conditional means in ordinary reasoning. The conclusion is counterintuitive because we are asking what follows if Reagan loses — a question the material conditional reading cannot address. The defence saves the form but loses the substance.
∴The material conditional defence is a Pyrrhic victory: it preserves MP by adopting a semantics that trivialises conditionals with false antecedents and cannot capture the reasoning about counterfactual scenarios that makes McGee’s argument compelling.
Argument II — The false-premise defence makes MP useless for ordinary reasoning
1.The false-premise defence: MP requires true premises; P2 is only probable; therefore MP does not apply. But ordinary reasoning almost never proceeds from certain premises.
2.A rule that only validates from certainty is idle: it cannot guide practical deliberation, planning, or explanation, all of which involve credible rather than certain premises.
3.The epistemic counterexample survives: high credence in P1 and P2, low credence in C. This violates probabilistic coherence. The defence rescues alethic MP only by conceding epistemic MP.
∴The false-premise defence makes MP useless: it survives by restricting the rule to certain premises, which excludes the ordinary uncertain-premise reasoning for which a logic rule is most needed; the epistemic counterexample remains untouched.
Argument III — The equivocation defence is contested, does not carry to parallel cases, and makes P1 trivially true
1.Equivocation diagnosis: “a Republican” is specific (Reagan) in P1 and non-specific in P2. Under specific reading, P1 says “If Reagan wins, then if not Reagan, Anderson wins” — trivially true (false antecedent on the consequent).
2.Making P1 trivially true removes its epistemic content: it is no longer doing any work. McGee’s argument depended on P1 being genuinely informative. A trivially true P1 makes the argument trivially valid, not substantively invalid.
3.The diagnosis does not carry to parallel cases: antibiotic and three-verdict cases do not use indefinite descriptions. The equivocation story is election-specific and cannot explain the systematic failure.
∴The equivocation defence is election-specific, makes P1 trivially true, and cannot explain parallel cases; it is a local repair that neither vindicates MP generally nor explains the systematic pattern of failures.
Argument IV — The restrictor defence displaces the problem rather than solving it; surface-form MP failures persist even on Kratzer’s semantics
1.Kratzer: ‘if’ is a restrictor, not a connective; MP applies to connectives; therefore there are no instances of MP in P1 and it cannot be violated.
2.But Schulz (2018) shows that even under the restrictor view, surface-form MP inferences can go wrong. The displacement of the problem is a technical manoeuvre, not a solution to the underlying semantic pathology.
3.The natural language inference from P1 and P2 to C is a valid-seeming inference that produces a false conclusion. Whether this is technically MP failure or restrictor-semantics failure is a labelling dispute. The inference goes wrong in exactly the way McGee describes.
∴The restrictor defence displaces rather than solves the problem: even on Kratzer’s semantics, the natural language inference goes wrong; relabelling the failure does not vindicate the underlying inference pattern.

I want to offer the probabilistic explanation that I believe gives McGee’s result its deepest foundation. McGee showed that modus ponens fails by the standard criterion of deductive validity: the premises can be credible while the conclusion is not. Once the correct analysis is in place, the apparent counterexample to modus ponens evaporates: what McGee has found is not a failure of a logical rule but a revealing feature of the semantics of natural language modal operators.

The restrictor view of conditionals, which I have developed since the early 1980s, holds that ‘if’-clauses in natural language are not propositional connectives that combine two propositions into a conditional proposition. They are restrictors: they restrict the domain of a modal operator, adverb of quantification, or other operator that is present (explicitly or implicitly) in the sentence. When you say “If it rains, the picnic will be cancelled,” there is no binary propositional connective “if—then” that takes “it rains” and “the picnic will be cancelled” as its arguments. There is a modal operator (roughly “will” or an implicit epistemic necessity) whose domain of evaluation is restricted to rain-worlds by the ‘if’-clause.

I want to defend modus ponens from a more nuanced position than either Sinnott-Armstrong’s group or Kratzer. My 2015 paper in the Journal of Philosophy argues that modus ponens is valid under an informational conception of logical consequence — and that this is in fact the correct conception for natural language reasoning. Under this conception, McGee’s counterexamples, Kolodny and MacFarlane’s Miners Paradox, and Willer’s related cases all fail to undermine MP. The apparent failures arise from an illicit context shift in the information state, not from a genuine failure of the inference rule.

The informational conception of consequence says: an argument is valid iff accepting the premises as information requires accepting the conclusion as information. “Accepting as information” is not the same as “believing with high probability.” It means incorporating the content into your information state — treating it as settled. When you genuinely accept P2 — “A Republican will win” — as settled information, not merely as highly probable, the informational context changes in a way that makes the conclusion follow.

i. the informational conception of validity

Informational validity — an argument is valid iff accepting premises as settled information requires accepting conclusion; this differs from truth-preservation and probability-preservation; under it, MP is validThe informational conception of validity is motivated by the observation that we primarily use logic not to track the truth conditions of sentences but to guide rational transitions between information states. An inference is valid when accepting the premises as settled commits you to accepting the conclusion as settled. This is different from the truth-preservation conception (classical logic) and from the probability-preservation conception (Adams). Under the informational conception, modus ponens is valid: if you have settled in your information state that “If A then B” and also settled that A, you must settle that B. The key word is “settled.” McGee’s premises are not settled: they are highly probable. The informational conception does not apply to probable premises; it applies to incorporated information. Under genuine information incorporation, MP is valid.

ii. why mcgee’s counterexample fails on the informational reading

McGee fails informationally — if you genuinely incorporate “a Republican wins” into your information state as settled, Carter is eliminated; the conclusion follows in the updated context; the failure is not logical but reflects incomplete information incorporationHere is why McGee’s argument does not work under the informational conception. Suppose you genuinely incorporate P2 — “A Republican will win” — into your information state as settled, not merely as highly probable. This means your information state now excludes all Carter-wins worlds. In this updated information state, where the only possible outcomes are Reagan-wins and Anderson-wins, the conclusion “If it’s not Reagan, it will be Anderson” is true: the only alternative to Reagan winning is Anderson winning, since Carter has been excluded. Under genuine information incorporation of P2, the conclusion does follow. McGee’s apparent counterexample arises only when P2 is not genuinely incorporated but merely treated as highly probable — which keeps Carter-wins worlds in the picture, making the conclusion seem false. But this is not an application of the informational modus ponens; it is an attempt to apply an information-theoretic rule to a probabilistic premise.

iii. the context-shift illusion

Context-shift illusion — the appearance of MP failure arises from evaluating the conclusion in a different context from the premises; legitimate logic does not permit context shifts between premises and conclusion; the failure is contextual not logicalThe deeper diagnosis of why McGee’s argument seems to produce a counterexample is that the premises and the conclusion are evaluated in different contexts. P1 and P2 are evaluated against the background of the full epistemic context — one where Reagan is ahead but Carter remains a real possibility. The conclusion “If not Reagan, Anderson” is then judged false against this same full context, where Carter is still live. But a legitimate application of modus ponens requires evaluating the conclusion in the context updated by the premises. When we update by P2 (settling that a Republican wins), the context changes: Carter is removed, and the conclusion becomes acceptable in the updated context. The apparent failure arises from evaluating C in the original context rather than the premise-updated context. This is an illegitimate context shift, not a genuine failure of the inference rule.

I published my response to McGee in 1987 in Analysis, arguing that the election example is not a counterexample to modus ponens but a case of equivocation — a fallacy of shifting the semantic value of the phrase “a Republican” between the two premises. The argument looks valid because we do not notice this shift. Once the shift is identified, the argument either commits an equivocation or becomes a trivially valid but epistemically uninteresting inference. Modus ponens is not the culprit.

The distinction I draw is between the specific and the non-specific reading of indefinite descriptions. “A Republican” can be used specifically, to pick out a particular individual — in this context, most naturally Reagan, who is the expected winner. Under the specific reading, “If a Republican wins” means “If Reagan wins.” It can also be used non-specifically, as a genuine existential: “if some Republican or other wins.” These two readings give rise to two different premises, and the argument does not maintain the same reading across both.

The equivocation diagnosis — formal statement.

Reading 1 (specific): “a Republican” = Reagan specifically.

P1 (specific): If Reagan wins, then if it’s not Reagan who wins, Anderson will win.
Under this reading, P1 is trivially true: its antecedent (“Reagan wins”) and the antecedent of the nested conditional (“not Reagan wins”) are jointly unsatisfiable. A conditional with a necessarily false antecedent is vacuously true. P1specific is trivially true, but epistemically empty.

P2 (specific): Reagan will win.
C (by MP on specific reading): If it’s not Reagan who wins, Anderson will win. This conclusion is vacuously true under specific reading too (Reagan wins).

Reading 2 (non-specific): “a Republican” = some Republican or other.

P1 (non-specific): If some Republican wins, then if it’s not Reagan, Anderson wins.
This is the substantively interesting reading — but now P2 must also be non-specific.
P2 (non-specific): Some Republican will win. (This is credible given polling.)
C (by MP on non-specific reading): If it’s not Reagan, Anderson wins. This is false.

The argument only generates a false conclusion when P1 is read non-specifically and P2 is read non-specifically. But is P1 under the non-specific reading actually true?
i. the equivocation: which reading makes the argument work?

Equivocation — under specific reading, P1 is trivially true and C is trivially true; under non-specific reading, P1 may not be true; the argument requires shifting readings to generate the apparent counterexampleFor the argument to be a genuine counterexample, we need: P1 genuinely true (not trivially), P2 true, C false. The specific reading gives us P1 trivially true and C trivially true — no counterexample. The non-specific reading gives us P1 substantively true (credibly), P2 credibly true, but C false — apparent counterexample. The proposition will say: use the non-specific reading throughout and the argument works. But this is where the equivocation enters. Under the non-specific reading, P1 says “If some Republican wins, then if it’s not Reagan, Anderson wins.” Is this true? It seems true because we are thinking of the race structure: only two Republicans are running, so if a Republican wins and it is not Reagan, it must be Anderson. But notice: we are reasoning from the race structure, which is a background fact. P1 under the non-specific reading is not a logical truth but a contingent claim that presupposes the particular structure of this race.

ii. p1 under the non-specific reading is not obviously true

P1 questionable — P1 under non-specific reading presupposes the race structure; if we abstract from that structure, P1 is not clearly true; the apparent credibility of P1 smuggles in background assumptionsOnce we hold the non-specific reading constant across both premises, P1 is not as clearly true as McGee claims. P1 (non-specific) says: if some Republican wins, then if it is not Reagan, Anderson wins. This claim is true only given the specific structure of the 1980 race — that only Reagan and Anderson are Republicans running. But the claim is being evaluated under the supposition that some Republican wins, and under this supposition we are meant to identify the winner. The claim seems true only because we are importing background knowledge about the race. If we took the antecedent seriously — suppose some Republican wins — and asked who could win, we would need to consider: could it be some Republican write-in candidate? Could the race have unexpected third-party entrants? The specific reading forecloses these questions; the non-specific reading opens them. P1 under the non-specific reading is a substantive empirical claim about the completeness of the Republican candidate field, and it is not obviously true without invoking race-specific background assumptions that shift the reading back toward specific.

iii. the equivocation across premises

Equivocation across premises — the argument gets its force by using the specific reading for P1 (making it credible via race structure) and the non-specific reading for P2 (making it credible via polls); these two readings are not the same premise and the argument commits equivocationThe equivocation that powers McGee’s argument works as follows. We read P1 in a way that makes it credible: we use the specific structure of the race — only two Republicans running — to establish that if a Republican wins and it is not Reagan, it is Anderson. This is essentially a specific reading: we are reasoning about the particular candidates in this particular race. We then read P2 in a way that makes it credible: polls show a Republican will win. This is fine. But when we apply modus ponens, we detach the consequent of P1. The consequent of P1, under the race-specific reading, says: if it’s not Reagan, Anderson wins — where “wins” is evaluated in the restricted context of Republican candidates only. But the conclusion C is evaluated across all candidates, including Carter. The shift from “wins among Republicans” to “wins the election overall” is the equivocation. It is a subtle shift in the domain of evaluation, not a failure of modus ponens.

iv. the test: does any reading make all three claims true or false simultaneously?

Uniformity test — no uniform reading gives true premises and false conclusion; specific reading gives trivially true P1 and true C; non-specific reading gives questionable P1; the counterexample requires an illegitimate mix of readingsThe decisive test for whether McGee’s argument is a genuine counterexample is whether any uniform reading of the key terms gives us true premises and a clearly false conclusion. Under the specific reading throughout: P1 is trivially true, P2 is true (Reagan wins), C is vacuously true (Reagan wins). No counterexample — the conclusion is true. Under the non-specific reading throughout: P1 is a substantive empirical claim about the Republican candidate field — true given the race structure, but not obviously true in the way required for a logical counterexample. Its truth depends on background assumptions that are not logical in character. C is false. But P1’s truth under this reading requires those background assumptions, and incorporating them into the evaluation of C changes the result. No uniform reading produces a clean counterexample with true premises and a clearly false conclusion. The apparent counterexample requires mixing readings — and mixing readings is equivocation.

The argument equivocates. Under any uniform reading, the counterexample fails. MP stands. I yield the floor.
