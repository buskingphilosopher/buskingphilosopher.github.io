Chair,

I want to begin by drawing a distinction that I think the opposition will either deny or, more dangerously, quietly concede and then forget. A reason is agent-neutral if it can be stated without reference to the agent for whom it is a reason: “pain is bad” gives every agent a reason to prevent pain, regardless of whose pain it is. A reason is agent-relative if it essentially refers to the agent: “your child’s suffering matters more because it is your child.” The motion asks whether the latter kind of reason can be a genuine moral reason. I want to argue that it cannot — and that this conclusion, far from being a cold philosophical technicality, tracks something deep about the nature of morality itself.

“Common-sense morality is collectively self-defeating. When each of us acts on the agent-relative reasons common sense endorses — prioritising our own, our family, our community — the total outcome is worse by the lights of what each of us most deeply cares about: that things go well. This is not a paradox to be accepted. It is a reductio of the structure of agent-relative moral reasoning.”
i. the collective self-defeat of agent-relative morality

Self-defeat — agent-relative morality undermines its own aims collectivelyIn Reasons and Persons I argued that common-sense morality — the familiar view that we have stronger reasons to benefit those near and dear to us, to honour our special obligations, to give our own projects extra weight — is collectively self-defeating. Here is why. If everyone acts on agent-relative reasons, each prioritising their own, then the global distribution of attention, resources, and care is determined by the morally arbitrary facts of who happens to stand in which relationships to whom. The child with loving, wealthy parents is well cared for; the child with indifferent or impoverished parents is not. Each parent, acting on their agent-relative reasons, does what their theory tells them to do. But the outcomes, aggregated, are worse by any standard those same parents would endorse from a detached perspective. The theory tells each agent to do X, and when they all do X, everyone is worse off than if they had each done something else. This is the signature of a theory that has got the structure of reasons wrong.

ii. the separateness of persons objection, reconsidered

Separateness of persons — the Rawlsian objection answeredThe opposition will invoke Rawls’s argument that impartialism — including the agent-neutral view I defend — fails to take seriously the separateness of persons: that it treats the distribution of welfare across persons as if it were the distribution of welfare across a single person’s lifetime, and that this elides the moral significance of individual boundaries. I want to meet this argument directly. The separateness of persons is not a ground for agent-relative reasons. It is, if anything, a ground for the opposite. Taking the separateness of persons seriously means recognising that each person’s suffering has equal weight regardless of their relationship to me. The Rawlsian objection, properly understood, is an objection to certain aggregative forms of consequentialism — the objection is that we must not trade one person’s severe suffering against many others’ minor benefits. This is a constraint on how agent-neutral reasons are aggregated, not a licence for agent-relative ones.

iii. the convergence thesis: what survives critical scrutiny

Convergence — Kantian, contractualist, and consequentialist theories align at impartialist conclusionsIn On What Matters, I argued that the great ethical theories — Kantian deontology, Scanlonian contractualism, and consequentialism — converge on their most defensible formulations at a set of shared conclusions. One of those conclusions is the priority of agent-neutral reasons in the moral domain. Kant’s Categorical Imperative demands that we act only on principles we could will to be universal laws — a fundamentally agent-neutral structure. Scanlon’s contractualism demands that we act only on principles that no one could reasonably reject from a position that gives each person equal standing — again, agent-neutral. The convergence is not accidental. It reflects the fact that morality, as distinct from prudence or personal preference, is essentially a framework for adjudicating among competing claims from a position that does not privilege the claimant. The agent-relative theorist must explain why morality should privilege some claimants by brute positional luck. I have not seen a satisfying answer.

iv. what agent-relative reasons actually are

Category error — agent-relative reasons are prudential or personal, not distinctively moralI want to close with what I believe is the deepest point. Agent-relative reasons are real. I do not deny that I have a stronger reason, of some kind, to comfort my own child than a stranger’s. But the question is what kind of reason this is. My claim is that this reason belongs to the domain of prudence and personal attachment — to the web of commitments, affections, and projects that constitute a life — rather than to morality proper. Morality enters when we ask how those personal attachments should be constrained and balanced against the equal claims of others. At that point, the agent-relative element must be subordinated to an agent-neutral framework, because the distinctive function of morality is to adjudicate from a position that does not privilege any particular first-person perspective. Agent-relative reasons are genuine reasons; they are not genuine moral reasons. The motion is correct.

Thank you.

Argument I — Common-sense morality is collectively self-defeating because it is agent-relative
1.Common-sense morality assigns each agent stronger reasons to benefit those nearby: family, friends, community members. Each agent acts on these agent-relative reasons.
2.When all agents act on agent-relative reasons, the global distribution of care and resources is determined by the morally arbitrary facts of who happens to be related to whom; children of indifferent or poor parents are systematically neglected.
3.This outcome is worse by standards all those agents would themselves endorse from any impartial perspective; the theory tells each to do X and the result of all doing X is worse than alternative distributions.
∴Common-sense morality, being agent-relative, is collectively self-defeating; this structural defect is evidence that it has misidentified the nature of moral reasons rather than correctly captured them.
Argument II — The separateness of persons supports agent-neutrality, not agent-relativity
1.The Rawlsian “separateness of persons” objection holds that impartialism wrongly treats welfare distribution across persons as commensurable with distribution within a single life.
2.But taking persons seriously as separate means recognising that each person’s suffering has equal weight independent of their relationship to the agent; this is a reason for more even attention, not for privileging those contingently proximate to me.
3.The separateness objection is properly an objection to certain aggregative procedures within consequentialism, not a licence for agent-relative discrimination; the opposition runs it backwards.
∴The separateness of persons, correctly understood, motivates agent-neutral concern for each individual’s claims; it does not ground the agent-relative reasons the opposition defends.
Argument III — Kantian, contractualist, and consequentialist theories converge on agent-neutral conclusions
1.Kant’s Categorical Imperative requires acting only on universalisable maxims — an agent-neutral structure. Scanlon’s contractualism requires principles no one could reasonably reject from equal standing — also agent-neutral.
2.This convergence across the three major ethical frameworks on agent-neutral conclusions is not coincidental; it reflects the essential function of morality as a framework adjudicating competing claims from a position that does not privilege the claimant.
3.The agent-relative theorist must explain why this convergent structure of all major moral theories is systematically mistaken; the burden of proof is substantial.
∴The convergence of Kantian, contractualist, and consequentialist theories on agent-neutral conclusions is strong evidence that impartiality is a structural feature of morality, not a doctrinal preference of one tradition.
Argument IV — Agent-relative reasons are genuine but belong to prudence, not morality proper
1.Agent-relative reasons — to help my child, honour my commitments, pursue my projects — are real reasons. The proposition does not deny this. They belong to the domain of prudential and personal attachment.
2.Morality’s distinctive function is to adjudicate among competing first-person claims from a position that does not privilege any particular perspective; this function requires agent-neutral reasoning by definition.
3.When agent-relative commitments conflict with others’ equal claims, morality adjudicates by subordinating the agent-relative to an agent-neutral standard; the agent-relative thus operates within, not alongside, a morally primary agent-neutral framework.
∴Agent-relative reasons are genuine prudential or personal reasons; moral reasons, as such, are agent-neutral because the role of morality is precisely to adjudicate from the impartial position that agent-relative reasons cannot provide.

Chair,

I will make my argument with a thought experiment, because I think this debate is one in which the abstract formulations have run ahead of the concrete cases that motivate them. Suppose you are walking past a shallow pond in which a child is drowning. You can save the child at trivial cost to yourself — ruining your shoes, being late for an appointment. Most people accept, without hesitation, that you are morally required to save the child. Now: the children dying of preventable disease in low-income countries today are equally within your causal reach — a donation of modest size to an effective health intervention will save a child’s life with something close to the same certainty. The opposition’s agent-relative view says you have substantially weaker reasons to save the distant child than the drowning one. I want to press hard on what grounds that asymmetry.

The drowning child — the thought experiment and its generalisation Singer’s original 1972 argument: if it is in our power to prevent something bad from happening without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought to do it. This principle is agent-neutral: it makes no reference to the physical distance, relationship, or proximity of the agent to the person in need. Distance, kinship, and prior relationship are morally arbitrary features — facts about the causal and social structure of the world that bear no independent moral weight.
i. the arbitrariness of the agent-relative boundary

Arbitrariness — distance, kinship, and proximity as morally arbitrary featuresThe agent-relative view holds that I have stronger moral reasons to benefit those near to me — my children, my neighbours, my compatriots — than those far away. But when I press for the principled basis of this hierarchy, what do I find? Distance is a fact about geography, not about the moral worth of persons. Kinship is a fact about biological or social relationship, not about the intrinsic importance of the interests at stake. Nationality is a fact about which state’s bureaucracy issued one’s documents. None of these features bear any direct relationship to what matters morally, which is the degree to which a person’s interests — their suffering, their flourishing, their life — is at stake. The child dying of malaria in a country I will never visit has interests that matter equally, by any criterion that does not simply smuggle in the conclusion. The agent-relative view must either identify what is morally special about proximity and kinship, or admit that it is encoding a bias, not a principle.

ii. the expanding circle and the direction of moral progress

Moral progress — expanding circle as evidence for agent-neutral directionMoral progress, historically, has consistently moved in the direction of expanding the circle of moral consideration and weakening the grip of agent-relative special treatment. We once thought it obvious that we had stronger reasons to protect members of our tribe than strangers. We once thought kinship and caste determined moral standing. We once thought national boundaries settled the question of who mattered. At each stage, moral reflection revealed these boundaries to be morally arbitrary — projections of contingent social fact onto the structure of reasons. The history of moral progress is a history of the gradual triumph of agent-neutral considerations over agent-relative ones. To endorse agent-relative reasons as fundamental is to arrest this progress at the current boundary and declare that the parochialism that remains — family, community, nation — is, unlike those previously abandoned, principled.

iii. the demandingness objection is not an objection to the motion

Demandingness — distinguishing what morality requires from what psychology permitsThe opposition will argue that an agent-neutral moral view is impossibly demanding — that requiring each person to give equal weight to every other person’s interests collapses the personal life entirely and produces a moral theory no one can live by. I accept that this is a serious practical problem. But it is not an objection to the motion. The question is not what people can sustainably do, but what morality genuinely requires. Physics is not refuted by the fact that most of us cannot do quantum mechanics; ethics is not refuted by the fact that most of us cannot achieve full impartiality. The gap between what morality requires and what psychology permits is real, but the correct response is to acknowledge it honestly — not to rewrite moral theory to fit our psychological limitations and call the result a principled framework.

iv. what agent-relative moral theory actually entails

Entailments — agent-relative view licenses morally troubling partialitiesConsider the entailments of a genuinely agent-relative moral view taken seriously. If I have stronger moral reasons to benefit my children than strangers’ children, how much stronger? By what factor? If the answer is “any finite factor,” then at some margin I am morally permitted to let thousands of strangers die to provide a trivial benefit to my child. If the answer is that there is no precise factor — that agent-relative reasons simply “count for more,” without specifying how much more — then the view has no action-guiding content whatsoever. The agent-relative view either licenses monstrous partiality at its margins or dissolves into vagueness. Neither is a satisfactory moral theory.

Argument I — Distance, kinship, and proximity are morally arbitrary; they do not bear on what matters
1.The agent-relative view gives extra moral weight to proximity, kinship, and nationality. But distance is a geographical fact, kinship a biological or social fact, and nationality a bureaucratic one; none bears any direct relationship to the interests at stake.
2.The drowning child and the distant dying child have equal interests, equally at stake; what differs is only a set of facts about the causal and social structure of the world that carry no independent moral weight.
3.The agent-relative view must identify what is morally special about these features; in the absence of such a principled account, it is encoding a bias rather than a moral principle.
∴The morally arbitrary character of the features that agent-relative reasons track — distance, kinship, proximity — is strong evidence that agent-relative reasons are parochial projections rather than genuine moral principles.
Argument II — The history of moral progress is the history of expanding agent-neutral consideration
1.Moral progress has consistently moved by recognising previously accepted agent-relative boundaries — tribe, caste, race, sex — as morally arbitrary and expanding the circle of equal consideration accordingly.
2.At each stage, the boundaries abandoned were defended by those who held them as principled rather than parochial; the same structure now applies to the remaining agent-relative boundaries of family and community.
3.Endorsing agent-relative reasons as fundamental requires claiming that the remaining parochialism — family, community, nation — is, unlike those previously abandoned, genuinely principled; this requires a positive argument the opposition has not provided.
∴The direction of moral progress is the progressive triumph of agent-neutral over agent-relative considerations; endorsing agent-relative reasons at this stage requires a principled account of why this particular boundary is morally real when previous ones were not.
Argument III — Demandingness is evidence of moral truth, not a refutation of it
1.The opposition argues that agent-neutral morality is impossibly demanding and therefore false or inapplicable; but the gap between moral requirement and psychological capacity is not a refutation of the former.
2.We do not revise the laws of physics when they exceed human computational capacity; we do not revise the demands of logic when people cannot follow the argument. There is no principled reason why ethics should be uniquely revised to fit our failures of will.
3.Acknowledging the gap between what morality requires and what psychology permits is honest; rewriting moral theory to eliminate the gap and calling the result a principled framework is evasion.
∴The demandingness of agent-neutral morality is evidence of its truth and our weakness, not evidence of its falsity; accepting demanding moral conclusions and acknowledging our failure to meet them is the appropriate response.
Argument IV — Agent-relative views either license monstrous partiality or dissolve into vagueness
1.If agent-relative reasons give me stronger but finitely stronger reasons for my own: at some margin I am permitted to let many strangers die to provide trivial benefit to mine. If the weighting is infinite: the view is fanatical partiality. Either entailment is morally unacceptable.
2.If the agent-relative weight is unspecified — my reasons are simply “stronger,” by no particular factor — then the view provides no determinate guidance in any case of conflict and has no action-guiding content.
3.A moral theory that either licenses monstrous partiality at its margins or dissolves into indeterminacy at its core has failed as a theory; the dilemma applies to any specification of the agent-relative view.
∴The agent-relative view faces a dilemma: specify the weight and face monstrous entailments, or leave it unspecified and forfeit action-guidance; neither horn is a satisfactory moral theory.

Chair,

I want to make a distinction the opposition has systematically blurred: the distinction between the criterion of moral correctness and the decision procedure an agent should use. My position — which I have called sophisticated consequentialism — holds that the criterion of moral correctness is agent-neutral: an act is right if and only if it produces the best outcomes impartially assessed. But the decision procedure that best reliably produces such outcomes may, and typically will, include strong attachments, personal commitments, and partial concerns that look like agent-relative reasons. The opposition takes appearances for reality. Because moral agents should cultivate partiality as a reliable means to agent-neutral ends, it concludes that partiality is morally fundamental. This is the genetic fallacy applied to ethical psychology.

“The sophisticated consequentialist holds that someone who deliberates about whether to help their drowning friend by calculating impersonal welfare functions has thereby shown that they value their friend too little. The warm, spontaneous, partial response is the right response — and it is the right response because, at the level of criterion, it is what agent-neutral morality recommends. The justification is impartialist even when the motive is not.”
i. the criterion/decision-procedure distinction

Criterion vs decision procedure — the key distinction sophisticated consequentialism drawsConsider the case of a man who, seeing his friend drowning, pauses to calculate whether saving this particular friend produces more aggregate welfare than the alternative uses of his energies. Bernard Williams would say this man has shown that he has “one thought too many” — that his friendship is conditional, and therefore not a genuine friendship at all. Williams intends this as a reductio of agent-neutral morality: if impartialism requires this calculation, it has misunderstood the moral significance of friendship. But Williams’s argument proves less than he thinks. It shows that the decision procedure of explicit impartial calculation is inappropriate in contexts of personal relationship. It does not show that the criterion of rightness is anything other than agent-neutral. The criterion tells us that cultivating genuine friendship, with its partial concern, is what agent-neutral morality recommends — because a world of people with genuine attachments produces better outcomes, and the man who pauses to calculate is producing worse ones.

ii. the alienation objection answered

Alienation — agent-neutral criterion doesn't require agent-neutral motivationIn my 1984 paper, I argued against the objection that consequentialism alienates agents from their personal commitments by requiring them to stand perpetually outside their own projects in moral assessment. The alienation objection assumes that an agent whose fundamental justification is agent-neutral must live with agent-neutral motivations — must experience their love for their child as “the child whose welfare is one unit in the global welfare function.” But this is a non-sequitur. The sophisticated consequentialist holds that the best life, from the standpoint of agent-neutral value, is one in which agents have deep, genuine, partial attachments. The justification is impartialist; the psychology need not be. There is no incoherence in holding that the moral criterion is agent-neutral while the psychological states that best realise that criterion include warmth, partiality, and commitment.

iii. the self-effacement of agent-relative theory

Self-effacement — agent-relative morality requires agent-neutral justification to be plausibleHere is a pressure point for the opposition. When they argue that agent-relative reasons — the reasons of partiality, friendship, and special obligation — are genuine moral reasons, not merely prudential or psychological facts, they owe us an account of why they are moral reasons. That account, I submit, inevitably appeals to agent-neutral considerations. Why does the parent-child relationship generate genuine moral reasons? Because the institution of parent-child attachment produces better outcomes for children than alternative care arrangements. Why does friendship generate genuine moral obligations? Because a world of people with genuine, partial friendships goes better than a world of calculating strangers. The agent-relative theorist cannot justify their agent-relative reasons without appealing to agent-neutral facts about what makes things go well. The agent-neutral justification is foundational; the agent-relative reasons are derived.

iv. moral theory and moral psychology

Moral psychology — the empirical claim about what dispositions best serve agent-neutral valueThe opposition sometimes argues that agent-neutral morality is psychologically unrealistic — that beings like us cannot function as fully impartial reasoners and that a moral theory must be liveable to be genuine. I agree that moral theory must engage with moral psychology. But the lesson I draw is different. We should ask, empirically, which psychological dispositions — which patterns of attachment, partiality, and commitment — in beings like us, in worlds like ours, reliably produce the best outcomes assessed agent-neutrally. This is an empirical question, not a foundational one. The answer will include substantial partiality, rich attachments, and strong personal commitments. But these are recommended by an agent-neutral criterion, not by an agent-relative one. The foundation remains impartialist; the psychology it recommends is happily partial.

Thank you.

Argument I — The criterion/decision-procedure distinction: agent-neutral criterion, agent-relative heuristics
1.Williams’s “one thought too many” objection shows that explicitly impartial deliberation is the wrong decision procedure in contexts of personal relationship; it does not show that the criterion of moral rightness is anything other than agent-neutral.
2.Sophisticated consequentialism holds that cultivating genuine, partial attachments is what agent-neutral morality recommends at the level of criterion, because agents with genuine attachments produce better outcomes than calculators.
3.The distinction between criterion and decision procedure allows agent-neutral foundations to recommend agent-relative psychology without making the agent-relative psychology foundational.
∴Williams’s integrity objection refutes only the naive consequentialist decision procedure, not the agent-neutral criterion; sophisticated consequentialism preserves impartialist foundations while recommending partial psychology.
Argument II — The alienation objection: agent-neutral criterion does not require agent-neutral motivation
1.The alienation objection assumes that an agent whose fundamental moral justification is agent-neutral must live with agent-neutral motivations, experiencing love as welfare-unit calculation.
2.This is a non-sequitur: the best life from the standpoint of agent-neutral value is one with deep, partial attachments; the criterion justifies warmth and commitment even though the criterion itself is impartial.
3.There is no incoherence in holding that the moral criterion is agent-neutral while the psychological states that best realise that criterion are partial and warm; the opposition conflates justification with motivation.
∴Sophisticated consequentialism escapes the alienation objection by distinguishing the agent-neutral criterion from the agent-relative psychology it recommends; the objection only lands against a naive theory that Railton does not hold.
Argument III — Agent-relative theories self-efface: their justification appeals to agent-neutral considerations
1.When the opposition claims agent-relative reasons are genuine moral reasons, they owe an account of why. Any plausible account — why parent-child relationships matter, why friendship generates obligation — appeals to agent-neutral facts about human flourishing and welfare.
2.If the justification of agent-relative reasons is agent-neutral, then agent-neutral considerations are foundational; agent-relative reasons are derived from, not alternatives to, the agent-neutral framework.
3.An agent-relative theory that justifies itself by appeal to agent-neutral foundations is not a genuine alternative to agent-neutral morality; it is a derivative account within it.
∴Agent-relative moral theories self-efface under scrutiny: their own justification reveals that the foundational reasons are agent-neutral; this shows that agent-neutral reasons are primary even by the lights of theories that purport to reject them.
Argument IV — Which dispositions best serve agent-neutral value is an empirical question, not a foundational one
1.The question of which psychological dispositions — which attachments, which patterns of partiality — in beings like us, in worlds like ours, best realise agent-neutral value is empirical, not a question of foundational moral theory.
2.The empirical answer includes substantial partiality, rich attachments, and strong commitments; these are recommended by the agent-neutral criterion even though they are agent-relative in psychological character.
3.Treating the empirical answer — that partial psychology is recommended — as if it were a foundational claim — that agent-relative reasons are foundationally moral — commits the genetic fallacy: confusing what recommends a disposition with the disposition itself as a ground of reasons.
∴The fact that agent-neutral morality recommends partial psychology does not make agent-relative reasons foundationally moral; the foundation remains agent-neutral and the partial psychology is a recommended means, not an alternative foundation.

Chair,

I introduced the terminology that organises this debate, and I want to begin by clarifying what the motion actually asserts. It claims that agent-neutral reasons are the only genuine moral reasons. Note the scope: not that agent-neutral reasons exist — I have always accepted this — but that they exhaust the moral domain. This is a far stronger claim than anything I argued in The Possibility of Altruism, and stronger than anything the argument from impartiality can establish. What I want to show is that agent-relative reasons are genuinely moral reasons — not merely prudential, psychological, or heuristic — and that the proposition’s attempt to relegate them to non-moral domains misunderstands the structure of practical reason.

“The objective standpoint reveals something about what matters; but it does not reveal everything that matters. The fact that my child is suffering is a reason for me to act that is not fully captured by the agent-neutral fact that a child is suffering somewhere. The first-person perspective is not an illusion to be transcended; it is a genuine locus of value that a complete account of practical reason must accommodate.”
i. the dual-standpoint theory of reasons

Dual standpoint — both objective and subjective perspectives generate genuine reasonsIn The View from Nowhere, I argued that the tension between subjective and objective standpoints — between the first-person perspective of an agent embedded in particular relationships and projects, and the impersonal perspective that sees all persons as equally real — is a genuine and irreducible feature of practical reason. It is not to be resolved by declaring one perspective the real one and the other a useful fiction or a mere psychological fact. When I recognise that it is my child in pain, I recognise something the agent-neutral formulation — “a child is in pain” — fails to fully capture. The indexical content of the first-person perspective is not illusory; it is a genuine feature of my situation that generates genuine reasons. The proposition wants to dissolve this first-person content into the impersonal description; but what is dissolved in the process is the agenthood of the agent whose moral reasons we are trying to understand.

ii. the problem with the criterion/decision-procedure move

Criterion/procedure — why the distinction fails to vindicate agent-neutral foundationsRailton argues that agent-neutral reasons are foundational at the level of criterion, while agent-relative psychology is merely recommended as the best means. I want to identify a critical problem with this move. If the agent-neutral criterion is foundational, then the value of my particular attachments — to my child, my friend, my community — is entirely derivative: they matter only because and insofar as they produce better impersonal outcomes. But this means the value of my child to me is fully explicable in terms of the contribution my attachment makes to impersonal welfare. This is not how the value of a child to a parent works, and it is not how parental love that misses this child-specific value is love at all. The criterion/procedure distinction saves the psychological appearances; it does not save the correct account of what the love is a response to. Agent-relative reasons are not heuristics for agent-neutral ends; they are responses to genuine agent-relative value.

iii. what the proposition cannot explain: agent-relative obligations

Special obligations — agent-relative duties that resist agent-neutral reductionConsider the special obligations generated by promises, contracts, and voluntary commitments. If I promise to meet you for lunch and an opportunity arises to produce marginally greater aggregate welfare by breaking the promise, the agent-neutral view seems committed to saying I should break it. But this misunderstands the nature of promissory obligation. When I promise, I create a specific obligation to you — not a general obligation to the world to produce good outcomes that happens to have you as a contingent locus. The obligation is indexed to our particular relationship and the specific act of undertaking. Agent-neutral morality can accommodate this only by treating the disvalue of promise-breaking as an agent-neutral bad — but this seems to get the phenomenology and the normative structure precisely backwards. The promise is a reason because of its particular relational character, not because breaking it has bad impersonal effects.

iv. the asymmetry between promoting and doing harm

Doing/allowing — agent-relative structure of the deontological constraintThe distinction between doing harm and allowing harm — between killing and letting die, between actively injuring and failing to prevent injury — is one of the most deeply embedded features of commonsense morality, reflected in law, in institutional practice, and in the moral intuitions of virtually every population studied. Agent-neutral morality, if it is consistent, must deny this distinction: if the badness of an outcome is agent-neutral, it should not matter whether I am its proximate cause or a passive bystander. Yet the distinction seems not merely to reflect our psychology — it seems to track something morally real about the agent’s specific relationship to an outcome. The agent-relative structure of this doing/allowing asymmetry is evidence that agent-relative reasons are genuinely moral, not merely prudential or heuristic.

Argument I — The dual-standpoint theory: both objective and first-person perspectives generate genuine reasons
1.The first-person perspective of an agent embedded in particular relationships — it is my child, my friend — contains indexical content that the agent-neutral formulation “a child is suffering” does not fully capture.
2.This indexical content is not an illusion; it is a genuine feature of the agent’s situation that generates reasons. Dissolving it into the impersonal description dissolves the agenthood of the agent whose reasons we are examining.
3.The tension between the subjective and objective standpoints is an irreducible feature of practical reason, not to be resolved by declaring one perspective the real one and the other a useful fiction.
∴The first-person perspective generates genuine reasons not captured by agent-neutral descriptions; the proposition’s attempt to dissolve this perspective into the impersonal standpoint misunderstands the structure of practical reason.
Argument II — The criterion/procedure distinction fails to vindicate agent-neutral foundations
1.If agent-neutral reasons are foundational, the value of my particular attachments is entirely derivative: they matter only because my attachment contributes to impersonal welfare. The value of my child to me is explained by the impersonal contribution of parental attachment.
2.But a love that values its object only for its contribution to impersonal welfare is not love of the child; it is love of a welfare-producing relationship. This is not the correct account of what parental love is a response to.
3.Agent-relative reasons are not heuristics for agent-neutral ends; they are responses to genuine agent-relative value — the value of this child, this friend, this relationship — that the criterion/procedure distinction fails to accommodate.
∴Railton’s criterion/procedure distinction saves psychological appearances but misrepresents what agent-relative attachments are responses to; genuine partiality responds to agent-relative value, not to agent-neutral value that happens to be promoted by partiality.
Argument III — Promissory obligations have an essentially agent-relative structure that resists agent-neutral reduction
1.A promise creates a specific obligation to the promisee — not a general obligation to produce good outcomes that happens to have that person as a locus. The obligation is indexed to the particular relationship and the specific act of undertaking.
2.Agent-neutral morality can accommodate promise-keeping only by treating the disvalue of promise-breaking as an agent-neutral bad; but this gets the normative structure backwards: the reason to keep a promise is its particular relational character, not its impersonal effects.
3.If agent-neutral reduction of promissory obligation is incorrect — if what makes promise-breaking wrong is the betrayal of a particular relationship, not impersonal consequences — then agent-relative reasons are irreducible moral reasons.

I want to begin by identifying what I take to be the deepest mistake of the agent-neutral position, and it is a mistake about what morality is for. The agent-neutral view assumes that morality is a system of reasons that operates from a standpoint external to any particular agent — from what Nagel calls “the view from nowhere.” Its fundamental move is to step outside the agent’s particular situation, relationships, and commitments and ask what reasons exist from that impersonal vantage point. “Morality is a system of reasons that addresses agents as they actually are — beings with particular commitments, attachments, and projects that partly constitute their identities. ii. jim and the indians: what the case actually shows

It requires him to treat his deep commitment against killing innocents — a commitment that is not a preference but a constitutive feature of who he is — as simply one input into an impersonal calculation that he must be prepared to override whenever the numbers demand it. The utilitarian says this is obvious; I say the obviousness is the problem. iii. internal and external reasons

Internal reasons — the motivational condition on genuine reasons for actionMy argument about internal and external reasons is directly relevant here. A genuine reason for action must be capable of motivating the agent for whom it is a reason — it must connect with something in that agent’s “motivational set,” their existing commitments, desires, and values. An “external” reason — one that purports to apply to an agent regardless of their actual motivational psychology — cannot explain why that agent should act; it can at most explain what we might say to them or how we might try to change them. The agent-neutral view generates, paradigmatically, external reasons: reasons that apply to all agents regardless of their particular attachments. But external reasons of this kind have a problematic relationship to motivation and action. Moral life is not organised around a foundational kind of reason. My contribution to this debate is more constructive than Williams’s and more moderate than Nagel’s. I want to offer what I believe is the strongest positive argument for why agent-relative reasons are genuine moral reasons — and I want to do so in a way that meets the proposition on its own terms, engaging directly with Parfit’s self-defeat argument and Railton’s criterion/procedure distinction. My argument centres on what I call the “independence of the personal point of view” — the claim that the fact that a cost falls on a particular agent, rather than on some other agent, is a morally significant fact that agent-neutral theories systematically fail to accommodate.

The agent-centred prerogative — what it claims Scheffler’s agent-centred prerogative holds: each agent is morally permitted to assign extra weight to their own interests and projects, relative to the weight assigned in impartial assessments, in deciding what to do. The prerogative is not unlimited; it is proportional. The rationale is not psychological convenience but a principled claim about the moral significance of the personal point of view as a distinct and irreducible standpoint within moral reasoning.
i. the independence of the personal point of view

Personal point of view — a rational strategy, not mere psychologyThe proposition treats the personal point of view — the perspective from which my commitments, interests, and relationships appear with special salience — as a mere psychological fact to be accommodated instrumentally within an agent-neutral framework. I argue that it is more than this: it is a genuinely distinct evaluative standpoint whose independence from the impersonal standpoint provides the rationale for agent-relative permissions. The personal point of view is not an illusion to be overcome or a heuristic to be used and discarded. It is a perspective from which costs and benefits have a different character — from which what is required of me is not the same kind of fact as what is required of agents in general. Giving extra weight to one’s own interests and projects is a rational strategy for taking seriously the fact that one is a particular agent with a particular life, not merely an instance of the abstract type “agent.”

ii. the prerogative is not unlimited: meeting parfit

Prerogative limits — moderate agent-relative view escapes Parfit’s self-defeat objectionParfit’s self-defeat argument targets a version of agent-relative morality that gives agent-relative reasons unlimited priority over agent-neutral ones. My view does not do this. The agent-centred prerogative is proportional: I may assign extra weight to my own interests, but not unlimited weight. At some threshold of cost to others, the prerogative gives way to agent-neutral requirements. This moderate structure escapes the self-defeat problem: it does not require each agent to pursue their own interests and special relations without limit, and it therefore does not generate the collectively disastrous outcomes Parfit describes. Parfit’s reductio assumes that agent-relative morality is maximally self-concerned; but no sophisticated defender of agent-relative reasons holds this view.

iii. the asymmetry thesis: prerogatives without restrictions

Asymmetry — prerogatives are more defensible than restrictions; both exceed agent-neutral moralityOne of my most important findings is what I call the asymmetry between agent-centred prerogatives and agent-centred restrictions. Prerogatives — permissions not to produce the best outcome — have a principled rationale in the independence of the personal point of view. Restrictions — prohibitions on producing the best outcome even when one could — are harder to justify from a similar rationale. This asymmetry matters for the debate because it shows that the agent-relative position is not all-or-nothing. Even if agent-centred restrictions are hard to justify, the prerogative stands: morality does not require agents always to produce the best outcomes, because the costs of doing so fall on particular agents with particular lives, and this distributional fact is morally significant. The proposition must show not merely that some agent-relative claims fail, but that all do — including the prerogative, whose rationale is better established than the restrictions.

iv. what the proposition must deny

What proposition entails — demanding to sacrifice my child's surgery to benefit more strangersLet me press the proposition on a specific case. I have a child who needs surgery that will cost me a significant sum. I could spend that sum on an effective anti-malaria intervention that would save several strangers’ lives. The agent-neutral view — if it is consistent — says I am morally required to save the strangers. The prerogative says I am morally permitted to fund my child’s surgery instead. Now I want to ask: what exactly is wrong with funding my child’s surgery? If the answer is “nothing at all, practically speaking,” then the proposition has conceded that agent-relative reasons are genuine moral reasons that confer genuine permissions. If the answer is “it is morally impermissible to prioritise your child,” then the proposition has a consequence that virtually no one is prepared to accept and that cannot be grounded in any widely shared moral commitment.
