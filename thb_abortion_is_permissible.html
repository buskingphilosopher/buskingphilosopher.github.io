Chair,

I want to begin by making an unusual concession — one that I believe actually strengthens my argument. I am going to grant, for the sake of argument, that the foetus is a person from the moment of conception. The anti-abortion argument typically works hard to establish this premise. I will give it for free. My claim is that abortion is permissible even so. For the argument from foetal personhood to the impermissibility of abortion contains a gap, and that gap is large enough to drive a full philosophical defence of abortion through.

The violinist thought experiment You wake up and find yourself back-to-back in bed with an unconscious, famous violinist who has a fatal kidney ailment. The Society of Music Lovers has kidnapped you and connected your circulatory system to his, so your kidneys are extracting poisons from his blood. You are told: if you are unplugged now, he will die; but in nine months he will have recovered and can safely be unplugged. The violinist has a right to life. But does that right include the right to the use of your body for nine months? Does it follow that you may not unplug yourself?
i. the gap in the standard anti-abortion argument

The gap — right to life does not include right to use another's body; this is the gap the anti-abortion argument overlooksThe standard anti-abortion argument runs: every person has a right to life; the foetus is a person; therefore the foetus has a right to life; therefore abortion is impermissible. But the move from “the foetus has a right to life” to “therefore abortion is impermissible” requires a further premise that is never stated: that the right to life includes the right to use whatever the person needs to stay alive. If a person needs your kidneys to live, does their right to life give them a right to the use of your kidneys? Plainly not. Your right to decide what happens to your body is also a right, and the right to life does not automatically override it. The violinist thought experiment makes this viscerally clear: you would not be unjust in unplugging yourself from the violinist, even though he would die. The right to life, in short, is not a right to be given whatever one needs to sustain life at the expense of another person’s bodily autonomy.

ii. the scope of the argument: rape, responsibility, and the people-seeds

Scope — argument applies clearly to rape; extends with nuance to voluntary pregnancy; the people-seeds case shows even voluntary acts don't grant unlimited use rightsThe violinist case most directly parallels pregnancy from rape: you did not consent to the violinist’s presence, just as a rape victim did not consent to pregnancy. But I want to address voluntary pregnancy too. Suppose you open your windows knowing that people-seeds float through the air and occasionally take root in the upholstery — if one takes root, are you obligated to sustain it indefinitely because you knowingly opened the windows? The anti-abortionist wants to say that voluntary sexual intercourse is like opening the windows: you knew pregnancy could result, so you bear responsibility. But there is a difference between knowing something might happen and inviting it or granting it rights. Someone who takes a reasonable risk has not thereby consented to all consequences of that risk. Even if voluntary pregnancy involves more responsibility than rape-pregnancy, it does not follow that the pregnant person must carry to term in all cases. The question is always whether the extent of the demand is one she could reasonably be required to meet.

iii. good samaritanism and the limits of obligation

Good Samaritanism — morality does not require us to be Good Samaritans; it may require we not be Splendid Samaritans; the law cannot compel heroic sacrificeThere is a distinction between being a morally good person and being morally required to act in a certain way. A minimally decent Samaritan will help someone in minor distress at minimal cost. But even decent morality does not require what I call Splendid Samaritanism — extraordinary sacrifice at great personal cost for the benefit of another. The law famously does not require Good Samaritanism in most jurisdictions: you are not legally required to jump into a freezing river to save a stranger. If the law does not require it, and ordinary morality does not require it, why would we say that a pregnant person is morally required to sustain another person’s life for nine months at cost to her health, freedom, and future prospects? This does not show that such sustained sacrifice is not admirable — it may be supererogatory, morally praiseworthy — but it does show it is not obligatory.

iv. limits of my argument

Limits — Thomson acknowledges her argument has limits; doesn't vindicate all abortions; third trimester cases may differ; she is not claiming abortion is always permissibleI want to be honest about what my argument does not establish. It does not show that all abortions are morally permissible regardless of circumstances. The violinist case involves nine months of inconvenience; it does not directly address cases where the pregnancy is late-term and the foetus is mature. It does not establish that it is permissible to abort a seven-month foetus because you want to take a trip to Europe. The discomfort of pregnancy is not, in some cases, a proportionate reason for termination. My argument establishes that abortion is not simply ruled out by the foetus’s right to life — it establishes that bodily autonomy generates a genuine moral claim that must be weighed. How it is weighed in specific cases is a further question. The anti-abortionist who thinks the weighing always goes the same way — always against abortion — owes us a very different argument from the one they have been giving.

Argument I — The right to life does not include the right to use another person’s body
1.The standard anti-abortion argument infers from “the foetus has a right to life” to “abortion is impermissible.” This requires a premise: that the right to life includes the right to whatever one needs to stay alive, including use of another’s body.
2.The violinist case shows this premise is false: the violinist has a right to life but not to the use of your kidneys; you may unplug yourself without violating his rights.
3.The right to life is a right not to be killed unjustly, not a right to be given whatever one needs to sustain life at another’s bodily expense.
∴The standard anti-abortion argument has a gap: the foetus’s right to life does not entail the right to use the pregnant person’s body; the inference from personhood to impermissibility fails without this premise, and the premise is false.
Argument II — Voluntary pregnancy does not grant unlimited use rights; the people-seeds case
1.If you knowingly open windows through which people-seeds float and one takes root in your upholstery, you knew this could happen — but you have not granted it a right to remain. Knowing a risk does not constitute consenting to all its consequences.
2.Similarly, voluntary sexual intercourse knowingly risking pregnancy does not constitute consent to full gestation; at most it shifts the moral calculus, but does not eliminate the pregnant person’s bodily autonomy claim.
3.Even if voluntary pregnancy generates more responsibility than rape-pregnancy, the question remains whether the demand is one the pregnant person can reasonably be required to meet — a question of proportionality, not simply of causation.
∴Voluntary pregnancy does not grant the foetus an unlimited right to bodily support; the people-seeds case shows that knowingly risking an outcome does not constitute consenting to sustain it indefinitely.
Argument III — Good Samaritanism is not morally required; law and ordinary morality do not demand heroic sacrifice
1.Neither law nor ordinary morality requires Splendid Samaritanism — extraordinary, costly sacrifice for another’s life. You are not required to jump into a freezing river to save a stranger.
2.If extraordinary bodily sacrifice for strangers is not morally required in other contexts, it requires argument to establish that nine months of bodily use for a foetus is uniquely required.
3.Sustaining a foetus for nine months at cost to health, freedom, and future prospects is at minimum a very substantial sacrifice; the question of whether it is obligatory, praiseworthy, or supererogatory must be settled by argument, not assumed.
∴Bodily sacrifice for another’s survival is not generally morally required; establishing that pregnancy is uniquely obligatory requires a positive argument that the standard anti-abortion case has not provided.
Argument IV — Thomson’s limits: the argument does not vindicate all abortions; proportionality matters
1.Thomson explicitly does not claim all abortions are permissible; late-term abortions of viable foetuses for minor inconvenience may not be vindicated by her argument; proportionality between the foetus’s moral status and the reason for abortion matters.
2.The argument establishes that bodily autonomy is a genuine moral claim that must be weighed against foetal interests; it does not determine how the weighing comes out in every case.
3.What it does establish is that the anti-abortionist cannot simply point to foetal personhood and declare the case closed; a genuine weighing is required, and this is a significant philosophical achievement.
∴Thomson’s argument shows that bodily autonomy is a genuine moral counter-weight to foetal interests; it does not vindicate all abortions but shows that foetal personhood alone does not settle the debate, and that a weighing is always required.

Chair,

Thomson’s argument is powerful but concedes too much. She grants, for the sake of argument, that the foetus is a person. I want to show that this concession is not necessary, and that attending carefully to what personhood actually requires reveals that the foetus does not qualify. More than this: it reveals that the anti-abortionist’s key move — from “human being” to “person” to “full moral rights” — contains a serious equivocation that, once identified, collapses the anti-abortion argument before it gets started.

“The moral community consists of all and only people, rather than all and only human beings. It is persons, and not genetic humans, who are protected by the right to life. The question of the foetus’s right to life is not resolved by establishing that it is human in the genetic sense — a question about biology — but by establishing whether it is a person in the moral sense.” — Warren, The Monist (1973)
i. the genetic/moral equivocation

Genetic equivocation — 'human being' means two things: genetic human and moral person; the anti-abortionist conflates themThe anti-abortionist’s argument turns on the word “human.” They argue: the foetus is a human being; human beings have a right to life; therefore the foetus has a right to life. But “human being” is ambiguous between two senses. In the genetic sense, it means a member of the biological species Homo sapiens. In the moral sense, it means a person — a member of the moral community, a being with full moral rights. The foetus is unquestionably human in the genetic sense; its DNA is human, it is of human origin. But being genetically human is neither necessary nor sufficient for being a moral person: there could be non-human persons (sufficiently advanced artificial intelligences, extraterrestrials) and there are human beings who are not persons (those in permanent vegetative states with no remaining consciousness). The anti-abortionist’s argument equivocates: the first premise is true in the genetic sense, the second is true in the moral sense, and the conclusion requires that they be the same sense — which they are not.

ii. the five criteria for personhood

Five criteria — consciousness, reasoning, self-motivated activity, capacity to communicate, self-awareness; fetus satisfies none; fish satisfy moreI suggest that the traits most central to personhood — membership in the moral community — are roughly these five: (1) consciousness, and in particular the capacity to feel pain; (2) reasoning, the developed capacity to solve complex problems; (3) self-motivated activity, relatively independent of genetic or external control; (4) the capacity to communicate messages of an indefinite variety of types and on indefinitely many topics; (5) self-concepts and self-awareness. Not all five are necessary for personhood — a being that satisfies none is certainly not a person; a being that satisfies (1) and (2) probably is. A first-trimester foetus satisfies none of these criteria. It has no consciousness, no reasoning, no self-awareness, no capacity for communication. A mature fish satisfies criterion (1) more clearly than an early-stage foetus does. The claim that a foetus is a person is simply not supported by any examination of what personhood actually requires.

iii. the potential personhood objection

Potential persons — potential personhood cannot override actual personhood; the right of actual persons over potential ones is analogous to self-defenceThe anti-abortionist has a reply: even if the foetus is not currently a person, it has the potential to become one. Potential persons, on this view, have rights that must be taken into account. I deny this. Potential persons have, at most, potential rights — and potential rights cannot override actual rights of actual persons. Consider the analogy: I am not permitted to murder someone on the grounds that it would produce many potential persons who would not otherwise exist. The rights of actual people override the interests of potential people. The pregnant person is an actual person with actual rights of bodily autonomy; the foetus is at most a potential person. The actual person’s rights take precedence. Moreover, if potential personhood generated full moral rights, it would be difficult to explain why contraception is permissible — sperm and egg, combined in conception, would produce a person, so preventing conception might seem equally impermissible. The potentiality argument proves far too much.

iv. the infanticide problem and warren’s postscript

Infanticide — Warren acknowledged her criteria seem to permit infanticide; she added a postscript arguing that societal interests in adoption prevent this conclusionI want to be honest about the most pressing objection to my account: if the foetus is not a person because it lacks my five criteria, then neither is a newborn infant, who is also unconscious of moral concepts, lacks reasoning, and lacks full self-awareness. My criteria seem to permit infanticide. In a postscript to my original paper, I argued that this conclusion does not follow because there are independent grounds against infanticide even if infants are not full persons. Society has a strong interest in protecting infants — both because of the affection that exists for them and because of the adoption alternative that means infants who are unwanted by their biological mothers are wanted by others. These practical considerations prevent the infanticide conclusion without retreating from my account of personhood. This is admittedly less satisfying than a unified theoretical account, and critics have been right to press me on it. But the infanticide objection does not restore the foetus to personhood; it identifies a limitation of the criteria, not a refutation of their application to the foetal case.

Argument I — The genetic/moral equivocation: the anti-abortionist conflates two senses of “human”
1.The anti-abortion argument moves from “the foetus is a human being” to “the foetus has a right to life.” But “human being” is ambiguous: genetic member of Homo sapiens (biological), vs. person with full moral rights (moral).
2.The foetus is unquestionably human in the genetic sense. But genetic humanity is neither necessary nor sufficient for moral personhood: there may be non-human persons, and there are human beings (PVS patients) who are not persons.
3.The anti-abortionist’s argument equivocates: “the foetus is human” (true in the biological sense) plus “humans have rights” (true in the moral sense) does not yield a valid inference because the two uses of “human” are different.
∴The anti-abortion argument rests on an equivocation between genetic and moral senses of “human being”; this equivocation collapses the argument before it reaches the question of bodily autonomy.
Argument II — Five criteria for personhood: the foetus satisfies none; fish satisfy more
1.Personhood requires roughly: (1) consciousness and capacity for pain; (2) reasoning; (3) self-motivated activity; (4) communicative capacity; (5) self-concepts and self-awareness. A being satisfying none of these is certainly not a person.
2.A first-trimester foetus satisfies none of these criteria: no consciousness, no reasoning, no self-awareness, no communicative capacity. A mature fish satisfies criterion (1) more fully than an early-stage foetus.
3.If the criteria are correct, the early-stage foetus is not a person and lacks full moral rights, regardless of its genetic humanity; the central question of the abortion debate is answered by examining what personhood actually requires.
∴The five criteria for personhood show that the early-stage foetus is not a person: it satisfies none of them; the anti-abortion conclusion therefore cannot be derived from the premise of foetal personhood, because that premise is false.
Argument III — Potential personhood cannot override actual personhood
1.Even if the foetus has the potential to become a person, potential rights are not actual rights; an entity that may become a person does not thereby have the rights of a person now.
2.Actual persons’ rights take precedence over potential persons’ interests: we do not permit murder to produce potential persons, nor are we required to sacrifice actual persons’ rights to benefit potential ones.
3.The potentiality argument, applied consistently, would make contraception impermissible (sperm and egg together would produce a person); the argument must be limited in some principled way, and no such limit has been provided that saves the foetus while excluding sperm-egg pairs.
∴Potential personhood generates at most potential rights, which cannot override the actual rights of actual persons; the potentiality argument either proves too much (extending to contraception) or is arbitrary in its scope.
Argument IV — The infanticide objection: Warren’s postscript and its limits
1.Warren’s five criteria seem to imply that newborn infants are not persons (they lack reasoning, self-awareness, and communicative capacity); this appears to permit infanticide, an unwelcome conclusion.
2.Warren’s postscript argues that independent practical considerations — societal affection for infants, the adoption alternative — prevent the infanticide conclusion even if infants are not full persons by her criteria.
3.This postscript is less theoretically unified than the main account but does not restore the foetus to personhood; it identifies a limit of the criteria without showing they misidentify the foetus’s moral status.
∴The infanticide objection to Warren’s account is genuinely difficult; her postscript addresses it practically rather than theoretically; but it does not refute the application of the personhood criteria to the foetal case.

Chair,

Thomson opened the right door but did not go far enough through it. She showed that even granting foetal personhood, bodily autonomy generates a powerful moral claim. I want to do three things: extend her argument to cover voluntary pregnancy more rigorously than she did; explore when killing and letting die are morally equivalent in the abortion context; and identify more precisely when late-term abortions reach a threshold where the permissibility argument has limits. My approach is what I call non-consequentialist: it attends to the structure of moral relationships, not merely to outcomes.

“If the foetus that is in her body is killed in an abortion, the killing would take from it only life it is getting from the use of the woman’s body, a use it had no right to have even to save its life since it could permissibly have been left to die. This killing occurs for the purpose of ending the use. This combination of factors makes it permissible for the woman or a doctor to kill the foetus even if the foetus is assumed to be a person.” — Kamm, The Philosophers’ Magazine (2023)
i. extending thomson: the voluntary pregnancy case

Voluntary pregnancy — Kamm uses a 'benefit-burden' approach; being responsible for the foetus's existence creates obligations but not unlimited onesThomson’s violinist case involves kidnapping — clear non-consent. Critics argue that voluntary pregnancy is relevantly different: you are responsible for the foetus’s existence. I take this objection seriously. Being responsible for bringing a person into a situation of dependence does generate some obligations that a kidnapping victim does not have. If I negligently cause a car accident that leaves someone paralysed and dependent on my care, I have obligations I would not otherwise have. So too, voluntary pregnancy generates some obligations. But the question is the extent and nature of those obligations. Even in the car accident case, the victim does not thereby acquire the right to use my body indefinitely; their right against me is a right to certain care and compensation, not a right to unlimited bodily support. The same asymmetry applies in pregnancy: responsibility for conception generates real obligations but does not give the foetus the right to nine months of bodily use against the pregnant person’s will.

ii. when killing equals letting die: the moral structure of abortion

Killing equals letting die — when what is killed would have only lived from the use of another's body it had no right to, killing and letting die are morally equivalentThere is ordinarily a serious moral difference between killing and letting die. Actively causing someone’s death is generally worse than failing to prevent it. But this difference disappears under specific structural conditions. Suppose you could permissibly let the foetus die by not sustaining it — as Thomson’s violinist case establishes. If what the foetus would lose by being killed is only the life it is getting from bodily support it has no right to, then killing it to end that use is not substantially different from letting it die by withdrawing the support. The killing takes from it only what it was getting from a use of the pregnant person’s body that was not its right. In this specific structural context — where the life at stake is entirely dependent on unconsented bodily use — the killing/letting die distinction largely collapses. This is why abortion, even if active termination, is not morally equivalent to murder in other contexts.

iii. the innocent threat framework

Innocent threat — the foetus, though not an aggressor, can be seen as an innocent threat to bodily autonomy; one may use force against innocent threats under certain conditionsMy most distinctive contribution is the innocent threat framework. The foetus is not a willing aggressor — it has no intentions at all. But through no fault of its own, it threatens a woman’s bodily autonomy, and possibly her life or health. There is a general principle in non-consequentialist ethics: one may use force, even lethal force, against an innocent threat — a being that poses a threat without moral responsibility for doing so. The classic case is a person being used as a human shield: the shield is innocent, but if the only way to stop the threat is to shoot through the shield, this may be permissible. Applied to pregnancy: the foetus is an innocent threat to bodily autonomy, and the use of force to end the threat — through abortion — may be permissible even though the foetus is not at fault. This framework, unlike Thomson’s, does not rely entirely on what the pregnant person consented to; it relies on the structure of the threat itself.

iv. viability and the limits of the argument

Viability — once fetus can survive outside the womb, it is no longer permissible to kill it; the argument for abortion becomes an argument for removal, not terminationThe argument I have developed has a principled limit that Thomson’s does not clearly articulate. Once the foetus can survive outside the womb — even with mechanical support — the pregnant person’s right is to end the use of her body, not necessarily to have the foetus killed. At viability, what the pregnant person is owed is removal of the foetus from her body; killing it in the process is an additional act whose justification becomes harder to sustain. The innocent threat argument does not entail the right to kill an innocent threat when the threat can be ended by other means. This gives viability genuine moral significance: it marks the point at which the permissibility of abortion (understood as termination of life) diverges from the permissibility of ending the bodily relationship. After viability, the right is to removal; the right to the foetus’s death is not automatically entailed.

Argument I — Voluntary pregnancy generates limited obligations; responsibility does not grant unlimited bodily use rights
1.Being responsible for bringing a person into a situation of dependence generates some obligations; voluntary pregnancy creates more obligations than rape-pregnancy.
2.But even where responsibility is clear (a car accident that paralyses someone), the victim does not thereby acquire unlimited rights over the body of the responsible party; responsibility generates specific obligations, not unlimited bodily access.
3.By analogy, voluntary pregnancy generates real obligations to the foetus but does not grant it the right to nine months of bodily use against the pregnant person’s will; the extent of obligation is limited by proportionality.
∴Voluntary pregnancy generates genuine obligations that modify the moral situation compared to rape-pregnancy, but it does not grant unlimited bodily access; the permissibility argument survives this objection with appropriate modification.
Argument II — Killing equals letting die when what is killed would only have lived from unconsented bodily use
1.Ordinarily, killing is worse than letting die. But this distinction collapses when the life at stake is entirely dependent on bodily support the subject has no right to.
2.If the foetus could permissibly be left to die (by withdrawing bodily support it has no right to), and killing it takes from it only the life it would have gotten from that unconsented use, then killing is not substantially worse than letting die in this context.
3.This structural analysis explains why abortion, though active, is not morally equivalent to murder: it removes only what the foetus was receiving without right, not a life it had an independent claim to.
∴In the abortion context, the killing/letting die distinction largely collapses because the foetus’s life is entirely dependent on bodily use it has no right to; abortion therefore lacks the moral gravity of murder even when the foetus is granted personhood.
Argument III — The innocent threat framework: force may be used against innocent threats under structural conditions
1.One may use force against an innocent threat — a being that poses a genuine threat without moral responsibility for doing so; the human shield case illustrates this.
2.The foetus, through no fault of its own, threatens the pregnant person’s bodily autonomy (and possibly life or health); it is an innocent threat in the morally relevant sense.
3.The innocent threat framework grounds the permissibility of abortion structurally — through the nature of the threat — rather than through consent; it therefore applies more broadly than Thomson’s account to cases of voluntary pregnancy.
∴The foetus is an innocent threat to bodily autonomy; force may be used against innocent threats under the appropriate structural conditions; abortion is therefore permissible under the innocent threat framework even in voluntary pregnancy.
Argument IV — Viability marks the limit: the right is to removal, not necessarily to the foetus’s death
1.Once the foetus can survive outside the womb, what the pregnant person is owed is the termination of the bodily relationship — removal — not necessarily the death of the foetus.
2.The innocent threat argument entails the right to end the threat by appropriate means; if the threat can be ended by removal, killing the foetus to end it is harder to justify after viability.
3.Viability therefore marks a principled moral threshold: before it, removal and termination are unavoidably the same act; after it, they diverge and the right to termination specifically requires additional justification.
∴Viability marks a principled moral threshold where the right to abortion as bodily removal diverges from the right to foetal death; after viability, the former survives the argument but the latter requires additional justification not automatically provided.

Chair,

The debate about abortion has been stalled, on both sides, by a single question: is the foetus a person? Pro-choice philosophers say no, and conclude abortion is permissible. Anti-abortion philosophers say yes, and conclude it is impermissible. Both sides have been unable to resolve this question to the satisfaction of the other, partly because “person” is a contested philosophical concept and partly because the debate has become entrenched. My strategy is to bypass this question entirely. I want to show that abortion is immoral without establishing that the foetus is a person, by attending to what makes killing wrong in the clearest cases we already agree about.

“The loss of one’s life is one of the greatest losses one can suffer. The loss of one’s life deprives one of all the experiences, activities, projects, and enjoyments that would otherwise have constituted one’s future. Therefore, killing someone is wrong, primarily because the killing inflicts one of the greatest possible losses on the victim. The future of a standard foetus includes a set of experiences, projects, activities, and such which are identical with the futures of young children and adults who are killed — and killing such children and adults is paradigmatically wrong.” — Marquis, Journal of Philosophy (1989)
i. the deprivation account of the wrongness of killing

Deprivation account — killing is wrong because it deprives the victim of their future goods; this applies to fetuses by the same logicWhy is it wrong to kill a normal adult human being? Not primarily because they are a person, or because they have current desires, or because they are conscious — these are symptoms rather than the fundamental reason. The fundamental reason is that killing deprives the victim of the value of their future: the experiences, activities, projects, and enjoyments they would have had. Death is a misfortune for the victim precisely because it eliminates all the goods that their life would otherwise have contained. Now consider the foetus. A standard foetus has a future that includes a set of experiences, projects, activities, and enjoyments identical in character — though not in content — to the futures of the children and adults we all agree it is wrong to kill. If what makes killing adults wrong is the deprivation of their valuable futures, and the foetus has exactly such a valuable future, then killing the foetus is wrong for the same reason. The argument does not depend on whether the foetus is currently a person; it depends on whether it has a future like ours — and it does.

ii. the contraception objection

Contraception objection — if FLO grounds the wrongness of killing, contraception also deprives a potential being of a future like ours; Marquis's reply distinguishes subjects of deprivationThe most serious objection to my account is the contraception objection: if depriving a being of a future-like-ours (FLO) is what makes killing wrong, and if contraception prevents a future-like-ours from existing, then contraception is as wrong as killing. This conclusion is clearly unacceptable. My reply is that the FLO argument requires that there be a subject who is deprived — an actual entity that would have had the future. In the case of contraception, no such subject exists at the time of the contraceptive act: there is no entity — neither sperm alone nor egg alone — that has a future-like-ours that is taken from it. The sperm has no individual future-like-ours; neither does the egg. Only at conception does an entity with an individual future-like-ours come into existence. From that point, killing deprives an existing individual of its future. Contraception prevents that individual from existing at all — a different act with a different moral structure.

iii. the sentience objection: why desire and consciousness do not fix the threshold

Sentience objection — consciousness or desire cannot be the criterion; we think killing unconscious or non-desiring humans is wrong tooSome critics argue that what makes killing wrong is that it frustrates the victim’s desires, or that it eliminates a conscious subject. Since foetuses lack desires and consciousness, killing them is not wrong. But notice what this account entails. A temporarily unconscious human being has no current desires or consciousness. Is it permissible to kill them? Clearly not. A human being in dreamless sleep lacks occurrent desires and conscious experience. Is it permissible to kill them while they sleep? Again, clearly not. What we care about in these cases is not the current psychological states of the victim but their future: what they will experience, value, and do. It is this future that is eliminated by killing, and this is what the FLO account correctly identifies. The sentience and desire criteria fail precisely because they cannot account for our clear intuitions about killing sleeping or temporarily unconscious people.

iv. engaging thomson: why the right to life is not the only relevant consideration

Against Thomson — FLO grounds a stronger claim than 'right to life'; it is about what the victim loses, which generates an obligation that is harder to override by bodily autonomyThomson grants the foetus a right to life and then argues that this right is overridden by bodily autonomy. My account generates a different and stronger anti-abortion argument than the simple right-to-life. What is at stake is not merely that the foetus has a right — it is that killing the foetus deprives it of all the goods of its future life: a loss comparable in gravity to the loss suffered by a murdered adult. My approach is different from Marquis’s but complementary. I want to argue that the concept of personhood is being applied inconsistently by those who seek to exclude the foetus from the moral community. Any criterion proposed for the relevant threshold — viability, sentience, birth, self-awareness — either applies too narrowly, excluding beings we clearly think have rights, or applies too broadly, including beings we do not think have rights. The probability argument — Noonan’s key move Once a being is conceived, it has approximately an 80% chance of developing into a fully-formed human adult who will participate in human experience. A sperm alone has perhaps a 1-in-200-million chance of producing such a human being; an egg alone has a similar probability. The probability shift at conception — from tiny chance to near-certainty — is not merely quantitative but marks the coming-into-being of a being for whom the full human future is genuinely in prospect. The right to life, on Noonan’s view, attaches at this probability shift.
i. the arbitrariness of alternative thresholds

Arbitrary thresholds — every proposed criterion other than conception either over- or under-includes; conception alone is non-arbitraryConsider the main alternative thresholds proposed for the beginning of morally significant life. Viability: the ability to survive outside the womb. But viability depends on the state of medical technology, not on anything intrinsic to the foetus; it varies by decade and by country. A foetus that is not viable in 1950 becomes viable with 1990 technology; surely its moral status does not depend on which decade it happened to be conceived in. Sentience: the capacity to feel pain. But if sentience is the criterion, newborn infants who lack developed neural pain systems would have uncertain status, and we would need to deny rights to permanently anaesthetised humans. Self-awareness: the basis of Warren’s account. But this excludes infants, the severely mentally disabled, and those in deep sleep. Each threshold either excludes beings we are certain have moral status, or is arbitrary in its placement along the developmental continuum. Conception is the only non-arbitrary threshold: it marks the coming-into-being of a new, genetically distinct human organism.

ii. the continuum argument

Continuum — development is continuous from conception; any threshold along the continuum is arbitrary; must start at the beginningHuman development from conception to adulthood is a biological continuum with no sharp discontinuity that could serve as a non-arbitrary threshold for moral status. The only point at which there is a genuine discontinuity — the only point at which something new comes into existence — is conception, when a new genetically distinct organism is formed. Drawing a line later than this requires explaining why that particular point, and not the earlier one, marks the morally relevant transition; and no convincing explanation has been offered that does not smuggle in the conclusion.

Experience objection — some argue only those who can experience social relationships merit rights; Noonan shows this proves too much and misidentifies what grounds rightsSome philosophers argue that membership in the human community — the capacity for social relationships and shared experience — is what grounds rights. On this view, the foetus lacks rights because it has no social relationships, no experience, no participation in shared life. But this argument proves too much. A human being abandoned at birth on a desert island, with no social relationships and no experience of shared life, would still have rights. An isolated hermit has rights. Rights are not conferred by participation in social life; they belong to beings of a certain kind, and human beings belong to that kind. Moreover, the experience criterion would strip rights from the severely isolated, the severely mentally impaired, and the comatose. It locates moral status in something that can be lost — social participation — rather than in something intrinsic to the organism. I want to offer the most philosophically rigorous anti-abortion argument available: what we call the substance view. Our position proceeds from a metaphysical analysis of what kind of entity the embryo or foetus is, and then derives conclusions about its moral status from that analysis. We believe this approach cuts deeper than either the personhood debate or the rights-weighing debate, because it locates the question of moral status in the ontological category to which the embryo belongs, rather than in its current psychological properties or current social relationships.

“The embryo is a whole, distinct, and living human organism. It differs from a sperm cell or an egg cell, which are not whole organisms but rather parts of organisms. It differs from a skin cell or a liver cell for the same reason. The embryo is a complete human organism at the earliest stage of its development. To deny it full moral status on the grounds of its current developmental stage is to confuse what it is with what stage of development it is currently at.” — Lee & George, The Wrong of Abortion (2005)
i. the substance view: what the embryo is

Substance view — the embryo is a complete human organism; its kind, not its current properties, determines its moral statusThe substance view holds that the kind of entity something is determines its moral status, not its current accidental properties. A human embryo from the moment of conception is a whole, distinct, living human organism — a member of the human species at the earliest stage of its development. It is not a part of another organism (as a sperm or egg is); it is not a collection of cells without organisational unity (as a tumour is). It is a self-directing, actively developing whole organism that is directing its own development from within, in accordance with its nature as the kind of thing it is. This organism is identical with the human adult it will become: the same individual persists from conception through all developmental stages. If that adult human being has full moral status — and we all agree it does — then the embryo has full moral status, because the embryo and the adult are the same individual.

ii. against body-self dualism: what you are, not what you currently experience

Body-self dualism — Warren's view implies a dualism between the person and the body; the substance view rejects this; you are your bodyWarren’s five criteria for personhood — consciousness, reasoning, self-awareness — all locate personhood in psychological properties. This implicitly commits her to a kind of body-self dualism: the entity with moral status is the psychological subject, and the body is merely its vehicle. But this dualism is deeply problematic. You are not a psychological subject that happens to inhabit a body; you are a bodily organism of a particular kind. When you sleep, your psychological properties are suspended; you are not thereby a different entity or an entity with diminished rights. What you are — a human organism — is what you are throughout your existence. Your moral status derives from the kind of entity you are, not from the psychological properties you happen to exhibit at any given moment. This means that from the moment the human organism comes into existence, it has the moral status appropriate to human organisms.

iii. the active/passive potentiality distinction

Active vs passive potentiality — the embryo has an active intrinsic potentiality for rationality; sperm/egg have only passive potentiality; this is the morally relevant distinctionWarren argues that potential persons have at most potential rights — and since a sperm or egg is equally a potential person, the potentiality argument proves too much. This is a good objection against crude potentiality arguments. But the substance view avoids this objection by distinguishing active from passive potentiality. The embryo has an active intrinsic potentiality for rationality, self-awareness, and all the other features of full human development: it is already the kind of entity that will develop these features if not prevented from doing so, because that development is directed from within by its own nature. A sperm cell has only a passive potentiality: it requires transformation by fusion with an egg before it has any intrinsic developmental trajectory. The sperm is not a potential human being in the morally relevant sense because it is not currently directing itself toward human development. The embryo is. This active/passive distinction is not arbitrary; it tracks a real difference in the ontological status of the entities.

But this presupposes a dualistic picture in which the self and the body are separate — the body is the self’s property, an instrument the self controls. The substance view rejects this: you are not a self that has a body; you are a bodily organism. On this view, the pregnant person’s body is not simply her property; it is constitutive of who she is. And the foetus is also a bodily organism with full human status. The question is not whether one organism’s property rights over a body override another organism’s right to life; it is how two distinct human organisms with full moral status — mother and child — share the same physical space and what follows from that for the ethics of the relationship.
