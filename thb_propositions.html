Logical Arguments

Mr President,

The question before this House is not merely technical. It is the foundational question of logic, of epistemology, and of the philosophy of language taken together. What is a proposition? And the first thing to be said is what it is not. It is not a sentence — sentences are signs, marks in a particular language, and the same thought can be expressed in German, in English, in any language or in none. It is not a mental image or an act of thinking — mental events are private, variable, and causally efficacious in ways that the bearer of truth can never be. Two people thinking the same thought have not produced the same mental event; they have apprehended the same thought. That distinction between apprehending and producing is everything.

"The thought, in itself immaterial, clothes itself in the material garment of a sentence and thereby becomes comprehensible to us. We say that the same thought is clothed in different garments by different peoples. And the thought itself neither belongs to my inner world as an idea, nor to the outer world of perceivable things. It belongs to a third realm."
i. the argument from objectivity — against psychologism

Anti-psychologism — thoughts are not mental; objectivity requires the Third RealmIf propositions were mental entities — ideas, representations, acts of judging — then the truth of a mathematical theorem would depend upon the existence of a mind that entertains it. The Pythagorean theorem would have become true when Pythagoras first proved it, and would cease to be true if all minds were destroyed. This is absurd. The Pythagorean theorem was true before Pythagoras was born; it will be true after the last mind is extinguished. This timelessness is the mark of a thought, and it cannot be the mark of anything mental, which exists in time, has causal effects, and belongs to the inner world of a particular subject. Ideas are private; thoughts are public. Ideas can be neither true nor false; thoughts are essentially truth-evaluable. The psychologistic position conflates the act of thinking with the content thought — but these are as different as the grasping of a hammer is from the hammer itself. The objectivity of logic, of mathematics, of any science demands an objective category of truth-bearer. The Third Realm is not an extravagance; it is a necessity.

ii. sense and reference — the solution to Frege's puzzle

Sense-reference distinction — informativeness of identity; cognitive value of thoughtsConsider the sentence "Hesperus is Phosphorus." The planet Venus appears in the evening sky under the name "Hesperus" and in the morning sky under the name "Phosphorus." Ancient astronomers discovered — it was a discovery, a genuine extension of knowledge — that these are the same heavenly body. Now: what is the content of this discovery? If signs contribute only their referents to propositions, as my opponents on the opposition's second bench maintain, then "Hesperus is Phosphorus" expresses the same proposition as "Hesperus is Hesperus" — for both refer to Venus, and both express the self-identity of Venus. But "Hesperus is Hesperus" is trivially a priori, known without investigation; "Hesperus is Phosphorus" required empirical astronomy. They have different cognitive values. This difference in cognitive value is explained by the difference in sense: the mode of presentation by which Venus is given to thought under "Hesperus" is different from the mode by which it is given under "Phosphorus." Propositions are structured by senses, not by referents. This is why identity can be informative. No account that places mere objects — the referents of names — into the proposition can explain why the discovery of Venus's identity was a discovery at all.

iii. the compositionality of sense

Compositionality — sense of sentence composed from senses of parts; function-argument structureA proposition is not a mere collection of parts. Its parts — the senses of the component expressions — are unified by the function-argument structure that logic makes explicit. Concept-expressions are essentially unsaturated: they contain a gap that must be filled by an object-expression to yield a complete thought. The sense of "is a planet" is an incomplete entity, predicative in character, which reaches out to be completed by a singular sense. The resulting thought is a determinate entity with a truth value — either the True or the False — depending on whether the object falls under the concept. This function-argument architecture, which I introduced into logic, is the deepest account of propositional unity available. The thought's unity is not a mystery — it arises from the saturated/unsaturated distinction between its logical parts, which is itself a structural feature of the Third Realm, not a feature of any human cognitive act.

iv. the grasp relation — how minds access the Third Realm

Grasping — non-causal cognitive relation to abstract object; not creation but apprehensionThe natural objection to the Third Realm is: how do we access it? If thoughts are abstract, causally inert, outside space and time, what is the nature of our cognitive contact with them? I acknowledge this is the most difficult question my account faces. My answer is that the relation of grasping a thought is sui generis — a distinctive cognitive relation that is not reducible to ordinary causal perception. When a mathematician proves a theorem, something in her consciousness is aimed at the thought; but this aiming is not itself the thought, which exists independently of whether it is ever grasped. The difficulty of explaining grasping is not a reason to deny the Third Realm — it is a reason to take the epistemology of abstract objects seriously as a philosophical problem in its own right. The alternative — locating truth-bearers in minds — generates absurdities far worse than any difficulty attending the grasp relation.

v. against the opposition's alternatives

Against Russell and Quine — false propositions, individuation, eliminativism all failThe opposition offers two alternatives. Russell places the objects themselves — the actual referents — into the proposition. This generates the problem of false propositions: if a proposition about Pegasus contains Pegasus, and Pegasus does not exist, what does the false proposition contain? Nothing, it seems — which means it is no proposition at all. Russell's attempt to solve this through his multiple relations theory of judgment, eliminating propositions in favour of a direct relation between a mind and the constituents, fails decisively: as his own student Wittgenstein observed, that account cannot explain what makes a judgment say something rather than merely listing objects. You can have a mind, Desdemona, loving, and Cassio as your relata without having the judgment that Desdemona loves Cassio — you need the proposition, and the multiple relations theory has no way to generate it. As for Quine's eliminativism: the demand for identity conditions is a methodological preference dressed as a necessary constraint. We can individuate thoughts perfectly well — two sentences express the same thought if and only if a rational speaker who understands both cannot coherently accept one and reject the other. That is a demanding criterion, but a criterion nonetheless.

Argument I — Anti-psychologism: the objectivity of truth requires the Third Realm
1.Mathematical and logical truths are timelessly true — true before any mind entertains them, true if all minds are destroyed.
2.Mental entities exist in time, are causally efficacious, belong to particular subjects, and cannot be true or false in themselves; they cannot play the role of timeless truth-bearers.
3.The objectivity of logic demands an objective category of truth-bearer — entities that are neither mental nor physical but abstract: the Third Realm of thoughts.
∴Propositions must be mind-independent abstract objects; psychologism about propositional content generates absurdities (mathematical truth becomes historically contingent) that the Third Realm avoids.
Argument II — Frege's puzzle: cognitive significance requires sense, not mere reference
1."Hesperus is Phosphorus" is informative — it required empirical discovery. "Hesperus is Hesperus" is trivially a priori. They have different cognitive values.
2.If propositions contain only referents (as neo-Russellians hold), both sentences express the same proposition — Venus's self-identity — and cannot differ in cognitive value. This is a reductio of the direct reference view.
3.Propositions must be structured by senses — modes of presentation — not by referents; this preserves the cognitive difference between informativeness and triviality.
∴The informativeness of identity statements proves that propositions are individuated by sense, not by reference; direct reference theories cannot account for cognitive significance.
Argument III — Compositionality and unity: the saturated/unsaturated distinction
1.Concept-expressions are essentially unsaturated — they contain a logical gap requiring completion by an object-expression; this function-argument structure is a feature of the senses themselves.
2.The unity of a proposition arises from the saturation of an unsaturated concept-sense by an object-sense — a structural relation in the Third Realm, not a result of any cognitive act performed on a mere list of items.
3.This account of unity is not a separate posit — it follows from the function-argument architecture that is the deepest structural feature of logical analysis.
∴The unity of the proposition is explained by the saturated/unsaturated distinction within the Third Realm itself; no cognitive act of predication is required — unity is a structural feature of the sense.
Argument IV — The grasp relation: difficulty of access does not refute the Third Realm
1.The objection that abstract objects are causally inert and therefore inaccessible is serious, but it applies to mathematics, logic, and universals generally — not just propositions.
2.The grasp relation is sui generis: a distinctive cognitive relation through which minds apprehend (rather than create) thoughts — the difficulty of explaining it is a problem for epistemology, not a refutation of the metaphysics.
3.The alternatives — psychologism (truth becomes mind-dependent) and eliminativism (no truth-bearers at all) — generate absurdities worse than the epistemological difficulty they avoid.
∴The difficulty of explaining cognitive access to abstract objects is a genuine challenge but not a decisive objection; it is outweighed by the absurdities generated by psychologist and eliminativist alternatives.
Argument V — Against the opposition: false propositions and the unity failure of Russell; and Frege's individuation criterion for thoughts
1.Russellian propositions containing objects have no account of false propositions about non-existents — a Pegasus-proposition would contain nothing.
2.The multiple relations theory — eliminating propositions in favour of mind-constituent relations — cannot explain why a judgment says something rather than merely listing objects (Wittgenstein's decisive objection).
3.Thoughts are individuated by a coherent criterion: two sentences express the same thought iff a rational bilingual speaker cannot coherently accept one while rejecting the other — a demanding but genuine identity condition, contra Quine's demand for extensional individuation.
∴Both Russellian and eliminativist alternatives fail harder than Fregean abstractionism; the Third Realm is the only available position that accounts for propositional unity, cognitive significance, and the objectivity of truth simultaneously.

Mr President,

I defend propositions against Quine's eliminativism and against the deflationist's attempt to dissolve the problem. But I find myself in a peculiar position toward my colleague Frege: I believe that his account of propositional unity — however elegant — locates the source of that unity in the wrong place. Frege places it in the abstract structure of sense itself, in the saturated/unsaturated distinction that belongs to the Third Realm independent of any mind. I want to argue that unity must be located instead in acts of predication — cognitive and linguistic acts that human beings and other rational creatures perform — and that propositions are best understood as types of such acts rather than as self-subsistent abstract entities that we merely grasp.

"The problem of the unity of the proposition — what makes a set of constituents into something with a truth value, rather than merely a list — is not solved by placing the constituents in a third realm and calling their combination a 'function-argument structure.' That merely relocates the problem. The question becomes: what makes the function-argument structure itself unified? And the answer cannot be another abstract relation, on pain of infinite regress. The answer must ultimately be: someone predicated something of something."
i. the unity problem — why Frege's solution fails

Unity problem — function-argument structure relocates rather than solves; regress threatensFrege's account of propositional unity relies on the saturated/unsaturated distinction. Concept-expressions are unsaturated; they contain a gap; object-expressions fill the gap; the result is a unified thought. But this simply redescribes the phenomenon it is supposed to explain. What makes the unsaturated concept-sense genuinely gap-containing rather than merely another abstract object that happens to be related to others? If the unity of the thought derives from the unsaturated nature of the concept-sense, we need an account of what it is for a sense to be genuinely unsaturated — and that account cannot itself invoke another abstract relation that requires explanation. As Bradley observed, every relation requires a further relation to relate it to its relata, generating an infinite regress. The only way to stop the regress is to invoke something that is not merely an abstract relation but an act — a genuine doing, a predication that carries the unity in itself because unity is constitutive of what an act of predication is. Frege's Third Realm cannot stop the regress; acts of predication can.

ii. the act-type theory — propositions as types of predications

Act-type theory — propositions as types of predicative acts; objective through type-token structureMy positive account identifies propositions with types of acts of predication. When I judge that Mars is red, I perform a token act: I predicate redness of Mars. That token act is a concrete mental event — it occurs at a time and belongs to me. But the proposition that Mars is red is the type of which my token act is an instance. Types are abstract — they are not identical with any of their tokens — but they are not mysterious Fregean entities requiring a special grasp relation. They are individuated by the constitutive features of their token acts: the object referred to, the property predicated, and the predicative mode itself. The type-token structure gives propositions the objectivity that Frege correctly demands — the same proposition can be tokened by different agents at different times — without requiring that propositions exist in a causally inert Third Realm that minds can only access through an inexplicable grasping.

iii. the Frege-Geach problem — force and content

Frege-Geach — embedded propositions; cancellation; assertion vs. suppositionA central challenge for any act-based theory of propositions is the Frege-Geach problem: in conditionals, questions, and other non-assertoric contexts, the proposition occurs embedded without being asserted. "If it is raining, we should stay inside" — the proposition that it is raining is not asserted; it is entertained, supposed, placed in the antecedent of a conditional. If propositions are acts of predication, what is their status in these embedded positions? My answer involves distinguishing between the basic act of predication — which I call a type of act regardless of whether it is performed assertorically — and the illocutionary force with which it is performed. An act of predication performed in the context of a conditional is a cancelled or suspended predication: the predicative content is present, but its assertoric commitment is withheld by the surrounding logical structure. This preserves the compositional role of the proposition without requiring that it be a self-subsistent abstract entity with truth conditions independent of any act.

iv. responding to Quine — individuation of act-types

Against Quine — act-types have clear individuation conditions via their constitutive featuresQuine's demand — no entity without identity — challenges propositional realism to provide identity conditions for propositions. The Fregean account struggles here: two sentences express the same Fregean thought just in case a rational speaker who understands both cannot accept one while rejecting the other, but this is a notoriously difficult criterion to apply with precision. The act-type account provides a cleaner answer. Act-types are individuated by their constitutive features: the object of reference, the property predicated, and the logical mode of predication. Two token acts are of the same type — express the same proposition — if and only if they predicate the same property of the same object in the same logical mode. This gives identity conditions that are explicit and do not depend on the intensional notions that Quine rightly regards as opaque. The act-type approach meets Quine's methodological challenge without conceding his eliminativist conclusion.

v. agreement with the proposition — propositions are necessary

Against eliminativism — propositional attitude psychology; logical inference; objective truth require propositionsDespite my disagreements with Frege about what propositions are, I agree emphatically that the concept of a proposition is indispensable — against Quine, Horwich, and the deflationists who would dissolve it. Propositional attitude psychology — belief, desire, hope, fear — requires that the objects of these attitudes be individuated finely enough to explain behaviour: why I act on my belief that water is drinkable in all and only the situations where that specific content is relevant. Sentential paraphrase cannot do this — sentences are too coarse-grained and too language-relative. Logical inference requires something to serve as the content shared between an occurrence of a sentence as premiss and its occurrence as conclusion: only a proposition — individuated by meaning, not by orthographic identity — can play this role. And objective truth requires an objective truth-bearer. These theoretical pressures converge on the necessity of propositions, whatever their ultimate metaphysical nature.

Mr President,

I want to argue for a position that is in some respects even more realist than the proposition's: that propositions are real, structured entities with genuine metaphysical weight, and that they contain not Fregean senses — those ghostly intermediary entities, modes of presentation — but the very objects, properties, and relations they are about. When I judge that Venus is a planet, my judgment is about Venus. Not about a mode of presentation of Venus. Not about a Fregean sense that presents Venus to me. About Venus itself. The proposition I entertain contains the planet, its property, and the predicative relation — and nothing else is needed. Frege's senses are a theoretical extravagance invented to solve a problem that can be solved more economically.

"Frege's puzzle — how can 'Hesperus is Phosphorus' be informative while 'Hesperus is Hesperus' is trivial, if both refer to Venus? — has a solution that does not require us to populate a Third Realm with senses. The difference in informativeness is a difference in how we are acquainted with Venus, in how the proposition is presented to us — it is a psychological and pragmatic difference, not a semantic one. The propositions are the same. Our epistemic access to them differs. Frege confused the proposition with the way it is grasped."
i. the original Russellian case for object-containing propositions

Russellian propositions — objects as constituents; propositional aboutness is directIn the Principles of Mathematics, I argued that a proposition about a particular individual — say, Socrates — must contain Socrates himself as a constituent, not some description or mode of presentation of Socrates. The reason is that propositions are individuated by what they are about, not by how they are presented. If two propositions that are both about Socrates differ only in their mode of presentation, they are still both about Socrates — and if they are both about Socrates and agree on all the properties attributed, they are the same proposition. Modes of presentation are epistemologically significant but semantically idle: they affect how we grasp the proposition, not what the proposition is. Frege's senses are the result of assimilating the grasp of a proposition to the proposition itself — a confusion between an act of thought and its content.

ii. direct reference — Kaplan, Salmon, and Soames

Direct reference — names contribute referents; senses semantically idle; MillianismDavid Kaplan's logic of indexicals demonstrates that terms like "I," "here," and "now" are directly referential: they contribute their referents — the actual speaker, location, time — to the proposition expressed, with no Fregean sense as intermediary. The same holds for proper names. Nathan Salmon and Scott Soames argued in the 1980s that the proposition expressed by "Hesperus is Phosphorus" and the proposition expressed by "Hesperus is Hesperus" are identical — both contain Venus, the identity relation, and Venus again. Frege's puzzle is generated by the false assumption that semantic content must include whatever is relevant to the cognitive significance of an utterance. Cognitive significance is a matter of how propositions are apprehended, associated with mental files, and connected to prior beliefs — it is pragmatic and psychological. The semantic content is the proposition: the structured complex of objects, properties, and relations.

iii. the cognitive significance problem — the neo-Russellian response

Ways of taking — guises, mental files; cognitive significance is pragmatic not semanticSalmon's account of why "Hesperus is Phosphorus" seems informative while "Hesperus is Hesperus" seems trivial, despite expressing the same proposition, invokes what he calls "ways of taking" a proposition — the psychological mode in which the proposition is presented to a particular believer. When an ancient astronomer believes the proposition that Venus is Venus (= the proposition that Hesperus is Phosphorus) under the guise "Hesperus is the evening star" but not under the guise "Phosphorus is the morning star," he believes the same proposition under one guise and fails to believe it under another. This is not a failure of the semantic theory; it is an accurate description of the psychology. Propositions can be believed under multiple guises, and the same person can have different attitudes to the same proposition under different guises. Cognitive significance is a fact about guises, not about propositions.

iv. against the unity objection — my own troubled history

Unity problem — Russell's struggle; multiple relations; Wittgenstein's objection; structured proposition responseI must be honest: the unity of the proposition is the problem that tormented me throughout my career. In the Principles of Mathematics I held that propositions are genuine complexes containing their objects; I struggled to explain what unified them beyond a mere list. My multiple relations theory of judgment — according to which belief is not a two-place relation between a mind and a proposition but a multi-place relation between a mind and the various constituents — was my attempt to dissolve the problem by eliminating propositions. Wittgenstein's objection is fair: that account cannot explain what makes a judgment say something, as opposed to merely listing objects. The contemporary neo-Russellian response, developed by Soames and Jeffrey King, is to locate unity in the semantic relations that expressions in sentences stand in — the structured proposition is the fact that the object stands in a certain grammatical relation to the property. This is an ongoing research programme, not a solved problem. But the difficulties are not uniquely Russellian: Frege's Third Realm faces equally serious questions about what makes the function-argument structure genuinely unified rather than merely two abstract objects in a further abstract relation.

v. the false proposition problem — the descriptivist solution

False propositions — Russellian descriptions; empty names; negative existentialsThe standard objection to object-containing propositions is the problem of false and empty propositions: "Pegasus flies" seems to express a proposition, but Pegasus does not exist, so the proposition would contain nothing. My response in the theory of descriptions is to argue that apparent proper names like "Pegasus" are disguised definite descriptions — "the winged horse of Bellerophon" — and that sentences involving them are analyzable into quantified sentences that contain no irreducibly singular terms. "Pegasus flies" is equivalent to "there is exactly one winged horse of Bellerophon, and it flies" — a perfectly well-formed quantified proposition that is false and contains no empty terms. The neo-Russellian account extends this: for genuine proper names of existing individuals, the proposition contains the individual; for apparent names that are really descriptions, the proposition contains the description's constituents. This distinction is principled and tractable.

Mr President,

Both the proposition's speakers and my colleague Russell agree on one thing: that propositions exist. They disagree about what kind of thing they are — Fregean senses in a Third Realm, or Russellian complexes containing objects. I want to challenge the shared assumption. My argument is that the concept of a proposition, as both the proposition and the first opposition speaker employ it, fails the most basic test of philosophical hygiene: we have no clear account of when two sentences express the same proposition, and without such an account, we are quantifying over entities we have no means of individuating. No entity without identity is not pedantry. It is the condition of intelligible discourse about any domain.

"Propositions have been introduced to do theoretical work — to serve as the objects of propositional attitudes, the contents of sentences, the primary bearers of truth. I do not deny that these theoretical roles are real. I deny that any single kind of entity can play all of them simultaneously, and I doubt that the entity the philosophers have in mind — the Fregean thought, the Russellian complex — is adequately characterized to do any of them. The sentence will do; propositions are a confusion between sentences and their meanings, reified into a spurious intermediate category."
i. the individuation problem — when do two sentences express the same proposition?

Individuation — synonymy undefined; intensional identity conditions opaque; Quine's argumentMy central argument against propositions proceeds through the individuation problem. Propositions exist only if we have a clear standard for when two sentences express the same proposition. The Fregean criterion — same thought iff a rational bilingual speaker cannot accept one while rejecting the other — is intensional through and through: it presupposes the notion of rationality, of understanding, and ultimately of synonymy. But synonymy is exactly what we cannot explain without circularity. In Word and Object, I demonstrate that the totality of dispositions to verbal behaviour is compatible with radically divergent translation manuals — manuals that translate the same sentences of a foreign language onto sentences of English that differ not merely in connotation but in truth value. If there is no fact of the matter about correct translation — if the "inscrutability of reference" and "indeterminacy of translation" are genuine — then there is no fact of the matter about when two sentences express the same proposition, and the concept of a proposition is fraudulent.

ii. ontological economy — propositions as theoretical posits multiply beyond necessity

Occam's razor — sentences sufficient for truth-bearers; propositional entities not neededThe philosophical tradition has multiplied abstract entities in proportion to its tolerance for obscurity. Propositions were introduced to explain what two sentences in different languages have in common when they say the same thing. But if we cannot specify when two sentences say the same thing, propositions cannot serve this explanatory purpose — they are the explanandum masquerading as an explanans. The working logician has no need of propositions as a third category between sentences and the world. Sentences can serve as truth-bearers directly; truth can be defined for sentences through Tarski's schema; logical relations can be defined over sentences. Everything the proposition is supposed to do can be done by extensional means — by sentences and their logical relations — without the indigestible surplus of abstract propositional entities.

iii. Stalnaker and the possible worlds alternative

Possible worlds — propositions as sets of possible worlds; formal tractability; hyperintensionality problem acknowledgedRobert Stalnaker has proposed what is in many respects a more tractable alternative to both Fregean and Russellian propositions: identifying a proposition with the set of possible worlds at which it is true — a function from possible worlds to truth values. This account has the enormous virtue of formal tractability: it integrates propositions into the possible worlds framework that modal logic, conditional logic, and game-theoretic semantics all employ; it gives clear identity conditions for propositions (same proposition iff true at exactly the same worlds); and it avoids the mysterious grasp relation of the Third Realm and the unity problem of structured propositions. Its acknowledged difficulty is hyperintensionality: since all necessarily true propositions are true at all possible worlds, they are all identified with the same proposition — the set of all possible worlds. This means the proposition that 2+2=4 and the proposition that there are infinitely many prime numbers are the same proposition, which is absurd. Stalnaker acknowledges this difficulty and proposes handling it through the pragmatics of assertion; but it remains the most serious objection to the possible-worlds account.

iv. deflationism about truth — Horwich's minimal theory

Deflationism — truth as disquotation; no substantial truth property; propositions not requiredPaul Horwich's minimal theory of truth deflates the motivation for propositions by deflating the notion of truth itself. Truth is not a substantial property requiring rich truth-bearers — propositions with the metaphysical weight the proposition's speakers ascribe to them. It is a purely logical device of disquotation: to say that the proposition that snow is white is true is simply to say that snow is white. The truth predicate is a device for semantic ascent and descent, not a predicate that attributes a substantial property to a substantial entity. On this account, we do not need to know what propositions are or how they are individuated in order to use the truth predicate; we need only the schema "the proposition that P is true iff P," which generates all the instances of the truth predicate we will ever need. The elaborate metaphysics of abstract propositional objects is not required by a correct theory of truth — it is required only by a substantialist theory of truth that the deflationary picture has reason to abandon.

v. the residual need — what to do about propositional attitudes

Propositional attitudes — sentential attitude reports; de re vs de dicto; eliminativist strategyThe strongest case for propositions comes from propositional attitudes — belief, desire, hope, and so on. If belief is a two-place relation between a believer and something believed, we need to know what that something is. I do not deny the phenomenon. I question whether the something must be a Fregean thought or Russellian complex, as opposed to a sentence in a language — including the internal language of thought that cognitive science studies. Attitude reports in ordinary language are not transparent windows onto propositional contents; they are oblique constructions whose semantics is complex and context-dependent. The apparent need for propositional objects of attitudes may be an artefact of our surface grammar, not a deep metaphysical requirement. A naturalistic theory of mind that identifies belief states with functional states defined over sentence-like representations in a language of thought can discharge the explanatory work that Fregean or Russellian propositions were invoked to do — without the metaphysical commitment to abstract objects that neither side of this debate has adequately defended.

Argument I — The individuation problem: no coherent identity conditions for propositions
1.Propositions exist only if we have a clear standard for propositional identity — a criterion for when two sentences express the same proposition.
2.Every proposed criterion — synonymy, necessary equivalence, Fregean coherent acceptance — is intensional and circular: it presupposes the very notions of meaning and understanding that propositions are invoked to explain.
3.Quine's indeterminacy of translation shows there is no fact of the matter about synonymy: translation manuals compatible with all verbal behaviour can diverge radically in truth value — so "same proposition" has no determinate extension.
∴"No entity without identity": propositions fail the individuation test and should be rejected as ontological posits; the concept is not adequately characterized to be admitted into serious theory.
Argument II — Ontological economy: sentences suffice as truth-bearers
1.Every theoretical role assigned to propositions — truth-bearing, serving as objects of attitudes, figuring in logical relations — can in principle be filled by sentences, supplemented by contextual parameters where needed.
2.Tarski's schema defines truth for sentences without invoking propositions; logical entailment is defined over sentences; attitude reports can be analysed as relations to sentence-types.
3.Propositions are the explanandum — what two sentences have in common when they "say the same thing" — masquerading as an explanans; they presuppose the synonymy they were supposed to explain.
∴By ontological economy, propositions should be rejected: they are multiply poorly defined and their theoretical work is done by extensional means at the level of sentences.
Argument III — Possible worlds propositions: formally tractable but hyperintensionally inadequate
1.Stalnaker's identification of propositions with sets of possible worlds provides clear identity conditions (same proposition iff true at exactly the same worlds) and formal tractability in modal and conditional logic.
2.The hyperintensionality problem is decisive: all necessarily true propositions are true at all possible worlds, so all necessary truths are identified as the same proposition — an absurd consequence.
3.The possible-worlds account is a useful formal tool but does not provide the fine-grained propositional individuation that the proposition's speakers require for their metaphysical picture.
∴The possible-worlds account is formally superior to Fregean and Russellian alternatives but fails hyperintensionally — neither it nor any standard account provides adequate identity conditions for propositions as traditionally conceived.
Argument IV — Deflationism: the truth predicate requires no substantial truth-bearing propositions
1.Horwich's minimal theory: truth is exhausted by instances of the schema "the proposition that P is true iff P" — truth is a logical device of disquotation, not a substantial property of substantial entities.
2.Deflationism removes the most powerful motivation for abstract propositions: if truth is not a substantial property, we do not need rich abstract truth-bearers with the metaphysical weight the proposition's speakers assign to them.
3.The deflationary schema works for any sentence-shaped item; it does not require prior determination of what propositions are or how they are individuated.
∴Deflationism about truth undermines the primary motivation for abstract propositional realism; if truth is disquotational, the metaphysics of the Third Realm is theoretically otiose.
