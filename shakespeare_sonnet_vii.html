Sonnet VII

William Shakespeare — Sonnets (1609)

Structure & Context
Form: Shakespearean sonnet — ABAB CDCD EFEF GG; iambic pentameter throughout Sequence: Seventh procreation sonnet; the first to use an extended natural allegory rather than direct accusation or financial metaphor Extended allegory: The sun's arc from dawn to dusk maps onto youth, middle age, and old age; the turn at line 13 applies the allegory to the young man directly Polyptoton: 'Looks' (line 4), 'looks' (line 7), 'look' (line 12), 'unlook'd' (line 14) — the word migrates in grammatical form across the sonnet, tracking the withdrawal of attention Classical allusion: 'Weary car' refers to the chariot of Phoebus Apollo — the classical sun god; the allusion imports the myth of the sun as a driver who tires Volta: Line 13 — 'So thou' — the allegory is applied directly to the young man for the first time; the couplet is the bluntest in the sequence so far

Lo, in the orient when the gracious light Lifts up his burning head, each under eye Doth homage to his new-appearing sight, Serving with looks his sacred majesty; And having climbed the steep-up heavenly hill, Resembling strong youth in his middle age, Yet mortal looks adore his beauty still, Attending on his golden pilgrimage; But when from highmost pitch, with weary car, Like feeble age, he reeleth from the day, The eyes ('fore duteous) now converted are From his low tract and look another way: So thou, thyself outgoing in thy noon, Unlook'd on diest unless thou get a son.
