Mr President,

I want to clarify what I actually claimed before the opposition attempts to characterise me as a defender of a thesis I never held. The debate before this House proceeds as if I, Kant, held that the necessary, the a priori, and the analytic are identical — that these three categories perfectly coincide. This is a misreading, and an important one. I never claimed the analytic was the only form of a priori knowledge. My entire project in the Critique of Pure Reason was founded on the recognition that there is synthetic a priori knowledge — knowledge that is a priori (not grounded in experience) but not analytic (not true merely by virtue of the meanings of its terms). The question that consumed me was how such knowledge is possible. Kripke's "necessary a posteriori" is a different and more radical claim — and I want to examine whether it is coherent.

On the three distinctions and how they relate

The three distinctions: analytic/synthetic, a priori/a posteriori, necessary/contingentI drew three distinctions that have been conflated by both sides of this debate. Analytic judgements are those where the predicate is contained in the concept of the subject — "All bachelors are unmarried" is analytic because the concept of being unmarried is part of what we mean by bachelor. Synthetic judgements add something beyond the concept — "7 + 5 = 12" is synthetic because the concept of 12 is not contained in the concept of 7+5. The a priori/a posteriori distinction concerns the source of justification: a priori knowledge is justified independently of experience; a posteriori knowledge is justified through experience. The necessary/contingent distinction concerns what must be the case: necessary truths could not have been otherwise; contingent truths could have been otherwise. These are three distinct distinctions, and I always recognised that synthetic a priori knowledge exists — mathematics and the pure principles of natural science are my primary examples.

On what I actually claimed — and what Kripke needs to refute

What Kant actually claimed: synthetic a priori is possible; a posteriori knowledge is always contingentWhat I did claim — and what Kripke needs to refute — is that a posteriori knowledge is always knowledge of contingent truths. This seems natural: if something is knowable only through experience, it could in principle have been otherwise — experience could have taught us something different. The idea that there could be truths both necessary and discoverable only through empirical investigation seemed to me incoherent, because necessity must be grounded in something prior to experience — in the structure of pure reason itself. A "necessary" truth that could only be known through chemistry is either not genuinely necessary or not genuinely a posteriori, and Kripke owes us an explanation of how both can be true simultaneously.

On rigid designation — a linguistic not a metaphysical result

Rigid designation: a semantic fact about names, not a metaphysical discoveryKripke's argument for the necessary a posteriori rests on the doctrine of rigid designation — the claim that names refer to the same object in all possible worlds. I want to suggest that this is a semantic observation about how names function in our language, not a metaphysical discovery about how the world must be. When Kripke says that "water is H₂O" is necessary, he seems to be saying that the word "water" as used in any possible world refers to H₂O. But this is a fact about our linguistic conventions — about how we have decided to use the word "water" — not a metaphysical fact about the essence of water independent of our ways of describing it. The necessity, on this reading, is a product of how we have set up our conceptual framework, not a discovery of how the world necessarily is. This is, I think, closer to Carnap's reading than to what Kripke intends — but it is the most defensible version of what Kripke has shown.

On the metre stick — a genuine insight in Kripke's favour

The metre stick: contingent a priori as a genuine phenomenon Kant missedI am willing to concede that Kripke's example of the contingent a priori is more troubling for my framework than the necessary a posteriori. When we stipulate that "one metre" means the length of stick S at time t, we can know a priori that stick S is one metre long — this knowledge follows from the stipulation without any empirical investigation. But the proposition is contingently true — the stick could have been a different length. If I am right that the a priori is coextensive with the necessary, this case is a counterexample. My response is that knowledge gained through stipulation is a special case — the agent who fixes the reference knows a priori that the stick is one metre, but this is not knowledge of a necessary truth; it is knowledge of a truth about a reference-fixing convention, and the necessity condition fails precisely because the convention could have been otherwise. The apparent a priori character of the knowledge is an artefact of the reference-fixing act.

On why the trichotomy survives in its essential form

The trichotomy survives: genuine necessity is always grounded in pure reasonMy deeper claim survives Kripke's challenge: genuine, robust necessity — the kind that holds in all possible worlds without qualification — must be grounded in something prior to empirical investigation. When we say water is necessarily H₂O, we are either making a claim that follows from our linguistic conventions (in which case it is not deeply metaphysical) or we are making a claim about the essential nature of water that could only be established by combining empirical chemistry with a metaphysical thesis about essences that itself requires a priori justification. Either way, the empirical chemistry alone does not establish the necessity — something more is required, and that something is prior to experience. The trichotomy, properly understood, remains intact.

Mr President,

I want to approach this debate from the perspective of linguistic frameworks. My core claim — which I defended against Quine's attack and which I believe survives Kripke's challenge in a modified form — is that what we call "necessary truths" are truths that are constitutive of the linguistic framework we are using to describe the world. They are not metaphysical discoveries about mind-independent essences; they are rules we have adopted, explicitly or implicitly, for the use of our terms. Kripke's great contribution is to show that our linguistic frameworks are more complex than I assumed — that names and natural kind terms function differently from the descriptions I focused on. But the philosophical upshot of his discoveries can be understood within a broadly linguistic-conventional picture, and does not require the robust metaphysical essentialism he endorses.

On linguistic frameworks and internal questions

Linguistic frameworks: necessity is internal to a chosen linguistic systemIn my framework pluralism, I distinguished internal questions — questions asked within a linguistic framework, whose answers are determined by the rules of that framework — from external questions about which framework to adopt, which are not answered by facts but by pragmatic considerations. The truths we call "necessary" are those that are constitutive of our linguistic framework — the rules that define how our terms are used and how our sentences are evaluated. "All bachelors are unmarried" is necessary not because of some metaphysical fact about bachelors but because the rule for "bachelor" specifies that it applies only to unmarried persons. If Kripke's "necessary a posteriori" truths are to be understood as genuinely necessary, they must either be internal to a linguistic framework — in which case they are conventional — or they are external metaphysical claims — in which case they go beyond what can be philosophically justified.

On what Kripke's semantics actually establishes

Kripke's semantics: rigid designation is a semantic discovery about natural language, not a metaphysical revelationKripke has made a genuine and important semantic discovery: natural language names and natural kind terms function as rigid designators — they refer to the same individual or kind in all possible worlds, rather than picking out whatever satisfies a cluster of descriptions. This is an interesting and important finding about the semantics of natural language. But it does not follow that the necessity of "water is H₂O" is a mind-independent metaphysical fact about water's essential nature. What follows is that, given how "water" functions in our language, the proposition "water is H₂O" is true in every possible world — because "water" picks out the same stuff H₂O in every world where that stuff exists. This is a semantic fact grounded in our linguistic conventions about the use of "water," not a discovery that water has an essence independent of how we describe it.

On the metaphysical essentialism Kripke requires

Against essentialism: essential properties are not discoverable by empirical science aloneTo move from "water is H₂O in all possible worlds" (a semantic claim about rigid designation) to "water is essentially H₂O" (a metaphysical claim about what water must be), Kripke requires a doctrine of natural kind essentialism: the view that natural kinds have intrinsic essential properties discoverable by empirical science. But this doctrine is not itself an empirical discovery. It is a metaphysical thesis — the thesis that kinds have essential properties in some philosophically robust sense, not merely that we choose to describe them in terms of their molecular structure. Science discovers that water is composed of H₂O. It does not discover that it could not have been otherwise. The modal claim is added by the philosopher, not read off from the chemistry. And a modal claim added by the philosopher is precisely the kind of claim that requires a priori justification — which reintroduces the connection between necessity and the a priori that Kripke claims to have severed.

On "Hesperus is Phosphorus" — my preferred analysis

Hesperus is Phosphorus: identity between names is necessary but the discovery is of reference, not of essenceKripke's most compelling example is "Hesperus is Phosphorus" — the Morning Star and the Evening Star are the same planet, Venus. This is known a posteriori (by astronomical investigation) but if true, necessary (since identity is reflexive and rigid designators ensure it holds in all possible worlds). My analysis: what we discovered empirically is that "Hesperus" and "Phosphorus" have the same referent — Venus. Once we know this, the identity is necessary by the logic of identity and rigid designation. But the necessity is generated by the semantic rules — rigid designation — combined with the logical principle that everything is necessarily identical to itself. The empirical discovery was not of a necessary truth but of a contingent fact about reference: the fact that two names have the same referent. The necessity was already there in the logic; what we discovered was which terms are co-referential. This is a semantic discovery with logical consequences, not a discovery of metaphysical necessity by empirical means.

On what remains of the trichotomy

What remains: necessity is always conventional or logical; the a posteriori always contingent at the level of genuine discoveryI want to be honest about where the pressure is greatest. Kripke's cases are genuine challenges to simple versions of the analytic/necessary equivalence. But what survives is the weaker claim: the necessity in every one of Kripke's examples is traceable either to logical principles (the necessity of identity) or to semantic conventions about how names function (rigid designation). The empirical investigation always discovers contingent facts — that two names have the same referent, that a substance has a given molecular structure. The necessity is generated by combining those contingent discoveries with a priori logical and semantic principles. This is not a collapse of the trichotomy; it is a more sophisticated version of it, in which a posteriori discoveries interact with a priori frameworks to generate the appearance of necessary a posteriori truths.

Mr President,

I find myself in an unusual position: I am arguing for the proposition — that the necessary, the a priori, and the analytic do not coincide — but not because I accept Kripke's framework. I accept it because I reject the entire framework of analytic, a priori, and necessary as a coherent set of categories. The motion claims these categories do not coincide. I go further: they should all be abandoned, because the distinctions themselves are not coherent. Kripke and I agree on a negative conclusion — the old trichotomy is broken — but we disagree entirely about what to put in its place. Kripke wants to replace it with a robust metaphysics of rigid designation and essential properties. I want to dissolve the whole framework into a naturalistic picture of belief revision in which no statement is immune to revision in principle.

On the two dogmas of empiricism

Two Dogmas: the analytic/synthetic distinction is indefensibleIn my 1951 paper "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" I argued that the analytic/synthetic distinction — the cornerstone of the logical empiricist programme — is philosophically indefensible. Every attempt to explain analyticity in terms of synonymy, meaning, or logical truth either presupposes synonymy (which is equally in need of explanation) or trivialises the notion. The supposed boundary between statements true by meaning alone and statements true because of how the world is has never been drawn, and I argued it cannot be drawn. This is not a minor technical difficulty. It is a fundamental problem for any philosophical framework that relies on a principled distinction between what is known a priori through meaning analysis and what is known empirically through investigation of the world.

On the web of belief

Web of belief: no statement is immune to revision; the periphery/centre distinction replaces the analytic/syntheticMy positive picture replaces the analytic/synthetic distinction with a web of belief. Our total theory of the world is a fabric of sentences related by logical and inferential connections. Some sentences — close to the centre of the web — are highly resistant to revision because revising them would require extensive readjustment throughout the web. Others — at the periphery — are more easily revised when they conflict with experience. But no statement is in principle immune to revision. Not "7 + 5 = 12." Not "nothing can be red and green all over." Not the principles of logic themselves. The difference between logical principles and empirical claims is pragmatic — a matter of how costly revision would be — not metaphysical. There is no sharp line between what is constitutive of meaning and what is substantive empirical commitment.

On why Kripke's "necessary a posteriori" is not a solution

Against Kripke: necessary a posteriori presupposes modal metaphysics my naturalism rejectsKripke's "necessary a posteriori" is supposed to represent a genuine third category — truths that are both necessary and empirically discoverable. But this category only makes sense against the backdrop of a robust modal metaphysics: a commitment to objective, mind-independent facts about which worlds are possible and which properties are essential to which kinds. I am deeply sceptical of this metaphysics. Talk of possible worlds and essential properties is intelligible only relative to a descriptive framework — relative to how we choose to describe and taxonomise the world. It is not a feature of the world itself, independent of any description. Kripke has not discovered that water is essentially H₂O; he has shown that given how we use "water" as a rigid designator and given the physics of H₂O, we are not prepared to call XYZ-stuff "water." This is a fact about our language and our dispositions — not about modal reality.

On essentialism as a disguised linguistic convention

Essentialism: talk of essential properties is framework-relative, not mind-independent metaphysicsWhen scientists say that gold is essentially the element with atomic number 79, they are not reporting a mind-independent metaphysical fact that obtains independently of any descriptive framework. They are reporting that within our current scientific framework, we classify substances by their atomic structure rather than by their appearance or other superficial properties. If our scientific interests and practices were different, we might classify substances differently — and different classification schemes would yield different "essences." The essential properties of gold are essential relative to a framework of description, not in some framework-independent sense. What Kripke calls the necessary a posteriori is always the discovery that, given our chosen framework of description, something is necessarily thus — which is just analyticity within the framework, dressed up in the language of metaphysical necessity.

On what the debate should really be about

The real debate: abandon the trichotomy and adopt naturalism about meaning and modalityThe real lesson of this entire debate — of the failures of both Kant and Carnap and of the problems with Kripke's metaphysics — is that the traditional framework of analytic, a priori, and necessary has outlived its usefulness. A naturalistic epistemology has no need for a category of truths immune to empirical revision. It replaces the a priori with the highly resilient. It replaces the analytic with the definitional. It replaces the necessary with the law-like. And it treats modal talk — possible worlds, essential properties — as a useful but ultimately deflationary shorthand for our habits of description and inference, not as a map of mind-independent modal reality. Kripke's challenge to the old trichotomy is real. But his proposed replacement — robust metaphysical essentialism grounded in rigid designation — is just a new metaphysics where an old one used to be. I propose we have no metaphysics at all.

Logical Arguments

Mr President,

The question before this House is whether the categories of the necessary, the a priori, and the analytic coincide. I want to argue not merely that they do not coincide but that the assumption that they do has been the source of sustained philosophical confusion — confusion that infected the theory of reference, the theory of knowledge, and the philosophy of mind. I shall do this by presenting examples of truths that are necessary but knowable only through empirical investigation, and examples of truths that are knowable a priori but are not necessary — showing that the three categories genuinely come apart, and that this coming-apart is philosophically illuminating rather than merely technical.

On the distinction between epistemic and metaphysical modality

Epistemic vs metaphysical modality: conceivability is not possibilityThe most important preliminary clarification is the distinction between epistemic and metaphysical modality. When we say something is possible, we may mean either that it is conceivable — that we can consistently imagine it — or that it is genuinely possible in the sense that there is a possible world in which it obtains. These two notions do not coincide. It seems conceivable — epistemically possible — that water might have turned out not to be H₂O. We can imagine waking up to discover that scientists were wrong, that water has a different molecular structure. But this conceivability does not show that it is genuinely possible for water to be other than H₂O. The conceivability reflects our epistemic situation — we can describe a world where our word "water" picks out something other than H₂O — but it does not show that our world could have been one where water had a different molecular structure. Kant conflated epistemic and metaphysical modality, and his conflation is what makes the necessary a posteriori seem incoherent.

On rigid designators and the necessary a posteriori

Rigid designation: names pick out the same object in all possible worldsA rigid designator is a term that refers to the same object in every possible world in which that object exists. Proper names like "Aristotle" and "Nixon" are rigid designators — they pick out the same individual in every possible world where that individual exists. Definite descriptions like "the teacher of Alexander" are not rigid — they pick out whoever happens to satisfy the description in each possible world, which may be different individuals in different worlds. Natural kind terms — "water," "gold," "heat" — are also rigid designators: "water" picks out the same substance H₂O in every possible world, not whatever substance happens to satisfy some description of water. This is the key semantic insight. Once we recognise that natural kind terms are rigid, we can see that "water is H₂O" is necessary — it is true in every possible world — even though it is a posteriori — we could not know it without empirical chemistry.

On Hesperus and Phosphorus

Hesperus is Phosphorus: necessary but discovered empiricallyThe clearest example of the necessary a posteriori is "Hesperus is Phosphorus" — the ancient names for what we now know to be Venus, observed as the morning star and the evening star respectively. The ancient astronomers who used these names did not know they referred to the same planet. They discovered this empirically — by tracking the movements of the celestial body. The discovery was a genuine empirical discovery, not something they could have known from the armchair. And yet, once we recognise that "Hesperus" and "Phosphorus" are both rigid designators referring to Venus, we see that their identity is necessary — in every possible world where Hesperus exists, it is Phosphorus. This is not a trivial consequence of the logic of identity; it is a substantial claim about the metaphysics of the case. And it could not have been known without looking at the sky.

On the contingent a priori — the metre stick

The contingent a priori: the metre stick introduces a term by reference-fixingThe contingent a priori is equally important and equally neglected. Suppose we introduce the term "one metre" by reference to a particular stick S at a particular time t — we stipulate that "one metre" is to mean the length of stick S at time t, whatever that length is. Given this stipulation, we can know a priori that stick S is one metre long at t — we know this simply by knowing how the term was introduced. But this truth is contingent — stick S could have been a different length; it was the particular stick it happened to be, and its length was a contingent matter. So we have a truth that is knowable a priori but is not necessary. The traditional view that the a priori is coextensive with the necessary is directly refuted by this case. There is genuine a priori knowledge of contingent truths — and this is not a curiosity but a reflection of how reference-fixing works in natural language.

On the mind-body problem — the implications of Kripke's framework

The mind-body problem: type identity theory fails because mental and physical terms are both rigidThe most important implication of the framework is for the mind-body problem. The type identity theorist says that pain is C-fibre firing — that mental state types are identical to physical state types. If this identity is true and both "pain" and "C-fibre firing" are rigid designators, then the identity is necessary — in every possible world, pain is C-fibre firing. But this seems obviously false — we can clearly conceive of pain without C-fibre firing and C-fibre firing without pain. The type identity theorist might try to explain away this conceivability as mere epistemic illusion — as we explained away the conceivability of water not being H₂O. But there is an asymmetry. When we discover that water is H₂O, we can explain the apparent conceivability of water not being H₂O: we were conceiving of a twin world where a water-like substance has a different molecular structure. But there is no analogous explanation for pain — the conceivability of pain without C-fibre firing does not reflect a confusion about reference; it reflects the genuine phenomenal character of pain, which is directly apprehended and not susceptible to the same referential explanation. The necessary a posteriori helps the mind-body problem here not by solving it but by showing why type identity theory is more problematic than it seemed.

Mr President,

Kripke has presented the framework. I want to extend it and show that the necessary a posteriori is not a philosophical curiosity confined to proper names and identity statements. It pervades our entire relationship to natural kinds — to the substances, species, and stuffs that make up the natural world. My central contribution is to show that the meanings of natural kind terms are not in the head — that what we mean by "water" is not determined by anything in our psychological states but by the actual nature of the substance we are referring to. This is semantic externalism, and it is the key to understanding why so much of our knowledge of the natural world is knowledge of necessary truths discovered empirically.

On the Twin Earth thought experiment

Twin Earth: meanings ain't in the head — reference is determined by the worldConsider a planet exactly like Earth in every observable respect — Twin Earth — except that the stuff in its lakes and rivers, though it looks and tastes like water and plays the same functional role in the lives of its inhabitants, is not H₂O but a different compound, XYZ. In 1750, before chemistry, an English speaker on Earth and her counterpart on Twin Earth are in exactly the same psychological state when they use the word "water." Their mental representations, their concepts, their beliefs — everything internal to their minds — is identical. But do they mean the same thing by "water"? I say no. The Earth speaker's "water" means H₂O; the Twin Earth speaker's "water" means XYZ. This is because meaning is determined not by what is in the head but by the actual stuff in the environment that the word is connected to through its causal history. Meanings ain't in the head.

On what externalism shows about the necessary a posteriori

Externalism and the necessary a posteriori: what we mean determines the modal factsThe externalist picture shows why the necessary a posteriori is ubiquitous for natural kind terms. When a speaker uses the word "water," what she means is determined by the actual substance — H₂O — that her use of the word is causally connected to, not by whatever descriptions she associates with the word. Given this, "water is H₂O" is necessary — there is no possible world where what she means by "water" is other than H₂O, because the meaning of the word is fixed by the actual substance. But she could not have known this a priori — no amount of introspection or conceptual analysis would reveal the molecular structure of the substance she is referring to. The necessary a posteriori is the direct consequence of semantic externalism: what we refer to determines the modal facts, but what we know about what we refer to is discovered empirically.

On Carnap's response — the linguistic framework defence

Against Carnap: the linguistic framework is fixed by the world, not by our conventionsCarnap has argued that the necessity of "water is H₂O" is a consequence of our linguistic conventions about how "water" functions — it is conventionally necessary rather than metaphysically necessary. But this gets the direction of explanation backwards. Our linguistic practices do not determine what "water" refers to by laying down a convention that it applies to H₂O. What determines the reference of "water" is the actual substance — H₂O — that our uses of the word are causally connected to, going back to initial baptisms and paradigm cases. The convention, if there is one, is downstream of the reference, not constitutive of it. Carnap assumes that we legislate meanings into nature; externalism shows that nature legislates meanings into us.

On Quine's naturalism — a partial ally and a partial enemy

Against Quine: the web of belief cannot accommodate the necessary a posterioriQuine is right that there is no sharp analytic/synthetic distinction — that no statement is in principle immune to revision. But he is wrong to conclude that this eliminates the necessary a posteriori or reduces it to pragmatic resilience. The necessity of "water is H₂O" is not a matter of how costly it would be to revise — it is a matter of what "water" means, which is determined by the external world. Even in Quine's web, some revision is not merely costly but incoherent: to revise "water is H₂O" would require either changing what we mean by "water" — switching to a new linguistic practice — or discovering that the stuff in our lakes and rivers is not H₂O, which would not be a revision of the identity statement but a discovery that we had been wrong about what our word picks out. The necessity is not pragmatic but semantic.

On the division of linguistic labour

Division of linguistic labour: ordinary speakers defer to experts on natural kind referenceA further feature of natural kind terms that supports the necessary a posteriori is the division of linguistic labour. Most people who use the word "gold" could not distinguish gold from fool's gold by inspection. They use the word to refer to the natural kind — the stuff that metallurgists and chemists identify as the element with atomic number 79 — even though they lack the knowledge to identify it directly. The meaning of "gold" for an ordinary speaker is partially determined by the practices of the experts who can identify the kind. This means that the necessary truth "gold is the element with atomic number 79" holds for every speaker who uses the word "gold," regardless of whether they know any chemistry. The ordinary speaker's word refers to the natural kind; the natural kind has its essential properties necessarily; so the speaker's word is necessarily connected to those properties even though she discovers them only through deference to experts. The necessary a posteriori pervades ordinary language.

Mr President,

I want to bring Kripke's framework to bear on a case that tests it in a different and, I think, philosophically deeper direction — the case of consciousness and the philosophy of mind. The knowledge argument, which I introduced through the thought experiment about Mary, was designed to show that physicalism is false: that there are facts about phenomenal consciousness that are not captured by any physical description of the world, however complete. Chalmers has connected this argument to Kripke's framework in a way that illuminates both the power and the limits of the necessary a posteriori — and I want to show why the case of phenomenal consciousness presents a different and harder problem for physicalism than the case of natural kinds.

On Mary's Room — the knowledge argument

Mary's Room: complete physical knowledge does not yield phenomenal knowledgeMary is a brilliant scientist who has lived her entire life in a black-and-white room. She knows every physical fact there is to know about colour vision — every wavelength, every neural response, every functional role of colour experience. She knows all the physics and neuroscience and functional psychology. But she has never seen red. When Mary finally leaves her room and sees a red rose, does she learn something new? I claim she does: she learns what it is like to see red. She learns a phenomenal fact — a fact about the qualitative character of experience — that was not captured by her complete physical knowledge. If physicalism is true, complete physical knowledge should yield all facts. But it does not yield phenomenal facts. Therefore physicalism is false.

On how Kripke's framework connects to Mary

Kripke and Mary: phenomenal concepts do not reduce to physical concepts even a posterioriThe physicalist might respond with the lesson of the necessary a posteriori: just as "water is H₂O" is necessary but a posteriori, "pain is C-fibre firing" might be necessary but a posteriori — we discover the identity empirically, but once discovered, it holds in all possible worlds. But Kripke already showed that this response fails. The reason "water is H₂O" can be both necessary and a posteriori is that we can explain the apparent conceivability of water not being H₂O as a referential illusion — we were imagining a twin world where a water-like substance has a different structure. No such explanation is available for phenomenal properties. The conceivability of pain without C-fibre firing does not reflect a confusion about reference. It reflects the direct phenomenal character of pain — something that is not susceptible to the same twin-world explanation.

On two-dimensional semantics and phenomenal concepts

Two-dimensional semantics: primary and secondary intensions come apart for phenomenal conceptsChalmers has developed a two-dimensional semantic framework to make this precise. Every concept has two intensions: a primary intension, which picks out what the concept applies to at any world considered as actual; and a secondary intension, which picks out what the concept applies to at all worlds given that the actual world is fixed. For natural kind terms like "water," the primary and secondary intensions come apart — the primary intension picks out "the watery stuff around here," which might be XYZ at some possible world, while the secondary intension picks out H₂O at all worlds. This coming-apart is what explains why natural kind identities are necessary a posteriori. But for phenomenal concepts like "the experience of seeing red," the primary and secondary intensions do not come apart in the same way — the phenomenal character is what it is in any world where it is considered, and there is no referential fixing that would make the identity with a physical state necessary.

On why Mary's case is worse for the physicalist than water

Mary vs water: the conceivability of inverted qualia cannot be explained awayThe key contrast is this. When we discover that water is H₂O, we can explain away the intuition that water might not be H₂O: we were imagining a world where the word "water" picks out something other than H₂O — a referential illusion, not a genuine possibility. When we consider the conceivability of phenomenal properties being different from their physical realisations — inverted qualia, zombies — there is no analogous explanation. The conceivability of a creature physically identical to me but lacking all phenomenal experience — a philosophical zombie — does not reflect confusion about reference. Phenomenal concepts refer directly to their phenomenal character, not via any referential chain that could pick out something different in another world. The zombie is conceivable because phenomenal consciousness is not reducible to any physical description, however complete.

On what this shows about the three categories

Phenomenal knowledge and the three categories: Mary learns something necessary that was not knowable a prioriThe knowledge argument connects to the motion's question in the following way. What Mary learns when she leaves the room — what it is like to see red — is not merely a piece of contingent information about her current brain state. It is a necessary truth about the phenomenal character of colour experience — that it has the qualitative character it has, necessarily. And it was not knowable a priori from her physical knowledge, however complete. If Mary's case is right, then there are truths about phenomenal consciousness that are necessary — they concern the essential phenomenal character of experience — and a posteriori — they require the experience itself to be known. This extends Kripke's necessary a posteriori into the domain of consciousness and shows that the categories come apart not only in physics and chemistry but at the very heart of what makes us conscious beings.
