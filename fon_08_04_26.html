Find Out Now Voting Intention — 8th April 2026

Published April 9, 2026

Methodology and sample

Find Out Now surveyed 2,869 GB adults on 8th April 2026 — a notably larger raw sample than the 2,330 used the previous week. The fieldwork is a single day rather than two days, which compresses any within-fieldwork news variation. The headline figures are drawn from 1,592 respondents who passed the turnout filter: stated likelihood of voting at "definitely" or "very likely," with 2024 non-voters only included if they say "definitely." The pass rate of 55.5% is virtually identical to last week, confirming consistent filter application. The margin of error remains within 4 points at the 9-in-10 confidence level; within 2 points at the 2-in-3 level.

One structural note for those comparing this poll to the April 1st edition: the bottom age band has been widened from 18-24 to 18-29. This means the youngest cohort in this poll contains five additional years of respondents, making direct age comparison with last week's youngest band invalid. The analytical conclusions about the Green-Reform age inversion are directionally confirmed by this poll, but the specific percentage for 18-24 year olds cannot be extracted from this data.

The analytically correct framing of these week-on-week changes is that there are none. Every movement is within the margin of error. The picture that emerges from two consecutive Find Out Now polls is one of striking stability: Reform in the mid-twenties, Greens at twenty, Conservatives and Labour in the mid-to-high teens, Lib Dems around ten. This stability is itself a finding of substance. It means the fluctuations seen across different houses — More in Common putting Reform at 30.3%, YouGov at 24% — are house-effect artefacts, not real political movement. Within a single consistent methodology, the numbers are not moving.

The ordering of parties is unchanged and remains analytically significant: Labour are third, behind both Reform and the Greens. The Conservative-Labour gap has narrowed marginally from 2.5 to 1.5 points but remains outside statistical significance. A governing party polling third with a sample of nearly 1,600 filtered respondents is not a rounding error or a methodological quirk. It is the central political fact of this dataset.

The age crossbreak: confirming the structural pattern

The age crossbreak on this poll, using six bands with an 18-29 bottom bracket, confirms the findings of every poll examined in this series while adding one new piece of precision.

Among 18-29 year olds: Greens 38.6%, Labour 17.1%, Reform 14.1%, Conservative 6.0%. Among 65-74 year olds: Reform 35.8%, Conservative 21.3%, Labour 13.0%, Greens 7.3%. The age inversion between Reform and the Greens is as steep and as consistent as it has been in every poll analysed this week.

The new finding is the crossing point. Reform overtakes the Greens in the 40-54 band (Reform 21.9%, Greens 20.5%) — a 1.4-point Reform lead. This is the earliest crossover point any poll this week has identified, and it is worth holding alongside More in Common's finding that the crossing occurs in the 45-54 band in their data. The two results are not inconsistent — More in Common's 35-44 and 45-54 bands straddle this poll's 40-54 band — but together they suggest the crossover occurs somewhere in the early-to-mid forties, not the early fifties as earlier polls had implied.

The 30-39 cohort deserves specific attention. Labour leads this band at 20.3%, with the Greens at 30.2% and Reform at just 14.8%. Among 30-39 year olds, Labour is the second party. This is the one age bracket where Labour's traditional coalition still holds with any coherence, and it is demographically concentrated in urban areas with high mortgage debt and children — voters who have specific economic grievances that Labour's policy platform at least nominally addresses.

The gender gap: a notable shift

Reform's gender gap has narrowed from 8.6 points (April 1st) to 5.0 points this week. Their male share has fallen from 29.8% to 27.2% and their female share has risen from 21.2% to 22.2%. The male decline accounts for most of the movement.

This narrowing is notable enough to flag but not yet large enough to conclude it is real. The male and female subgroups each have roughly 700-800 respondents in the filtered sample, giving a margin of error of approximately ±3-4 points for each subgroup. The 2.6-point fall in Reform's male share is at the edge of statistical significance for that subgroup. If the same pattern appears in next week's poll, it becomes analytically significant. If it reverts, it was noise. One week is insufficient to conclude.

What can be said with more confidence is that Reform's gender gap in this poll (5.0 points) is smaller than the comparable figure from YouGov's MRP (10 points: 29% male, 19% female on their raw question) and Find Out Now's own previous poll. The two most recent FON polls give Reform gender gaps of 8.6 and 5.0 points respectively — an average of 6.8 points, which is the most reliable estimate available from this house.

Reform's vote flow: the retention story

Reform's retention rate has fallen from 82.6% on April 1st to 76.3% this week. This is the most analytically significant movement in the entire crossbreak dataset, and it is worth examining carefully before concluding it is real.

The Reform 2024 voter subgroup in the filtered headline sample has 211 respondents this week versus 181 last week. The larger cell gives slightly more precision, but a 6.3-point retention fall is still within the margin of error for a subgroup of 211 people (approximately ±6-7 points at the 2-in-3 level). What can be said is that both polls, taken together, suggest Reform's retention among its 2024 voters is somewhere in the high seventies to low eighties — lower than the Green Party's retention and higher than every other party's.

The composition of Reform's current support remains structurally unchanged. It draws 76.3% from 2024 Reform voters, 39.1% from 2024 non-voters (slightly down from 39.9% last week), and 26.5% from 2024 Conservatives. The non-voter component is the critical one: nearly four in ten Reform supporters in the filtered headline sample come from people who did not vote in 2024. Find Out Now includes them only if they now say "definitely" — the most selective filter applied to any demographic group in this methodology. Whether that 20.3% of 2024 non-voters who say they will definitely vote this time actually materialises on polling day remains the fundamental unanswerable question.

Labour's retention: the first positive signal

Labour's retention has edged from 46.1% to 48.4% in one week. This is within the margin of error and cannot be described as a meaningful recovery. But the direction has reversed for the first time across the polls examined in this series. Every other poll published this week — YouGov, More in Common, Find Out Now on April 1st — showed Labour retention either static or declining. This week's small uptick, even if not statistically significant in isolation, is directionally notable.

Where Labour is losing remains unchanged: 28.5% of their 2024 voters are now with the Greens, 6.4% with the Lib Dems, 2.7% with the Conservatives. The leftward flow to the Greens is the dominant story and it is not meaningfully different from last week. The 0% recorded for 2024 Labour voters now with Reform this week (down from 8.1% last week) is almost certainly a sampling artefact in a small cell — it is implausible that zero 2024 Labour voters now intend to vote Reform — and should not be interpreted as a real movement.

The constituency question: a methodologically significant change

The don't-know rate on the constituency question has fallen from 34.4% last week to 32.3% this week — a 2.1 point decline. This is the constituency question finding that most analysts will not have noticed, and it is the most analytically interesting methodological signal in the entire dataset.

The constituency question asks respondents to think specifically about their own seat. A falling don't-know rate means more respondents have a formed view about their local contest than they did a week ago. There are three possible explanations: genuine increased political engagement following the week's heavy polling coverage; a difference in the daily timing of the single-day April 8th fieldwork versus the two-day April 1st fieldwork; or normal sampling variation. It would require three or four consecutive polls showing the same direction of travel to conclude this is a real trend.

What can be said is that the constituency-question Reform figure of 17.6% — compared to a headline of 24.9% — confirms a structural 7.3-point gap between national sentiment and local voting intention for Reform. This gap is the single most important number for any seat-level projection, because it tells you that even if national polling shows Reform leading comfortably, their constituency-level vote intention is consistently and substantially lower. That gap will not fully close unless Reform can build local campaign infrastructure capable of making their national support feel locally relevant to voters who, when prompted to think about their own seat, are less certain.

The regional picture: one number to ignore

The North East shows Reform at 26.6% this week versus 45.5% last week — a 19-point fall in one region in seven days. This is not a real movement. The North East filtered N is 67 respondents. At that sample size, the margin of error is approximately ±12 points at the 2-in-3 confidence level. The 45.5% last week and 26.6% this week are both within the plausible range of the same underlying true value — which is somewhere around 30-35% based on multiple polls and by-election data from the region. Regional crossbreaks from standard-sized polls should never be read as precise measurements. They are directional indicators only, and only become reliable estimates when aggregated across multiple polls.

The most consistent regional finding across both FON polls this week is Reform's strength in the East of England (35.8%) and East Midlands (30.3%). These are the regions where Reform's support is most geographically concentrated in seats with realistic winning paths, and their consistency across multiple polls in this series gives them more analytical weight than the volatile North East figure.

Summary: five findings that matter

1. The picture is stable, not volatile. Across two consecutive Find Out Now polls using identical methodology, no party has moved outside the margin of error. This confirms that the house-effect spread seen between pollsters this week reflects methodological differences, not real political movement.

2. Labour remain third. For the second consecutive FON poll, Labour are behind both Reform and the Greens. At 16.0% they are 1.5 points behind the Conservatives (17.5%) and 3.7 points behind the Greens (19.7%). This is the structural finding of the week.

3. Reform's retention has fallen but remains the story beneath the story. 76.3% retention among their 2024 voters — lower than last week but still among the highest of any party. The non-voter component at 39.1% of Reform's current support remains the central unanswerable question: will they show up?

4. The Green-Reform age crossover occurs in the 40-54 band. Both this poll and More in Common's data, read together, place the crossover point where Greens overtake Reform in the early-to-mid forties — earlier than previously estimated.

5. The constituency don't-know rate is falling. From 34.4% to 32.3% in one week. Too early to conclude this is a trend, but worth tracking. If it continues declining it will suggest voters are beginning to think more concretely about their local contest, which historically benefits parties with strong local infrastructure — the Lib Dems and Conservatives — over parties whose support is diffuse and nationally oriented.
