A Rhetorical Analysis of ChatGPT

Published March 21, 2026

It is the distinctive role of the schoolmaster to diagnose the rot in his pupils' Latinity, and to offer the corrective pruning knife. Where once the school exercise book reflected an earnest, if clumsy, striving toward the clarity of Addison or the robust architecture of Dr Johnson, it now exhibits a slick, glassy uniformity. The culprit is not a lack of effort, but the insidious influence of that ubiquitous digital oracle, ChatGPT.

Unlike a writing human being, a machine merely simulates. It has ingested the great corpus of human persuasion and distilled it into a series of predictable, mechanical tricks. Our ancestors studied the Progymnasmata to learn how to think. The next generation, however, is learning, by overexposure to large language models, merely to mimic the posture of thought.

Let us subject a typical specimen of this artificial prose to a strict classical dissection, identifying the telltale devices that betray the machine’s hand – devices that are actively corrupting the natural cadences of our language.

I. Epanorthosis

The most exhausting of the machine's habits is the syntax formulation of "It is not X, but Y" (or "it is not just X, it is Y").

In classical terminology, this is known as epanorthosis. And in the hands of a master – say, Cicero or Macaulay – this device is a scalpel. It is used to sever a genuinely held misconception from the truth. The machine, however, uses it as a blunt instrument to create a false sense of profundity where none exists. It sets up a straw man (X) that no sensible reader ever believed, simply to dramatically "reveal" the alternative (Y).

You can usually tell when an advert, an email, or any piece of writing has been ChatGPTed when it contains an unusual abundance of epanorthosis. If you haven't spotted this one already, you're about to spot it all the time. I have begun to call the proliferation of this technique in the public messaging we see all around us all of the time the Marks & Spenserfication of our language: "This is not just food, this is M&S food."

Observe the following ChatGPT-generated text: "The question is not how we open the door, but how we cross the threshold."

This is style without function. It is a statistical trick designed to sound authoritative and directional, convincing the young that writing is not about discovering truth, but about maintaining an aggressive posture of insight.

It manufactures an artificial conflict between two ideas that were never actually at war ("navigating" versus "transforming") simply to create the illusion of a sharp dialectic. This constant, rhythmic snapping back and forth – not this, but that — tires the ear and insults the reader's intelligence by setting up a straw man to dramatically knock down.

Instead of

"The question is not how we open the door, but how we cross the threshold."

we would do much better to write

"We must cross the threshold."

This revision entirely collapses the performative throat-clearing of the false alternative. By uniting the crisis and the institutions into a single, forward-moving directive, the prose abandons its posture of profundity and delivers an honest, muscular command.

II. Tricolon

Traditional English lessons emphasise that structural parallelism should rise naturally from the weight of the ideas. The machine, conversely, suffers from an incurable addiction to the tricolon (the rule of three). More specifically, it is overenamoured with a tricolon of asymmetric noun phrases or verbs. If ChatGPT is asked to describe a garden, it will inevitably praise its "vibrant colors, serene atmosphere, and rich history." If it speaks of an idea, it will "foster growth, inspire change, and encourage dialogue."

This is what we call isocolon (clauses of equal length and structures) executed without an ounce of poetic judgment. It offers rhythm without depth. It teaches children that three mediocre adjectives are always superior to one precisely chosen noun. It is a formulaic lullaby that puts the critical intellect to sleep.

Consider the following ChatGPT-generated sentence:

"Our framework aims to foster growth, inspire change, and encourage dialogue."

If ChatGPT had listened in English class, it would have known to write the much better:

"Our framework forces change."

This revision ruthlessly deletes all redundant rhetorical padding. By collapsing three flabby, tentative actions into the single, muscular verb "forces," the sentence exchanges corporate politeness for genuine authority. It commands attention because it dares to be singular, precise, and direct, proving that a single arrow hits the mark far better than a scattershot of three dull pebbles.

III. Hyperbole

Our traditional grammar schools have long waged war against the "vague-grandiose." We instruct our students to use concrete, Anglo-Saxon verbs. The machine rejects this discipline entirely, favoring an inflated, pseudo-academic vocabulary that relies heavily on Pleonasm (redundancy) and Hyperbole.

The machine never looks at a problem. It "delves" into it. A collection of facts is never a list. It is a "tapestry." An important moment is never significant. It is "pivotal" or a "testament" to a "multifaceted reality."

This is the very definition of Sophistry: using elevated vocabulary to conceal a vacuum of original thought. When the next generation adopts these words, they may believe themselves to be expanding their vocabulary. They will, however, be merely clothed in a uniform of intellectual pretense. They write sentences that sound remarkably like an introduction to a corporate brochure, utterly devoid of the specific, gritty details that mark true English prose.

"By delving into the nuances of this multifaceted issue, we reveal a vibrant tapestry of human history."

becomes

"Examining these records reveals how our ancestors lived."

This revision strips away the sophomoric baubles of hyperbole in favor of honest, plain English. By replacing the pretentious "delving into the nuances of this multifaceted issue" with the quiet dignity of "examining these records," the sentence stops posturing. It grounds the reader in actual reality rather than a fog of corporate grandeur.

IV. Hypophora

The machine is deeply fond of hypophora, the device of raising a question and immediately answering it oneself ("Why does this matter? It matters because...").

In classical oratory, hypophora is used to anticipate an opponent's objection in the heat of debate. The machine uses it mechanically to force a transition because it lacks the capacity for organic narrative flow. It teaches the young writer to be irritatingly self-conscious, constantly interrupting their own prose to explain what they are doing.

"Why does this matter? It matters because economic stability drives societal progress."

becomes

"Stable economies naturally foster stable societies."

This revision banishes any theatrical hand-wringing expressed by overused hypophora. By transforming the machine's clumsy question-and-answer routine into a single, declarative truth, the prose regains its stride. The assertion stands on its own merits, commanding belief through its crisp structure rather than a forced dialogue.

V. Periphrasis

A traditional English lesson demands economy of style. We instruct our pupils to treat words as currency, spending them only when they buy genuine meaning. The machine, possessing an infinite treasury of worthless tokens, defaults to periphrasis: using a clumsy phrase where a single, potent word is required.

Consider the following ChatGPT-generated sentence:

"It is important to bear in mind that this provides us with an opportunity to gain a deeper understanding of the situation."

This is the vice of macrologia (long-windedness) masquerading as courtesy. Look at the opening phrase: "It is important to bear in mind that..." This is a purely rhetorical placeholder, an empty vestibule before the house.

Furthermore, "provides us with an opportunity to gain" is a textbook pleonasm (redundancy). The machine uses these cushions because it cannot feel the weight of syllables. It teaches our children that bulk equals brains, and that a sentence is improved by lengthening the distance between the subject and the object.

To counter against macrologia and pleonasmus, we are taught to ruthlessly apply the classical knife of brevitas (conciseness).

"It is important to bear in mind that this provides us with an opportunity to gain a deeper understanding..."

becomes

"We must remember this clarifies the situation." or simply, "This reveals the truth."

By collapsing "provides an opportunity to gain an understanding" into the sharp, active verb "reveals" or "clarifies," the thought becomes an arrow rather than a cloud.

VI. Chiasmus

Our ancestors admired Chiasmus—the inversion of parallel structures (AB-BA)—when used to seal an argument with an unforgettable ring (as in, "Ask not what your country can do for you, ask what you can do for your country"). The machine, however, treats this rare jewel as a cheap plastic bead, using it to close every third paragraph with an empty summary.

Consider the following ChatGPT-generated sentence:

"We must not only shape our tools, but allow our tools to shape us."

While superficially pleasing to the untutored ear, the machine’s chiasmus is almost always intellectually fraudulent. It achieves a symmetry of syntax while contributing absolutely nothing to the sense.

It creates a closed loop of words that sounds like a philosophical conclusion but is, in reality, a semantic dead-end. It seduces the student into believing that reversing a sentence is the same thing as solving a problem. It is style divorced entirely from substance.

If a summary is required, it should be delivered through asyndeton (omitting conjunctions) or a sharp, declarative statement that moves the argument forward rather than spinning in a circle.

"We must not only shape our tools, but allow our tools to shape us."

becomes

"We create our instruments; we must endure their consequences."

Here, the false mirror is broken. In its place, we have two distinct, honest assertions that force the reader to think rather than merely tap their foot to the rhythm.

The Pedagogical Consequence

The tragedy of this artificial style is that it is highly effective at deceiving the untrained eye. It is grammatically flawless, flawlessly polite, and utterly hollow.

Traditional English composition teaches us that style must be the garment of thought. ChatGPT has inverted this: it provides a beautifully tailored garment and assumes the thought is present. If we allow our students to internalise these pompous rhetorical devices, we will raise a generation of fluent sycophants: individuals capable of speaking at great length without ever saying anything at all. We must return them to the parsing of sentences, the rigorous choice of the single right word, and the honest clarity that has always been the hallmark of our language.
