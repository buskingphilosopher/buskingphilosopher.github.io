Mr President,

I want to begin with an experiment — the very experiment that gave the discipline of practical criticism its name. In my lectures at Cambridge in the nineteen-twenties, I distributed to my students poems they had never seen before, with the authors' names removed, and asked them to read and respond to them in writing. The results were, to put it gently, alarming. My students — intelligent, educated, well-read young men — produced responses that bore almost no relationship to what the poems actually said or did. They imported biographical information they had invented. They read political attitudes into texts that had none. They responded to the prestige of names they wrongly identified rather than to the poems in front of them. The experiment revealed something important: that most people do not, in fact, read. They perform a ritual of reading while conducting a quite different mental activity. My response to this discovery was not despair but a project: to develop a discipline of close, attentive, trained response to the actual words on the page.

On the two uses of language

Referential vs emotive language: scientific language refers to facts; poetic language organises emotion — the two are fundamentally distinct usesThe first thing any literary theory must get right is the nature of literary language. This is where most theories go wrong, and it is where the opposition's approach will go wrong in this debate. Language has two fundamentally different uses. In its referential or scientific use, language makes statements about the world — statements that can be checked against reality and assessed as true or false. In its emotive use, language does something entirely different: it organises and communicates attitudes, feelings, and the complex dispositions of response that we bring to experience. Poetry belongs to the emotive use. When Keats writes "Beauty is truth, truth beauty," this is not a statement that can be verified or falsified by any investigation of the world. It is what I have called a pseudo-statement: a form of words that functions not to assert a fact but to organise and communicate a quality of experience. If you try to evaluate such language by the standards of referential language — asking whether it is historically accurate, or sociologically representative, or ideologically sound — you are simply applying the wrong criterion. You are asking of a hammer whether it makes a good saw.

On practical criticism as method

Practical criticism: the trained close reader attends to the words themselves — biographical and historical knowledge is secondary and often distortingThe method that follows from this understanding of literary language is practical criticism: close, disciplined attention to the actual words of the text, as they interact and reverberate with each other and with the trained reader's responses. The remarkable thing about my experiment with unattributed poems was not merely that students read poorly. It was that their readings improved significantly when the name of the author was removed. Released from the distorting influence of literary reputation, historical prestige, and biographical assumption, readers were forced to attend to what was actually there. This is not to say that historical knowledge is worthless — it can inform and deepen a reading. But it is secondary. The primary data is the language of the poem itself, and the primary skill is the capacity to respond to that language with accuracy, sensitivity, and trained discrimination. My opposition colleagues will argue that this is impossibly naive — that no reading is free from historical and social presupposition. They are right that it is an ideal. But it is the right ideal to aim for, and approximating it makes criticism better. The alternative — treating the poem primarily as a social or historical document — is simply to abandon the poem.

On the ordering of experience

Poetry orders impulses: its value is the equilibrium it achieves among the reader's responses — an ordering that no social analysis can substitute forGreat poetry, I argued in the Principles of Literary Criticism, achieves something that no social document and no historical record can: it orders, harmonises, and reconciles the competing impulses of the reader's mind into a complex equilibrium. The reader who has engaged properly with King Lear has not merely received social information about Jacobean England. They have experienced a particular organisation of responses to suffering, injustice, love, and dissolution that no paraphrase and no historical gloss can reproduce. This ordering function of literature is its distinctive contribution to human experience. It is also why literary criticism cannot be a branch of social history. Social history tells us about the conditions under which a poem was produced. Practical criticism tells us what the poem does — what experience it offers to the trained reader who opens themselves to it. These are different projects, and confusing them is the defining error of the sociological approach to literature.

On what Watson saw in this tradition

Watson's argument: the Cambridge tradition pioneered systematic literary theory — not despite its practicality but through itGeorge Watson's argument in Never Ones for Theory is one that those on my side of this debate should embrace. Watson observed that the British — and the Cambridge tradition especially — had an embarrassed, self-deprecating relationship with the idea of theory. We denied that we were doing theory at all. We described what we were doing as practice, as discrimination, as close reading, as the cultivation of trained response. And in this very denial we were, Watson argued, doing something philosophically more sophisticated than most continental theory: we were grounding our claims about literature in the actual encounter between trained readers and actual texts, rather than in systems derived from linguistics, Marxism, or psychoanalysis and then applied to texts from the outside. The proposition's case is Watson's: that this tradition of empirical, practice-based literary attention is not the absence of theory but the most honest form of theory — one that keeps faith with what literature actually is and does.

On the emotive function and its independence

Emotional independence: the emotive meaning of a poem does not depend on the truth of its referential content — it can move us even when its statements are falseOne of the most important consequences of the distinction between referential and emotive language is this: the emotive function of a poem is independent of the truth or falsity of any statements it makes. When Milton writes of Satan, we do not need to believe in Satan for the poem's emotional organisation to work. When a poem asserts that the nightingale never knew the weariness and fever and fret of the world, we do not need to assess whether nightingales have such experiences. The poetry makes demands not on our factual beliefs but on our capacity for emotional response — and it is in the organisation of that response, the bringing of it to a kind of ordered fullness, that the poem's value consists. The opposition wants to evaluate poetry by assessing the historical conditions of its production, the class ideology it embodies, or the social values it reinforces. But these evaluations are entirely independent of the emotive work the poem does — and it is the emotive work that is its literary identity. To replace emotive analysis with ideological analysis is to cease to be a literary critic and to become a social historian wearing literary costume.

On misreading and its causes

Sources of misreading: biographical, doctrinal, stock response — these corrupt the reading before the text has had its chance to workMy practical criticism experiment identified a number of common sources of misreading. There is the biographical reading, which treats a poem as a document about the author's life and experiences. There is the doctrinal reading, which evaluates a poem according to whether its expressed beliefs agree with the reader's. There is what I called the stock response, in which readers bring pre-formed emotional attitudes to certain subjects — patriotism, death, childhood — and respond to those attitudes rather than to what the poem actually does with them. And there is irrelevant association, in which the reader's private memories and feelings overwhelm the text entirely. Every one of these misreadings involves allowing something external to the text to supplant the actual encounter with it. The sociological reading, in which a poem is evaluated as a product or expression of historical conditions, is simply another variant of the same error on a larger scale.

On the value of literary criticism

Value of criticism: trained literary response is a form of intelligence that enriches the quality of experience — this is its justification, independent of social utilityI want to close by addressing the question of value. Why does any of this matter? My answer is that the cultivation of literary response — the training of the capacity to attend to and respond to the full organisation of a complex text — is itself a form of human development that enriches the quality of experience in ways that cannot be substituted by any other activity. Science tells us about the world. History tells us about the past. Social analysis tells us about the structures of society. Literature, when properly received, organises and deepens our experience of being human in ways that none of these can. And the criticism that serves this function — practical criticism, close reading, the cultivation of discriminating response — is not an obstacle to understanding literature socially. It is the precondition for understanding it at all. You cannot analyse what you have not first properly read.

Mr President,

I wrote Seven Types of Ambiguity when I was twenty-one or twenty-two, at Cambridge, having been set onto the path by my supervisor I.A. Richards. The book was composed in a few weeks, and I do not now think all of it is right. But the central claim seems to me as true as it ever did: that the richness of meaning in great poetry is a property of the words themselves — the result of the complex histories, associations, syntactic positions, and resonances that words carry — and that the critic's task is to be intelligent enough to trace what is already there.

On ambiguity as the defining property of literary language

Ambiguity is intrinsic: multiple meanings coexist in the text by virtue of the language itself — not imported from outside by the reader or the historical analystI defined ambiguity as any verbal nuance, however slight, that gives room for alternative reactions to the same piece of language. The seven types I described range from simple alternative meanings through increasingly complex situations where the syntax, the context, or the explicit development of an idea makes several simultaneous meanings possible. My claim is that these multiple meanings are intrinsic to the language — they are not projections of the reader's biography or reflections of the social conditions under which the poem was produced. They are there in the words, in the grammar, in the complex histories of meaning that the words carry into the poem. When Shakespeare writes "Bare ruined choirs, where late the sweet birds sang," the richness of that line comes from the multiple senses of "bare" and "ruined" and "choirs" and from the complex syntax — not from anything the reader imports or any historical condition the analyst identifies. A social historian may tell us much about the dissolution of the monasteries as context. But the complex resonance of the line is the poem's own.

On the structure of complex words

Complex words: words carry complex histories of meaning that are activated in the poem — the critic's task is to trace these, not to identify social or historical influencesIn my later book The Structure of Complex Words, I examined how certain key words in English — "wit," "sense," "honest," "dog" — carry within their ordinary usage a complex of meanings, emotional tones, and logical implications that literary texts activate and bring into play with each other. When Pope uses "wit," he is not merely referring to cleverness. The word has accumulated a whole history of meanings — wit as perception, as understanding, as sexual knowledge, as social cleverness — and Pope's use of it depends on and exploits this accumulated complexity. This is a property of the English language and its history, not of Pope's class position or the social conditions of Augustan England. The sociological analyst who locates "meaning" in historical conditions is simply describing the conditions under which the language's complexity was formed — not the complexity itself, which is in the words and is available to any reader trained enough to respond to it.

On the critic's intelligence and what it attends to

Critical intelligence: the critic traces what is already in the text — not what the text means sociologically but what it does with languageWhat does a literary critic actually do? My answer is: they read very carefully, bringing as much intelligence and as much knowledge of the language as they can, and they trace the play of meaning that the language itself enables. I am not a systematic critic. I distrust systems — systems, as my colleague Raymond Williams would say, tend to get in the way of what you are actually trying to see. My method is to follow the words wherever they lead, to notice what is odd or complex or resistant, and to try to say clearly what several meanings are simultaneously in play. This is not impressionism. It is disciplined attention to the text. And it is emphatically not the same as asking what social position the text occupies or what class ideology it reflects. Those are perfectly legitimate questions. But they are not literary questions in the sense that they leave the literary character of the text — its precise organisation of language — entirely untouched.

On pastoral and the complexity of social feeling in literature

Pastoral: even socially embedded literary forms work through linguistic complexity — the form is what makes the social content available at allI want to concede something to the opposition. In Some Versions of Pastoral, I argued that pastoral is a social form — that it encodes a set of class relationships and social attitudes, and that understanding it requires understanding those relationships. This might seem to support the opposition's case. But I think the concession is more limited than it appears. What pastoral does with class feeling — the way it uses the artifice of the simple life to mediate the complex attitudes of a sophisticated society — is a linguistic and formal achievement. The social content is the raw material; the literary form is what transforms it into something that works as literature. You cannot understand pastoral as literature by understanding the class relationships it encodes — you must also understand how the form works, how the language manages the contradictions, how the specific words activate the specific feelings. The social analysis describes what went in; only literary analysis tells you what came out.

On reading and rereading

Rereading: great poetry yields more on each reading — this shows that the richness is in the text, not in what the reader imports from outsideThere is a simple phenomenological argument for the proposition's position that I want to press. Great poetry rewards rereading. Indeed, the mark of literary greatness is precisely that it yields more on the tenth reading than the first — that there is always more to find, always another layer of meaning, always an aspect of the language's organisation that one has not yet fully attended to. This is not what happens when one reads a social document or a historical record. A census or a factory inspection report does not reward the same kind of rereading. The richness that rewards rereading is a property of the literary text's language — its complex deployment of ambiguity, its management of multiple meanings, its exploitation of the full resources of the tongue. And these properties are independent of historical context. They are there whenever the reader is trained enough to find them.

On my quarrels with the opposition — and with my own side

Partial concessions: social context informs but does not constitute literary meaning — the text's complexity precedes its ideological functionI want to be honest about where I part from my colleague Richards and where I have some sympathy with the opposition. I think Richards draws the line between referential and emotive language more cleanly than it can be drawn. Complex literary language does refer as well as evoke — the same words often do both at once. And I think the social and historical context does matter for understanding many texts, particularly for understanding the specific histories of words that I traced in The Structure of Complex Words. But the crucial point is this: social context informs literary meaning, it does not constitute it. The complex meanings of a poem are not identical with the social conditions that shaped the language in which it is written. They are properties of the specific text, of the specific arrangement of words in that specific poem, and they are available to a trained reader regardless of whether that reader has identified the social conditions. The social conditions tell you where the materials came from; the poem tells you what was made with them.

On the proposition's final claim

Final claim: the literary text is the primary object — social analysis is preparatory, not constitutive, of literary meaningMy final position is this. Literary meaning is primarily a function of the text — of the specific arrangement of words, their histories of meaning, their syntactic relationships, and the complex play of ambiguity and resonance that the text achieves. Social analysis is preparatory: it tells us about the conditions under which the language and its complexity were formed. But the meaning is in the text. It is what you find when you read carefully enough, and it is independent of whether you have first performed the sociological analysis. The opposition's approach — treating the text primarily as a social or historical document — is not wrong. It simply addresses a different object: the social conditions of the text, not the text itself. The literary critic's object is the text. And texts, unlike their conditions, are objects that reward the kind of sustained, attentive, intelligent engagement that my kind of criticism tries to provide.

Mr President,

I am, I suppose, the most improbable advocate for the proposition in this debate. My colleagues Richards and Empson are systematic men, men of Cambridge who applied to literature the precision of scientific psychology and linguistics respectively. I am not systematic. I am a reader — a voracious, promiscuous, insatiable reader who has read widely in the literatures of several languages and several centuries, and whose theory of literature, such as it is, grows from that experience rather than from any prior philosophical commitment. And it is precisely because my approach begins from the experience of reading — from what actually happens when a trained and receptive reader encounters a great text — that I believe the proposition is correct.

On reading as receiving

Receiving the text: the literary reader receives what the author gives — they lay aside their own ego and enter the world of the work; this is the opposite of imposing a social analysisIn An Experiment in Criticism, I proposed that we judge books by the kind of reading they invite and receive, rather than by the kind of writing that went into them. And I distinguished two fundamentally different types of reading. The unliterary reader uses a book — they raid it for the plot, for information, for entertainment of a superficial kind, for confirmation of existing attitudes. The literary reader receives a book — they lay aside their own ego, their own presuppositions and preferences, and open themselves to the world the author has created. Literature exists, on this account, to give us access to experiences and worlds beyond our own — to let us see through other eyes, feel with other hearts, know what it is to be a human being very different from ourselves. The great gift of literature is the enlargement of our being. Now I want to press a question: is this enlargement — this self-transcendence, this entry into another world — better served by attending closely to the text that enables it, or by attending to the social conditions under which the author produced it? I think the answer is obvious. The social conditions are the conditions of production. The text is the door into the other world. If you want to go through the door, you attend to the door.

On myth and its independence from history

Myth transcends history: the mythical in literature operates independently of the historical conditions of its production — it speaks to permanent human conditionsI want to press a deeper point about the kind of meaning that the greatest literature achieves. There is a category of literary effect that I have called mythical — the sense, in reading certain stories and poems, that one is in touch with something that matters in a way that transcends the particular and the historical. I am not speaking only of works that explicitly draw on myth, though they are the clearest cases. I am speaking of any literary work in which the particular story opens onto something universal — in which the characters and events take on an archetypal weight that makes them reverberate beyond their historical moment. When Tolkien and I discussed these matters over many years, we agreed that the power of myth is precisely its independence from history. The story of the Fall, the quest, the return, the descent into darkness and the ascent — these patterns speak to human beings across millennia because they address permanent conditions of human experience. To subject them primarily to historical or sociological analysis is to do to them what you would do to a piece of music by insisting on studying the composer's household accounts.

On the personal heresy — against biographical reductionism

Against the Personal Heresy: the poem is not a window into the poet's mind — it is an object with its own independent life, best understood by attending to it directlyI engaged in a long controversy with E.M.W. Tillyard, published as The Personal Heresy, over the question of whether what poetry expresses is the author's personality or something more impersonal — a vision of life that has an independent existence as a poem. Tillyard argued for the former; I argued strenuously for the latter. My position was that when we read a poem we are not primarily encountering the poet's mind — we are encountering a made object, an artifact with its own structure and its own meaning, which are not reducible to the biographical facts about its maker. A sonnet is not a disguised autobiography. It is a particular arrangement of language that achieves a particular effect, and that effect is open to anyone who can read well enough to receive it. The biographical approach to literature — which is a subspecies of the historical and social approaches — treats the poem as evidence about a person or a period rather than as a thing worth attending to in its own right. It is the wrong approach not because biography is uninteresting but because the poem is more interesting.

On Logos and Poiema — the made thing and what it says

Logos and Poiema: the literary work is both something said and something made — what it says cannot be separated from how it is made; social analysis attends only to the LogosI distinguish, following ancient usage, between the Logos — the meaning or what-is-said — and the Poiema — the poem or made thing, the specific linguistic artefact. The insight I want to press is that in literature these two are inseparable. The meaning of a great poem is not separable from its particular language, its rhythm, its imagery, its specific way of being arranged in words. You cannot paraphrase Keats without losing everything that makes it Keats. You cannot translate Hopkins without losing everything that makes it Hopkins. The made-ness of the thing is constitutive of its meaning. Now the opposition's approach — the social and historical analysis of literature — tends to address the Logos at the expense of the Poiema. It asks what a text says about society, what it reveals about class relations, what ideological function it performs. In doing so, it treats the poem as essentially a paraphrasable message delivered in literary form — when in fact it is precisely the literary form that is the message. The social historian of literature has access to the Logos but is systematically blind to the Poiema. The reader who attends to the made thing has access to both.

On the danger of ideology in criticism — both kinds

Against ideological criticism: imposing a framework — whether Marxist or Christian or any other — onto literature is using the text rather than receiving itI want to be careful here, because I am a Christian and I believe that literature illuminates the human condition in ways that are consistent with and sometimes anticipatory of Christian truth. Someone might think I am arguing for a Christian literary criticism — which would itself be an ideological approach. I am not. The great strength of An Experiment in Criticism is precisely that it argues for a kind of reading that lays down the reader's prior commitments and receives what the text actually offers. A Christian reading that imposes Christian meanings on texts that have none is as much a misreading as a Marxist reading that imposes class analysis on texts that resist it. The criterion is not whether the reading agrees with any particular ideology but whether it does justice to what the text actually is and does. And what a text actually is and does is discovered by attending to it, by receiving it, by allowing it to work on you — not by measuring it against an external standard.

On the opposition's best argument — and why it fails

Against contextualism: knowing the historical context enriches reading but does not constitute the meaning — the text must be encountered on its own terms firstThe opposition will argue — and it is their best argument — that reading any text requires historical and cultural knowledge. To understand Shakespeare you need to know something about the Elizabethan world. To understand Pope you need to know something about the Augustan social world. I grant this entirely. I am, after all, a scholar of medieval and Renaissance literature, and I know perfectly well that reading Chaucer without understanding his historical context is to miss much of what he is doing. But the concession is more limited than it appears. Historical knowledge is the preparation for reading; the reading is the encounter. You need to know what the words meant in their time in order to receive what the poem does — but what the poem does is still the poem, not its historical context. The knowledge of context is in the service of the encounter with the text; it is not a substitute for it. The social historian who knows everything about Augustan England but has never actually submitted to Pope's language has not read Pope. They have read about Pope's world.

In reading well, we get out of ourselves. We see with other eyes, feel with other hearts, inhabit other minds, enter other worlds. We become, for the duration of our reading and beyond it, more than ourselves. The sociological approach, for all its virtues, tends to confirm the prior commitments of its practitioners. What it cannot find — because it does not look for it — is the thing the text is actually doing: opening a door into a world that was not there before. And that world, and the enlargement it offers, is why literature is worth studying at all.

Mr President,

I am in the strange position of agreeing with a good deal of what my colleagues on the proposition have said and yet believing the motion they are defending is wrong. Richards is right that criticism requires close, disciplined attention to the words of the text. Empson is right that the richness of literary language is intrinsic to it and cannot be reduced to paraphrase. Even Lewis is right that reading requires a kind of self-transcendence. Where all three go wrong is in thinking that the encounter between text and trained reader is separable from the life, the civilisation, the moral world that both text and reader inhabit. There is no such separation. The text and the reader are both embedded in a living tradition, a whole way of valuing and responding to experience, that makes the encounter possible at all. To abstract the text from this context is not to get closer to it — it is to make it unintelligible.

On literature and life — the inseparability

Literature and life are inseparable: the great novelist's moral seriousness cannot be separated from her intense interest in life — text and world are one objectThe first thing I want to establish is a point about what literary greatness consists in. I have argued, and I continue to argue, that the great English novelists — Jane Austen, George Eliot, Henry James, Joseph Conrad — are great because their work embodies and communicates what I can only call an intense moral interest in life. Not merely in language, not merely in form, but in life — in the actual possibilities of human experience, the actual moral texture of living in the world with other people. When Jane Austen works out her technique, the technique serves and is inseparable from her subject-matter — and the subject-matter is life, actual social and moral life, the specific qualities of experience available to a particular kind of person in a particular kind of society. This is not a concession to the sociological approach. It is a claim about what literature, at its greatest, is: not a self-enclosed language game but a sustained, disciplined engagement with the actual possibilities and actualities of human experience. To read literature properly is to read it as this engagement — not as an autonomous verbal artifact.

On moral seriousness as the criterion of greatness

Moral seriousness: the criterion for literary greatness is moral seriousness — the intensity of the author's engagement with the actual human stakes of experienceMy criterion for literary greatness is not primarily linguistic or formal. It is moral. The great writers are those who bring to their work an intense moral seriousness — who care, deeply and with full intelligence, about the human possibilities their work explores. This is not the same as endorsing a conventional morality. George Eliot's moral seriousness is not the morality of Victorian conventionality. Conrad's moral seriousness is not the morality of the British imperialist tradition he served. Their greatness consists in the depth and honesty of their engagement with the moral facts of human experience — including the moral facts that conventional morality obscures or evades. And this moral engagement is inseparable from the social world in which it occurs. Moral experience is not abstract — it is embedded in particular social relations, particular historical circumstances, particular ways of living together. To read these writers as though their moral world were purely a function of their language, rather than of the life their language is in contact with, is to miss what makes them great.

On close reading in service of discrimination

Close reading in service of life: Leavis uses close reading not as an end in itself but as a means of moral discrimination — attending to the text attends to the life it embodiesI want to address directly the proposition's claim that close reading — attending to the words of the text — is the distinctive method of literary criticism. I use close reading. I use it constantly and I consider it indispensable. But I do not use it as an end in itself. I use it as a means of discriminating the quality of the life that the text embodies. When I attend closely to the language of a passage in Henry James, I am attending to the language because the language is the medium through which a particular quality of moral intelligence and moral perception is communicated. The language is in the service of something beyond itself — of a vision of human life and human possibility that the language renders precisely but does not contain. Richards treats the text as a verbal artefact whose organisation of the reader's responses is its value. I treat the text as a medium through which a quality of life is transmitted. These are different things, and the difference matters enormously: it determines whether criticism is ultimately about language or about life.

On Wittgenstein and forms of life

Wittgenstein's forms of life: meaning is embedded in shared practices and forms of life — the meaning of a literary text cannot be separated from the form of life in which it participatesI want to recruit a witness whom the proposition may not expect me to invoke: Ludwig Wittgenstein. In his late work, particularly On Certainty, Wittgenstein argued that meaning is not a private mental event or a property of language in isolation but is embedded in shared practices, in forms of life — the lived contexts of activity, response, and commitment that give our words and concepts their sense. The proposition wants to locate literary meaning in the encounter between trained reader and autonomous text. But Wittgenstein's insight suggests that this encounter is itself embedded in a form of life — the form of life that constitutes a literary culture, a tradition of reading, a set of shared practices of response and evaluation. These practices are not neutral and universal. They are historically and socially specific. The "trained reader" that Richards and Empson invoke is trained in a particular tradition — one that reflects the values, the sensibility, and the social experience of a particular class in a particular period. There is no view from nowhere. The trained reader is always already inside a form of life, and that form of life is inside history and society.

On the tradition and its social grounding

The tradition is socially grounded: what we recognise as literary greatness is shaped by a tradition that is itself historically and socially constitutedMy concept of the Great Tradition has been criticised as the imposition of a particular class taste on the whole of English fiction. There is some justice in this criticism, which I have had to take seriously. But I want to turn it in a different direction. The very fact that a tradition of evaluation — a sense of what literature is capable of and what it must be held to — is historically and socially shaped, is an argument against the proposition, not for it. The proposition's trained reader is trained by a tradition. The tradition is not a purely formal or linguistic system. It embodies values, attitudes, and a sense of human possibility that are historically constituted — that reflect the experience of actual people living in actual societies at actual historical moments. To pretend that the encounter between text and trained reader is innocent of these historical and social determinations is to pretend that the tradition is somehow above history. It is not. No tradition is.

On mass civilisation and minority culture

Mass civilisation: the literary tradition is under threat from industrial mass culture — this threat is social, and the response must be aware of the social stakesI have argued throughout my career that the literary tradition — the tradition of serious engagement with the full possibilities of human experience through language — is under threat from what I have called mass civilisation: the industrial production and consumption of culture that substitutes easy stimulation for genuine engagement. This threat is not a purely aesthetic one. It is a social and moral threat. It reflects the way in which industrial capitalism organises and degrades human experience — producing people who are not capable of the kind of serious engagement with literature that the tradition requires. To respond to this threat effectively, literary criticism must be socially and morally serious. It must understand what is at stake in the literary tradition — not merely as a verbal or aesthetic achievement but as a form of human life that the social and economic organisation of modern society is systematically eroding. The proposition's approach — treating literary meaning as a function of text and trained reader alone — cannot address this threat because it cannot see it. It has no analytical vocabulary for the social forces that are destroying the very conditions of literary reading.

On what I concede and where the motion fails

Concession and critique: close reading is necessary but not sufficient — literary meaning is in the life the text embodies, and life is always social and historicalLet me be clear about what I concede. Close reading is necessary. The words of the text must be attended to. The quality of the language is evidence of the quality of the mind and sensibility behind it — and the quality of that mind and sensibility is what the critic must judge. But the judgment is not a purely linguistic or formal one. It is a judgment about the quality of life that the text embodies — about the depth, honesty, and seriousness of the author's engagement with actual human experience. And that judgment cannot be made without reference to what actual human experience is — which is always social, always historical, always embedded in a particular way of living. The motion asks us to say that literary meaning is primarily a function of text and reader encounter. I say it is primarily a function of the quality of life that the text embodies and that the reader brings to the encounter. And the quality of life is never outside society and history. It is inside them. That is why literature matters — and why the proposition's account of it ultimately fails.

On value pluralism and its implications for reading

Value pluralism: different civilisations embody incommensurable values — to understand literature across cultures requires entering its specific world of value, not applying universal critical standardsMy central philosophical claim is that human values are genuinely plural and genuinely incommensurable. There is no single overarching framework — no universal standard of reason, no universal aesthetic or moral criterion — in terms of which all genuine human values can be ranked and compared. Different civilisations, different historical periods, different human communities have embodied different constellations of values, and these different constellations are not merely different versions of some common human ideal that criticism can identify. They are genuinely different — sometimes complementary, sometimes in tragic conflict. Now this has profound implications for literary criticism. If a twelfth-century Norse poem and a seventeenth-century Japanese haiku and a nineteenth-century English novel embody genuinely different and incommensurable human values, then no single set of critical techniques — no training of response to language as such — can provide adequate access to all of them. To read them well requires entering the specific world of value that each one inhabits — which is a historical and cultural world, not a purely linguistic one.

On Vico and Herder — understanding across cultures requires historical imagination

Vico and Herder: understanding a text from another time or culture requires entering its specific world — this is a historical act, not a purely linguistic oneGiambattista Vico and Johann Gottfried Herder — two of the thinkers I have most admired and written about — understood something that the Cambridge critics seem not to have grasped. Vico argued that to understand the poetry of Homer you must imaginatively enter the world of archaic Greece — a world of values, perceptions, and imaginative possibilities so different from our own that no amount of attention to the language alone can gain you access to it. You must understand what the heroes meant to those who composed them, what the gods meant, what honour and shame meant — and these meanings are historical meanings, not linguistic ones. Herder made the same point about the specificity of cultures: each culture has its own spirit, its own way of experiencing the world, its own possibilities of meaning — and to understand a cultural product, you must understand it from within the culture that produced it. This is verstehen — empathetic understanding, historical imagination — and it is indispensable for literary criticism. The proposition's trained reader, attending to the language of the text, has not yet begun the most important task.

On the Hedgehog and the Fox — against monist criticism

Hedgehogs and foxes: monist criticism applies one big idea to all texts — pluralist criticism understands each text in its own termsIn The Hedgehog and the Fox, I borrowed Archilochus's distinction between the hedgehog, who knows one big thing, and the fox, who knows many small things. Tolstoy, I argued, was a fox who wanted to be a hedgehog — a writer of extraordinary pluralist sensitivity who was tormented by the desire for a unified explanation. The proposition's approach to literary criticism is quintessentially hedgehog criticism: it knows one big thing — that meaning is in the text and the trained reader's response — and it applies this one big thing to all texts. But great literature is not produced by hedgehogs. It is produced by those with an extraordinary, almost painfully sensitive awareness of the plurality and complexity of human experience. And it requires fox criticism — criticism that is responsive to the specific, the individual, the irreducibly particular — rather than hedgehog criticism that applies a universal method regardless of what the text is. Fox criticism is historical criticism. It understands that each text is different, that each historical moment has its own specific character, and that no universal method of trained response can substitute for the specific historical imagination that each text requires.

The "trained reader" that Richards and Empson invoke is not a culturally and historically neutral figure. It is a specific historical type: the educated Englishman, trained in the Cambridge English tradition, of the early to mid twentieth century. This reader has been trained in specific sensitivities, specific aesthetic values, specific expectations of what a good poem looks like and does. These sensitivities and values are not universal. They are historically constituted. They are the product of a particular educational tradition, a particular class formation, a particular cultural moment. When Richards found that his students read poorly, he was finding that they did not share his particular historically constituted sensitivities — not that they lacked some universal human capacity. The solution he proposed — training in close reading — was the development of a historically specific set of skills, not the cultivation of a human universal. To mistake the historically specific for the universal is one of the characteristic intellectual errors of the liberal tradition, and it is an error that has serious consequences for literary criticism.

On the Russian writers and the limits of formalism

The Russian writers: Tolstoy and Dostoevsky cannot be understood without their historical and cultural world — their greatness is inseparable from Russian history and the Russian experienceLet me press a concrete case. I have spent a great part of my intellectual life thinking about the Russian writers — Tolstoy above all, but also Turgenev, Herzen, and the nineteenth-century Russian intellectual tradition. Can anyone seriously maintain that Tolstoy's greatness is accessible through close reading of the language alone? Tolstoy's greatness is inseparable from his peculiar place in Russian history — from the Russian experience of serfdom and its abolition, from the Russian Orthodox tradition and his rebellion against it, from the specific character of the Russian intelligentsia in the nineteenth century, from the particular moral urgency that comes from living in a society undergoing profound and violent transformation. To read War and Peace as a linguistic artefact, attending to the language alone, is to lose everything that makes it what it is. You need, at minimum, the history of Russia in the Napoleonic period; you need the history of Russian culture and religion; you need the history of the Russian aristocracy and its relationship to the peasantry. None of this is accessible through training in response to language. It requires historical knowledge and historical imagination.

We are not arguing that the text is unimportant, or that close reading has no value, or that what the words actually say is irrelevant. We are arguing that the text cannot be understood in isolation from its historical and cultural world — that meaning is constituted not merely by the language but by the specific human world that the language inhabits and addresses. When I read a great poem, I attend to its language with the closest attention I can bring. But I also bring historical knowledge, cultural understanding, and imaginative empathy with the specific world the poem comes from. This combination — close attention to the text within historical and cultural understanding — is what genuine literary criticism requires. The proposition offers half of it and calls it the whole.

On liberty in literary criticism — negative and positive

In Two Concepts of Liberty, I distinguished between negative liberty — freedom from external constraint — and positive liberty — the substantive freedom to achieve something, to actualise a capacity, to participate fully in a way of life. The proposition's approach to literary criticism is an exercise in negative liberty: it frees criticism from social and historical determination by abstracting the text from its world. My approach, and the approach of the opposition generally, is an exercise in positive liberty: it seeks the substantive freedom to actually understand what the text is saying — which requires the historical imagination and cultural knowledge to enter the world it inhabits. Negative literary liberty — reading in isolation from history — is a real kind of freedom. But it is the lesser freedom. The greater freedom is positive: the freedom that comes from knowing enough history and enough of human experience to enter the world the text comes from, and to understand it from within.

∴Value pluralism implies that literary meaning is irreducibly historical and cultural — no universal method of trained textual response can substitute for the specific historical imagination that each text requires.
II — Vico and Herder: understanding a text from another culture requires historical imagination — verstehen, not linguistic training
1.Vico argued that to understand Homer you must imaginatively enter the world of archaic Greece — its specific values, perceptions, and imaginative possibilities — which are historical, not linguistic, in character.
2.Herder showed that each culture has its own spirit and its own possibilities of meaning — to understand a cultural product, you must understand it from within the culture that produced it.
3.Verstehen — empathetic historical imagination — is indispensable for literary criticism; the proposition's trained reader, attending to language alone, has not yet begun this most important task.
∴Vico and Herder show that understanding across historical and cultural difference requires historical imagination, not merely linguistic attention — the proposition's method is insufficient for the most demanding cases of cross-cultural literary understanding.
III — Hedgehogs and foxes: monist criticism applies one method to all texts — pluralist criticism understands each in its own terms
1.Hedgehog criticism knows one big thing and applies it to all texts; fox criticism knows many small things and is responsive to the specific, individual, and irreducibly particular character of each text.
2.Great literature is produced by those with extraordinary sensitivity to the plurality of human experience — and it requires fox criticism, not hedgehog criticism, to do it justice.
3.Fox criticism is historical — it understands that each text and each historical moment has its own specific character that no universal method can substitute for.
∴The proposition's universal method of trained textual response is hedgehog criticism — it applies one big thing to all texts and systematically fails the plurality and specificity that great literature both exhibits and requires from its readers.
IV — The "trained reader" is a historical fiction: Richards's model reader is a specific historical type, not a universal ideal
1.The "trained reader" invoked by Richards and Empson is not a culturally neutral figure — it is the educated Englishman trained in the Cambridge English tradition of the early twentieth century.
2.This reader has been trained in historically specific sensitivities and aesthetic values that are not universal — they are the product of a particular educational tradition, class formation, and cultural moment.
3.Mistaking the historically specific for the universal is a characteristic error of liberal intellectual culture — and it has serious consequences when it misrepresents a particular tradition's trained response as a universal critical method.
∴The proposition's "trained reader" is a historical construction, not a universal ideal — identifying the historically specific with the universal is itself an ideological move that the historical and cultural analysis the opposition recommends is designed to unmask.
V — The Russian writers: Tolstoy cannot be understood without Russian history — close reading alone is grossly insufficient
1.Tolstoy's greatness is inseparable from his specific place in Russian history — serfdom, Russian Orthodoxy, the Russian intelligentsia, a society undergoing violent transformation; these are not decorative context but constitutive of the work's meaning.
2.To read War and Peace as a linguistic artefact, attending to language alone, is to lose everything that makes it what it is — the historical and cultural world of Russia in the Napoleonic period is not optional context.
3.Historical knowledge and historical imagination are required for the greatest literary works — these cannot be acquired through training in linguistic response and cannot be substituted by it.
∴The major achievements of world literature require historical knowledge for their understanding — this is incompatible with the proposition's claim that literary meaning is primarily accessible through trained response to the text; the greatest literature falsifies the proposition.
VI — The opposition does not deny the text: we insist it must be read within its historical and cultural world
1.The opposition is not arguing that the text is unimportant or that close reading has no value — close attention to language is indispensable.
2.The opposition argues that the text cannot be understood in isolation from its historical and cultural world — meaning is constituted not by language alone but by the specific human world the language inhabits.
3.Genuine literary criticism requires both: close attention to the text and historical and cultural understanding of the world it comes from; the proposition offers half of this and calls it the whole.
∴The opposition's position is inclusive of the proposition's method while extending it — close reading embedded in historical understanding is the complete method; close reading alone is a necessary but insufficient part of that method.
VII — Two concepts of literary criticism: negative freedom from context versus positive freedom to understand through historical imagination
1.The proposition offers negative liberty — freedom from social and historical determination by abstracting the text from its world; the opposition offers positive liberty — substantive freedom to actually understand the text by entering its world.
2.Positive liberty in criticism — the substantive freedom to understand — requires historical knowledge and cultural imagination; negative liberty purchases autonomy at the cost of genuine understanding.
3.The greater freedom for the critic is positive: knowing enough history and human experience to enter the world the text comes from, and understand it from within.
∴The two concepts of liberty apply to literary criticism: the proposition's negative freedom from context is real but lesser; the opposition's positive freedom — the substantive capacity to understand — requires and includes historical imagination as an indispensable component.

Raymond Williams and E.P. Thompson were friends, colleagues, and sometimes antagonists. We come to this question from slightly different angles — Williams from literary and cultural criticism, Thompson from social history — but we share a conviction that the proposition before this House rests on what Williams called the "specialization" of culture: the abstraction of certain human practices from the whole way of life in which they are embedded, and the treatment of that abstraction as though it were the real thing. The "text alone" is not the real thing. It is an abstraction. The real thing is the living, complex, socially and historically embedded practice of making and receiving literature — a practice that is always part of what Williams called "a whole way of life," never separable from it, and never properly understood in abstraction from it. I speak for both of us, though Thompson will have some words of his own to add.

On culture as a whole way of life — Williams

Whole way of life: culture is not a specialised aesthetic domain but the whole way in which a society lives and makes meaning — literary texts are part of this wholeMy central argument in Culture and Society was against what I called the specialization of culture — the tendency, accelerating since the Romantic period, to treat culture as a distinct domain of aesthetic activity separated from the "ordinary" world of work, society, and practical life. This specialization is itself a historical product — a consequence of industrialisation, which separated the making of art from the making of other things and created the category of the "artist" as someone distinct from ordinary workers. Against this specialization, I argued that culture is the whole way of life of a people — the total complex of social practices, values, meanings, and ways of experiencing the world that constitutes how a society actually lives. A literary text is part of this whole. It is not a self-enclosed verbal artefact floating above the social world — it is a practice, embedded in the whole complex of practices that constitute a way of life. To understand a literary text, you must understand the whole of which it is a part. That is not an external addition to literary understanding. It is literary understanding.

On "structure of feeling"

Structure of feeling: literature records and embodies the living social experience of its time — the texture of experience that formal social analysis does not captureI developed the concept of "structure of feeling" to describe something that formal social analysis typically cannot capture: the specific quality of living, the texture of experience, the characteristic ways in which people in a particular place and time actually feel and respond to their world. This is not identical with the explicit ideology or the formal social organisation of a period — it is more intimate, more personal, more immediate than either. And it is precisely here — in the structure of feeling — that literature makes its specific contribution. Literature at its best records and embodies the structure of feeling of its time: the actual quality of experience as it was lived, with all its tensions, contradictions, and possibilities. This is why literature is not reducible to its formal social conditions — and also why it cannot be understood as a purely formal linguistic phenomenon. It is the record of a form of life in its full density, and it must be read as such.

On "keywords" — contested meanings as social history

Keywords: the meanings of crucial words are contested and historically changing — understanding literature requires understanding these contestsIn Keywords, I traced the histories of a vocabulary of words — "culture," "class," "art," "aesthetic," "ordinary" — that are central to the discourse of literature and criticism. What I found was that these words are not stable carriers of neutral meanings. They are contested sites: words whose meanings have been fought over, changed, and redefined in the course of social and intellectual history. "Art" did not always mean what it means now. "Culture" carries a complex history of contested definitions that shapes how we use and understand it. "Literature" itself is a contested term — what counts as literature and what does not is a historical and social question, not a purely formal one. When Richards trains his readers to respond to "literature," he is training them to respond to something whose definition is already shaped by a long history of social and cultural contestation. The "trained reader" is not a neutral receiver of textual meaning. He or she is a social and historical product, trained to value certain kinds of texts and responses and to discount others.

On experience and class consciousness — Thompson

Thompson: experience shapes consciousness — class is a cultural as well as an economic formation, and literature is part of how it is made and contestedE.P. Thompson speaking: My argument in The Making of the English Working Class was that the working class did not merely happen to it — that it was not merely an economic category defined by its relationship to the means of production. The working class made itself — through experience, through struggle, through the formation of cultural practices, traditions, values, and ways of understanding the world. Literature — ballads, hymns, political pamphlets, chapbooks, eventually novels and poetry — was part of this process of self-making. It was part of how a class came to understand itself, to articulate its experience, to pass on its knowledge and values to succeeding generations. Now I ask: can you understand this literature — the literature of the English working class — by attending to the language alone? By training your responses as a reader? You cannot. You must understand the experience it records and the consciousness it helped to form. You must understand what it meant to be a handloom weaver in 1820, a Luddite in 1811, a Chartist in 1848. The language is the record of that experience — not a self-contained verbal object but a trace of lives lived under specific and often brutal historical conditions.

On Wittgenstein's On Certainty — the opposition's philosophical foundation

Wittgenstein's On Certainty: all meaning is embedded in forms of life — the certainties we take for granted in reading are social and historical, not natural or universalGeorge Watson includes Wittgenstein in his account of the English theoretical tradition, and rightly so — though Wittgenstein's implications are more subversive of the proposition's case than Watson perhaps recognised. In On Certainty, Wittgenstein argued that the certainties on which our practices of meaning-making are grounded are not universal truths but the inherited, unreflected presuppositions of specific forms of life. "The game of doubting itself presupposes certainty." What we take for granted as readers — what counts as a good reading, what kinds of response are appropriate, what makes a text literary rather than merely verbal — these certainties are the inherited presuppositions of a specific form of life. They are not natural. They are not universal. They are the "bedrock" of a particular practice of reading that is historically and socially constituted. When Richards's students read badly, they were not failing to be human in the universal sense — they were failing to have been formed by the specific form of life that Richards's criticism assumed. Wittgenstein shows us that the proposition's "trained reader" is inside a form of life, not above it.

On whose literature and whose tradition

Whose tradition?: the literary canon reflects the experiences and values of a socially powerful minority — the question of whose literature we are reading is inseparable from the question of how we read itI want to press a question that the proposition entirely ignores: whose literature are we talking about? When Leavis identifies the Great Tradition of the English novel, he is identifying a set of texts produced by educated middle-class writers and addressed primarily to educated middle-class readers, within a tradition of literary culture that was the preserve of a socially powerful minority. The great bulk of cultural production in England — the oral traditions, the popular literature, the ballads, the chapbooks, the broadsheets — is invisible in this tradition. It is not that these texts were judged and found wanting. They were simply not within the purview of the critical tradition that was doing the judging. To ask of a literary text "what does it mean?" without asking "to whom does it mean it?" and "from within what tradition of meaning-making?" is to beg the most important questions. Literary meaning is not neutral and universal. It is produced within specific traditions, for specific communities, from specific social and historical positions.

On what we do not deny — and where the motion goes wrong

Final position: the text matters enormously — but it is part of a whole that must be understood as a whole; the motion severs what cannot be severedLet me be clear about what the opposition does not deny. We do not deny that texts matter — that the specific words of a poem or novel are the primary evidence from which the critic works. We do not deny that close reading is an indispensable skill. We do not deny that literature is a distinctive mode of human expression with its own character and its own achievements. What we deny is the severance — the abstraction of the text from the whole way of life of which it is a part, and the treatment of the text-in-isolation as the proper object of literary criticism. Literary meaning is made — not merely by the writer deploying a language, and not merely by the reader trained to respond to it — but by the whole complex of social, historical, and cultural practices within which both writer and reader are embedded. The motion asks us to say that meaning is primarily in the text and the reader encounter. We say it is primarily in the whole — the whole way of life, the whole form of experience, the whole history of practice and struggle from which both text and reader emerge. And that whole is always, in every instance, a social and historical whole.
