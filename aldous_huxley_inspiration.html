Inspiration

Aldous Huxley — Selected Poems (1925)
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
Structure & Context
Title: 'Inspiration' derives from the Latin inspirare — to breathe into; classically it referred to divine breath entering the poet. Huxley gives this abstraction a physical body: a grasshopper Form: Three quatrains of alternating longer and shorter lines — the short lines enact compression and brevity, the long lines the expansiveness of the Alpine scene Argument: The poem moves from overwhelming, generalised brightness (stanza 1) to a single, blazing exception (stanza 2) to its instantaneous extinction (stanza 3) — the same arc as inspiration itself Setting: Alpine meadows at noon — a landscape of maximum light, where shadow is suppressed and colour intensified; the extreme conditions make the grasshopper's blue legible as something extraordinary Sound: The first stanza is heavy with open vowels and long syllables; the third stanza shifts to short, clipped sounds that enact the brevity of the event Context: Huxley was 21 when he first published poetry; the poem anticipates his later philosophical interest in moments of heightened perception

Noonday upon the Alpine meadows Pours its avalanche of Light And blazing flowers: the very shadows Translucent are and bright. It seems a glory that nought surpasses— Passion of angels in form and hue— When, lo! from the jewelled heaven of the grasses Leaps a lightning of sudden blue. Dimming the sun-drunk petals, Bright even unto pain, The grasshopper flashes, settles, And then is quenched again.
