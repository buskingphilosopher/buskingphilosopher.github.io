Fellow contributors,

Taylor Swift’s songwriting is notable for its direct, clear, and powerful expressions of grief. Three songs in particular — “marjorie,” “Bigger than the Whole Sky,” and “epiphany” — offer something rarer than emotionally resonant lyrics: they connect to contemporary philosophical discussions of grief in ways that both heighten our appreciation of the songs and deepen our understanding of a notoriously complex emotional state.

“marjorie” (evermore, 2020). A song about Swift’s maternal grandmother, Marjorie Finlay, who died in 2003. Swift relates scattered memories and inherited advice, laments not having asked more questions while she could, and reaches an ambiguous conclusion: “I know better, but you’re still around.”

“Bigger than the Whole Sky” (Midnights, 2022). A song of unresolved grief for a person Swift has never fully met — widely interpreted, though never confirmed, as being about miscarriage — containing a self-admonition: “so I’ll say words I don’t believe.”

“epiphany” (folklore, 2020). A song drawing parallels between her grandfather’s experience in the Pacific campaign of World War Two and the collective grief of Covid-19 healthcare workers, built around the repeated line: “some things you just can’t speak about.”
i. grief and the transformation of practical identity

Grief as identity reconstruction — CholbiMichael Cholbi’s philosophy of grief proposes that grieving is a process that transforms, but does not end, our relationship with the deceased. When someone dies, our relationship to them is not simply terminated: just as we continue to be related to people separated from us in space, we remain related to those separated from us in time. The past is real and we are related to it, but the nature of the relationship changes fundamentally. Cholbi connects the depth of grief to how central the deceased was to our practical identity — our sense of who we are as it arises in the context of making decisions and choices. Grief hits hardest when a central plank of that identity falls away. We lose our sense of who we are and how to live. “marjorie” is precisely such an account: Swift’s grandmother was not a close daily companion, but she was central to Swift’s conception of herself as a musician with inherited “backlogged dreams” to fulfil.

ii. the value of grief and self-knowledge

Grief as self-knowledge — anti-grief pill argumentPeople often reflect that, although grief is among the worst forms of suffering in the moment, they would not take an “anti-grief pill” that completely destroyed it. Cholbi proposes that the value of grief consists in the self-knowledge it generates — knowledge of who we are, learned through discovering why the deceased mattered to us and why, often, they mattered so much more than we had realised. “marjorie” exemplifies this: had Swift taken such a pill in 2003, she might never have achieved the self-knowledge about Marjorie’s significance to her identity that the song beautifully crystallises. Swift performs the song on the Eras Tour, leaving fans in no doubt as to Marjorie’s continuing relevance to her life — a public act of memorialisation that is itself a philosophical argument in action, not just a setlist choice.

iii. mythmaking and the limits of false belief

Mythmaking in grief — Bortolotti on delusion and value“Bigger than the Whole Sky” presents a darker and philosophically richer situation. The song’s second verse ends with a self-admonition — “so I’ll say words I don’t believe” — apparently directed at the chorus, which entertains the supernatural possibility that the deceased has far more significance and duration than appearances suggest. This touches on a real debate in philosophy of mind. Lisa Bortolotti and her collaborators have argued that delusions can sustain the sense of “meaning and purpose” in the life of a griever, adding “coherence, directedness and belonging” to the process. The song recognises this as part of grieving, for better or worse. But the Cholbian framework cautions: if too many false beliefs take root, the self-knowledge that makes grief valuable will be put at risk. A few embellishments are inconsequential; a wholesale alternative mythology risks leaving us with a false picture of why the person mattered at all.

iv. collective grief and the limits of representation

Collective grief — epiphany and the unspeakable“epiphany” extends the analysis to collective grief — the grief we feel as a group about losses that have befallen us all. Drawing parallels between the Pacific campaign of World War Two and the suffering of Covid-19 front-line workers, Swift achieves something philosophically important: a gesture of humility toward experiences too traumatic for those living them to put into words. The title itself is ambiguous — the soldiers and doctors “dream of some epiphany,” longing for an understanding their proximity to suffering has not granted them. This resonates with the philosophical problem of collective practical identity: when a society loses over 200,000 people, its shared self-conception — as prepared, as caring for the vulnerable — is also shattered. Collective grief is, like individual grief, an active process of reconstruction. “epiphany” does not provide philosophical analysis of this; it provides an act of recognition, which is the necessary precondition for analysis to begin.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Grief transforms but does not end our relationship to the deceased
1.Cholbi: grief is a process that transforms our relationship to those we have lost — it does not terminate it. The past is real and we remain related to it; the nature of the relationship changes.
2.The depth of grief reflects how central the deceased was to our practical identity — our sense of who we are in the context of decisions and commitments. Loss of a central figure shatters that identity.
3.“marjorie” describes a late, successful stage of this transformation: Marjorie continues to provide advice (through remembered maxims), continues to shape Swift’s goals (inheriting “backlogged dreams”), and is now publicly memorialised on the Eras Tour.
∴Swift’s “marjorie” is a philosophical document of successful grief: a transformed but continuing relationship, not the termination of one.
Argument II — Grief is valuable because it generates self-knowledge
1.People would not take an “anti-grief pill” even though grief is among the worst suffering; the value must lie in what grief achieves.
2.Cholbi: the value lies in the self-knowledge grief generates — we learn who we are by discovering why the deceased mattered to us, why they mattered more than we knew, and how our practical identity is structured.
3.Without grieving Marjorie, Swift might never have crystallised the self-knowledge — about inherited aspiration, about the shape of her musical identity — that “marjorie” so precisely articulates.
∴Swift’s “marjorie” exemplifies the philosophical argument for grief’s value: it is the record of a self-knowledge achieved through grief that could not have been achieved without it.
Argument III — Mythmaking in grief has value and risk; false belief is sometimes unavoidable
1.Bortolotti: delusions and confabulations in grief can sustain meaning, purpose, coherence, and a sense of connection — they should not be rushed to judgment or pathologised.
2.“Bigger than the Whole Sky” depicts a griever recognising that she is saying “words I don’t believe” — the chorus — yet repeating them anyway, over and over, without catharsis.
3.Cholbi’s framework sets a limit: if false beliefs persist through the entire process, the self-knowledge that makes grief valuable is put at risk. Mythmaking is permissible early; it must ultimately yield to truth.
∴“Bigger than the Whole Sky” philosophically maps the terrain between the value and the danger of mythmaking in grief, without resolving it — which is precisely its philosophical honesty.
Argument IV — Collective grief is an active process of reconstructing shared practical identity
1.Collective grief is felt as a group about losses that have befallen us all; it is less studied than individual grief and perhaps harder to understand, but philosophically continuous with it.
2.A collective practical identity — the shared self-conceptions that regulate how we act together — can be shattered by loss just as an individual identity can. It too must be reconstructed.
3.“epiphany” performs an act of collective recognition rather than analysis — a humility before the unspeakable, and a gesture toward the memorialisation that reconstruction requires.
∴“epiphany” is a philosophical contribution to the theory of collective grief: it identifies the problem of representing what “just can’t be spoken about,” and enacts the necessary first step of acknowledgement.

Ladies and gentlemen,

After being awarded an honorary doctorate by NYU in 2022, Taylor Swift gave a commencement speech in which she spoke of the challenge of figuring out who we are: “we are so many things, all the time.” Cultural commentators have added “philosopher” to the long list of things Taylor is. I want to examine whether they are right — and I want to do so rigorously, because the answer is more interesting than it first appears.

“It is not enough to just state your life hack; you must also provide a systematic or methodical way of critically reflecting on it, considering more carefully how you arrived at your belief, and providing reasons to support your view.” — Julian Baggini, as cited in this chapter
i. the ‘life hack’ criterion is too permissive

Life hack criterion — too permissive; everyone qualifiesOne popular understanding of philosophy defines it as sharing words of wisdom and guiding principles for how to live. Under this definition, Taylor is obviously a philosopher. All her albums contain life guidance: from “A Place in This World” to “Vigilante Shit,” she consistently offers prescriptions and principles. Her 2022 NYU commencement speech gave us the instruction to embrace cringe, to treat mistakes as generative, and the memorably Stoic observation that “jet lag is a choice.” But if philosophy just means sharing life wisdom, then virtually everyone qualifies: friends, taxi drivers, strangers on public transport. Edward Craig has called this understanding of philosophy “so broad as to be close to meaningless.” There must be something more.

ii. critical reflection is the additional criterion

Philosophy as critical reflection — systematic scrutiny of beliefsThe additional criterion is critical reflection: the systematic investigation of one’s beliefs and their foundations, providing reasons that can be scrutinised, judged, assessed, accepted or rejected. This is what distinguishes the philosopher from the advice-giver. And here, I argue, Swift’s songwriting does qualify — not in every song, but in the body of work taken as a whole. The Reputation era is not merely a record of events; it is a sustained, self-critical examination of how reputational damage occurs, what one owes to one’s public image, and whether identity can be coherent when it is perpetually contested. The folklore and evermore albums constitute a philosophical investigation into memory, narrative, and self-interpretation conducted through the vehicle of persona and fictional character. This is philosophy in the mode of literature and song, not the mode of the academic paper — but both modes are legitimate.

iii. the medium matters: music as philosophical practice

Music as philosophy — first-person exploration, not just illustrationPhilosophy has traditionally been conducted in argument and prose. But there are powerful precedents for philosophy conducted in other modes: Plato’s dialogues, Kierkegaard’s pseudonymous personae, Nietzsche’s aphorisms. What these share with Swift’s songwriting is the use of a medium that embodies, rather than merely describes, the philosophical content. When Swift records “Anti-Hero” — “it’s me, hi, I’m the problem, it’s me” — she is not merely describing self-criticism; she is performing a first-person philosophical exploration of the conditions under which self-knowledge becomes unbearable. Whether this counts as philosophy depends, as I argued, on whether it meets the criterion of systematic critical reflection. I believe it does, at least in the best cases — and the best cases are numerous enough to be decisive.

iv. the qualified verdict

Qualified verdict — Swift is a philosopher in the qualified senseMy conclusion is deliberately qualified. Not everything Swift has produced is philosophy; not every lyric is a philosophical argument. But the same is true of academic philosophers: not every paper advances a significant position, and not every footnote is philosophically interesting. Judging an entire body of work requires attending to its best instances. At her best — in the sustained examination of reputation and identity, in the genre experiments of folklore and evermore, in the direct philosophical confrontations of “The Man” and “mad woman” — Taylor Swift is doing something that deserves the name philosophy. That this philosophy reaches stadiums of 70,000 people rather than journal subscribers of 700 is not a mark against it. It is, if anything, a mark in its favour.

Thank you.

Argument I — The life-hack criterion is necessary but not sufficient for philosophy
1.One definition of philosophy: sharing words of wisdom and guiding principles for how to live. Under this definition, Swift clearly qualifies — her work is saturated with life guidance from debut to Tortured Poets.
2.But this criterion is too permissive: friends, taxi drivers, and strangers also share life wisdom. Craig: understanding philosophy this way makes it “so broad as to be close to meaningless.”
3.An additional criterion is required that distinguishes philosophical wisdom from ordinary advice-giving.
∴Sharing life wisdom is a necessary but insufficient condition for being a philosopher; Swift must be shown to satisfy the additional criterion of critical reflection.
Argument II — Swift satisfies the additional criterion: systematic critical reflection
1.Baggini: doing philosophy requires a systematic investigation of beliefs, providing reasons that can be scrutinised, judged, assessed, accepted, or rejected — not merely stating a view.
2.Swift’s Reputation era constitutes a sustained, self-critical examination of reputational damage, public identity, and the coherence of selfhood under contestation. The folklore/evermore albums are a philosophical investigation of memory and self-interpretation conducted through persona and fiction.
3.Both satisfy the criterion of systematic critical reflection, in the medium of music rather than the medium of academic prose — but the medium does not disqualify the activity.
∴At her best, Swift satisfies the philosophical criterion of critical reflection; she is a philosopher in a qualified but genuine sense.
Argument III — Music is a legitimate medium for philosophy
1.Philosophy has been conducted in modes other than argument and prose: Plato’s dialogues, Kierkegaard’s pseudonymous personae, Nietzsche’s aphorisms all use form to embody philosophical content.
2.Swift’s songwriting, in its best instances, embodies philosophical exploration rather than merely describing it: “Anti-Hero” performs first-person philosophical investigation of self-knowledge; “The Man” enacts feminist epistemology through counterfactual persona.
3.The legitimacy of a philosophical medium is determined by whether it generates systematic critical reflection, not by its institutional form.
∴Music is a legitimate medium for philosophy; Swift’s best work meets the criterion, and the reach of that work (stadiums vs. journals) is philosophically irrelevant to its legitimacy.
Argument IV — The qualified verdict: philosopher at her best, not in every lyric
1.Not every work by a philosopher is philosophical: academic philosophers produce papers that do not advance significant positions, and footnotes that are not philosophically interesting.
2.Judging a body of work requires attending to its best instances. Swift’s best instances — Reputation, folklore, evermore, “The Man,” “mad woman” — constitute genuine philosophical investigation.
3.The verdict must be qualified: Swift is a philosopher at her best, in a way that her best work demonstrates beyond reasonable doubt.
∴Taylor Swift is, in a qualified but genuine sense, a philosopher: one whose medium is song, whose audience is a stadium, and whose systematic critical reflection on identity, grief, reputation, and knowledge meets the criteria philosophy demands.

Fellow contributors,

Taylor Swift has been called many things: a genius, a mastermind, a victim, a villain. What strikes me philosophically is not any one of these characterisations but rather the goodwill that underlies all of them — the moral credit that Swift has accumulated, spent, and attempted to recover across her career. I want to argue that goodwill, far from being a mere sociological observation about celebrity, is a philosophically substantive concept: a finite, socially distributed moral resource that can be depleted, and whose depletion has real ethical consequences.

“Goodwill is not a gift that, once given, persists forever. It is a resource that must be maintained, that can be drawn upon, and that can run out. Taylor Swift’s career is an extended philosophical demonstration of this truth.”
i. goodwill as a moral resource

Goodwill — finite moral resource, Kantian origins and contemporary applicationThe concept of goodwill has deep roots in moral philosophy. For Kant, a good will was the only thing good without qualification: the will to act from duty for its own sake. In contemporary moral psychology, the concept is more contextual: goodwill is the positive moral regard one person or group extends to another, premised on a history of trustworthy, benevolent, or admirable conduct. This goodwill is not unlimited. It can be extended and it can be drawn upon, and when it is drawn upon excessively — when the account is overdrawn — the moral relationship changes. “This Is Why We Can’t Have Nice Things” is Swift’s most explicit reflection on this: she had extended goodwill (the “nice things”), it was exploited, and the consequence was its withdrawal. This is not a vindictive or petty response — it is the rational behaviour of an agent managing a finite moral resource.

ii. the social distribution of goodwill

Social distribution — goodwill as collective and public, not merely dyadicWhat makes goodwill philosophically interesting in the context of public figures like Swift is that it is not merely dyadic — not simply a relationship between two individuals. It is socially distributed. Swift’s goodwill is held, in a sense, collectively: by her fans, by critics, by the broader public, and by the cultural institutions that engage with her work. When the Kanye West phone call was leaked in 2016, the goodwill — the moral credit — Swift had accumulated was attacked not by a single individual but by a social coalition. The depletion of her goodwill was a collective act, and its recovery required collective restitution. The Reputation album, from this perspective, is not merely a personal artistic statement but a philosophical argument about how public goodwill operates and what it takes to restore it.

iii. the limits of restoration

Limits of restoration — some goodwill cannot be fully recovered once lostNot all moral resources are equally renewable. Some forms of goodwill, once depleted, leave a residue — a wariness, a second-guessing, a reduced willingness to extend benefit of the doubt — that persists even after apparent restoration. Swift’s repeated experiences of having her goodwill exploited — by ex-partners, by industry rivals, by media narratives — have left visible marks on the philosophical content of her work. The woman who wrote “You Belong with Me” and the woman who wrote “The Man” inhabit different moral worlds, not because the facts of the world have changed but because the accumulation of goodwill-depleting events has made certain formerly available trusting stances no longer tenable. This is philosophically significant: it is evidence that goodwill is not merely a strategic resource but a constitutive element of one’s moral relationship to the world.

iv. why this matters beyond celebrity

Beyond celebrity — goodwill depletion in everyday moral lifeI want to insist that what Swift’s career illustrates is not a special feature of celebrity culture. Goodwill depletion, social distribution, and limited restorability are features of moral relationships at every scale: between friends, colleagues, communities, and nations. The celebrity context makes the dynamics unusually visible and the stakes unusually high — which is precisely why it is philosophically useful. By attending closely to how goodwill functions in Swift’s career, we learn something about the structure of moral relationships that more abstract accounts of trust and cooperation tend to obscure. Swift’s songs are philosophical case studies in applied moral philosophy, conducted before an audience of millions.

Thank you.

Argument I — Goodwill is a finite moral resource that can be depleted and drawn upon
1.Goodwill is the positive moral regard one agent extends to another, premised on a history of trustworthy, benevolent, or admirable conduct; it is not unlimited.
2.When goodwill is drawn upon excessively — when it is exploited without reciprocation or betrayed by bad conduct — the moral account is overdrawn and the relationship changes.
3.“This Is Why We Can’t Have Nice Things” describes exactly this: the extension of goodwill, its exploitation, and its rational withdrawal. This is not petty but philosophically coherent behaviour.
∴Goodwill is a finite moral resource; Swift’s career traces its extension, depletion, and withdrawal in ways that illuminate the structure of moral relationships generally.
Argument II — Goodwill is socially distributed, not merely dyadic
1.Public goodwill is held collectively: by audiences, critics, cultural institutions, and the broader public. It is not a relationship between two individuals but between a public figure and a social formation.
2.The 2016 phone-call episode depleted Swift’s goodwill through a collective act of social coalition, not through an individual’s private judgment. Its restoration, likewise, required collective restitution.
3.The Reputation album is, philosophically, an argument about the mechanisms of public goodwill: how it is held collectively, how it is attacked collectively, and what artistic and personal labour is required to recover it.
∴Public goodwill is a socially distributed moral resource; its dynamics cannot be understood through dyadic models of trust alone, and Swift’s career is an extended case study in its collective nature.
Argument III — Some goodwill cannot be fully restored once depleted

2.The philosophical content of Swift’s work across eras reflects this: the moral world of the “Taylor’s Version” songwriter is structurally different from that of the debut-era songwriter, not because the facts changed but because accumulated depletion has made certain trust-stances untenable.
3.This means goodwill is not merely strategic but constitutive: it shapes what moral stances are available to an agent, not just what is instrumentally advantageous.
∴The limited restorability of goodwill once depleted is a philosophically significant feature of moral relationships; Swift’s career is evidence that goodwill is constitutive of moral selfhood, not merely a strategic tool.
Argument IV — Goodwill dynamics in celebrity illuminate moral relationships at every scale
1.The dynamics illustrated by Swift’s career — depletion, social distribution, limited restorability — are not unique to celebrity but are features of moral relationships at every scale.
2.The celebrity context makes these dynamics unusually visible and the stakes unusually high, making it philosophically useful as a case study in applied moral philosophy.
3.Abstract accounts of trust and cooperation tend to obscure these dynamics; attending to concrete, high-stakes cases reveals structure that theory alone cannot.
∴Swift’s songs about goodwill, reputation, and forgiveness are not merely celebrity autobiography; they are philosophical case studies that illuminate the structure of moral relationships generally, conducted before an audience of millions.

Ladies and gentlemen,

Taylor Swift’s “mad woman” opens with a line that is worth sitting with: “what a shame she went mad, no one likes a mad woman.” The song depicts a woman whose anger is dismissed as madness by those around her — a dismissal that functions to delegitimise her emotional response and to protect the person she is angry at from accountability. I want to argue that this is not merely a sociological observation about gender bias. It is an instance of what philosophers have called affective injustice, and that Swift’s song constitutes a serious philosophical engagement with it.

“You needed to control me, you couldn’t let me know you. You made a rebel of a careless man’s careful daughter. You are somebody that I don’t want around.” — Taylor Swift, “mad woman”
i. anger as a moral emotion

Anger as moral emotion — justified response to injusticeAnger, in the philosophical tradition, is not merely a disruptive emotion to be managed or suppressed. It is a moral emotion: it is a response to perceived wrongdoing, and when that wrongdoing is real, the anger is warranted. Aristotle argued that failing to be angry at genuine injustice is itself a moral failure — it reflects either moral insensitivity or a failure to understand that one has been wronged. The figure in “mad woman” has been wronged; her anger is warranted; and the social response is to diagnose her anger as madness rather than engage with its content. This is a classic move in the suppression of legitimate grievance, and it has a long history in the treatment of women’s emotions specifically.

ii. affective injustice as epistemic injustice

Affective injustice — epistemic wrong done to emotional testimonyMiranda Fricker’s influential account of epistemic injustice identifies two forms: testimonial injustice (giving someone’s word insufficient credibility due to prejudice) and hermeneutical injustice (lacking the conceptual resources to understand one’s own experience). Affective injustice adds a third form: systematically dismissing or discrediting someone’s emotional responses, particularly their anger, as evidence of irrationality rather than treating them as morally significant communications. When the figure in “mad woman” is told she has “gone mad,” what is being denied is not only her testimonial credibility but her affective credibility — the standing of her anger as a legitimate moral signal. This is an injustice distinct from, though related to, the epistemic injustices Fricker describes.

iii. the gendered structure of mad-woman discourse

Gendered injustice — mad woman trope in history and SwiftThe “mad woman” trope has a specific historical and gendered structure. From the hysterical women of nineteenth-century medicine to the “crazy ex-girlfriend” of contemporary popular culture, the attribution of madness to women expressing strong emotions — particularly anger — has functioned as a tool of social control: a way of delegitimising women’s emotional testimony without engaging its content. Swift’s song makes this structure explicit. The line “every time you call me crazy, I get more crazy” captures the self-fulfilling structure of the attribution: the more the woman protests, the more her protest is taken as evidence of her instability. This is not a logical error by the woman; it is a rigged epistemic game played against her.

iv. song as refusal: the philosophical significance of form

Song as philosophical refusal — the form enacts what the content describesWhat makes “mad woman” philosophically distinctive is not only its content but its form. By writing and performing this song, Swift is not merely describing affective injustice; she is refusing it. The song is an act of affective assertion in the face of a social structure designed to delegitimise such assertions. This is philosophically significant because it demonstrates that the appropriate response to affective injustice is not merely analytical description but active counter-testimony: the insistence, in public, on the legitimacy of one’s anger. The song’s viral reception — its adoption by millions of women who recognised their own experiences in it — suggests it functioned precisely as such counter-testimony, at a collective scale.

Thank you.

1.Anger is a moral emotion: a response to perceived wrongdoing that, when the wrongdoing is real, is warranted and morally significant.
2.Aristotle: failure to be angry at genuine injustice is itself a moral failure — evidence of insensitivity or of failure to recognise that one has been wronged.
3.The figure in “mad woman” has been genuinely wronged; her anger is warranted; the social response is to diagnose the anger rather than engage with the wrongdoing.
∴The “mad woman” dismissal is doubly unjust: it ignores the wrongdoing that warranted the anger and compounds it by treating a morally appropriate response as a symptom of pathology.
Argument II — Affective injustice is a form of epistemic injustice distinct from Fricker’s two types
1.Fricker identifies testimonial injustice (deflated credibility for testimony) and hermeneutical injustice (lacking concepts for one’s own experience). Both are epistemic wrongs grounded in identity prejudice.
2.Affective injustice is the systematic dismissal of someone’s emotional responses — particularly anger — as evidence of irrationality rather than treating them as morally significant communications.
3.Denying affective credibility is distinct from denying testimonial credibility: it targets not what the person says but the emotional register in which they say it, disqualifying the form of the communication before engaging its content.
∴Affective injustice is a philosophically distinct third form of epistemic injustice; “mad woman” illustrates it precisely: the figure’s anger is dismissed before its content is engaged.

∴The mad-woman trope is a rigged epistemic game: its rules ensure that any protest by the angry woman is taken as further evidence of her irrationality, making the game unwinnable from within.
Argument IV — The song is an act of affective counter-testimony, not merely description
1.The appropriate response to a rigged epistemic game is not to play by its rules but to refuse the game: to assert affective credibility publicly, in the face of the social structure designed to deny it.

3.The song’s collective reception — adopted by millions of women who recognised their own experiences — demonstrates that it functioned as collective counter-testimony, challenging the social structures of affective injustice at scale.
∴The philosophical significance of “mad woman” lies in its form as well as its content: it is an act of affective refusal that cannot be absorbed by the epistemic game it refuses, and that achieves collective philosophical work by doing so.

Ladies and gentlemen,

Taylor Swift’s songs about heartbreak are frequently songs about forgiveness — or about the refusal of it. From “Should’ve Said No” on her debut to “Don’t You” on The Tortured Poets Department, she has returned repeatedly to the question of whether and under what conditions one can and should forgive someone who has broken one’s heart. I want to argue that this is a philosophically serious engagement with one of the genuinely contested questions in moral philosophy: whether forgiveness is an obligation, a gift, or something else entirely.

The philosophical question. When someone wrongs us, do we owe them forgiveness if they express genuine remorse? Or is forgiveness a gift that we may bestow but are under no obligation to give? And is the refusal to forgive — the stance Swift dramatises in song after song — ever morally permissible, or does it always represent a failure of moral generosity?
i. forgiveness is not the same as condoning or excusing

Forgiveness distinguished from condoning — distinct moral conceptsThe first philosophical clarification is a distinction. Forgiveness is distinct from condoning (treating the wrong as not a wrong), from excusing (attributing the wrong to circumstances rather than character), and from reconciliation (restoring the relationship). One can forgive someone without condoning what they did, without excusing them, and without resuming the relationship. Swift’s songs frequently exhibit this distinction: in “Back to December,” the narrator forgives (or attempts to) without resuming the relationship; in “Should’ve Said No,” forgiveness is considered and declined. The songs presuppose that forgiveness and reconciliation are separable, which is philosophically correct and often overlooked in popular discussion.

ii. is forgiveness an obligation?

Forgiveness as obligation — rival views and Swift’s implicit positionThere are two broad philosophical positions. The first holds that when a wrongdoer genuinely repents — when they acknowledge the wrong, express remorse, and commit to changing — forgiveness becomes an obligation for the wronged party. The second holds that forgiveness is always a supererogatory gift: it goes beyond what morality requires and cannot be owed. Swift’s songs tend to occupy the second position. In “Should’ve Said No,” the partner asks to be forgiven; Swift’s narrator declines. The song does not present this as morally problematic — it does not suggest she is failing in a duty. The refusal is presented as a legitimate exercise of the victim’s prerogative. This is philosophically interesting: Swift implicitly endorses a view of forgiveness as a gift, not an obligation.

iii. the cost of forgiving: why it is difficult

Cost of forgiveness — emotional labour and the standing of the victimForgiveness is not merely a decision; it is an achievement. i. what is an easter egg? the problem of authorial intention

Easter eggs and authorial intention — Wimsatt & Beardsley vs. intentionalismAn Easter egg, in Swift’s usage, is a piece of information intentionally hidden by the artist that rewards attentive fans who discover it. This immediately raises a philosophical question: how do we distinguish a genuine Easter egg from a coincidence that fans have mistakenly interpreted as intentional? The debate between intentionalism and anti-intentionalism in philosophy of art is directly relevant here. Wimsatt and Beardsley’s “intentional fallacy” argued that the author’s intention is irrelevant to the meaning of a text; what matters is the meaning the work has as a public object. Applied to Easter eggs: on this view, whether Swift “meant” a particular clue is irrelevant to whether it functions as a clue. But this seems wrong. An Easter egg is constitutively intentional: if Swift did not mean it, it is not an Easter egg but a coincidence. ii. the epistemology of fan knowledge

Fan epistemology — testimony, pattern-recognition, and warranted beliefThe Swifties’ interpretive practices raise questions about the epistemology of fan knowledge. When a Swiftie claims to have decoded an Easter egg, are they expressing knowledge or opinion? For the claim to count as knowledge, it must be true, believed, and justified. The first condition is the hardest: an Easter egg claim is true only if Swift intended the clue. But Swift rarely confirms or denies — and when she does confirm, she confirms only what has already been published. The Swifties operate in an environment of radical epistemic uncertainty, relying on pattern recognition, intertextual knowledge, and community consensus. This is not entirely unlike scientific reasoning in data-poor environments, where background knowledge and pattern recognition must substitute for direct confirmation. The epistemology of fan knowledge is philosophically rich precisely because it is both seriously motivated and genuinely uncertain.

Co-construction of meaning — Swift’s Easter eggs as collaborative artSwift has explicitly acknowledged that she plants Easter eggs and enjoys watching fans decode them. This creates an unusual philosophical situation: the artist has designed her work partly as a puzzle for an interpretive community to solve.
