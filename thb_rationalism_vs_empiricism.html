Mr Speaker,

The question before this House is whether the mind contributes innate structure to knowledge or whether it is, as my opponents would have it, a passive wax tablet on which the senses inscribe their impressions. I shall argue not merely that rationalism is more defensible but that empiricism, pursued honestly to its conclusion, destroys the very possibility of the certain knowledge it claims to explain.

My method in the Meditations is to doubt everything that can be doubted — the testimony of the senses, the existence of the external world, even the truths of mathematics when a malicious demon might be deceiving me at every turn. What survives this radical sceptical assault? Precisely one thing: the existence of the thinking self. Cogito, ergo sum. The certainty of my own existence as a thinking thing is delivered not by the senses but by pure rational self-reflection. This is the first and most fundamental demonstration that reason, not sense, is the faculty capable of delivering genuine certainty.

The structure of the rationalist argument.

The senses are systematically unreliable: they deceive us about shape, size, colour, distance, and even the existence of the objects they apparently reveal. Any epistemology that grounds knowledge in sensation is therefore building on sand. What the senses provide is not knowledge but provisional, revisable, fallible opinion.

Certain knowledge requires a foundation immune to doubt. The rationalist identifies this foundation in: (i) the immediate self-evidence of the thinking self; (ii) clear and distinct ideas, whose truth is guaranteed by the non-deceptive nature of God; (iii) innate concepts — God, infinity, substance, mathematical truths — that are present in the mind prior to all experience and could not have been derived from any finite sensory input.
i. the unreliability of the senses

Sensory unreliability — senses systematically deceive; knowledge requires an extra-sensory foundation; empiricism builds on sandThe empiricist programme begins by trusting the senses. But the senses have deceived us before and will deceive us again: the straight oar appears bent in water, the distant tower appears small, the dream appears real until we wake. The empiricist cannot simply dismiss these cases as peripheral: they reveal a structural feature of sensory experience, namely that what appears is not reliably what is. If sensory experience is to be the foundation of all knowledge, the empiricist must explain how we distinguish reliable from unreliable appearances. But every proposed criterion for distinguishing them — vividness, coherence, repeatability — is itself derived from experience and is therefore susceptible to the same doubt. The sceptical regress cannot be stopped by experience alone. The only escape is to identify a source of certainty that does not depend on the senses: the clear and distinct perception of the intellect.

ii. the cogito as an innate rational insight

Cogito — certainty delivered by pure reason not sensation; first secure foundation; empiricism cannot replicate itThe cogito is not derived from sensory experience. When I attend carefully to my own existence as a thinking thing, the certainty I achieve is immediate and indefeasible: it is impossible for me to doubt my own existence at the very moment I am doubting, because doubting is a form of thinking, and thinking requires a thinker. This certainty is delivered by pure rational reflection on the nature of thought itself. No empiricist account can replicate it: the certainty of the cogito does not depend on any sensory input, is not defeasible by any counter-observation, and could not have been reached by induction from experience. It is a paradigm case of a priori knowledge: knowledge acquired by reason alone, without dependence on the senses.

iii. the innateness of the idea of God and infinity

Innate idea of God — infinity cannot be derived from finite experience; must be innate; Locke's derivation story fails hereConsider the idea of an infinite and perfect being. No sensory experience could have supplied this idea: everything I encounter in experience is finite, imperfect, and bounded. The idea of infinity is not merely the negation of finitude — I cannot reach infinity by accumulating finite experiences or by negating them. Yet I possess this idea, and I can think with it. The only explanation consistent with my having the idea is that it was placed in me by an infinite being itself — that the idea is innate, stamped on my mind at creation. This is not a theological eccentricity; it is an epistemological argument. The content of the idea of infinity exceeds what any finite sensory input could provide. Innate ideas are the only explanation for concepts whose content is not derivable from experience.

iv. clear and distinct ideas as the criterion of truth

Clear and distinct ideas — a priori criterion of truth; rational clarity outreaches empirical verification; rationalism's epistemic advantageOnce the existence of a non-deceptive God is established, a criterion of truth becomes available: whatever I perceive clearly and distinctly is true. This criterion is rationalist through and through: clarity and distinctness are properties of rational intellection, not of sensory appearance. The truths of pure mathematics and geometry — that the angles of a triangle sum to two right angles, that the properties of a circle follow necessarily from its definition — are perceived with perfect clarity and distinctness by the intellect, and are therefore certain. They are not made certain by any number of observations; they are already certain, prior to any observation, by virtue of the rational structure of the ideas themselves. This is what empiricism cannot account for: the apodeictic certainty of necessary truths.

Mr Locke has proposed that the mind at birth is a blank sheet of paper, innocent of all characters, waiting for the senses to write upon it. I wish to show that this celebrated image is not merely false but incoherent — that the very act of receiving and interpreting sensory impressions presupposes structural resources in the mind that the senses cannot themselves have supplied.

My counter-image is this: the mind is not a blank sheet but a block of veined marble. The veins are already present before the sculptor begins work; they determine which shapes the marble can take and which forms will emerge under the chisel. Experience is the sculptor — it reveals and actuates what is latent — but the structure it reveals was never absent. The innate ideas and principles of the intellect are the veins; they are prior to experience without being independently conscious prior to the occasions that trigger them.

“There is nothing in the intellect that was not first in the senses — except the intellect itself.” — Leibniz, New Essays, 1704
i. the marble block and the limits of the blank slate

Marble block — innate structural tendencies; experience reveals, not creates; blank slate cannot explain necessary truthsLocke’s blank slate cannot explain how the mind comes to know necessary truths. Suppose the mind really is blank at birth: how does it come to know that every event has a sufficient reason, that contradictions cannot both be true, that equals added to equals yield equals? Locke will say: by abstraction from experience. But this answer fails immediately. Necessary truths are necessarily true — they hold in all possible cases, not merely in the cases we have observed. Experience, however extensive, can only establish what has been the case, not what must be the case in every possible case. The generalization from observed regularities to necessary principles requires an inferential step that experience alone cannot license. The marble block metaphor captures this: the veins of necessary connection are already in the stone; the sculptor does not create them but merely traces what was latent.

ii. the principle of sufficient reason is not derived from experience

Sufficient reason — cannot be inductively derived; operative in all reasoning including against Locke; must be innateConsider the principle of sufficient reason: nothing occurs without a reason why it is so rather than otherwise. This principle is employed in every act of reasoning, including the empiricist’s own reasoning about the origin of ideas. If Locke argues that all ideas come from experience, he implicitly relies on the principle that there must be a reason why an idea has the content it has — namely that it was produced by a corresponding experience. But the principle of sufficient reason is not itself derivable from experience: we cannot observe the reason for everything, and no finite sequence of observations could establish that everything has a reason. The principle is therefore operative in the mind prior to any experience. It is a paradigm case of an innate logical principle that underlies, rather than derives from, experiential reasoning.

iii. the petites perceptions and the unconscious structure of mind

Petites perceptions — innate structure operates below consciousness; Locke conflates consciousness with mentality; innateness not disproved by lack of awarenessLocke’s most common objection to innate ideas is that children and idiots are not aware of them; if the principles were truly present in the mind, one would expect universal conscious recognition. But this objection rests on a confusion between being in the mind and being consciously attended to. I distinguish petites perceptions — minute, unconscious perceptions that accumulate to produce conscious impressions — from the fully conscious ideas they give rise to. Innate structural principles may be present in the mind and operative in its processing without ever being objects of explicit conscious attention. The absence of conscious recognition of innate principles is therefore no evidence against their presence; it merely shows that the mind has a depth that consciousness does not exhaust.

iv. necessary truths require a faculty that transcends sense

Necessary truths — require a faculty of pure intellect; sense only gives contingent particulars; Leibniz's modal argument against empiricismSensory experience gives us contingent particulars: this flame is hot, this stone is hard, this piece of gold dissolves in aqua regia. It never gives us necessity. But the truths of logic and mathematics are not merely general; they are necessary — they could not be otherwise in any possible world. No accumulation of contingent particular facts could ever yield knowledge of a necessary truth, because the modal gap between “has always been the case in my experience” and “must be the case in all possible cases” cannot be bridged by induction. The intellect must therefore possess a faculty — the faculty of pure reason — that apprehends necessity directly, not through the detour of sense. This faculty and its deliverances are innate in the only sense that matters: they are prior to, and not derivable from, sensory experience.

2.The blank slate model requires that all content, including necessary principles, be derived from experience; but experience cannot supply the modal force of necessity.
3.The marble block metaphor correctly represents the situation: structural tendencies (the veins) are present prior to experience, which reveals them rather than creating them.
∴The blank slate cannot account for knowledge of necessary truths; the marble block model, in which innate structural tendencies are prior to experience, is the only adequate account.

2.This principle cannot itself be derived from experience: no finite sequence of observations establishes that everything has a reason, and the principle must hold universally and necessarily to do its logical work.
3.A principle that is presupposed by empirical reasoning but not derivable from it must be present in the mind prior to experience — it is innate in the epistemologically relevant sense.
∴The principle of sufficient reason is an innate logical principle, operative prior to experience and presupposed by the very empiricist arguments deployed against innateness.

2.The modal gap between “has always been observed to be the case” and “must be the case in every possible world” cannot be bridged by induction; no number of observations licenses the modal claim.
3.Knowledge of necessary truths therefore requires a faculty of pure reason that apprehends necessity directly; this faculty is prior to and not derivable from sensory experience.
∴The modal gap is decisive: knowledge of necessity requires a faculty that transcends sense; empiricism cannot bridge this gap, and the rationalist faculty of pure reason is required to explain how necessary truths are known.

The debate between rationalism and empiricism has been transformed in the twentieth century by the scientific study of language acquisition. What was once a purely philosophical dispute can now be settled — at least in significant part — by empirical evidence. And the evidence, I shall argue, decisively favours the rationalist position.

Every normal human child, regardless of culture, native language, or environment, acquires a grammar of extraordinary complexity within the first few years of life. The grammar they acquire is not taught: parents do not consciously instruct children in the rules of phrase structure, in the principles governing long-distance dependencies, in the conditions on wh-movement. The child induces these rules from a stimulus that is impoverished relative to the grammar it generates. This is the Poverty of the Stimulus argument, and it is the single most powerful argument in the modern debate for the nativist position.

The Poverty of the Stimulus argument, schematically.

Premise 1: Every normal child converges on a rich, abstract, recursive grammar by age five or six.
Premise 2: The input available to the child (parental speech, corrective feedback) is insufficient to determine the grammar uniquely: it is finite, contains errors and false starts, and does not include direct instruction in the abstract principles the child commands.
Premise 3: The child’s grammar is underdetermined by the input — infinitely many grammars are consistent with the input the child receives.

Conclusion: The child must bring to the task of language acquisition innate constraints — a Universal Grammar — that restrict the hypothesis space to those grammars that could be human languages. Without such constraints, acquisition is impossible.
i. the poverty of the stimulus as a scientific demonstration of nativism

Poverty of stimulus — input underdetermines grammar; Universal Grammar required; scientific, not just philosophicalThe poverty of the stimulus argument is not a philosophical thought experiment; it is a scientific claim that can be tested and has been tested. The claim is that the input children receive during acquisition is insufficient to determine the grammar they acquire. Evidence includes: the fact that children never make certain errors that would be predicted by simple inductive learning (they never treat “Is the man who is tall happy?” as the question form of “The man who is tall is happy,” moving the first auxiliary rather than the main-clause auxiliary); the fact that no instruction in these rules is needed; the fact that acquisition converges across radically different languages and environments. None of this is explicable if the child is simply associating stimuli with responses. A prior structural framework is required.

ii. the universality of language structure

Universal Grammar — structural universals across languages; cannot be explained by shared experience; must be biologically specifiedAll human languages, however superficially different, share deep structural properties: they all have recursive phrase structure, they all have a distinction between noun and verb phrases, they all have ways of expressing predication, quantification, and modification. These universals are not explicable by appeal to shared experience: speakers of different languages have radically different environments and cultures. Nor are they explicable by functional pressure alone: many of the specific structural principles of Universal Grammar (such as structure-dependence) have no functional motivation — they simply are the principles that all human languages obey. The only explanation is that these structural properties are biologically specified, part of the genetically determined capacity for language that all normal humans possess.

iii. against behaviourism and associationist empiricism

Against Skinner — Verbal Behavior refuted; associationist learning cannot produce recursive grammars; productivity argumentThe empiricist approach to language acquisition reached its most developed form in Skinner’s Verbal Behavior (1957), which proposed that language is a set of verbal responses reinforced by the environment. My review of that work demonstrated its inadequacy point by point. Language is not a set of learned stimulus-response associations: it is a generative system that allows the production and comprehension of indefinitely many novel sentences, including sentences of arbitrary complexity, that the speaker has never encountered before. No reinforcement history could produce this creative capacity. The child who has acquired a grammar can generate and understand sentences that were never in the input. This productivity of language is inexplicable on any empiricist, associationist account; it requires a finite set of abstract recursive rules that is not derivable from the finite stimulus.

iv. the Cartesian connection: innate ideas vindicated by linguistics

Cartesian linguistics — Descartes's innate ideas vindicated by modern linguistics; rational grammar tradition; mind contributes structureThe rationalist tradition from Descartes through the Port-Royal grammarians anticipated the central insight of modern generative linguistics: that language use is a creative capacity grounded in rule-governed structure, that the rules are too abstract to be derived from experience, and that they must therefore be innate. What I have done in the scientific study of language is to provide a rigorous, empirically testable specification of what those innate structures are. The Cartesian insight — that the mind contributes abstract structural properties to its engagement with the world — is not a piece of armchair metaphysics; it is a scientific hypothesis with specific, testable predictions. And it has been confirmed.

Argument I — The poverty of the stimulus proves that the input underdetermines the grammar; a Universal Grammar is required
1.Every child acquires a rich, abstract, recursive grammar from input that is finite, degenerate, and insufficient to determine the grammar uniquely; infinitely many grammars are consistent with the observed input.
2.Children uniformly converge on grammars that obey specific abstract principles (e.g. structure-dependence) for which there is no positive evidence in the input and which they are never explicitly taught.
3.The only explanation for this convergence under underdetermination is innate constraints — a Universal Grammar — that restrict the hypothesis space of possible human grammars.
∴The poverty of the stimulus is a scientific demonstration that language acquisition requires innate structural constraints; empiricist learning theory cannot explain the convergence of acquisition under radically underdetermined input.
Argument II — Structural universals across languages require a biologically specified Universal Grammar
1.All human languages share deep structural properties (recursive phrase structure, noun/verb distinction, predication, quantification) despite radical surface differences and radically different cultural environments.
2.These universals cannot be explained by shared experience (environments differ too radically) or by functional pressure alone (many structural principles have no functional motivation).
3.The only explanation is that these properties are biologically specified — part of the genetically determined language faculty.
∴Structural universals across languages are evidence for a biologically specified Universal Grammar; they cannot be explained by experience-based learning and confirm the nativist hypothesis.
Argument III — The productivity of language refutes behaviourist associationism; generative grammar cannot be learned from a finite stimulus

The empiricist picture of the mind is of a general-purpose learning device that accumulates associations from experience and constructs its competences by induction from sensory input. I want to argue that this picture is not merely incomplete but radically wrong. The mind is not a general-purpose learning machine; it is an architecture of specialized, informationally encapsulated, domain-specific modules, each of which processes a specific type of input in ways that are structurally prior to, and independent of, conscious experience.

This is not merely a philosophical thesis. It is a scientific hypothesis with specific architectural predictions, and the evidence from cognitive neuropsychology, psycholinguistics, and developmental psychology strongly supports it. The modularity of mind is the contemporary form of the rationalist claim that the mind contributes innate structure to cognition.

“The question is not whether there is a modular organization of the mind but whether, and to what extent, it is innate. The answer, I believe, is: to a very great extent.” — Fodor, The Modularity of Mind, 1983
i. the properties of cognitive modules

Module properties — domain-specific, informationally encapsulated, innate, fast, mandatory; architecture prior to experienceA cognitive module, in my technical sense, has several defining properties. It is domain-specific: it processes only one type of input (syntactic structures, visual contours, facial configurations). It is informationally encapsulated: its processing is not influenced by beliefs held elsewhere in the cognitive system; it takes only its proprietary inputs and computes its outputs independently of the subject’s higher-level knowledge. It is mandatory: its operation is compelled by the presence of its triggering stimulus, not subject to voluntary control. It is fast: it operates below the level of deliberate reasoning. And it is associated with specific neural architecture: damage to the relevant neural substrate disrupts the module specifically, not cognition generally. These properties collectively show that the processing architecture is not assembled from experience; it is prior to experience and constrains what experience can contribute.

ii. informational encapsulation refutes associationist empiricism

Encapsulation — Müller-Lyer persists despite knowledge; module outputs not revisable by belief; associationism cannot explain thisThe most striking evidence for informational encapsulation is the persistence of perceptual illusions despite full conscious knowledge that the percept is illusory. The Müller-Lyer illusion — in which two lines of equal length appear different because of the arrowhead configurations at their ends — persists even in subjects who know the lines are equal, have measured them, and have been told the mechanism of the illusion. If perception were assembled from associations and beliefs, the illusion should dissolve when the relevant beliefs are in place. It does not. This shows that the perceptual system is informationally encapsulated: it processes its input in isolation from the subject’s beliefs. The perceptual architecture is prior to and independent of the experience-derived beliefs that could correct it. This is precisely the rationalist structure: mental processing that does not await the deliverances of experience.

iii. double dissociations and the neural basis of modules

Double dissociations — selective damage proves modularity; Williams syndrome, prosopagnosia show domain-specific innate systemsNeuropsychological evidence provides the most direct support for the modularity hypothesis. Double dissociations — cases in which damage to one neural region impairs one cognitive capacity without affecting others, while damage to a different region produces the complementary pattern — show that cognitive capacities are implemented in distinct, specialized neural systems. Prosopagnosia (inability to recognize faces despite preserved object recognition) dissociates from object recognition, showing that face recognition is a dedicated module. Williams syndrome (preserved language and social cognition with severely impaired spatial reasoning) dissociates language from general intelligence. These patterns are impossible on the empiricist picture of a general-purpose associative device; they require innate, specialized systems that can be selectively damaged.

iv. against the empiricist concept-empiricism

Against concept-empiricism — Fodor's concept-nativism; concepts are not assembled from sensory experience; radical concept nativismThe modularity thesis has an implication that empiricists find particularly unwelcome: radical concept nativism. If concepts are the outputs of encapsulated modules, they are not assembled from sensory primitives in the way that empiricist concept-empiricism requires. The concept <dog> is not built up from associated sensory features — brown, four-legged, barking — into a complex associative bundle. It is a primitive, computationally atomic unit, triggered by the module’s exposure to the relevant stimuli. The concept has its content not in virtue of its sensory associations but in virtue of its inferential and causal relations within the cognitive system. This is a thoroughly rationalist picture: the conceptual structure of the mind is prior to and not constructed from sensory experience.

2.These properties cannot be assembled from experience: informational encapsulation, in particular, means that the module’s processing is not modified by experientially acquired beliefs.
3.The processing architecture must therefore be structurally prior to and independent of the experiential input it processes.
∴The modular architecture of the mind is innate and structurally prior to experience; the empiricist picture of a general-purpose experiential learning device is architecturally incompatible with the defining properties of cognitive modules.

The contemporary form of the rationalist thesis concerns the a priori: the class of propositions knowable by reason independently of experience. Empiricists have repeatedly claimed that the a priori is either empty (nothing is knowable a priori) or trivial (only tautologies and definitional truths are a priori). My task is to show that the a priori is neither empty nor trivial but is, on the contrary, the load-bearing architecture of all our knowledge of the world.

The scrutability thesis is this: all truths about the world are a priori scrutable from a minimal base of truths. To say that a truth T is scrutable from a base B is to say that a fully informed, ideally rational agent who knows all the truths in B could in principle determine the truth-value of T by a priori reasoning alone — without any additional empirical investigation beyond the base.

The scrutability thesis and the Aufbau project rehabilitated.

The proposition asks us to believe that the human mind arrives in the world furnished with ideas — of God, infinity, mathematical truth, logical principles — that are present prior to all experience and that no experience could have produced. I find this doctrine not only false but superfluous: wherever the rationalist invokes an innate idea, the empiricist can provide a better, simpler account by tracing the idea to its sensory or reflective origin.

Let me be clear about what the tabula rasa means and does not mean. I do not claim that the mind is empty at birth in some literal sense, as if it had no biological nature or capacities. This is the empiricist thesis, and I shall defend it against the rationalist on its own ground.

The empiricist origin of ideas: sensation and reflection.

All simple ideas come from one of two sources. External sensation: the senses report on the external world, giving us ideas of qualities like colour, sound, heat, shape, and motion. Internal reflection: the mind attends to its own operations — perceiving, thinking, doubting, willing — and forms ideas of those operations.

Complex ideas are formed by combining, abstracting from, and relating simple ideas. The idea of “God” is formed by taking the ideas of existence, duration, knowledge, power, pleasure, and happiness derived from reflection, and enlarging each idea to infinity by repetition. There is no need to posit any innate content; the compositional apparatus of the mind, working on experiential materials, is sufficient to generate all the ideas we possess.
i. the argument from universal consent fails

i. Hume’s Fork stated

Hume's Fork — all propositions are either relations of ideas (a priori, empty) or matters of fact (a posteriori, contingent); no synthetic a prioriRelations of ideas are propositions whose truth or falsity is determined by the meanings of the terms alone: “All bachelors are unmarried,” “the angles of a triangle sum to 180 degrees” (given the definitions of geometry). Their denials are self-contradictory. They are knowable with certainty a priori, but they are empty of factual content: they tell us nothing about what actually exists in the world, only about the relations among ideas. Matters of fact are propositions about what actually exists: “the sun will rise tomorrow,” “fire causes heat.” Their denials are conceivable; they are contingent; and they are knowable only through experience. There is no middle category: a proposition that is both certainly true by reason alone and genuinely informative about the world is a philosophical fiction.

My idea of causation is derived entirely from the observed regularity of conjunction: I see A followed by B, repeatedly, and my mind forms a habit of expecting B upon the occurrence of A. The problem of induction is not merely a puzzle to be solved but a demonstration of the limits of both reason and experience: neither can supply the ultimate justification for our most important empirical inferences. I wish to take Hume’s devastating critique of rationalist metaphysics and give it the rigorous logical form it deserves. The verification principle of meaning provides the sharpest available tool for exposing the cognitive vacuity of rationalist claims: any sentence that is neither analytically true nor empirically verifiable is not false but literally meaningless — it is not a genuine proposition at all but a pseudo-proposition, a grammatically well-formed string of words with no cognitive content.

This principle does not merely dismiss rationalist metaphysics; it explains precisely why it fails. The rationalist proposition that “every event has a sufficient reason” is not refuted by counter-evidence because no evidence could count against it; but equally, no evidence could confirm it. A proposition that is immune to all possible evidence is empty of empirical content. It is, in the relevant sense, nonsense.

The verification principle stated.

A sentence S has literal (cognitive) meaning if and only if one of the following holds:
(1) S is analytic: true in virtue of the meanings of its terms; its denial is self-contradictory.
(2) S is empirically verifiable: there exist in principle possible observations that would confirm or disconfirm S.

All other sentences — the metaphysical pronouncements of rationalist philosophy, theological claims, aesthetic value judgements in their cognitive pretensions — are pseudo-propositions. They may express emotions, prescriptions, or attitudes, but they make no genuine claims about how the world is.

Verification principle — only analytic or empirically verifiable sentences have cognitive meaning; rationalist metaphysics is meaninglessThe verification principle is not itself a first-order claim about the world; it is a proposal about the correct use of the word “meaningful” in the context of cognitive discourse. It captures a genuine distinction between sentences that, as it were, earn their keep — by being either logically necessary or empirically answerable — and sentences that merely resemble genuine propositions without actually engaging with how the world is. When the rationalist asserts that “the mind has innate ideas derived from rational intuition,” I ask: what observable difference would this make? What would the world look like if this were true rather than false? If no possible observation distinguishes the two cases, the assertion is empty.

I am an empiricist, and I am about to demolish one of empiricism’s own most cherished dogmas. My aim is not to rehabilitate rationalism — the conclusion of my argument remains firmly empiricist — but to replace an indefensible form of empiricism with a more robust one. The distinction between analytic and synthetic truths, on which both Ayer’s positivism and the rationalists’ claim to a priori knowledge depend, is a dogma that does not withstand scrutiny.

I identify two dogmas of empiricism. The first is the belief in a fundamental cleavage between analytic truths (true by meaning) and synthetic truths (true by fact). The second is reductionism: the belief that each meaningful statement has a determinate empirical content that could in principle be confirmed or disconfirmed by specific observations. Both dogmas are indefensible, and abandoning them leads to a holism that I take to be the correct form of empiricism.

“The totality of our so-called knowledge or beliefs, from the most casual matters of geography and history to the profoundest laws of atomic physics or even of pure mathematics and logic, is a man-made fabric which impinges on experience only along its edges.” — Quine, Two Dogmas, 1951
i. the analytic–synthetic distinction is untenable

No analytic-synthetic distinction — synonymy is circular; no non-circular definition of analyticity; the distinction collapsesThe notion of analytic truth requires the notion of synonymy: a sentence is analytic if it becomes a logical truth when synonyms are substituted for synonyms. But what is synonymy? It cannot be defined as intersubstitutability salva veritate in all contexts (including intensional ones) without circularity: intensional contexts are defined using belief and necessity operators, and necessity is defined in terms of analyticity. Every attempted definition of analyticity either circles back through synonymy, necessity, or meaning in a way that presupposes what it is trying to explain. The distinction between truths that hold “come what may” and truths that are vulnerable to experience cannot be drawn with any precision; it dissolves on examination into a spectrum of degrees of entrenchment.

ii. holism: beliefs face experience as a corporate body

Holism — no belief faces experience alone; any belief can be held in face of recalcitrant evidence; including mathematics and logicThe second dogma — reductionism — holds that each statement has its own determinate empirical content. But this is false. When experience conflicts with our web of beliefs, we do not know which belief to revise: any belief can in principle be maintained in the face of recalcitrant experience by making adjustments elsewhere in the web. Conversely, any belief — including beliefs in mathematics and logic — is in principle revisable in the face of strong enough reason. Non-Euclidean geometries were adopted when needed for the physics of spacetime; quantum logic has been proposed as a revision of classical logic in the light of quantum mechanics. The belief that logic and mathematics are immune to revision is just the belief that they are very deeply entrenched — a matter of degree, not of kind.

iii. pragmatism about theory choice

Pragmatism — theory choice is pragmatic; no fact of the matter about which beliefs are analytic; empiricism without dogmaIf there is no sharp distinction between analytic and synthetic truths, then there is no fact of the matter about which beliefs are held “in virtue of meaning” and which are held “in virtue of fact.” Theory choice is pragmatic: we choose the theory that best systematises our experience, that is most conservative of our prior commitments, that is most simple, and that is most fruitful in generating testable predictions. This pragmatic criterion applies uniformly to all beliefs, including those of mathematics and logic. The rationalist claim that mathematical truths are known by a faculty of pure reason entirely independent of experience is replaced by the holist claim that mathematical beliefs are the most deeply entrenched, most resistant to revision, and most widely applied nodes in our corporate web of belief — but they are not immune from revision in principle.

The claim that the mind innately represents the categories of space, time, causation, and substance is a theoretical claim about the mind’s architecture, and it is answerable to the evidence of cognitive science, developmental psychology, and linguistics. Innateness hypotheses are not established by armchair reflection on the nature of cognition; they are empirical claims that compete with empiricist learning theories on empirical grounds. The debate is scientific, not philosophical, and science — including the cognitive science of concept acquisition — has not settled definitively in the rationalist’s favour.

I want to step back from this entire debate and ask a prior question: are the philosophical problems that generate the dispute between rationalism and empiricism genuine problems, or are they artefacts of the systematic misuse of perfectly ordinary words? My thesis is that many — not all, but many — of the most celebrated moves in this debate rest on linguistic confusions that disappear once we attend carefully to how the relevant words actually function in ordinary life.

I direct my attention primarily at the empiricist side of the debate — specifically at Ayer’s sense-data theory, which represents the most developed attempt to give the empiricist programme a rigorous linguistic foundation. But my critique is not offered as a defence of rationalism; it is offered as a diagnosis of what has gone wrong in the debate on both sides. Once the linguistic confusions are dissolved, the question “does the mind contribute innate ideas to knowledge?” will turn out to be in need of careful reformulation before any answer can be given.

The Ayer programme and its linguistic foundation.

Ayer’s case for sense-data rests on what I call the “argument from illusion.” The argument proceeds: since we sometimes misperceive objects (the stick looks bent in water), and since in such cases we are directly aware of something (a bent-looking appearance), but not the physical object itself, we must always be directly aware of sense-data rather than physical objects — even in the non-illusory case.

I shall show that this argument rests on a series of misconceptions about the ordinary use of “looks,” “appears,” “directly perceives,” and “real,” and that once these misconceptions are corrected, the argument for sense-data dissolves.
i. the argument from illusion demolished

Argument from illusion fails — “looks bent” doesn’t entail awareness of bent object; ordinary use of “looks” does not presuppose sense-dataThe argument from illusion begins from the fact that the stick in water “looks bent.” It infers that we are directly aware of something bent — a sense-datum. But this inference is illicit. In ordinary speech, “looks bent” does not commit us to the existence of a bent entity of which we are aware. It is a perfectly ordinary locution meaning roughly “appears to be bent though it may not be.” The “looks”-locution serves precisely to signal uncertainty or qualification about the actual character of the object; it does not introduce a new category of mental objects (sense-data) that serve as the direct objects of awareness. Ayer’s inference from “the stick looks bent” to “we are aware of something bent (namely a sense-datum)” is simply a misreading of the logic of “looks.”

ii. the ordinary use of “real” and “illusion”

“Real” and “illusion” — context-dependent in ordinary use; philosophers misuse them to generate pseudo-problems; Austin's diagnostic methodThe word “real” has no context-independent meaning; it is what I call a trouser-word — it acquires its meaning from the contrasts it is being used to draw in a particular context. A real duck is not an artificial duck, not a toy duck, not a reflection of a duck; but which contrast is relevant depends entirely on the conversational context. When philosophers ask “do we ever perceive real objects, or only sense-data?” they are using “real” without specifying any contrast, producing a question that is in ordinary terms unintelligible. Similarly, “illusion” covers a heterogeneous range of phenomena — mirages, afterimages, hallucinations, misidentifications, tricks of perspective — that are very different from one another and that do not all require the same explanation. Lumping them together under “illusion” generates a spurious philosophical problem.

iii. the philosopher’s use of “directly perceive”

“Directly perceive” — philosopher’s usage has no ordinary correlate; contrast-class empty; the sense-data thesis is not a genuine discoveryAyer argues that we “directly perceive” sense-data but only “indirectly perceive” physical objects. But what contrast is being drawn? In ordinary language, “directly” in perceptual contexts marks a contrast with perception through instruments, through intermediaries, through descriptions. I directly see the house (I am standing in front of it), but indirectly see the far side of the moon (through a telescope or a photograph). Ayer’s use of “directly perceive” draws no such contrast; it is not that sense-data are perceived without instruments while physical objects are perceived with them. The “directly”/“indirectly” distinction is being deployed in a technical sense that has no clear relationship to any contrast that matters in ordinary perceptual discourse. The sense-data thesis is not a discovery but a re-description in confused terminology.

iv. implications for the rationalism–empiricism debate

Both sides confused — rationalism and empiricism presuppose a problematic picture of perception; linguistic therapy dissolves, not solves, the debateMy critique of Ayer is not a defence of rationalism. The rationalist programme is equally susceptible to linguistic diagnosis. When Descartes says the senses are unreliable and must be transcended by reason, he is relying on a contrast between “genuine” and “merely apparent” perception that is no less confused than Ayer’s. When Locke says the mind is a “blank slate” that receives impressions from the senses, the metaphor generates more darkness than light: it assimilates the mind to a writing surface in a way that distorts rather than illuminates the phenomena of cognition. Both traditions have been asking badly formed questions — “does the mind contribute innate ideas to knowledge?” — that presuppose a picture of the mind as a container of items called “ideas” that is itself a philosophical mythology. The debate needs not a victor but a diagnostician.

The most pressing contemporary form of the rationalism–empiricism debate concerns the origin of human concepts. Are our concepts — our representations of things in the world, of dogs, of red, of justice, of cause — innate structures contributed by the mind independently of experience? Or are they constructed dynamically from perceptual and emotional experience? I defend the latter thesis: radical concept empiricism.

My account draws on cognitive psychology and neuroscience to show that human concepts are perceptual representations — what I call proxytypes. A concept is not an innate, abstract template; it is a representation grounded in the sensorimotor, emotional, and contextual features of our experience of the things the concept applies to. The evidence from cognitive psychology, developmental science, and embodied cognition strongly favours this view over the nativist alternatives championed by Chomsky and Fodor.

Proxytypes: the empiricist account of concepts.

A proxytype is a representation that stands in for a concept in cognitive processing. It is typically a context-sensitive, multi-modal record of experience with the relevant category: visual, auditory, tactile, affective, and motor features derived from encounters with members of the category. The proxytype for <dog> is not a definition or a set of necessary and sufficient conditions; it is a rich, multi-modal representation assembled from actual experiences of dogs.

This is entirely empiricist in spirit: concepts are not given prior to experience but constructed out of it. The construction is dynamic: the proxytype changes as experience with the category accumulates. And the construction is embodied: conceptual content is grounded in the sensorimotor and emotional systems of the body, not in an abstract, amodal symbol system independent of perception.
i. the proxytype theory and the evidence from cognitive psychology

Proxytypes — perceptual representations; grading effects; embodied cognition; typicality and context-sensitivity inconsistent with nativismThe empirical evidence from concept learning strongly supports the proxytype account. Typicality effects — the finding that some members of a category (robins, for birds) are judged more typical than others (penguins) — are inexplicable on the nativist view that all category members equally trigger an innate concept template. They are explained naturally by the proxytype account: typical members are those that most closely match the accumulated perceptual record of experience with the category. Graded category membership, context-sensitivity of categorisation, and the role of emotional and affective features in concept learning all point in the same direction: concepts are constructed from experience, not given prior to it.

ii. against Chomsky: statistical learning solves the poverty of the stimulus

Statistical learning — distributional information in input is sufficient for grammar learning; poverty of stimulus overstated; connectionist models succeedChomsky’s poverty of the stimulus argument claims that the input to language acquisition is insufficient to determine the grammar the child acquires. But this claim is empirically contested. Research on statistical learning — beginning with Saffran, Aslin, and Newport’s (1996) demonstration that infants extract statistical regularities from speech streams within minutes — shows that children are extraordinarily powerful statistical learners. The distributional properties of natural language input carry far more information than Chomsky’s argument acknowledges: word order, co-occurrence patterns, prosodic cues, and cross-situational regularities together provide a much richer signal than the impoverished input Chomsky describes. Connectionist models of grammar learning, trained on naturalistic input without innate grammatical constraints, successfully acquire many of the grammatical patterns that Chomsky attributed to Universal Grammar.

iii. against Fodor: perception is top-down permeated

Against encapsulation — perception is top-down permeated by belief; categorical perception; expertise effects; Fodor's encapsulation thesis is falseFodor’s informational encapsulation thesis holds that perceptual modules process their inputs in isolation from higher-level beliefs and conceptual knowledge. But the evidence from perceptual psychology is against this. Categorical perception — the finding that categories learned through experience affect what subjects perceive, not just what they identify — shows that conceptual knowledge penetrates perceptual processing. Expert chess players perceive board configurations differently from novices; radiologists perceive X-ray images differently from medical students; wine experts perceive flavour properties that novices cannot detect. In each case, experientially acquired conceptual knowledge changes what is perceived, not merely how it is labelled. This top-down permeability of perception contradicts encapsulation and supports the empiricist account of the mind as a system whose processing is shaped by accumulated experience.

iv. embodied cognition against abstract nativism

Embodied cognition — abstract concepts grounded in sensorimotor experience; metaphor and simulation; nativist abstract concepts are explanatory fictionsThe most radical empiricist claim, and the one most directly in conflict with Fodor’s concept nativism, concerns abstract concepts. Fodor argues that even abstract concepts like cause, justice, and democracy are innate atomic primitives. But research on embodied cognition and conceptual metaphor (Lakoff and Johnson, Barsalou) shows that abstract concepts are grounded in concrete sensorimotor experience through systematic metaphorical mappings. We understand causation through the metaphor of physical force; we understand justice through spatial metaphors of balance and level ground; we understand argument through the metaphor of combat. These mappings are not arbitrary: they are structured by the sensorimotor experiences that constitute the bodily basis of cognition. Abstract concepts are not innate atoms; they are experientially grounded constructions, assembled by the same mechanisms that construct concrete perceptual concepts.
