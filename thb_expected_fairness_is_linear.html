Mr Speaker,

The motion before this House asks whether fairness under uncertainty is correctly captured by the linear expected-claim metric. I shall argue that it is — not as an approximation, not as a useful heuristic, but as the correct formal statement of what fairness requires when we must make decisions whose outcomes are uncertain.

Let me begin by stating the metric precisely. Each person i has a claim of strength cᵢ to a benefit. The probability that person i receives that benefit under policy P is pᵢ. The fairness score of policy P is:

F(P) = Σᵢ pᵢ × cᵢ

The policy that maximises F(P) is the fairest policy. This is not mere number-crunching. It expresses a deep moral principle: a claim is satisfied to the degree that the benefit it grounds is likely to be received. Fairness demands that we attend to both the strength of the claim and the probability of its satisfaction.

The foundational case: equal claims, equal chances.

Alice and Bob each have claim strength 1.0 to a single dose of a life-saving drug. We can give it to Alice with certainty or to Bob with certainty, or we can randomise.

Policy A (certain to Alice): F = 1.0×1.0 + 0.0×1.0 = 1.0
Policy B (certain to Bob): F = 0.0×1.0 + 1.0×1.0 = 1.0
Policy L (50/50 lottery): F = 0.5×1.0 + 0.5×1.0 = 1.0

All three score identically — as they should. No policy is fairer than any other when claims are equal. The lottery is recommended not because it scores higher but because it expresses equal respect procedurally. The metric correctly shows they are equivalent in the dimension of expected claim satisfaction.
i. the metric correctly captures the claims-probability interaction

Claims × probability — fairness is exactly the expected degree to which claims are satisfied; the metric is not an approximation but the correct formal statementConsider two patients with different claim strengths competing for a scarce ICU bed. Patient A has claim strength 0.9 (severe, immediate need) and a 0.4 probability of survival with treatment. Patient B has claim strength 0.3 (moderate need) and a 0.8 probability of survival with treatment. The linear metric gives us:

F(treat A) = 0.4 × 0.9 = 0.36      F(treat B) = 0.8 × 0.3 = 0.24

Treat A. Now suppose A’s probability of benefit rises to 0.6:

F(treat A) = 0.6 × 0.9 = 0.54

The metric responds correctly to both dimensions. It is not merely sensitive to claim strength and probability separately but to their joint product, which is exactly what fairness requires. The opposition will argue that this misses something about distribution. I submit that it misses nothing: the joint product of probability and claim strength is the complete measure of expected fairness.

ii. equal claims demand equal chances: broome’s proportionality principle

Proportionality principle — equal claims → equal probabilities; unequal claims → proportional probabilities; this follows directly from the metricThe metric entails a proportionality principle: when two persons have claims in ratio cᵢ : c₃, they should receive probabilities in the same ratio. If Alice has claim 0.8 and Bob has claim 0.4, Alice should receive twice the probability of benefit:

p_Alice / p_Bob = c_Alice / c_Bob = 0.8 / 0.4 = 2

So if p_Bob = 0.33, p_Alice = 0.67, and F = 0.67×0.8 + 0.33×0.4 = 0.536 + 0.132 = 0.668. This is a principled and substantive constraint on how probabilities should be distributed. No ad hoc non-linearity is required: the proportionality principle emerges directly from the structure of the linear metric. The opposition’s non-linear amendments cannot match this systematic elegance.

iii. the triage calculation: the metric delivers clear, correct verdicts

Triage — metric resolves the one vs. many problem; works out exactly where aggregation overcomes stronger individual claims; gives a threshold, not a vague intuitionConsider the famous case: one patient facing imminent death (claim 1.0, survival probability 0.9 with treatment) versus four patients facing serious but less immediate risk (claim 0.6 each, survival probability 0.5 each with treatment).

F(treat one) = 0.9 × 1.0 = 0.90
F(treat four) = 4 × (0.5 × 0.6) = 4 × 0.30 = 1.20

Treat the four. Now reduce each of the four’s survival probability to 0.3:

F(treat four) = 4 × (0.3 × 0.6) = 4 × 0.18 = 0.72

Now treat the one. The metric yields a precise threshold at which aggregation overcomes the stronger individual claim. This is exactly what a decision-guiding theory must deliver. The opposition’s theory gives only vague invocations of “separateness”. Mine gives a number.

iv. pandemic vaccine allocation: the metric navigates complex multi-group trade-offs

Vaccine allocation — complex multi-group case; metric ranks policies correctly by adjusting claim weights; the ethical disagreement becomes transparent as a disagreement over claim magnitudesA government has 100 vaccine doses. Policy A gives all doses to elderly people (100 people, 80% survival probability, claim 1.0). Policy B gives doses to essential workers (300 people, 30% survival probability, claim 0.6).

F(Policy A) = 100 × (0.80 × 1.0) = 80.0
F(Policy B) = 300 × (0.30 × 0.6) = 54.0

Policy A wins. Now suppose essential workers bear societal risk, raising their claim to 1.5:

F(Policy B*) = 300 × (0.30 × 1.5) = 135.0

Now Policy B wins overwhelmingly. What has happened? The ethical disagreement — are essential workers’ claims stronger? — has been made precise and transparent. The metric does not resolve every ethical dispute, but it isolates exactly where the dispute lies: in the claim magnitudes, not in the structure of the aggregation. This is the metric’s great virtue.

2.The joint product pᵢ × cᵢ measures the expected degree to which person i’s claim is satisfied; summing across persons gives the total expected claim satisfaction of a policy.

∴F(P) = Σ pᵢ × cᵢ is the complete and correct measure of fairness under uncertainty; policies should be ranked by this metric.
Argument II — Proportionality: equal claims entail equal probabilities; the metric delivers a principled allocation rule

2.The linear metric entails exactly this: optimal probability allocation is proportional to claim strength, yielding pᵢ / p₃ = cᵢ / c₃ at the fair distribution.
3.This proportionality principle is both principled and action-guiding; it does not require the ad hoc non-linear adjustments the opposition proposes.

2.The linear metric yields a precise threshold at which the aggregate expected claim satisfaction of treating many outweighs that of treating one with a stronger claim.

Argument IV — The metric makes ethical disagreements transparent as disagreements over claim magnitudes
1.Different ethical theories (utilitarian, egalitarian, prioritarian) disagree primarily about how to assign claim strengths, not about whether to aggregate across persons.
2.The linear metric provides a common mathematical language in which these disagreements can be expressed and compared numerically.
3.By isolating the source of disagreement (claim magnitudes) from the structure of aggregation, the metric clarifies rather than obscures ethical debate.
∴The metric’s greatest contribution is making ethical disagreements precise: different values translate into different claim assignments, not into different structural approaches to aggregation.

My colleague Broome has established the formal structure of the linear metric. My role is to defend it against the aggregation-aversion of the opposition and to show that the practical and philosophical objections to summing claim satisfaction across persons cannot be sustained.

The opposition will invoke the separateness of persons. They will say that fairness is not merely a matter of how much claim satisfaction exists in the world but of who bears the unsatisfied claims. This sounds deep. But when pressed to translate it into a decision procedure, the opposition retreats into incoherence. Let me show why.

The aggregation threshold: where numbers matter.

Suppose we face N patients each with claim strength 0.5 and survival probability 0.6 with treatment, versus one patient with claim strength 1.0 and survival probability 0.9 with treatment.

F(treat one) = 0.9 × 1.0 = 0.90
F(treat N) = N × (0.6 × 0.5) = N × 0.30

The threshold is at N = 3: three moderate-claim patients with 60% survival probability just outweigh one strong-claim patient with 90% survival probability. At N = 4 the aggregate wins clearly: F(treat four) = 1.20 vs 0.90.

The opposition says numbers should not matter in this way. But this means letting three people die to give priority to one. That is not fairness. It is the worship of individual salience over aggregate welfare.
i. the separateness of persons is not a reason against aggregation

Against separateness objection — separateness doesn't prohibit aggregation; it requires that each person's interests be counted; linear metric does exactly thisScanlon and Voorhoeve invoke the separateness of persons as though it were a trump against aggregation. But separateness is a reason to count each person’s interests — not a reason to refuse to count them together. The linear metric respects separateness precisely: each person’s claim strength and survival probability enters the sum as a distinct term. No person’s interests are merged with anyone else’s or subordinated to a social total that ignores individuals. The sum is the sum of individual expected claim satisfactions. Separateness of persons is violated by a theory that ignores individuals, not by one that aggregates them correctly.

ii. the climate adaptation case: aggregation delivers real lives

Climate adaptation — aggregation gets the right answer; refusing to aggregate would leave more people worse off; the opposition's theory is practically paralysingA government must allocate £1 billion. Option A protects 100,000 people from flooding with probability 0.8 (claim 0.7 each). Option B protects 40,000 people in highly vulnerable communities with probability 0.95 (claim 1.0 each).

F(Option A) = 100,000 × (0.8 × 0.7) = 56,000
F(Option B) = 40,000 × (0.95 × 1.0) = 38,000

Option A. Now raise the vulnerable community’s claim to 1.6 (extreme poverty, prior disadvantage):

F(Option B*) = 40,000 × (0.95 × 1.6) = 60,800

Now Option B. The metric has correctly identified the turning point: claim strength of 1.6 for the vulnerable group overcomes the numerical advantage of Option A. The opposition’s non-linear model would require us to specify how the distribution of unsatisfied claims within each option is penalised — a calculation so complex that real-world policymakers would abandon it in favour of gut instinct. The linear metric delivers answers.

iii. weighted aggregation handles prioritarian intuitions without structural complexity

Weighted claims handle priority — making claim strength a function of wellbeing level captures prioritarian concerns without abandoning additivityThe opposition points to the intuition that the worse-off have stronger claims and that this cannot be captured additively. But it can: simply set claim strength as a function of the person’s welfare level. If Alice is at welfare level 20 and Bob is at level 80, and we set c = 1/welfare, then:

c_Alice = 1/20 = 0.05      c_Bob = 1/80 = 0.0125

Wait — that gives lower claims to the worse-off, which is backwards. Better: set c = (max_welfare − welfare)/max_welfare. With max = 100:

c_Alice = (100−20)/100 = 0.80      c_Bob = (100−80)/100 = 0.20

Alice gets four times the claim weight. This is priority built directly into the linear metric through claim specification. No non-linear transformation of the aggregate is required. The prioritarian intuition is already absorbed.

iv. non-aggregationism leads to paralysis in real policy

Paralysis — Scanlon-style contractualism cannot generate rankings in complex multi-person cases; the metric is the only action-guiding framework available to policymakersConsider a public health authority choosing between five policies, each protecting different numbers of people at different probability levels and with different claim strengths. The linear metric produces a ranking in seconds. The contractualist framework asks us to consider the strongest complaint each person could make against each policy, compare them pairwise, and derive a verdict — a procedure that generates cyclic rankings in multi-person cases and that Scanlon himself has never resolved. In a pandemic with millions of lives at stake and decisions required within hours, a morally serious framework must be computationally tractable. The linear metric is. The opposition’s framework is not.

I want to make a concession and then deploy it against the opposition. The concession is this: the opposition is right that the worse-off have stronger claims, and right that numbers alone cannot always override the severity of individual deprivation. My prioritarian modification of the linear metric takes this seriously. The deployment is this: once we accept that claim strengths should be weighted by the degree of deprivation, the additive framework handles every case the opposition raises — and handles it better, because it delivers precise verdicts.

Let me introduce the prioritarian version of the expected-claim metric. For each person i at welfare level wᵢ, define the priority-weighted claim as:

cᵢ* = cᵢ × (1 / wᵢ^α)

where α > 0 is the priority parameter (higher α = more priority to the worse-off). The fairness metric becomes:

F*(P) = Σᵢ pᵢ × cᵢ*
i. numbers matter, but the worse-off count for more

Priority weighting — the prioritarian metric weights claim satisfaction by welfare level; numbers still matter but the worse-off count more per unit; this is additive and preciseConsider the classic trolley-problem structure: five people at welfare level 50 (moderate deprivation) versus one person at welfare level 10 (severe deprivation). Each has a base claim of 1.0. Survival probability with treatment is 0.9 for either group. With α = 0.5:

cᵢ*(welfare 50) = 1.0 × (1/50^0.5) = 1.0 × 0.141 = 0.141
cᵢ*(welfare 10) = 1.0 × (1/10^0.5) = 1.0 × 0.316 = 0.316
F*(save five) = 5 × 0.9 × 0.141 = 0.635
F*(save one) = 1 × 0.9 × 0.316 = 0.284

Save the five. But increase α to 2 (much stronger priority):

cᵢ*(welfare 50) = 1/50² = 0.0004      cᵢ*(welfare 10) = 1/100 = 0.01
F*(save five) = 5 × 0.9 × 0.0004 = 0.0018
F*(save one) = 1 × 0.9 × 0.01 = 0.009

Now save the one. The priority parameter α controls exactly how much extra weight the worse-off receive. This is the opposition’s intuition fully captured within the linear framework.

Vaccine priority — priority-weighted metric recommends elderly over workers when welfare level is properly measured; the metric handles the ethical intuition without non-linearityReturn to the pandemic vaccine case. Elderly patients are at welfare level 30 (severe health risk). Essential workers are at welfare level 60 (moderate risk). Base claim 1.0 each. Survival probability: elderly 0.8 per dose, workers 0.3 per dose. With α = 1:

c*(elderly) = 1/(30^1) = 0.033      c*(workers) = 1/(60^1) = 0.017
F*(100 elderly doses) = 100 × 0.8 × 0.033 = 2.67
F*(300 worker doses) = 300 × 0.3 × 0.017 = 1.50

Treat the elderly. But suppose we identify a subgroup of workers at welfare level 15 (extreme vulnerability, front-line hospital workers with no PPE):

c*(vulnerable workers) = 1/15 = 0.067
F*(100 vulnerable worker doses) = 100 × 0.3 × 0.067 = 2.00

Still treat the elderly. At what point does severity of deprivation tip the balance? When workers reach welfare level 25:

c* = 1/25 = 0.040      F* = 100 × 0.3 × 0.040 = 1.20

The prioritarian metric identifies every threshold precisely. No non-linear structural amendment is required.

iii. rejecting the repugnant conclusion with prioritarian weighting

Repugnant conclusion — strict utilitarianism says a huge population of barely-satisfied claims beats a small very-well-satisfied population; prioritarian weighting correctly rejects thisThe opposition might think linear aggregation entails the Repugnant Conclusion from population ethics: that a vast number of people with minuscule claim satisfaction outweighs a small number with high satisfaction. Standard linear aggregation does imply this. But the priority-weighted metric does not. With α > 0, the very small welfare levels of a marginally-surviving population receive less priority weight, not more, because they are calculating over a near-zero benefit to each person. Let N people each have welfare level 0.01 (barely surviving), claim 1.0, and probability 0.5:

F*(N barely-satisfied) = N × 0.5 × (1/0.01) = 50N

Hmm — this actually inflates their score. The solution: use benefit received, not welfare level, in the priority function. If the benefit increment is tiny (say 0.001 units of welfare), then c* = 1/(0.001) × ε → 0 as the benefit approaches zero. The metric can be specified to avoid the Repugnant Conclusion while retaining additivity throughout.

I close the government’s case. My colleagues have established that linear expected-claim satisfaction, properly specified with priority weighting, delivers correct verdicts in every case the opposition raises. My role is to formalise this claim within the framework of social welfare functions and to show that the opposition’s non-linear amendments are, at best, special cases of the government’s framework and, at worst, structurally inconsistent.

The prioritarian social welfare function (SWF) approach applies a strictly concave transformation g to each person’s individual welfare before aggregation:

W(P) = Σᵢ g(Eᵢ[u])

where Eᵢ[u] is person i’s expected utility under policy P, and g is concave (g'' < 0). This is the government’s framework. It is linear at the SWF level (we sum g-transformed utilities) but non-linear at the individual utility level, giving diminishing marginal moral weight to each additional unit of welfare. It delivers all the distribution-sensitivity the opposition demands without abandoning additive aggregation.

Formally absorbing the opposition: distribution X vs distribution Y.

Temkin’s challenge: Distribution X gives Alice deficit 1.0, Bob deficit 0.0. Distribution Y gives both deficit 0.5. Linear metric: both score F = 0.5 + 0.5 = 1.0. Temkin claims Y is fairer and the linear metric misses this.

But with a concave g (say g(x) = −x²):
W(X) = g(−1.0) + g(0.0) = −1 + 0 = −1.0
W(Y) = g(−0.5) + g(−0.5) = −0.25 + −0.25 = −0.50

W(Y) = −0.50 > W(X) = −1.00: Y is correctly judged fairer.

The concave SWF, which is a form of linear aggregation at the social level, captures Temkin’s intuition. The opposition has won nothing; they have described a special case of our framework.
i. the concave SWF formally absorbs temkin’s concentration objection

Concave SWF absorbs Temkin — applying g(x) = −x² to deficits captures the intuition that concentration is worse; this is additive at the SWF level; opposition's objection is already inside our frameworkTemkin’s objection is that Distribution X (Alice bears all the unfairness) and Distribution Y (unfairness shared equally) score identically on the linear metric. This is correct for the plain F = Σ p × c metric. But the prioritarian SWF handles this naturally. Using g(deficit) = −deficit² as our concave transformation:

Distribution X: W = −(1.0)² + −(0.0)² = −1.00
Distribution Y: W = −(0.5)² + −(0.5)² = −0.25 + −0.25 = −0.50

W(Y) = −0.50 > W(X) = −1.00. Y is correctly identified as fairer. Now Temkin will introduce the general principle: any distribution with equal deficits is fairer than a distribution with the same total deficit concentrated on fewer people. The SWF with concave g confirms this for any concave function. The government’s framework contains Temkin’s objection as a special case.

ii. the sufficientarian threshold case

Sufficientarian threshold — adding a step-function to g captures the intuition that falling below a threshold is morally special; still additive at the SWF levelOtsuka will argue that fairness becomes especially urgent when someone falls below a moral threshold — that a person with deficit above 0.5 has a qualitatively worse situation than one with deficit below 0.5. This can be handled by a step function in g:

g(deficit) = −deficit² if deficit ≤ 0.5
g(deficit) = −deficit² − 2 if deficit > 0.5

Now consider Distribution X (deficit 1.0, deficit 0.0) vs Distribution Y (deficit 0.5, deficit 0.5):

W(X) = (−1 − 2) + 0 = −3.00
W(Y) = −0.25 + −0.25 = −0.50

The threshold creates an enormous jump in moral cost when someone falls below the line. The sufficientarian intuition is captured perfectly within the additive SWF framework. Otsuka has described a special case of our g-function, not a challenge to additive aggregation.
