What this tool does

This is a working implementation of Tsebelis's veto player theory combined with spatial voting models (the Downs-Black median voter tradition) and Borda count aggregation.

The mathematics

  • Policy space: a 2D Euclidean space. Each axis represents a policy dimension (e.g. left–right, interventionist–laissez-faire).
  • Ideal points: each voter is placed at their most preferred policy position.
  • Indifference circles: the radius ri for voter i = √((x_worst − x_i)² + (y_worst − y_i)²), where "worst" is their least-ranked policy. This is the Euclidean distance to their threshold — any policy closer than this is preferred to the status quo.
  • Winset W(SQ): the set intersection of all indifference circles. A pixel (x,y) is in the winset if and only if dist((x,y), voter_i) ≤ r_i for every voter i.
  • Borda score: score(policy j) = Σ over voters of (n − 1 − rank_voter(j)), where rank is 0-indexed and n is the number of policies. The winner maximises this sum.

Key results from veto player theory (Tsebelis 2002)

  • Policy change requires a non-empty winset. If no point lies inside all circles simultaneously, the status quo is stable.
  • The winset shrinks as (a) the number of veto players increases, or (b) the ideological distance between them grows.
  • The Borda winner is the consensus candidate: it may not be anyone's first choice, but it minimises aggregate dissatisfaction — useful when the winset is empty.
  • Borda is manipulable (Gibbard–Satterthwaite theorem): rational players can bury rivals by ranking them last, distorting the aggregate result.

How to use this tool

  1. Go to Build. Place 2–6 policy options by clicking the canvas (type a label first). Then switch to voter mode and place 2–5 voters.
  2. Go to Circles. Rank the policies for each voter in preference order. The tool computes each voter's indifference circle radius from the Euclidean distance to their last-ranked policy.
  3. Go to Winset to see the intersection region — or confirm that no winset exists.
  4. Go to Borda count to see the consensus candidate with full arithmetic.
  5. Go to Proof to verify any point on the canvas manually and inspect all the working.

Theoretical context

Veto player theory was developed by George Tsebelis in Veto Players: How Political Institutions Work (Princeton UP, 2002). Spatial voting models derive from Anthony Downs (An Economic Theory of Democracy, 1957) and Duncan Black (The Theory of Committees and Elections, 1958). The Borda count dates to Jean-Charles de Borda (1784); its manipulability was formally proved by Gibbard (1973) and Satterthwaite (1975).
