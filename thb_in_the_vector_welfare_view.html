Logical Arguments

The aim of my 2018 paper in Mind was to show that the supposedly ironclad impossibility results in population axiology are not, in fact, ironclad. They depend on assumptions that can be questioned — and one way to question them, the most interesting way, is to model welfare as a vector rather than as a scalar.

The Repugnant Conclusion is a theorem: if you accept certain adequacy conditions on a population axiology, the Repugnant Conclusion follows. Arrhenius’s contribution has been to make this result increasingly clean and powerful. But a theorem is only as good as its premises. I want to show that Lexical Totalism — a view on which welfare has two components ordered lexically — satisfies all of Arrhenius’s adequacy conditions while also, quite legitimately, denying the Repugnant Conclusion. This is not a trick; it is a substantive result about the structure of value.

Lexical Totalism: the framework.

Welfare level Wx = [hx, lx]: h measures “higher” goods (autonomy, meaning, profound connection); l measures “lower” goods (sensory pleasure, comfort). Both are additive within their dimension.

Ordering: Wx > Wy iff hx > hy, or hx = hy and lx > ly. A larger higher-component always wins; l breaks ties only.

Population total: a population X summing to vector [Σhx, Σlx] beats population Y summing to [Σhy, Σly] iff Σhx > Σhy, or equality on h and Σlx > Σly.

Repugnant Conclusion avoided: A = 10bn at [100, 100] vs Z = 1030 at [0.01, 100]. A has vastly larger h-sum; Z has zero h-sum. A wins. Quality does not get swamped by quantity.
i. how lexical totalism escapes arrhenius’s sixth theorem

Escaping impossibility — LT satisfies all six adequacy conditions; no Fine-Grainedness assumedArrhenius’s sixth and most powerful theorem assumes that welfare levels are finitely fine-grained: that between any two welfare levels there is a finite chain of small steps, each of which is a welfare level in its own right. This assumption is doing crucial work. Lexical Totalism violates it: there is no finite chain of small steps from the welfare level [0, 1000] to the welfare level [1, 0], because the h-component of the second is higher despite its dramatically lower l-value, and no sequence of marginal increases in l can bridge the gap. The levels are in different dimensional “leagues.” Arrhenius’s theorem therefore cannot reach Lexical Totalism. And this is not an ad hoc evasion: the failure of Fine-Grainedness is a prediction of any view that takes seriously the Millian intuition that some kinds of welfare are categorically superior to others, not merely quantitatively greater.

ii. the millian motivation is independent and strong

Millian motivation — independent of population ethics; grounded in individual welfare structureThe vector welfare view is not merely a technical device for escaping paradoxes. It is independently motivated by plausible claims about what makes individual lives go well. Mill argued that some pleasures are qualitatively superior to others: that the pleasure of reading Homer is not merely quantitatively greater than the pleasure of playing push-pin but is of a categorically higher kind. If this is right for individual welfare — and I believe it is — then the vector representation is the natural formal expression of that intuition. We represent an individual life as having two separable dimensions of value, with the higher taking lexical priority. The population axiology is then simply the extension of this individual welfare theory to populations. The motivation for the view comes from the philosophy of well-being, not from the desire to avoid a mathematical result.

iii. responding to cowie’s objection to strong lexical totalism

Against Cowie on SLT — Overwhelmingly Bad case shows SLT needs refinement, not that the vector programme failsCowie argues that Strong Lexical Totalism has an implausible result: it ranks Overwhelmingly Bad — one person with a tiny higher-good plus arbitrarily many with only lower-bads — above All Good, a large population with only lower goods. I accept this is a genuine problem for Strong Lexical Totalism. But I deny it undermines the vector programme as a whole. Cowie himself notes that the view might be modified by a “negative priority” condition: a sufficient quantity of lower-bad outweighs any amount of higher-good. This modification is well-motivated and leaves the central insight intact. The vector framework remains the right structural approach; we need to get the weighting conditions right within it. This is a problem of refinement, not of abandonment.

iv. against cowie’s sceptical conclusion

Against scepticism — paradoxes show the view needs work, not that there are no truthsCowie moves from the failure of the current versions of the vector view to a conclusion of axiological scepticism: population ethics paradoxes give us grounds to doubt that moral axiology has true answers at all. This conclusion is too quick. The history of ethics is full of theories that faced apparently devastating counterexamples and were subsequently refined into more adequate positions. The existence of counterexamples to current formulations of vector totalism shows that we have not yet found the right formulation — not that there is no right formulation. The paradoxes are a research programme, not a reductio. Cowie’s sceptical conclusion would be warranted only if we had genuinely exhausted the space of available modifications. We have not.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Lexical Totalism escapes Arrhenius’s theorems by legitimately denying Finite Fine-Grainedness
1.Arrhenius’s impossibility theorems assume Finite Fine-Grainedness: between any two welfare levels there is a finite sequence of small steps, each a welfare level.
2.Lexical Totalism denies this: no finite chain of marginal improvements in lower-good quantity bridges the gap between levels in different lexical tiers (e.g. [0, 10&sup6;] and [1, 0]). The dimensions are in different “leagues.”
3.This denial of Fine-Grainedness is independently motivated by the Millian view that some welfare kinds are categorically, not merely quantitatively, superior — it is not an ad hoc evasion.
∴Lexical Totalism is a genuine escape from Arrhenius’s impossibility results: it satisfies all six adequacy conditions while denying the Repugnant Conclusion, by denying a contestable background assumption on independently Millian grounds.
Argument II — The vector view is independently motivated by the philosophy of well-being
1.Mill argued that some pleasures are qualitatively, not merely quantitatively, superior. This claim about individual welfare is plausible independently of population ethics.
2.The vector representation is the natural formal expression of this Millian intuition: two additive dimensions of welfare, with higher goods taking lexical priority over lower. This is not a device invented to escape paradoxes but a principled theory of well-being.
3.Extending this individual welfare theory to populations gives Lexical Totalism. The population axiology is motivated by and continuous with the philosophy of well-being.
∴The vector welfare view has independent normative motivation in the philosophy of well-being; it is not a mathematical trick but a substantive theory whose population-ethical implications are a further discovery, not the original design goal.
Argument III — Cowie’s objection to Strong LT shows the need for refinement, not abandonment
1.Cowie: SLT entails Overwhelmingly Bad (OB) > All Good (AG) because OB has a nonzero h-component. This is counterintuitive and constitutes an objection to SLT.
2.But the Millian vector programme is not committed to SLT specifically. A “negative priority” condition — a sufficient quantity of lower-bad outweighs any higher-good — can be added without abandoning the vector structure or the Millian motivation.
3.Cowie himself acknowledges this modification; the objection shows SLT needs refinement within the vector framework, not that the framework is wrong.
∴Cowie’s objection is a challenge to one particular version of the vector programme, not to the programme as a whole; the appropriate response is refinement, not abandonment and not scepticism.
Argument IV — Paradoxes are a research programme, not grounds for axiological scepticism
1.Cowie infers from the failure of current vector formulations to axiological scepticism: there are no true answers in population ethics. This inference requires that the space of modifications has been exhausted.
2.The space of modifications has not been exhausted. Weak LT, lexical-threshold views, negative-priority conditions, and combinations thereof remain to be fully explored and assessed.
3.The existence of counterexamples to current formulations shows we have not found the right formulation, not that there is no right formulation. The paradoxes are a research programme that has not yet reached its conclusion.
∴Cowie’s inference from axiological paradoxes to global scepticism is too quick: it mistakes the incompleteness of current theory for the impossibility of any adequate theory.

I want to defend a more sophisticated version of the vector programme than either Thomas’s Lexical Totalism or the Strong/Weak variants that Cowie targets — and to show why it is more resistant to his objections.

My 2022 paper develops what I call the lexical-threshold view. The core idea is that welfare has a threshold — call it the excellence threshold — such that lives above the threshold have an h-component and lives below it do not. But crucially, for lives near the threshold, there is genuine incommensurability: we cannot say precisely which lives are above and which below the threshold, or exactly how much h-value a given life has. This incommensurability is not a defect of the view; it is a prediction of any theory that takes seriously the vagueness inherent in the distinction between an excellent life and a very good but non-excellent one.

“The lexical-threshold view grounds incommensurability in the structure of well-being itself — in the vagueness of the boundary between different kinds of welfare — rather than invoking it as an ad hoc device. This gives the view explanatory depth that the simpler lexical views lack.”
i. the lexical threshold and principled incommensurability

Lexical threshold — excellence threshold with principled incommensurability; not ad hocConsider the distinction between an excellent life and a very good but not excellent life. This distinction is real — we make it all the time in assessing literary characters, historical figures, and our own aspirations — but it is vague. There is no precise moment at which a good life crosses over into an excellent one. The lexical-threshold view models this: lives clearly above the threshold have a positive h-component; lives clearly below it have h = 0; lives near the threshold are incommensurable with one another in a way that resists precise numerical comparison. This incommensurability is not introduced to save the theory from paradox — it is a prediction of the independently plausible thesis that the excellences of life are genuinely vague in this way.

ii. how the threshold view avoids cowie’s overwhelming bad case

Avoids Overwhelmingly Bad — negative welfare is not positively valued by the threshold; OB has no genuine h-goodCowie’s Overwhelmingly Bad case involves one person with a welfare level [1, 0] — which in the notation of Strong Lexical Totalism has a nonzero h-component, giving OB a higher sum-h than All Good. The problem is that [1, 0] is supposed to represent “one unit of higher good with no lower goods,” which may seem like a genuinely excellent feature of a life. But the Overwhelmingly Bad population also contains arbitrarily many lives with only lower-bads [0, −k]. On the lexical-threshold view, the question is whether one life with a small excellence genuinely outweighs any number of lives containing massive suffering. The threshold view says: no, because the incommensurability near the threshold means we cannot straightforwardly rank a tiny h-advantage against an arbitrarily large l-disadvantage without further principled criteria. A negative-priority condition — mass lower-bad suffering eventually outweighs any h-good — can be derived from, rather than added ad hoc to, the threshold structure.

iii. the repugnant conclusion remains blocked

RC still blocked — excellent lives have genuine h-component; Z-lives are below thresholdThe key advantage of the lexical-threshold view is preserved: the Repugnant Conclusion is blocked. The Z-world — containing vast numbers of lives barely worth living — consists of lives that fall well below the excellence threshold. These lives have h = 0 on any plausible threshold view. The A-world — ten billion lives of genuine human flourishing — consists of lives clearly above the threshold. A’s h-sum therefore exceeds Z’s (which is zero) regardless of how many Z-people there are. The Repugnant Conclusion — that Z is better than A — is false. And this result is robust: it is grounded in the independently plausible claim that lives barely worth living fall below the excellence threshold, not in a technical choice of parameters.

iv. against cowie and thornley: incommensurability is not a free lunch but it is not a paradox

Incommensurability — not ad hoc, not a paradox, but a genuine feature of value structureBoth Cowie and Thornley press the objection that lexical views generate radical incompleteness or incommensurability: we cannot compare many pairs of populations that intuitively seem comparable. I accept that incommensurability is a cost. But it is not a paradox, and it is not ad hoc. Thornley’s point that Archimedean views face an analogous dilemma — either radical incompleteness or a precise critical threshold that makes two extra mosquito bites morally catastrophic — shows that incommensurability is not a peculiar defect of lexical views. It is a feature of the entire problem space. If all available routes involve some form of incompleteness or some counterintuitive result, then the choice between views must be made on other grounds: which incompleteness is most theoretically motivated, which counterintuitive result is most tolerable. On those grounds, principled incommensurability near the excellence threshold is preferable to either the Repugnant Conclusion or a razor-sharp critical level.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The excellence threshold and principled incommensurability are independently motivated
1.The distinction between an excellent life and a very good but non-excellent life is real but vague: there is no precise moment at which a life crosses the excellence threshold.
2.The lexical-threshold view models this vagueness: lives clearly above the threshold have a positive h-component; lives near the threshold are incommensurable. This is a prediction of independently plausible facts about the vagueness of excellence, not an ad hoc device.
3.The resulting incommensurability is therefore principled: it reflects genuine indeterminacy in welfare structure rather than a chosen parameter designed to avoid paradox.
∴The lexical-threshold view’s incommensurability is independently motivated by the philosophy of well-being; it is not introduced ad hoc and cannot be dismissed as a mere device for escaping paradox.
Argument II — The threshold view avoids Cowie’s Overwhelmingly Bad case through principled negative priority
1.Cowie’s OB case requires that a single person’s tiny higher-good outweighs arbitrarily many lives of severe lower-bad suffering. Strong LT entails this; Weak LT entails the weaker version that generates his duplication objection.
2.The lexical-threshold view derives a negative-priority condition from the threshold structure itself: near the threshold, incommensurability prevents a tiny h-advantage from cleanly dominating a massive l-disadvantage; sufficiently great lower-bad suffering eventually outweighs any higher-good.
3.This negative-priority condition is derived from the structure of the view, not added ad hoc — making the response to Cowie principled rather than merely stipulative.
∴The lexical-threshold view is more resistant to Cowie’s OB objection than Simple or Weak LT: negative priority is derived from the threshold structure, not externally imposed.
Argument III — The Repugnant Conclusion remains robustly blocked
1.Z-lives (barely worth living) fall clearly below the excellence threshold on any plausible account of what excellence requires. Their h-component is 0.
2.A-lives (genuinely flourishing, excellent human lives) fall clearly above the threshold. Their h-component is positive. A’s h-sum exceeds Z’s (= 0) regardless of how many Z-people there are.
3.This result is robust and independently grounded: it follows from the plausible claim that lives barely worth living lack excellence, not from a choice of parameters.
∴The lexical-threshold view robustly blocks the Repugnant Conclusion: since Z-lives have h = 0 and A-lives have h > 0, no number of Z-people can make Z better than A.
Argument IV — Against Cowie and Thornley: incommensurability is a feature of the problem space, not a peculiar defect
1.Cowie objects that lexical views generate unacceptable incommensurability. Thornley shows that Archimedean views face an analogous dilemma: either a razor-sharp critical threshold (counterintuitive) or radical incompleteness (= symmetric incommensurability).
2.If all available routes in population axiology involve some form of incompleteness or counterintuitive result, the choice between views must be made on which incompleteness is most theoretically motivated and which counterintuitive result is most tolerable.
3.Principled incommensurability near the excellence threshold (= the lexical-threshold view) is better motivated than a razor-sharp critical level (Archimedean) and more tolerable than the Repugnant Conclusion (standard totalism).
∴Incommensurability is not a peculiar failure of lexical views but a general feature of the problem space; on a comparative assessment, the incommensurability of the lexical-threshold view is the most theoretically motivated and least counterintuitive option available.

I want to set the stage for what is at stake, philosophically, in this debate — because I think the stakes have sometimes been underestimated or mislocated.

The Repugnant Conclusion is repugnant. I mean this not as a rhetorical label but as a considered axiological verdict. Consider: a world containing ten billion people who live lives that are genuinely wonderful — full of love, achievement, understanding, and joy — and compare it with a world containing ten to the power of thirty people who live lives that are barely worth living: each day contains a modest surplus of pleasure over pain, but nothing more. The conclusion that the second world is better strikes me, on reflection, as wrong. Not mildly wrong. Deeply, obviously wrong. Any theory that entails it has misidentified what matters in a population.

“I believe that the Repugnant Conclusion can be avoided. We should look for some way to escape it. The most promising approach is to give greater weight to the quality of lives than to their number — which requires us to identify what quality consists in and how it relates to welfare in a way that does not trivially reduce to quantity.” — Parfit, 2016
i. why the repugnant conclusion is genuinely repugnant

RC genuinely repugnant — not intuition-pumping; reflects deep truth about welfare qualityThere is a tendency in the recent technical literature to treat the Repugnant Conclusion as merely counterintuitive — as one counterexample among many that a sophisticated theory must somehow accommodate or explain away. I resist this treatment. The intuition that a world of flourishing lives is better than a world of barely-worth-living lives is not a mere gut reaction that reflective scrutiny might dissolve. On the contrary: the more carefully I think about what those lives would actually be like, the more confident I become that a life barely worth living — a life containing a modest daily surplus of contentment, nothing more — lacks something crucial that the excellent life possesses. It lacks depth, richness, and the higher kinds of engagement with the world that constitute full human flourishing. A theory that cannot represent this difference has gone wrong at the foundations.

ii. the diminishing value view: why it was abandoned and whether it should have been

DVV — Egyptology objection was real but may have been over-weighted in the literatureThe earlier Millian strategy was the Diminishing Value View: the value added by extra lives of lower quality diminishes as numbers increase, eventually approaching zero. This view has intuitive appeal and avoids the Repugnant Conclusion. It was largely abandoned because of what I call the Egyptology problem: if we suddenly discover a large population of ancient Egyptians whose welfare was very high, the DVV seems to imply that this discovery should substantially affect our current moral priorities. This seems absurd. But I wonder whether the DVV was abandoned too quickly. The Egyptology problem is a problem for the view as standardly formulated; it may not be a problem for refined versions that constrain how past-population discoveries can affect current obligations. The vector programme represents one refinement; whether it is the only refinement or the best one remains to be seen.

iii. what any adequate solution must preserve

Adequacy constraints — must avoid RC; must be transitive; must respect separability; must rank OB below AGI want to specify, as precisely as I can, what any adequate solution to the population ethics problem must preserve. First: it must deny the Repugnant Conclusion. A world of flourishing lives is not worse than a world of barely-worth-living ones, no matter how numerous. Second: it must be transitive. The betterness relation on populations must be transitive, or practical reasoning about population policy becomes incoherent. Third: it must satisfy some version of separability — whether or not population Y contains additional people should not affect whether adding person Z to population X makes things better. Fourth: it must rank Overwhelmingly Bad below All Good. Cowie is right that a theory that ranks one person with a tiny higher good above a vast population of contented lives has gone wrong. These four constraints together constitute the minimum adequacy conditions that any theory worth taking seriously must satisfy.

iv. on cowie’s sceptical conclusion: the right response to hard problems

Against scepticism — hard problems demand better theories, not abandonment of theoryCowie’s conclusion is that population ethics paradoxes support axiological scepticism and ultimately moral error theory. I find this response to the difficulty too quick. When a scientific theory faces apparently irresolvable paradoxes — as Newtonian mechanics faced the perihelion of Mercury — the right response is not to conclude that there is no truth about the structure of spacetime. It is to look for a better theory. The same applies here. The paradoxes of population ethics show that our existing theories are inadequate. They do not show that there is no adequate theory. Cowie’s error theory would be supported only if we had principled grounds for thinking the space of theories has been exhausted. We do not. What the paradoxes show is that population ethics is hard — which we always knew — not that it is indeterminate.

Argument I — The Repugnant Conclusion is genuinely repugnant: quality of life is not reducible to quantity
1.A world of ten billion flourishing lives — full of love, achievement, understanding, joy — and a world of 10³&sup0; lives barely worth living differ not merely quantitatively but in the kinds of welfare they contain.
2.A life barely worth living lacks depth, richness, and the higher kinds of engagement that constitute full human flourishing; this lack is a qualitative, not merely quantitative, difference from an excellent life.
3.Any theory that ranks the world of barely-worth-living lives above the world of flourishing lives has misidentified what matters in a population at the level of foundations, not merely at the level of parameters.
∴The Repugnant Conclusion is a deep axiological error, not a mildly counterintuitive result to be accommodated; any adequate theory of population ethics must deny it.
Argument II — The DVV was abandoned too quickly; the Egyptology objection may be answerable
1.The DVV avoided the Repugnant Conclusion by letting the value of extra lives diminish as their welfare quality declines and as population grows. It was abandoned because of the Egyptology problem: past-population discoveries seem irrelevantly to affect current obligations.
2.But the Egyptology objection may apply to the standard formulation rather than to all DVV variants; constraints on how past-population facts bear on current obligations may address it.
3.The vector programme is the most developed current alternative, but it is not the only option and may not be the final one. The DVV deserves reconsideration alongside the vector programme.
∴The abandonment of the DVV may have been premature; the theoretical space for Millian quality-priority views is richer than the current focus on vector models alone suggests.
Argument III — Four minimum adequacy conditions constrain the solution space
1.Any adequate population axiology must: (i) deny the Repugnant Conclusion; (ii) preserve transitivity of the betterness relation; (iii) satisfy a plausible separability condition; (iv) rank All Good above Overwhelmingly Bad.
2.Simple Totalism satisfies (ii–iv) but fails (i). Strong Lexical Totalism satisfies (i–iii) but fails (iv). Weak Lexical Totalism satisfies (i–ii) but — as Cowie shows — fails (iv) by an extended argument.
3.The lexical-threshold view with negative priority is the most promising candidate to satisfy all four conditions; whether it fully succeeds is the central open question in the field.
∴The four adequacy conditions constitute a useful diagnostic tool: they locate precisely where existing theories fail and indicate what further modifications must achieve; they do not show that the conditions are jointly unsatisfiable.
Argument IV — Against Cowie’s scepticism: hard problems demand better theories, not the abandonment of theory
1.Cowie infers axiological scepticism from the failure of current theories to jointly satisfy all adequacy conditions. This inference requires that the space of theories has been exhausted.
2.The space of theories has not been exhausted: the lexical-threshold view, negative-priority supplements, variable-dimension vector approaches, and combinations thereof remain to be fully developed and assessed.
3.The analogy with science: irresolvable paradoxes in an existing theory call for a better theory, not for the conclusion that there is no truth about the domain. The same holds in axiology.
∴Cowie’s sceptical conclusion is not supported by the current state of population ethics; the paradoxes show that adequate theory is hard to find, not that there is no adequate theory to be found.

I want to present the argument I gave at LSE with its full force, then respond to the most natural defences.

The vector-based view was proposed by Thomas, Nebel, Carlson, and others as the most promising strategy for escaping the Repugnant Conclusion while satisfying Arrhenius’s adequacy conditions. It avoids the Repugnant Conclusion by ensuring that quantity of lower-quality lives can never swamp quality: the higher-component sum always takes priority. I want to show that this strategy generates its own catastrophic paradoxes — paradoxes that are not merely analogous to the original problem but are, in a clear sense, worse.

The Two Main Objections.

Against Strong Lexical Totalism. Overwhelmingly Bad (OB) = one person with welfare [1, 0] (one unit of higher good, no lower goods), plus x·109 people each with [0, −k] (massive lower suffering). All Good (AG) = y people each with [0, m] (great lower goods but no higher goods), y and m very large. SLT entails OB > AG because OB’s h-sum = 1, AG’s h-sum = 0. But AG is obviously better. Contradiction.

Against Weak Lexical Totalism. By pairwise comparison, AG is better than OB. By duplication: create n identical pairs and group them. By Separability (or its near-equivalent): the All Good Group is better than the Overwhelmingly Bad Group. But WLT entails the OB Group ≥ the AG Group because the OB Group’s h-sum = n > 0 = AG Group’s h-sum. Contradiction: WLT is false.
i. the strong lexical totalism objection

SLT entails OB > AG — one unit of higher good beats any amount of lower good; absurdStrong Lexical Totalism holds that a larger h-sum always makes for a better population, with l-sums breaking ties only. This means that any population with even a single person whose life contains even a trace of higher good — the barest moment of genuine autonomous engagement, of philosophical reflection, of deep aesthetic response — is better than any population, however large, in which every life contains only lower goods. But now consider Overwhelmingly Bad: one person with a tiny higher good and a billion people in agony. Overwhelmingly Bad has h-sum = 1; All Good has h-sum = 0 (its members have only sensory pleasure, no higher goods). SLT ranks Overwhelmingly Bad above All Good. This is a more catastrophic result than the Repugnant Conclusion it was designed to replace: it is not merely counterintuitive, it is morally monstrous to say that one unit of higher good outweighs any amount of lower suffering.

ii. the weak lexical totalism objection: the duplication argument

WLT duplication — pairwise comparisons + grouping + Separability entails OB Group ≥ AG Group; contradictionWeak Lexical Totalism weakens the threshold: the h-advantage must exceed some minimum δ before it dominates. This avoids the one-against-many problem but generates a different paradox. Start from the pairwise verdict: All Good is better than Overwhelmingly Bad. Create n duplicate pairs. Group them: n OB populations form the Overwhelmingly Bad Group; n AG populations form the All Good Group. By a principle of additive separability for populations — Premise 3 of my argument, which I show is very hard to deny without rejecting Separability entirely — the All Good Group is better than the Overwhelmingly Bad Group. But Weak LT entails the opposite: the OB Group has h-sum = n > 0, the AG Group has h-sum = 0, and since n ≥ 0 > 0, WLT entails OB Group ≥ AG Group. Contradiction. WLT is false.

iii. premise 3 is hard to deny: the separability connection

Premise 3 defence — denying it requires denying Separability; Separability is hard to denyThe most natural resistance to my argument is to deny Premise 3: that if W is better than X and Y is better than Z, then W∪Y is better than X∪Z (all else equal). I argue this premise is very hard to deny. If we deny it, we must deny Separability: the principle that whether population A is better than B depends only on their non-overlapping parts. And Separability is highly attractive: it says that people who exist in both populations are irrelevant to the comparison between those populations. Denying Separability has strange implications for population policy: it would mean that the welfare of people who will exist regardless of our choices affects the evaluation of those choices. Premise 3 is not only independently plausible; it follows directly from one direction of Separability. And one direction of Separability is very hard to abandon.

iv. the sceptical conclusion: what these failures tell us

Sceptical conclusion — multiple paradoxes from independently plausible premises; better explanation is that there are no moral truthsWhat do these results tell us? The natural reaction is to search for a further modification — some supplementary condition that preserves the vector structure while blocking both the SLT and WLT paradoxes. Thomas himself gestured at a negative-priority condition; Nebel’s lexical-threshold view is a more sophisticated attempt. I do not rule out that some such modification will work. But I think it is worth stepping back and considering an alternative explanation of why the paradoxes keep coming: not that we have not found the right theory yet, but that there is no right theory to be found — that our moral intuitions about population ethics are genuinely inconsistent, and the paradoxes are the formal expression of this inconsistency. This is the explanation I have been developing in my work on moral error theory: the impossibility results in population axiology are best explained not by the incompleteness of our theories but by the inconsistency of our moral commitments.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Strong Lexical Totalism entails Overwhelmingly Bad > All Good: a catastrophic result
1.Strong Lexical Totalism: a larger h-sum always makes for a better population. Overwhelmingly Bad (OB): h-sum = 1 (one person with trace higher good), plus massive lower-bad suffering. All Good (AG): h-sum = 0 (vast population with only lower goods), l-sum very large and positive.
2.Since OB’s h-sum (= 1) > AG’s h-sum (= 0), SLT entails OB > AG — Overwhelmingly Bad is better than All Good. But clearly AG is better: it contains no suffering and a vast quantity of positive welfare.
3.This result is more morally catastrophic than the Repugnant Conclusion: it says that one unit of higher good, accompanied by any amount of suffering, outweighs any amount of positive lower welfare. SLT has not escaped paradox; it has generated a worse one.
∴Strong Lexical Totalism is false: its entailment that Overwhelmingly Bad is better than All Good is a catastrophic result that is more counterintuitive than the Repugnant Conclusion the view was designed to avoid.
Argument II — Weak LT falls to the duplication-and-grouping argument via Separability
1.AG is pairwise better than OB (granted). By duplication and grouping: n paired (AG, OB) sets, grouped into All Good Group (n AGs) and Overwhelmingly Bad Group (n OBs). By Premise 3 (aggregative separability): All Good Group is better than OB Group.
2.But WLT entails: OB Group h-sum = n ≥ 0 = AG Group h-sum. Since n > 0, WLT entails OB Group ≥ AG Group. This contradicts the conclusion of step 1.
3.The contradiction is derived using only: (i) the pairwise verdict, (ii) Premise 3 (near-Separability), and (iii) WLT’s own definitions. The argument is valid; one of these must be false. WLT’s verdict (iii) is the right thing to reject.
∴Weak Lexical Totalism is false: the duplication-and-grouping argument derives a contradiction from WLT, the pairwise verdict, and a near-Separability principle that is very hard to deny.
Argument III — Premise 3 is hard to deny: denying it requires denying one direction of Separability
1.Premise 3: if W > X and Y > Z, then W∪Y > X∪Z (all else equal). If we deny this, we must deny one direction of Separability: that X > Y iff X∪Z > Y∪Z for all Z.
2.Separability is highly attractive: people who exist in both populations are morally irrelevant to comparing those populations. Denying it has strange policy implications: the welfare of people who exist regardless of our choices would affect the evaluation of those choices.
3.A weaker version of Premise 3 also suffices and is even harder to deny: if each of W⊂1;, W⊂2;...W⊂n; is better than any of X⊂1;, X⊂2;...X⊂n;, then their union is better than the other’s union.
∴Premise 3 is very hard to deny without rejecting Separability, which has strong independent support; the duplication argument is therefore compelling and WLT’s rejection of its conclusion is the right response.
Argument IV — The best explanation of recurring paradoxes is moral inconsistency, not theoretical incompleteness
1.Multiple vector formulations (SLT, WLT) generate catastrophic paradoxes from independently plausible premises. Each modification proposed generates new problems. The pattern repeats across the entire field.
2.Two explanations are available: (a) we have not yet found the right theory (theoretical incompleteness); (b) our moral intuitions about population ethics are genuinely inconsistent (moral inconsistency). Explanation (b) predicts that the paradoxes will continue regardless of how theories are modified.
3.The persistent recurrence of paradoxes across radically different theoretical frameworks — Totalism, Average Utilitarianism, DVV, Vector Totalism — is better explained by (b) than by (a): it is unlikely that so many theories have failed through mere incompleteness rather than because the intuitions they aim to satisfy are inconsistent.
∴The best explanation of the recurring paradoxes is that our moral intuitions about population ethics are genuinely inconsistent; this supports axiological scepticism and, by further argument, moral error theory.

My argument complements Cowie’s but approaches the problem from a different direction.

Cowie shows that specific versions of the vector view generate catastrophic paradoxes. I want to show something more structural: that lexical views, as a class, face a dilemma that gives us no reason to prefer them over Archimedean views, because Archimedean views face an entirely analogous dilemma. If this is right, then the debate between lexical and Archimedean views is a standoff, and the vector programme offers no stable escape from the original predicament.

“The lexical dilemma gives us little reason to prefer lexical views over Archimedean views. Even if we give up on lexicality — even if we accept that quantity can always substitute for quality at some rate — problems of exactly the same kind remain.” — Thornley, 2022
i. the lexical dilemma stated

Lexical dilemma — either implausible universal dominance or radical incompletenessLexical views face the following dilemma. Suppose we say that lives with positive h-components are lexically superior to lives with h = 0. Then consider a sequence of lives starting with a very good life that has a positive h-component and descending by small steps to lives with progressively less h-good and more l-good. At some point in this sequence, the h-component reaches its minimum positive value. Call this life “the threshold life.” On the strong version of lexicality, the threshold life — which is only marginally above the threshold — is better than any number of lives that are only marginally below the threshold. This implies that an enormously large population of lives just below the threshold is not as good as a single life just above it. This seems implausible: how can a single extra moment of genuine autonomy make a life categorically superior to a vastly comfortable life that lacks it only marginally? The alternative is to say the comparison is indeterminate — but then the “at least as good as” relation is radically incomplete.

ii. the archimedean dilemma is analogous

Archimedean dilemma — razor-sharp critical level or symmetric incompletenessArchimedean views hold that quantity can always substitute for quality at some (possibly very large but finite) rate: for any life A and any life B worse than A, some sufficiently large number of B-lives makes a B-population at least as good as any A-population. These views seem to avoid the lexical dilemma by making comparisons always determinate. But they face an analogous problem. The Archimedean principle requires a precise boundary between good lives and bad lives — a critical level such that lives above it contribute positively to population value and lives at or below it do not. But this boundary must be razor-sharp: just two extra mosquito bites could flip a long turbulent life from good to bad. No plausible account of welfare can support such precision. And if we relax precision — say there is a range of lives whose status is indeterminate — then Archimedean views also generate radical incompleteness, symmetrically with the lexical dilemma.

iii. why this is a problem for the vector programme specifically

Against vector programme — vector views are lexical; therefore face the lexical dilemma; no escapeThe vector programme is a form of lexical view: welfare levels are vectors with a lexically prior h-component. It therefore faces the lexical dilemma. The h-threshold — the level at which a life’s h-component becomes positive — must either be razor-sharp (one nanosecond of autonomous reflection tips a life into a categorically higher league) or generate radical incompleteness in the ordering. Thomas’s and Nebel’s moves toward “incommensurability” near the threshold acknowledge the second horn of the dilemma but do not resolve it. Acknowledging that some populations are incomparable is not the same as providing a theory that satisfactorily orders the populations that are comparable. And the incomparability near the threshold is not a minor technical inconvenience; it infects population comparisons whenever one population is better than another in h-terms but worse in l-terms near the threshold.

iv. the standoff and its implications for the debate

Standoff — lexical and Archimedean views both face symmetric dilemmas; vector programme no betterMy conclusion is that the lexical dilemma and the Archimedean dilemma are symmetric: both families of views face a choice between implausible precision and radical incompleteness. This means the vector programme offers no stable escape from the original predicament in population axiology. If you were drawn to the vector programme because lexical views avoid the Repugnant Conclusion, you should ask: at what cost? The cost is either an implausibly sharp h-threshold or a radically incomplete ordering. And the Archimedean view faces exactly the same cost via its critical level. The vector programme has not resolved the problem; it has relocated it. Cowie’s sceptical conclusion therefore has support from two directions: the specific paradoxes he identifies, and the more structural symmetry I identify. Together they suggest that the problem is not simply one of finding a better theory but may be evidence of deeper inconsistency in our axiological commitments.

I want to explain why the vector programme does not escape my impossibility results, even when it escapes the specific formulations I presented in earlier work.

My six impossibility theorems show that no population axiology can jointly satisfy a small set of intuitively compelling adequacy conditions. Thomas argued in 2018 that Lexical Totalism escapes these theorems by denying Finite Fine-Grainedness — the assumption that between any two welfare levels there is a finite chain of small steps. I accept this narrow point. But I want to show that my results can be extended into the domain of risky choices — choices among population prospects — in a way that reaches lexical views without depending on Finite Fine-Grainedness.

The Population Prospect Extension. All our population-affecting actions have non-zero probabilities of bringing about different populations. It is therefore population prospect axiologies that are practically relevant — theories that rank probability distributions over populations. My amended impossibility theorems show that there is no satisfactory population prospect axiology. Crucially, these theorems do not depend on Finite Fine-Grainedness and therefore apply to lexical views including the vector programme.
i. why every population-affecting action is a prospect

Prospects are necessary — all real decisions affect populations under uncertainty; axiology must address prospectsConsider any real-world policy decision that affects population size or composition: climate policy, healthcare allocation, migration policy, pandemic response. Each such decision is made under uncertainty: different policy choices lead to different population outcomes with different probabilities. If we are interested in population axiology as a guide to real-world decision-making — not merely as an abstract theoretical exercise — we need an account of how to rank population prospects, not merely population outcomes. The assumption of certainty that governs most population axiology is therefore an idealisation that must be lifted if the theory is to be practically applicable.

ii. the extended impossibility results

Prospect impossibility — extends to lexical views; Fine-Grainedness not needed; State-Wise Dominance does the workIn my extended results, I use the axiom of State-Wise Dominance: if prospect P produces a better population than prospect Q in every possible state of the world, then P is better than Q. This axiom is extremely plausible — it would be bizarre to prefer a prospect that leads to worse outcomes in every scenario. Using State-Wise Dominance, any impossibility theorem in population axiology can be lifted to an impossibility theorem for population prospect axiology. The crucial point is that this extension does not require Finite Fine-Grainedness. Lexical views escape my original theorems by denying Fine-Grainedness. They cannot escape the extended prospect theorems by the same move. The prospect extension closes the loophole that Thomas identified.

iii. what the extended results show about the vector programme

Vector programme reached — no satisfactory prospect axiology; lexical structure insufficientThe extended impossibility results show that there is no satisfactory population prospect axiology — no theory that jointly satisfies a small set of compelling adequacy conditions for ranking population prospects. This applies to prospect extensions of vector views. Even if we grant that welfare has a lexical two-component structure, any theory that ranks probability distributions over populations in a way that satisfies the adequacy conditions will violate at least one of them when applied to prospect comparisons. This is a stronger and more general result than my original theorems: it applies across the full space of population axiologies including lexical and vector views, without depending on controversial assumptions about the structure of welfare.

Thomas-Cowie debate — both right and both wrong; the deeper problem is more general than either recognisesThomas is right that Lexical Totalism escapes my original theorems by denying Fine-Grainedness.
