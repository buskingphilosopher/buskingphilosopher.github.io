Mr President,

We are here to debate how a society should aggregate the wellbeing of its members into a single evaluative standard. The question is not whether to aggregate. The question is how to do it honestly.

I want to defend the utilitarian social welfare function — the view that the goodness of a social state is the sum of the wellbeing of the individuals within it — as the conclusion that follows from taking seriously the equal moral worth of persons and individual wellbeing as the fundamental currency of social evaluation.

On equal moral worth and the separateness of persons

The argument from equal moral worthThe equal moral worth of persons means that a unit of wellbeing gained by one person is worth exactly as much as a unit gained by any other. To say otherwise — to say that a gain to a worse-off person is worth more than an equal gain to a better-off person — is not to respect persons equally. It is to discriminate between them on the basis of their position in a welfare distribution. Each person's wellbeing enters the utilitarian social welfare function separately and equally: this is what equal moral worth requires.

On diminishing marginal utility

The argument from diminishing marginal utilityAn extra hundred pounds means more to a poor person than to a rich one. But this argument supports utilitarianism, not prioritarianism. If redistribution to the poor produces more wellbeing, summing wellbeing already favours that redistribution. Prioritarianism adds an extra positional weighting on top — claiming that even if wellbeing gains were identical, we should still prefer gains to go to the worse-off. It is this specifically prioritarian claim that I reject. It introduces a value beyond wellbeing into a framework that is supposed to be about wellbeing.

On what grounds the prioritarian weighting

The argument from unexplained positional weightingThe prioritarian says a unit of wellbeing at welfare level five is worth more than a unit at welfare level fifteen. But ex hypothesi the wellbeing gain is identical. The extra weight must come from the person's positional rank — but no satisfactory account has been given of why being worse off, independent of wellbeing, generates additional moral weight. The observation that it is worse to be worse off supports concern for absolute badness of low welfare levels — not the specifically prioritarian claim that gains to the worse-off count for more.

On the repugnant conclusion

The argument from the repugnant conclusionThe repugnant conclusion is not distinctive to utilitarianism. Prioritarianism also sums positively weighted wellbeing, and additional people's positive wellbeing is always a positive contribution. The problem belongs to variable-population ethics generally, and the right response is to examine what it tells us about population ethics — not to abandon sum-ranking in fixed-population contexts where its implications are far less contested.

On tractability and theoretical determinacy

The argument from tractability and determinacyThe utilitarian SWF generates determinate recommendations. Prioritarianism requires the specification of a weighting function — how much extra weight to give to the worse-off as a function of welfare level — and the prioritarian principle itself does not determine this choice. This is not a merely practical problem. It is a theoretical indeterminacy at the heart of prioritarianism. A framework that cannot tell us, even in principle, how much priority to give the worse-off has deferred its central question rather than answering it.

Sum the wellbeing. Weight each person equally. Follow the argument where it leads.

Mr President,

My colleague Professor Broome has made the philosopher's case for the utilitarian social welfare function. I want to make a different kind of case — the case from rational choice theory, from the foundations of decision under uncertainty, and from what I believe is the most rigorous derivation of utilitarian conclusions in the history of moral philosophy.

I want to show you that utilitarianism is not merely intuitively plausible, or theoretically elegant. It is the conclusion that follows necessarily from axioms about rational behaviour that virtually everyone accepts.

On the veil of ignorance and rational choice

The argument from equal moral worthSuppose you do not know which position in society you will occupy — you are behind a veil of ignorance. A rational person in this position would not choose Rawls's maximin principle, which requires infinite risk aversion. Rational decision-making under uncertainty — as von Neumann and Morgenstern showed — is expected utility maximisation. If each social position is equally likely, then maximising expected utility means maximising average utility across all positions. And maximising average utility just is the utilitarian social welfare function. Maximin is a phobia masquerading as a principle of justice.

On the aggregation theorem

The argument from Harsanyi's aggregation theoremIn 1955, I proved what is now known as Harsanyi's Aggregation Theorem. If three conditions hold — individual rationality (preferences satisfy the von Neumann-Morgenstern axioms), social rationality (social preferences also satisfy those axioms), and the Pareto principle (if everyone is indifferent between two lotteries, society is indifferent) — then it follows mathematically that the social welfare function must be a weighted sum of individual utility functions. With equal weighting, we get the utilitarian SWF. This is not a philosophical argument that can be resisted by appeal to intuition. It is a mathematical theorem.

On the objection from fairness

The argument from unexplained positional weightingThe standard objection is that utilitarianism allows the sacrifice of some individuals for aggregate gain. But this rests on a misunderstanding. The utilitarian SWF takes every person's utility seriously and allows none to be systematically discounted. The objection relies on contrived extreme cases — the utility monster, organ harvesting — that are highly unlikely to obtain in practice. When a mathematical theorem and a contrived intuition conflict, the appropriate response is not always to abandon the theorem. Sometimes it is to revise the intuition.

On cardinality and interpersonal comparison

The argument from tractability and determinacyOpponents will object that the utilitarian SWF requires cardinal utility and interpersonal comparisons. This objection has less force than it once did. The von Neumann-Morgenstern framework provides a natural method for cardinal measurement through preferences over lotteries. And the interpersonal comparison problem is not unique to utilitarianism — any SWF, including the prioritarian, requires interpersonal comparisons. The prioritarian must compare not just utility levels but the moral weights attached to them, which is, if anything, a more demanding requirement.

On the priority view as irrational risk aversion

The argument from prioritarianism as disguised risk aversionBehind the veil of ignorance, every gain in any position contributes equally to expected utility. A gain of ten units of wellbeing in position one is worth exactly as much as a gain of ten units in position ten. The utilitarian SWF correctly captures this impartiality. The prioritarian SWF gives extra weight to gains in lower positions — which means, from behind the veil, it is giving extra weight to what happens to you in worse positions. This is just risk aversion — a preference for distributions skewed toward better outcomes in worse positions. There is no rational basis for this particular risk attitude over any other. Prioritarianism is not a principle of justice. It is a risk attitude dressed up as one.

Mr President,

My opponent's defence of the utilitarian social welfare function is careful and sophisticated. It is also wrong — and wrong in ways that matter deeply for how we think about justice, distribution, and the moral evaluation of social states.

I want to defend prioritarianism: that benefiting people matters more the worse off those people are. Not as sentiment, and not as confusion with egalitarianism, but as the conclusion that follows from taking seriously what moral philosophy says about wellbeing, value, and our duties to one another.

On equal moral worth — a misappropriated argument

The argument from equal moral worthMy opponent argues that equal moral worth supports utilitarianism, because weighting the gains of the worse-off more heavily is discrimination. This gets the implication of equal moral worth precisely backwards. Consider: two people are drowning. One is a strong swimmer; one is a non-swimmer in immediate mortal danger. Equal moral worth does not tell us to flip a coin. It tells us to save the non-swimmer first — because the urgency of her need is greater. The prioritarian weighting is precisely an expression of equal moral worth, correctly understood: equal moral consideration sensitive to how badly off that person is, because being badly off makes claims on assistance more urgent. This is not discrimination. It is responsiveness.

On diminishing marginal utility — a distinction that is not a confusion

The argument from diminishing marginal utilityMy opponent conflates two distinct claims. Diminishing marginal utility is empirical — an extra pound produces more wellbeing for a poor person. Prioritarianism is normative — a unit of wellbeing gained by a worse-off person contributes more to social welfare than an identical unit gained by a better-off person. These are independent. Suppose we could equalise the marginal utility of resources across all persons — make an extra pound produce identical wellbeing gains for everyone. In that world, the utilitarian would be indifferent between giving resources to the worst-off or best-off person. The prioritarian would not be indifferent. I submit the prioritarian's intuition is correct and the utilitarian's indifference is a failure of moral sensitivity.

On what grounds the prioritarian weighting

The argument from unexplained positional weightingMy opponent challenges prioritarianism to explain what grounds the extra weight given to the worse-off. The challenge can be met. The weighting is grounded in the moral significance of the absolute level of wellbeing at which a person finds herself. Being badly off creates stronger claims on the moral concern of others. The worse off you are, the greater the urgency of your situation and the greater the moral value of improving it. This is not an invocation of something beyond wellbeing. It is a claim about the relationship between absolute welfare level and the moral weight of gains at that level — the recognition that a commitment to wellbeing, taken seriously, must be sensitive to where in the welfare distribution a gain occurs.

On prioritarianism's independence from egalitarianism

The argument distinguishing prioritarianism from egalitarianismThe egalitarian values equality as such. If two people have identical wellbeing levels, reducing both by the same amount makes the world neither better nor worse — equality is preserved. The prioritarian values absolute welfare levels weighted by a strictly concave function. Reducing both equally is straightforwardly bad — less wellbeing is worse, and no distributional story changes this. Prioritarianism does not value equality as such. It values the wellbeing of worse-off individuals more — avoiding the levelling-down objection that afflicts egalitarianism. It is the sophisticated middle position between utilitarian indifference to distribution and egalitarian indifference to absolute levels.

On indeterminacy — a challenge for both sides

The argument from indeterminacyMy opponent argues that prioritarianism faces indeterminacy because the weighting function is not determined by the prioritarian principle. True. But the utilitarian faces an exactly parallel challenge: the SWF requires specification of a cardinal wellbeing measure, and different measures give different social rankings. The choice is not determined by the utilitarian principle. This is the measurement problem, at least as serious as the weighting problem. All serious normative frameworks face deep indeterminacy in application. Indeterminacy in application is the normal condition of ethical theory meeting complexity — not a decisive objection to prioritarianism specifically.

On tractability — practical convenience is not moral truth

The argument from tractability and determinacyTractability matters — but it is not a substitute for truth. Cost-benefit analysis as currently practised is implicitly utilitarian, and it is deeply inadequate as a guide to distributive justice. It assigns value proportional to willingness to pay — a function of income — and thereby systematically underweights the interests of the poor. This is not a feature. It is a flaw following directly from the additive, unweighted structure of the utilitarian framework. The tractability of utilitarianism in cost-benefit analysis is partly a product of its moral failures. A framework that ignores the stronger claims of the worse-off is easier to operationalise than one that takes them seriously. This is not a recommendation.

On the utilitarian's indifference to distribution

The argument from distributional indifferenceThe utilitarian is indifferent between a world where one person gains ten units of wellbeing and a world where ten people each gain one unit — provided the sum is the same. This indifference to distribution in the fixed-population case is precisely what prioritarianism rejects. A gain of ten units to one already well-off person is not morally equivalent to ten gains of one unit to ten worse-off persons, even if the sums are equal. The distribution matters. A framework for social evaluation that cannot see this has missed something important about what it means to care about persons.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I find myself disagreeing with both speakers who have preceded me — and doing so for reasons that expose a shared assumption underlying both the utilitarian and prioritarian positions that deserves to be challenged.

Professor Adler has correctly identified that the utilitarian SWF is morally inadequate. But the prioritarian SWF makes a different and equally serious error. It focuses exclusively on absolute welfare levels and ignores the relational facts about inequality that matter morally. I want to argue for an egalitarian social welfare function — one sensitive not just to absolute welfare levels but to the relations between persons and the intrinsic moral significance of equality.

On what egalitarianism is — and what it is not

The argument distinguishing prioritarianism from egalitarianismBoth my opponents will invoke Parfit's levelling-down objection. I accept that a crude egalitarianism — valuing equality as an end in itself regardless of absolute welfare levels — faces this objection. But this is not the only form of egalitarianism available. The view I defend is closer to relational egalitarianism: what matters is not merely how well off each person is in isolation, but how persons stand in relation to one another. Equality matters because inequality — of the relevant kind, in the relevant domains — is a wrong done to persons, not merely a sub-optimal pattern in a distribution.

On what prioritarianism misses

The argument from distributional indifferenceThe prioritarian SWF gives extra weight to gains accruing to worse-off persons. This captures something important — the urgency of helping those badly off. But it misses something equally important. Suppose we can either give a large benefit to one person at level five, raising her to level fifteen; or give smaller benefits to five people at level five, raising each to level eight. The total weighted welfare gain may be similar. But there is something morally significant about the second option that neither framework captures: it treats the five people as claimants with equal standing, rather than allowing the greater benefit to one to simply dominate the smaller benefits to many. The separateness of persons applies equally against a prioritarianism that aggregates weighted welfare.

On the claims-across-persons problem

The argument from unexplained positional weightingT.M. Scanlon has argued that what we owe to each other cannot be captured by aggregative frameworks of any kind. When we weigh claims of different persons against each other, we are not performing arithmetic. We are asking what each person has reason to demand, and what each person can reasonably be expected to accept. Both the utilitarian and the prioritarian make the same fundamental error: they aggregate. They produce a number and rank distributions by it. But moral reasoning about distributions requires comparative evaluation of claims, person by person, in a way that respects the separateness of persons not merely by counting them separately but by taking seriously that each person's claim is owed a response she herself could accept.

On levelling-down — a more careful response

The argument from indeterminacyThe levelling-down objection has force against a view that treats equality as independently valuable, separable from and potentially overriding the welfare of persons. But the egalitarian view I defend does not treat equality this way. It treats equality as valuable because of what inequality does to persons — what it means for persons to stand in relations of significant inequality, to be marked as having lesser standing. When we level down, we do not produce the relational good of equality — we produce equal misery in which relational facts about standing may be unchanged or worse. The levelling-down objection does not touch relational egalitarianism.

On the difference between priority and equality

The argument from equal moral worthPrioritarianism captures the intuition that we should weight the interests of those badly off more heavily. But it does so in an entirely individualistic way — asking only about absolute welfare levels in isolation, not about how persons stand in relation to one another. Two distributions identical in their vector of absolute welfare levels are ranked identically by the prioritarian, regardless of whether those levels are achieved through a history of exploitation, discrimination, or systematic subordination. This is not just a practical objection. Equality matters not merely as a pattern in a distribution but as a feature of relationships between persons. A society in which some are systematically disadvantaged — even if absolute levels are acceptable — is one in which persons do not stand as equals. Neither the utilitarian nor the prioritarian SWF can capture this relational fact.

On what egalitarianism requires in practice

The argument from tractability and determinacyI am not arguing that egalitarianism requires perfect equality of outcomes. I am arguing that a SWF sensitive to distributional inequality — to the gaps between persons and the relations those gaps constitute — is more adequate than one considering only absolute welfare levels, weighted or unweighted. In practice: a policy raising total welfare while increasing inequality may be worse than one raising it less while reducing inequality — not because equality is traded off against welfare, but because the relational facts about equality are themselves part of what a welfare-sensitive account of social goodness must incorporate. Neither the utilitarian tractability argument nor the prioritarian weighting argument can see this.

The conclusion

The argument from diminishing marginal utilityBoth the utilitarian and prioritarian SWFs take individual welfare seriously. They differ in whether they weight it by absolute level. What neither takes seriously is the relational dimension of equality — the fact that how persons stand in relation to one another is itself a matter of moral significance that cannot be captured by any function of the vector of individual welfare levels, however weighted. An adequate SWF must be sensitive to this relational dimension. Neither the utilitarian nor the prioritarian framework provides it.

Thank you.
