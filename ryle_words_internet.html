Ryle, Words and the Internet

Published April 6, 2026

Policymakers talk of cleaning up the internet.

But the internet can’t be cleaned in the way that the streets get cleaned. They can’t wash it with water and soap to rid it of dirt.

Of course what they mean by ‘clean’ is to clear the internet of harmful material, make it harder to access disinformation and remove illegal sites.

So by using the word ‘clean’, policymakers are employing a metaphor to relate it to another, more ordinary and familiar way of cleaning. It’s an analogy to give sense to the policy process to people unfamiliar with computer systems but familiar with the cleaning of streets, or showering or washing dishes, etc.

Consider now that minds also can’t be ‘clean’ in the same way that bodies can. Physically it would be possible to wash the physical brain clean, but that would have little effect in ridding a mentally disturbed mind of its ills.

So once again we have a metaphor from the domain of physical experience, applied to the mind, the domain of phenomenal or abstract experience.

According to Gilbert Ryle, this is the cause of so much philosophy. Things appear to us confusing and philosophical not because they really are so, mostly it’s because we have used words appropriate to one domain of language to describe another and mistaken the metaphor for a literal description.

So when we talk of the mind and the body being real, we forget that the sense in which they are real is not the same. When we get confused about whether the mind is ‘real’, this is because we are applying the standards of reality appropriate to the domain of physical objects (can I touch it, can I feel it, etc.) to the reality appropriate to another domain (abstract entities like the mind).

Just as the internet is as real as your computer, you can’t clean the internet in the way you can clean your monitor by wiping your screen with a cloth. But it would be wrong to judge the reality of the internet according to whether you can wipe it clean with a cloth. No one would accept this as a good reason for doubting the existence of the internet if you did.

In The Concept of Mind, Gilbert Ryle made an analogy between the mind and the university. He said it would be odd if, after giving a friend a tour of your university, pointing out to them the student union, the lecture halls and the student accommodation, they seemed puzzled and asked you ‘ok, so where is the university?’ You would feel they have missed the point. ‘The university is all of it!’ would be a suitable reply.

Similarly, with the mind. When we ask where the mind is, we are sometimes asking for its physical location. In this, we are noting that we found a physical location for the brain, and are therefore inferring that all ‘real’ things have a corresponding physical location. But after failing to ‘find’ the mind within the body – even within the brain – we apply this reality condition to the mind and find ourselves committed to the view that the mind isn’t real.

The issue with this is that there are several other entities we haven’t found a location for, and for which no serious person considers this fact to be a good reason to reject their reality. Consider numbers. No one has ever found the number 2. Only instance of 2, like 2 chairs or 2 people. But it would be strange to stop using the word 2 because it hasn’t been found in physical space.

Similarly instances of the mind can be found, such as human art or buildings or complex inventions, all of which is evidence for minds because things without minds (trees, rocks, etc) just don’t seem capable of producing such impressive things.

So can we, in the same way we infer from the instances of 2 to the number 2, infer from the instances of the mind all around us to the reality of the mind itself?
