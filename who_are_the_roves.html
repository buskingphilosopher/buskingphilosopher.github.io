Who Are The Roves?

Published March 20, 2026

A complete unknown was caught in a photo at Strongrooms Studio with Cameron Winter of Geese last month. Many people are asking who this band is. And they turns out to be only one of London's most incredible forgotten bands you definitely never heard of in the first place.

They're called The Roves and I think you'd better put on your headphones for this one.

That there was from their latest release Pope and The Computer. But the band is integral to understanding London at the moment. They're not new, they've seen the rise and fall of bands. And their back catalogue stretches on to tell that tale.

You see, lately there's a curious electrical hum that seeps out of crumbling saw-dust pubs across London, reminiscent of the outlaw chic and hoodlum existentialism of Exile On Main Street.

That whole schizo crazy world of good old rock n' roll pulsates from the guitar amps of The Roves. Easily the most brilliant songwriters in Britain right now, they are hardly known at all. But listen to Needle Factory and it'll be difficult to fault that credential.

They have attracted a cult following of the leaders of many of London's finest bands, and they don't want you to know. The Roves gigs are where they go to be anonymous and let loose out of the way of the cameras.

Fresh out of the recording studio with Geese's Cameron Winter at Strongroom Studios, their next performance is in the air but they will play again soon.

London is screaming desperately for something to follow the Black Midis and the Black Country New Roads and it seems they've settled on 70s rock n' roll to fill that void.

In a political climate much like that of the 60s, with crystal clear crackdowns on pedestrian Palestinian protestors and elections known merely to swap one dusty old elite with another, it's no wonder The Roves are being chosen as the soundtrack to this carbonated youth-led revolt.

Given the blazing oversaturation of quasi-tribute bands to the Windmill Brixton boom, no one in London is yearning for yet another band sporting incoherent honking baritone saxophones over glitch-bubblegum autosynth kitch.

Leave that to the fin-tech bros 10 years behind the demand curve. The rest of us are sliding down an indifference curve taking us somewhere else entirely. The Roves are offering a glimpse of a different future, distorted with brighter tones and devoid of your usual gloom-core cringe straight from the office in Canary Wharf.

The band was formed by brothers Tom and James, both on guitar and vocals. On bass is ... and on drums the gentle Welshman ...

I first met James in a strange ornate pub near Maida Vale grand and opulent like a palace and yet too large for its clientelle. I counted about 8 of us in the pub, the bar staff included alongside myself, James and Oscar. He was unassuming but I was introduced to him as the great song writer of the little-known band The Roves, one of two bands which inspired the band I had only recently joined, The Paris Match. The other band had also disbanded. They were a much more well-known band with a tangible cult following, called The Dada Movement.

The Roves had disbanded already by this point, but Oscar had only incredible things to say about them. After a good innings of gigging and recording 10 years ago, it seemed they'd come to accept there just wasn't enough interest in rock n' roll anymore. It was time to let go and pursue something else. A familiar story.

Kicked out at closing time, we wandered down a long generic white-columned West London street where we got ourselves invited in to one of the houses by the Russian oligarchs who lived there. Lavished with free drinks, we partied for some time with their family before I lime biked home.

After months of nagging the band to reunite, they obliged and a handful of my band went to see them live at The Old Dispensary in Camberwell. We were the only supporters there but heck were we loud. Oscar sang along to all the songs as if it was Oasis stood before us. He was practically on the stage for most of it, arms up screaming the words.

But still they had no drummer or bassist. Their set was the two brothers on acoustic guitars and the true glory of their songs was yet to be experienced. Problem for them was their old bassist and drummer had moved their lives to Wales having left their musical dreams behind them. Convincing them to get back into the game because one band of 5 members was digging their backcatalog was hardly going to be enough. Oscar would keep telling me The Roves need need need to get the band back together and they're just moping around... Umming and erring...

Then came our chance. We had been invited to Paris for fete de la musique and had two shows either side of that. A week-long tour booked, the first time The Paris Match would be playing there with the full band. It was going to be incredible. And who would we bring along for our support act?

That's right. The Roves.
