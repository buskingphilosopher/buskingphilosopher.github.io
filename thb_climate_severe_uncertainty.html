Logical Arguments

Mr President,

Climate change is not, at its core, a problem of insufficient technological capability or aggregate economic will. It is a problem of distributive justice — specifically, who is permitted to impose the burden of a gamble on whom. The frameworks that have dominated climate economics for three decades have treated this as an optimisation problem: maximise the present discounted value of global welfare subject to emission constraints. That framing is wrong, and its wrongness has trillion-dollar consequences.

On what the DICE model actually assumes

DICE converts genuine ambiguity into subjective expected utility — the move Ellsberg showed is rationally and morally contestableThe DICE model and integrated assessment models in its tradition assume that we can assign credible probability distributions to climate outcomes — to the likelihood of crossing tipping points like AMOC collapse, to the economic toll of warming at different magnitudes. These are not known probabilities. The collapse of the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation does not have an actuarial table. What the DICE model must do to generate its elegant cost-benefit outputs is convert genuine severe uncertainty into a tractable probability distribution and then maximise expected aggregate utility across that distribution. This is the move that our work on egalitarianism under severe uncertainty identifies as both rationally and morally contestable. Severe uncertainty is not merely a technical limitation to be managed with subjective probability assignments. It is a morally distinct condition — and encoding it as if it were ordinary risk systematically understates the burden placed on those who cannot buy their way out of the worst outcomes.

On the unequal distribution of the burden of ambiguity

The world's poor cannot hedge against climate ambiguity — the rich buy air conditioning and sea walls; the poor bear the full uncertainty directlyHere is the moral core of our position. When an international body adopts a climate policy under severe uncertainty, it distributes not only the risk of harm but the ambiguity about the probability of harm — and it distributes these very unequally. A wealthy nation in a temperate zone can substantially protect itself against a wide range of climate outcomes: air conditioning, sea walls, insurance, relocation, access to global food markets when local agriculture fails. A subsistence farmer in the Sahel, a fishing community on a low-lying Pacific island, a garment worker in coastal Bangladesh — these people have no such hedges. They bear the ambiguity directly. This is not merely a risk inequality. It is an uncertainty inequality, and our pluralist egalitarian framework holds that it demands a distinct moral response.

On what the correct policy response looks like

Policy output: strict global emissions caps; egalitarian adaptation finance ensuring no region bears a disproportionate share of the climate gambleThe policy implications follow directly. First, global emissions caps must be set not at the level that maximises expected aggregate welfare — the DICE model output — but at the level that prevents the imposition of unacceptably unequal burdens of ambiguity on those who cannot protect themselves against climate tails. Second, international climate funds — the Green Climate Fund, loss-and-damage mechanisms, adaptation finance — must be allocated not by aggregate welfare-maximising criteria but by criteria that ensure no region is left to bear an unequal share of the climate gamble. The specific, living people of Tuvalu, of the Maldives, of sub-Saharan Africa, are not entries in a global utility function. They are persons to whom we owe a specific duty: not to impose on them an uncertainty about their survival that we ourselves are not prepared to bear.

On the ex-post dimension: if catastrophe strikes, who absorbs it?

Ex post equality: a just climate policy must ensure that if the worst happens, the burden is shared uniformly — not absorbed entirely by those least able to bear itOur view demands attention to both ex ante fairness — the distribution of chances before outcomes are known — and ex post fairness — the distribution of actual harm once outcomes are realised. The DICE framework, by focusing on expected values, gives no independent weight to ex post equality: it is indifferent between a world where climate harm is distributed equally and one where the same expected aggregate harm is concentrated entirely on the global poor. We are not indifferent between these worlds. A climate policy that produces moderate expected aggregate harm but concentrates it on the most vulnerable — because the rich have already purchased their adaptation — is morally worse than one distributing it more equally. This is a concrete policy demand: loss-and-damage mechanisms must be treated not as charitable donations but as legally binding obligations to share the ex post burden that our collective emissions created.

On why Nordhaus's Pareto argument fails under uncertainty aversion

Under uncertainty aversion, rational agents may prefer strict equal-burden policies over higher expected welfare policies that concentrate ambiguity on the poorThe DICE model's defenders invoke a Pareto for Prospects argument: any strict cap policy that reduces expected aggregate welfare makes everyone worse off in expectation, including the global poor who benefit most from growth. But under uncertainty aversion — which is rationally and morally permissible — an individual facing genuine ambiguity about their survival does not simply prefer the option with higher expected value. A subsistence farmer who faces genuine uncertainty about whether their region will become uninhabitable in thirty years is not irrational to prefer a policy that eliminates that worst-case scenario over one that marginally increases their expected income. The Pareto argument fails precisely because the people it claims to protect are precisely the people whose uncertainty aversion is most rationally grounded.

On the Green Climate Fund and loss-and-damage as policy instruments

Concrete instruments: GCF scaled by ambiguity-burden equity; legally binding loss-and-damage; adaptation finance allocated by inequality criteria, not aggregate welfare criteriaLet me be concrete. The Green Climate Fund must be scaled to reflect equity in the distribution of the ambiguity burden — not what wealthy nations find economically convenient. Loss-and-damage mechanisms established at COP27 are the right architecture, but they remain voluntary and inadequate. Our framework requires they be made legally binding: wealthy high-emission nations created the uncertainty now threatening low-emission vulnerable nations, and the costs of that uncertainty must be borne proportionally by those who generated the ambiguity. Adaptation finance must be directed to populations bearing the greatest share of the uncertainty burden — not allocated by aggregate welfare-maximising criteria.

On the political economy objection

The DICE model's moderate outputs are not politically neutral — they encode the preferences of those who can hedge climate ambiguity into the architecture of what counts as optimalWe are told our framework is impractical — that politically achievable climate policy must work within IAM optimisation, and the DICE model's moderate carbon tax is the realistic best. This argument deserves a direct response. The DICE model's outputs are not politically neutral reference points. They are the outputs of a framework that systematically encodes the interests of those who can hedge against climate ambiguity — the wealthy, the powerful, those whose economic activity generated the problem — into the architecture of what counts as optimal. The moderate carbon tax is not a compromise between ideal justice and political reality. It is the policy preference of those who can afford to treat climate change as a manageable expected-value problem, imposed on those who cannot.

Mr President,

My colleagues have made the egalitarian case against integrated assessment models with great force. I want to reinforce it from a different direction — from decision theory itself. The claim is not merely that the DICE model reaches wrong distributional conclusions. It is that the DICE model uses the wrong decision procedure from the very first step, and that correcting this error — replacing risk-neutral expected utility with risk-weighted expected utility — independently generates far more aggressive climate policy, even before any egalitarian consideration enters the picture.

On the inadequacy of risk-neutral expected utility for tail risks

Risk-neutrality is a substantive normative commitment — treating a 10% chance of tipping-point catastrophe as merely 10% of a catastrophe is not a neutral modelling choiceThe DICE model and its IAM cousins are risk-neutral: they treat a ten-percent chance of a catastrophic climate tipping point as ten percent of a catastrophe — a figure to be weighed linearly against other welfare considerations. Lara Buchak's Risk-Weighted Expected Utility framework demonstrates why this risk neutrality is not a technical default but a substantive normative commitment, and one that many rational agents do not and should not share. REU replaces the linear probability weighting with a risk function that can overweight the probability of bad outcomes across the full distribution — not merely at the extreme tail, as Rawls's maximin does, but continuously across a Hurwicz-style curve. REU is not an irrational deviation from expected utility theory. It is an alternative, rationally permissible decision procedure — one responsive to the genuine badness of bad outcomes in a way that risk-neutral EU theory is not.

On the mathematical case for high front-loaded carbon prices

When bad climate outcomes are rationally overweighted, high present carbon prices are mathematically justified as insurance premiums — regardless of expected value calculationsWhen an REU agent evaluates climate policy, the mathematics changes significantly from the DICE output. Under risk-neutral expected utility, a moderate gradually-rising carbon tax is optimal because it equates the marginal cost of abatement with the probability-weighted marginal benefit of avoided damage. Under REU with a sufficiently risk-averse risk function, the overweighting of catastrophic climate outcomes shifts the optimal carbon price substantially upward. Tipping cascades — collapse of major climate systems, runaway warming, continent-scale crop failure — receive weights that exceed their objective probabilities. The result is that high, front-loaded carbon prices are mathematically justified as rational insurance premiums: what a risk-averse but perfectly rational agent would pay today to reduce the probability of outcomes they weight more heavily than raw probability suggests. This is not the precautionary principle invoked as a vague injunction. It is a precise, tractable policy output derived from a defensible departure from risk-neutral expected utility.

On why REU survives the Harsanyi objection

REU does not violate Pareto for Prospects if individual preferences are themselves REU — the aggregation theorem is conditional on EU axioms that REU legitimately rejectsHarsanyi will object — and his modern followers in climate economics do object — that any departure from expected utility maximisation violates rational choice axioms and, via the Aggregation Theorem, Pareto for Prospects. This objection does not reach REU. Buchak's framework maintains internal consistency and avoids Dutch book vulnerabilities by modifying the probability-weighting function rather than violating the dominance axiom. More critically: if individuals themselves have REU preferences — empirically well-supported — then the Aggregation Theorem generates REU-weighted social welfare, not expected-total-utility social welfare. The objection from rational consistency is only as strong as the claim that EU theory is the uniquely rational decision procedure — a claim Buchak's work directly and decisively challenges.

On the Priority View and the distribution of climate benefits

The Priority View independently demands front-loaded carbon pricing: avoided climate harm matters more the worse-off the recipientParfit's Priority View adds a further, independent argument for aggressive front-loaded action. On the Priority View, it matters more — not just equally, but more — to benefit people the worse off they are. Applied to climate: the avoided harm of a degree of warming matters more when experienced by a subsistence farmer in the Sahel whose entire livelihood depends on rainfall reliability than when experienced by a wealthy temperate-zone nation with full adaptation capacity. When we give extra moral weight to benefits flowing to the worst-off, the optimal carbon price rises further still — because the people at the bottom of the global welfare distribution are precisely those who bear the concentrated climate harm that high carbon prices most directly prevent. Priority and REU reinforce each other: both independently demand carbon prices far above DICE model outputs.

On carbon border adjustments and revenue recycling

Policy architecture: high carbon price + carbon border adjustments to prevent leakage + priority-weighted climate dividend recycled to the poorestLet me be specific about the policy architecture. A high front-loaded carbon price must be accompanied by carbon border adjustments to prevent carbon leakage — the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, already partially implemented by the European Union, provides the model. Without border adjustments, high domestic carbon prices simply shift emissions abroad while destroying domestic industrial competitiveness. Revenue from carbon pricing — which at high levels generates substantial fiscal flows — must then be recycled as a priority-weighted climate dividend: directed toward the lowest-income households and nations, not returned to the general revenue of wealthy governments. This architecture — high price, border adjustment, priority-weighted dividend — is technically workable and partially deployed in existing policy frameworks.

On the DICE model's structural underweighting of catastrophic risk

IAM damage functions cannot model non-marginal civilisational harms — they produce systematic underestimates that compound across decades of policy inertiaThere is a further, purely technical problem with DICE-style models that reinforces the philosophical critique. The damage functions in standard IAMs are calibrated on historical economic data and assume climate damages are approximately quadratic in temperature — manageable costs rising smoothly with warming. This is almost certainly wrong for tail outcomes. Beyond approximately two degrees of global average warming, non-linear tipping cascades — ice-sheet collapse, permafrost methane release, ecosystem collapse — could generate damages that are not marginal losses but structural transformations of the economy's productive capacity. IAM damage functions cannot model these: they are calibrated on a world that still functions, not one that has crossed a civilisational threshold. Risk neutrality combined with structural underestimation of tail damages produces a systematic bias toward insufficient mitigation — a bias that compounds across decades into an enormous accumulated error.

On the coalition case for REU-based climate policy

Climate-vulnerable nations — AOSIS, LDC group, CVF — have long argued for precisely this framework; the UNFCCC loss-and-damage architecture supports it politicallyThe political economy objection — that our framework is impractical — deserves a direct answer. The climate-vulnerable nations of the world — the Alliance of Small Island States, the Least Developed Countries group, the Climate Vulnerable Forum — represent over a billion people and have, for decades, argued for a framework that treats catastrophic tail risks and unequal harm distribution as primary policy constraints. Their UNFCCC negotiating positions are, in philosophical terms, REU-based and egalitarian. They succeeded in establishing the loss-and-damage mechanism at COP27 and secured, at least nominally, the principle that adaptation finance must reflect the needs of the most vulnerable. The framework we describe is not politically alien. It is the position of the majority of the world's nations, systematically outweighed in climate negotiations by the economic power of the nations whose frameworks happen to produce moderate, delay-compatible policy outputs.

Mr President,

I want to defend the integrated assessment model approach — not as a perfect solution to the hardest problem in global governance, but as the most honest attempt we have to translate moral commitments about the welfare of all people, present and future, into concrete, implementable policy. The proposition's philosophical frameworks are elegant. But elegance divorced from economic reality does not build sea walls, fund solar panels, or feed the billion people who will be born in the next twenty years into a world that will be warmer than the one we inhabit.

On what integrated assessment models actually do

IAMs run Monte Carlo simulations across thousands of parameter combinations — they do not produce a single expected value pretending to certainty; the Stern Review is itself an IAMThe proposition characterises integrated assessment models as naively converting severe uncertainty into neat probability distributions. This misrepresents what careful IAM practitioners actually do. Modern IAMs — including the updated DICE model — run extensive Monte Carlo simulations across thousands of parameter combinations, explicitly modelling uncertainty ranges for climate sensitivity, economic damage functions, discount rates, and technological trajectories. They produce distributions of outcomes and policy recommendations robust across a wide range of assumptions. The charge that IAMs ignore tail risks is empirically false: the Stern Review, which the proposition largely endorses, is itself an IAM-based analysis. It reaches different conclusions not because it abandons expected utility but because it uses different parameter values — most critically a near-zero discount rate. The disagreement between Nordhaus and Stern is within the IAM framework, not between it and some philosophically superior alternative.

On the growth case for moderate mitigation

Front-loaded high carbon prices are regressive — they tax energy use that the global poor need for development, diverting resources from present poverty to future climate insuranceThe proposition's most serious practical failure is its treatment of development. A high front-loaded carbon price is, in economic terms, a substantial tax on energy use at precisely the moment when two billion people in South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and Southeast Asia are attempting to industrialise and electrify. The energy systems of developing nations are still predominantly fossil-fuel based, because fossil fuels are cheap, reliable, and already built out. A high carbon price makes electricity more expensive, industrial production more costly, and growth slower. The proposition cannot escape this arithmetic by citing carbon dividends: if the carbon price is high enough to be genuinely effective, the revenue required to fully compensate energy-cost increases for the bottom quintile of global income is not available from the pricing mechanism alone. Aggressive mitigation, in the real world of underdeveloped climate finance, is a policy that slows development for the world's poorest in exchange for climate protection they will be better placed to afford once they are richer.

On the optimal carbon tax trajectory

Updated DICE models recommend $50-150/tonne now rising to several hundred by mid-century — a substantial transformation of energy economics, not a modest or delay-compatible signalThe DICE model's policy recommendation is not a low permanent carbon tax. It is a rising trajectory: starting at a level that reflects present marginal damages and rising as the marginal damage of cumulative emissions increases. The current optimal DICE carbon price, in updated model versions, is in the range of $50 to $150 per tonne of CO₂, rising to several hundred dollars by mid-century. This is not a modest, delay-compatible number. It is a substantial price signal that, if implemented globally, would transform energy systems and investment patterns exactly as the proposition wants — without the front-loading that imposes development costs on nations still poor. The disagreement is not between action and inaction. It is between a rising-price trajectory that preserves growth while accelerating the transition and a front-loaded regime that sacrifices present development for future climate insurance.

On the impossibility of REU and maximin as practical frameworks

REU and maximin are policy-empty without probability inputs — they cannot generate determinate carbon prices without the IAM-style modelling they criticiseThe proposition's REU approach and Rawls's maximin face a decisive practical objection: they cannot generate determinate policy without making precisely the kind of probability-and-damage assignments they criticise the DICE model for making. REU requires calibrating a risk function — choosing the specific degree of bad-outcome overweighting. This choice requires some assessment of the probability distribution of climate outcomes. If we overweight catastrophic tails but assign those tails zero probability, the optimal carbon price is zero. If we assign them probability one, the optimal price is infinite. Buchak's framework is mathematically rigorous but policy-empty without parameter values — and those parameter values must come from exactly the kind of probabilistic climate modelling that REU theorists claim is illegitimate under severe uncertainty. The proposition has not escaped the expected-utility framework; it has added philosophical sophistication while remaining dependent on the same empirical inputs.

On the carbon border adjustment as partial vindication of the DICE approach

The EU's CBAM — the proposition's central policy instrument — was designed within the welfare-optimising framework the proposition claims is morally bankruptThe proposition cites the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism as evidence its policy architecture is practically deployable. I take this as partial vindication of the IAM approach. The CBAM was designed within a standard welfare-optimising policy framework: the EU Emissions Trading System it protects is a cap-and-trade mechanism whose price — currently around €60-80 per tonne — is set by market forces within a quantity constraint, not by REU calculations. The CBAM protects this price signal from carbon leakage by applying a carbon cost to imports from non-covered jurisdictions. This is precisely the kind of economically coherent, Pareto-improving policy architecture that integrated assessment modelling supports. The proposition cannot simultaneously cite CBAM as proof of its approach while claiming that welfare-optimising frameworks are morally bankrupt.

On the real cost of legally binding loss-and-damage at the implied scale

Annual L&D costs estimated at $400-600bn/year by 2030, rising to trillions — legally binding obligations at this scale are politically non-viable and would collapse the climate finance architecture entirelyThe proposition demands legally binding loss-and-damage mechanisms at a scale reflecting the full ambiguity burden imposed on the world's poorest. Let us be honest about what this means in practice. The total estimated annual loss-and-damage costs from climate change in developing nations are in the range of $400 billion to $600 billion per year by 2030, rising to trillions by mid-century, according to the most credible estimates. Legally binding obligations of this magnitude would be politically non-viable in every major democratic polity. The result of demanding legally binding transfers at this scale would not be justice. It would be the collapse of the climate finance architecture entirely, as wealthy nations withdrew from multilateral mechanisms rather than accept open-ended liability. The proposition's position is not the morally non-negotiable minimum it presents itself as — it is the politically unreachable ideal that makes the practically achievable impossible.

On what the DICE model actually defends

DICE recommends: substantial rising carbon price; significant international climate finance; clean-technology investment; adaptation targeted at the most vulnerable — not indifference to harm but a welfare-consistent path to a low-carbon worldLet me be clear about what the integrated assessment model approach actually recommends — as opposed to the caricature the proposition has constructed. It recommends a substantial and rising carbon price designed to reach several hundred dollars per tonne by mid-century; significant international climate finance, sized by welfare-maximising calculation; investment in clean technology at a scale that accelerates cost reduction curves and makes low-carbon development accessible to industrialising nations; and adaptation funding targeted at the most vulnerable communities. This is not indifference to climate harm. It is a welfare-consistent path to a low-carbon world that takes seriously the development needs of the present poor as well as the climate needs of the future poor. The proposition's framework — demanding front-loading, liability-based transfers, and rejection of growth-compatible transition — would produce slower development, less clean-technology investment, and a climate finance architecture in ruins.

Mr President,

I want to argue that both sides of this debate have made a fundamental error — one that my theory of justice as fairness is designed to avoid. Both the proposition and the Nordhaus line operate within a welfare-aggregative framework. My claim is that this is the wrong question entirely. The question of climate justice is not a question about optimal welfare distributions under uncertainty. It is a question about what principles free and equal persons would choose for the basic structure of their international arrangements if they did not know which nation, which generation, or which position in the global distribution they would occupy.

On the original position extended to climate

Behind a climate veil of ignorance — not knowing one's nation, generation, or vulnerability — rational contractors face genuine severe uncertainty and would choose principles protecting the worst climate positionImagine parties in an original position extended to the global climate problem. They do not know whether they will be born into a wealthy temperate nation with full adaptation capacity or a low-lying Pacific island with none. They do not know whether they belong to the generation that does the emitting or the generation that absorbs the warming. They do not know whether they are a subsistence farmer dependent on monsoon reliability or a technology worker in a climate-controlled city. Behind this climate veil of ignorance, they cannot assign precise probabilities to their position — the distribution of climate vulnerability is itself a function of the very policy choices whose principles they are selecting. They face genuine severe uncertainty about their place in the climate order. My claim is that rational contractors in this condition would not choose to maximise expected aggregate welfare. They would choose principles that make the worst climate position as tolerable as possible.

On why this generates immediate, radical decarbonisation

The worst climate outcome — civilisational destruction for low-lying island nations and monsoon-dependent agricultural communities — is existentially worse than the worst mitigation outcome of economic disruptionThe policy implication of maximin applied to climate is radical and immediate. The worst climate outcome — the destruction of low-lying island nations, the collapse of monsoon-dependent agricultural systems serving hundreds of millions, the cascade of tipping points into a four-degree world — is not merely a large negative number in a welfare calculation. It is, for the communities it destroys, an existential catastrophe: the end of their way of life, their culture, their national existence. No rational person behind the veil — not knowing whether they will be a Tuvaluan or a Texan — would accept policies that risk this for the marginal welfare gains of continued high-emission growth. Maximin demands we choose policies whose worst outcome is better than the worst outcome of alternatives. The worst outcome of continued substantial fossil fuel emissions through the 2030s is existential destruction. The worst outcome of immediate radical decarbonisation is economic disruption. These are not comparable.

On legally binding wealth transfers as a primary-goods obligation

A stable climate is a primary good — wealthy nations have unilaterally degraded it for the world's most vulnerable, generating a binding obligation of restoration that is not defeasible by welfare-aggregation argumentsRowe and Voorhoeve are right that legally binding wealth transfers are required — but I ground this claim differently. The wealthy nations of the world have, through their emissions, destroyed or severely degraded a primary good of the world's most vulnerable nations: a stable, predictable climate that is the condition for agricultural production, water security, and coastal habitation — without which no life plan can be pursued. This is not a welfare transfer to correct a distributional inequality. It is a primary-goods obligation: the restoration of a basic condition of social cooperation that wealthy nations have unilaterally degraded. The Difference Principle, applied to the global climate, requires that the allocation of the atmospheric commons be governed by principles that maximise the position of the least advantaged. In a world where the least advantaged suffer most from climate change and have emitted least to cause it, the Difference Principle demands massive, immediate, legally binding transfers.

On why both Nordhaus and the proposition use the wrong currency

Both welfare-optimising IAMs and pluralist egalitarianism operate in welfare space — the correct currency for climate justice is primary goods and basic institutional structureMy objection to both the Nordhaus line and Rowe and Voorhoeve is identical: they both operate in the wrong currency. Nordhaus asks how to maximise expected aggregate welfare subject to carbon constraints. Rowe and Voorhoeve ask how to distribute the burden of welfare uncertainty fairly. Both take welfare as the fundamental unit of account and argue about how to aggregate or distribute it. But welfare is not the right metric for principles of justice. The right question is what institutional arrangements — what rules governing the global atmospheric commons, what distribution of emissions rights, what obligations of technology transfer — free and equal persons would choose if they did not know their position in the global climate order. The answer is not a welfare distribution or an uncertainty-sharing arrangement. It is a set of primary-goods entitlements: to a stable climate, to the technological means of low-carbon development, to compensation for atmospheric commons that wealthy nations have already consumed beyond their fair share.

On the concrete policy architecture justice as fairness demands

Rawlsian climate policy: net-zero by 2050 to protect the most vulnerable; legally binding technology transfer; binding reparations for historic over-use of atmospheric commonsWhat does justice as fairness demand in concrete policy terms? First, a global cumulative emissions cap set at the level consistent with protecting the most vulnerable nations from existential climate risk — currently estimated at approximately 1.5 degrees of global warming, requiring net-zero emissions by approximately 2050. Second, legally binding technology transfer obligations from wealthy to developing nations, so that the transition to clean energy does not require a choice between development and climate safety. Third, a formal accounting of historic over-use of the atmospheric commons by wealthy nations, generating binding reparations obligations that fund both mitigation and adaptation in the nations that have emitted least and suffered most. These are not aspirational targets. They are the minimum requirements of what rational contractors behind a climate veil of ignorance would demand.

On why Nordhaus's growth argument fails the original position test

Behind a genuine veil of ignorance, no rational contractor would accept civilisational risk for the least climate-resilient in exchange for growth gains for the most climate-resilientNordhaus argues that aggressive front-loaded mitigation sacrifices development for the world's poorest. This argument fails the original position test in a specific and decisive way. Behind the veil, a rational contractor does not know whether they will be among those who benefit from growth that moderate mitigation preserves or among those who bear the existential risks that moderate mitigation accepts. Given genuine severe uncertainty about which position they will occupy, and given the radical asymmetry between worst outcomes — economic disruption versus civilisational destruction — no rational contractor would choose a policy that accepts civilisational risk to the most vulnerable in exchange for growth benefits to the least vulnerable. The growth argument is only compelling from behind a veil you know is not a genuine veil — if you already know you are in a wealthy, temperate, high-adaptation-capacity nation. That is precisely why the veil of ignorance is the right device for generating principles of climate justice.

On the agreement and disagreement with the proposition

I agree that legally binding transfers from wealthy to vulnerable nations are required and are not voluntary charity but binding obligation. I agree that the welfare-optimising framework of standard climate economics systematically encodes the interests of those who can hedge. Where I disagree is on the theoretical basis — and the disagreement matters for policy. Rowe and Voorhoeve ground their demands in welfare inequality calculations that wealthy nations can contest on welfare grounds.
