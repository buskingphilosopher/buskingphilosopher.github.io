Mr President,

The motion before us asserts something that, once seen clearly, should strike every honest person as obviously true and as disturbingly at odds with the dominant tradition in moral philosophy. We are asked whether factors beyond a person's control can legitimately affect the moral assessment we make of them. My answer is yes — not reluctantly, not as a concession to untidy common sense, but as a positive claim about the structure of moral life as it is actually lived. The opposition, I suspect, will invoke Kant. I want to explain why Kant, on this matter, is not only wrong but systematically and revealingly wrong.

On what moral luck is

Moral luck: luck that affects not just outcomes but the moral assessment of the agent — praise, blame, guilt, and regretLet me be careful about the phenomenon I am defending. Moral luck is not merely the familiar observation that outcomes are affected by chance. It is the stronger claim that the moral assessment of an agent — the degree of blame or praise they deserve, the appropriateness of guilt or admiration — is affected by factors outside their control. The drunk driver who kills a child is, in the relevant sense, more blameworthy than the equally drunk driver who reaches home without incident. The two drivers made the same decision, displayed the same culpable recklessness, exercised identical bad judgment. And yet something morally significant differentiates them — not merely legally, not merely in terms of practical consequences, but in the moral assessment that the agent themselves, if reflective, ought to endorse. The driver who killed someone has more reason for guilt, more reason to make reparations, more reason to feel that their life has been indelibly marked by what they did. If you deny this, I think you are not attending carefully to the phenomenology of moral life.

On agent-regret and its moral significance

Agent-regret: a distinctive reactive attitude that presupposes causal involvement, not merely bad outcomes in one's vicinityI introduced the concept of agent-regret to name a particular reactive attitude that bears on this debate. When the lorry driver who, through no fault of his own, runs over a child, experiences something qualitatively different from the bystander who merely witnesses the same event — this is not an irrational response to be corrected by philosophical therapy. The driver's regret is agent-regret: it involves a first-personal relationship to the harm done, a sense that one was causally involved in a way that demands a response that mere sympathy does not demand. The opposition will say this is merely causal, not moral. But I want to press the question: what is the difference between moral and causal involvement in a way that should matter to us practically? The driver owes something — to the family, to themselves — that the bystander does not owe. This special obligation flows from causal involvement under conditions of luck. It is a moral obligation, not merely a psychological pressure to be managed.

On the case of Gauguin

Gauguin: whether abandoning his family was justified depends on whether his artistic gamble succeeded — a retrospective moral judgmentConsider the case I have made famous. Gauguin abandons his family and travels to Tahiti to pursue his painting. He acts on a conviction — not a certainty — that he has within him something of artistic significance that can only be realised through this rupture. He cannot know this in advance. If he turns out to have the talent, if the work is great, then his decision was justified — and justified in a way that is not merely a matter of its good consequences but of what his life, retrospectively, was about. If he fails — if the painting is mediocre and the family is merely ruined — then his decision was not merely unfortunate but wrong, and he has a kind of personal story in which he was the agent of pointless destruction. The Kantian says the moral assessment must be fixed at the moment of decision, on the basis of available reasons and intentions. I say that this imposes a false cleanness on moral life. We judge Gauguin not only at the moment of departure but across the arc of the life his decision set in motion. Whether he was justified depends, in part, on outcomes he could not control.

On the fragility of goodness

Fragility of goodness: a good life, good character, and moral achievement can be undone by luck — this is tragic, not correctableMartha Nussbaum, in a related vein, speaks of the fragility of goodness — the extent to which a flourishing human life is vulnerable to fortune. I want to connect this to moral assessment directly. It is not just that good people can be made to suffer by luck. It is that what a person achieves morally — the kind of person they actually become, the relationships they sustain, the commitments they honour — is partly a function of circumstances they did not choose. A person of excellent character who is placed in circumstances that destroy their commitments, corrupt their relationships, and break their capacity for trust is not merely unlucky. They are a different moral story from the person of identical initial character who is lucky enough to inhabit circumstances that allow their good qualities to flower. The opposition's view requires us to strip away all this circumstance and evaluate the bare will in isolation. I submit that this bare will is an abstraction that corresponds to nothing in actual human moral life.

On why Kantianism is not neutral but systematically distorting

Kantian distortion: the demand to isolate the will from luck does not discover moral truth — it substitutes a moral fiction for realityThe Kantian tradition insists that moral assessment must be confined to what lies within the agent's control — the quality of their will, the purity of their intention. It does not treat this as a sad concession to epistemic limits; it treats it as a principled account of where morality lives. I want to diagnose this as a systematic distortion rather than a moral insight. The desire to confine moral assessment to the domain of pure will is driven by a desire for moral safety — a desire to have a domain in which one cannot be unlucky, in which one's moral standing is immune to the vicissitudes of fortune. This desire is understandable, psychologically. But it does not correspond to the structure of moral life. By constructing a moral theory around the immunisation of the will from luck, Kantians buy moral safety at the cost of moral truth. They describe an idealised agent — the noumenal self — and assess that fiction, while the actual person, embedded in circumstances they did not choose, with dispositions shaped by history and luck, goes morally unassessed. This is not moral philosophy; it is moral consolation.

On constitutive luck and the limits of responsibility

Constitutive luck: the character, temperament, and dispositions one has are themselves matters of luck — yet we hold people responsible for themThere is a deeper form of luck that the opposition's framework cannot handle: constitutive luck — luck in what kind of person one is. My temperament, my capacity for empathy, my tendency to courage or cowardice under pressure, my susceptibility to certain temptations — these were not chosen by me in any robust sense. They were shaped by genetics, upbringing, early experience, none of which I controlled. Yet we hold people responsible for acting from these constitutive features. We praise courage and blame cowardice even though the courageous person did not choose to have the disposition that manifested in courageous action. The Kantian says we must subtract all luck from responsibility. But if we follow this to its conclusion, we subtract everything — character, history, situation — and end with a noumenal will that is so empty it can bear no moral predicates at all. The proposition does not claim this is tidy. It claims it is true.

On what we should conclude

Conclusion: moral luck is ineliminable; the task is not to deny it but to develop a moral psychology adequate to itI do not claim that the acknowledgment of moral luck makes moral assessment easy or that it vindicates every pre-theoretical intuition about blame and praise. It does not. What I claim is that any moral theory that systematically denies moral luck — that insists on confining moral assessment to the domain of the uncontrolled will — purchases its elegance at the price of truth. The moral life is lived under conditions of luck. We are assessed, we assess ourselves, and we assess others in the light of outcomes, circumstances, and constitutive features that were not chosen. This does not make morality arbitrary; it makes it vulnerable — as vulnerable as any human practice that takes place in the world rather than in a philosophical fiction. The task is not to eliminate this vulnerability by definitional fiat, but to develop a moral psychology rich enough to be honest about it.

Mr President,

Williams has approached the question from the direction of moral phenomenology — the texture of moral experience as actually lived. I want to complement that approach by taking the opposition's strongest weapon — the philosophical concept of control — and showing that it generates, not a refutation of moral luck, but a paradox that neither side can escape, and that the attempt to escape it systematically misrepresents the structure of human agency.

On the four types of moral luck

Four types of moral luck: resultant, circumstantial, constitutive, and causal — all pervade ordinary moral assessmentLet me map the territory more precisely than Williams has done. Moral luck manifests in at least four distinct ways. First, there is resultant luck — luck in how things turn out, the drunk driver case. Second, there is circumstantial luck — luck in the situations one faces. The person who becomes a Nazi collaborator is not necessarily worse in character than someone who happened to live in a different country; they were placed in circumstances that elicited conduct whose moral gravity was not available to their counterpart. Third, there is constitutive luck — luck in who one is, one's inclinations, capacities, and temperament. Fourth, and most radically, there is causal luck — luck in how one is determined by antecedent causes, including one's own prior history. All four of these pervade ordinary moral assessment. We praise and blame people for outcomes, for the situations they faced, for the character they have, and implicitly for the causal history that made them who they are. The control principle — that one is only morally responsible for what one controls — demands that we excise all four. But once we excise all four, there is nothing left to assess.

On circumstantial luck and moral history

Circumstantial luck: whether one is ever tested by genuinely hard moral situations is itself a matter of luckThe circumstantial case is especially striking. Consider: most people living comfortable, middle-class lives in stable democracies will never face a situation that tests their courage, their integrity, or their capacity for genuine moral self-sacrifice in any deep way. The question of what they would do under extreme pressure — whether they would collaborate with an oppressive regime, whether they would risk their lives for a stranger, whether they would maintain their principles when the cost was high — simply never arises. But that these questions do not arise is not itself a moral achievement; it is a matter of good fortune. Meanwhile, those who are placed in extreme situations — soldiers, those living under occupation, victims of sudden disasters — are morally tested in ways that reveal character that comfortable lives never illuminate. We assess the people who faced these tests more richly than we can assess those who did not, because the luck of circumstance gave some agents more moral history to be assessed on. This is not a distortion of moral assessment; it is what moral assessment over a whole life looks like.

On the paradox of moral luck

The paradox: we simultaneously accept that moral assessment requires control and that luck pervades what we actually assess — these cannot both be trueHere is the paradox I want to place at the centre of this debate. We are committed to the control principle — the view that a person is only morally assessable for what they control. And we are simultaneously committed to a wide range of moral assessments that depend on luck. These two commitments are in genuine tension, and I do not think the tension can be resolved by analytical ingenuity. Those who deny moral luck must do one of two things: either they must revise our ordinary practices of praise and blame so radically that only a vanishingly small domain of conduct remains morally assessable, or they must argue that the ordinary practices are systematically wrong — that we should stop blaming the drunk driver who killed a child any more than the one who did not, that we should assess Nazi collaborators no differently from those who never faced occupation, that we should treat Gauguin's moral story as closed at the moment of departure. Both options represent significant departures from what we actually take moral assessment to involve. The proposition does not claim the paradox is resolved; it claims the resolution cannot come from denying moral luck.

On the objective view and the subjective perspective

Objective view: expanding the causal/deterministic perspective progressively erodes the basis for moral assessment of any kindThere is a broader philosophical pressure at work in the denial of moral luck that deserves naming. When we take an objective, scientific view of human agents — seeing them as physical systems embedded in causal histories they did not choose, governed by laws they did not legislate — the space available for genuine moral responsibility contracts. If I am the product of my genes, my upbringing, my neurology, my social circumstances, in what sense did I "choose" anything in a way that grounds moral assessment? The determinist argument is not new. What I want to observe is that the denial of moral luck is not a stable halfway position between full moral responsibility and deterministic elimination of responsibility. It is a step along a path that, followed consistently, leads to the elimination of moral assessment altogether. If we deny moral luck because luck undermines control, we must also deny that any assessment based on constitutive or causal factors is legitimate — and that is nearly all assessment.

On reactive attitudes and the practice of holding responsible

Reactive attitudes: practices of blame and praise are constitutive of our moral community; they are not theories waiting to be correctedP. F. Strawson argued that reactive attitudes — resentment, indignation, gratitude, love — are constitutive of the practice of holding people responsible, and that this practice is not subordinate to some metaphysical theory of free will that might vindicate or undermine it. I want to extend this to moral luck. The reactive attitudes that track moral luck — the special guilt of the agent who, by bad luck, caused harm; the admiration for those who, by good fortune, had their character revealed in circumstances of genuine trial — are not theoretical errors waiting to be corrected by the control principle. They are part of the structure of moral community. A moral philosophy that declares these attitudes systematically mistaken has set itself against the practice it was supposed to illuminate. It has chosen a philosophical principle over the moral life it was meant to describe.

On causal luck and the limits of desert

Causal luck: if determinism is true, or even possible, then no one ever deserves anything in a way immune to luckThe deepest form of moral luck — causal luck — puts maximum pressure on the control principle. If an agent's decision is causally determined by prior states of the world that they did not control — their neural states, their history, the physics of the moment — then the decision itself was not controlled in any ultimate sense. The agent could not have decided otherwise, given the prior conditions. The control principle, applied here, yields the conclusion that no agent is ever morally responsible for anything — a thoroughgoing moral scepticism. The opposition is typically unwilling to accept this conclusion, but they owe us an account of where to draw the line. What counts as "sufficient" control for moral assessment? Any line they draw will be arbitrary unless it can be shown that there is a relevant difference between the forms of luck they permit into moral assessment and the forms they exclude. I do not believe such a difference can be shown without inviting moral luck back in.

On what the paradox reveals about moral philosophy

The paradox is genuine: moral philosophy must acknowledge both horns rather than dissolving the tension by fiatI began by saying I wanted to frame a paradox rather than resolve it. Let me be honest about what this means. I do not think the proposition can offer a neat, principled account of exactly how luck modulates moral assessment, or how to weigh luck-influenced outcomes against pure intentions in a single formula. What the proposition can offer — and what I think is more philosophically honest — is the claim that moral luck is real, that the paradox it generates is genuine, and that anyone who claims to have dissolved the paradox by denying one horn has not engaged with the problem seriously. Our moral lives are lived under conditions of luck. Our assessments track luck. The control principle, taken seriously, would abolish moral assessment altogether. Neither conclusion is acceptable. That is the condition of moral philosophy with respect to luck — suspended between two unacceptable options, with the actual practice of moral life somewhere in between.

Mr President,

I want to approach this debate by separating two questions that the proposition has conflated: the question of whether luck affects our lives in ways that are morally significant for purposes of distributive justice, and the question of whether luck affects the degree of moral desert — the blame and praise — we should receive. The first question has an affirmative answer; the second, I shall argue, does not. Luck is deeply relevant to what we owe each other. It is not relevant to what we deserve from each other in the currency of moral blame.

On the distinction between luck-sensitive justice and luck-sensitive desert

Justice vs desert: luck egalitarianism concerns the distribution of resources, not the distribution of moral blame — these are separate questionsThe luck egalitarian tradition — associated with Dworkin, Cohen, and my own work — holds that justice requires neutralising the effects of brute luck on people's life prospects. Someone who is born with a disability, into poverty, or into a disadvantaged social class, suffers from unchosen bad luck; justice requires some compensatory redistribution. This is a claim about distributive justice — about what people are owed in terms of resources and opportunities. It is emphatically not a claim about moral desert. It does not say that the person who suffers bad luck is less blameworthy for their choices, or that the person who enjoys good luck deserves less praise for their achievements. The currency of luck egalitarianism is material welfare; the currency of moral assessment is something else — the quality of one's responsiveness to reasons, the exercise of whatever capacities one has for moral attention and deliberation. Williams and Nagel have moved, without argument, from the first currency to the second.

On the distinction between brute luck and option luck

Brute vs option luck: only luck that bypasses choice is relevant to justice; moral agency operates in the domain of option luckDworkin's distinction between brute luck — luck that befalls one regardless of one's choices — and option luck — luck that arises from deliberate gambles one undertakes — is fundamental here. Brute luck is what justice must compensate; option luck is what falls within the domain of personal responsibility. When we assess moral agents, we are assessing them in the domain of option luck — in the domain of choices made, risks taken, decisions endorsed. The drunk driver chose to drive while intoxicated. That was an exercise of whatever agency they possessed, and it is appropriately assessed as such. Whether they happened to hit someone or not is brute luck that affects their causal history but not the moral quality of the choice that put them on the road. Conflating the assessment of the choice with the assessment of its outcomes — as the proposition does — confuses the domain of justice with the domain of desert.

On the Kantian control principle and its proper scope

Control principle: moral blame should track what agents controlled — this is not a desire for moral safety but a requirement of fairnessWilliams dismisses the Kantian control principle as a desire for moral safety — a philosophical construction driven by the wish to immunise the self from fortune. I want to offer a more principled defence. The control principle says that agents should be praised or blamed only for what lay within their control because this is a requirement of fairness. To blame someone for an outcome they could not have prevented, or to praise someone for a benefit they had no part in producing, is to treat them as a means to some social end — the end of providing incentives, or of expressing collective emotion — rather than as an end in themselves. The control principle is not a fiction; it is a demand that we respect persons as rational agents whose moral standing depends on what they do with their agency, not on what fortune does to them. This is not Kantian consolation; it is Kantian respect.

On resultant luck and the criminal law

Resultant luck in law: outcome-luck in punishment reflects practical necessity, not moral truth — we should not confuse the twoThe proposition is on strongest ground with resultant luck — the drunk driver case. I want to concede a limited point here while resisting the broader inference. It is true that our legal and social practices track outcomes in ways that reflect moral luck. The driver who killed a child is punished more severely than the one who did not. But I want to argue that this outcome-sensitivity reflects practical and expressive considerations — the need for a law that can be applied to observable events, the expressive function of punishment in acknowledging the harm done — rather than a deep truth about desert. If we were designing a perfectly just system of moral assessment, unencumbered by practical constraints, we would assess the two drivers equally — both exercised equally culpable recklessness. That our practices depart from this ideal tells us something about the limitations of our practices, not about the metaphysics of desert.

On constitutive luck and the threshold of agency

Constitutive luck and agency: what matters is whether one has sufficient rational agency for responsibility — not whether that agency was itself luckyThe constitutive luck argument — that one's character is unchosen, so one cannot be blamed for acting from it — proves too much if it is accepted. It implies that no one is ever responsible for anything, since all action flows from constitutive features one did not choose. But moral responsibility does not require that one chose one's character from some prior, characterless standpoint. It requires that one has the relevant capacities — the capacity to respond to reasons, to recognise moral demands, to deliberate and choose — and acts from those capacities. The fact that one was lucky or unlucky in acquiring those capacities is not what is assessed; what is assessed is how one exercises the capacities one has. A person who has the full range of rational and moral capacities, and exercises them badly, is appropriately blamed, regardless of the lucky or unlucky history that produced those capacities.

On the Gauguin case and retrospective justification

Gauguin rebuttal: moral justification cannot be retrospective; this confuses moral assessment with narrative assessment of a lifeWilliams's Gauguin case asks us to accept that the moral justification of a decision can depend on subsequent outcomes outside the agent's control. I want to resist this sharply. What changes when Gauguin's paintings are great is not the moral assessment of his decision — whether it was right to abandon his family — but our narrative assessment of his life as a whole, our sense of whether his story is one of tragedy or redemption, of waste or achievement. These are genuine and important assessments. But they are not moral assessments in the sense that determines blame and desert. The husband and father Gauguin abandoned was wronged; that wrong is not retroactively healed by the quality of subsequent canvases, however magnificent. Conflating the narrative arc of a life with the moral assessment of decisions made within it is an error — an aestheticisation of ethics that substitutes the structure of the well-told story for the structure of moral reasoning.

On luck egalitarianism as the proper home of luck in ethics

Proper home of luck: luck belongs in distributive justice, not in desert; this preserves both fairness and responsibilityLet me state the positive view. Luck is enormously morally significant — but its significance is in the domain of what we owe each other in terms of resources, opportunities, and fair starting positions. A society that ignores the luck of birth, that allows the brute luck of circumstance to determine life prospects without any redistributive response, is unjust. This is where luck egalitarianism lives. But once we have compensated for brute luck at the level of distribution, the remaining domain of choice and agency is one in which desert, responsibility, and the control principle apply. To import luck into the desert domain — to say that agents deserve less blame because their character was constitutively lucky, or that outcomes modulate desert regardless of choices — is to double-count luck. It allows luck to operate both in the domain of justice, where it belongs, and in the domain of desert, where it corrupts the very concept of responsibility.

3.The proposition moves without argument from luck's relevance to justice to its relevance to desert — these are separate questions with different answers.

1.Brute luck (unchosen misfortune) must be compensated by justice; option luck (luck arising from deliberate choices) falls within personal responsibility.
2.Moral assessment targets choices made and risks taken — the domain of option luck — not the unchosen results of unchosen circumstances.
3.Conflating the moral quality of a choice with the unchosen outcome of that choice conflates option luck with brute luck — the wrong domain for each.
∴The brute/option luck distinction shows where responsibility is correctly located — in the domain of deliberate choice; the proposition errs in allowing brute luck to modulate assessments that should track only the quality of choice.
III — Control principle as fairness, not consolation: blaming beyond control treats persons as means
1.Blaming someone for outcomes they could not have prevented treats them as an instrument — for expressing collective emotion or providing incentives — rather than as a rational agent.
2.Treating persons as ends requires that moral assessment track what they did with their agency, not what fortune did to them.
3.The control principle is therefore not a desire for moral safety but a requirement of respect for persons as rational agents.
∴The control principle is a demand of Kantian respect, not consolation — it protects agents from being used as vehicles for social expression; accepting moral luck in the desert domain violates persons' standing as rational agents.
IV — Legal outcome-sensitivity reflects practical constraints, not moral truth about desert
1.Legal practices that track outcomes (punishing the driver who killed more severely) reflect the practical need to respond to observable harms and the expressive function of law.
2.These practical considerations are distinct from the metaphysics of desert — in a perfect system, both drivers would be assessed equally, as their choices were equally culpable.
3.The proposition mistakes a departure from moral ideal — driven by practical necessity — for a moral ideal itself.
∴Outcome-sensitivity in law and social practice reflects practical, not moral, truth — our practices depart from the ideal of desert-tracking punishment, and we should acknowledge this rather than canonise the departure.
V — Constitutive luck and agency: responsibility requires sufficient rational capacity, not unchosen capacity
1.Moral responsibility requires that an agent have the relevant rational and moral capacities — to respond to reasons, deliberate, and choose.
2.What is assessed is how one exercises these capacities — not whether one was lucky or unlucky in acquiring them.
3.The constitutive luck argument proves too much: it implies no one is ever responsible, since all capacities have a causal history.

VI — Gauguin rebuttal: narrative assessment of a life is not moral assessment of a decision
1.Whether Gauguin's artistic success makes his life's story one of achievement or waste is a narrative, not a moral, assessment.
2.The moral wrong to his family — the abandonment — is not retroactively healed by the greatness of subsequent paintings.
3.Williams conflates the aesthetics of a life's narrative arc with the moral assessment of decisions made within that life.
∴The Gauguin case demonstrates aestheticisation of ethics, not moral luck — substituting the structure of a well-told story for moral reasoning; narrative justification and moral justification are not the same.
VII — Luck's proper domain is justice, not desert; double-counting luck corrupts both
1.Justice requires compensating for brute luck in the distribution of resources and opportunities — this is luck egalitarianism's correct insight.
2.Once brute luck is compensated at the level of distribution, the remaining domain of choice and agency is governed by desert and the control principle.
3.Importing luck into the desert domain — after it has already operated in the justice domain — double-counts luck and corrupts the concept of responsibility.
∴The proper moral home of luck is distributive justice, not desert — luck egalitarianism is the right answer to the luck question; moral luck in the blame domain is not a further insight but a confusion of domains.

Mr President,

I want to challenge the proposition from a different direction. Williams and Nagel have assumed that the Kantian control principle — the view that moral assessment requires control — is the only alternative to moral luck, and that because the control principle is too demanding, we must accept luck. I want to argue that there is a third position: that moral responsibility is grounded not in the impossible demand for luck-free agency, but in the more tractable and morally plausible concept of sanity — the capacity to act in accordance with reasons. This position neither accepts moral luck in the way the proposition recommends, nor retreats to an unachievable Kantian ideal.

On the reason-responsiveness account of responsibility

Reason-responsiveness: moral responsibility requires the ability to act in accordance with reasons — not freedom from causal historyMy account of moral responsibility is built around reason-responsiveness: an agent is morally responsible for an action to the degree that they were able to recognise and respond to the moral reasons that bore on that action. This is not a demand that they be free from all causal determination — it is a demand that the causal process running through them include the appropriate sensitivity to moral reasons. A person who acts badly but has the full capacity for moral reasoning and simply fails to exercise it appropriately is responsible. A person who acts badly because a brain tumour has destroyed their capacity for reason-responsiveness is not. The difference is not between the lucky and the unlucky; it is between the sane and the impaired. This draws the line for responsibility in the right place — at the boundary of rational agency — without importing luck into the assessment of those who fall on the responsibility side of the line.

On the asymmetry between moral heroes and moral monsters

Asymmetry of assessment: we do not fully praise moral heroes who could not have done otherwise, but we do blame moral monsters who could not have done otherwiseHere is an asymmetry that my account explains and the proposition's cannot. Consider the moral saint — someone so deeply good, so thoroughly formed by virtue, that they could not bring themselves to act wrongly. We admire such a person, but we do not give them the fullest moral credit, because their goodness did not require them to overcome temptation, to choose against inclination, to exercise the will in the face of competing desires. Compare the moral monster — someone whose character is so thoroughly corrupt that they could not have acted well. We blame them fully. The asymmetry here is revealing: we grade praise by the difficulty of the achievement, but we do not grade blame by the difficulty of avoiding wrongdoing in the same way. The proposition has no principled account of this asymmetry. My reason-responsiveness account explains it: what matters for blame is whether the agent had the capacity to respond to moral reasons, not whether good action would have been easy or hard.

On excusing conditions as capacity-failures, not luck-failures

Excusing conditions: insanity, compulsion, and coercion excuse because they impair reason-responsiveness — not because they introduce luckWilliams and Nagel observe that we excuse agents who act under compulsion, insanity, or extreme coercion — and they interpret this as recognition that luck modulates moral assessment. I want to offer a different interpretation. We excuse the insane person not because their action was the result of bad luck — a brain they did not choose — but because insanity destroys the very capacity for reason-responsiveness that is the basis of moral assessment. We excuse the coerced person not because coercion introduces luck into their causal history, but because coercion bypasses the normal operation of their rational agency. In both cases, the excuse works by showing that the normal conditions for moral responsibility — reason-responsiveness, rational agency — were absent. This is not an acknowledgment that luck matters; it is an acknowledgment that agency matters, and that some conditions prevent the relevant kind of agency from operating.

On constitutive luck and the sanity requirement

Sanity vs luck: what matters is not whether one's character was luckily formed but whether it meets the threshold of rational sanityNagel's constitutive luck argument says: my character was formed by luck, so I cannot be blamed for acting from it. I want to press the distinction between the threshold of rational sanity and what lies below it. Below the threshold — psychopathy that destroys moral cognition, developmental conditions that prevent normal reason-responsiveness — we do not hold people responsible, and this is not because of luck but because the relevant capacities are absent. Above the threshold — in the normal range of human agents, however differently their characters were formed — we do hold people responsible, and we are right to do so. What matters is not the history of how one's character came to be, but whether one's character now includes the relevant capacities for moral agency. The proposition conflates the genetic story of a character's formation with the synchronic capacity of that character to respond to reasons.

On circumstantial luck and the standard of reasonable response

Circumstantial luck and moral standards: we assess how agents responded to circumstances, not whether they were lucky to face themNagel's circumstantial luck case — the Nazi collaborator who faced bad moral luck by being born in Germany — does reveal something important, but not what Nagel claims. What we assess in the collaborator is how they responded to the circumstances they faced — whether they exercised their reason-responsive capacities to resist, to find alternatives, to preserve their moral integrity under pressure. Those who did this deserve praise; those who did not deserve blame. This assessment does not depend on whether we think they were lucky or unlucky to face those circumstances — that is a separate question of distributive justice, of the unfairness of the circumstances they were placed in. The moral assessment of their response to circumstances is, and should be, independent of the assessment of whether the circumstances were fair. Conflating these is the proposition's central error.

On the limits of the luck-elimination project

Limits of luck-elimination: perfect luck-neutrality is impossible, but imperfect approximation through reason-responsiveness is achievableI want to make a methodological concession that does not concede the substantive point. It is true that no account of moral responsibility can fully eliminate the influence of luck — the boundaries of reason-responsiveness are themselves affected by luck, and there will be hard cases at the margin. But the aim of moral philosophy is not to achieve a perfectly luck-free account; it is to provide an account that tracks the morally relevant features of agency as well as possible. The reason-responsiveness account does better than the proposition's moral luck view at tracking what we actually care about when we hold people responsible — namely, whether their actions reflected the operation of their rational and moral capacities. Accepting moral luck as a positive feature of the assessment rather than as a regrettable approximation gives up on the project of getting it right, and settles for a description of our existing imperfect practices.

On Zimmerman and the problem of resultant luck

Zimmerman: the moral assessment of attempts is more just than the assessment of outcomes — luck should not differentiate equally reckless choicesMichael Zimmerman has argued, in a vein I endorse, that the most morally adequate system of assessment would judge on the basis of what agents tried to do — their attempts — rather than on the basis of what they succeeded in doing. The drunk driver who killed a child and the drunk driver who did not made the same attempt — they both attempted to drive while intoxicated, with the same disregard for the risks they were imposing on others. The morally relevant act is the attempt; the outcome is a matter of resultant luck that should not, on any fair view, differentiate them morally. Our legal system tracks outcomes partly for practical reasons; a morally ideal system would track attempts, as the law of attempts recognises in some areas. The proposition cites our outcome-tracking practices as evidence that moral luck is real. I say they are evidence that our practices are imperfect, and that the norm we should be approximating is attempt-based assessment, not luck-sensitive desert.

I want to stand Nagel's paradox on its head. He presents it as a problem that threatens the denial of moral luck — showing that the control principle, pushed too far, eliminates responsibility. I want to argue that it is equally, and more devastatingly, a problem that threatens the acceptance of moral luck — showing that the proposition's move of acknowledging luck does not save responsibility but merely relocates the regress. Moral luck, as the proposition defends it, does not resolve the problem of moral responsibility; it surrenders it.

On the basic argument

The basic argument: to be truly responsible, you must be responsible for who you are — but the regress of self-determination cannot be stoppedMy basic argument is this. For an agent to be truly morally responsible for an action, they must be responsible for the state of themselves that is the proximate cause of the action — their character, their deliberative dispositions, their values. But to be responsible for those, they must have been responsible for the actions or decisions that shaped them. And to be responsible for those, they must have been responsible for the prior state of themselves that shaped those. The regress does not stop. At some point in an agent's history, there is a state of themselves for which they were not responsible — because they did not choose it, because it preceded any choice they could have made. And if that unchosen prior state is part of the causal chain leading to any action, the action is not one for which they are ultimately responsible. This is the self-determination regress. The proposition does not resolve it; it merely gives up on the demand for ultimate responsibility, calls the residue "moral luck," and declares it acceptable. I am not persuaded.

On why accepting moral luck relocates, not resolves, the problem

Relocation not resolution: moral luck pushes the problem back one step — we still need to explain why luck-infected agency grounds responsibilityWilliams says: accept that moral agents are shaped by luck, and assess them on the basis of the characters they have, luck-shaped as those characters may be. But this move does not resolve the problem of why these luck-shaped characters generate genuine moral responsibility — it simply accepts that they do, without explaining why. The drunk driver who killed a child is more blameworthy, on Williams's view, because of the outcome of his recklessness. But the recklessness itself was shaped by constitutive luck — temperament, upbringing, neural architecture. Williams accepts this. So the moral assessment is of a luck-shaped choice that produced a lucky or unlucky outcome. At every layer, luck pervades the assessment. What, precisely, is left that deserves the name "moral" responsibility? The proposition has not said; it has simply said that our practices track this luck-pervaded agency, and called that moral responsibility. But that is a description of what we do, not a justification for doing it.

On determinism and the impossibility of ultimate responsibility

Determinism: if determinism is true, no one is ever ultimately responsible for anything — accepting moral luck does not change thisNagel gestures at the determinist challenge — that causal luck, if taken seriously, eliminates the basis for all moral assessment. I want to press this point more firmly. If determinism is true, then every action is the product of prior causal states that the agent did not control. The agent's character, their deliberations, their choices — all are causally necessitated by prior conditions extending back before their birth. In this case, there is no action for which they are genuinely, ultimately responsible in the sense that the moral luck framework requires. The proposition responds: accept that responsibility is luck-pervaded, not luck-free, and assess accordingly. But this response is only satisfying if we can explain what the remaining "responsibility" consists in — what it is that the agent did that grounds the moral assessment, beyond being a causally located node in a network of luck-shaped processes. I do not think this explanation has been given, and I am not sure it can be.

On compatibilism and its limits

Compatibilism: determinism-compatible responsibility is possible, but it does not vindicate the full weight of moral praise and blame our practices assumeThe standard response to the determinist challenge is compatibilism: even if determinism is true, responsibility is compatible with it, because responsibility requires only the right kind of causal process running through the agent — reason-responsiveness, in Wolf's formulation, or some similar condition. I do not deny that this is a coherent position. What I deny is that it vindicates the full weight of moral assessment — the retributive blame, the deep guilt, the sense that an agent genuinely deserved their fate — that our practices assume. Compatibilist responsibility is a thinner concept than what the proposition claims to be defending. Williams argues that the drunk driver who killed a child deserves more guilt than the one who did not. But on any compatibilist account, neither driver deserves anything in the deep metaphysical sense — what they deserve is a function of what it is useful to do to them given social purposes. Moral luck, endorsed by the proposition, does not rescue desert; it abandons it.

On the phenomenology of deep guilt and its presuppositions

Deep guilt presupposes ultimate responsibility: the phenomenology Williams appeals to has metaphysical commitments that his own view cannot sustainWilliams appeals to the phenomenology of agent-regret and guilt as evidence that moral luck is real. But the phenomenology he invokes — the sense that one genuinely deserves opprobrium, that one truly could have done otherwise, that one's moral standing has been permanently altered by one's choices — is phenomenology that presupposes exactly the kind of ultimate responsibility that the self-determination regress shows to be unavailable. When the driver who killed a child feels deep, genuine guilt — not merely social obligation but the sense that they are diminished as a moral agent — they are implicitly feeling as though they had ultimate control over the chain of events. The phenomenology presupposes libertarian freedom. But neither Williams nor Nagel is a libertarian. They cannot both appeal to the phenomenology of ultimate guilt and deny the metaphysical picture that phenomenology presupposes.

On what follows: moral practices without metaphysical foundations

Practices without foundations: our moral responsibility practices may lack ultimate metaphysical grounding — this is uncomfortable but honestI want to be clear about the implications of the basic argument. I am not arguing that we should abolish the practices of praise and blame, guilt and admiration, resentment and gratitude. These practices are too deeply embedded in human social life to be abolished by philosophical argument — as P. F. Strawson, in a different vein from my own, argued persuasively. What I am arguing is that these practices lack ultimate metaphysical justification. We engage in them because we cannot do otherwise, because they are constitutive of our form of life, because the alternative — the objective stance taken universally — would be a form of human relationship that is both impossible to sustain and impoverishing if sustained. But to say that practices are humanly necessary is not to say they are metaphysically grounded. The proposition has claimed that moral luck is a real feature of the moral landscape. I am arguing that what is real is a set of practices that presuppose moral responsibility but that cannot, on reflection, be given the metaphysical grounding that would make those presuppositions true.

On what the debate should conclude

Conclusion: the honest position is moral scepticism about ultimate desert — not the acceptance of moral luck as a positive moral factThe debate between the proposition and the opposition, as I have reconstructed it, is a debate between two unstable positions. The proposition accepts moral luck and calls it a feature; I have argued that this acceptance offers no account of what ground moral assessment has once luck pervades it. The opposition's standard Kantian position demands luck-free control; Nagel has argued that this leads to elimination of all assessment. My own view is that the honest position is a modest moral scepticism about ultimate desert: our practices of blame and praise are deeply entrenched, humanly necessary, but metaphysically without foundation. Accepting moral luck is not a solution to this predicament — it is a form of comfort that mistakes a description of our practices for a justification of them. The motion should be defeated, not because the control principle is easily defended, but because accepting moral luck offers no real improvement on the problem it claims to solve.
