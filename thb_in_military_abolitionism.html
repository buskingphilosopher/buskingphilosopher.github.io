Motion before the House
"This House Would Abolish the Standing Military"
Proposition Speaking for the motion
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I want to begin by clearing away a misunderstanding that will otherwise sabotage this entire debate. Military abolitionism is not pacifism. I am not standing here to tell you that violence is always wrong, that force is never justified, or that we should love our enemies into submission. I am making a harder and more interesting argument than that.

I am arguing that even if you accept that war can sometimes be just — even if you accept that force is sometimes morally necessary — the institution of the standing military is not the right way to provide it. The army, the navy, the air force: these are not morally neutral means. They are an institution with a logic of their own, and that logic is, on careful examination, inimical to the values it purports to serve.

On what militaries actually do

The argument from military expansionismThe overwhelming majority of what military forces do, historically and in the present, is not defence. It is not the repelling of invaders. It is the projection of power — the maintenance of empire, the enforcement of economic interests, the intimidation of weaker states, the propping up of client regimes, the conduct of wars of choice dressed in the language of necessity. The United States has been at war for all but a handful of years since 1945. Britain has deployed military force in every decade since the Second World War. These are not defensive operations. They are the expression of standing military capacity seeking employment.

The argument from military expansionismAn institution that can project force will project force. Militaries are expensive. They require justification. They develop constituencies — defence contractors, military academies, officer classes, think tanks funded by arms manufacturers — whose institutional interests are served by the existence of threats. This is what Eisenhower meant when he warned of the military-industrial complex: not a conspiracy, but an institutional argument. Structures of incentive produce predictable behaviours, and a standing military produces predictable pressure toward its own use.

On the moral corruption of military institutions

The argument from moral corruptionThe standing military requires, for its functioning, that human beings be trained to kill on command. Not to deliberate about whether killing is justified in a particular case. To kill on command. The virtue the military most requires of its members is not courage, and it is not justice. It is obedience. This is not an incidental feature of military organisation — it is a structural necessity. An army in which every soldier deliberates independently about whether each order is morally justified is not an army. It is a philosophy seminar.

The argument from moral corruptionThe result is an institution systematically organised to produce people who will commit acts that, outside the military context, we would recognise as murder, on the authority of a chain of command whose decisions they are not equipped to evaluate. We then express surprise when atrocities occur. Abu Ghraib. My Lai. The Haditha killings. These are not aberrations. They are the predictable outputs of an institution that trains people to subordinate moral judgement to institutional authority.

On the availability heuristic and war

The argument from the availability heuristicHuman beings — and the states they constitute — reach for the tools that are available to them. When a problem presents itself as a security threat, states reach for military responses not because military responses are necessarily the most effective, but because military capacity is what they have. The existence of a standing military does not merely give states the capacity to use force. It shapes how states conceptualise their problems. When you have a hammer, problems start to look like nails. When you have a carrier strike group, problems start to look like targets. Abolition would force states to develop and invest in the full range of responses — diplomatic, economic, institutional, legal — rather than defaulting to the military instrument that is permanently available and permanently lobbying for its own use.

On alternatives

The argument from alternative security arrangementsThe question "if we abolish standing militaries, how do we defend ourselves?" assumes that the only alternative to a permanent standing army is defencelessness. This assumption is false. Switzerland has long maintained a militia-based defence, distributing military capacity across the civilian population rather than concentrating it in a professional standing force. Costa Rica abolished its military in 1948 and has not been invaded. Iceland has no standing army. These cases demonstrate that the standing military is not the only conceivable arrangement for national security. The argument for abolition is not for defencelessness — it is for defence organised differently: through civilian-based defence, international institutions, and genuine collective security arrangements.

The argument from the security dilemmaThe security dilemma — that one state's defensive armament is another state's offensive threat — is not a law of nature. It is a consequence of organising security through competitive military accumulation. A world genuinely committed to collective security through international institutions, rather than maintaining standing forces as a hedge against those institutions' failure, would be a less dangerous world. The standing military is not the solution to insecurity. It is, in significant part, its cause.

On just war theory's self-defeat

The argument from just war theory's self-defeatJust war theory sets demanding conditions for the legitimate use of force — just cause, right intention, last resort, proportionality, competent authority. But applied to an institution rather than an individual act, just war theory reaches a troubling conclusion. An institution whose existence reliably produces unjust wars — wars of aggression, wars of economic interest, wars in which combatants are trained to bypass the moral reasoning just war requires — cannot be justified by the just war tradition, even if individual wars occasionally meet the criteria. The standing military is structured to produce unjust outcomes: the incentive structures, the institutional pressures, the trained incapacity for moral deliberation, the availability bias. A just war theorist who takes seriously both the conditions for just war and this institutional analysis should, on reflection, be a military abolitionist.

I am not asking you to be a pacifist. I am asking you to take the just war tradition seriously enough to follow it where it leads.

Thank you.

Opposition Speaking against the motion
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr President,

My opponent has made a sophisticated argument. He has been careful to distinguish his position from pacifism. He has invoked institutional analysis, cognitive psychology, and just war theory. He deserves a careful response. But careful does not mean deferential. On examination, the case for military abolitionism rests on empirical claims that are questionable, logical moves that do not hold, and a vision of international order that is a fantasy — and a dangerous one.

On what militaries actually do — a selective reading

The argument from military expansionismWe are told that the overwhelming majority of what militaries do is project power rather than provide defence. This is presented as an empirical observation. It is, in fact, a selective reading of history. The standing military of Finland deterred Soviet aggression for decades. The British military participated in the liberation of occupied Europe. The militaries of South Korea, Taiwan, and the Baltic states exist in the presence of neighbours who have demonstrated, repeatedly, that the appetite for territorial expansion is real and active. The Polish military is not a colonial instrument seeking employment. It is a nation's answer to a geography that has made it the invasion corridor of Europe for three centuries. The institutional argument — that militaries create pressure toward aggressive use — may describe some militaries in some contexts. The policy conclusion of abolition is not proportionate to that diagnosis even where the diagnosis is correct.

On moral corruption — institution vs. alternative

The argument from moral corruptionThe proposition argues that military institutions are morally corrupting because they require obedience rather than moral deliberation. This is a genuine tension — but it applies to every institution operating under conditions of urgency and hierarchy. The surgeon who stops mid-operation to deliberate about whether the patient deserves to be saved is not a moral hero. She is a danger. The firefighter who refuses a command because he has not personally assessed the risk is a liability. The requirement of disciplined obedience in high-stakes institutional contexts is a structural necessity, not corruption. And the military has, over centuries, developed mechanisms to manage the tension: military law, the laws of armed conflict, the obligation to refuse manifestly unlawful orders, the Nuremberg precedent. These mechanisms are imperfect — Abu Ghraib happened. But the conclusion from institutional failure is institutional reform, not abolition. We do not abolish police forces because police brutality exists.

On the availability heuristic — an argument that proves too much

The argument from the availability heuristicThe proposition deploys cognitive psychology: when you have a hammer, problems look like nails. Perhaps. But this argument proves too much. States with large diplomatic services bias toward diplomatic solutions that may be inappropriate. States with powerful sanctions regimes bias toward economic coercion. The existence of any institutional tool creates pressure toward its use. The solution is civilian oversight ensuring appropriate tool selection — not abolition of all institutional capacity. Moreover, the availability heuristic cuts in precisely the opposite direction when applied to the abolitionist scenario. A state that has abolished its military faces a world in which other states retain military capacity and in which the tools available to it are limited. A strongly worded diplomatic note does not stop a tank.

On alternatives — the fantasy of civilian-based defence

The argument from alternative security arrangementsThe proposition cites Switzerland, Costa Rica, and Iceland. These examples do not bear the weight placed on them. Switzerland has compulsory military service for all male citizens and a militia of considerable size, surrounded by NATO members. Costa Rica abolished its military after a civil war in a region where neighbours had no territorial ambitions toward it, with American security guarantees providing an implicit backstop. Iceland is a NATO member, defended by the collective military capacity of its allies including the most powerful military in human history. None of these cases demonstrates that a state can be secure in a contested neighbourhood without military capacity. They demonstrate that small states in benign strategic environments can free-ride on security provided by others. Civilian-based defence has never been demonstrated to work against a determined modern military aggressor.

On the security dilemma — condition, not construction

The argument from the security dilemmaWe are told the security dilemma is not a law of nature but a consequence of competitive military accumulation. True in theory. A fantasy in practice. The security dilemma reflects a structural feature of anarchic international relations: the absence of a central authority capable of enforcing agreements. States arm not because they are belligerent but because they cannot credibly commit to one another not to arm, and because the cost of being wrong about a neighbour's intentions is existential. The proposition's remedy — invest in international institutions — begs the question. International institutions capable of guaranteeing security require member states to contribute military force. The United Nations has no army. NATO's collective defence guarantee is only as credible as the national militaries underpinning it. You cannot build collective security on the ruins of national military capacity.

On just war theory — a misreading

The argument from just war theory's self-defeatJust war theory is a framework for moral evaluation of acts of force, not a framework for institutional design. It tells us when war is justified — not how to organise the permanent institutions of a state. Moreover, the proposition's institutional claim — that standing militaries are structured to produce unjust wars — is an empirical claim requiring empirical evidence, not enumeration of cases where force was misused. One could equally argue that abolition would produce more unjust outcomes: more successful aggressions, more genocide conducted without possibility of armed humanitarian intervention, more oppression of populations who cannot defend themselves. The empirical case for abolition requires showing not merely that militaries sometimes produce unjust outcomes, but that abolition would produce better outcomes overall. That case has not been made.

The real world

The argument from UkraineIn February 2022, Russian tanks crossed into Ukraine. The Ukrainian military resisted. Civilians took up arms. The country did not fall in three days, as Russian planners expected, because Ukraine had invested in military capacity, military training, and military culture. A Ukraine that had abolished its military in pursuit of the proposition's vision would not exist today as a sovereign state. Its population would be living under occupation — or worse. This is not a hypothetical. This is what the world actually looks like. And any argument for military abolition that cannot account for this fact — that cannot explain what Ukraine should have done instead — is not a serious argument for a serious world.

I oppose the motion.

Thank you.
