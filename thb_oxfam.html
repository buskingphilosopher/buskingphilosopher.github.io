Mr President,

I have been making the same argument for fifty years, and I have not yet heard a satisfactory reply. Let me state it simply, as I did in 1972, and then deal with the objections that have accumulated since.

The Drowning Child. You are walking past a shallow pond when you notice a small child has fallen in and is drowning. You could easily wade in and save her at no risk to yourself, but your shoes would be ruined — they cost £200. You pass by. Are you morally responsible for the child’s death? Of course you are. Now: a child is dying of a preventable disease in a country you have never visited. You could save her life for less than £200 by donating to an effective charity. You do not donate. How is this case morally different?
i. the basic argument

Singer’s basic argument — no morally relevant difference between drowning child and distant povertyMy argument has two premises and a conclusion. First: if it is in our power to prevent something very bad from happening, without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought, morally, to do it. Second: extreme poverty and death from preventable causes are very bad. Conclusion: if we can prevent death from extreme poverty without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we are morally required to do so. The premises are almost impossible to deny. The conclusion follows. The only question is whether our comfort, our luxury consumption, and our financial reserves are “of comparable moral importance” to a child’s life. They are not. A child’s life outweighs my new car, my holiday, my restaurant meals. Since I can save a child’s life by foregoing these things, I am obligated to do so. This is not a demand for sainthood. It is the application of a principle most people already accept — the drowning child principle — to circumstances they prefer not to think about.

ii. why distance does not matter

Distance irrelevance — physical proximity is morally arbitraryThe most common objection is that the distant child is not my responsibility in the way the drowning child before me is. But this rests on a belief that physical distance has moral significance. Why would it? The child’s suffering is equally real, equally preventable, equally bad whether she is a metre from me or ten thousand kilometres away. The only things that change with distance are my knowledge of her existence and the directness of my causal connection to her death. But my knowledge is now complete — I know she exists, I know she is dying, I know I can save her. And the causal directness argument proves too little: I did not push the drowning child in either. In both cases my failing to act makes me causally relevant to the death. Distance is morally arbitrary. Nationality is morally arbitrary. That she was born in Bangladesh rather than Birmingham is not a fact about her moral worth.

iii. nussbaum’s reductio: a reply

Against Nussbaum — reductio proves too much and misunderstands the argumentNussbaum argues that if everyone followed my advice, Oxfam would rule the world — a dystopian prospect. There are several problems with this response. First, it proves too much: the fact that a moral principle would have odd consequences if universally followed is not an argument against the principle, since in the actual world most people will not follow it. The drowning child principle, universalised, would have every bystander leaping into every pond simultaneously — but this absurdity does not undermine the obligation of the particular person who is present. Second, Nussbaum’s scenario assumes that charitable organisations like Oxfam would remain the mechanism of redistribution even as incomes collapse and everyone gives everything. But of course if the structural causes of poverty were addressed at that scale, the nature of the intervention would change completely. Third, and most importantly: Nussbaum herself concedes that I am right that the poor have urgent claims. Her objection is about the mechanism of relief, not about whether we owe something. I accept that and welcome it.

iv. supererogation is a convenient fiction

Supererogation — the category of "optional charity" is a moral invention to protect the comfortableMy deepest challenge is to the category of supererogation — the idea that some good acts are “above and beyond the call of duty,” praiseworthy but not required. I suspect this category was invented not by careful moral reasoning but by the accumulated self-interest of the comfortable in finding some domain where their inaction is not culpable. Why should saving a child’s life be supererogatory when done at a distance and obligatory when done nearby? The answer that the comfortable person gives — that the distant child is not their problem — is not a moral argument. It is a rationalisation. The work being done by “not my problem” is the work of moral luck: I am comfortable because I was born here rather than there, and I dress up this geographical accident as a moral boundary.

Thank you.

Argument I — The basic argument: if you can prevent something bad without comparable sacrifice, you must
1.If it is in our power to prevent something very bad without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought morally to do it.
2.Children dying from extreme poverty are a very bad thing that we can prevent by sacrificing far less than a child’s life is worth.
3.Luxury consumption, comfort, and surplus wealth are not of comparable moral importance to a child’s life.
∴We are morally obligated to give up surplus wealth to prevent child deaths from extreme poverty; failing to do so is not a neutral omission but a moral failure equivalent in kind to letting a child drown.
Argument II — Distance is morally arbitrary: proximity does not generate moral significance
1.Physical proximity to a person in distress does not add anything to the moral badness of their suffering or to the moral significance of our capacity to help.
2.The only differences between near and far cases are knowledge and causal directness; with modern communications, knowledge of distant suffering is complete; and causal directness is not morally decisive in the near case either.
3.Nationality and geography are morally arbitrary facts about birth; they cannot generate special moral responsibilities that override equal moral worth.
∴There is no morally relevant difference between the drowning child nearby and the dying child far away; the same obligation applies to both.
Argument III — Against Nussbaum’s reductio: it proves too much and misunderstands the scope of the claim
1.A moral principle’s implications under universal compliance are not the right standard for evaluating it; the drowning child principle generates absurdities when universalised but is obviously correct in the individual case.
2.Nussbaum’s “Oxfam world” scenario assumes the mechanism (charitable giving) would remain constant as scale changes, but structural global change would operate differently from individual charitable donations.
3.Nussbaum concedes that the poor have urgent claims; her objection is about mechanism, not about obligation itself.
∴Nussbaum’s reductio is not an argument against the moral obligation; it is a criticism of the delivery mechanism, which can be addressed without abandoning the obligation.
Argument IV — Supererogation is a convenient fiction protecting comfortable inaction
1.The category of supererogation (praiseworthy but not obligatory) was not derived by careful moral reasoning; it reflects the accumulated interest of the comfortable in having some zone of non-culpable inaction.
2.There is no principled reason why saving a life should be obligatory nearby and supererogatory from a distance; the only factor is the agent’s comfort with the idea that the distant child is “not their problem.”
3.“Not my problem” is a rationalisation dressing up geographical moral luck as a moral boundary.
∴The supererogation category, as applied to global poverty relief, is philosophically undefended; it exists to protect comfortable inaction, not to track a genuine moral distinction.

Mr President,

Martha Nussbaum has reviewed my book with what she calls admiration but what feels rather more like the elegant destruction of a building she has just finished praising. Her review is intelligent and her concerns are genuine. I want to answer them directly — because I think she has misread both my argument and its implications, and that her objections, though superficially powerful, dissolve under careful examination.

“Nussbaum notes that I address the reader with the claim that an affluent person must contribute most of the money and property she now has — and most of what comes her way in the future — to vitally effective groups like Oxfam and Unicef. She asks: what would the world be like if all the people addressed actually followed this advice? The answer she gives is a world ruled by Oxfam. What she never asks is: and is that worse than a world in which tens of millions of children die annually from preventable causes?”
i. nussbaum’s reductio assumes compliance would be uniform but deployment would not change

Reductio reply — compliance without systemic change is not the right scenario to imagineNussbaum invites us to imagine a world where everyone followed my advice. She then describes the dystopian consequences: Oxfam and UNICEF ruling the world, no cultural production, no individual flourishing, the dissolution of everything that makes a human life worth living. This is a vivid scenario but a confused one. If everyone in affluent nations gave most of their surplus wealth to effective global aid organisations, this would not produce an “Oxfam world.” It would produce a world in which extreme poverty had been substantially reduced, global inequality had fallen sharply, the conditions for genuine political development had improved, and the scale of charitable intervention would have changed entirely — because the problem it was addressing would have changed. Nussbaum imagines the demand remaining constant while the supply of giving increases indefinitely. But the demand for emergency relief would fall dramatically as the structural conditions producing it were addressed.

ii. the surface/depth distinction: our intuitions are unreliable

Surface/depth — intuitions are systematically distorted by irrelevant featuresMy central philosophical contribution is the distinction between the “surface” and “depth” of moral intuitions. Our surface intuitions — those that tell us to save the drowning child but not to send money to Oxfam — are systematically distorted by features of cases that are morally irrelevant: physical proximity, dramatic presentation, the identifiability of the victim, the presence of other bystanders. The Vintage Sedan cases I analyse in the book demonstrate that people who refuse to donate money to save a child’s life will happily accept letting a child die to save a car. When I manipulate the cases to vary only the morally relevant features, the intuitions change dramatically. The lesson is not that we should abandon moral intuition but that we should distinguish between deep moral insights and surface psychological responses to morally arbitrary features of presentation.

iii. the objection from individual flourishing

Flourishing objection — addressed: some personal expenditure is legitimately retainedNussbaum argues that my position requires the impoverishment of individual lives — the destruction of cultural production, personal relationships, artistic endeavour, and all the dimensions of a fully human existence. But I explicitly allow individuals to retain what is necessary for their own meaningful functioning. The duty is to give surplus wealth — that beyond what is required for a genuinely good life — not to reduce oneself to subsistence. Nussbaum’s portrait of an “Oxfam world” requires reading my position as demanding that everyone reduce themselves to the poverty level. I do not hold this. A person may live well, pursue projects, maintain relationships, and sustain cultural commitments, while also giving most of what is genuinely surplus to those whose survival depends on it. This is demanding, but it is not the nullification of the self that Nussbaum describes.

iv. the capabilities critique: nussbaum gets the better of me here

Concession — Nussbaum’s capabilities framework genuinely improves on UngerI want to be honest about where Nussbaum’s critique genuinely lands. She is right that I have focused too exclusively on keeping people alive — on preventing death — and have paid insufficient attention to the richer question of what it means for people to live genuinely flourishing lives. Her capabilities approach, in which the relevant question is not “are they alive?” but “can they exercise the full range of human capacities?”, is a genuine improvement on my framework. If I had adopted it, my argument would be harder to caricature as demanding mere biological survival for the poor while demanding cultural vitality for the rich. The capabilities framework is not an argument against giving; it is an argument for what the giving should aim at. Nussbaum and I are, at that level, in agreement: the goal is a world in which all people can live fully human lives. We disagree about what follows from that about individual giving obligations.

Thank you.

Argument I — Against the Oxfam-world reductio: demand would fall as giving increased
1.Nussbaum’s scenario imagines the demand for emergency relief remaining constant while the supply of giving increases to enormous scale.
2.If affluent individuals gave at the scale Unger envisions, extreme poverty would be substantially reduced, changing the nature and scale of the relief needed.
3.A world in which everyone gives generously is not a world ruled by Oxfam; it is a world in which Oxfam’s mission has been substantially accomplished.
∴Nussbaum’s reductio assumes the problem is permanent while the giving scales up; but the problem is precisely what the giving is designed to eliminate.
Argument II — Surface/depth: moral intuitions are systematically distorted by morally irrelevant factors
1.Surface intuitions respond to features of cases that are morally irrelevant: physical proximity, dramatic presentation, identifiability of the victim, number of bystanders.
2.When cases are varied to control for these irrelevant features and vary only the morally relevant ones, the intuitions change systematically, revealing their deep structure.
3.The deep moral insight is that preventable deaths are equally bad regardless of how vividly they are presented; surface intuitions that suggest otherwise are artefacts of psychology, not moral perception.
∴The surface intuition that we need not give to Oxfam tracks morally irrelevant psychological factors; the depth moral principle requires giving wherever preventable deaths can be avoided.
Argument III — The flourishing objection: surplus, not all, wealth is required
1.Unger requires giving most surplus wealth — that beyond what is needed for a genuinely good and flourishing life — not reducing oneself to subsistence.
2.A person may live well, pursue projects, maintain relationships, and sustain cultural commitments while giving most of what is genuinely beyond the requirements of a meaningful life.
3.Nussbaum’s portrait of an Oxfam world requires reading Unger as demanding near-total impoverishment; this misreads the “surplus” threshold he actually invokes.
∴The flourishing objection to Unger relies on a misreading; the position demands the sacrifice of genuine surplus, not the nullification of the self or the elimination of all personal flourishing.
Argument IV — Concession to Nussbaum: capabilities framework improves on bare survival
1.Unger focuses primarily on preventing death; Nussbaum’s capabilities framework asks what it means to live a fully human life, not merely to survive.
2.The capabilities framework is a genuine improvement on the bare-survival focus of Unger’s analysis; it enriches the account of what effective giving should aim at.
3.This concession does not weaken the obligation to give; it specifies what the giving should aim to produce.
∴Nussbaum’s capabilities critique is a genuine improvement to Unger’s framework at the level of ends, not an objection to the obligation to give; adopting it strengthens rather than defeats the case for demanding obligations.

Mr President,

Both Singer and Unger frame the moral problem as one of positive duties: we have an obligation to help those in need. I want to offer a different and, I believe, stronger foundation for demanding obligations to the global poor: not the positive duty to help, but the negative duty to stop harming. This reframing is not a weakening of the demand. It may strengthen it. We are not being asked to be Good Samaritans. We are being asked to stop being perpetrators.

“The citizens of affluent nations are not merely failing to help the global poor. Through the institutions their governments support, the trade rules they benefit from, and the political arrangements they participate in, they are actively imposing a global order that foreseeably and avoidably produces and perpetuates severe poverty. This is not a failure of charity. It is a violation of the negative duty not to harm.”
i. the global institutional order causes poverty

Global institutional order — affluent countries actively impose poverty-causing arrangementsThe global poor are not poor because of bad weather, insufficient effort, or cultural deficiency. They are poor in substantial part because the global institutional order — the trade rules, the financial architecture, the resource and borrowing privileges of governments, the patent systems, the subsidy regimes — has been designed by and for the benefit of wealthy nations and systematically disadvantages poor ones. TRIPS patent rules prevent developing countries from producing cheap medicines. Agricultural subsidies in the EU and US undercut developing-country farmers. The “resource privilege” that allows any government, however corrupt and unelected, to sell its country’s natural resources and receive international recognition for those contracts — creating the incentive structure for resource coups — is enforced by wealthy-nation courts and banks. We did not merely fail to help the poor. We built and maintain the system that keeps them poor.

ii. negative duties are stronger than positive duties

Negative duty strength — not harming is more demanding than helping; negative duties are less deniableThe Singer/Unger framework asks affluent individuals to do something generous — to give. Wealthy-nation citizens can respond that they have no special relationship to the global poor, that giving is supererogatory, that they did not cause the poverty. My framework leaves no such escape. You did not merely fail to help the global poor: you voted for governments that maintain and defend the global institutional order that harms them; you purchase goods produced under trade agreements that exploit them; your pension funds benefit from financial arrangements that extract wealth from them; your government’s military and diplomatic power enforces the property rights of corporations that operate to their detriment. You are a participant in an ongoing harm. The negative duty not to participate in that harm — or to compensate for the harm your participation produces — is not a demand for generosity. It is a demand for basic justice.

iii. the global resources dividend as a modest practical proposal

Global Resources Dividend — concrete policy proposal, not utopian demandI want to show that the practical implications of my argument are considerably more modest than Nussbaum’s caricature of Singer and Unger would suggest. I propose a Global Resources Dividend: a small levy on the use of natural resources — approximately 1% of global GDP — to be redistributed to the global poor. This would be sufficient, by UN estimates, to eliminate severe poverty worldwide. The demand is not that affluent individuals give most of their wealth. The demand is that they support modest institutional reforms that redirect a tiny fraction of global economic output toward those who have been impoverished by the institutional order from which the rest of us benefit. The GRD is not charity. It is restitution.

iv. answering nussbaum: structural justice, not aid dependency

Structural justice vs. Oxfam dependency — Pogge’s approach avoids Nussbaum’s dependency critiqueNussbaum’s most powerful objection to Singer and Unger is that their approach creates dependency on wealthy-nation charity rather than building local capacity, political development, and genuine agency. I share this concern, which is why my approach focuses on institutional reform rather than on individual charitable giving. The GRD does not create an Oxfam world — it creates a world in which the terms of the global economy are marginally more just, allowing developing nations to fund their own infrastructure, healthcare, and education rather than depending on the goodwill of affluent donors. My approach is precisely the structural analysis that Nussbaum calls for. We disagree about the source of the obligation — I think it is a duty of justice, she thinks it is a duty of compassion — but we agree that the solution is institutional change, not individual philanthropy.

Thank you.

Argument I — The global institutional order foreseeably and avoidably produces severe poverty
1.The global trade rules, financial architecture, resource privileges, and patent systems were designed by and for wealthy nations and systematically disadvantage developing ones.
2.TRIPS rules, agricultural subsidies, resource privileges, and financial extractivism are enforced by wealthy-nation governments, courts, and international institutions.
3.The global poor are poor in substantial part because of this institutional order, which affluent citizens participate in, benefit from, and support through their political engagement.
∴Affluent citizens are participants in an institutional order that foreseeably and avoidably causes severe poverty; this grounds a negative duty to stop harming, not merely a positive duty to help.
Argument II — Negative duties are stronger: the obligation is not to be generous but to stop harming
1.Negative duties (not to harm) are generally accepted as stronger and less deniable than positive duties (to help); they admit fewer exceptions and are not subject to demandingness objections in the same way.
2.Affluent citizens vote for, benefit from, and participate in institutional arrangements that harm the global poor; this makes the obligation to reform those arrangements a negative duty, not a charitable positive duty.
3.The arguments that “I have no special relationship to the global poor” and “giving is optional” do not apply to negative duties; one cannot claim that one’s harming of another is optional.
∴Grounding the obligation in negative duties rather than positive ones strengthens the case for demanding obligations; affluent citizens are not being asked to be generous but to stop participating in harm.
Argument III — The Global Resources Dividend: a modest, realistic proposal
1.A 1% levy on global natural resource use — the Global Resources Dividend — would, by UN estimates, generate sufficient funds to eliminate severe poverty worldwide.
2.The GRD is not individual charity but institutional reform; it is restitution for harm, not beneficence toward strangers.
3.The demand on individuals is to support this reform politically and economically, not to give most of their wealth to Oxfam; the practical implication is far more modest than Singer’s or Unger’s.
∴The GRD shows that obligations to the global poor can be met through modest institutional reforms rather than individual near-total wealth transfer; the practically effective demand is much less extreme than Nussbaum’s reductio suggests.
Argument IV — Structural justice avoids Nussbaum’s dependency critique
1.Nussbaum objects that Singer/Unger create dependency on wealthy-nation charity rather than building genuine local agency and capability.
2.Pogge’s institutional reform approach addresses this: the GRD allows developing nations to fund their own institutions rather than depending on the goodwill of affluent donors.
3.The disagreement between Pogge and Nussbaum is about the source of obligation (justice vs. compassion), not about the conclusion that structural change is required.
∴Pogge’s structural justice approach satisfies Nussbaum’s dependency critique while maintaining demanding obligations; they agree on the remedy (institutional change) but disagree on its ground (justice vs. compassion).

Mr President,

Peter Unger’s book is a work of genuine moral passion and considerable philosophical skill. I said so in my review and I say so here. He is right that we owe far more to the global poor than comfortable convention admits. I am not here to defend the comfortable. I am here to argue that Unger’s argument — and Singer’s underlying framework — is philosophically deficient in ways that are not merely pedantic but practically damaging: they produce a picture of our obligations that, if taken seriously, would undermine the very conditions for human flourishing they are trying to protect.

“Unger doesn’t even try to imagine the world that would result if people took his advice. This would appear to be because he has assumed that people will not take his advice — so the argument is designed only to make us feel guilty and give a bit more, not to actually solve the problem. This is philosophy as a sophisticated form of fundraising, not as a genuine guide to how human societies should be structured.”
i. the reductio: what an oxfam world would actually be like

Oxfam world reductio — universal compliance produces absurd and self-defeating resultsLet us take Unger seriously and ask what happens if everyone addressed by his argument actually followed it. All affluent persons give most of their surplus wealth to Oxfam and UNICEF. Universities lose their endowments, research collapses, medical innovation stalls. Hospitals are underfunded. The cultural institutions that produce the knowledge, art, and science that sustain human civilization are stripped of resources. Tax revenues collapse as consumer spending falls. Governments can no longer fund public services. The conditions for political democracy — an educated citizenry, independent media, functioning institutions — erode. And Oxfam, UNICEF, and their like receive more money than they can possibly deploy effectively, becoming the de facto governors of global resources. This is not a world in which the poor flourish. It is a world in which everyone is impoverished while power is concentrated in the hands of unaccountable NGOs. The scenario is so unstable that it refutes itself.

ii. the dependency critique: charity is the wrong instrument

Dependency critique — charity creates dependency and undermines local political agencyMy deeper objection is not to the scale of the obligation but to its instrument. Singer and Unger assume that the appropriate mechanism for addressing global poverty is individual charitable giving channelled through international aid organisations. But there is an enormous body of development economics research suggesting that aid dependency is itself a significant cause of the pathologies that keep poor countries poor: it reduces government accountability (why raise taxes when Oxfam will fund the hospital?), it undermines local political development (why organise a civil society when the NGO will do it?), and it creates incentive structures that prioritise the preferences of donors over the needs of recipients. The capabilities approach I favour insists that what the global poor need is not money transferred from rich individuals but the structural conditions — political institutions, property rights, rule of law, genuine markets — in which they can themselves develop their capabilities. This requires political action, not charitable giving.

iii. the capabilities critique: bare survival is not the goal

Capabilities — what the poor need is not money but conditions for flourishingUnger’s analysis is focused almost exclusively on keeping people alive — on preventing death from starvation and preventable disease. The capabilities approach I have developed with Amartya Sen insists that this is a radically impoverished account of what it means for a human life to go well. The goal is not mere survival but the ability to exercise the full range of distinctively human capacities: to form relationships, to engage in political life, to pursue education and work, to exercise practical reason, to live in environments that support health and dignity. A strategy that transfers money from rich individuals to aid organisations focused on bare survival may produce more of the latter while doing nothing for the former — or may actively undermine it by creating the dependency relations I have described.

iv. what we actually owe: structural change, not individual sacrifice

Structural obligations — political reform, not individual sacrifice, is what is owedI am not arguing that we owe nothing to the global poor. I am arguing that what we owe is not individual sacrifice but structural political change. The obligation is to support institutions and policies — fair trade rules, pharmaceutical access, debt relief, genuine development financing — that enable poor countries to develop the political and economic conditions for human flourishing. This is more demanding in one sense than Singer and Unger’s view: it requires sustained political engagement, not merely writing a cheque. But it is also more respectful of the agency and dignity of those it aims to help: it treats the global poor as political agents who need the conditions for self-determination, not as objects of charity who need our money. Singer and Unger have the right problem but the wrong solution, and the wrong solution will, if generalised, do more harm than good.

Mr President,

I want to approach this debate from a different direction from Nussbaum. She challenges Singer and Unger at the level of practical consequences and political philosophy. I want to challenge them at a more fundamental level: at the level of what morality is for, and what it means for a moral theory to be adequate to the nature of human beings as agents with their own projects, commitments, and identities. My claim is that any moral theory that demands the kind of impartial maximisation that Singer and Unger require has misunderstood what morality is, and has replaced the moral life of an integrated human agent with the calculative operations of a welfare-maximising machine.

“The utilitarian picture of the world is one in which each person is merely a location through which utility passes. My own plans, my relationships, my commitments, my history — all of these are merely inputs into the impartial calculation, to be weighed against the welfare of others and sacrificed whenever the calculus demands it. What this picture omits is precisely what makes a human life worth living: integrity, the sense that one’s actions express one’s own values and character rather than the outputs of a welfare-optimisation programme.”
i. the integrity objection

Integrity objection — utilitarian maximisation destroys personal integrity and alienates the agent from their projectsA moral theory must be compatible with the kind of life a person can actually live as an integrated human agent with their own projects and commitments. My objection to utilitarian maximisation is not that it is too demanding in a quantitative sense — that it asks us to give too much money. It is that the entire structure of the theory is incompatible with having the kind of commitments, relationships, and long-term projects that constitute a meaningful human life. Utilitarianism treats my commitment to my children, my partner, my friends, my life’s work as merely contingent inputs into an impartial calculation. If sacrificing those commitments would produce more utility, I am required to sacrifice them. But this is not a life anyone is living or can live. It is a bureaucratic fantasy of moral purity that requires the agent’s complete alienation from everything that makes their life their own.

ii. ground projects and the meaning of life

Ground projects — without them life has no meaning; they cannot be treated as impartially fungibleI have argued that what gives human life its meaning is what I call ground projects: fundamental commitments that are so constitutive of a person’s identity that their loss would not merely be bad for them but would make the question of their continued existence seem pointless. A person’s relationship with their children, their vocation, their deep creative or intellectual commitments — these are not merely preferences that can be traded off against strangers’ welfare in an impartial calculation. They are what the person is. A theory that requires their systematic subordination to impartial welfare maximisation is not asking us to be morally better. It is asking us to stop being a person. This is not an acceptable demand, and a moral theory that makes it has misunderstood the relationship between morality and the human agent whose conduct it is supposed to guide.

iii. negative responsibility is an incoherent expansion

Negative responsibility — utilitarian expansion of negative responsibility is incoherentSinger’s argument relies on the principle that failing to prevent harm is morally equivalent to causing it, provided prevention would involve no comparable sacrifice. This is the doctrine of negative responsibility: I am as responsible for the deaths I could have prevented as for the deaths I caused. This doctrine, once fully accepted, provides no principled stopping point. I am responsible for every preventable bad outcome within my causal power to affect. There is no limit to the expansion of obligation this entails. A person who accepts negative responsibility in full has no life left that is their own — every action is potentially a failure to do something that would have prevented a worse outcome somewhere. This is not a moral theory. It is a recipe for paralysis, followed by a choice of which large effective charity to give everything to and call it moral life.

iv. what the objection is not

Not selfishness — the integrity objection is not a defence of comfortable inactionI want to be clear that my integrity objection is not a defence of comfortable inaction or of the status quo. I am not saying that we owe nothing to the global poor or that our current behaviour is acceptable. I am saying that the framework of impartial welfare maximisation is the wrong way to understand what we owe — that it produces an account of moral life incompatible with human agency, and that the energy spent resisting its impossible demands could be better used constructing a more adequate account of our genuine obligations. I believe we have substantial obligations of justice and solidarity toward the global poor. I believe our current behaviour falls far short of those obligations. I am opposed to the framework Singer and Unger use to specify those obligations, not to the conclusion that we should be doing more.

Argument I — Integrity: impartial maximisation alienates the agent from their constitutive commitments
1.A moral theory must be compatible with the kind of life a person can live as an integrated human agent; it must leave room for the commitments and projects that constitute the agent’s identity.

Mr President,

I want to offer what I take to be the most philosophically rigorous defence of the demandingness objection to Singer and Unger — one that does not rely, as Williams’ version sometimes does, on the particular intuitions of individuals about their special commitments. My argument is more structural: there are agent-centred prerogatives that any adequate moral theory must acknowledge, and these prerogatives are not merely concessions to human weakness but are grounded in a genuine moral insight about the relationship between agents and the good they produce.

“The demandingness objection is not a charge that consequentialism is psychologically impossible to follow. It is the deeper charge that consequentialism treats persons as mere sites for the production of impartial good, ignoring the special moral weight that accrues to each agent in their own life. This is not a failure of generosity. It is a moral error about what persons are.”
i. agent-centred prerogatives

Agent-centred prerogatives — each agent permitted to weight their own projects more heavily than impartial goodIn The Rejection of Consequentialism I argue that any plausible moral theory must include agent-centred prerogatives: permissions for each agent to weight their own interests and projects more heavily in their deliberations than the impartial optimisation of overall good would require. A prerogative is not a right to ignore the good of others. It is a permission to give one’s own interests, relationships, and projects a moral weight in one’s deliberations that exceeds their weight in an impartial calculation. This permission is not unlimited: it does not permit one to ignore severe harms one can cheaply prevent. But it does permit one to favour one’s own children, one’s own projects, and one’s own flourishing in ways that impartial consequentialism would condemn. The permission is grounded in the morally significant fact that each person’s life is their own, lived from a particular standpoint with particular attachments, and that morality must accommodate this rather than abolishing it.

ii. the asymmetry objection to consequentialism

Asymmetry objection — consequentialism recognises agent-relative restrictions but not agent-relative prerogativesMany consequentialists have moved to accommodate the integrity objection by acknowledging agent-relative restrictions: prohibitions on performing acts that, though they would maximise good, involve using persons as mere means. This is an acknowledgment that agents are not merely channels for the impartial good. But it is inconsistent. If an agent’s particular standpoint generates moral reasons not to perform certain welfare-maximising acts, it should generate equally strong moral reasons to give their own welfare and projects special weight in positive deliberations. If an agent may not harm one to save five, why must the agent sacrifice their own projects for the welfare of others? The same acknowledgment of the moral significance of the agent’s standpoint that grounds restrictions should ground prerogatives. Consequentialism’s acceptance of the former while rejecting the latter is internally inconsistent.

iii. the demandingness objection is a genuine moral objection, not a rationalisation

Demandingness not a rationalisation — Singer is wrong to dismiss it as comfortable selfishnessSinger dismisses the demandingness objection as a convenient rationalisation for the comfortable — as the self-interested resistance of the affluent to genuinely impartial moral demands. This is unfair. The demandingness objection is not the claim “morality cannot ask this much of me because I find it inconvenient.” It is the claim “morality cannot ask this much of anyone because a moral theory that does so has misunderstood what persons are and what morality is for.” The prerogative I defend is not a licence for selfishness; it is a structural feature of adequate moral theory. A person of scrupulous moral integrity, who takes their obligations to others with the utmost seriousness, is nonetheless permitted to give special weight to their own ground projects. This is not a moral failure. It is a morally significant feature of having a life of one’s own.

iv. what prerogatives do and do not permit

Prerogatives are not unlimited — they require substantial but not maximal givingAgent-centred prerogatives do not permit unlimited self-favouring. They do not permit one to spend frivolously while others die from preventable poverty when one could easily help. The prerogative is to weight one’s own projects and interests by a multiplier — to give them several times the weight an impartial observer would assign — not to give them infinite weight. This means that substantial giving is required: genuinely giving until the further sacrifice would impair not my luxury but my capacity for a genuinely human life. The amount required is significant — probably considerably more than most affluent people currently give. But it is not the near-total sacrifice that Singer and Unger demand. It is a morally demanding but humanly liveable standard, and that combination is what a genuine moral theory must offer.

Thank you.
