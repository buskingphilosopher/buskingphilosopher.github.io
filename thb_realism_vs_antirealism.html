Mr President,

I want to begin by framing what I take to be the central diagnostic failure of most realist responses to the antirealist challenge. It is not that realists have been wrong in their positive commitments — that the world exists independently of the mind, that our theories can be straightforwardly true of it, that truth is not reducible to epistemic warrant. They have been right about all of this. The failure is that most realists have not taken the antirealist challenge seriously enough to understand what it is. They have dismissed it as obviously question-begging, as a philosopher’s puzzle with no grip on real inquiry, or as a mere verbal dispute. In my book I argue that this dismissal is a strategic error, and that the antirealist challenge — particularly in its Dummettian form — is a genuine philosophical threat that requires a substantive, carefully constructed response.

The Representation Problem (Khlentzos) If naturalistic realism is true — if the world exists independently of the mind — how can mental representation of that world be explained? The problem is not merely epistemological (how do we know about it?) but semantic: how do our thoughts and utterances manage to refer to mind-independent entities at all? If we are entirely natural systems embedded in a natural world, what is the mechanism by which our mental states “reach out” to hook onto features of that world? This is what Khlentzos calls the representation problem, and he argues it is the real ground on which the realism/antirealism debate must be fought.
i. taking the antirealist challenge seriously

The representation problem — realists dismiss it at their perilKhlentzos’s central diagnostic: realists who are also naturalists — who believe the mind is a natural system causally embedded in the natural world — face a special version of the representation problem. If naturalism is true, our mental representations are physical states of a physical system. But physical states have physical causal relations; they do not by themselves have semantic properties like reference and truth. How does the physical state “mean” something rather than something else? How does the word “electron” in a physicist’s theory manage to refer to electrons rather than to quark configurations or energy fields or nothing at all? The antirealist argues that without a satisfactory answer to this question, the realist has no rational basis for claiming that our theories are “true” of a mind-independent reality. The realist who dismisses this as a trivial puzzle has not understood why it poses a genuine threat to naturalistic realism.

ii. truth is non-epistemic

Non-epistemic truth — truth can transcend all possible evidenceThe antirealist — in Dummett’s version — holds that truth cannot transcend what is in principle epistemically accessible: a sentence is true only if, in principle, we could recognise it as true. Against this I defend a radically non-epistemic conception of truth. A sentence is true if and only if it corresponds to facts in the world: if the entities referred to by its constituent terms stand in the relation expressed by the predicate. Whether those facts are recognisable by us — whether we have or could have evidence for them — is entirely irrelevant to whether they obtain. The truth that “Socrates sneezed in his sleep the night before he drank the hemlock” either obtains or it does not, and it does not become indeterminate merely because no evidence is available. Facts in the world do not wait for us to be able to recognise them before they become determinate. This non-epistemic conception of truth is what distinguishes the realist from the antirealist at the deepest level.

iii. against physicalism and functionalism as solutions

Against physicalism/functionalism — they fail to solve representationI examine and reject current physicalist and functionalist solutions to the representation problem. The functionalist says: a mental state represents X if it plays the causal-functional role that we use to pick out X. The problem is that causal-functional roles are multiply realisable and underdetermine content: a given functional role is compatible with indefinitely many possible representational contents. The physicalist says: reference is grounded in causal co-variation between mental states and worldly features. But causal co-variation is too coarse-grained to determine reference: my beliefs co-vary with distal causes, proximal causes, and a range of property clusters all of which might be the “real” referent. Neither view provides a satisfactory naturalisation of representation, and without such a naturalisation the naturalistic realist cannot explain how our thoughts and sentences refer to the mind-independent world she posits.

iv. the correspondence theory of truth — my positive proposal

Correspondence theory — facts as properties of the worldMy positive contribution is a version of the correspondence theory of truth. Truth requires mind-independent links between our truth-bearers and the things in the world that they represent. I understand facts not as abstract items that hover between language and world but as genuine properties of the world: relational structures consisting of objects standing in relations. A true sentence is one whose constituents successfully refer to such objects and correctly characterises a relation they stand in. This account is neither verificationist — it makes no reference to epistemic accessibility — nor deflationary — it assigns a genuine, substantive semantic role to the truth predicate, not merely the disquotational role. It is the account that the realist needs to resist the antirealist’s claim that any coherent notion of truth must be epistemically constrained.

Argument I — The representation problem: naturalistic realists cannot dismiss the antirealist challenge
1.Naturalistic realism: the world exists mind-independently and our mental states are physical states of a natural system.
2.Physical states have causal relations but not intrinsic semantic properties; how a physical state comes to “mean” something rather than something else is not explained by its physical nature alone.
3.Without a solution to the representation problem, the naturalistic realist has no rational basis for claiming our theories refer to and are true of the mind-independent world she posits.
∴The representation problem is the genuine ground of the antirealist challenge to naturalistic realism; dismissing it without a solution leaves naturalistic realism rationally ungrounded.
Argument II — Non-epistemic truth: truth cannot be reduced to epistemic warrant
1.The antirealist constrains truth to what is in principle epistemically accessible; if we cannot in principle recognise that p, then p has no determinate truth value.
2.A non-epistemic conception of truth: a sentence is true if and only if the mind-independent facts it represents obtain, regardless of whether those facts are recognisable.
3.Facts in the world do not become indeterminate because we cannot access them; the antirealist’s epistemic constraint on truth imports a covert idealism that the realist is entitled to reject.
∴Truth is non-epistemic; verification-transcendence is a genuine feature of reality, not a philosopher’s fiction, and the realist’s use of the principle of bivalence for undecidable sentences is rationally defensible.
Argument III — Against physicalism and functionalism as solutions to representation
1.Functionalism: a mental state represents X if it plays the functional role that picks out X. But functional roles underdetermine content: the same role is compatible with indefinitely many representational contents.
2.Physicalism / causal covariation: representation is grounded in causal relations between mental states and worldly features. But causal covariation is too coarse-grained: a mental state co-varies with distal causes, proximal causes, and multiple property clusters.
3.Neither view determines a unique referent; both leave the representation problem unsolved.
∴Standard physicalist and functionalist naturalisations of representation fail; a satisfactory solution to the representation problem requires a more robust correspondence theory of the kind Khlentzos defends.
Argument IV — Correspondence theory: facts as properties of the world
1.Facts are relational structures: objects standing in relations; they are genuine properties of the world, not intermediaries between language and world.
2.A true sentence is one whose constituents successfully refer to such objects and correctly characterises a relation they stand in; this is the mind-independent link that realism requires.
3.This account is neither verificationist (no epistemic accessibility required) nor deflationary (truth plays a substantive semantic role); it is the conception of truth that grounds the realist’s resistance to antirealism.
∴The correspondence theory, properly understood as grounding truth in mind-independent structural facts, provides the realist answer to the antirealist challenge without collapsing into verificationism or deflationism.

Mr President,

I want to resist what I regard as a methodological confusion that runs through the entire antirealist programme and that Khlentzos, for all his virtues, still does not fully escape. That confusion is what I call “semantic ascent” as a method for deciding metaphysical questions. Both Dummett and Putnam conduct their debate about realism at the level of language and meaning — about truth conditions, about the meanings of sentences, about what speakers can manifest. I want to insist that realism is a metaphysical thesis, not a semantic one, and that the antirealist’s semantic arguments — however sophisticated — simply do not reach their intended target.

“Realism is the view that entities of the relevant kinds exist mind-independently. It has nothing directly to do with truth or reference. The mistake of philosophers from Dummett to Putnam is to think that you can argue about whether stones, electrons, and mathematical objects exist by arguing about the semantics of sentences about stones, electrons, and mathematical objects. You cannot. Metaphysics precedes and is independent of semantics.”
i. realism as a metaphysical thesis

Realism as metaphysical — independent of semanticsIn Realism and Truth I argue for a sharp separation between realism as a metaphysical thesis and any associated semantic theses about truth and reference. Realism, as I define it, is the view that the entities posited by our best science — stones, electrons, magnetic fields, biological species — exist mind-independently and have their properties mind-independently. This is a thesis about what there is and what it is like, not a thesis about how our language hooks onto it. The antirealist challenges realism by arguing that our use of sentences cannot manifest grasp of evidence-transcendent truth conditions; or that the model-theoretic argument shows reference is indeterminate. These are arguments about semantics and the philosophy of language. They presuppose realism is a thesis about the relationship between language and world. It is not. It is a thesis about the world itself, and semantic arguments cannot touch it.

ii. the dismissal of “just more theory”

Just more theory — Putnam’s model-theoretic problem dissolvedPutnam’s model-theoretic argument is perhaps the most technically sophisticated antirealist argument available. It purports to show that even if our theory is empirically ideal — consistent with all possible evidence — there will be infinitely many interpretations of the theory that satisfy it, and no facts fix which interpretation is the “intended” one. The realist’s response — that causal or natural properties fix reference — Putnam dismisses as “just more theory”: it is simply another theory that itself admits multiple models. But this dismissal is question-begging. The realist is not appealing to a theory of reference in order to fix reference within that same theory. She is appealing to a metaphysical fact: that certain natural, causal relations obtain between mental states and mind-independent entities, and that these relations constitute reference. This is a first-order metaphysical claim, not a theory open to model-theoretic attack.

iii. realism without tears: the common-sense ground

Common-sense realism — empirical support for the existence of mind-independent objectsRealism, on my view, has excellent empirical support that does not depend on semantic argument. The best evidence for the existence of mind-independent objects is simply the extraordinary success of our scientific practices: the predictive power of our theories, the ability to manipulate unobserved entities, the convergence of independent measurement techniques on the same theoretical values. These successes are best explained by the hypothesis that our theories are approximately true of mind-independent entities. The antirealist can dispute this — and Bas van Fraassen’s constructive empiricism offers an elegant agnostic alternative — but the dispute is then an empirical and methodological one, not a linguistic or semantic one. The realist is not obliged to answer Dummett’s manifestation argument before she can be a realist about electrons; she needs only the inference to the best explanation that scientific success provides.

iv. what distinguishes me from khlentzos

Devitt vs. Khlentzos — Devitt resists engaging semantic antirealism on its own termsKhlentzos argues that naturalistic realists have been too complacent in the face of Dummett and Putnam; they have dismissed the challenges without understanding them. I think this diagnosis is partially right but the prescription is wrong. Engaging the antirealist’s semantic arguments on their own terms — constructing ever more sophisticated correspondence theories to answer Dummett, refining our theories of representation to answer Putnam’s model-theoretic argument — concedes too much. It accepts that the debate is fundamentally semantic, which it is not. The correct realist response to Dummett is not to produce a better theory of how speakers can manifest grasp of evidence-transcendent truth conditions. It is to point out that whether electrons exist is not decided by what speakers can manifest. The representation problem is a genuine puzzle, but solving it is not a precondition for rational realism.

Argument I — Realism is metaphysical, not semantic: antirealist semantic arguments miss the target
1.Realism is the view that entities of the relevant kinds exist mind-independently with mind-independent properties; it is a thesis about what there is, not about how language hooks onto it.
2.Dummett’s and Putnam’s antirealist arguments are conducted at the level of semantics: what speakers can manifest, what reference is fixed by, how truth conditions are grasped.
3.Semantic arguments cannot establish or undermine a first-order metaphysical thesis about the existence and nature of mind-independent entities.
∴The antirealist’s semantic arguments do not reach the target: they are arguments about language and meaning, not about whether electrons, stones, and species exist mind-independently.
Argument II — Against Putnam’s “just more theory”: causal reference is a metaphysical fact, not a theory
1.Putnam’s model-theoretic argument: any empirically ideal theory has infinitely many satisfying interpretations; adding a theory of causal reference is “just more theory” susceptible to the same attack.
2.The realist’s response is not to add a theory of reference but to invoke a metaphysical fact: certain causal relations obtain between mental states and worldly features, and those relations constitute reference.
3.A metaphysical fact about causal relations is not a theory open to model-theoretic attack; Putnam’s “just more theory” dismissal begs the question by treating the realist’s metaphysical claim as if it were another semantic theory.
∴Putnam’s “just more theory” response presupposes the question is about theories of reference; the realist’s appeal to causal facts is a metaphysical, not a semantic, claim that model theory does not reach.
Argument III — Empirical support for realism: the no-miracles inference
1.The predictive success of our scientific theories, the convergence of independent measurement techniques, and our ability to manipulate unobserved entities are extraordinary facts that require explanation.
2.The best explanation of these facts is that our theories are approximately true of mind-independent entities: the entities posited actually exist and have roughly the properties the theories attribute to them.
3.This inference to the best explanation provides empirical support for realism that does not depend on prior resolution of semantic questions about manifestation or reference-fixing.
∴Realism has robust empirical support through the no-miracles argument; the antirealist’s semantic challenges are not a precondition the realist must meet before this support is available.
Argument IV — Against Khlentzos: engaging antirealism on semantic terms concedes too much
1.Khlentzos argues that realists must engage the representation problem and develop a correspondence theory to answer antirealist semantic arguments.
2.But engaging on semantic terms accepts that the realism debate is fundamentally semantic; it is not — it is fundamentally metaphysical.
3.The representation problem is a genuine philosophical puzzle but not a precondition for rational realism; the correct response is to separate the metaphysical question (does the world exist mind-independently?) from the semantic question (how does language refer to it?).
∴Devitt’s methodological separation of metaphysical realism from semantic questions is more defensible than Khlentzos’s engagement strategy; engaging semantic antirealism on its own terms validates a framing that realists should resist.

Mr President,

My contribution is to address directly the representation problem that Khlentzos identifies as the Achilles heel of naturalistic realism, and to show that a naturalistic theory of mental representation — one grounded in causal and informational relations between mental states and worldly features — can provide a satisfactory solution. I disagree with Devitt that the representation problem can simply be sidestepped. I agree with Khlentzos that it must be engaged. But I think the engagement yields a more optimistic result than Khlentzos’s somewhat austere correspondence theory suggests.

i. the causal-informational theory of content

Causal-informational content — Papineau’s naturalistic representationMy positive proposal is a teleological/causal theory of mental content. A mental state represents a worldly feature F if: (1) the state was selected by evolution or learning because it co-varies with F in the organism’s environment, and (2) the state is used by the organism’s cognitive system as a guide to action in ways that are sensitive to F’s presence or absence. This is not mere causal covariation — Khlentzos is right that covariation is too coarse-grained. It is covariation selected-for in virtue of its reliability: the state represents F because it was constituted to track F as a biologically or cognitively beneficial function. The teleological element solves the disjunction problem: the state misrepresents when it is tokened in the absence of F, because its function is to track F, even though it is causally connected to the disjunction F-or-G.

ii. solving the disjunction problem

Disjunction problem — teleological content individuates by function, not causeThe disjunction problem is the core difficulty for causal theories of content. A frog’s bug-detecting state is caused both by actual bugs and by small black dots; does it represent “bug” or “small black dot” or “bug-or-small-black-dot”? Simple causal covariation cannot say. The teleological account answers: the frog’s state represents bugs because its evolutionary function is to detect bugs for the purpose of eating — small black dots trigger it accidentally, by mimicking the feature it was selected to track. Misrepresentation is possible because the function fixes the representational content independently of the causes of any particular tokening. This is not just a solution to a technical puzzle: it shows that naturalistic realism has the resources to explain how a physical system embedded in the world can genuinely represent features of that world in a semantically determinate way.

iii. truth as reliable representation

Truth as reliable representation — naturalistic correspondenceOnce we have a naturalistic theory of content, we can give a naturalistic account of truth. A sentence is true if the features of the world it represents — as determined by the causal-teleological theory of content — actually obtain. Truth is not correspondence to abstract facts in any spooky sense; it is the obtaining of the worldly conditions that the sentence’s constituent representations are constituted to track. This account is fully naturalistic: it invokes only causal relations, biological functions, and physical states. It is also non-epistemic: whether the worldly conditions obtain is independent of whether we can recognise that they obtain. And it grounds the realist’s use of bivalence for undecidable sentences: a sentence about Socrates’ sneezing habits is determinately true or false because the worldly conditions it represents either obtain or do not, regardless of epistemic access.

iv. against dummett’s manifestation argument

Against Dummett — teleological content manifests realist understandingDummett’s manifestation argument claims that if speakers grasp evidence-transcendent truth conditions, this grasp cannot be manifested in their linguistic behaviour, since their behaviour is constrained to what they have epistemic access to. My naturalistic account provides a direct rebuttal. A speaker’s understanding of “Socrates sneezed in his sleep the night before he drank the hemlock” consists not in a direct confrontation with the past event but in grasping the compositional semantic values of the sentence’s component expressions — values fixed by causal-teleological relations to worldly features. This grasp is manifested in the speaker’s appropriate use of the sentence in inference, in her acceptance of excluded-middle instances, and in her understanding that evidence might be gathered that would settle the question. The manifestation is of compositional semantic competence, not of epistemic access to the specific fact. Dummett’s argument requires that understanding a sentence involves epistemic confrontation with its truth condition; the naturalistic account shows it does not.

Argument I — Teleological content: representation fixed by function, not mere covariation
1.A mental state represents F if it was selected (by evolution or learning) because it co-varies reliably with F in the organism’s environment and is used as a guide to action in F-sensitive ways.
2.The teleological element — selected-for reliable tracking of F — individuates content more finely than causal covariation alone: the state represents F, not the disjunction F-or-G that also causes it.
3.This provides a naturalistic account of semantic determinacy: a physical system can represent a specific worldly feature in virtue of the biological or cognitive function of its states.
∴The teleological theory of content provides the naturalistic solution to the representation problem that Khlentzos seeks: it grounds determinate representation without invoking non-natural semantic relations.
Argument II — The disjunction problem: misrepresentation is possible under the teleological account
1.A frog’s bug-detecting state is caused by both bugs and black dots; simple causal covariation cannot determine which is represented.
2.The teleological account determines that the state represents bugs: its evolutionary function is to detect bugs; black dots trigger it accidentally by mimicking the tracked feature.
3.Misrepresentation is possible: when triggered by a black dot, the state is tokened but its representational function is not fulfilled — it represents bugs but is caused by something else.
∴The teleological account solves the disjunction problem by fixing representational content via function rather than cause; misrepresentation becomes possible, making the account genuinely semantic rather than merely causal.
Argument III — Truth as obtaining of represented worldly conditions: naturalistic and non-epistemic
1.Once content is fixed by the teleological theory, truth is the obtaining of the worldly conditions the sentence’s constituent representations are constituted to track.
2.This account is fully naturalistic (invoking only causal relations and biological functions) and non-epistemic (whether the conditions obtain is independent of whether we can recognise them).
3.It grounds bivalence for undecidable sentences: worldly conditions either obtain or do not, regardless of epistemic access.
∴The naturalistic correspondence account of truth is both realist and scientifically respectable; it vindicates the realist’s commitment to evidence-transcendent truth without invoking non-natural semantic relations.
Argument IV — Against Dummett: compositional semantic competence manifests realist understanding
1.Dummett claims: understanding a sentence with evidence-transcendent truth conditions cannot be manifested in linguistic behaviour, since behaviour is epistemically constrained.
2.The naturalistic account shows: understanding a sentence involves grasping the compositional semantic values of its components (fixed by causal-teleological relations), not epistemic confrontation with the specific fact.
3.This grasp is manifested in inference behaviour, acceptance of excluded-middle instances, and understanding of how evidence would bear on the sentence — all available without epistemic access to the specific past event.
∴Dummett’s manifestation argument fails against the naturalistic account: understanding consists in compositional semantic competence, not epistemic confrontation with the specific fact, and this competence is manifested in standard linguistic behaviour.

Mr President,

My contribution to this debate begins not with metaphysics but with the philosophy of language, because I believe that the philosophy of language is the foundation of all philosophy, including metaphysics. The question of what it is for a sentence to be true — and in particular, whether truth can outrun all possible epistemic access — is not a free-standing metaphysical question. It is constrained by what a satisfactory theory of meaning can and cannot say. And I want to argue that a satisfactory theory of meaning cannot assign evidence-transcendent truth conditions to sentences, and therefore that the realist’s conception of truth is incoherent.

“An aspect of our use of language that cannot in principle be correlated with any observable linguistic behaviour can play no role in a proper theory of meaning. If a speaker’s grasp of an evidence-transcendent truth condition cannot be manifested in anything she does with the sentence, then that grasp is not a real cognitive state but a philosopher’s fiction.”
i. the manifestation argument

Manifestation argument — understanding must be manifested in useA theory of meaning, I maintain, must conform to the Wittgensteinian principle that meaning is use: there can be no element of linguistic understanding that is not in principle manifestable in observable behaviour. This is not a behaviourist claim about the reduction of mental states to behaviour. It is a methodological claim about what a theory of meaning must account for: the public, communicable, learnable character of linguistic understanding. If we say that a speaker “understands” a sentence with an evidence-transcendent truth condition — a condition she could never in principle recognise as obtaining — then there is nothing in her behaviour that could distinguish her from a speaker who associates no truth condition with the sentence at all. The grasp of an evidence-transcendent truth condition is behaviourally inert. And behaviourally inert cognitive states are no cognitive states at all: they play no role in the explanation of action, utterance, or inference. The realist’s evidence-transcendent truth conditions are empty posits.

ii. the acquisition argument

Acquisition argument — realist meaning cannot be learnedA companion argument concerns the acquisition of linguistic competence. We learn the meanings of sentences through a process of exposure to their use in a public linguistic practice. We learn to use sentences by learning in what conditions they are appropriately asserted, what inferences they license, and how they interact with other sentences. If the meaning of a sentence were constituted by an evidence-transcendent truth condition — a condition that in principle outstrips any evidence we could have — then this condition could play no role in the learning process. We could not acquire it by observation of use; the use would be the same whether or not the truth condition obtained. The realist must either say that the meaning of sentences is innate — an implausible rationalism — or that the meaning is acquired from use without being determined by it — which makes meaning mystically unconstrained by anything learnable.

iii. bivalence fails for undecidable sentences

Bivalence — fails for undecidable sentences; classical logic should be revisedThe realist’s commitment to bivalence — the principle that every sentence is either true or false, regardless of whether we can determine which — fails for what I call “disputed class” sentences: sentences about inaccessible regions of the past, about unsurveyable mathematical totalities, about other minds. These sentences cannot in principle be verified or falsified. The realist says they are nevertheless determinately true or false. But on a meaning-use account of truth, there is nothing that makes them true or false: no recognisable condition obtains that constitutes the truth value. The appropriate response is not to cling to bivalence but to revise our logic: to adopt intuitionistic logic, which allows the assertion of excluded middle only when we have a proof procedure. This is not scepticism. It is semantic honesty about what our theories of meaning can coherently commit us to.

iv. answering papineau’s compositionality reply

Against compositionality reply — semantic values must themselves be graspablePapineau argues that a speaker manifests grasp of evidence-transcendent truth conditions by grasping the compositional semantic values of the sentence’s components, not by epistemic confrontation with the specific fact. But this reply merely relocates the problem. The semantic values of the sentence’s component expressions — the reference of “Socrates,” the extension of “sneezed” — are themselves either epistemically accessible or not. If the reference of “Socrates” includes its past-temporal instances — Socrates as he was on the night before the hemlock — then grasping that reference requires grasping something that outruns epistemic access, and the manifestation problem reappears at the sub-sentential level. The compositionality reply is a solution only if compositional semantic values are themselves epistemically accessible; but for undecidable sentences, they are not.

Mr President,

I want to press against realism at a different level from Dummett — not at the level of the theory of meaning but at the level of the theory of reference. Even if we grant, for the sake of argument, that a realist notion of truth is coherent — which I dispute — a further problem immediately presents itself. How does our language, or our thought, succeed in referring to mind-independent objects at all? The realist owes us a theory of reference: an account of what determines that our words pick out the specific mind-independent entities they are supposed to pick out. And I want to argue that no such theory is available without circularity, and that this is what my model-theoretic argument establishes.

The Model-Theoretic Argument (Putnam) Suppose our theory T is empirically ideal: consistent with all possible evidence. Löwenheim-Skolem type results entail that any consistent theory has models of arbitrarily large or small cardinality. If any model satisfies T, there are infinitely many models that satisfy it, with different objects playing the roles of “cat,” “mat,” “electron,” etc. What determines which model is the “intended” interpretation? The realist says: facts about causal or natural relations. But this is “just more theory” — it too admits multiple models, and the problem reappears at the second level.
i. reference is indeterminate under metaphysical realism

Model-theoretic argument — reference indeterminate under metaphysical realismThe model-theoretic argument shows that reference cannot be fixed from a “God’s eye” perspective outside all conceptual schemes. No matter what constraints we add to our theory — operational constraints, theoretical constraints, causal constraints — there will be multiple models satisfying all those constraints, with different objects playing the role of the theory’s terms. If there is no further fact — accessible from outside the theory — that fixes which model is intended, then reference is indeterminate. And since meaning determines reference, meaning is indeterminate too. The metaphysical realist thinks there is such a further fact: the “real” referent is fixed by mind-independent reality. But what is the mechanism by which this fixing occurs? The realist has no answer that does not invoke another theory susceptible to the same model-theoretic attack.

ii. the brain-in-a-vat argument against metaphysical realism

Brain in a vat — metaphysical realism implies radical sceptical scenariosMetaphysical realism makes possible a form of radical scepticism that I argue is self-defeating. If the meaning of our words is determined by mind-independent reality — not by anything internal to our conceptual practice — then it is logically possible that our words refer to a radically different mind-independent reality than the one we take them to refer to. We might be brains in a vat, systematically deceived. The metaphysical realist must allow this as a genuine possibility, because she holds that there is a gap between how we conceive reality and how reality really is. But I argue that the brain-in-a-vat hypothesis is semantically self-undermining. If we were brains in a vat, our word “vat” would not refer to vats in the real world but to whatever feature of our vat-induced experience plays the role of “vat” in our vocabulary. The hypothesis, if true, would ensure that we could not successfully entertain it. Metaphysical realism generates sceptical scenarios that cannot be coherently entertained.

iii. internal realism as the alternative

Internal realism — truth within a conceptual scheme; no God’s-eye viewMy positive alternative is internal realism: the view that truth is what an epistemically ideal theory would assert. There is no God’s-eye view from outside all conceptual schemes that determines which mind-independent facts our sentences correspond to. Truth is what would be warranted under epistemically ideal conditions — conditions of maximal information, coherence, and rationality. This is not a simple verificationism: it does not reduce truth to what we currently can verify. It distinguishes between truth and current warranted assertibility by relativising truth to epistemically ideal conditions. But it does not allow truth to transcend epistemic accessibility in principle: there is no truth that even an epistemically ideal agent could not in principle recognise. Internal realism thus preserves what is valuable in our talk of truth without incurring the incoherences of metaphysical realism.

iv. answering devitt: causal reference is “just more theory”

Just more theory — Devitt’s causal facts are themselves model-theoretically vulnerableDevitt argues that the realist can appeal to metaphysical facts about causal relations to fix reference, and that this is not a theory susceptible to model-theoretic attack. I reject this. The causal relations that Devitt invokes are themselves described in a theory: a theory of causation, of natural kinds, of physical properties. This theory, like any other, admits multiple models. In one model, the causal relation between my “water” uses and the liquid in my environment fixes reference to H2O; in another model of the causal theory, the same uses fix reference to something else. The facts about causal relations that are supposed to fix reference are themselves interpreted within a model. Devitt’s appeal to metaphysical facts about causation does not escape the model-theoretic problem; it simply relocates it to a higher-order theory.

1.Internal realism: truth is what would be warranted under epistemically ideal conditions — maximal information, coherence, and rationality.
2.This distinguishes truth from current warranted assertibility (truth can diverge from current best opinion) while keeping truth epistemically accessible in principle (no truth is in principle unrecognisable).

∴Internal realism preserves a substantive notion of truth (non-deflationary, sensitive to evidence, distinguishable from current warranted assertibility) while avoiding the incoherences of metaphysical realism’s evidence-transcendent truth.

Mr President,

I approach this debate differently from Dummett and Putnam, and I want to register my distance from both realism and global antirealism at the outset. I believe the classical framing of the debate — either there is mind-independent truth or there is not — is too coarse to capture what is actually at stake in different domains of discourse. In Truth and Objectivity I propose a new framework: rather than asking whether truth is mind-independent in general, we should ask whether, in each specific domain, truth has the features that would distinguish a genuinely realist from a merely minimally truth-apt discourse.

“The question is not: ‘Is there mind-independent truth?’ as if this admitted a single yes or no. The question is: for a given discourse — science, ethics, comedy, mathematics — does its truth exhibit the marks of genuine objectivity? And different discourses may answer differently.”
i. minimal truth and the rejection of deflationism

Minimal truth — richer than deflationism, thinner than realist correspondenceMy starting point is a notion of minimal truth: any predicate that satisfies the disquotational schema (“P” is true iff P), whose norm of use coincides with warranted assertibility, and which potentially diverges in extension from warranted assertibility, qualifies as a truth predicate. This is richer than deflationism — it allows that truth and warranted assertibility can come apart, so truth is not simply reducible to what we are currently entitled to assert — but thinner than the realist’s correspondence theory, which requires mind-independent facts as truthmakers. The key question for any domain of discourse is then: does its truth predicate have only minimal properties, or does it have richer properties that would make realism about that domain defensible? This is a domain-by-domain question, not a global one.

ii. superassertibility: a minimum truth standard

Superassertibility — truth as ideally warranted assertibilityThe central concept of my analysis is superassertibility: a statement is superassertible if it is warranted by some accessible state of information S and remains warranted under any improvement of S. Superassertibility is the minimal truth standard: it requires only that we could, in principle, acquire warrant for the statement and that this warrant would survive any further enquiry. For some domains — perhaps comedy, perhaps basic observation — superassertibility may be all there is to truth: a statement is true just in case it would be recognised as true by any suitably equipped, ideally informed enquirer. For science, by contrast, the question is whether truth transcends even ideal enquiry — whether there are facts that no possible enquiry would reveal. This is where genuine scientific realism is at issue, and I am sceptical that we can establish it.

iii. cognitive command: a test for realism

Cognitive Command — disagreement must be due to error for realism to holdI propose Cognitive Command as a criterion for realism about a domain: a discourse exhibits Cognitive Command if every dispute within it is such that at least one party must be in error — suffering from bad evidence, reasoning failures, or failure to appreciate relevant facts. If a domain exhibits Cognitive Command, then the disagreements that arise within it are disagreements about facts that are what they are independently of our responses to them. If it does not — if disputes within it can persist between fully informed, fully rational parties without any party being in error — then the domain is not genuinely realist. My claim is that the Cognitive Command criterion distinguishes genuinely factual domains from domains where “truth” is a projection of our attitudes, without prejudging the answer for any specific domain in advance.

iv. against khlentzos: the correspondence theory is too robust

Against correspondence — Khlentzos’s theory commits realism beyond what we can justifyKhlentzos argues that naturalistic realism requires a correspondence theory of truth grounded in mind-independent facts as structural properties of the world. I find this commitment too robust and, for many domains, unjustifiable. The correspondence theory in Khlentzos’s sense requires that for a sentence to be true, there must exist a mind-independent fact — a relational structure of objects — that the sentence corresponds to. But for domains like mathematics, comedy, and arguably ethics, it is not clear that we have any conception of the mind-independent facts that would serve as correspondents. In mathematics, Khlentzos’s correspondence realism seems to require Platonic objects; in ethics, it requires moral facts. The pluralist approach I favour allows these domains to have genuine truth predicates — predicates with more than merely disquotational content — without committing to the heavy metaphysical machinery of mind-independent structural facts. Realism should be earned by specific arguments for specific domains, not assumed globally in advance.
