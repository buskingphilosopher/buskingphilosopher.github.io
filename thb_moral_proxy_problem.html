Logical Arguments

Mr President,

I want to introduce a distinction that I believe almost everyone in this debate has so far failed to make, and that failure generates most of the confusion in the ethics of AI decision-making. The distinction is between the policy level at which an AI system is programmed — the rules, dispositions, and decision procedures that designers and regulators instil in the system before it is deployed — and the individual-decision level at which the system operates in particular cases. These two levels are governed by different normative considerations and implicate different human agents as moral principals. Confusing them produces both bad theory and bad practice.

The Moral Proxy Problem (Thoma, 2022) Artificial agents that are not themselves moral agents make decisions on behalf of human agents — they act as moral proxies. The Moral Proxy Problem arises because it is often unclear for whom the AI is proxying: for low-level agents (the individual user whose car this is, or the individual patient whose treatment this decides) or for high-level agents (the designer, distributor, or regulator who controls what the system does across millions of deployments). Which answer we give changes the entire structure of the ethical problem — from an individual choice scenario to an expected aggregate policy problem.
i. the self-driving car as the paradigm case

Two levels — self-driving car programming vs. individual crash decisionsConsider how a self-driving car should handle an unavoidable collision scenario — the trolley problem on wheels. Asked at the individual-decision level, the question is: given this specific situation, with these five people on the left and this one person on the right, which way should the car swerve? This framing imports the entire apparatus of individual ethical decision-making: deontological side-constraints, individual rights, act-consequentialist calculation. But this framing is wrong for a crucial structural reason. The car’s programmer does not decide how it should act in this specific situation. They decide how cars of this type should act in situations of this general class. This is a policy decision: an expected-value calculation across the full distribution of such situations, their frequencies, the probabilities of different outcomes, and the aggregate effects on population safety. The ethical evaluation of that policy cannot be read off from the ethical evaluation of any individual case within it.

ii. why the proxy’s identity changes the ethics

Proxy identity — whose perspective structures the decision problemThe proxy question — whether the AI acts as a proxy for the individual user or for a high-level regulator — is not merely a legal question about liability. It determines how the decision problem should be structured. If the car acts as a proxy for its owner, then the car should make decisions as the individual owner would make them: with the owner’s specific risk preferences, the owner’s specific values, and the owner’s specific weighing of passengers against pedestrians. If the car acts as a proxy for a regulator setting population-level road safety policy, then the car should make decisions as that regulator would: weighing expected harm reduction across millions of journeys, factoring in distributional effects, and constrained by what any member of the affected population could reasonably accept. These are structurally different decision problems. The motion’s claim is that the second framing — the policy framing — is the correct one, because the policy is where human moral agency is actually located in AI systems.

iii. the risk aversion dilemma

Risk aversion dilemma — inconsistent human risk attitudes; AI must be programmed to oneMy paper identifies a specific problem that makes the proxy question especially acute. Human agents typically display inconsistent risk attitudes: they are highly risk-averse for small-stakes gambles and much less so for large-stakes ones. Expected utility theory — the standard for rational risk-taking — does not prescribe a specific level of risk aversion; it requires only that risk attitudes be coherent. When programming an AI system, designers must choose a specific risk profile. If they choose to mirror individual users’ inconsistent attitudes, they get an incoherent system. If they rationalise those attitudes to a coherent standard, they are imposing a particular risk preference that may not reflect any individual user’s values. The only way out of this dilemma is to recognise that the AI is not proxying for individual users at all — it is proxying for a high-level agent whose risk profile is determined by public policy considerations about acceptable population-level risk, not by averaging individual preferences.

iv. what distinguishes me from hooker and ord

Thoma vs. Hooker/Ord — proxy structure, not just rule-following or aggregate outcomesHooker argues for policy-level evaluation on rule-consequentialist grounds: policies produce better expected outcomes than individual-decision calculations. Ord argues for it on longtermist grounds: aggregate expected effects across millions of AI decisions swamp any individual-case analysis. Both are consequentialist arguments for the policy level. My argument is structurally prior and ethically more fundamental: it is not that policy-level evaluation produces better consequences. It is that the human agency implicated in AI programming is essentially of the policy type. The programmer does not and cannot decide individual cases; she decides classes of cases. The moral evaluation of her decisions must therefore match the structure of the decisions she actually makes. This is a point about the nature of the decision-making situation, not about which ethical theory produces better outcomes when applied to it.

∴The risk aversion dilemma shows that the individual-proxy framing generates an insoluble programming problem; the high-level policy framing dissolves it by relocating the moral principal to the regulator level.

3.A correct match between decision type and evaluative framework is not a contingent consequence of applying the right framework; it is a precondition for any ethical evaluation of AI programming to be coherent.
∴The structural argument for policy-level evaluation is independent of and prior to any consequentialist argument; it holds even if policy-level evaluation occasionally produces worse individual outcomes.

Mr President,

I want to defend the motion from the perspective of rule-consequentialism — the view that moral evaluation should target the rules and policies that agents follow rather than individual acts. Rule-consequentialism is often mischaracterised as a merely strategic variant of act-consequentialism, a heuristic for producing better act-consequentialist outcomes. In my account, it is not. It is a fundamental claim about where moral evaluation properly belongs: at the level of the code of rules whose general acceptance produces the best expected outcomes. Applied to AI, this claim is not merely strategic but structural, and it dovetails with Thoma’s proxy argument in ways that I think are mutually reinforcing.

“Rule-consequentialism holds that an act is right if and only if it accords with the set of rules whose general acceptance in the relevant community would produce the best expected consequences. This is not act-consequentialism with shortcuts. It is a genuinely different standard — one that sometimes requires performing acts that are not optimal considered in isolation, because the rule requiring them produces better results overall than any alternative rule would.”
i. why rule-following agents outperform act-optimising agents

Rule-consequentialism — rules outperform act-by-act calculation in AI contextAct-consequentialism requires that each decision maximise expected welfare in context. But for AI systems operating at scale, this is not merely practically infeasible — it is self-defeating. An AI car programmed to calculate expected welfare from scratch at every decision point would need to: estimate the identities, preferences, and future welfare trajectories of all potentially affected parties; weigh these against each other according to some interpersonal welfare metric; and arrive at an optimal decision in milliseconds. More fundamentally, an AI known to be maximising expected welfare in every individual decision is an AI whose behaviour is predictable and therefore manipulable. Pedestrians, other drivers, and adversarial actors who know the car will always swerve to minimise expected harm can exploit this predictability in ways that make the expected harm larger. A rule-following car whose rules are public is both more predictable to legitimate actors and less manipulable by adversarial ones.

ii. the rule-consequentialist defence of constraints

Rule-consequentialist constraints — why some act-optimal violations are policy-prohibitedRule-consequentialism explains a phenomenon that pure act-consequentialism cannot: why it is sometimes correct for an AI to follow a rule even when violation would produce a better outcome in a specific case. Consider an AI medical diagnosis system that, in a specific case, could produce a better expected outcome by falsifying a test result to reduce a patient’s anxiety about a condition that is, in this specific case, very likely benign. Act-consequentialism says: falsify. Rule-consequentialism says: do not, because a rule permitting diagnostic AI systems to falsify results under any conditions produces catastrophically bad expected outcomes when generalised, regardless of how well calibrated the individual falsification decision is. The policy-level prohibition is stronger than the act-level calculation, and rightly so.

iii. against macaskill: individual optimisation destroys social trust

Against act-consequentialism — individual optimisation destroys trust in AI systemsMacAskill will argue that maximising expected welfare in every individual decision is what consequentialism requires, and that rule-following is merely instrumental. But this argument ignores the constitutive role of rule-following in producing the social trust that makes AI beneficial at all. An AI medical system that is known to potentially falsify results under some expected-welfare calculation — even a well-calibrated one — is a medical system whose outputs cannot be trusted. The consequentialist benefit of AI systems in medicine, law, and transport depends on their reliability and predictability as rule-followers. This reliability is not merely instrumentally valuable; it is constitutive of what makes these systems beneficial. A rule-consequentialist policy evaluation captures this; act-consequentialist individual evaluation destroys it.

iv. what distinguishes rule-consequentialism from deontology here

Rule-consequentialism vs. deontology — rules justified by outcomes, not intrinsic prohibitionsKamm’s deontological view holds that some individual decisions are intrinsically impermissible regardless of their consequences: the AI car must not use a person as a means even if doing so would save five lives. I agree with the verdict but not the ground. The reason an AI should not be programmed to use individuals as mere means is not that such use is intrinsically prohibited by a constraint that operates at the individual-decision level. It is that a policy permitting such use would, when generalised and adopted across all AI systems of the relevant type, produce catastrophically bad expected outcomes through erosion of social trust, manipulation, and the normalisation of instrumental treatment of persons. The policy is wrong because its consequences are bad; the deontological language is tracking a rule-consequentialist truth.

Thank you.

Argument I — Rule-following outperforms act-optimisation for AI at scale
1.Act-consequentialist AI must calculate expected welfare from scratch in each context; this is computationally infeasible and behaviorally predictable in ways that invite manipulation.
2.An AI known to always swerve to minimise expected harm can be exploited by adversarial actors who manufacture situations in which the swerve is harmful; rule-following is less manipulable.
3.Rule-consequentialism holds that the right policy is the one whose general acceptance produces the best expected outcomes; this is a distinct standard from act-consequentialism, not a heuristic for it.
∴Rule-consequentialism’s policy-level evaluation is required not merely for practical reasons but for structural ones: rule-following produces better expected outcomes than act-optimisation when deployed at scale.
Argument II — Policy prohibitions can override act-level calculations: the diagnostic AI case

2.A rule permitting AI systems to falsify diagnostic results under any expected-welfare calculation produces catastrophically bad expected outcomes when generalised; rule-consequentialism prohibits it.
3.The policy-level prohibition is stronger than the act-level calculation; rule-consequentialism requires following the prohibition even when individual violation would be act-optimal.
∴Rule-consequentialism generates genuine constraints on AI individual decisions that cannot be derived from act-level expected welfare calculations; policy evaluation has independent normative priority.

2.An AI known to potentially deviate from standard behaviour when individual expected welfare calculation supports it cannot be trusted; this defeats the consequentialist case for deploying it.
3.Rule-consequentialist policy evaluation captures the social trust dimension; act-consequentialist individual evaluation destroys it.

Argument IV — Rule-consequentialism vs. deontology: same constraints, different grounds
1.Deontological constraints on AI (e.g. the car must not use persons as means) are justified by rule-consequentialism through the bad aggregate outcomes of policies permitting such use when generalised.

∴Rule-consequentialism and deontology converge on policy-level constraints for AI but diverge on their grounds; rule-consequentialism is more flexible in calibrating those constraints to the actual aggregate stakes.

Mr President,

I want to defend the motion from a longtermist and aggregate-consequentialist perspective. The argument is simple but I think it is devastating for any view that evaluates AI decisions case by case. The decisions made by AI systems are not isolated events — they are instances of policies operating at enormous scale. A single AI system may make millions of decisions per day. A class of AI systems may make trillions of decisions per year. The expected aggregate effects of the policy those systems implement dwarfs the importance of any individual decision within it by so many orders of magnitude that individual-decision evaluation is not merely misleading but morally dangerous.

i. the scale argument

Scale — trillions of AI decisions; policy effects dominate individual effectsConsider autonomous vehicle fleets operating in a major city. A single fleet might make fifty million navigation and risk-allocation decisions per day. Each of these decisions is an instance of the policy programmed by the manufacturer. The question of how to evaluate the ethics of any one of those decisions — whether the car should have swerved left rather than right in this particular near-miss — is essentially irrelevant compared to the question of whether the policy that produced millions of such decisions is the right policy. The aggregate effect of the policy across those millions of decisions, compounded across years of deployment and across all cities where the fleet operates, determines the actual welfare impact. Individual-decision evaluation captures an astronomically small fraction of what matters. Policy evaluation captures almost everything.

ii. the AI safety dimension

AI safety — policy errors compound; individual-decision errors are isolatedFrom an AI safety perspective, policy-level evaluation is not merely appropriate but urgent. A policy error — a systematically wrong decision procedure embedded in an AI system — propagates across every deployment of that system. It is not corrected by individual-decision evaluation because by the time we notice a pattern of bad outcomes, the policy has already made millions of harmful decisions. The difference between a policy that correctly manages risk and one that systematically underweights a specific class of harm — say, pedestrian safety relative to passenger safety — is not visible in any individual decision. It is only visible in aggregate statistics across population-scale deployments. This is why AI safety researchers focus almost exclusively on policy and system design, not on whether any individual decision was optimal. The motion is not just philosophically correct; it is what AI safety demands.

iii. the longtermist perspective

Longtermism — early AI policy decisions lock in paths; individual decisions are reversibleFrom a longtermist perspective, the moral evaluation of AI policy has a further dimension that individual-decision evaluation entirely misses: lock-in effects and path dependence. The policies written into AI systems today are not easily reversible once those systems are widely deployed. They become embedded in infrastructure, legal frameworks, and social expectations. The decision to design self-driving cars to prioritise passengers over pedestrians, or to design credit-scoring AI to weight certain demographic proxies, or to design diagnostic AI to optimise for particular outcome metrics — these are policy decisions whose effects will propagate for decades. Individual-decision evaluation has no resources to capture this temporal dimension. Policy evaluation, by contrast, must consider not only expected aggregate welfare across the current distribution of situations but across the long-run trajectory of deployment and the lock-in effects that policy choices produce.

iv. against danaher: responsibility gaps don’t dissolve the policy question

Against Danaher — responsibility gaps don’t eliminate policy evaluation; they relocate itDanaher argues that AI systems create responsibility gaps that make the motion’s framing inapplicable: if no one is responsible for individual decisions, the question of how to evaluate them is moot. I want to resist this move. Even if individual AI decisions do not attract full moral responsibility to any human agent, the policy decisions that produce those individual decisions clearly do attract responsibility — to designers, regulators, and companies. The responsibility gap, if it exists, is at the individual-decision level. It does not exist at the policy level. Danaher’s argument, properly understood, is therefore not an objection to the motion but a reinforcement of it: if individual decisions lack responsible human agents, that is all the more reason to locate moral evaluation at the policy level where such agents do exist.

Thank you.

Mr President,

The motion asks us to evaluate AI decision-making at the policy level rather than the individual-decision level. I want to argue that this proposal, while it captures something important, ultimately conflates what is strategically useful with what is morally correct — and that this conflation, in the context of AI, is particularly dangerous because it can be used to justify policies that produce bad individual outcomes in the name of aggregate optimality.

“Expected value reasoning tells us that the right act is the one that maximises expected welfare, where welfare is understood as a genuine measure of what matters morally. Policy evaluation, where it matters, is simply a special case of this: the right policy is the one whose expected aggregate welfare is highest. But the fundamental standard is always expected welfare, act by act.”
i. policy evaluation is act evaluation at scale

Act-consequentialism — policy is a derivative concept; act-evaluation is fundamentalThe rule-consequentialist and policy-level arguments in this debate present policy evaluation as if it were a distinct normative standard from act-by-act expected value. But it is not. The right policy is simply the policy whose expected aggregate welfare, summed across all the individual decisions it produces, is highest. Evaluating the policy just is evaluating the aggregate of the individual decisions it produces. There is no additional normative level. The policy level is a derivative concept — it is the act-level expected value calculation applied to a class of acts rather than a single act. Thoma’s structural argument that AI programming is “essentially” a policy decision is true as a practical description; it is not a normative argument for a distinct evaluative standard.

ii. the problem with policy-level evaluation: hidden individual injustices

Hidden injustices — policy optimisation can sacrifice identifiable individualsHere is the most serious objection to policy-level evaluation: it can justify individual decisions that are clearly wrong when evaluated on their own terms, in the name of aggregate policy optimality. Suppose a policy of always swerving left in ambiguous collision scenarios produces fractionally better expected outcomes across the fleet than a more complex decision procedure — but in this specific case, swerving left will certainly kill a child who is identifiably present on the left side. Policy-level evaluation says: swerve left, because the policy is optimal. Individual-decision evaluation says: do not swerve left in this case, because a child will certainly die and this overrides the policy-level calculation. The motion’s framing has no resources to capture this. The child is not an abstraction in a policy calculation; she is a real person with a claim on the AI’s behaviour that the aggregate statistics cannot erase.

iii. the proxy problem is a red herring

Proxy problem — dissolves under expected value reasoningThoma’s moral proxy problem — whether the AI proxies for individual users or for high-level regulators — is philosophically interesting but not decisive. Expected value reasoning cuts through it: the right policy is the one that maximises expected welfare across all affected parties, whether we describe the AI as proxying for users or for regulators. The question of whose perspective structures the decision problem is secondary to the question of whose welfare is affected by the decision. What matters morally is not the identity of the principal but the welfare of the people whose lives are at stake in each individual decision. The proxy framing is a question about institutional accountability, not about the fundamental standard of moral evaluation.

iv. against hooker: rule-consequentialism is unstable

Against rule-consequentialism — collapses into act-consequentialism or into arbitrary rule-worshipHooker’s rule-consequentialism faces the classic instability objection: if a rule produces suboptimal outcomes in some cases, a rule-consequentialist should endorse adding an exception to the rule for those cases. Iterate this process and the rule becomes: “follow the rule unless breaking it produces better consequences, in which case add an exception.” This collapses into act-consequentialism. The only way to avoid this collapse is to refuse to add exceptions — to treat rules as inviolable regardless of their individual-case consequences. But this is not rule-consequentialism; it is arbitrary rule-worship that cannot be justified by its consequentialist foundations. Applied to AI, this means: either the AI should act optimally in every individual decision (act-consequentialism) or it should follow rules even when the outcomes are demonstrably worse (rule-worship). There is no stable intermediate position.

Mr President,

I oppose the motion, but for reasons that are entirely different from MacAskill’s. Where MacAskill says we should evaluate individual AI decisions by their expected welfare, I say that some individual AI decisions are intrinsically impermissible — forbidden by deontological side-constraints that operate at the individual-decision level — and that no policy-level calculation can make them permissible. I also want to argue that the motion, as stated, is not merely wrong but potentially dangerous: it suggests that programming an AI to perform an otherwise impermissible act could be vindicated by appeal to the policy-level goodness of the rule that licenses it.

“The doctrine of double effect does not ask whether the policy of permitting this kind of act produces good consequences in the aggregate. It asks whether, in this specific situation, this specific act involves the agent in using an innocent person as a mere means. No aggregate calculation changes the answer to that question.”
i. deontological constraints operate at the individual-decision level

DDE at individual level — constraints cannot be laundered through policyThe Doctrine of Double Effect, and the broader deontological tradition I work in, identifies constraints on individual acts that cannot be overridden by aggregate considerations. The constraint against using innocent persons as mere means applies to the individual act of using a person in a particular way, in a particular situation. It is not a constraint on policies; it is a constraint on acts. When an autonomous vehicle is programmed to use a pedestrian’s body as a traffic barrier to save passengers, the individual act of doing so is impermissible under DDE — regardless of whether the policy that produces this act is, at the aggregate level, welfare-maximising. The policy-level calculation cannot launder the individual impermissibility. The wrong is done to the specific person used, not to some statistical aggregate.

ii. the loop case applied to AI

Loop case — AI trolley variants show DDE operates at decision levelConsider the AI analogue of the trolley loop case I have analysed elsewhere. A self-driving car can divert onto a loop track; the diverted car will be stopped by a pedestrian’s body, saving five passengers. The policy-level evaluation says: program the car to divert, because this policy saves more lives across the fleet. The individual-decision evaluation under my Principle of Permissible Harm asks: does this specific act use the pedestrian’s body as a means to stopping the car, or is the body’s stopping the car a foreseen side-effect of a legitimate redirection? This is a question about the causal and intentional structure of the specific act — a question the policy-level evaluation cannot ask, because it aggregates across acts rather than examining any individual act’s structure. Deontological evaluation is necessarily individual-decision evaluation; it cannot be translated to the policy level without losing precisely what makes it deontological.

iii. against thoma: the proxy problem does not dissolve individual rights

Against Thoma — proxy identity doesn’t affect individual rights claimsThoma argues that the AI proxies for a high-level regulator setting population-level policy, and that this relocates the ethical decision from the individual to the policy level. But this argument cannot dissolve individual rights claims. When the AI kills a pedestrian, the pedestrian’s right not to be killed as a mere means is not erased by the fact that the decision was made at the policy level by a regulator. Rights claims are claims held by individuals against actions taken against them. They are not claims against policies in the abstract; they are claims against acts. The regulatory framing determines who is responsible for the act; it does not determine whether the act violates a right. Individual-decision evaluation is the only framework capable of asking whether a right was violated; policy evaluation cannot see rights at all.

iv. the aggregation problem in AI context

Aggregation problem — AI policy evaluation aggregates what cannot be aggregated across personsThe aggregation problem I have analysed in the trolley literature applies with full force to AI policy evaluation. Policy evaluation aggregates welfare gains and losses across persons: it says that five lives saved outweigh one life lost in the policy’s expected aggregate. But this aggregation treats persons as mere receptacles for welfare that can be traded against each other. Each person has a claim that cannot simply be outweighed by summing others’ welfare gains. The small gains to many cannot justify imposing large losses on the few, even at the policy level. Scanlon’s contractualism shows why: no person who would be sacrificed by the policy could reasonably accept the policy. This contractualist objection operates at the individual-decision level; it cannot be dissolved by moving the evaluation to the policy level.

Mr President,

I want to oppose the motion by challenging a presupposition that all the other speakers share: that moral evaluation of AI decision-making must be located at one level or the other — either the policy or the individual decision. Both sides of this debate accept this framing. I want to argue that it is the wrong framing, and that understanding why reveals something important about the ethics of AI that both sides miss. My position draws on my work on techno-responsibility gaps: the possibility, sometimes actual and sometimes desirable, that AI decisions are not located within any human agent’s moral responsibility at all — and that this can, in some circumstances, be a feature rather than a bug.

i. the responsibility gap thesis

Responsibility gap — AI decisions can fall outside any human agent’s moral responsibilityAs AI systems become more complex, adaptive, and autonomous, a gap opens between the human decisions made at the policy level and the AI decisions made at the individual level. The programmer who designs a machine-learning system cannot fully specify or anticipate the individual decisions it will make; the decisions emerge from interactions between the trained model and the deployment environment. This gap is not merely legal or institutional. It is moral: when an AI system makes a decision that its programmers could not have foreseen and that no user directed, there may be a genuine sense in which no human agent is fully morally responsible for that specific decision. The motion assumes that moral evaluation must land somewhere — at the policy level if not the individual level. But this assumption may be false. Some AI decisions may not be locatable within any human moral framework at all.

ii. why responsibility gaps can be virtuous

Virtuous gaps — delegation to AI can remove moral burden of tragic choicesMy 2022 paper argues that techno-responsibility gaps can sometimes be morally welcome rather than problematic. Human moral life is pervasively tragic: we frequently face situations in which any choice leaves an ineliminable moral remainder, a residue of wrong-doing that attaches to the agent regardless of what they choose. The surgeon who must triage multiple patients with insufficient resources, the soldier who must choose which building to protect, the driver who must decide which direction to swerve — all face tragic choices whose burden is real and often severe. When AI systems take over these tragic choices, they enable a form of delegation that removes the human moral burden without requiring that it be relocated to a programmer or regulator. The responsibility gap is not a defect to be filled; it is a genuine moral benefit of using autonomous systems for tragic choices.

iii. the motion presupposes a continuous chain of agency

Against the motion — presupposes continuous agency chain from policy to decisionBoth Thoma and Hooker assume that there is a continuous chain of human agency running from the policy decision to the individual AI decision: the programmer decides the policy; the policy produces the individual decision; the individual decision is therefore the programmer’s decision, one level removed. The motion targets the policy because that is where the human decision is. But modern AI systems break this chain. A deep learning system trained on data does not execute a policy in the way a rule-following system does. It develops representations and decision procedures that are not fully interpretable by their creators, and that produce decisions the creators did not anticipate and in some cases do not understand after the fact. The continuous agency chain is a fiction for modern AI. Targeting “the policy” when no clearly specified policy exists is targeting a phantom.

iv. what the debate should actually be about

Correct framing — governance, accountability structures, not moral evaluation of decisionsThe right question for AI ethics is not “at which level should we locate moral evaluation?” but “what governance and accountability structures should surround AI systems whose decisions fall partially or wholly outside human moral agency?” This is a question about institutional design, regulatory frameworks, redress mechanisms, and the conditions under which AI delegation is appropriate — not about the moral evaluation of individual decisions or policies. Thoma’s proxy problem is an important step toward this framing, but her conclusion — that we should evaluate the policy as a high-level agent’s decision — still assumes that the policy is the location of moral agency. For many modern AI systems, even this assumption is too strong. The ethics of AI is more fundamentally an ethics of institutions and governance than an ethics of decisions at any level.
