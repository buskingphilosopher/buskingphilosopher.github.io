Find Out Now Voting Intention Poll — 1st–2nd April 2026

Published April 3, 2026

Methodology and sample

Find Out Now surveyed 2,330 GB adults on 1st and 2nd April 2026, weighted to be nationally representative by gender, age, region, ethnicity, and 2024 general election vote. The headline voting intention figures are drawn from 1,294 respondents who passed the turnout filter — those stating they would "definitely" or "very likely" vote. Respondents who did not vote in 2024 are included only if they say they would "definitely" vote this time. Don't knows are prompted a second time and included if they give a valid vote choice; otherwise excluded. The margin of error means a 9 in 10 chance the true value lies within 4 points, a 2 in 3 chance within 2 points.

One methodological note of particular relevance: Find Out Now recently updated their main voting question to explicitly reference the respondent's own constituency. This brings their approach closer to YouGov's, and the constituency sheet in this dataset confirms the consequence — a 34.4% don't know rate on that question, which is substantially higher than on a simple national voting intention question. The headline figure is the constituency question with that don't-know population squeezed and the turnout filter applied. This is not a flaw; it is a deliberate methodological choice with a defensible rationale. But it means the headline figures are not directly comparable to pollsters using simpler question designs, and any house-to-house comparison should account for this.

Reform UK leads on 25.9%, with the Greens second on 20.0% — a gap of 5.9 points. Labour are third on 15.4%, behind the Greens by 4.6 points. The Conservative party sits between the Greens and Labour on 17.9%.

The most important headline observation is not Reform's lead — it is Labour's position. A governing party polling in third place, behind a party that holds no seats in Westminster outside of Brighton, is without modern precedent in British polling. For context, even at the depths of the Conservative collapse in 2022–23, they never fell below second place in national voting intention polls. Labour's third-place position on this poll is partly a function of Find Out Now's methodology — their constituency prompt and turnout filter tend to compress the major party figures — but it is nonetheless a number that demands explanation rather than dismissal.

The age crossbreak: two parties, one demographic axis

The age crossbreak is the most analytically striking section of this dataset. Reform UK and the Green Party show near-perfect mirror-image age gradients.

Among 18–29 year olds, the Greens lead on 42.1%, with Reform on 10.8%. Among 65–74 year olds, Reform lead on 40.4% with the Greens on 5.0%. This is not a modest skew — it is a 31.3 point Green advantage among the youngest age group and a 35.4 point Reform advantage among the 65–74 bracket. The two parties' lines cross somewhere in the 40–54 age group, where they are within 1.6 points of each other (Reform 19.0%, Greens 20.6%).

The electoral implication is significant. Older voters have historically higher turnout rates. In the 2024 general election, turnout among over-65s was approximately 20 percentage points higher than among under-25s. If that differential is replicated at the next election, the Greens' enormous lead among younger voters is partially discounted at the ballot box, and Reform's strength among older cohorts is amplified. The polling cannot tell us whether this turnout differential will hold, or whether the political mobilisation of younger voters by the Greens represents a genuine structural shift. It is the right question to hold in mind when reading every subsequent poll.

Labour's age profile is less dramatic but analytically revealing: they poll at 18.7% among 18–29 year olds and 10.0% among those 75 and over. They have lost the young to the Greens and the old to Reform and the Conservatives. Their remaining support is concentrated in the 30–39 group (23.7%) — a cohort that skews urban, graduate, and economically anxious. That is not the coalition that wins elections under first-past-the-post.

The gender gap

Reform UK's 8.6 point gender gap — 29.8% among men, 21.2% among women — is the largest in this dataset and consistent with what social science research tells us about populist right parties across comparable democracies. In France, Germany, the Netherlands, and Sweden, right-populist parties consistently over-perform with men by margins of 6–12 points. The gender gap on this poll is therefore not anomalous — it is structurally expected, and its persistence across successive British polls suggests it is not methodological noise.

The Conservative gender pattern has reversed: they now poll 5.1 points higher among women than men (20.7% vs 15.6%). This reversal from the historical pattern — Conservative men outnumbering Conservative women — is one of the more quietly remarkable findings in recent British polling and has received insufficient analytical attention.

The Greens show a female skew of 5.0 points (22.7% women, 17.7% men), which, combined with their youth skew, defines their demographic coalition with some precision: young, female, and — given their retention rate of 84.3% among 2024 Green voters — increasingly stable within that coalition.

The vote flow matrix: what the retention rates tell us

The 2024 GE vote crossbreak allows us to construct a picture of where each party's current support has come from. The key figures are retention rates — what proportion of each party's 2024 voters are still with them.

- Green Party: 84.3% retention — the highest of any party in this dataset

- Reform UK: 82.6% retention — a very high figure for a party whose headline number has fallen from a peak of around 36%

- Conservative: 63.5% retention

- Liberal Democrats: 55.8% retention

- Labour: 46.1% retention — the lowest of any major party

Labour's 46.1% retention rate is the single most consequential number in this poll. It means that of those who voted Labour in July 2024, fewer than half currently intend to do so again. Their lost voters have distributed as follows: 30.1% to the Greens, 8.1% to Reform, 7.4% to the Liberal Democrats, 4.2% to the Conservatives, and 17% into don't know or wouldn't vote.

This pattern is analytically important for what it rules out as well as what it confirms. Labour's vote loss is not primarily a rightward defection to Reform — only 8.1% of their 2024 voters have moved there. The dominant flow is leftward, to the Greens, who are drawing from Labour, the Liberal Democrats, and 2024 non-voters simultaneously. This is a more complex electoral problem for Labour than a simple right-wing insurgency, because the policy remedies for stemming Green defection are different from — and in some cases directly opposed to — those for stemming Reform defection.

Reform's vote flow is equally interesting. Their current support draws 82.6% from 2024 Reform voters, 26.3% from 2024 Conservatives, and — critically — 39.9% from 2024 non-voters. That non-voter component is the most methodologically uncertain element of their headline figure. Find Out Now filters non-voters harshly, including only those who say they would "definitely" vote this time. Even so, the question of whether people who did not vote in 2024 will actually turn out at the next election remains unanswerable from polling data alone. If that 39.9% component behaves as 2024 non-voters historically have and stays home, Reform's true vote share at an election would be materially lower than this poll suggests.

The constituency question: the methodological seam

The Voting sheet in this dataset records responses to a question explicitly framed around the respondent's own constituency, before any turnout filtering or don't-know squeezing. The figures it produces are substantially lower for all parties, and the don't-know rate is 34.4%.

On the constituency question: Reform UK 16.7%, Conservative 10.7%, Green Party 13.5%, Labour 9.4%, Liberal Democrats 5.9%.

The gap between these constituency figures and the headline figures — Reform drops 9.2 points, the Greens drop 6.5 points, Labour drops 6.0 points — is the clearest illustration of what the YouGov-Farage dispute was about. When people think about their actual local contest rather than their abstract national preference, a large fraction moves to don't know. The headline figure is that don't-know mass redistributed according to turnout likelihood and stated voting preference. Whether that redistribution captures reality better than simply leaving them as don't knows is the unresolved methodological question of this electoral cycle.

What this dataset allows us to say with precision is that the gap between national sentiment and local voting intention is largest for Reform and smallest for the Liberal Democrats. The Lib Dems drop only 4.3 points on the constituency prompt, which is consistent with their long-standing strength in tactical voting scenarios — their supporters tend to know what they are doing locally and commit to it.

The regional picture: one finding demands attention

Reform's regional variation contains one number that warrants specific attention. In the North East, this poll puts Reform on 45.5% — by far their highest regional figure, and 19.6 points above their national headline. The North East has a filtered N of only 50 respondents in this dataset, which means the margin of error on this regional figure is very wide — approximately ±14 points at the 2-in-3 confidence level. The finding should not be read as precise. But it is consistent with what we know from council by-election results in the region and from previous polling, and it points to the North East as a geography where Reform's support is both concentrated and deep in a way that could prove electorally efficient under first-past-the-post.

The South West shows the opposite picture for Reform: 15.8%, their lowest English regional figure. This is Lib Dem territory, and the data confirms it — the Lib Dems poll 18.4% in the South West, their highest regional figure in this poll, while the Greens reach 27.3%, their second-highest figure. The South West is the region where anti-Reform tactical voting between the Lib Dems and Greens would be most straightforwardly decisive, and where the arithmetic most clearly favours coordination.

Directional signals: what has changed

This poll was conducted in the context of continued Reform decline from their peak. Their 25.9% headline here compares with figures of 30–36% seen from various houses at the high-water mark in late 2024 and early 2025. Crucially, their 82.6% retention rate among 2024 Reform voters tells us this decline is not defection by committed Reform supporters — it is the erosion of softer, more marginal support, particularly among 2024 non-voters whose stated intention to turn out was always the most uncertain element of Reform's poll position.

The Greens at 20.0% on a pollster that has not historically been among their most generous is the more surprising finding in this poll. Their 84.3% retention rate — the highest in the dataset — and their 30.1% capture of former Labour voters suggests their current position is not a temporary protest spike but is being consolidated into a more durable coalition. Whether that coalition is geographically concentrated enough to translate into seats remains the critical unanswered question.

Summary assessment

This poll contains six analytically significant findings that go beyond the headline:

1. Labour are third. This is the first finding, and the most politically significant.

2. Reform's decline is soft support softening, not hard support leaving. Their 82.6% retention rate among 2024 voters means the people who committed to Reform in 2024 are still there. What has fallen is the non-voter cohort who said they would turn out for Farage.

3. The Greens are consolidating. An 84.3% retention rate, 30.1% capture of former Labour voters, and 20.0% on a pollster that runs them conservatively points to a party building a durable coalition rather than riding a temporary wave.

4. Labour's vote loss is primarily leftward. 30.1% of their 2024 voters have gone to the Greens. Only 8.1% have gone to Reform. The policy problem is therefore primarily on the left flank.

5. The constituency prompt drops Reform 9.2 points and leaves 34.4% as don't knows. This is the clearest single data point in the ongoing methodological debate about how to read Reform's headline numbers.

6. The North East at 45.5% for Reform, if even partially correct, is the most electorally significant regional finding. Under first-past-the-post, a 45% regional concentration turns votes into seats far more efficiently than a 26% national spread.

The headline — Reform leads by 6 — is the least interesting thing about this poll.
