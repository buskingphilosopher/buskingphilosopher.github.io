London

William Blake — Songs of Experience (1794)

Structure & Context
Form: Four quatrains in common metre — the metre of Protestant hymns, used here against the Church Rhyme: ABAB throughout — regular, locked, inescapable, mirroring the poem's vision of entrapment Voice: First-person witness — the speaker wanders and observes, but is himself inside the city he describes Companion poem: Part of Songs of Experience, the darker counterpart to Songs of Innocence (1789) Context: Written during the French Revolution; Blake was a radical who saw London's institutions — Church, Palace, law — as engines of oppression Central argument: Oppression is not just imposed from outside but internalised — the 'mind-forg'd manacles' are the most important image in the poem

I wander thro' each charter'd street, Near where the charter'd Thames does flow, And mark in every face I meet Marks of weakness, marks of woe. In every cry of every Man, In every Infant's cry of fear, In every voice, in every ban, The mind-forg'd manacles I hear. How the Chimney-sweeper's cry Every black'ning Church appalls; And the hapless Soldier's sigh Runs in blood down Palace walls. But most thro' midnight streets I hear How the youthful Harlot's curse Blasts the new-born Infant's tear, And blights with plagues the Marriage hearse.
