Chair,

The Epicurean argument against the badness of death is superficially compelling: when death has come, there is no one left to be harmed. But I want to suggest that this argument rests on a tacit assumption that is very far from obvious — the assumption that something can be bad for you only if it produces a bad experiential state. Once we reject that assumption, the argument dissolves. And we have good independent reason to reject it.

“If death is an evil, it is the loss of life, rather than the state of being dead, or non-existent, or unconscious, that is objectionable. This asymmetry is important. If it is good to be alive, then it is a misfortune to lose that good — even if there is no one left to feel the loss.”
i. the experiential requirement is false

Experiential requirement — counterexamples show deprivation can harm without experienceConsider a man who is betrayed by his closest friend. The betrayal is kept entirely secret; he never learns of it, never feels different, and dies in cheerful ignorance. Has he been harmed? Our intuition says emphatically yes. His trust was exploited, his relationship was counterfeit, his life contained something deeply bad that he was simply unaware of. If this is right — if the betrayed man is harmed even without any experiential signature of the harm — then the Epicurean’s core premise is false. Harm does not require experience of harm. An objective account of well-being, on which what matters is not just how your life feels from the inside but what it actually contains, makes this result natural. Death is the most extreme instance of a deprivation: the total and permanent elimination of everything good about a life. That it comes without an experiential calling card does not make it less a loss.

ii. the deprivation account

Deprivation account — death harms by removing future goods, not by producing a bad stateMy positive account is this. Death is bad for a person because it deprives them of the goods of life — the pleasures, relationships, achievements, and experiences they would have had if they had continued to live. The harm of death is not a positive bad that death produces; it is the absence of goods that life would have produced. This is entirely consistent with the fact that the dead feel nothing. Consider the analogy of a man born without the capacity to see or hear. His condition is bad, even though he has never experienced sight or hearing and therefore does not miss them as absences. He is deprived of goods he would otherwise have had. Death is a more radical version of the same structure: the permanent elimination of a subject’s capacity to receive any goods whatsoever.

iii. the no-subject problem and how to meet it

No-subject problem — Nagel's response: the misfortune accrues to the ante-mortem personThe opposition’s most powerful objection is the no-subject problem: if there is no one left after death, there is no one to be harmed by it. I want to make a careful distinction. The person who is harmed by death is not the post-mortem non-entity. It is the person who existed, who had projects and relationships and a future in prospect, and who was deprived of all of this. The harm accrues to the person who lived, not to a spectral survivor. When we say death is bad for someone, we are saying something about what the world contains for that person — and the world, after their death, contains nothing for them that life would have contained. The subject of the deprivation is the ante-mortem person: the being who existed and whose future was cancelled. That the cancellation is later than the subject does not mean the subject is not its locus.

iv. the asymmetry with prenatal non-existence

Asymmetry — prenatal non-existence differs from posthumous non-existence relevantlyLucretius argued that since we do not lament our prenatal non-existence, we should not lament the posthumous kind either. But there is a relevant asymmetry. The individual who dies had a life, had ongoing projects, relationships, and a future continuous with their past. The prenatal non-existent had no such thing. Death cuts off a life in progress; non-birth never begins one. The relevant comparison is not between two stretches of non-existence but between two conditions of the same existing person: having more of the life that is already in progress versus having it terminated. The person who is deprived of future life is an existing person; there is no existing person who is deprived of prenatal life. The asymmetry is real and the symmetry argument fails.

Thank you.

Argument I — The experiential requirement is false: betrayal harms the unknowing victim
1.A man secretly betrayed by his closest friend, who never learns of it and dies in cheerful ignorance, has nonetheless been harmed: his trust was exploited and his life contained something genuinely bad.
2.If harm can occur without any experiential signature — as in the betrayal case — then the Epicurean premise that harm requires experience of harm is false.
3.Death is the extreme case of a deprivation that carries no experiential signature; the absence of experience at death is not evidence that death is not a harm, any more than the betrayed man’s ignorance shows he was not harmed.
∴The Epicurean’s foundational premise — harm requires experience of harm — is refuted by the betrayal case; once rejected, the inference from “death is not experienced” to “death is not a harm” collapses.
Argument II — Deprivation account: death harms by removing future goods, not by adding a bad state
1.Death is bad not because it produces a bad experiential state but because it deprives the person of the goods — pleasures, relationships, achievements — they would have had if they had continued to live.
2.Deprivation of goods can constitute harm without experience: a person born blind is harmed by lacking sight even though they never experience the absence as such; death is the maximal version of this structure.
3.The absence of an experiential locus for the harm is a feature of this type of harm — privative harm — not evidence that no harm has occurred.
∴The deprivation account provides a coherent explanation of how death harms without producing a bad state; the Epicurean argument fails because it assumes that all harm must be of the experiential type.
Argument III — The no-subject problem: the subject of harm is the ante-mortem person
1.The Epicurean objection: there is no subject left after death to be harmed, so death cannot be bad for anyone. But we need not identify the subject of harm with a post-mortem entity.
2.The subject of the deprivation is the ante-mortem person: the being who existed, who had projects and a future in prospect, and whose future was cancelled. The harm accrues to this person.
3.That the cancellation occurs after the person’s life does not mean the person was not its subject; misfortunes can be identified with the person whose prospects they eliminate, not with a post-mortem survivor.
∴The no-subject problem dissolves once we identify the subject of deprivation as the ante-mortem person; there is a subject of harm, and it is the person whose future life was eliminated by death.
Argument IV — The asymmetry with prenatal non-existence: existing persons differ from non-existent ones
1.Death cuts off a life already in progress — a continuous life with established projects, relationships, and a future. Prenatal non-existence never starts such a life.
2.The relevant comparison for death is between more of an existing life and its termination; the relevant “comparison” for prenatal non-existence is between an existing person and a merely possible non-existent one — and no existing person is deprived of prenatal life.
3.The asymmetry is real: there is an existing subject who is deprived by death and no existing subject who is deprived by non-birth; the Lucretian symmetry argument treats these two cases as equivalent when they are structurally different.
∴The prenatal/posthumous symmetry argument fails because the two cases are structurally asymmetric: an existing life is curtailed by death; no existing life is delayed by non-birth; this asymmetry defeats Lucretius’s argument.

Chair,

I want to press on what I take to be the hardest problem for the deprivation account — not the no-subject problem, which Nagel has answered, but the timing problem. And I want to show that this problem, though genuinely difficult, does not defeat the deprivation account but rather reveals something important about the category of harm we are dealing with.

The timing problem — stated precisely If death is bad for the person who dies, it must be bad at some time. There are four candidates: (i) before death, when the person is alive and has not yet died; (ii) during death, which is at most an instant; (iii) after death, when the person does not exist; (iv) no time at all. (i) seems wrong because the harm hasn’t happened yet; (iii) seems wrong because there is no one there to be harmed; (ii) is trivially brief; (iv) seems to commit us to timeless facts that many find metaphysically uncomfortable. This is the puzzle that Kagan addresses with the “eternal misfortune” and “rational regret” solutions.
i. why the timing problem does not refute the deprivation account

Timing problem — solution: some facts obtain eternally, not at particular timesThe timing problem assumes that every genuine fact must be located at a particular time. But this assumption is far less secure than it appears. Consider: it is true that Julius Caesar was stabbed 23 times on the Ides of March 44 BCE. When is this fact true? It was true in 44 BCE, it was true yesterday, it is true now, it will be true in a thousand years. The fact is not located at a time; it obtains eternally, timelessly. The fact that death is bad for you is similarly a fact that obtains eternally — it was always true, and will always be true, that your death deprived you of goods you would otherwise have had. The timing problem assumes a presentism about facts that we have no good reason to accept. Once we reject it, the problem dissolves: the badness of death is an eternally obtaining fact about the person’s life as a whole, not a time-indexed state someone is in.

ii. what death is bad relative to: the counterfactual comparison

Counterfactual — death is bad relative to the life that would have continuedThe deprivation account compares two possibilities: the life you actually had, which was ended by death at a particular time, and the life you would have had if you had not died then. The gap between them — the goods that the second life would have contained and the first did not — is the measure of death’s badness. This comparison is entirely coherent. We make such comparisons routinely: losing your job is bad because you would have had income, satisfaction, and colleagues if you had kept it. The fact that you do not experience the difference — after the job loss, you simply do not have those goods — does not mean the comparison is not real. Death is the job loss writ permanent and total: the elimination of every future good relative to the life you would have had.

iii. the bill gates problem and what makes death specifically bad

Bill Gates problem — not all deprivations are harms; death is special because of rational regretHere is a puzzle: Bill Gates did not write you a cheque for a million dollars today. You are thereby deprived of a million dollars you would have had if he had. But it seems silly to say you have been harmed by this. If deprivation alone is sufficient for harm, shouldn’t this be a harm too? The answer is that not all deprivations are harms. What distinguishes the harmful ones is not merely the size of the gap but something about the rational structure of the person’s situation. A helpful test: it is rational to regret your death in advance, from within your life, even now, because you have an ongoing stake in your future. The goods you will be deprived of are goods you are oriented toward — goals, relationships, projects — in a way that makes their elimination a genuine loss from the inside of your life, not merely a cosmic deprivation of something that was never yours to lose. The million dollars you never expected is different in this respect.

iv. the epicurean cure and why we should resist it

Epicurean cure — why Epicurean equanimity is the wrong response to deathEpicurus offered his argument as a cure for the fear of death: if death is nothing to us, we need not fear it. I want to suggest that even if we should not fear death in all the ways we do, the Epicurean cure is too drastic. The reason death is the supreme misfortune — as most of us recognise it to be — is precisely that it eliminates a person who is in the midst of something: a life with ongoing stakes. The appropriate response to this is not Epicurean equanimity but a kind of sober acknowledgement of what is at stake. If we genuinely believed the Epicurean conclusion, we would treat murder as trivially different from robbery, a life-threatening disease as no worse than a minor one, and the premature death of a young person as no more regrettable than the death of one who has lived fully. These consequences are not available to us, and the fact that they follow from the Epicurean view is itself evidence against it.

Thank you.

Argument I — The timing problem dissolves if we reject presentism about facts
1.The timing problem assumes every genuine fact must be located at a particular time. But many facts obtain eternally: that Caesar was stabbed 23 times on the Ides of March is true now, was true then, and will be true always.
2.The badness of death is similarly an eternally obtaining fact about the person’s life as a whole: it was always true that this person’s death deprived them of goods they would otherwise have had.
3.The demand to locate the badness of death at a particular time assumes a presentism about facts that we have no independent reason to accept; once rejected, the timing problem does not arise.
∴The timing problem assumes presentism about facts; presentism is not well-motivated; once rejected, the badness of death is an eternally true fact about the person’s life and the problem dissolves.
Argument II — The counterfactual comparison is coherent and routinely made
1.The deprivation account compares the life actually had with the life that would have continued; the gap — the goods absent from the shorter life — measures death’s badness. This is a coherent comparison.
2.We routinely make such comparisons in other domains: losing a job is bad relative to keeping it; an illness is bad relative to health; these comparisons are neither mysterious nor controversial.
3.The fact that the person does not experience the gap — after death they simply do not have the goods — does not make the comparison incoherent; it is a feature of privative harm, as in the betrayal and blindness cases.
∴The counterfactual comparison at the heart of the deprivation account is coherent, routinely made in other domains, and not undermined by the absence of experiential awareness of the gap.
Argument III — Death is a harm, not merely a deprivation: rational regret distinguishes the two
1.Not all deprivations are harms: Bill Gates’s failure to write me a cheque deprives me of money I would have had if he had, but this is not a harm. Some further condition beyond mere deprivation is needed.
2.Death meets the further condition: it eliminates goods the person is actively oriented toward — goals, relationships, projects in progress — making their elimination a rational object of regret from within the ongoing life, not merely a cosmic shortfall of possible goods.
3.The structure of rational regret — the fact that anticipating death rationally involves grief at what will be lost, not merely at a possible good that might not materialise — marks the difference between harmful deprivation and merely missing out.
∴Death meets the conditions for harmful deprivation that arbitrary non-receipt does not; the rational regret criterion distinguishes them and vindicates the deprivation account against the “Bill Gates” objection.
Argument IV — The Epicurean cure has consequences we cannot accept: murder, disease, premature death
1.If Epicurus is right that death is nothing to us, murder is only trivially worse than robbery, a life-threatening disease is no worse than a minor one, and the premature death of a young person with decades ahead is no more regrettable than the death of one who has lived fully.
2.These consequences are not available to us: they conflict with the most deeply held and practically necessary moral judgements we make about the value of life, the wrongness of killing, and the tragedy of early death.
3.When a philosophical argument has consequences of this kind, the appropriate response is to reject a premise rather than accept the consequences; the Epicurean premise — that harm requires experiential badness — is the premise to reject.
∴The Epicurean conclusion has morally unacceptable consequences that we cannot in practice endorse; this is strong evidence by modus tollens that the Epicurean premise is false and that death can indeed be bad for the one who dies.

Chair,

I want to sharpen the deprivation account by giving it a precise comparative possible-worlds formulation that I believe answers all four puzzles the opposition will raise: the no-subject problem, the timing problem, the Lucretian symmetry problem, and what I call the “Intrinsic Value Problem” — the question of whether death has intrinsic or merely extrinsic badness. On each, the comparative account gives a principled and philosophically defensible answer.

“The correct formulation of the deprivation account is this: death is bad for a person at a world if and only if the closest possible world in which that person does not die then is better for that person than the actual world. Puzzles about timing, subjecthood, and comparison are dissolved by this formulation because the comparison is between the actual world and a nearby counterfactual one, assessed from the eternal standpoint of the person’s overall welfare.”
i. the comparative possible-worlds formulation

Possible-worlds account — comparison between actual and closest non-death worldConsider two possible worlds. In W1 — the actual world — you die at forty-two of a heart attack. In W2 — the closest world in which you do not die then — you live to eighty-seven, enjoy decades of further work and relationships, and die peacefully. W1 is worse for you than W2: the total well-being across your life in W1 is lower than the total in W2. Death in W1 is therefore bad for you: it is the event responsible for the fact that you live in a world worse for you than the nearest alternative. Notice that this formulation sidesteps the timing problem entirely. We are not asking when death is bad; we are asking whether it is bad, and the answer is determined by a comparison between worlds, not by locating a time-indexed experiential state in one of them. The comparison is made from a God’s-eye perspective on the person’s life as a whole.

ii. the eternal misfortune thesis

Eternal misfortune — it was always true, and will always be, that this death was bad for this personIf the comparative account is correct, then the badness of your early death is an eternal fact. It was true on the day you were born that your death at forty-two would be bad for you. It is true now. It will be true after you die. The fact does not become true at the moment of death; it was always true, in the same way that it was always true that Keats would die before completing the poetry he had the capacity to write. This “eternal misfortune thesis” strikes some people as strange — how can something be a misfortune before it has happened? But I submit that the strangeness dissolves when we recognise that we are making a comparison between worlds, not locating an experiential state. The comparison between the world Keats actually inhabited and the nearest world in which he lived longer is always valid, and always yields the verdict that his early death was bad for him.

iii. answering the lucretian symmetry problem

Lucretian symmetry — the possible-worlds account distinguishes late birth from early death asymmetricallyLucretius argued that late birth (not having been born earlier) should be as bad as early death, if the deprivation account is correct — since both deprive you of goods you would have had in the relevant counterfactual world. But the possible-worlds account resolves this asymmetry. The closest world in which you are born earlier is not simply a world where you begin slightly sooner; it is a world where a numerically different person is born, because the identity of a person is determined by the specific circumstances of their conception. The sperm and egg that produced you could not have united earlier and produced you; they would have produced someone else. There is no close possible world in which you are born earlier. But there are close possible worlds in which you, the same person, live longer. The asymmetry is principled, not ad hoc.

iv. the intrinsic value problem and extrinsic badness

Extrinsic badness — death bad instrumentally by causing absence of goods, not by containing a bad stateThe opposition sometimes argues that death cannot be bad for you because it does not intrinsically contain anything bad — no pain, no suffering, no negative experiential state. The possible-worlds account accepts this: death is not intrinsically bad. It is extrinsically bad — bad because of what it causes, which is the absence of all further goods. This is not a concession; it is a feature. Many things are bad not because of what they positively contain but because of what they prevent. A roadblock on the only route to the hospital is bad not because of any intrinsic feature of the roadblock, but because of what it prevents. Death is the ultimate roadblock: it prevents the person from receiving any further goods whatsoever. Its badness is extrinsic but no less real for that.

Thank you.

Argument I — Possible-worlds formulation: death is bad when the closest non-death world is better for you
1.Death is bad for a person at a world if and only if the closest possible world in which that person does not die then is better for that person overall. This formulation is precise and avoids reference to time-indexed experiential states.
2.The comparison between actual and counterfactual worlds is made from the standpoint of the person’s total welfare across their life, not from a time-indexed point of view — so the timing problem does not arise within this formulation.
3.This formulation is a natural generalisation of comparisons we already make: every misfortune is bad because it puts us in a worse world than the closest world where it did not occur.
∴The possible-worlds formulation of the deprivation account is precise, free from the timing problem, and a natural generalisation of comparative reasoning we already endorse in other domains.
Argument II — Eternal misfortune: it was always true that early death was bad for the person who dies early
1.The comparison between actual and counterfactual worlds is timelessly valid; it was true on the day of your birth, and will be true after your death, that dying at forty-two is worse for you than the alternative.
2.This “eternal misfortune thesis” does not require the badness to be located at a particular time; it is a comparative fact about worlds that obtains atemporally, like mathematical truths.
3.The apparent strangeness of pre-mortem misfortune dissolves when we recognise that we are comparing world-states, not describing experiential events; the comparison is as valid before the death as after it.
∴The eternal misfortune thesis follows from the possible-worlds formulation; it is neither strange nor paradoxical, but a consequence of treating the badness of death as a comparative fact about worlds rather than a time-indexed experiential state.
Argument III — Lucretian symmetry resolved: late birth deprives a different person, not you
1.The closest world in which you are born earlier is one in which a numerically different person is born, because personal identity is fixed by the specific circumstances of conception — this is Parfit’s non-identity point applied to birth.
2.There is no close possible world in which you, the same person, are born earlier. There are close possible worlds in which you, the same person, live longer; the comparison for death is available, the comparison for late birth is not.
3.The asymmetry is principled and follows from the metaphysics of personal identity: the person deprived by death exists; no existing person is deprived by late birth.
∴The Lucretian symmetry argument fails because the possible-worlds comparison is available for death (there is a nearby world where you live longer) but not for late birth (there is no nearby world where you are born earlier and are still you).
Argument IV — Extrinsic badness: death bad by causing absence of goods, not by containing a bad state
1.Death does not intrinsically contain anything bad; it is not a painful experience, a negative state, or a positive evil. The opposition is right about this. But intrinsic badness is not the only kind.
2.Death is extrinsically bad: bad because of what it causes, which is the permanent absence of every future good. A roadblock is bad not because of its intrinsic character but because of what it prevents; death is the ultimate roadblock.
3.The Epicurean argument that death is not bad because it contains nothing bad conflates intrinsic with extrinsic badness; the deprivation account is explicitly about extrinsic badness and is not refuted by this conflation.
∴Death’s badness is extrinsic — a function of what it causes rather than what it contains — and this is not a concession to the Epicurean but the correct account of a type of harm that is real and important.

Chair,

I want to make a case for Epicurus that is not the caricature his opponents typically refute. Epicurus did not argue that nothing bad can happen to people. He argued something more specific and more philosophically serious: that the state of being dead — the state of non-existence — cannot be a harm for the person who is dead, because being in a state requires existing, and the dead do not exist. This is a claim about the metaphysics of harm, not about whether bad things ever befall mortals.

“Death is nothing to us, since when we exist death is not present, and when death is present we do not exist. The argument is not that nothing bad happens to people. It is that the condition of being dead is not one that has a subject who can suffer it. Harming requires a subject to be harmed. Non-existence has no subject. This is not sophistry. It is the minimum that a coherent metaphysics of harm requires.” — Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus
i. the subject requirement on harm

Subject requirement — harm is a relational property requiring an existing subjectThe core Epicurean argument: harm is a relational property. To say that X is harmed is to say something about X — a subject that exists and is in a condition that is bad for it. If X does not exist, there is nothing for harm to attach to. The proposition will say that the ante-mortem person is the subject of harm. But this is not coherent as stated. The ante-mortem person existed and then died. After death, there is no subject with properties. You cannot attribute a harm to a person who no longer exists any more than you can attribute a current debt to a company that has been dissolved. The ante-mortem person is gone; you cannot reach across the moment of death and attribute post-mortem conditions to them. The proposition is trading on a grammatical illusion: the fact that we use the same name before and after death to refer to what is in fact two different situations: a person, and then nothing.

ii. why the betrayal analogy fails

Betrayal analogy failure — the betrayed man still exists; the dead man does notNagel’s betrayal case — a man secretly betrayed who never learns of it — is meant to show that harm does not require experiential awareness. I accept this point in the betrayal case. But notice the crucial disanalogy with death. The betrayed man exists throughout his life. The harm attaches to a subject who is present and living. The trust he exercised, the relationship he valued, the projects he pursued — all of these were real features of an existing person’s actual life. The harm of betrayal is a feature of his actual life even if he never registers it experientially. Death is entirely different. After death, there is no subject. The “harm” of death has no subject to attach to. The betrayal case shows that harm can be experientially silent; it does not show that harm can be subjectless. These are quite different claims, and conflating them is the proposition’s central error.

iii. the symmetry argument revived

Symmetry argument — prenatal and posthumous non-existence are metaphysically parallelLucretius’s symmetry argument is more powerful than its critics acknowledge. The proposition’s response — that we cannot be born earlier without being a different person — proves too much. By the same Parfitian logic, we cannot die later without being a different person: the person who lives to eighty-seven is psychologically distinct from the person who would have died at forty-two, had they different experiences and memories over those additional decades. If the non-identity argument defeats late birth, it equally defeats the claim that the same person would have been better off living longer. The proposition cannot use non-identity to block one symmetry while ignoring it in the other. Either personal identity is robust enough to ground both comparisons, or it is not robust enough to ground either.

iv. the experiential requirement and its vindication

Experiential requirement vindicated — welfare is essentially connected to the subject's perspectiveThe experiential requirement on welfare is not an arbitrary add-on. It reflects a deep truth about what it means for something to be good or bad for a subject. When we say something is good for you, we mean it enriches your life as you live it — that it connects to your experience, your perspective, your ongoing existence as a subject. An objective account of welfare that is entirely disconnected from the subject’s perspective loses the “for you” in “good for you.” If the goods you were deprived of by death are goods you will never experience, never be oriented toward, never benefit from in any subjective sense, then in what sense are they goods for you rather than simply good things that will not exist? The “for” in “bad for you” requires a subject; and the subject of death is absent.

Thank you.

Argument I — The subject requirement: harm is relational and requires an existing subject
1.Harm is a relational property: for X to be harmed, X must exist as a subject to which the harm attaches. A company dissolved last year cannot currently be in debt; a dissolved subject cannot currently be harmed.
2.The dead do not exist; therefore there is no subject to which post-mortem harm can attach. The proposition’s “ante-mortem subject” move uses the same name for two situations — a person, then nothing — without bridging the gap in the subject.
3.You cannot attribute a harm to a person who no longer exists by pointing to the person who used to exist; the subject at the time of harm must be present, and at death, no subject is present.
∴The subject requirement on harm is not met by death: there is no subject at or after the moment of death to whom the deprivation attaches; the proposition’s ante-mortem subject move is a grammatical illusion, not a philosophical solution.
Argument II — The betrayal analogy fails: the betrayed man exists; the dead man does not
1.The betrayal case shows that harm can be experientially silent: the harmed person exists and is in a bad condition even without knowing it. This is accepted.
2.But the betrayed man exists throughout his life as a subject; the harm attaches to a present, living subject. After death, the subject no longer exists. This is the critical disanalogy.
3.The betrayal case shows harm can be experientially silent; it does not show harm can be subjectless. Death is subjectless; the analogy therefore fails to license the inference from the betrayal case to the death case.
∴The betrayal analogy is disanalogy: it shows harm can be experientially silent in a living subject, but death is not merely experientially silent — it is subjectless; the proposition needs an analogy where there is no subject, not merely no awareness.
Argument III — Non-identity cuts both ways: you are not the same person who would have lived longer
1.The proposition uses non-identity to defeat Lucretius: you cannot be born earlier and be the same person. But the person who lives to eighty-seven is psychologically distinct from the person who dies at forty-two — they have different experiences, memories, and character over those additional decades.
2.If non-identity defeats the prenatal comparison, it equally destabilises the post-mortem comparison: the proposition cannot use non-identity selectively to block one direction of the symmetry while ignoring it in the other.
3.Either personal identity is robust enough to ground both comparisons — and then the symmetry argument re-applies — or it is not robust enough to ground either — and then the deprivation account loses its subject.
∴The non-identity response to Lucretius is self-defeating: it relies on a thin notion of personal identity that equally undermines the proposition’s claim that the same person would have been better off living longer.
Argument IV — The experiential requirement is vindicated by what “for you” means
1.“Good for you” and “bad for you” are not impersonal value claims; they are claims about how things stand for a subject — how a thing connects to the perspective, experience, and ongoing existence of a particular being.

I want to press on the internal pressures within the proposition’s own account. I am not a simple Epicurean. I think death can be bad for the person who dies in some cases, and not bad in others. My contribution is to show that the deprivation account — as defended by Nagel, Kagan, and Feldman — is over-inclusive: it counts as bad many deaths that we have no reason to consider bad, and it struggles to explain why some deaths are more bad than others in a way that matches our settled moral intuitions. These are internal difficulties that the proposition must address.

The over-inclusion problem — stated The deprivation account, applied consistently, implies: (a) deaths of very elderly people who have lived fully are still bad for them, since there were future goods they could have had; (b) deaths of people in permanent vegetative states are bad for them, since they had potential future goods; (c) deaths of people whose remaining life would have been pure suffering are bad for them, since they were “deprived” of that suffering. These implications sit uneasily with the account’s intent.
i. the problem of death for the very old

Very old — deprivation account struggles to explain why old people's deaths are not badThe deprivation account implies that the death of a ninety-seven-year-old who has lived a rich and full life is still bad for her, since in the nearest possible world where she does not die then, she lives a few more months and thereby receives some additional goods. But our moral intuitions say that such a death — peaceful, late, after a completed life — is not a misfortune in the way that the death of a forty-two-year-old is. There is a genuine moral difference between an untimely death and a timely one. The deprivation account, applied uniformly, cannot explain this difference because it evaluates deaths solely by the quantity of future goods lost, regardless of whether the life was already complete. If the gap between the actual and counterfactual world is smaller, the death is less bad — but the account gives us no principled way to say that any death, no matter how late and peaceful, is not bad at all.

ii. the desire account and what death frustrates

Desire account — death bad when it frustrates actual desires, not merely possible future onesA more refined account of when death is bad focuses not on possible future goods in the abstract but on the specific desires, plans, and projects that are frustrated by death. Death is bad for you when it cuts off things you were actually pursuing, relationships you were actually in, goals you were actually working toward. This desire-based refinement handles the case of the very old differently: if she had no further projects in prospect, no desires oriented toward the future, and had genuinely completed her life, then her death does not frustrate an actual desire. The ninety-seven-year-old and the forty-two-year-old differ in what death takes from them not merely in quantity of possible goods but in the density of their actual engagement with a future that is now cut off.

iii. the problem of welfare and the subject

Welfare problem — possible-worlds account loses contact with the subject's actual welfareFeldman’s possible-worlds account compares the actual world with the nearest alternative and reads off the badness of death from the welfare difference. But welfare is not a property of worlds; it is a property of subjects-in-worlds. A world in which you live longer and receive additional goods is only better for you if those goods connect to you in the right way — if there is a subject, continuous with you, who is actually the recipient of those goods. Feldman’s “God’s eye view” comparison aggregates welfare across the whole life and attributes the difference to death. But this is not obviously the right framework: it can imply that a death which relieves a person of a period of terrible suffering is bad for them — because the aggregate welfare across the actual world is lower than the counterfactual one even when that counterfactual included suffering. This seems like a deficiency in the framework, not a discovery about death.

iv. what this debate actually shows

Conclusion — death is bad in some cases but the deprivation account is incompleteI am inclined to say that death can be bad for the person who dies — but only in specific conditions: when the life was in the midst of something, when projects were active and relationships were live, when the person was psychologically oriented toward a future that is now eliminated. For elderly deaths, deaths of people with no remaining desires, and deaths that relieve suffering, the deprivation account either gives wrong verdicts or gives the right verdict for the wrong reasons. The motion, strictly read — “death can be bad for the person who dies” — is likely true. But the mechanism the proposition offers is over-inclusive, and the Epicurean residue — that the subject requirement is not as easily met as Nagel suggests — remains a genuine problem that the proposition has not fully dissolved.

My position is the most nuanced on this panel: I believe death is sometimes bad for the person who dies, sometimes not, and that how bad it is varies in ways the proposition’s crude deprivation account cannot explain. My time-relative interests account holds that the badness of death is a function of two things: the future goods the death prevents, and the degree of psychological unity that the person who dies has with the future self who would have received those goods. This formulation has consequences that are simultaneously counterintuitive, illuminating, and, I believe, correct.

“The reason a person has a strong egoistic interest in their own future is that they are psychologically continuous with their future self — they share memories, plans, values, and projects. If that continuity is weak or absent, the future self is, in a morally relevant sense, not quite the same person. A newborn infant has almost no psychological connections to the adult it will become. Its death is therefore less bad for it than the death of that adult would be for the adult — not because infant life matters less, but because the degree of self-regarding interest in one’s own future is a function of the psychological connections one has with it.”
i. time-relative interests: why psychological continuity matters

Time-relative interests — badness of death depends on psychological unity with future selfConsider why I care more about my welfare next year than about the welfare of a numerically distinct person who happens to be similar to me. The answer is psychological continuity: I will remember being me, my plans and values will persist, my relationships will continue. There is a robust web of psychological connections between present me and future me that grounds a distinctive kind of self-concern. Now, the degree of this self-concern varies with the strength of psychological continuity. An infant has almost no psychological connections to the adult it will become: no memories, no plans, no shared values. A person with advanced Alzheimer’s disease has few connections to the self they were. If the badness of death for me is partly a function of the degree to which I have a self-regarding interest in my future — and it must be, if psychological continuity is what grounds self-concern — then the badness of death is not uniform. It varies with the psychological architecture of the dying subject.

ii. the implications for the proposition

Implications — death of an infant is less bad than death of adult; deprivation account gets this wrongThe standard deprivation account implies that the death of a healthy infant is the worst possible death: the infant has a potential full human life ahead of it, so the gap between the actual world and the nearest alternative is maximal. But our moral intuitions say otherwise: though the death of an infant is certainly a tragedy, most of us feel that the death of a twenty-five-year-old with established projects, relationships, and a formed personality is worse. The deprivation account, applied to purely possible future goods, cannot explain this difference because it is indifferent to the strength of the dying subject’s psychological connections to the future it is being deprived of. The time-relative interests account explains the difference: the twenty-five-year-old has robust psychological continuity with the future from which they are deprived; the infant has almost none.

iii. the Epicurean residue in the time-relative account

Epicurean residue — time-relative interests partially vindicates Epicurean intuitions about non-personsMy account partially vindicates the Epicurean intuition, though not the Epicurean argument. Epicurus held that death is nothing to us. I hold that death is very little to beings who have very weak psychological connections to their future selves. For creatures with no psychological continuity — those who live entirely in the present, with no memories and no future-directed concerns — death is barely bad at all. This is not because their lives have no value, but because they have no time-relative interest in their own future to be frustrated. The Epicurean who holds that death is nothing to a being entirely without self-concern — a fully present-tense creature — has identified something real, even if the argument for applying this to all mortals fails. Humans with robust psychological continuity are not such creatures; but the intuition that grounded Epicureanism is not without foundation.

iv. what the correct account of the badness of death looks like

Correct account — badness of death = future goods lost × degree of psychological unityThe correct account of when and how much death is bad for the person who dies is a product of two factors: the goods the death prevents (as the deprivation account correctly identifies) and the strength of the psychological connections between the dying person and the future self who would have received those goods (which the pure deprivation account ignores). Death is worst when both factors are high: a young adult in the midst of an engaged and forward-looking life has the most to lose and the strongest psychological connections to the future from which they are being deprived. Death is least bad when either factor is low: in the very old with no remaining forward-looking projects, or in beings with weak psychological continuity. This is a more discriminating and more accurate account than the proposition has offered, and it partially explains why the Epicurean challenge has been so persistent: the pure deprivation account misses an essential dimension of the phenomenon.
