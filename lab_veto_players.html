Why Labour Cannot Remove Starmer—or Keep Him

The Burnham paradox, the blocked by-election, and why a 200-year-old theory of political deadlock explains what Westminster commentators cannot.

28 May 2026

On 24 January, the Labour Party's National Executive Committee voted 8–1 to block Andy Burnham from standing in a parliamentary by-election. The decision was meant to protect Keir Starmer from a leadership challenge. Four months later, Burnham was approved as the party's official candidate for a different seat—the most obvious path to a leadership contest.

This is not a reversal of strategy. It is the precise moment when the strategy revealed itself as impossible.

By mid-May, over 95 Labour MPs had openly called on Starmer to resign. The Health Secretary resigned in protest. Four junior ministers followed. Yet the Prime Minister refused to go. He cannot be forced to resign—the machinery of party democracy doesn't work that way. But he cannot govern either, surrounded by a mutinous backbench. He is trapped, and so is the party.

This is a veto player deadlock. And if you want to understand why Burnham is both blocked and unstoppable, why Starmer can resist 95 MPs, and what happens next, you need to learn to see the Labour Party not as a collection of ambitious individuals, but as a system of people with overlapping but incompatible zones of acceptable outcomes. That is not metaphorical. It is geometrical. And it is teachable in an afternoon.

What is a veto player?

George Tsebelis, a political scientist at the University of California, published a book in 2002 called Veto Players: How Political Institutions Work. It contains one of the most useful ideas in political science, and almost nobody outside academic circles knows it.

A veto player is simple to define: it is anyone whose agreement you need before something can change.

In a marriage, both spouses are veto players over the decision to stay together. In a board of directors, each member is a veto player over any new policy. In Parliament, the government, the opposition, and various procedural committees all act as veto players over different types of legislation. They don't have equal power—a single vote in a 650-seat House is not the same as holding the Prime Ministership. But the structure of their vetoing power is the same.

Tsebelis's central theorem is deceptively simple: the more veto players there are, and the further apart their ideal preferences are, the more difficult it becomes to change anything. The status quo becomes stable not because anyone actively loves it, but because no alternative satisfies all the veto players simultaneously.

In normal times, Labour has one primary veto player: the leader. The leader decides strategy, the party follows (usually), and opposition comes in the form of backbench grumbling that everyone ignores. But when a leader becomes genuinely unpopular—when their approval rating hits −46, when they preside over a party descending from a 172-seat landslide to potential third-place finishes in multiple councils—the structure changes. Suddenly there are many veto players, and they want different things.

What you just saw: the geometry of political impossibility

In the first view, you see six dots: Keir Starmer far to the left (pro-continuity), the National Executive Committee near him, the 95+ rebellious MPs on the right (pro-change), the Labour membership even further right, Wes Streeting slightly to the right (positioning for a leadership run), and Angela Rayner similarly positioned.

In the second view—Acceptable Outcomes—something crucial appears: dashed circles around each dot. These are not decorative. In veto player theory, they represent indifference curves—the set of all possible outcomes that player would prefer to the current disaster (the status quo of permanent crisis).

Notice something: Starmer's circle and the NEC's circle are small and to the left. The circles representing the 95+ MPs and the membership are larger and far to the right. They barely touch.

In the third view—The Deadlock—this becomes visual. The red zone shows where Starmer loyalists would be happy (him staying, or an orderly transition to someone on the left). The green zone shows where the rebels would be happy (him gone, Burnham or someone acceptable in his place). The yellow zone in the middle—where all six circles would overlap—is empty. There is no outcome all six players prefer to the status quo.

This is deadlock. Not because anyone is being stubborn. Because the geometry of their preferences makes agreement mathematically impossible.

Why the NEC veto collapsed—and why Starmer might still survive

For two months, the NEC held firm. It blocked Burnham from Gorton and Denton. The statement said it wanted to avoid "an unnecessary election" for his replacement as mayor of Greater Manchester. The real reason was obvious: if Burnham got back into Parliament, he could challenge Starmer immediately.

But something interesting happened.

The fact that the media was informed of the NEC decision before I was tells you everything you need to know about the way the Labour Party is being run these days.
Andy Burnham, January 2026

By January, barely a month after Starmer's local election disaster, the cost of the veto had become visible. Burnham, the most popular Labour politician by any measure (the only senior figure with positive approval ratings), was being blocked by a backroom committee. The public saw it as cowardice. Westminster saw it as panic. Within two months, when Josh Simons resigned as MP for Makerfield, the NEC reversed course. On 15 May, it approved Burnham's candidacy.

This is Tsebelis's prediction in action: a veto player loses its veto when exercising it becomes visibly more costly than accepting defeat. The NEC didn't want Burnham in Parliament. But keeping him out was becoming more damaging than letting him in.

Yet this should not be read as a sign that Starmer's position is collapsing. In fact, he may survive precisely because the opposition veto players cannot coordinate.

The 95+ MPs want him gone. But they do not want the same successor. Streeting supporters want Streeting. Rayner supporters want Rayner. Burnham's backers—who include the membership, as we'll see—want Burnham. As long as they cannot agree on a single challenger, Starmer's indifference curve (him staying) overlaps with the no-change zone, and the status quo persists despite being genuinely disastrous for the party.

This is a second Tsebelis insight: fragmented opponents can keep a leader in place even when a united opposition could remove them instantly. Starmer survives not because he commands support, but because his enemies cannot agree on who should replace him.

The Borda winner: the outcome no one wants but everyone can tolerate

When no single outcome satisfies all veto players simultaneously—when the all-player winset is empty—political scientists turn to something called the Borda count. Named after an 18th-century French mathematician, it works like this:

Imagine the five possible paths Labour could take: Starmer continues; a managed transition to Burnham; Streeting wins a contested leadership election; Rayner wins; or an immediate forced removal that traumatises the party. Each veto player ranks these five options in order of preference. You award points: 1st choice gets 4 points, 2nd gets 3, 3rd gets 2, 4th gets 1, 5th gets 0. The option with the most total points is the Borda winner—and Tsebelis and his followers have observed that in many real-world negotiations, the Borda winner is what actually happens.

It doesn't win because anyone loves it. It wins because it is the least bad for the most people.

Look at the fourth tab in the diagram above. You see five possible outcomes ranked by all six veto players. The Borda winner is a managed transition to Burnham. Not because the NEC wanted it (they preferred him staying out forever). Not because the membership's preferred outcome is "managed" (they'd rather have seen Burnham challenge immediately). But because it:

  • Gives the 95+ MPs what they want: Starmer gone
  • Gives the membership what they want: Burnham as leader
  • Gives Streeting and Rayner second-choice outcomes (a contested election they can enter)
  • Gives the NEC a controlled process (Burnham gets in via a regular by-election, approved selection, not a coup)
  • Gives Starmer a dignified exit rather than a public meltdown

No one gets their ideal. Everyone gets something they can live with. This is the defining quality of a Borda winner in politics: it is not enthusiasm. It is distributed tolerance.

Why this matters: Prediction markets currently put Burnham at 47.6% to be PM in 2026. Polls show him as voters' preferred replacement (20% first choice, far ahead of Rayner at 5% and Streeting at 3%). Bookmakers have moved Angela Rayner to favourite at 9/4 (suggesting a contested election, which would likely favour Rayner with unions). But Borda count logic predicts the outcome will be closer to what the Burnham camp, the membership, and the pragmatists want: a June by-election victory, followed by a managed handover. The Makerfield by-election is not a parliamentary procedure. It is the winset opening.

Lessons from Labour's deadlock

The conventional reading of Labour's crisis is one of catastrophic leadership failure and personal weakness. Starmer won a 172-seat landslide, squandered political capital immediately through welfare cuts and Gaza war positioning, and now faces removal.

But veto player theory reveals something more structural: Starmer cannot be removed because the machinery that would remove him—multiple factions with incompatible preferences—cannot coordinate simultaneously. And he cannot govern because the rebels are numerous enough to make the party unmanageable without him leaving.

He is trapped in what Tsebelis calls the policy stability paradox: the same fragmentation that makes change difficult also makes continuity impossible.

The second lesson is about the Burnham paradox itself. Why was he blocked in January and approved in May? Not because the party's leadership changed its mind about him (they didn't—they still fear his popularity). But because the cost of blocking him rose above the cost of allowing him in. Veto players lose their vetoes when exercising them becomes more expensive than accepting defeat.

The third lesson is prediction. If Burnham wins Makerfield—which he almost certainly will—and if he then stands in a leadership contest, Borda count logic and structural incentives suggest he will win. Not because he is everyone's first choice. Because he is the least-bad choice for the most factions. The 95+ rebels get him. The membership gets him. Even centrists can accept him (he is not the hard-left choice). The unions prefer him to Rayner going through a messy contested election. The only losers are Starmer loyalists and those betting on Streeting or Rayner—but they are outvoted.

This is not prediction by opinion polling or Westminster gossip. It is prediction by geometry.

What this means for you

You do not need to understand vector algebra or formal game theory to use veto player analysis. You need to ask three questions:

  1. Who has to agree for something to change? (These are your veto players.)
  2. How far apart are their preferences? (The greater the distance, the smaller the winset.)
  3. Is there any outcome all of them prefer to the status quo? (If no, the status quo is locked in.)

Apply this to almost any political deadlock and it clarifies things. It explains why the US House often cannot pass budget bills (too many veto players in the House, Senate, and Presidency with incompatible ideologies). It explains why coalition governments in proportional systems often produce conservative policies (any member can veto change). It explains why reforming the NHS, the civil service, or any entrenched system takes decades (bureaucratic actors, politicians, unions, and the public all have veto power over different elements).

Most importantly, it explains why Starmer is stuck, why Burnham is inevitable, and why neither outcome was ever really in doubt—once you understand the geometry.

The June by-election and beyond

The Makerfield by-election in June will be a formality. Burnham will win. The question then becomes: does a sitting Prime Minister simply step aside to let him take over, or is there a formal leadership contest?

If Starmer's goal is to preserve Labour's unity and the party's dignity, he might resign and allow Burnham to take over by consensus—no contest, no blood. This would be the Borda winner in its purest form: everyone gets something acceptable, no one is humiliated.

If Starmer insists on contesting, he will likely lose to Burnham in the membership vote (Burnham is preferred by 20% vs. Starmer's implied ~3%). But Streeting and Rayner entering the race would split the anti-Starmer vote, potentially allowing a surprise second-round outcome (in Labour's electoral system, if no candidate gets 50% on the first count, the ballot runs to a second round). This is the contested-election scenario that bookmakers currently price at 4/6 odds.

Either way, the geometry points toward the same destination: Andy Burnham as Prime Minister, sometime in the second half of 2026. Not because he is the choice of the powerful, but because he is the least-regretted choice of the most factions. This is how deadlock resolves: not through dramatic last stands, but through the slow geometry of who can no longer afford to veto.
