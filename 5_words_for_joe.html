Ludwig Wittgenstein argued that the meaning of a name is not some fixed essence it points to, but arises from its use within language; in his later work, especially Philosophical Investigations, he rejects the idea that names simply label objects in a one-to-one correspondence, instead suggesting that names function within what he called ‘language games,’ where their meaning depends on context, practice, and shared human activity. So to understand a name is not merely to identify a referent, but to grasp how it operates within a form of life.

Baruch Spinoza presents a strikingly deterministic view on fate: everything that happens follows necessarily from the nature of God or substance, meaning that what we call ‘fate’ is not a mysterious external force but the unfolding of a rational, causal order; human freedom, for Spinoza, does not consist in escaping fate but in understanding necessity, and through this understanding achieving a kind of intellectual love of the inevitable.

But Spinoza also offers a nuanced account of hope, defining it as an inconstant joy arising from the idea of a future or past outcome whose result we doubt. On this view hope is inseparable from fear, since both stem from uncertainty. For Spinoza the ideal is not to live dominated by hope but to cultivate rational understanding that frees us from the emotional oscillation between hopeful anticipation and fearful anxiety.

Søren Kierkegaard has an interesting way of reframing prayer as a transformative inward act, rather than a means of influencing God. On his view, the function of prayer is not to change the divine will but to change the one who prays, deepening their subjective relationship with God and clarifying their own dependence, so that prayer becomes an existential expression of faith rather than a transactional request.

Jean-Paul Sartre then later situated regret within his broader existentialist framework of radical freedom and responsibility. He thought that because humans are condemned to be free, every choice defines who we are. Regret then arises when we recognise that we could have chosen otherwise. Yet Sartre warns against allowing regret to become what he called ‘bad faith,’ a way of denying one’s responsibility, insisting instead that individuals must own their past choices while continuing to project themselves forward through new acts of freedom.
