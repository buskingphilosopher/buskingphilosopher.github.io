Mr President,

We are gathered to debate whether Bayesianism — the view that rational degrees of belief are probabilities, updated by conditionalization — constitutes the correct normative theory of scientific reasoning. I shall argue that it does, and that the alternatives on offer fail to provide a coherent substitute.

The Bayesian framework is deceptively simple in statement. A rational agent's degrees of belief — credences — must satisfy the axioms of probability calculus, and upon learning evidence E, the agent should update by conditionalizing:

P_new(H) = P_old(H | E) = P_old(H ∧ E) / P_old(E)

This is not dogma. It is the unique updating rule that is consistent with both the agent's prior probability function and the formal constraints on rational belief. Let me explain why.

On the Dutch Book argument

The Dutch Book argument for probabilismThe Dutch Book argument — due to Ramsey and de Finetti — establishes that if an agent's credences violate the probability axioms, a clever bookmaker can construct a set of bets that the agent will accept but which guarantee a net loss regardless of how the world turns out. This is a synchronic incoherence: the agent is effectively paying a penalty for irrationality at every moment. The probability axioms are not arbitrary constraints; they are the conditions that must be satisfied if an agent is to avoid being exploitable in this most basic way. An agent who accepts that rationality requires non-exploitability accepts the probability axioms.

On dynamic coherence and conditionalization

The diachronic Dutch Book for conditionalizationA parallel diachronic argument establishes conditionalization as the uniquely rational updating rule. If an agent plans to update on evidence by any rule other than conditionalization, a Dutch Book can be constructed across time — bets placed before and after learning the evidence that guarantee a net loss. This was established by David Lewis and Paul Teller. The agent who updates by conditionalization is dynamically coherent; the agent who does not will be exploitable across time regardless of the evidence she receives. Conditionalization is thus not merely one updating rule among many — it is the one demanded by diachronic rationality.

On the representation theorem

The argument from representation theoremsThe Dutch Book argument is sometimes thought to be too pragmatic — it grounds epistemic norms in practical exploitability. A more foundational approach comes from the representation theorems of Ramsey, Savage, and Jeffrey. If an agent's preferences over acts satisfy certain structural axioms — completeness, transitivity, the sure-thing principle — then there exists a probability function and a utility function such that the agent acts as if she is maximising expected utility with respect to those functions. The probability function is the agent's belief representation; the utility function is her value representation. The axioms are conditions of practical rationality, and Bayesianism is their epistemic consequence.

On Jeffrey's objection — and my response to it

Bradley's response to Jeffrey's objection from uncertain evidenceRichard Jeffrey, my distinguished opponent, raised a genuine challenge to standard Bayesian conditionalization: in many real situations, evidence does not arrive in the form of a proposition learned with certainty. When I glance at a piece of cloth in dim light and form a probabilistic judgment about its colour, there is no proposition E I learn with probability 1. Jeffrey's response was to propose Jeffrey conditionalization, which allows updating on a probability distribution over a partition. I accept this as a genuine extension of the Bayesian framework — not a refutation. It shows that Bayesianism is generalisable rather than brittle. The commitment is to coherent probabilistic updating, not to the specific form of strict conditionalization.

On the problem of the priors

The argument from convergence on priorsThe objection from the subjectivity of priors is the most persistent. If agents with radically different priors reach radically different posteriors, Bayesian agents cannot claim to have reached objective conclusions. But this objection understates the power of Bayesian convergence theorems. Under repeated conditionalization on evidence that distinguishes among hypotheses, agents with different priors — provided they do not assign prior probability zero to the true hypothesis — will converge to the same posterior. The subjectivity of priors is asymptotically washed out by the evidence. Moreover, the existence of prior disagreement is not distinctive to Bayesianism; every framework for scientific inference permits initial disagreement. The Bayesian framework is unique in providing a rigorous mechanism for that disagreement to be resolved by evidence.

I urge the House to vote for the proposition. Bayesianism is not one approach among many equally coherent alternatives. It is the theory of rational belief under uncertainty, and the alternatives have not produced a serious rival.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleague Professor Bradley has established the formal foundations of Bayesianism through the Dutch Book and representation theorems. I want to press deeper and ask what Bayesian credences are about — what connects our subjective degrees of belief to the objective features of the world that make those beliefs reasonable or unreasonable. This is the question that I have tried to address through what I call the Principal Principle, and it is, I believe, the deepest contribution that Bayesian philosophy of science can offer.

On the Principal Principle

The Principal PrincipleThe connection between subjective credence and objective chance is governed by what I call the Principal Principle: a rational agent's credence in a proposition, conditional on the proposition that the objective chance of that proposition is x and on any admissible background information, should be x. This principle is not derivable from the probability axioms alone — it is a substantive constraint connecting the Bayesian framework to the objective physical world. Without it, Bayesian credences float free of reality. With it, Bayesian epistemology is anchored in objective features of the world.

On Humean supervenience and the metaphysics of chance

The argument from Humean supervenienceThe sceptic will say that objective chances are mysterious — what are they, and how do they ground credences? My answer is that chances supervene on the complete history of occurrent facts — the Humean mosaic of point-by-point local qualitative facts. The best-systems analysis of laws and chances identifies them with what emerges from the best summary of that mosaic: the system of propositions that best balances simplicity, strength, and fit. This is not an inflationary metaphysics of nomic necessity; it is a deflationary account on which chance is a natural feature of the world, fully available to serve as the anchor of rational credence.

On the problem of old evidence

The response to the problem of old evidenceClark Glymour raised a penetrating objection to Bayesian confirmation theory: when Einstein showed that general relativity predicted the precession of Mercury's perihelion — a datum already known for decades — this seemed to confirm the theory. But on standard Bayesian conditionalization, evidence already known and assigned probability 1 cannot raise the probability of any hypothesis. How can known evidence confirm? The Bayesian reply is to distinguish the agent's credence function at the time of the theory's evaluation from the historical credence that gave the evidence probability 1. Counterfactual reasoning — asking what probability the agent would have assigned to the evidence absent knowledge of the theory — restores the confirmatory power of old evidence without abandoning the Bayesian framework.

On chance, credence, and the success of science

The argument from the success of scienceThe opponents of Bayesianism must explain why science works — why the process of updating beliefs on evidence has produced reliable theories about the world. The Bayesian explanation is direct: by the Principal Principle, agents who track objective chances through their credences, and update by conditionalization, will reliably learn truths about high-probability features of the world. The convergence of scientific communities on common theories is explicable as the washing out of prior disagreement through repeated conditionalization on shared evidence. No rival account of scientific inference provides as elegant or complete an explanation of the success of science.

On the unity of theoretical and practical reason

The argument from the unity of theoretical and practical reasonOne of the great virtues of the Bayesian framework, rarely sufficiently appreciated, is that it unifies theoretical and practical reason under a single structure. Theoretical rationality governs how we update beliefs; practical rationality governs how we act on them. The Bayesian account provides a single framework — credences, utilities, and expected utility maximisation — within which both are explained. The alternative frameworks proposed by my opponents — van Fraassen's constructive empiricism, for example — sever the connection between belief and action in ways that make rational agency deeply puzzling. The Bayesian framework is the only one that keeps theoretical and practical reason together.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I find myself in an unusual position. I am one of the architects of the Bayesian framework, and I have spent my career extending and refining it. And yet I am here to argue against the motion — not because I reject probability theory as a framework for rational belief, but because the motion as stated — that Bayesianism is the correct normative theory — makes a claim that goes far beyond what the Bayesian framework has established, and papering over those gaps has caused serious confusion in philosophy of science.

I want to press three lines of objection that I regard as genuine and unresolved.

On strict conditionalization and the nature of evidence

The problem of probabilistic evidenceStandard Bayesian conditionalization assumes that evidence arrives in the form of a proposition that the agent comes to believe with probability 1. But this is an idealisation that almost never describes actual epistemic situations. When I see that the sky is red at dusk, I do not come to assign probability 1 to any precisely formulated proposition. My experience shifts a whole range of credences simultaneously, in ways that are not straightforwardly captured by conditionalising on a single proposition. I proposed Jeffrey conditionalization to handle this case — and my opponents tonight have helpfully accepted it. But this acceptance reveals a concession: standard Bayesian conditionalization is inadequate as a description of how evidence actually works, and the Bayesian framework required substantial modification before it could even address the real phenomenology of learning.

On the problem of the priors — more serious than convergence suggests

The problem of the priors as a genuine objectionProfessor Bradley has invoked the convergence of posteriors as a response to the problem of priors. But this response is far too quick. The convergence theorems require the true hypothesis to have non-zero prior probability. For this to be guaranteed, we would need some principled method for assigning prior probabilities to all hypotheses in our space — including hypotheses not yet conceived. The Bayesian framework gives us no such method. The problem of the priors is not merely that agents begin with different priors; it is that the framework cannot tell us what a rational prior is, yet requires priors for its machinery to operate. Without a solution to this problem, Bayesianism is an engine that needs fuel it cannot produce.

On probability zero and unexpected evidence

The problem of zero-probability evidenceA related problem is more acute: what happens when an agent receives evidence to which she assigned probability zero? Standard conditionalization breaks down — dividing by zero is undefined. Yet scientists regularly receive evidence that was genuinely unexpected — not merely improbable, but outside the space of possibilities their prior probability function was defined over. Kuhnian paradigm shifts, the discovery of quantum mechanical phenomena, the detection of gravitational waves — all represent the arrival of evidence that no prior Bayesian framework could have adequately anticipated. A normative theory that cannot handle the most significant episodes of scientific progress is not adequate as a theory of scientific reasoning.

On what Bayesianism explains and what it does not

The limits of Bayesian confirmation theoryBayesian confirmation theory tells us how credences should change given a prior and evidence. It is essentially a theory of updating, not a theory of the rationality of hypotheses independent of an agent's starting point. It cannot tell us whether any particular hypothesis deserves high or low credence in the first place — only how credences should evolve. But the substantive questions of scientific reasoning — why does general relativity deserve our credence, what makes the standard model of particle physics well-confirmed — cannot be answered merely by pointing to updating dynamics. They require an account of the rationality of prior plausibility judgements, which Bayesianism does not provide.

On radical probabilism as the honest Bayesian position

The argument for radical probabilismMy preferred response to these problems is what I call radical probabilism: giving up the claim that Bayesian updating is the uniquely rational response to evidence, and accepting that rational agents may have a wider range of permissible responses to their epistemic situations. The Dutch Book arguments establish that credences must be probabilities; they do not establish that conditionalization is the uniquely rational updating rule. We should retain probabilism — the probability axioms as constraints — while acknowledging that the normative story about updating is underdetermined by the formal framework. This is honest Bayesianism. The motion's claim that Bayesianism is the correct theory demands more than the arguments deliver.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleague Richard Jeffrey has offered the opposition's technical case from inside the Bayesian tradition. I want to approach the question from a different angle — from the philosophy of science itself, and from the view that Bayesianism, even in its most sophisticated forms, cannot provide an adequate account of scientific understanding because it systematically mischaracterises the epistemic aims of science.

I speak from the standpoint of constructive empiricism. I do not dispute that the probability axioms are coherence constraints on rational credences. What I dispute is whether the Bayesian framework, so understood, is the framework within which scientific understanding is best articulated.

On the epistemic aims of science

The argument from the epistemic aims of scienceThe Bayesian picture of science represents it as the enterprise of updating credences over a fixed hypothesis space in response to evidence. But this picture imposes a deeply inappropriate structure on scientific practice. Scientists do not approach their discipline with a prior probability distribution over all possible theories; they operate within frameworks — paradigms, research programmes, theoretical traditions — that determine what counts as evidence, what counts as a hypothesis, and what questions are worth asking. Bayesianism, by treating the hypothesis space as given and the prior as the agent's starting point, ignores the most epistemically significant activities of science: the construction of new conceptual frameworks, the development of new observational techniques, and the revision of what counts as evidence.

On the problem of empirical adequacy versus truth

The argument from empirical adequacyThe motion asks us to believe that Bayesianism correctly describes how credences in scientific theories ought to evolve. But this requires that we are assigning credences to the truth of scientific theories — credences that the theories correctly describe unobservable entities and processes. I have argued extensively that the aim of science is not truth but empirical adequacy: a theory is acceptable if it saves the phenomena — if it correctly predicts what is observable. Bayesian agents assigning credences to the truth of theories are making epistemic commitments that the evidence cannot justify. The Bayesian framework, by requiring credences over all of logical space, commits its users to beliefs about unobservable matters that outrun the evidential warrant.

On the problem of symmetry and the reference class

The symmetry argument against Bayesian priorsEven granting that credences should be probabilities, the question of what determines rational prior probabilities remains deeply problematic in a way that is not addressed by either the Dutch Book argument or the Principal Principle. The Principle of Indifference — assigning equal probability to symmetric cases — famously generates contradictions when the same situation is described under different parameterisations. The Bayesian response has been to abandon indifference principles and retreat to subjective priors. But this concedes that the framework cannot determine what rational initial credences are — only that, once chosen, they must be updated by conditionalization. This is a framework that can tell you how to reason but not what to believe, and that is an insufficiently normative theory.

On probability and acceptance

The argument from the distinction between probability and acceptanceIsaac Levi and others have argued that the Bayesian framework conflates two importantly distinct epistemic states: having a credence in a proposition and accepting a proposition for the purposes of reasoning. Scientific practice involves the acceptance of theories as working hypotheses — not the assignment of high credences to them. Newton's mechanics was accepted as a framework for reasoning despite widespread awareness of anomalies. This acceptance is not captured by any credence assignment, however high. The Bayesian framework, by reducing all epistemic states to credences, cannot model the distinction between degrees of belief and commitments to theoretical frameworks that is central to scientific practice.

On voluntarism about belief and the reflection principle

The argument from the reflection principleThe Bayesian framework is committed to a form of epistemic voluntarism — the view that agents can rationally adopt any prior they choose, provided they subsequently update correctly. My own Reflection Principle — that an agent's current credence in a proposition should equal her expectation of her future credence — has been proposed as a rationality constraint on Bayesian agents. But this principle generates paradoxes when agents have imperfect self-knowledge or face the possibility of non-standard updating. The Bayesian framework generates more internal puzzles than its proponents acknowledge, and these puzzles are symptoms of a deeper tension between the formal elegance of the theory and the complexity of actual epistemic practice.

I conclude not that the probability calculus is wrong, but that Bayesianism as a complete normative theory of scientific inference systematically overstates its achievements and understates its difficulties. The House should reject the motion.

Thank you.
