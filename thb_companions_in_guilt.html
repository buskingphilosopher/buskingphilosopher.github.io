Mr President,

The companions in guilt argument has become, in recent analytic metaethics, one of the most frequently deployed responses to debunking arguments against moral realism. Its structure is familiar: if the evolutionary debunking argument threatens moral knowledge, it equally threatens mathematical knowledge; since we are not prepared to be mathematical sceptics, we should not be moral sceptics either; the moral realist is therefore in good company, and the debunking argument is self-undermining. I want to show tonight that this argument fails — not because debunking arguments are unproblematic, but because the parity claim at the argument’s heart cannot be established. Morality and mathematics are not genuine companions in guilt. There are asymmetries between them that are decisive, and the realist who takes comfort from the comparison has not understood the specific character of the challenge she faces.

On what the companions in guilt strategy requires

The parity thesis: companions in guilt requires genuine parity between moral and mathematical epistemology; the argument fails if the two domains differ in any epistemologically relevant respectLet me be precise about what the companions in guilt argument must establish if it is to succeed. It is not enough to show that both morality and mathematics face some epistemological difficulties. Almost every domain of enquiry faces epistemological difficulties. The argument must show that the epistemological difficulty posed by the debunking argument — the difficulty of explaining how our evaluative attitudes could track mind-independent moral facts given that those attitudes were shaped by evolutionary forces entirely insensitive to such facts — is the same difficulty, or an equally severe one, as that faced by mathematical knowledge. If there is any epistemologically relevant asymmetry between the two domains, the companions in guilt strategy collapses: the moral realist cannot use mathematics as a shield against a challenge that mathematics does not in fact face. My claim is that there are several such asymmetries, and that they are not merely matters of degree but of kind. The guilt is not shared. The comparison is misleading, and moral realism must face the debunking argument on its own.

On the first asymmetry: causal relevance

Causal asymmetry: moral facts, on realist accounts, are causally inert; mathematical facts are also causally inert; but the debunking argument exploits causal irrelevance differently in each caseThe first asymmetry concerns causal relevance. The debunking argument targets moral realism by observing that if moral facts are mind-independent and causally inert — if they do not figure in causal explanations of our moral beliefs — then there is no explanation of how our moral beliefs could be tracking those facts rather than merely reflecting the evolutionarily shaped attitudes of our ancestors. The companions in guilt theorist responds that mathematical facts are equally causally inert: mathematical truths do not cause our mathematical beliefs either, and yet we do not conclude that mathematics is epistemologically hopeless. But this response misses a crucial difference. Mathematical beliefs, even if mathematical facts are causally inert, can be given a satisfying epistemological story through the notion of a priori rational insight: we come to know mathematical truths by reflection on the concepts involved, not by causal commerce with mathematical objects. No parallel story is available for morality on a realist account: there is no clear account of how a priori reflection on moral concepts yields knowledge of mind-independent moral facts, and the intuitions that supposedly provide access to such facts are precisely what the debunking argument puts in question.

On the second asymmetry: the genealogical explanation

Genealogical asymmetry: evolution provides a complete genealogical explanation of our moral attitudes without reference to moral facts; no parallel complete explanation of our mathematical beliefs bypasses mathematical truthThe second and deeper asymmetry concerns the genealogical explanation itself. Street’s Darwinian dilemma identifies a specific problem for the moral realist: evolution can explain why we have the evaluative attitudes we have entirely without reference to whether those attitudes are accurate. Natural selection favoured creatures that cared about their survival, their offspring, and their social group — regardless of whether caring about these things was morally required or merely advantageous. The complete causal story of why we have the moral attitudes we have makes no reference to moral facts. If such a complete non-moral causal story can be told, the realist owes us an explanation of why we should think the attitudes it generates are tracking moral facts at all. No parallel genealogical story is available for core mathematical beliefs. The belief that 2 + 2 = 4 cannot be fully explained by evolutionary pressures without some reference to the fact that 2 + 2 = 4: creatures with systematically false arithmetic beliefs would have been selected against in a world where quantities are as they are. The evolutionary explanation of mathematical beliefs is not completely insulated from mathematical truth in the way that the evolutionary explanation of moral attitudes appears to be insulated from moral facts.

On the independence principle and undercutting defeat

Undercutting defeat: learning that a belief was formed by a process entirely insensitive to the truth of its content provides undercutting defeat for that belief; the moral case satisfies this condition; the mathematical case does notThere is a formal epistemological point that sharpens the asymmetry. The debunking argument is best understood as providing undercutting defeat for moral beliefs. An undercutting defeater does not show that a belief is false; it undermines the basis on which the belief was formed by showing that the belief-forming process was insensitive to the truth of the belief’s content. The principle is sometimes called the independence principle: if you discover that the process by which you came to hold a belief was entirely independent of the truth of that belief — that you would have held the same belief even if it had been false — you have a defeater for that belief. The evolutionary story about moral attitudes satisfies this condition: there is strong reason to think we would have had substantially similar moral attitudes even if those attitudes did not track any mind-independent moral facts, because the evolutionary pressures that shaped them were entirely insensitive to moral truth. The parallel claim about mathematics does not hold with anything like the same force. Mathematical truth is not independent of the world in the relevant sense: the structure of the physical world constrains which mathematical beliefs would have been evolutionarily adaptive, providing at least an indirect connection between mathematical belief-formation and mathematical truth.

On Hanson’s response and why it fails

Against Hanson: Hanson argues the asymmetries are either question-begging or insufficient; Cowie replies that the asymmetries are established independently of moral realism and are sufficient to defeat the parity claimHanson has argued, with great care and ingenuity, that the asymmetries I have identified either beg the question against the companions in guilt strategy or are insufficient to establish the required difference. She contends, first, that the claim that evolution provides a genealogically complete explanation of moral attitudes but not of mathematical beliefs presupposes a view about the relationship between mathematical truth and physical reality that is itself contested; and second, that even if the asymmetries are real, they may be matters of degree rather than kind, insufficient to defeat parity. I want to press back on both points. The genealogical asymmetry does not presuppose moral anti-realism: it relies only on the observation that the evolutionary explanation of moral attitudes is more complete — makes fewer references to the subject matter — than the evolutionary explanation of mathematical beliefs. This is a claim about the structure of our best causal explanations, not about the metaphysics of moral facts. And asymmetries of degree can be sufficient to defeat parity: if the epistemological challenge to morality is significantly more severe than that to mathematics, the companions in guilt strategy fails even if it does not fail absolutely. A companion who is somewhat guilty does not provide the same comfort as a companion equally guilty.

On companions in guilt as a dialectical strategy

Dialectical failure: companions in guilt is a purely defensive strategy; even if it succeeded, it would neutralise the debunking argument without vindicating moral realism; the realist still owes a positive epistemologyI want to make a broader point about the dialectical role of the companions in guilt strategy. Even if it succeeded — even if the parity claim were fully established — it would not vindicate moral realism. It would at most neutralise the debunking argument by showing that it proves too much. But this is a purely negative move: it removes one objection without providing any positive account of how moral knowledge is possible, what moral knowledge is knowledge of, or how our moral intuitions come to be reliable guides to moral facts. The moral realist who relies on companions in guilt has not answered the epistemological challenge; she has merely deflected it onto a neighbouring domain in the hope that the company will be exculpating. A defendant who points to other defendants has not established her innocence. The proper response to the debunking argument is not to find a companion in guilt but to offer a positive moral epistemology that explains how moral beliefs can be justified in the face of the evolutionary story. No such epistemology is provided by companions in guilt, and no such epistemology, I submit, is available.

On what follows from the failure of companions in guilt

The upshot: the failure of companions in guilt does not immediately establish moral error theory or anti-realism; but it leaves the moral realist without her best defensive resource and forces engagement with the debunking argument on its own termsLet me be clear about what follows from the failure of the companions in guilt strategy. It does not immediately establish moral anti-realism or moral error theory. The debunking argument must still be assessed on its own terms, and there are substantive questions about whether it establishes what it claims to establish. But the failure of companions in guilt removes what has seemed to many moral realists their most powerful defensive resource. Without the comparison to mathematics, the realist must confront directly the question of how our evolutionarily shaped moral attitudes could be tracking mind-independent moral facts — and this is a very hard question, which the realist has not, in my assessment, answered convincingly. The companions in guilt strategy offered the realist a way of changing the subject: instead of explaining moral knowledge, she could point to the parallel difficulty in mathematics and suggest that both domains face the same problem. That option is no longer available. The moral realist stands alone before the debunking argument, and the argument does not look less formidable for having been stripped of its mathematical foil.

Mr President,

I want to approach this debate from the origin of the challenge itself. The companions in guilt response has been directed at my Darwinian dilemma for realist theories of value, and I need to say clearly why the response fails to engage with the specific structure of that argument. The dilemma I posed is not a generic epistemological challenge about the difficulty of knowing facts in a given domain. It is a specific challenge arising from the particular relationship between evolutionary explanation and evaluative attitudes — a relationship that has no genuine parallel in the case of mathematics. The companions in guilt theorist has misread the dilemma and then proposed a solution to the misreading.

On the structure of the Darwinian dilemma

The Darwinian dilemma: either our evaluative attitudes are adaptive because they tracked mind-independent moral facts (the tracking account), or they are adaptive for non-moral reasons (the adaptive link account); the tracking account is implausible, the adaptive link account undermines moral realismThe Darwinian dilemma has the following structure. Take the vast majority of our evaluative attitudes: the tendency to value our offspring, to care about the welfare of our social group, to feel aversion to danger. These attitudes were selected for by natural selection. Now the moral realist must explain why these evolutionarily selected attitudes tend to track mind-independent moral facts. There are two options. First, the tracking account: our attitudes track moral facts because natural selection favoured creatures whose attitudes were sensitive to moral facts. But this is implausible: there is no mechanism by which moral facts — if they are the mind-independent, non-natural facts the realist posits — could have exerted selection pressure on our ancestors. Second, the adaptive link account: our attitudes are adaptive for reasons entirely independent of their moral truth — they are selected because they promoted survival and reproduction, not because they tracked moral facts. This undermines the claim that our evaluative attitudes are reliable guides to moral facts. The dilemma is inescapable. Companions in guilt does not resolve it; it merely claims that mathematics faces the same dilemma. But it does not.

On why mathematics does not face the Darwinian dilemma

Mathematics escapes the dilemma: mathematical truth is constrained by physical reality in a way that moral truth is not; mathematical facts can exert indirect selection pressure through the physical world; moral facts cannotThe companions in guilt response claims that mathematical beliefs face the same Darwinian dilemma as moral beliefs. But the dilemma bites for a specific reason: moral facts, on realist accounts, are causally inert and entirely disconnected from the physical world in a way that prevents them from figuring in any explanation of our moral attitudes. Mathematical facts do not stand in this relationship to the physical world — at least, not obviously. Even if we are Platonists about mathematics — even if we think mathematical facts are abstract and mind-independent — it remains true that mathematical truth constrains what patterns are possible in the physical world. Creatures with false beliefs about basic arithmetic would have miscounted their food stores, their enemies, their allies, and their offspring; they would have been selected against. The evolutionary explanation of core mathematical beliefs cannot be given without implicit reference to mathematical structure. This is not true of moral beliefs: even creatures with entirely different moral attitudes — who valued cruelty over kindness, deception over honesty, exploitation over reciprocity — could in principle have survived and reproduced in a world where the moral facts were as the realist says they are.

On constructivism as the correct response

Constructivism: the correct response to the Darwinian dilemma is not realism defended by companions in guilt, but constructivism; moral facts are constructed from within the evaluative standpoint and are not mind-independentMy own response to the Darwinian dilemma is not to deny that our evaluative attitudes were shaped by evolution, but to draw the correct metaethical conclusion from this fact. If our evaluative attitudes were shaped by natural selection, and if the facts about what we have reason to do or value are in some way grounded in our evaluative attitudes, then the debunking argument loses its force: there is no longer an explanatory gap between our attitudes and the facts they purportedly track, because the facts are not independent of the attitudes. This is the constructivist insight. On a Humean constructivist view, something counts as a reason for an agent if and only if it coheres with her evaluative attitudes on reflection; on a Kantian constructivist view, reasons are constructed from the standpoint of practical reason. Either way, the question “why do our evolved attitudes track moral facts?” does not arise, because the moral facts are not independent of the attitudes. The companions in guilt theorist assumes that moral realism is the correct view and then seeks to defend it against the debunking argument. I am challenging the assumption.

On the companions in guilt response to constructivism

Against the constructivist escape: Enoch and others argue that constructivism faces its own debunking argument; Street replies that the constructivist debunking argument has a different structure and is not successfulThe opponents of constructivism have attempted to turn the Darwinian dilemma against constructivism itself. If our evaluative attitudes are the input to the construction of reasons, and those attitudes were shaped by natural selection, then the reasons we construct from those attitudes are similarly shaped by natural selection — and constructed reasons have no claim to guide us that is independent of the contingent evolutionary history that produced the inputs. This is sometimes called the companions in guilt response to constructivism: constructivism is no better off than realism in the face of the evolutionary story. I want to dispute this. The problem for moral realism is that there is a gap between our evolutionarily shaped attitudes and the mind-independent moral facts those attitudes are supposed to track. Constructivism closes this gap by denying that the facts are mind-independent: the facts are constructed from the attitudes, so there is no gap to explain. The debunking argument against constructivism has a different structure: it must show not that there is an unexplained gap, but that the constructed facts have insufficient normative authority to guide action. This is a serious challenge — but it is not the Darwinian dilemma, and it does not follow from the evolutionary story without additional premises that the constructivist will dispute.

On the companions in guilt theorist’s options

The realist’s options: the realist can bite the bullet (our attitudes are reliable by coincidence), appeal to pre-established harmony, or adopt a third-factor account; all are implausibleThe moral realist who responds to my dilemma using companions in guilt must eventually answer the first horn of the dilemma directly: she must explain why our evolutionarily shaped evaluative attitudes tend to track mind-independent moral facts. The options available to her are limited. She might claim that the correlation is a massive coincidence — that natural selection happened to produce creatures whose attitudes track moral facts, even though moral facts played no role in the selection. This is desperately implausible. She might claim that God or some other designer ensured the correlation — but this imports theological premises that most analytic moral realists are not prepared to accept. She might appeal to a third-factor account: some common cause explains both the moral facts and the evolutionary outcome. But no plausible third factor has been identified. The companions in guilt move is a way of avoiding this dilemma by pointing to mathematics — but mathematics does not face the same dilemma, and pointing to it does not discharge the realist’s explanatory obligation.

On the significance of the debunking argument for metaethics

Significance: the Darwinian dilemma is not merely a puzzle for moral epistemology but a constraint on metaethical theorising; any adequate metaethics must be compatible with our evolutionary originsThe Darwinian dilemma is not, I want to insist, merely a puzzle about one epistemological challenge among many. It is a constraint on metaethical theorising: any adequate metaethics must be compatible with the fact that our evaluative attitudes are the products of natural selection operating on creatures with no sensitivity to mind-independent moral facts. This constraint rules out moral realism of the robust variety — the variety defended by Enoch, Shafer-Landau, and others in the opposition camp tonight. It does not rule out constructivism, quasi-realism, or various forms of anti-realism. The companions in guilt strategy attempts to use the parallel with mathematics to lift this constraint on moral realism: if mathematics is compatible with the evolutionary story, then so is moral realism. But the parallel is false, and the constraint remains. Metaethics must take evolutionary biology seriously as a constraint on what views are available, and robust moral realism does not meet that constraint.

On what the opposition must show

The opposition’s burden: to establish companions in guilt, the opposition must show that mathematical beliefs face the same complete insulation from truth in their evolutionary explanation as moral beliefs do; this has not been shownTo conclude. If the opposition tonight wishes to defend the companions in guilt strategy against my Darwinian dilemma, they must show that the evolutionary explanation of mathematical beliefs is similarly insulated from mathematical truth as the evolutionary explanation of moral attitudes is insulated from moral truth. They must show that a creature with systematically false core mathematical beliefs would have been equally well adapted — that mathematical truth played no more role in explaining mathematical belief than moral truth plays in explaining moral attitudes. This is a very implausible claim, and it has not been established by any defender of companions in guilt. The parity claim fails at the most fundamental level: the genealogical explanations have different structures, and the difference is epistemologically decisive. I invite the opposition to establish parity — and I predict they will not be able to do so.

Mr President,

My position differs from both Cowie’s and Street’s in an important way. I accept that the evolutionary debunking argument succeeds against moral realism — and I accept this as the foundation of moral error theory, the view that moral discourse systematically makes claims that are false because there are no moral facts of the kind those claims presuppose. The companions in guilt response interests me not as a defence of moral realism but as a challenge to error theory: the companions in guilt theorist argues that if debunking defeats moral realism, it equally defeats mathematical Platonism, and since we are not prepared to accept mathematical error theory, we should not accept moral error theory. I want to show that this challenge fails, and that mathematics and morality are not genuine companions in the way the argument requires.

On the structure of the genealogical debunking argument

The debunking argument: a genealogical explanation of our beliefs that does not cite the truth of those beliefs as part of the explanation undermines the epistemic standing of those beliefs; the moral case satisfies this condition; the mathematical case does not straightforwardly do soLet me begin by being precise about the structure of the debunking argument as I understand it, drawing on my work in The Evolution of Morality and subsequent papers. A genealogical debunking argument proceeds by offering a causal history of a class of beliefs that (a) is complete in the sense of not citing the truth of the beliefs as part of the explanation, and (b) gives us reason to think that the belief-forming process would have produced the same beliefs even if those beliefs had been false. When both conditions are met, the genealogy provides undercutting defeat for the beliefs in question: we have reason to doubt that the beliefs are epistemically reliable, and absent a supplementary positive epistemology, we should withhold assent. The question for companions in guilt is whether this structure applies equally to mathematical beliefs. I will argue that it does not — not because mathematical Platonism is obviously correct, but because the specific structure of the genealogical debunking argument does not apply to mathematics in the same form.

On the difference between the moral and mathematical cases

The mathematical difference: mathematical beliefs are involved in practical reasoning about physical reality in a way that connects their evolutionary selection to mathematical truth; moral beliefs are not similarly connected to moral truthThe key difference between the moral and mathematical cases turns on the role of each domain in practical reasoning about physical reality. Mathematical beliefs — particularly core arithmetic, geometry, and probabilistic reasoning — are directly employed in reasoning about the physical world: counting resources, estimating distances, assessing probabilities. The adaptive value of these beliefs is therefore tied to their accuracy in representing physical structure. And mathematical truth, even if it consists in abstract facts, is instantiated in or at least constrained by the structure of the physical world: there cannot be two apples and two apples that together make five apples, because the physical world does not permit it. This means that selection for accurate practical reasoning about the physical world is selection, indirectly, for mathematically true beliefs. The parallel does not hold for morality. Moral beliefs are not employed in reasoning about physical structure; they concern the evaluation of actions, states of affairs, and persons. And there is no mechanism by which moral facts — facts about what is right, wrong, obligatory, or permissible — are instantiated in or constrained by the physical world in a way that would make selection for practically effective moral beliefs selection for morally true beliefs.

On quasi-realism and the companions in guilt response

Quasi-realism: Blackburn argues that expressivism can earn the right to moral realist-sounding talk; companions in guilt is sometimes deployed by quasi-realists; but the quasi-realist faces a different version of the challenge than the realistI want to address a sophisticated version of companions in guilt that is sometimes pressed by quasi-realists in the tradition of Simon Blackburn. The quasi-realist holds that moral discourse expresses pro-attitudes rather than describing moral facts, but that through a process of “earning the right” to realist-sounding language, moral discourse can acquire the surface features of factual discourse without the metaphysical commitments. The companions in guilt strategy is sometimes used by quasi-realists to argue that even their position can make sense of moral knowledge: just as we can speak loosely of mathematical knowledge without being committed to Platonism, we can speak of moral knowledge without being committed to robust moral realism. But this is a different deployment of the argument from the one I am addressing. The realist uses companions in guilt to defend the epistemological credentials of mind-independent moral facts; the quasi-realist uses it to claim that a weaker notion of moral knowledge is available. My concern tonight is with the realist deployment, and against that deployment the asymmetries I have identified are decisive. The quasi-realist is not claiming that evolution tracks moral facts; she is not making the claim that requires defence.

On Enoch’s third-factor account and why it is insufficient

Enoch’s third-factor account: Enoch argues that there may be a common cause explaining both our evaluative attitudes and the moral facts; Joyce argues this is too speculative and unsubstantiated to bear the weight placed on itDavid Enoch has offered what he calls a “third-factor” account of the correlation between our evaluative attitudes and moral facts: perhaps there is some common explanation — facts about practical necessity, or about what is required for flourishing — that explains both why we have the evaluative attitudes we have and why those attitudes track moral facts. This is not straightforwardly a companions in guilt argument, but it is often combined with one: the claim is that if we can point to a third factor for morality, the companions in guilt theorist can point to a structurally similar third factor for mathematics. I want to press the following objection: the third-factor account is too speculative and unsubstantiated to bear the weight placed on it. Enoch does not identify a specific third factor; he merely argues that one might exist. This is a promissory note, not an epistemological account. And even if a third factor were identified, it would need to explain the correlation between evolutionarily shaped attitudes and mind-independent moral facts in a way that did not simply relocate the mystery. The parallel with mathematics does not help: the structure of physical reality provides a concrete, well-understood third factor explaining the correlation between mathematical beliefs and mathematical truth; no analogous concrete factor has been identified for morality.

On moral error theory as the correct upshot

Error theory: moral discourse presupposes mind-independent moral facts; since no such facts exist (given the failure of all realist epistemologies), moral discourse is systematically in error; this is compatible with moral discourse being practically indispensableMy own positive view is that the evolutionary debunking argument, combined with the failure of all proposed realist epistemologies including companions in guilt, supports moral error theory: the view that moral discourse systematically makes claims — that actions are objectively obligatory, that things are objectively valuable — that are false because there are no mind-independent moral facts of the relevant kind. This is a radical conclusion, and I do not make it lightly. But I want to insist that error theory is compatible with continuing to engage in moral discourse, to feel moral emotions, and to use moral language in the practical contexts where it serves important social and psychological functions. The fictionalist extension of error theory — which I have defended — holds that we can engage with moral discourse as a useful fiction: participating in moral practices without being committed to the literal truth of our moral utterances, in the way that a sophisticated reader of fiction can engage with fictional characters without believing in their existence. Companions in guilt, by contrast, attempts to maintain the literal truth of moral discourse — and that attempt, I have argued, fails.

On the argument from companions in innocence

Companions in innocence: the companions in guilt theorist might argue that if moral discourse is in error, so is mathematical discourse, and since we are not mathematical error theorists, we should not be moral error theorists; Joyce argues the disanalogies defeat this moveThe companions in guilt argument is sometimes presented in a positive form — what we might call “companions in innocence” — in which the claim is not merely that moral realism faces the same challenge as mathematical Platonism, but that both are vindicated together by whatever epistemological story makes sense of mathematical knowledge. If we can make sense of mathematical knowledge despite the evolutionary story, we can make sense of moral knowledge too. I have already argued that the evolutionary stories are different in structure. But I want to add that even if they were structurally similar, the analogy would not vindicate moral realism by borrowing mathematical credibility. What we need is an account of how moral knowledge is possible — and pointing to the fact that mathematical knowledge is possible does not provide that account. The validity of borrowing credibility across domains depends on the domains being genuinely analogous in the relevant epistemological respects, and I have argued that they are not.

On the future of the debate

The future of the debate: the companions in guilt strategy has forced moral realists to be more precise about their epistemology; this is a genuine contribution; but the asymmetries between the moral and mathematical cases remain unresolved by the strategyI want to close by acknowledging what the companions in guilt strategy has contributed to the debate. It has forced moral realists to be more precise about their epistemological commitments, and it has drawn attention to the genuine philosophical difficulty of mathematical epistemology — a difficulty that had perhaps been insufficiently appreciated. The debate between Cowie and Hanson, which I regard as the most technically precise engagement with the strategy in recent literature, has clarified what exactly the parity claim requires and where the asymmetries lie. But the strategy has not succeeded. The moral and mathematical cases are not genuinely analogous in the ways required for companions in guilt to work. The evolutionary explanation of our moral attitudes is more epistemologically threatening than the evolutionary explanation of our mathematical beliefs, and no amount of rhetorical comparison between the two will remove the threat. The debunking argument stands, and moral error theory remains, in my assessment, the most honest response to it.

Mr President,

Christopher Cowie is the most careful and technically precise critic of the companions in guilt strategy writing today, and I take his objections seriously. But I want to argue, as I have done in print, that his claimed asymmetries between the moral and mathematical cases either beg the question against the companions in guilt strategy or fail to establish a difference of the kind and severity required to defeat the parity claim. The debunking challenge to moral realism is genuinely symmetric with the debunking challenge to mathematical Platonism, and the companions in guilt strategy succeeds as a defensive manoeuvre. This does not mean that moral realism faces no epistemological challenges — it clearly does. But it means that the evolutionary debunking argument, as standardly presented, does not constitute a stronger challenge to moral realism than it does to mathematical Platonism. The moral realist and the mathematical Platonist stand or fall together.

On the structure of the parity claim

Parity thesis defended: the parity claim does not require identical epistemological situations; it requires that the debunking argument is no more threatening to morality than to mathematics; this weaker reading suffices for the defensive purposeBefore engaging with Cowie’s specific asymmetry objections, I want to clarify what the companions in guilt strategy actually requires. Cowie characterises the parity requirement as demanding genuine epistemic identity between the moral and mathematical cases: the same kind and severity of challenge. But this characterisation is too strong. The companions in guilt strategy is a defensive manoeuvre against a specific argument — the evolutionary debunking argument — and its success requires only that the debunking argument is no more threatening to moral realism than it is to mathematical Platonism. If the epistemological challenge to mathematical Platonism is at least as serious as the challenge to moral realism, then the debunking argument cannot be used selectively to undermine moral realism while leaving mathematical Platonism intact. The challenge would defeat both or neither. The weaker parity claim — that the challenges are comparably serious — is sufficient for the companions in guilt strategy to succeed as a dialectical move, even if the two cases are not epistemologically identical in every respect.

On Benacerraf’s challenge and the parallel with morality

Benacerraf’s challenge: if mathematical objects are abstract and causally inert, there is no explanation of how our mathematical beliefs could be tracking them; this is structurally the same challenge as the moral debunking argumentThe most powerful argument for the companions in guilt strategy is not the evolutionary argument at all — it is the structural parallel between moral debunking and what Paul Benacerraf identified as the epistemological challenge to mathematical Platonism. Benacerraf’s challenge, posed in his 1973 paper “Mathematical Truth,” is this: if mathematical objects are abstract, causally inert entities, then there is no explanation of how our mathematical beliefs could be reliably tracking such entities. Our mathematical beliefs were formed by processes — perception, reasoning, cultural transmission — that are causally sensitive only to physical objects and their properties. Abstract mathematical objects, by hypothesis, play no causal role in these processes. So why think our mathematical beliefs are tracking mathematical facts rather than merely being internally coherent or practically adequate? This challenge is structurally identical to the challenge posed by the evolutionary debunking argument to moral realism: in both cases, the belief-forming processes are causally insensitive to the facts the beliefs are supposed to be tracking. The companion in guilt is mathematics, not as a domain that faces some epistemological difficulty, but as a domain that faces the same epistemological challenge from the same source.

On Cowie’s genealogical asymmetry objection

Against Cowie’s genealogical asymmetry: the claim that mathematical truth constrains physical reality in a way that moral truth does not either presupposes mathematical Platonism or is compatible with a parallel claim about moral truth on robust realist accountsCowie argues that the evolutionary explanation of mathematical beliefs is not complete without reference to mathematical truth, because mathematical truth constrains physical reality in a way that makes mathematically false beliefs maladaptive. I want to challenge this argument on two fronts. First, the claim that mathematical truth constrains physical reality in the relevant sense is itself contested. It presupposes a specific view about the relationship between abstract mathematical objects and concrete physical reality — a view that the nominalist about mathematics will reject. If mathematics is taken to be a useful fiction or a formal system without commitment to abstract objects, then mathematical truth does not constrain physical reality in the way Cowie requires; it is merely that certain formal systems are more useful for modelling physical reality than others. Second, and more fundamentally, the robust moral realist can make a parallel claim: moral truth constrains the domain of reasons in a way that makes morally false beliefs practically suboptimal. The parallel is not obviously weaker. The claim that mathematical truth uniquely constrains reality in a way moral truth cannot is a contested metaphysical claim, not a neutral observation.

On the question-begging charge

Question-begging: Cowie’s asymmetries presuppose that moral facts are causally inert in a more radical way than mathematical facts, but this presupposition is exactly what is at issue between the realist and the debunkerThe deepest problem with Cowie’s asymmetry arguments is that they presuppose, in establishing the asymmetry, a view about the relationship between moral facts and physical reality that is exactly what is at issue between the moral realist and the debunker. Cowie says that moral facts are causally inert in a sense that mathematical facts are not — that moral truth does not constrain physical reality in the way mathematical truth does, and that therefore the evolutionary explanation of moral beliefs is more completely insulated from moral truth than the evolutionary explanation of mathematical beliefs is from mathematical truth. But this is to assume that the debunker’s picture of moral facts — as totally disconnected from the causal order — is correct. The moral realist can and should dispute this assumption: on some versions of moral realism, moral facts are naturalistically grounded in facts about wellbeing, flourishing, or rational agency, and are therefore not more causally disconnected from physical reality than mathematical facts are. The asymmetry Cowie claims to establish is itself a contested position, not a neutral premise from which to assess the relative severity of the two debunking challenges.

On the positive case for companions in guilt

Positive case: mathematical Platonism is accepted despite Benacerraf’s challenge because its theoretical benefits outweigh the epistemological cost; the same cost-benefit argument is available for moral realismI want to make a positive case for the companions in guilt strategy, not merely a defensive one. Mathematical Platonism is widely accepted by mathematicians and philosophers of mathematics despite Benacerraf’s challenge. The reason is that the theoretical benefits of Platonism — its explanation of the objectivity, necessity, and applicability of mathematics — are judged to outweigh the epistemological cost. The cost is real: we do not have a satisfying account of how we come to know abstract mathematical facts. But the benefit is also real: Platonism makes the best sense of mathematical practice and mathematical truth. The companions in guilt strategy invites us to make the same cost-benefit assessment for moral realism. Robust moral realism explains the objectivity of moral discourse, the possibility of genuine moral disagreement, and the authority of moral requirements over self-interest — these are significant theoretical benefits. The epistemological cost is also real: we do not have a fully satisfying account of how we come to know moral facts. But this cost is no greater than the cost of mathematical Platonism, and moral realism’s theoretical benefits are comparable. The cost-benefit assessment favours moral realism in the same way it favours mathematical Platonism.

On the dialectical significance of the companions in guilt move

Dialectical significance: the companions in guilt move forces the debunker to explain why she is not equally a mathematical sceptic; this shifts the dialectical burden and reveals the selectivity of evolutionary debunkingCowie accuses companions in guilt of being merely a defensive strategy that changes the subject rather than providing a positive moral epistemology. I want to dispute this characterisation. The companions in guilt move has positive dialectical force: it forces the debunker to explain why she is not equally a mathematical sceptic. If the debunker accepts mathematical Platonism while rejecting moral realism on epistemological grounds, she owes us an explanation of why the epistemological challenge is more decisive in the moral case. That explanation cannot simply point to the evolutionary story: the Benacerraf challenge is older than the evolutionary debunking argument and applies to mathematical Platonism in a form that does not depend on evolutionary considerations. The debunker must therefore show that the evolutionary debunking argument has force that Benacerraf’s challenge lacks — which requires establishing Cowie’s contested asymmetries. Companions in guilt therefore shifts the dialectical burden back onto the proposition, and the proposition has not discharged it.

On what a satisfying response to the debunking argument would look like

A satisfying response: the companions in guilt strategy is a first step; what is needed is a positive moral epistemology that parallels the best accounts of mathematical knowledge; the two projects inform each otherI want to close by acknowledging what Cowie is right about and what the companions in guilt strategy requires as a complement. He is right that the strategy is defensive: it neutralises the debunking argument without providing a positive moral epistemology. A full vindication of moral realism requires not only that the debunking challenge fails but that a positive account of moral knowledge is available. The companions in guilt theorist should be using the parallel with mathematics not only defensively but constructively: the best accounts of mathematical knowledge — the accounts that explain how we know mathematical truths despite the Benacerraf challenge — should serve as models for developing accounts of moral knowledge. Whether those accounts involve rational intuition, reflective equilibrium, or something else remains contested. But the project of developing a positive moral epistemology by analogy with mathematical epistemology is exactly what the companions in guilt strategy motivates. The parallel is not merely dialectically useful; it is methodologically illuminating. And this illuminating parallel is something that Cowie’s sceptical critique fails to appreciate.

I want to approach this debate from the standpoint of robust moral realism — the view that there are objective, mind-independent normative facts, and that we have access to them — and argue that the companions in guilt strategy is one component of a broader response to the epistemological challenge. I am somewhat less enthusiastic about the companions in guilt strategy than Hanson, because I think it is at best a defensive move that shifts the burden without discharging it. What I want to offer instead is a more aggressive response to the debunking argument: I want to challenge the debunker’s implicit assumption that the evolutionary story is epistemically threatening, and I want to defend the practical indispensability of normative realism as a reason to accept it even in the face of the epistemological challenge.

On the third-factor account

Third-factor account: there may be a common explanation of both our evaluative attitudes and the moral facts; this need not be implausible given similar third-factor accounts in other domains; the debunker bears the burden of showing no such account is availableThe debunking argument assumes that there is no explanation of the correlation between our evolutionarily shaped evaluative attitudes and mind-independent moral facts. I want to challenge this assumption. In other domains where we posit mind-independent facts that are not directly causally accessible to us, we accept third-factor accounts of the correlation between our beliefs and the facts. Consider: our beliefs about logical necessity, about modal facts, about the laws of nature. In all these cases, the facts are not directly causally accessible to us, and yet we accept that our beliefs about them are approximately true; and we accept this on the basis of the theoretical virtues of the resulting picture. The same structure is available for moral realism. Perhaps there is a common explanation of both the evaluative attitudes that were adaptively selected and the moral facts: perhaps what is adaptively beneficial for social creatures with our capacities tends to coincide with what is morally required, not because moral facts caused the evolution, but because both the moral facts and the adaptive outcomes are responses to the same underlying facts about the conditions for cooperative social life. This is speculative, but no more speculative than the parallel accounts we accept in other domains.

On the practical indispensability of normative realism

Practical indispensability: robust normative realism is indispensable for making sense of our practical lives; we cannot act and reason practically without presupposing objective normative facts; this indispensability gives us reason to accept the viewI want to make an argument that goes beyond the defensive posture of companions in guilt and challenges the framing of the debunking argument itself. The debunking argument asks: given the evolutionary story, why should we think our moral beliefs are tracking mind-independent moral facts? But this question presupposes that we are in a position to consider abandoning our commitment to objective normative facts — that such abandonment is a genuine option. I want to challenge this presupposition. Robust normative realism is, I claim, practically indispensable: we cannot act and reason practically without presupposing that some things really matter, that some considerations really are reasons for action, that our practical deliberation is responsive to genuine normative facts rather than merely to our contingently evolved preferences. The error theorist and the constructivist will say that we can make do with constructed norms or useful fictions. But I submit that the practical stance of treating normative facts as genuinely out there — as constraints on our deliberation rather than products of it — is not a philosophical luxury but a precondition of practical reasoning as we actually engage in it. This practical indispensability does not prove that normative realism is true, but it gives us a reason to accept it that is independent of, and perhaps stronger than, any epistemological argument.

On what the debunking argument actually establishes

What debunking establishes: the debunking argument shows that evolutionary pressures were not sensitive to moral truth; it does not show that our moral beliefs are false or unreliable; these are distinct claimsI want to challenge the inference that the proposition draws from the evolutionary story. Street argues that the evolutionary explanation of our moral attitudes shows that those attitudes are not tracking mind-independent moral facts. But this inference is too fast. The evolutionary story shows that natural selection was not sensitive to moral truth when it shaped our evaluative attitudes. It does not follow that our moral beliefs are false or unreliable. Consider: natural selection also shaped our perceptual faculties without being directly sensitive to the full range of facts those faculties track — the evolutionary explanation of colour vision does not refer to facts about surface spectral reflectance, only to facts about adaptive discrimination. And yet we do not conclude from this that colour beliefs are unreliable or systematically false. The debunking argument needs to show not merely that the belief-forming process was insensitive to the truth of the beliefs, but that this insensitivity prevents the beliefs from being reliable. And this further step requires showing that there is no third-factor account, no indirect connection between the adaptive outcome and the moral truth. The debunker has assumed the absence of such an account; she has not established it.

The constructivist says that moral demands are authoritative because they are constructed from our evaluative attitudes on reflection; but if those attitudes are contingent evolutionary products, their reflective output has no claim to absolute authority. The error theorist says that moral demands have no genuine authority and that we engage in moral practice as a useful fiction; but this concedes that when morality conflicts with self-interest, there is no fact of the matter about which takes precedence, which seems deeply wrong. Only robust realism — which holds that moral facts are genuinely out there, independently of our attitudes and evolutionary history — can vindicate the absolute authority of moral demands. This is not an epistemological argument, but it is a reason to accept robust realism that the debunking argument’s epistemological focus tends to obscure.

Mr President,

My approach to this debate begins from a place that both sides have perhaps insufficiently appreciated: the genuine depth of the epistemological problem for abstract objects of all kinds, mathematical and moral alike. Benacerraf’s 1973 paper “Mathematical Truth” identified what remains, fifty years later, one of the most serious open problems in the philosophy of mathematics: if mathematical objects are abstract and causally inert, how do we know anything about them? No satisfying solution has been found. I want to use this unresolved problem as the foundation of the companions in guilt argument in its strongest form: the problem of knowing abstract objects is equally severe for mathematics and for morality, and since we are not prepared to be mathematical sceptics, we are not licensed to be moral sceptics either. The correct response is acceptance of both — not scepticism about either.

On the unresolved Benacerraf problem

Benacerraf’s unresolved problem: fifty years after the challenge was posed, no satisfying epistemology for mathematical Platonism has been developed; yet we continue to accept mathematical knowledge as genuineBenacerraf’s challenge has not been solved. In the fifty years since its formulation, the philosophy of mathematics has produced many responses — structuralism, neo-logicism, plenitudinous Platonism, fictionalism — but no response has achieved consensus, and no response has given a fully satisfying account of how our mathematical beliefs could be tracking abstract mathematical facts given that those facts are causally inert and our belief-forming processes are causally sensitive only to concrete objects. The standard response among working mathematicians and many philosophers is a form of what we might call epistemological quietism: we accept that we have mathematical knowledge, we treat the mathematical practice as epistemologically legitimate, and we set aside the Benacerraf challenge as a philosophical puzzle that does not threaten mathematical practice even if we cannot solve it. I want to argue that the correct attitude toward the moral debunking argument is epistemological quietism of the same kind. We accept that we have moral knowledge, we treat moral practice as epistemologically legitimate, and we set aside the debunking challenge as a philosophical puzzle that does not threaten moral practice even if we cannot solve it. The companions are genuinely in the same epistemic boat.

On the epistemology of abstract objects and the symmetry with morality

Abstract objects symmetry: both mathematical objects (numbers, sets, functions) and moral facts (obligations, values, reasons) are abstract, mind-independent, and causally inert; the epistemological challenge is structurally identical for bothI want to press the structural symmetry between the epistemology of mathematical objects and the epistemology of moral facts with greater precision than is sometimes achieved in this debate. Mathematical objects — numbers, sets, functions — are abstract: they are not located in space and time, they do not have causal powers, and they are not perceivable by the senses. Yet we claim to know facts about them. Moral facts — that gratuitous cruelty is wrong, that justice requires impartiality, that kindness is a virtue — are also abstract: they are not located in space and time, they are not perceivable by the senses, and they do not have causal powers (on the realist account). The epistemological challenge in both cases is the same: how do we come to know non-causal, abstract facts by means of belief-forming processes that are causally sensitive only to the concrete world? The challenge is not essentially evolutionary. It predates Darwin and would apply even in a world without evolution. The evolutionary debunking argument is a specific, historically situated version of a much older challenge — the challenge of knowing abstract objects — and this older challenge applies with equal force to mathematics and to morality.

On the failure of nominalism as a solution for mathematics

Nominalism fails for mathematics: attempts to eliminate commitment to abstract mathematical objects have not succeeded; they either paraphrase away mathematics at unacceptable cost or fail to account for the truth of mathematical claimsOne response to Benacerraf’s challenge is to deny that mathematical objects exist at all — to be a nominalist about mathematics and say that mathematical claims, while useful, do not describe a domain of abstract objects. This is the mathematical analogue of moral error theory: mathematical discourse is in systematic error if Platonism is false and mathematical discourse presupposes it. But mathematical nominalism has faced severe difficulties. Hartry Field’s fictionalist programme — the most sophisticated attempt to show that science can be reformulated without commitment to mathematical objects — has not persuaded most philosophers of mathematics that the reformulation is adequate or attractive. The indispensability argument — that mathematical entities are indispensable to our best scientific theories — has proved difficult to defeat. And the epistemological objection to Platonism applies equally to the alternatives: even if we adopt a structuralist or fictionalist account of mathematics, we face the challenge of explaining how our mathematical beliefs are reliable in the relevant sense. The failure of mathematical nominalism is significant for the companions in guilt argument: if we are not prepared to accept mathematical error theory despite the Benacerraf challenge, we should not accept moral error theory on the basis of a structurally parallel challenge.

On the response from indispensability

Indispensability in morality: just as mathematical entities are indispensable to scientific theory, moral facts are indispensable to our best explanations of human behaviour and our practical self-understanding; the indispensability argument extends to moralityThe Quine-Putnam indispensability argument for mathematical Platonism holds that we are committed to the existence of mathematical entities because they are indispensable to our best scientific theories; we cannot paraphrase away quantification over numbers and sets without radical revision of physics, chemistry, and biology. I want to press the analogous argument for moral realism. Moral facts — specifically, facts about reasons for action, obligations, and values — are indispensable to our best explanations of human behaviour and to our practical self-understanding. We explain why Jones kept her promise by citing her sense of obligation; we explain why Smith donated to charity by citing her recognition that it was the right thing to do; we explain social institutions by citing the norms and obligations they embody. If moral facts are eliminated or replaced by constructed norms or useful fictions, our explanatory practice is impoverished in ways that have no analogue in physics or chemistry. The indispensability of moral facts to our best explanations of human social life is a reason to accept moral realism that parallels the reason to accept mathematical Platonism.

On the companions in guilt response to Street

Against Street: Street’s Darwinian dilemma applies equally to mathematical beliefs if we take seriously the view that mathematical truth constrains physical reality; Street must either accept the symmetric application or abandon the constraint claimStreet argues that the evolutionary explanation of our moral attitudes is insulated from moral truth in a way that the evolutionary explanation of mathematical beliefs is not — because mathematical truth constrains physical reality while moral truth does not. I want to press this claim harder than Hanson has. If mathematical truth constrains physical reality — if there cannot be 2+2=5 physical objects because mathematical truth rules it out — then mathematical truth is not purely abstract in the sense required by Platonism. A Platonist who says that mathematical truth constrains physical reality is making a claim about the relationship between abstract objects and concrete reality that is deeply puzzling: how do causally inert abstract objects constrain what happens in the causal order? If the Platonist cannot explain this, she has not solved Benacerraf’s challenge; she has relocated it. And if she can explain it, the moral realist can demand the same explanation for the relationship between moral facts and human practical life. The dilemma Street poses for the moral realist — either the facts causally constrain our beliefs (implausible for abstract facts) or they do not (and our beliefs are unreliable) — applies with equal force to the mathematical Platonist.

On epistemological quietism and moral practice

Quietism: the correct attitude toward both Benacerraf and the moral debunking argument is epistemological quietism; we accept both domains of knowledge as legitimate while acknowledging that we lack a satisfying philosophical account of how they are possibleI want to defend epistemological quietism as the correct response to both Benacerraf’s challenge and the moral debunking argument. The quietist does not claim to have solved the epistemological problem; she claims that the epistemological problem, while real, does not constitute a decisive reason to abandon either mathematical Platonism or moral realism. The grounds for this claim are: first, that the alternative views (nominalism, error theory, constructivism) face serious difficulties of their own; second, that the theoretical virtues of Platonism and moral realism — their explanatory power, their coherence with practice, their vindication of the authority of mathematical and moral claims — outweigh the epistemological cost; and third, that the epistemological challenge is not unique to these domains but affects knowledge of abstract objects generally, and we do not treat this as a decisive reason for scepticism across the board. Quietism is not intellectual timidity; it is the recognition that philosophical puzzles about the conditions of knowledge do not automatically defeat the claim to know.

On what the companions in guilt debate reveals about metaethics

What the debate reveals: the companions in guilt debate has shown that the epistemological challenge to moral realism is not uniquely severe; it is part of a broader challenge to the epistemology of abstract objects that no domain has fully resolvedI want to close by marking what I take to be the most important contribution of the companions in guilt debate to analytic philosophy. The debate has shown, conclusively in my view, that the epistemological challenge to moral realism is not a special, uniquely devastating challenge that singles out morality for scepticism. It is part of a broader challenge to the epistemology of abstract objects — a challenge that affects mathematics, logic, and modality no less than morality. No domain has fully resolved this challenge. The correct response — in mathematics as in morality — is not scepticism but acceptance combined with continued philosophical enquiry into how abstract knowledge is possible. The companions in guilt strategy, at its best, is not merely a defensive manoeuvre; it is an invitation to do epistemology at the right level of generality: to develop an account of how we know abstract facts, which will illuminate both mathematical and moral epistemology simultaneously. That project is more important and more interesting than the defensive use of the strategy, and it is what I hope this debate will motivate the participants on both sides to pursue.
