Mr President,

I want to say plainly what my position in this debate is, because I think it is liable to be misunderstood. I am not here as a defender of moral realism who is nervous about the debunking argument. I am a moral error theorist — I believe that our moral discourse systematically fails because there are no categorical moral facts of the kind it presupposes. My interest in the companions in guilt argument is not as a shield for realism but as a target. The companions in guilt argument is the most important objection to the moral error theory currently in circulation, and I want to show that it fails. If it fails, the error theory stands.

On what the companions in guilt argument actually says

The CG argument: error theory proves too much; if it defeats moral reasons, it equally defeats epistemic reasons; since epistemic error theory is unacceptable, the error theory should be rejectedThe companions in guilt argument — as it is most commonly pressed against moral error theory — does not primarily invoke mathematics. It invokes epistemic normativity. The argument runs as follows. Moral error theorists hold that there are no categorical moral facts — no facts about what we are morally required or morally permitted to do that hold independently of our desires and attitudes. But the same arguments that are supposed to establish this conclusion — arguments from the queerness of moral facts, from the independence of causal explanation from moral facts, from our evolutionary history — apply equally to epistemic reasons: reasons for belief, standards of rational inference, requirements of evidence-responsiveness. These epistemic reasons are no less metaphysically puzzling than moral reasons. But we cannot be epistemic error theorists: epistemic error theory would be self-undermining, for the very activity of reasoning to the error theory would be subject to it. Therefore the arguments for moral error theory prove too much, and error theory should be rejected. This is the companions in guilt strategy as its most sophisticated defenders — Terence Cuneo in The Normative Web (2007) and others — have pressed it. It is this argument I want to defeat.

On the internal tension in the argument

Cowie's dilemma: the CG theorist must understand epistemic reasons in a way that makes them genuinely analogous to moral reasons; but any such understanding either defeats the analogy or enables reductionMy argument against companions in guilt — what I have called the master argument, developed in my 2014 and 2016 papers — identifies an internal tension in the strategy. The companions in guilt theorist must understand epistemic reasons in a way that makes them a genuine companion for moral reasons. For the analogy to work, epistemic reasons must be as metaphysically robust as moral reasons: they must be genuinely categorical, not reducible to natural facts about reliability, truth-tracking, or evidential support. I now present a dilemma. Either epistemic reasons are reducible to evidential support relations — to facts about what evidence supports what beliefs — or they are not. If they are not so reducible — if epistemic reasons are robustly normative in the way the companions in guilt theorist needs — then the analogy may work, but epistemic error theory becomes available after all: there is no obvious obstacle to being an error theorist about robustly normative epistemic reasons if one is prepared to be one about moral reasons. If, on the other hand, epistemic reasons are reducible to evidential support relations, they can be naturalised in a way that moral reasons cannot, and the analogy fails: a companion who can be fully exonerated is not a companion in guilt at all.

On why epistemic reasons are reducible

Epistemic reductionism: epistemic reasons, unlike moral reasons, can be understood as evidential support relations naturalistically characterised; this breaks the parity the CG argument requiresI want to press the second horn of the dilemma. There is very good reason to think that epistemic reasons are, at bottom, relations of evidential support: to say that I have an epistemic reason to believe p is to say, roughly, that my evidence supports p. This is not a trivial claim, and I defend it at length in my 2014 paper and in Morality and Epistemic Judgement. The key observation is that evidential support is a relation that can be given a broadly naturalistic characterisation — in terms of truth-tracking, conditional probabilities, or reliable belief-forming processes — without residue. When we abstract away from the normative-sounding vocabulary, what remains is a set of relations among propositions and doxastic states that can be studied empirically. This is not true of moral reasons. The claim that gratuitous cruelty is morally wrong is not reducible to any naturalistic relation without distortion or without changing the subject. This asymmetry breaks the parity that companions in guilt requires. Epistemic normativity is innocent; moral normativity is not.

On entanglement and why it does not help

Entanglement: the CG theorist argues that moral and epistemic concepts are so intertwined in deliberation that they cannot be separated; Cowie argues this entanglement is superficial and does not defeat epistemic reductionismThe companions in guilt theorist has a response to epistemic reductionism. She argues that moral and epistemic concepts are so deeply entangled in deliberation — that our practices of giving and asking for reasons are simultaneously moral and epistemic — that they cannot be separated without distorting both. On this view, reductionism about epistemic reasons is not available without a corresponding reductionism about moral reasons, and since the latter is unacceptable, so is the former. I addressed this entanglement argument in my 2014 paper and I do not find it convincing. The entanglement of moral and epistemic vocabulary in ordinary deliberation is a surface feature of how we talk, not a deep metaphysical interdependence. We can prise apart the evidential relation — what the evidence actually supports — from the normative evaluation we make when we endorse an inference as “the right thing to believe.” The former is the epistemic reason properly so called; the latter is a moral or quasi-moral assessment superimposed on it. Entanglement in the phenomenology of reasoning does not establish that epistemic reasons share the metaphysical profile of moral reasons.

On the mathematics version of companions in guilt

The mathematics version: some CG theorists invoke mathematical facts rather than epistemic reasons; Cowie argues this version faces the same reductionist response and additional asymmetries concerning evolutionary explanationSome companions in guilt theorists, recognising the difficulties with the epistemic reasons version, have turned to mathematics as the companion. The idea is that mathematical facts are similarly abstract, similarly causally inert, and similarly resistant to naturalisation — yet we do not conclude that mathematical discourse is in error. I have examined this version in my chapter in the 2020 Routledge volume. The mathematics version faces two problems. First, it is not clear that mathematical Platonism is the default view: mathematical fictionalism and structuralism are serious positions, and a fictionalist about mathematics who is also a moral error theorist is not caught in any self-refutation. Second, even if we grant Platonism about mathematics, the evolutionary explanation of our mathematical beliefs differs structurally from the evolutionary explanation of our moral beliefs: mathematical truth is indirectly connected to physical structure in a way that moral truth is not, and this breaks the genealogical parity the companions in guilt argument requires.

On the upshot for moral error theory

Error theory vindicated: the failure of companions in guilt removes the most powerful objection to error theory; the error theorist is not committed to epistemic error theory and can continue to reason to her conclusionThe upshot of the failure of companions in guilt is that the moral error theorist is not hoist on her own petard. She can accept that epistemic reasons reduce to evidential support relations, endorse the standards of evidence and inference that constitute rational inquiry, and reason her way to the error-theoretic conclusion without any self-undermining. The companion who was supposed to share her guilt turns out to be innocent — but this is good news for the error theorist, not bad news. It means that her position is not internally incoherent. The error theory can be reasoned to, defended, and articulated using standards of reasoning that do not themselves presuppose categorical moral facts. The companions in guilt argument was the most important structural objection to error theory, and its failure clears the path for taking error theory seriously as the metaethical position that best accommodates our evolutionary history and our best metaphysics of normativity.

On what remains to be argued

The positive case: the failure of companions in guilt does not by itself establish error theory; that requires the positive debunking and queerness arguments; but it removes the most powerful defence against themLet me be clear about what the failure of companions in guilt does and does not establish. It does not by itself establish moral error theory. The positive case for error theory rests on the queerness arguments — that moral facts, as the realist conceives them, would be metaphysically and epistemologically queer — and on the evolutionary debunking arguments that Street, Joyce, and others have developed. What the failure of companions in guilt establishes is that these arguments cannot be deflected by the tu quoque move. The realist cannot say “your arguments prove too much, for they would equally defeat epistemic normativity.” She must engage with the queerness and debunking arguments on their own terms, and on their own terms they are, in my assessment, decisive. The failure of companions in guilt removes the realist’s most important defensive manoeuvre and leaves her facing the error theorist’s core arguments without cover.

I want to approach this debate from the origin of the challenge itself. The companions in guilt response has been directed at my Darwinian dilemma for realist theories of value, and I need to say clearly why the response fails to engage with the specific structure of that argument. The dilemma I posed is not a generic epistemological challenge about the difficulty of knowing facts in a given domain. It is a specific challenge arising from the particular relationship between evolutionary explanation and evaluative attitudes — a relationship that has no genuine parallel in the case of mathematics. The companions in guilt theorist has misread the dilemma and proposed a solution to the misreading.

On the structure of the Darwinian dilemma

The Darwinian dilemma: either our evaluative attitudes tracked moral facts (the tracking account) or they were adaptive for non-moral reasons (the adaptive link account); the tracking account is implausible, the adaptive link account undermines moral realismThe Darwinian dilemma has the following structure. Take the vast majority of our evaluative attitudes: the tendency to value our offspring, to care about the welfare of our social group, to feel aversion to danger. These attitudes were selected for by natural selection. Now the moral realist must explain why these evolutionarily selected attitudes tend to track mind-independent moral facts. There are two options. First, the tracking account: our attitudes track moral facts because natural selection favoured creatures whose attitudes were sensitive to moral facts. But this is implausible: there is no mechanism by which moral facts — if they are the mind-independent, non-natural facts the realist posits — could have exerted selection pressure on our ancestors. Second, the adaptive link account: our attitudes are adaptive for reasons entirely independent of their moral truth — they are selected because they promoted survival and reproduction, not because they tracked moral facts. This undermines the claim that our evaluative attitudes are reliable guides to moral facts. The dilemma is inescapable for the robust realist. Companions in guilt does not resolve it; it merely claims that mathematics faces the same dilemma. But it does not.

On why mathematics does not face the Darwinian dilemma

Mathematics escapes the dilemma: mathematical truth constrains physical reality; creatures with false arithmetic beliefs would miscalculate resources and be selected against; moral truth has no such physical footprintThe companions in guilt response claims that mathematical beliefs face the same Darwinian dilemma as moral beliefs. But the dilemma bites for a specific reason: moral facts, on realist accounts, are causally inert and entirely disconnected from the physical world in a way that prevents them from figuring in any explanation of our moral attitudes. Mathematical facts do not stand in this relationship. Even if one is a Platonist, it remains true that mathematical truth constrains what patterns are possible in the physical world. Creatures with false beliefs about basic arithmetic would have miscounted their food stores, their enemies, their allies, and their offspring; they would have been selected against. The evolutionary explanation of core mathematical beliefs cannot be given without implicit reference to mathematical structure. This is not true of moral beliefs: even creatures with entirely different moral attitudes — who valued cruelty over kindness, deception over honesty, exploitation over reciprocity — could in principle have survived and reproduced in a world where the moral facts were as the realist says they are. The genealogical explanations are asymmetric at the most fundamental level.

On constructivism as the correct response

Constructivism: moral facts are constructed from within the evaluative standpoint; the dilemma does not arise because there is no gap between evolved attitudes and the facts they constructMy own response to the Darwinian dilemma is not to deny that our evaluative attitudes were shaped by evolution, but to draw the correct metaethical conclusion from this fact. If our evaluative attitudes were shaped by natural selection, and if the facts about what we have reason to do or value are in some way grounded in our evaluative attitudes, then the debunking argument loses its force: there is no longer an explanatory gap between our attitudes and the facts they purportedly track, because the facts are not independent of the attitudes. This is the constructivist insight. On a Humean constructivist view, something counts as a reason for an agent if and only if it coheres with her evaluative attitudes on reflection; the facts about reasons are constructed from within the evaluative standpoint. The question “why do our evolved attitudes track moral facts?” does not arise, because the moral facts are not independent of the attitudes. The companions in guilt theorist assumes that moral realism is the correct view and then seeks to defend it against the debunking argument. I am challenging the assumption.

On the companions in guilt response to constructivism

Against constructivist debunking: the challenge to constructivism is structurally different from the Darwinian dilemma; it concerns normative authority, not unexplained correlationOpponents of constructivism have attempted to turn the Darwinian dilemma against constructivism itself. If our evaluative attitudes are the input to the construction of reasons, and those attitudes were shaped by natural selection, then the reasons we construct from those attitudes are similarly contingent — and constructed reasons have no claim to guide us that is independent of the evolutionary history that produced the inputs. I want to dispute this. The problem for moral realism is that there is a gap between our evolutionarily shaped attitudes and the mind-independent moral facts those attitudes are supposed to track. Constructivism closes this gap by denying that the facts are mind-independent. The challenge to constructivism has a different structure: it must show not that there is an unexplained gap, but that the constructed facts have insufficient normative authority to guide action. This is a serious challenge — but it is not the Darwinian dilemma, and it does not follow from the evolutionary story without additional premises that the constructivist will dispute.

On the realist’s options for explaining the correlation

The realist's options: coincidence, pre-established harmony, or third-factor account; all are implausible or unsubstantiatedThe moral realist who responds to my dilemma must eventually answer the first horn directly: she must explain why our evolutionarily shaped evaluative attitudes tend to track mind-independent moral facts. The options available to her are limited. She might claim that the correlation is a massive coincidence. This is desperately implausible. She might appeal to a pre-established harmony — God or some designer ensured the correlation — but this imports theological premises that most analytic realists are not prepared to accept. She might, following Enoch, appeal to a third-factor account: some common cause explains both the moral facts and the evolutionary outcome. Enoch proposes this in his 2010 paper in Philosophical Studies and develops it in Taking Morality Seriously (2011). But no plausible third factor has been identified. Enoch does not identify one; he argues that one might exist. A promissory note is not an epistemological account, and pointing to the parallel with mathematics does not discharge the obligation when the mathematical case has a concrete third factor — physical structure — that the moral case lacks.

On the Darwinian dilemma as a metaethical constraint

Metaethical constraint: any adequate metaethics must be compatible with our evolutionary origins; robust moral realism fails this constraint; constructivism and anti-realism do notThe Darwinian dilemma is not merely a puzzle about one epistemological challenge among many. It is a constraint on metaethical theorising: any adequate metaethics must be compatible with the fact that our evaluative attitudes are the products of natural selection operating on creatures with no sensitivity to mind-independent moral facts. This constraint rules out robust moral realism of the kind defended by Enoch, Shafer-Landau, and others. It does not rule out constructivism, quasi-realism, or anti-realism. The companions in guilt strategy attempts to lift this constraint by pointing to the parallel with mathematics. But the parallel is false — the genealogical explanations are asymmetric — and the constraint remains. Metaethics must take evolutionary biology seriously, and robust moral realism does not meet the resulting constraint.

On what the opposition must show

The opposition's burden: they must show that the evolutionary explanation of mathematical beliefs is as completely insulated from mathematical truth as moral explanation is from moral truth; this is implausible and unestablishedTo conclude. If the opposition wishes to defend companions in guilt against my Darwinian dilemma, they must show that the evolutionary explanation of mathematical beliefs is similarly insulated from mathematical truth as the evolutionary explanation of moral attitudes is from moral truth. They must show that a creature with systematically false core mathematical beliefs would have been equally well adapted — that mathematical truth played no more role in explaining mathematical belief than moral truth plays in explaining moral attitudes. This claim is very implausible and has not been established by any defender of companions in guilt. The parity claim fails at the most fundamental level of genealogical explanation, and no amount of pointing to the surface similarity between mathematical and moral abstractness will repair that failure.

I want to come at this debate from the perspective I took in my contribution to the 2020 Routledge volume that Cowie and Cosker-Rowland edited — a paper titled “Moral and Epistemic Normativity: The Guilty and the Innocent.” My position is that the companions in guilt argument fails not because both domains are epistemologically clear but because epistemic normativity, unlike moral normativity, can be given a naturalistically acceptable account that removes the alleged guilt. The epistemic companion is innocent.

On the companions in guilt argument and what it requires

The structure of the CG argument: epistemic facts are as problematic as moral facts; since epistemic error theory is unacceptable, moral error theory is too; the argument requires that moral and epistemic normativity are genuinely analogousThe companions in guilt argument, as it is directed at moral error theory, is primarily an argument from epistemic normativity. The claim is that moral facts and epistemic facts share the same metaphysical profile: both are categorical, irreducible, and mind-independent; both resist naturalistic reduction; both would be subject to queerness objections if the queerness objections were sound. Since epistemic error theory is clearly unacceptable — accepting it would undercut the very reasoning that supports the error theory — moral error theory must be rejected for the same reason. The argument requires genuine parity between the moral and epistemic cases. If any relevant asymmetry exists — if epistemic normativity has features that moral normativity lacks, or vice versa — the argument fails. I want to show that there is such an asymmetry, and that it is decisive.

On the asymmetry between moral and epistemic normativity

The asymmetry: epistemic norms can be given a naturalistic vindication through their connection to truth-conduciveness; moral norms cannot be similarly vindicated; the epistemic companion is innocentThe key asymmetry is this. Epistemic norms — norms of rational inference, of evidence-responsiveness, of belief-revision — can be given a naturalistic vindication that does not require positing categorical non-natural facts. The vindication runs through the goal of truth: epistemic norms are those that reliably lead to true beliefs, and this is a naturalistically respectable characterisation. We can explain why creatures ought to follow epistemic norms by pointing to the fact that following them tends to produce accurate representations of reality — a feature that is clearly adaptive. No parallel naturalistic vindication is available for moral norms. We cannot explain why creatures ought to follow moral norms by pointing to any adaptive or truth-conducive feature that does not already presuppose a moral framework. Epistemic norms are answerable to a natural standard — truth — that moral norms are not answerable to. This is the sense in which epistemic normativity is innocent: it can be exonerated by a naturalistically acceptable account. Moral normativity cannot be so exonerated, and error theory follows.

On the mathematics version and its failure

Mathematics version: mathematical Platonism faces the Benacerraf problem; but the error theorist's response is that fictionalism about mathematics is available, and the Benacerraf problem is structurally different from the moral debunking challengeThe companions in guilt argument is sometimes pressed using mathematics rather than epistemic normativity as the companion. The Benacerraf problem — how do we know abstract mathematical facts if they are causally inert? — is supposed to be structurally identical to the moral debunking challenge, and since we accept mathematical knowledge despite Benacerraf, we should accept moral knowledge despite the debunking argument. But this version of the argument faces a fundamental difficulty: mathematical fictionalism is a serious and defensible position. Hartry Field's programme, and subsequent developments of it, show that it is at least coherent to be a fictionalist about mathematics and to hold that mathematical discourse, while useful, does not describe a domain of abstract objects. A consistent error theorist about morality can be a fictionalist about mathematics, and neither position undermines the other. The companions in guilt move from mathematics therefore does not force a dilemma on the error theorist in the way the epistemic normativity version does.

On what epistemic error theory would actually entail

Epistemic error theory examined: the argument from self-refutation; the distinction between the self-refutation objection and the companions in guilt strategy; why epistemic error theory is unacceptable for independent reasonsI want to be careful about why epistemic error theory is unacceptable. The companions in guilt theorist sometimes runs together two different objections: the self-refutation objection and the companions in guilt objection. The self-refutation objection says that epistemic error theory is unacceptable because accepting it would undermine the very reasoning used to reach it: if there are no facts about what one epistemically ought to believe, then there is no fact about whether the evidence supports the error theory. The companions in guilt objection is different: it says that since epistemic error theory is unacceptable, and since moral and epistemic normativity are analogous, moral error theory is unacceptable for the same reason. My point is that even the self-refutation objection does not show that epistemic normativity is metaphysically robust in the way the companions in guilt argument needs. The self-refutation of epistemic error theory can be explained by the naturalistically available role of epistemic norms in truth-conducive reasoning, not by the existence of categorical non-natural epistemic facts. The two objections must be kept separate.

On the positive case for moral error theory

The normative web, as Cuneo describes it, abstracts away from the specific content of epistemic and moral norms and focuses on their shared structural features. The web is not as tightly woven as Cuneo suggests.

Mr President,

I want to begin by drawing a distinction that I think is essential to evaluating the companions in guilt debate clearly: the distinction between what I called, in my contribution to the 2020 Routledge volume, entailment-based and analogy-based companions in guilt arguments. These two kinds of argument have very different structures, face very different objections, and cannot be assessed as a single unit. Much of the confusion in this debate — on both sides — stems from failing to keep them apart. When they are distinguished, I think the opposition’s case is considerably stronger than the proposition has acknowledged.

On the two kinds of companions in guilt argument

Two kinds of CG: entailment-based arguments claim that accepting error theory about morality logically entails accepting it about the companion domain; analogy-based arguments claim only that the same considerations that support error theory about morality apply with equal force to the companionAn entailment-based companions in guilt argument claims that accepting moral error theory logically entails accepting error theory about the companion domain: moral and epistemic facts are so intertwined that one cannot hold error theory about the former without being committed to it about the latter. An analogy-based argument claims something weaker: that the same considerations that are supposed to support moral error theory — queerness, causal inertness, evolutionary debunking — apply with at least equal force to the companion domain, so that if we are not error theorists about the companion, we should not be error theorists about morality. Cowie’s master argument is primarily a response to the epistemic reasons companion in a near-entailment form. But the companions in guilt argument can take the analogy-based form with mathematics as the companion, and this version faces different objections. I want to show that the analogy-based version with mathematics survives Cowie’s arguments.

On the real problem with evolutionary debunking arguments

Hanson's equivocation claim: EDAs equivocate between two readings of their central claim; one reading is plausibly true but epistemically irrelevant; the other would have epistemic consequences but is falseIn my paper “The Real Problem with Evolutionary Debunking Arguments,” I argue that evolutionary debunking arguments against moral realism exploit an equivocation between two distinct readings of their central claim. The claim is that the evolutionary explanation of our moral beliefs is “independent of” or “insensitive to” moral truth. On the first reading, this means that the causal history of our moral beliefs does not include moral facts as causes: natural selection was not sensitive to moral truth in the way it was sensitive to, say, predator proximity. This reading is plausibly true — moral facts, if they are abstract and non-causal, do not enter into causal histories. But it has no direct epistemic relevance: the mere absence of moral facts from the causal history of our beliefs does not show that the beliefs are unreliable, because reliable belief-forming processes need not be causally sensitive to the facts they track. On the second reading, the claim is that our moral beliefs would have been the same even if the moral facts had been different — a counterfactual insensitivity claim. This reading would have epistemic consequences, but it is not established by the evolutionary story alone and requires independent argument. The debunking argument moves illicitly between these two readings, and once the equivocation is exposed, the argument loses its force.

On why the equivocation applies to mathematics too

Mathematics and the equivocation: the same equivocation afflicts any debunking argument applied to mathematical beliefs; this supports the companions in guilt moveThe equivocation I have identified in evolutionary debunking arguments about morality applies equally to any version of the debunking argument applied to mathematical beliefs. If the debunker argues that the evolutionary explanation of our mathematical beliefs is “insensitive to” mathematical truth in the first (causal) sense, this may well be true — abstract mathematical objects, like abstract moral facts, do not enter into causal histories. But this establishes nothing about the reliability of our mathematical beliefs, for the same reason it establishes nothing about our moral beliefs. If the debunker requires the second (counterfactual) reading — our mathematical beliefs would have been the same even if the mathematical facts had been different — this is very hard to establish and is implausible for core arithmetic. The companions in guilt move is vindicated by the equivocation analysis: both moral and mathematical debunking arguments exploit the same equivocation, and if the analysis is correct, both fail for the same reason.

On Cowie’s master argument and why it misses its target

Against Cowie's master argument: the dilemma presupposes that the only relevant CG argument is the epistemic reasons version; the analogy-based mathematics version is not subject to the master argument in the same formCowie’s master argument — the dilemma for companions in guilt using epistemic reasons — is a powerful response to the version of the argument that invokes epistemic normativity. But it does not touch the analogy-based version that uses mathematics as the companion. The master argument turns on whether epistemic reasons are reducible to evidential support relations: if they are, they can be naturalised and are not a genuine companion; if they are not, epistemic error theory becomes available. This dilemma is specifically designed for epistemic reasons. Mathematical facts do not face an analogous dilemma: there is no naturalistically respectable reduction of mathematical facts to physical or causal facts in the way there might be for epistemic support relations, and mathematical error theory is not obviously self-undermining in the way epistemic error theory is. Cowie has constructed a powerful master argument against one version of companions in guilt; the version using mathematics is not similarly constrained.

On the positive case: why the moral realist’s position survives

The positive case: the equivocation analysis undermines the debunking argument against moral realism; the moral realist can acknowledge causal inertness of moral facts without conceding counterfactual insensitivityThe positive case for the moral realist runs as follows. The debunking argument, properly understood, establishes only that moral facts do not enter into the causal history of our moral beliefs. This is something the non-naturalist moral realist can simply accept: moral facts are abstract and non-causal; of course they are not among the causes of our beliefs. What the debunker must further establish — and has not established — is that our moral beliefs would have been the same even if the moral facts had been different. This counterfactual claim cannot be read off from the causal story alone; it requires a substantive argument about the modal relationship between our evolved moral sensibilities and the moral facts. The realist can resist this further step by arguing that our moral faculties, however they evolved, are genuinely sensitive to moral facts in the way required for knowledge — not through causal tracking but through whatever mode of access is appropriate for abstract facts. The debunking argument has not closed off this possibility.

On the asymmetry between moral and aesthetic realism and what it reveals

Moral and aesthetic realism: arguments against moral realism do not obviously give us more reason to reject it than to reject aesthetic realism; the asymmetry claim is harder to defend than commonly assumedIn my paper on moral and aesthetic realism, I argue that it is surprisingly difficult to find principled points of contrast between the two domains that could justify treating moral realism as more philosophically problematic than aesthetic realism. This has implications for the companions in guilt debate: if the considerations that are supposed to undermine moral realism — queerness, evolutionary debunking, causal inertness — apply equally to aesthetic realism, and if we are not prepared to be aesthetic error theorists, we should not be moral error theorists either. The failure of the asymmetry claim between morality and aesthetics is a further companions in guilt move that extends the strategy beyond mathematics and epistemic normativity. The error theorist must explain why she singles out morality for error-theoretic treatment when the same arguments threaten closely analogous domains that she is not prepared to treat in the same way.

On what a satisfying response to the debunking argument requires

If the debunker requires the counterfactual reading, she needs an independent argument for it, and the companions in guilt move shows that any such argument will face parallel difficulties in mathematics and elsewhere. The moral realist’s task is not to answer a fully specified debunking argument but to resist the slide from the causal-inertness reading to the counterfactual reading — and the equivocation analysis is precisely the tool for doing so.

I approach this debate as a defender of robust moral realism — the view that there are objective, mind-independent normative facts that we can access — and I want to offer a response to the evolutionary debunking argument that is, I think, more direct than the companions in guilt strategy. My response does not merely point to a companion and say “you face the same problem.” It challenges the debunking argument at its source: in the assumption that the evolutionary story generates undercutting defeat for moral beliefs in the first place.

On the third-factor account

Third-factor account: a common cause may explain both our evaluative attitudes and the moral facts; this is no more speculative than analogous third-factor accounts accepted in other domainsThe central challenge posed by Street’s Darwinian dilemma is explanatory: the realist must explain the correlation between our evolutionarily shaped evaluative attitudes and the mind-independent moral facts. I accept the explanatory challenge and I propose what I call a third-factor account in my 2010 paper in Philosophical Studies and in Taking Morality Seriously. Perhaps there is a common explanation of both our evaluative attitudes and the moral facts — something that explains both why creatures like us were selected to have the attitudes we have and why those attitudes tend to track what is morally required. The most promising candidate is something in the vicinity of facts about what is practically necessary or what conditions cooperative social life for creatures with our capacities and needs. This is not obviously more speculative than the third-factor accounts we accept in other domains — consider our acceptance that our logical and mathematical faculties track logical and mathematical truth, not because those truths caused the evolution of those faculties, but because there is a common explanation available through the structure of the world and the demands of effective reasoning within it.

On deliberative indispensability

Deliberative indispensability: irreducibly normative truths are indispensable for practical deliberation; this indispensability gives us reason to believe in them independently of epistemological considerationsI want to make an argument that is independent of the third-factor account and that provides an independent basis for accepting robust normative realism. I call it the argument from deliberative indispensability, and I develop it in Taking Morality Seriously. The argument is modelled on the Quine-Putnam indispensability argument for mathematical entities: just as we are committed to mathematical entities because they are indispensable to our best scientific theories, we are committed to irreducibly normative truths because they are indispensable to practical deliberation. We cannot deliberate — cannot genuinely ask what we ought to do — without presupposing that there are facts of the matter about what we ought to do. The error theorist will say that we can deliberate using constructed norms or useful fictions. But I submit that the practical stance of treating normative facts as genuinely there — as constraints on deliberation rather than products of it — is not eliminable without distorting deliberation beyond recognition. Deliberative indispensability gives us reason to accept normative realism that is independent of, and survives, the evolutionary debunking argument.

On what the debunking argument actually establishes

What debunking establishes: the evolutionary story shows that natural selection was not sensitive to moral truth; it does not show that our moral beliefs are therefore unreliable; the inference requires ruling out third-factor accountsI want to challenge the inference that Street and Joyce draw from the evolutionary story. The evolutionary story shows that natural selection was not directly sensitive to moral truth when it shaped our evaluative attitudes. It does not follow that our moral beliefs are therefore unreliable. Consider a parallel: natural selection shaped our faculties for colour discrimination without being sensitive to facts about surface spectral reflectance. Yet we do not conclude that colour perception is unreliable: we accept that there is an indirect connection between the adaptive function of colour perception and the physical facts about reflectance. The debunker must show not merely that selection was not sensitive to moral truth but that there is no indirect connection — no third factor — between the adaptive outcome and the moral truth. She has assumed the absence of such a connection; she has not established it. And until she does, the inference from “selection was not sensitive to moral truth” to “our moral beliefs are unreliable” does not go through.

The debunker holds that moral beliefs are undermined by the evolutionary story because the belief-forming processes were not sensitive to moral truth. But this standard, if applied consistently, would undermine many belief-forming processes that the debunker accepts. Our faculties for logical reasoning, our modal intuitions, our sense of mathematical necessity: all were shaped by natural selection without direct sensitivity to logical, modal, or mathematical truth as such. The evolutionary debunker selectively applies her epistemological standard to morality while granting legitimacy to these other domains. The companions in guilt strategy exposes this selectivity and demands that it be justified. The debunker must either apply her standard consistently — embracing a sweeping scepticism that extends far beyond morality — or revise the standard in a way that accommodates mathematical and logical knowledge. On the authority of moral demands

Authority of moral demands: only robust realism can explain why moral requirements have unconditional authority over self-interest; this is an independent reason to accept realism that the debunking argument's epistemological focus missesThere is a further consideration in favour of robust moral realism that I develop in Taking Morality Seriously: only robust realism can vindicate the authority of moral demands over self-interest. When morality requires that we act against our interests — that we keep our promises when breaking them would benefit us, that we refrain from exploitation when exploitation would be advantageous — the realist can say that we are required to do so because the moral facts genuinely demand it, independently of our preferences. The constructivist says that moral demands are authoritative because they follow from our evaluative attitudes on reflection — but if those attitudes are contingent evolutionary products, their authority is conditional on our having those contingent attitudes. The error theorist says that moral demands have no genuine authority at all. Only robust realism gives moral demands the unconditional authority they appear to have, and this appearance is not a mere illusion that can be explained away by the evolutionary story without loss.

The debunking argument is powerful precisely because it exploits an epistemological naturalism: the view that belief-forming processes must be naturalistically explicable and causally sensitive to the truths they track. Given this background, the evolutionary story about moral beliefs looks very threatening. But epistemological naturalism is itself a contested philosophical commitment, not a deliverable of natural science. The companions in guilt strategy exposes this commitment by extending its implications: if naturalism requires scepticism about the abstract objects of mathematics, the response of most philosophers will be to revise naturalism rather than accept mathematical scepticism. Companions in guilt makes visible the hidden philosophical price of epistemological naturalism and invites us to pay that price by revising the naturalist assumption rather than by accepting scepticism about normativity.

Mr President,

I want to approach this debate from a starting point that both sides have perhaps insufficiently appreciated: the genuine depth and unresolved character of the epistemological problem for abstract objects. Paul Benacerraf’s 1973 paper “Mathematical Truth” identified what remains, more than fifty years later, one of the most serious open problems in the philosophy of mathematics: if mathematical objects are abstract and causally inert, how do we know anything about them? No satisfying solution has been found. I want to use this unresolved problem as the foundation of the companions in guilt argument in its strongest form: the problem of knowing abstract objects applies equally to mathematics and to morality, and since we are not prepared to be mathematical sceptics, we are not licensed to be moral sceptics either. The correct response is acceptance of both.

On the Benacerraf problem and epistemological quietism

Benacerraf's unresolved challenge: fifty years after it was posed, no satisfying epistemology for mathematical Platonism has been found; yet we accept mathematical knowledge; the correct attitude is epistemological quietismBenacerraf’s challenge has not been solved. In the fifty years since its formulation, the philosophy of mathematics has produced many responses — structuralism, neo-logicism, plenitudinous Platonism, fictionalism — but no response has achieved consensus, and none has given a fully satisfying account of how our mathematical beliefs could be tracking abstract mathematical facts given that those facts are causally inert and our belief-forming processes are causally sensitive only to the concrete world. Yet we continue to accept mathematical knowledge. We treat mathematical practice as epistemologically legitimate and set aside the Benacerraf challenge as a philosophical puzzle that does not threaten mathematical practice even if we cannot solve it. I want to argue that the correct attitude toward the moral debunking argument is epistemological quietism of the same kind. We accept that we have moral knowledge, treat moral practice as legitimate, and set aside the debunking challenge as a philosophical puzzle that does not threaten moral practice even if we cannot solve it. The companions are genuinely in the same epistemic boat.

On the structural identity of the epistemological challenge

Abstract objects symmetry: both mathematical objects and moral facts are abstract, mind-independent, and causally inert; the epistemological challenge is structurally identical for both domains and predates evolutionary considerationsI want to press the structural symmetry between the epistemology of mathematical objects and the epistemology of moral facts. Mathematical objects — numbers, sets, functions — are abstract: not located in space and time, not perceivable by the senses, causally inert. Moral facts — that gratuitous cruelty is wrong, that justice requires impartiality — are also abstract: not spatiotemporally located, not perceivable by the senses, causally inert on the realist account. The epistemological challenge in both cases is the same: how do we come to know non-causal, abstract facts by means of belief-forming processes that are causally sensitive only to the concrete world? This challenge is not essentially evolutionary. It predates Darwin and would apply even in a world without natural selection. The evolutionary debunking argument is a specific, historically situated version of a much older challenge — the challenge of knowing abstract objects — and this older challenge applies with equal force to mathematics and to morality.

On the failure of mathematical nominalism

Nominalism fails for mathematics: mathematical fictionalism has not persuaded most philosophers; if we are not mathematical error theorists despite Benacerraf, parity requires rejecting moral error theoryOne response to Benacerraf’s challenge is to be a mathematical nominalist or fictionalist: to hold that mathematical discourse, while useful, does not describe abstract objects. Hartry Field’s programme of showing that science can be reformulated without commitment to mathematical objects is the most serious attempt. But mathematical fictionalism has not persuaded most philosophers of mathematics: the indispensability argument has proved robust, and the technical difficulties of Field’s programme have been widely noted. The resistance to mathematical error theory is significant: despite the Benacerraf challenge, the philosophical community continues to treat mathematical Platonism as a serious and defensible position. I note this not to endorse Platonism unqualifiedly, but to make the companions in guilt point: if we are not prepared to accept mathematical error theory despite the Benacerraf challenge, consistency requires that we not accept moral error theory on the basis of a structurally parallel challenge — unless we can identify a relevant asymmetry. The proposition has argued for asymmetry; I will show that the asymmetry does not hold.

On the indispensability argument extended to morality

Moral indispensability: moral facts are indispensable to our best explanations of human action and social life; this argument parallels the Quine-Putnam argument for mathematical entitiesThe Quine-Putnam indispensability argument holds that we are committed to mathematical entities because they are indispensable to our best scientific theories: we cannot adequately formulate physics without quantification over numbers and functions. I want to extend this argument to moral realism. Moral facts — facts about reasons for action, obligations, and values — are plausibly indispensable to our best explanations of human behaviour and social life. We explain why a person kept a promise by citing their sense of obligation; we explain why an institution commands obedience by citing the norms it embodies; we explain historical events by citing the moral motivations of the agents involved. If moral facts are eliminated or replaced by constructed norms or useful fictions, our explanatory practice is impoverished in ways that may have no fully adequate substitute. The indispensability of moral facts to explanation of human agency is a reason to accept moral realism that parallels the reason for accepting mathematical Platonism.

On Street’s asymmetry claim and its difficulties

Against Street's asymmetry: the claim that mathematical truth constrains physical reality is puzzling for Platonism; if abstract mathematical objects constrain the causal order, this is as mysterious as any moral realist claimStreet argues that mathematical truth constrains physical reality — that there cannot be two apples and two apples making five — and that this gives mathematical beliefs an indirect connection to mathematical truth through the physical world that moral beliefs lack. I want to press on this argument. If mathematical truth, understood Platonistically as a body of abstract, causally inert facts, constrains what is possible in the physical world, this is itself deeply mysterious. How do causally inert abstract objects constrain the causal order? Street’s argument appears to require that abstract mathematical objects have some modal grip on physical reality — that they make certain physical configurations impossible. But this modal grip is exactly what Benacerraf’s challenge puts in question: if mathematical objects are causally inert, they cannot enforce anything. Street’s asymmetry either dissolves into the very puzzle she is trying to use as a disanalogy or it requires a substantive account of how abstract objects constrain physical reality — an account that the moral realist can then demand for moral facts as well.

On quietism as the correct response

Quietism defended: the philosophical puzzle does not defeat the claim to knowledge in either domain; theoretical virtues outweigh the epistemological cost; this is the attitude already applied to mathematicsI want to defend epistemological quietism as the correct response to both Benacerraf’s challenge and the moral debunking argument. The quietist does not claim to have solved the epistemological problem; she claims that the epistemological problem, while real, does not constitute a decisive reason to abandon either mathematical Platonism or moral realism. The grounds for this: first, that the alternative views face serious difficulties of their own; second, that the theoretical virtues of Platonism and moral realism — their explanatory power, their coherence with practice, their vindication of the authority and objectivity of mathematical and moral claims — outweigh the epistemological cost; and third, that the epistemological challenge is not unique to these domains but affects knowledge of abstract objects generally. Quietism is not intellectual timidity; it is the recognition that philosophical puzzles about the conditions of knowledge do not automatically defeat the claim to know, and that our practice of accepting mathematical and moral claims as knowledge is not irrational pending a philosophical solution.

On what the companions in guilt debate has achieved

The debate's contribution: companions in guilt has shown that the epistemological challenge to moral realism is part of a broader challenge to the epistemology of abstract objects; this is its deepest and most important insightI want to close by marking what I take to be the most important contribution of the companions in guilt debate to analytic philosophy. The debate has shown, conclusively in my view, that the epistemological challenge to moral realism is not a special and uniquely devastating challenge that singles out morality for scepticism. It is part of a broader challenge to the epistemology of abstract objects — a challenge that affects mathematics, logic, and modality no less than morality. No domain has fully resolved this challenge. The correct response — in mathematics as in morality — is not scepticism but acceptance combined with continued philosophical enquiry. The companions in guilt strategy, at its best, is an invitation to do epistemology at the right level of generality: to develop an account of how we know abstract facts, which will illuminate both mathematical and moral epistemology simultaneously. That project is more important than the purely defensive use of the strategy, and it is what I hope this debate will motivate on both sides.
