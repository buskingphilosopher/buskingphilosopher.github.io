“This House Would Mandate Genetic and Biomedical Interventions to Enhance the Cognitive and Moral Capacities of the Human Species”
Proposition (Pro-Mandate) • Savulescu · Bostrom  —  Opposition (Against) • Sandel · Agar · Buchanan · DeGrazia
Procreative Beneficence & Moral BioenhancementJulian Savulescu — Oxford Uehiro Centre for Practical Ethics
We have a moral obligation — Procreative Beneficence — to select children who will have the best possible lives, including the best possible moral and cognitive capacities. Moral bioenhancement is not merely permissible but obligatory: our evolved moral psychology is dangerously inadequate for the catastrophic risks of the twenty-first century. We must use the tools of genetics and neuropharmacology to build the moral and cognitive architecture that our situation demands. The mandate is justified because the stakes are existential.

Transhumanism, Human Dignity & the Continuity ArgumentNick Bostrom — Future of Humanity Institute, Oxford
Human biological nature is not sacrosanct. The history of civilisation is the history of transcending our biological limitations — through medicine, education, institutions, and technology. Cognitive and moral enhancement through genetic and biomedical means is continuous with this history, not a rupture from it. The concept of a fixed human nature that enhancement would violate is philosophically incoherent. The objections to mandated enhancement are, on inspection, either objections to coercion generally (which apply equally to education and vaccination mandates we already accept) or objections grounded in a fetishisation of the biological status quo.

Logical Arguments

Chair,

I want to begin with the moral situation we actually face, because the opposition will present this debate as if it were a philosophical seminar about abstract enhancement principles, and it is not. It is a debate about whether we are willing to use the tools available to us to address the most serious moral and existential risks facing our species. Those risks are real, they are escalating, and they are, in large part, the product of a moral psychology that evolution equipped us for a world that no longer exists.

“Safe, effective moral enhancements, if they existed, would be obligatory — not merely permissible. The stakes are too high to leave moral improvement to chance, culture, or the lottery of genetic endowment.”
i. procreative beneficence and the obligation to enhance

Procreative Beneficence — couples have an obligation to select children expected to have the best life; this extends to cognitive and moral capacities; failing to enhance when we can is a moral failure analogous to failing to treat preventable diseaseProcreative Beneficence holds that couples who are choosing between possible children — through IVF, pre-implantation genetic diagnosis, or selection — have a moral obligation to select the child who is expected to have the best life, other things being equal. This is not a controversial claim: most people already accept that we should avoid selecting children who will have severe genetic diseases when a healthy alternative is available. The principle extends naturally to positive selection for better cognitive and moral capacities, because these capacities are among the most important determinants of a good life and of the ability to live well in relation to others. The question is whether we are obligated not merely to avoid selecting for deficiency but to select for flourishing. My answer is yes. Failing to provide a child with the best available start in cognitive and moral capacity, when we could do so, is a moral failure — not as serious as neglect or abuse, but a failure nonetheless, analogous to declining available vaccination or nutrition.

ii. the mismatch between our evolved psychology and our situation

Evolved psychology mismatch — our moral psychology evolved for small-group, face-to-face, short-term interactions; it is systematically miscalibrated for the collective action problems, long-term risks, and scale of harm that define the twenty-first centuryOur moral psychology was shaped by evolution for the conditions of the Pleistocene: small hunter-gatherer groups, face-to-face interactions, immediate threats, short time horizons, and in-group/out-group dynamics that were adaptive in a world of competing bands. We inherited from this history a set of cognitive biases and motivational limitations that are profoundly maladaptive for our current situation. We are poor at responding to statistical risks that are spatially or temporally remote: climate change, pandemic preparedness, nuclear proliferation, and artificial intelligence risk all require exactly the kind of long-range, impartial, large-scale moral concern that our evolved psychology systematically underweights. We are subject to parochialism, tribalism, present bias, and scope insensitivity — the inability to respond proportionately to large numbers of distant sufferers. These are not character flaws to be corrected by better education. They are deep features of our motivational architecture, produced by millions of years of selection for a different environment. Biomedical and genetic interventions that could recalibrate this architecture are not luxuries. Given the risks, they are necessities.

iii. the mandate: why voluntary enhancement is insufficient

Mandate case — voluntary enhancement fails because of collective action problems: each individual has incentives to free-ride on others' moral enhancement while defecting themselves; only mandate resolves this; analogous to vaccination mandatesThe opposition will argue that enhancement should be voluntary rather than mandated. I want to press on why this is inadequate. Moral enhancement faces a collective action problem of the same structure as vaccination: each individual has an incentive to defect — to rely on others being morally enhanced while remaining unenhanced themselves, retaining competitive advantages over those whose moral inhibitions are strengthened. In a world where some are morally enhanced and others are not, the unenhanced may exploit the enhanced, undermining the entire project. The only solution to collective action problems of this structure is coordination through mandate — exactly as we mandate vaccination to achieve herd immunity against disease. We already accept that individuals may be required to be vaccinated against communicable diseases, to wear seatbelts, to submit to compulsory education. Each of these mandates overrides individual preference in the name of collective and individual benefit. The mandate for moral and cognitive enhancement is structurally identical.

iv. responding to sandel: the gift of giftedness misunderstood

Against Sandel — Sandel's giftedness argument proves too much: it condemns all medicine, education, and nutrition as failures to accept the "unbidden"; the distinction between treatment and enhancement is philosophically incoherentSandel argues that there is something morally significant about accepting the child we are given — that the drive to enhance is an expression of a hyperparenting hubris that destroys the “openness to the unbidden.” I want to say directly: this argument proves too much. Medical treatment, nutrition, education, exercise, language acquisition — all of these shape the child’s capacities in ways that nature did not determine. We do not say that teaching a child to read destroys the openness to the unbidden because they might have been illiterate. We do not say that treating childhood malnutrition is a failure to accept the gift of a slower-developing brain. Sandel draws a line between treatment and enhancement, but he cannot draw it coherently. The treatment/enhancement distinction tracks no morally relevant property of the interventions themselves; it tracks only our contingent familiarity with some interventions and unfamiliarity with others. As cognitive and moral enhancement becomes familiar, Sandel’s intuition will fade — as intuitions grounded in unfamiliarity always do.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Procreative Beneficence: the obligation to select for the best life extends to cognitive and moral enhancement
1.Procreative Beneficence: couples using reproductive technology have a moral obligation to select the child expected to have the best life, other things being equal. This is accepted for disease avoidance.
2.Cognitive and moral capacities are among the most important determinants of a good life and of beneficial social contribution. The principle extends to positive selection for these capacities.
3.Failing to provide the best available cognitive and moral start when we could do so is a moral failure, analogous to declining available vaccination or optimal nutrition.
∴Procreative Beneficence grounds an obligation to select for enhanced cognitive and moral capacities; declining to do so when we can is a morally significant failure, not a morally neutral choice.
Argument II — Our evolved psychology is systematically miscalibrated for twenty-first-century existential risks; biomedical correction is necessary
1.Our moral psychology evolved for Pleistocene conditions: small groups, immediate threats, short time horizons. It is systematically biased toward parochialism, present-focus, in-group preference, and scope insensitivity.
2.Twenty-first-century existential risks (climate change, pandemic, nuclear proliferation, AI risk) require exactly the long-range, impartial, large-scale moral concern that our evolved psychology systematically underweights.
3.These biases are deep motivational features, not correctable by education alone. Biomedical interventions that recalibrate the motivational architecture are necessary given the stakes.
∴The mismatch between our evolved psychology and our existential situation is severe; biomedical recalibration of moral motivation is not a luxury but a necessity given the stakes.
Argument III — Voluntary enhancement fails due to collective action problems; mandate resolves them, as vaccination mandates do
1.Moral enhancement faces a collective action problem: individuals have incentives to defect while others are enhanced, exploiting the morally enhanced. Voluntary uptake produces an unstable mixed population.
2.Collective action problems of this structure are resolved by mandate. We already mandate vaccination, seatbelts, and compulsory education on analogous grounds.
3.The mandate for moral and cognitive enhancement is structurally identical to accepted mandates: it overrides individual preference in the name of individual and collective benefit that cannot be achieved by voluntary means.
∴Voluntary enhancement is insufficient due to collective action problems; mandate resolves these problems on the same grounds as accepted vaccination and education mandates.
Argument IV — Sandel’s giftedness argument proves too much; the treatment/enhancement distinction is incoherent
1.Sandel argues that enhancement destroys “openness to the unbidden” and the giftedness of human capacities. But medicine, nutrition, and education all shape capacities nature did not determine, without attracting this objection.
2.The treatment/enhancement distinction tracks no morally relevant property; it tracks familiarity. Treating childhood malnutrition does not violate giftedness; cognitively enhancing a child does not violate it either.
3.An argument that condemns all capacity-shaping interventions proves too much; an argument that permits familiar ones while prohibiting unfamiliar ones is not principled but merely conventional.
∴The giftedness argument proves too much and rests on an incoherent treatment/enhancement distinction that tracks familiarity rather than any morally relevant property of the interventions.

Chair,

I want to press a question that lies beneath everything the opposition will say: what exactly is it about the biological status quo that makes it morally sacrosanct? What is the argument that the specific cognitive and moral capacities that evolution has equipped us with represent a peak or an ideal that should not be transcended? I have been pressing this question for twenty years, and I have never received a satisfying answer.

“We have an opportunity to move beyond the constraints of our evolutionary heritage. To fail to take this opportunity, when the means are available, is not humility; it is a failure of moral imagination and moral seriousness.”
i. the continuity argument: enhancement and human self-improvement

Continuity — the history of civilisation is the history of transcending biological limitations through culture, medicine, and technology; genetic and biomedical enhancement is continuous with this history; the objection to enhancement is objection to human self-improvement as suchConsider what human beings have already done to transcend the limitations of their biological nature. Writing externalises memory beyond the capacity of individual brains. Mathematics allows reasoning about quantities and relationships beyond intuitive grasp. Medicine eliminates diseases that evolution “intended” to kill us. Education transmits accumulated knowledge across generations, allowing each generation to start far beyond where biology alone would place them. Institutions — laws, contracts, markets, democratic procedures — structure collective action in ways that overcome the coordination failures our evolved psychology produces. None of these interventions left us less human, less ourselves, less authentic. They extended and enriched what human beings can be and do. Genetic and biomedical enhancement is continuous with this history. The only difference is that it operates at the level of the underlying biological substrate rather than through external tools and cultural structures. If the continuity thesis is right, the objection to biomedical enhancement is either an objection to this specific level of intervention (which requires justification) or an objection to human self-improvement as such (which is plainly wrong).

ii. against the species-norm argument

Against species norm — Buchanan argues enhancement violates species-typical norms; but species-typical norms have no moral authority; they reflect evolutionary contingency, not moral optimality; we are not obligated to remain within the parameters of our evolutionary heritageBuchanan and others appeal to what I call the species-norm argument: that there is something morally significant about the species-typical range of human capacities, and that enhancement that moves beyond this range is distinctively problematic. I want to challenge this directly. Species-typical capacities are whatever natural selection happened to produce in our lineage under the specific environmental pressures we faced. They are not optimal by any criterion except reproductive fitness in ancestral environments. They are not good for us in any morally relevant sense. They are not good for others. They are simply what happened. The fact that a capacity is species-typical gives it no moral authority. If a species-atypical capacity would enable us to live better, to treat each other better, to avoid catastrophic collective failures — there is no moral reason grounded in species typicality to forgo it. The worship of species typicality is a form of naturalistic fallacy: the inference from “this is how we naturally are” to “this is how we ought to be.”

iii. the expressive objection and disability

Expressive objection — the claim that enhancement expresses a negative judgement about those with lower capacities; Bostrom responds that enhancement expresses the value of the enhanced capacities, not a negative verdict on the unenhancedOne powerful objection to enhancement, pressed by disability rights theorists, is the expressive objection: that selecting for enhanced capacities expresses the judgement that people who currently have lower capacities are less valuable or less fully human. I want to separate two things. There is a question about what enhancement expresses, and there is a question about what it does. Enhancement expresses the value we place on certain capacities — cognitive clarity, emotional resilience, moral motivation — in the same way that treating disease expresses the value we place on health. It does not express a negative verdict on those who currently lack the enhanced level. A world in which everyone has access to better cognitive and moral tools is not a world that devalues those who currently have less. It is a world with less cognitive and moral suffering. The expressive objection would, if taken seriously, prohibit the treatment of intellectual disabilities — a conclusion essentially no one accepts.

iv. the mandate and Agar’s radical posthumanism objection

Against Agar — Agar argues radical enhancement produces posthuman beings who share too little with us to be in moral community; Bostrom replies that gradual enhancement preserves continuity; the posthuman is an extension of the human, not its replacementAgar argues that radical enhancement would produce beings so different from us that they would share too little with present humans to maintain moral community — that we would be producing our own successors rather than improving ourselves. This is a serious objection to radical enhancement. It is not a serious objection to the moderate, targeted enhancements that this motion actually defends. Enhancing cognitive processing speed, working memory, attention span, impulse control, and empathic accuracy does not produce a posthuman being. It produces a somewhat better human being — as cognitively and morally capable, relative to current humans, as current humans are relative to our pre-literate ancestors. We did not become posthuman when we acquired literacy. We became more fully what we could be. The radical posthumanism objection, even if it would succeed against Bostrom’s more extravagant speculations, does not succeed against the moderate enhancement mandate this debate concerns.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The continuity argument: biomedical enhancement is continuous with civilisation’s history of transcending biological limitations
1.Writing, mathematics, medicine, education, and institutions all transcend biological limitations without making us less human or less ourselves. They extend what human beings can be and do.
2.Genetic and biomedical enhancement operates at the level of the biological substrate rather than through external tools, but the moral logic is the same: extending human capacities beyond what evolution provided.
3.The objection to biomedical enhancement must therefore either provide principled grounds for distinguishing this level of intervention from accepted ones, or it is an objection to human self-improvement as such.
∴Biomedical enhancement is continuous with civilisation’s history of self-improvement; objectors must provide principled grounds for distinguishing it from accepted interventions or concede that the objection targets self-improvement generally.

∴The expressive objection fails: enhancement expresses the value of capacities, not condemnation of the unenhanced; the objection proves too much by equally condemning accepted medical treatments.
Argument IV — Agar’s radical posthumanism objection targets speculative extremes, not the moderate enhancement this motion defends
1.Agar argues radical enhancement produces posthuman beings too different from us for moral community. This might be a serious objection to speculative radical enhancement.
2.The motion concerns targeted enhancements to cognitive processing, memory, attention, impulse control, and empathic accuracy — not species-transforming radical posthumanism.
3.These moderate enhancements improve human capacities without producing beings discontinuous with humanity, just as literacy improved our capacities without making us posthuman.
∴Agar’s radical posthumanism objection does not apply to the moderate targeted enhancements this motion defends; it targets speculative extremes while the motion concerns achievable improvements in degree, not kind.
Giftedness, Hubris & the Ethics of CareMichael Sandel — The Case Against Perfection (2007)
The drive to enhance our children and ourselves through genetic and biomedical means represents a kind of hubris — a Promethean aspiration to remake human nature according to our own design that forecloses the “openness to the unbidden” that is morally essential to unconditional love, parental responsibility, and solidarity with the less gifted. The treatment/enhancement distinction is not incoherent; it tracks the distinction between correcting deficits relative to the human norm and exceeding that norm through technological intervention. The problem with enhancement is not primarily the risks; it is what it expresses about our relationship to human life as we receive it.

Justice, Species Norms & the Institutional AlternativeAllen Buchanan — Beyond Humanity? (2011) & Better Than Well (with Brock, Daniels & Wikler, 2000)
The case for mandated enhancement rests on a false dichotomy between biological enhancement and the status quo. A third option — institutional and social reform — is available and superior. Biomedical enhancement that is not preceded by institutional reform will exacerbate existing inequalities, producing a cognitively and morally enhanced elite while leaving the structurally disadvantaged behind. Even if cognitive and moral enhancement were desirable, the mandate is unjustified. Mandated biomedical intervention that alters the cognitive and motivational architecture of persons without their consent violates the deepest conditions of personal autonomy and self-authorship. Chair,

Savulescu says the treatment/enhancement distinction tracks only familiarity, and that as enhancement becomes familiar the intuition will fade. I want to suggest that this response shows, rather than refutes, what is at stake. The discomfort we feel about mandated genetic and biomedical enhancement is not a contingent reaction to unfamiliarity that will dissolve with exposure. It is a moral signal about something important: our relationship to the children we bring into being and to the human form we share.

“The deepest moral objection to enhancement is not the risk of designer babies or the hubris of playing God. It is something harder to name: the impulse to master and control the terms on which we receive life forecloses the kind of unconditional acceptance that love and solidarity require.” — Sandel
i. openness to the unbidden and unconditional love

Giftedness — the drive to enhance removes the unconditional character of parental love; love that depends on the child having the right genetic specifications is not love but conditional approval of a product we designedParental love, at its best, is unconditional: it does not depend on the child meeting specifications or exceeding benchmarks. A parent who loves their child because the child has been genetically optimised for the best possible life is not loving the child unconditionally; they are loving the product of their design choices. This matters morally because the unconditional character of parental love is one of its most important features: it models for the child a form of acceptance that is not contingent on performance, and it grounds the parent’s willingness to care for the child regardless of how the child turns out. The drive to design children with the best possible cognitive and moral capacities undermines this unconditional character. It transforms the parent from a steward of a new life into an engineer of a product whose specifications have been set in advance. Savulescu says this proves too much because we already vaccinate and educate. But vaccination and education respond to the child as they are; genetic optimisation designs what the child will be before they exist. The distinction is not familiarity; it is the difference between stewardship and engineering.

ii. the treatment/enhancement distinction reconsidered

Treatment vs. enhancement — the distinction tracks something real: treatment restores or approximates species-typical functioning; enhancement exceeds it; the moral significance is that species-typical functioning defines the norm relative to which we owe each other equal concernSavulescu says the treatment/enhancement distinction is incoherent because both treatment and enhancement shape capacities nature did not determine. But the distinction does track something morally real, even if it is not perfectly sharp. Treatment aims at restoring or approximating species-typical functioning — the range of cognitive, physical, and moral capacities that characterise a normal human life. Enhancement aims at exceeding this range. The moral significance of species-typical functioning is not that it is natural but that it defines the norm relative to which we recognise each other as fellow human beings deserving equal concern. A world in which some have genetically enhanced moral capacities and others have species-typical ones is not a world of equals: it is a world in which those with enhanced capacities may feel (and may be) morally superior to those without, with all the implications for solidarity and mutual recognition that this entails. The worry is not that enhancement will make people better; it is that it will make some people better than others in ways that undermine the equal moral standing that democratic life requires.

iii. solidarity and the unconditional character of equal moral standing

Solidarity — solidarity with the less gifted requires accepting that differential natural endowment does not determine moral worth; enhancement that ties moral standing to designed capacity rather than shared humanity erodes this solidarityConsider what grounds our moral solidarity with people whose cognitive and moral capacities differ from our own: people with intellectual disabilities, people with severe depression, people who have been deprived of moral education and whose moral psychology has been damaged by their circumstances. What grounds our equal moral concern for them is not their capacity level but their shared humanity. A world in which some people’s moral capacities have been biomedically enhanced to levels far exceeding those of unenhanced people is a world in which the basis of this solidarity is eroded. The enhanced can no longer claim that differential moral capacity does not determine moral worth, because they have deliberately engineered moral capacity differentials. The mandate does not merely enhance some people; it produces a world with a designed hierarchy of moral capacity, and the social and political implications of this hierarchy are not ones that Savulescu’s framework has adequately addressed.

iv. the hubris of moral designers

Hubris — who designs the moral enhancement, and by whose values? The mandate requires a prior agreement on what constitutes enhanced moral capacity; this agreement is itself a moral and political problem of the first orderSavulescu speaks of enhancing moral capacities as if it were clear what enhanced moral capacity consists in. But moral philosophy has been debating what virtue consists in for two and a half thousand years without reaching consensus. What exactly are we enhancing? Utilitarian impartiality and long-range concern? Deontological respect for persons? Communitarian solidarity and care? These are not the same capacities; in some situations they are in tension with each other. The person who has been biomedically enhanced to be a committed utilitarian will behave differently from the person enhanced to be a committed Kantian. The mandate requires that the designers of the enhancement programme settle these moral philosophical questions in advance and impose their answers on the entire species. This is not humility; it is the most breathtaking act of moral hubris imaginable.

Chair,

I want to engage directly with Bostrom’s continuity argument, because it is the most sophisticated defence of enhancement and the one that most needs careful critical attention. The argument runs: cognitive and biomedical enhancement is continuous with the cultural and technological self-improvement that has always characterised human civilisation, and therefore objections to it must either identify a principled distinction between this kind of improvement and the accepted kinds, or concede that the objection targets self-improvement as such. I want to show that there is such a principled distinction, and that the continuity argument breaks at precisely the point where enhancement becomes radical.

i. where the continuity breaks

Continuity breaks — cultural and technological self-improvement preserved cognitive continuity between generations; radical cognitive enhancement breaks this continuity; radically enhanced beings are not better versions of us but a different kind of beingBostrom says writing, mathematics, medicine, and education all extended human capacities beyond what biology alone provides, and that biomedical enhancement is continuous with these extensions. The key question is: what kind of continuity did these cultural and technological innovations maintain? They maintained cognitive continuity between generations. The child who learns to read and write is cognitively continuous with the illiterate parent: they can understand each other, reason together, share a moral and emotional world. The acquisition of literacy did not produce beings who were cognitively inaccessible to the unlettered. Radical cognitive enhancement — enhancement of the order of magnitude that Bostrom’s transhumanism envisions — would break this continuity. A being whose working memory, processing speed, and reasoning capacity are orders of magnitude beyond the current human range would not be cognitively accessible to unenhanced humans in the way that a literate person is accessible to an illiterate one. The enhanced being would not simply know more or reason faster; they would inhabit a cognitive world that unenhanced humans cannot enter.

ii. the obsolescence problem

Obsolescence — if radically enhanced beings are cognitively and morally superior to unenhanced ones, the latter become effectively obsolete; this is not enhancement of humanity but its replacement by a successor speciesConsider the implications of radical cognitive and moral enhancement for the relationship between enhanced and unenhanced humans. If the enhanced have vastly superior cognitive capacity, they will be able to solve problems that unenhanced humans cannot, make decisions that unenhanced humans cannot evaluate, and exercise moral judgement that unenhanced humans cannot assess. In what sense are unenhanced humans still full participants in the moral and political community of the enhanced? The political and moral structures of liberal democracy — equal standing, deliberation, mutual accountability — all presuppose rough cognitive parity between participants. A world with radically enhanced and unenhanced humans is not a world of rough cognitive parity; it is a world in which unenhanced humans are, relative to the enhanced, in the position that a child is to an adult, or that a chimpanzee is to a human. The enhanced will make decisions for the unenhanced, govern them, and determine what counts as adequate moral reasoning. This is not a world of expanded human flourishing; it is a world in which the unenhanced have been made effectively obsolete.

iii. liberal eugenics and the limits of parental choice

Liberal eugenics — even liberal enhancement frameworks must impose limits on parental choice; once we accept that some enhancements are permissible, we need a principled account of where the limit lies; the mandate problem shows this account is unavailableIn my earlier work on liberal eugenics, I argued that while some forms of genetic selection might be permissible within a liberal framework, there must be principled limits on parental enhancement choices — specifically, that enhancements which would make the child alien to human moral community should not be permitted. The mandate in this debate’s motion is the most extreme version of the liberal eugenics programme: it not only permits but requires enhancement of all children, eliminating the parental choice that liberal eugenics was supposed to preserve. The proposition cannot have it both ways. If enhancement is so clearly beneficial that it should be mandated for all, the liberal framework of parental choice is abandoned. If the liberal framework is maintained, the mandate is unjustified. And if the mandate is pursued, the question of where radical enhancement ends and human obsolescence begins cannot be deferred; it must be answered before the first intervention is administered.

Argument I — The continuity argument breaks at radical enhancement: cultural self-improvement preserved cognitive continuity; radical enhancement breaks it
1.Cultural and technological self-improvement (literacy, mathematics, medicine) maintained cognitive continuity between enhanced and unenhanced: a literate person is cognitively accessible to an illiterate one; they share a moral and emotional world.
2.Radical cognitive enhancement of the order Bostrom envisions would break this continuity: a being with orders-of-magnitude greater cognitive capacity would inhabit a cognitive world inaccessible to unenhanced humans.
3.The principled distinction between accepted self-improvement and radical enhancement is cognitive continuity. The continuity argument therefore succeeds up to the point of radical enhancement and fails beyond it.
∴The continuity argument breaks at radical enhancement; cognitive continuity is the principled distinction between accepted self-improvement and enhancement that produces beings cognitively inaccessible to unenhanced humans.
Argument II — Radical enhancement produces cognitive obsolescence of unenhanced humans; this is replacement, not improvement

I want to press what I think is the most practically important objection to the mandate: the justice objection. Savulescu and Bostrom present the debate as one between enhancement and the biological status quo. But this is a false dichotomy. There is a third option: institutional, social, and educational reform that improves the cognitive and moral performance of the existing population without the justice problems that mandated biomedical enhancement would generate.

i. the false dichotomy: enhancement vs. the status quo

False dichotomy — the choice is not between enhancement and the status quo; institutional reform, education, and resource redistribution can improve moral and cognitive performance without the justice problems of biomedical enhancementSavulescu argues that our moral psychology is dangerously inadequate for twenty-first-century challenges and that biomedical enhancement is necessary to address this inadequacy. But the moral and cognitive failures he identifies are not primarily the product of biological limitations. They are the products of unjust institutions, inadequate education, poverty, and maldistributed opportunity. The residents of wealthy, well-educated, politically stable societies already display the long-range concern, impartiality, and co-operative behaviour that Savulescu’s enhancement programme is supposed to produce. The residents of poor, poorly-educated, politically unstable societies do not — not because of their biology but because of their circumstances. If we want to improve the moral performance of the human species, the most direct route is not biomedical enhancement of the advantaged; it is the improvement of the institutions and living conditions that currently prevent the disadvantaged from exercising whatever moral capacities they already have.

ii. the access and inequality problem

Justice objection — biomedical enhancement that is not universally accessible will exacerbate existing inequalities; the cognitively and morally enhanced elite will compound their advantages; the mandate cannot guarantee universal access in the current global institutional contextSavulescu’s response is that the mandate resolves the access problem by requiring universal enhancement. This is the theoretically correct response. But it faces an overwhelming practical objection: there is no institutional mechanism for ensuring universal access to biomedical enhancement in a world of sovereign states, vastly unequal health systems, and contested global governance. The vaccination analogy fails here in a critical respect: even with enormous international effort, we have not achieved universal vaccination for existing vaccines. The COVID-19 pandemic showed, as clearly as any event in recent history, that wealthy countries will prioritise their own populations in accessing scarce medical interventions regardless of international obligations. A mandated biomedical enhancement programme in this world is not a universal enhancement programme; it is a programme for the wealthy and well-governed, which will produce exactly the cognitive and moral enhancement gap between elite and non-elite populations that Savulescu claims to want to prevent.

I want to focus on something that the rest of this debate has somewhat underweighted: the specific wrong of the mandate, as opposed to the wrong of enhancement as such. Savulescu and Bostrom are arguing for mandated enhancement, not merely permissible enhancement. I want to show that even if we were to grant everything the proposition says about the desirability of enhanced cognitive and moral capacities, the mandate would still be deeply wrong — because of what it does to the persons who are mandatorily enhanced, and because of what it does to the nature of moral agency itself.

i. personal identity and the conditions of self-authorship

Self-authorship — persons are self-authors of their character; mandatory biomedical alteration of cognitive and motivational architecture without consent violates the conditions of self-authorship; this is distinct from education, which works through the person's own rational processesA person’s character — their cognitive style, their moral motivations, their emotional responses — is not merely a set of properties they happen to have. It is, in an important sense, who they are. The conditions of personal identity over time are partly constituted by psychological continuity: by the persistence of memories, values, intentions, and character traits that make a later stage of a person continuous with an earlier stage. Mandatory biomedical alteration of a person’s cognitive and motivational architecture without their consent is not merely an interference with their preferences; it is an alteration of who they are. It changes the person rather than influencing them. This is categorically different from education and cultural influence, which work through the person’s own rational processes: they present reasons, make arguments, and rely on the person’s own capacity for reflection to produce change. Mandatory biomedical enhancement bypasses these rational processes entirely: it installs cognitive and moral dispositions directly into the motivational architecture, circumventing the reflective processes through which a person becomes the author of their own character.

ii. authentic moral agency vs. biomedical compliance

Authentic agency — a person whose moral motivations have been biomedically installed rather than developed through their own reflection is not a more moral person but a more compliant one; moral virtue requires that good acts flow from character the agent has developedThere is a distinction, drawn clearly in the Aristotelian tradition and in contemporary virtue ethics, between acting morally and acting compliantly. A person who does the right thing because they have developed virtuous character through experience, reflection, and moral education is acting morally. A person who does the right thing because a neuropharmacological intervention has installed the relevant disposition directly into their motivational system is acting compliantly — as compliantly as a thermostat that maintains the right temperature. The distinction matters morally: moral virtue, on the Aristotelian account, requires that good actions flow from a stable character that the agent has developed through their own choices and reflection. A character that has been installed rather than developed is not, in the relevant sense, the agent’s own character. The mandatorily enhanced person is not a more virtuous person; they are a person with better-calibrated motivational dispositions. The difference is the difference between a person and a well-designed mechanism.

iii. savulescu’s collective action argument and its limits

Against collective action mandate — the vaccination analogy fails: vaccination protects against external pathogens; moral enhancement alters the person's own constitutive psychology; the latter involves a kind of self-violation that the former does notSavulescu argues that voluntary enhancement fails because of collective action problems, and that the mandate is justified on the same grounds as vaccination mandates. I want to press the disanalogy. Vaccination protects against an external pathogen that threatens the vaccinated person and others. It does not alter the person’s own constitutive psychology, values, or character. The mandate for vaccination is a constraint on what a person may do with their body in relation to external threats; it is not a requirement that they change who they are. Mandatory moral and cognitive enhancement is categorically different: it requires that persons be altered at the level of their constitutive psychology. Savulescu is not merely requiring that people take a precaution against an external threat; he is requiring that they become different people — people with a different motivational architecture, different cognitive style, and different moral dispositions. No accepted mandate goes this far. The vaccination analogy does not hold.

iv. the open future and children

Open future — children have a right to an open future; mandatory cognitive and moral enhancement forecloses developmental possibilities and installs a specific character before the child can reflectively endorse it; this is a wrong to the future personJoel Feinberg identified a right of children to an open future: the right not to have their major life options foreclosed before they reach the age of rational self-determination. Mandatory genetic and biomedical enhancement is the most radical possible violation of this right. It installs cognitive and moral dispositions at the genetic level or in early development, before the child exists as a reflective agent, based entirely on the judgements of designers about what cognitive and moral architecture is best. The child who reaches adulthood will have a character that was determined for them before they were born, by people who are not them, according to values they were never given the opportunity to endorse or reject. This is not a gift; it is a foreclosure. Procreative Beneficence, as Savulescu formulates it, claims to give the child the best possible life. But a life whose fundamental character was determined before the child could participate in that determination is not the best possible life; it is a life whose most important dimension — who one is — was decided without one’s consent.
