A Guide to Phonaesthetics

Published April 11, 2026

The Plosives (Stops)

These are sounds where the breath is completely blocked and then released in a "burst." They are used for strength, aggression, and decisiveness.

Velar Plosives (/k/, /g/): Produced at the back of the throat (the soft palate).

Effect: Sharp, "clacking," or guttural. (e.g., "Cut the king's crown.")

Bilabial Plosives (/p/, /b/): Produced by the lips.

Effect: Explosive, punchy, or "popping." (e.g., "Build Back Better.")

Alveolar Plosives (/t/, /d/): Produced by the tongue against the ridge behind the teeth.

Effect: Tapping, clinical, or percussive. (e.g., "Deadly data.")

The Fricatives (Friction Sounds)

These involve air being forced through a narrow channel, creating turbulence. They often suggest tension, breathlessness, or smoothness.

Glottal Fricative (/h/): Produced in the throat (the glottis).

Effect: Breathless, ghostly, or urgent. It can sound like a sigh or a gasp. (e.g., "Horrid haunting haste.")

Sibilants (Sibilant Fricatives) (/s/, /z/, /ʃ/ as in "sh"):

Effect: Hissing, sinister, or calming. In poetry, this is sigmatism. (e.g., "The serpent slithered through the sea.")

Labiodental Fricatives (/f/, /v/): Teeth against the lower lip.

Effect: Airy, light, or fleeting. (e.g., "Flighty fancy.")

Dental Fricatives (/θ/ as in "thin", /ð/ as in "then"): Tongue between the teeth.

Effect: Soft, lisping, or muddy.

The Liquids and Nasals (The "Soft" Sounds)

These allow air to flow continuously and are used to create flow, harmony, or mourning.

Lateral Liquid (/l/):

Effect: Fluid, melodic, and lingering. (e.g., "Lullaby of leaves.")

Rhotic Liquid (/r/):

Effect: Rolling, driving, or growling. (e.g., "Roaring rage.")

Nasals (/m/, /n/, /ŋ/ as in "sing"):

Effect: Humming, somber, or resonant. These are often used for pathos or heavy emotion. (e.g., "Mournful murmurs.")

The Affricates

A hybrid of a plosive and a fricative — a stop followed immediately by a friction release.

Postalveolar Affricates (/tʃ/ as in "church", /dʒ/ as in "judge"):

Effect: Churning, heavy, or mechanical. (e.g., "Changing the justice.")

How to Analyse Alliteration:

Don't just name the sound; describe the physicality.

Basic Analysis: "The author uses alliteration with the letter 'k'.

Advanced Analysis: "The author utilizes a series of velar plosives, creating a discordant, 'clacking' auditory texture that mirrors the fracturing of the political landscape described in the text."
