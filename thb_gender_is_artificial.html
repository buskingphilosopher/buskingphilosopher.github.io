Mr President,

I want to begin with a fact that most people in this room do not know, and that changes the terms of the debate before it has started. Somewhere between 1.7% and 2% of all humans born are intersex — born with chromosomal, gonadal, or anatomical characteristics that do not fit neatly into the categories of male or female as standardly defined. This is roughly the same frequency as red hair. We do not consider the existence of red hair an anomaly requiring medical correction. And yet, for most of the twentieth century, intersex infants were subjected to surgical interventions designed not to treat any medical condition but to make their bodies conform to a binary that nature itself had declined to observe.

My argument is that nature does not generate the binary of man and woman. We generate it, impose it on nature, and then — in a move that should embarrass us — cite nature to justify the imposition.

On what sex actually is

Biology does not generate a strict binaryChromosomes: approximately 1 in 650 people have arrangements other than XX or XY — XXX, XXY, XYY, X0, and cellular mosaics. If the binary is defined by chromosomes, what category do these individuals belong to? Hormones: testosterone and oestrogen are both present in all human bodies; the difference is in concentration, not kind. The International Olympic Committee spent decades trying to find a hormonal threshold for athletic competition and failed — repeatedly, expensively, revealingly — because the threshold is not a natural fact waiting to be measured but a normative decision waiting to be made. Anatomy: the genitalia of intersex individuals form a continuous spectrum. The attempt to define what counts as a clitoris versus a small penis has required practitioners to make decisions — not measurements. These decisions are culturally informed and normatively structured. They are not readings of a natural fact.

On the origin of the binary in social practice

The binary is maintained by social practiceWhen a child is born, the first social act is assignment to a sex category — made on the basis of a brief visual inspection of genitalia, recorded on a legal document, triggering a cascade of social expectations: names, clothing, toys, emotional registers, professional expectations. The child does not encounter "sex" as a natural fact and then receive social treatment appropriate to it. The child receives a social assignment and is then shaped by that assignment in ways subsequently taken to confirm the natural fact. Nature provides variation. Society imposes dichotomy.

On "woman" as not a natural kind

Woman is not a natural kindThe category "woman" does not pick out a natural kind the way "gold" or "water" does. Gold is gold in virtue of its atomic structure — a shared underlying property. If "woman" were a natural kind, there would be some underlying property all and only women have that makes them women. No such property exists. XX chromosomes fail — post-menopausal women cannot gestate; women with complete androgen insensitivity syndrome are XY. Ovaries fail — women with oophorectomies. Capacity to gestate fails — excluded members. The category is held together not by a shared underlying property but by a cluster of overlapping, criss-crossing, family-resemblance features — and by social coordination around the use of the term. Family-resemblance categories are useful. But they are artificial in the relevant sense: they reflect a human way of carving up the world, not a binary nature itself has carved.

Mr President,

My argument is less about the biology of sex than about the history and philosophy of classification. I want to argue that the categories of man and woman are artificial not merely because biology fails to sustain them, but because they have a contingent, revisable, interest-laden history — precisely what we would expect from a human invention rather than a natural discovery.

On the looping effect of human kinds

The looping effect — human kinds respond to classificationNatural kinds do not change their behaviour in response to being classified. Gold does not alter its atomic structure because we call it gold. Electrons do not adjust their charge because physicists classify them as leptons. Human kinds are different: when human beings are classified, they respond. They take up the classification, resist it, modify it, redefine it, escape it, or inhabit it in unexpected ways. Hacking calls this the "looping effect." The categories of man and woman exhibit precisely this looping structure. The Victorian "woman" of medical discourse — nervous, delicate, confined to the domestic sphere, physiologically unsuited to intellectual exertion — is not the same category as the twenty-first century woman. Not merely because women's social circumstances have changed. But because the category itself has been modified by the people classified within it, by their resistance, their advocacy, their redefinition. Natural kinds do not do this. Gold is not modified by chemists' classifications. This is strong evidence that the category is a human construction.

On historical and cross-cultural contingency

Cross-cultural variation reveals the binary's contingencyThe binary of man and woman is not a universal human category in the anthropological sense. Many cultures have recognised categories that do not map onto it — the hijra in South Asian cultures, the two-spirit in many Native American traditions, the fa'afafine in Samoa, the bakla in the Philippines, the sekrata in Madagascar, the sworn virgins of the Balkans. The existence of these categories does not establish that binary sex is false — but it does establish that the binary is not an inevitable human response to a natural fact. Different human societies, confronting the same biological variation, have carved that variation differently. This is precisely what we would expect of an artificial category shaped by social purposes rather than natural necessity.

On Kuhn and theory-dependent natural kinds

Natural kinds are theory-dependent — and so are man and womanKuhn showed that even in natural science, what counts as a natural kind is theory-dependent. The category "planet" did not include Pluto until 1930 and excluded it again from 2006 — not because Pluto changed, but because the theoretical purposes served by "planet" changed. The category "fish" has been abandoned by professional biologists because it does not correspond to a monophyletic group. The category "jade" turned out to pick out two distinct minerals sharing surface properties. Scientific categories are revised, abandoned, and replaced when they fail to do the theoretical work required. The category "woman" has never been given a rigorous theoretical definition that picks out a natural kind. Every attempt has either excluded clear members or included clear non-members. This is what we would expect of an artificial category trying to pass as a natural one.

Mr President,

My colleagues have argued that the categories are artificial because biology does not sustain a binary and because their history reveals their constructed character. I want to ask a question we rarely ask about fundamental social categories: what are these categories for?

On the social role of gender categories

Gender categories organise social hierarchiesThe categories of man and woman do not merely describe a pre-existing division in human reality. They organise and maintain a particular social structure. They assign roles, distribute resources, allocate burdens, and legitimate hierarchies. The category "woman" has been used — historically and continues to be used — to identify a class of persons excluded from certain forms of power, assigned certain forms of labour, and subjected to certain forms of control. This is not a conspiracy theory. It is a sociological observation. The category is not a neutral description of a natural fact. It is a normatively loaded classification that does work in the social world — work that serves particular interests and disadvantages others.

On the woman who is not treated as a woman

Woman is not a natural kindConsider a thought experiment. A person is born with XX chromosomes, female reproductive anatomy, and all the biological features my opponents would cite as constituting "woman." But this person is raised as a man, presents as a man, is treated as a man throughout their life — never subjected to the distinctive treatment women experience, never expected to perform the labour associated with femininity, never subject to the sexual violence disproportionately directed at women. In what sense is this person a woman? If the category "woman" is merely biological, they are a woman regardless of social experience. But if "woman" is a social position — a location in a system of social practices, expectations, and distributions of power — then this person is not a woman in the sense that matters morally and politically. I argue that the morally and politically relevant sense is the social sense. "Woman" as social position is not a natural kind — it is a social position systematically naturalised to make it seem inevitable and beyond political contestation.

On the argument from malleability

Cross-cultural variation reveals the binary's contingencyIf "man" and "woman" were natural kinds, their content would be relatively fixed across cultures and historical periods — as the properties of gold are fixed. But what it means to be a man — the virtues, behaviours, emotional repertoires, professional roles assigned to men — differs dramatically. The Athenian aristocratic man, the Victorian English gentleman, the contemporary American working-class man, the Confucian scholar-official, and the Zulu warrior share the grammatical category "man" while having almost nothing else in common. If "man" were a natural kind, this variation would be inexplicable. If "man" is an artificial category whose content is shaped by social purposes and historical contingency, the variation is exactly what we would expect.

Mr President,

The proposition has made a sophisticated case. I want to make an equally sophisticated response — one that takes the philosophical challenge seriously without conceding a conclusion that does not follow from any of the arguments made.

On the confusion of "artificial" with "socially influenced"

Biology does not generate a strict binaryThe proposition has established that biological sex is variable rather than strictly binary, that the categories have a history and exhibit Hacking's looping effect, and that they do work in the social world. I accept all three claims. I deny that any of them establishes that the categories are artificial in the sense the proposition requires. Consider the category "river." A river is not a natural kind in the strict sense — geographers disagree about when a stream becomes a river, when a river becomes a delta, when a distributary is a separate river or a branch of the same one. The category has a history, is culturally variable, and reflects human interests in naming and navigating landscapes. But rivers are real. The difference between a river and a field is not artificial. "River" carves nature at a real — though fuzzy, interest-laden, historically variable — joint. The categories of man and woman may be like rivers. They may not carve nature at a perfectly sharp binary joint. But it does not follow that they carve nothing real.

On what sex differences actually are

The binary is maintained by social practiceThe proposition correctly notes that sex characteristics form a spectrum rather than a strict binary. But it has understated the degree to which, within that spectrum, the distribution is strongly bimodal. The distribution of most sexually differentiated characteristics — gonadal type, chromosomal arrangement, hormonal profile, secondary sexual characteristics — is not uniform across the human population. It forms two large clusters, with a relatively small number of people between them. The existence of people between the clusters does not establish that the clusters do not exist. The existence of twilight does not establish that there is no distinction between day and night. The bimodal distribution is itself a biological fact — and it is a fact that the categories of man and woman, however inexactly, track. The proposition's argument from intersex individuals establishes that the binary is not exhaustive. It does not establish that the binary is artificial.

On Hacking's looping effect and what it actually proves

The looping effect — human kinds respond to classificationThe looping effect argument is philosophically interesting, but I want to challenge what it establishes. The looping effect shows something about the causal consequences of classification — not about the ontological status of what is classified. Consider the category "patient zero" in epidemiology. The people classified as "patient zero" respond to the classification — seeking media attention, legal protection, or anonymity. The classification has social effects. But the underlying biological fact — that one person was the source of a particular chain of transmission — is real. The looping effect shows that social use of a classification has consequences. It does not show that the classification fails to pick out something real.

Mr President,

My colleague has challenged the proposition's argument from natural kinds. I want to make a different argument — one that takes the proposition's philosophical moves seriously and meets them on their own ground.

On the proposition's implicit standard for naturalness

Woman is not a natural kindThe proposition implicitly assumes that a category is natural only if it picks out a natural kind in the strict essentialist sense — defined by a shared underlying property necessary and sufficient for membership. By this standard, "woman" is not a natural kind. But by this same standard, almost nothing is a natural kind. The category "gene" is not a natural kind — after a century of molecular biology, there is no single agreed definition. The category "species" is not a natural kind — biologists have proposed over twenty definitions, none capturing all cases. The category "organism" is not a natural kind — colonial organisms and endosymbiotic relationships create genuine boundary cases. If the proposition's standard disqualifies "woman," it also disqualifies most of the categories of biology. If the proposition wants to say that biology's categories are also artificial, it is committed to a thoroughgoing anti-realism about biological classification that it has not defended and that most biologists would reject.

On the difference between artificial and imprecise

Biology does not generate a strict binaryThere is a distinction the proposition has elided: between a category being artificial and a category being imprecise or graded. The proposition has shown that the man/woman binary is imprecise. This is true. But imprecision is not artificiality. The category "mountain" is imprecise — geographers disagree about the minimum elevation for a mountain, there is no precise boundary between a mountain and a hill. But mountains are real. The Himalayas are not a social construction. Sex — the division of organisms into reproductive types based on gamete size — is a real biological phenomenon, conserved across hundreds of millions of years of evolution, grounded in a deep functional asymmetry: large gametes and small gametes. The categories of male and female track this asymmetry. The tracking is imperfect — some humans do not fit the binary — but imperfect tracking of a real phenomenon is not the same as tracking nothing real.

On the costs of the proposition's position

Gender categories organise social hierarchiesIf the categories of man and woman are entirely artificial — if they do not pick out anything real — then the claim that women, as a group, have been systematically disadvantaged becomes very difficult to state. The proposition's third speaker argued that "woman" picks out a social position in a hierarchy. But if "woman" is a pure social construction, then it picks out nothing independently of the social practices that constitute it. And if it picks out nothing independently of those practices, then the claim that women have been oppressed — that there is a group, identified independently of the oppressive practices, who has been subjected to those practices — becomes incoherent. You cannot simultaneously claim that "woman" is a pure social construction and that women have been materially disadvantaged. The material disadvantage requires a prior identification of the group that has been disadvantaged.

Mr President,

My colleagues have challenged the proposition's argument from natural kinds and from the costs of constructionism. I want to close with what I consider the most important philosophical move available to the opposition — one that concedes much of what the proposition says while denying its conclusion.

On kind pluralism

Natural kinds are theory-dependent — and so are man and womanThe proposition has argued as if there is one question — "are man and woman artificial categories?" — with one answer. This is the wrong framing. The terms "man" and "woman" do not pick out a single kind. They are used in multiple overlapping ways serving different purposes. There is a biological sense of "woman" — tracking sexually differentiated characteristics of female humans, imprecisely but genuinely. There is a social sense — tracking membership in a group subject to distinctive norms, expectations, and forms of treatment. There is a phenomenological sense — tracking a self-understanding, an identity, a way of experiencing one's body and place in the social world. These are distinct — and none of them is purely artificial. The biological sense is not purely artificial because it tracks real biological features. The social sense is not purely artificial because it tracks real social facts — which humans are assigned to which social positions. The phenomenological sense is not purely artificial because it tracks real psychological facts about self-understanding. The proposition has conflated these senses and argued that because none picks out a perfectly sharp natural kind, all are artificial. The conflation does not hold.

On what kind pluralism implies

Cross-cultural variation reveals the binary's contingencyIf "man" and "woman" pick out multiple overlapping kinds, the proposition's claim that they are artificial is neither straightforwardly true nor false. It is true that no single natural kind is picked out the way gold picks out a natural kind. It is false that the terms pick out nothing real. They pick out a cluster of real — biological, social, phenomenological — facts about human beings, through multiple overlapping descriptions serving different purposes. Most categories — biological, chemical, social — are better understood as cluster concepts, family-resemblance categories, or homeostatic property clusters. Man and woman are artificial by the strict essentialist standard. By any more realistic standard of what categories are and how they work, they are not.

On reconstructing rather than abandoning

Gender categories organise social hierarchiesThe genuine insight in the proposition's case is this: the categories of man and woman, as historically deployed, have been used to sustain and legitimate a social order in which the attributes of "woman" were defined in ways that excluded women from power, naturalised their subordination, and made their disadvantage appear as a consequence of nature rather than politics. This critique is correct, important, and needs to be heard. But the appropriate response to the ideological misuse of a category is not to declare it artificial and abandon it. It is to reconstruct the category — to separate what is genuinely tracked from what is ideologically imposed, to use it in ways that serve the interests of those classified within it. The categories of man and woman are real, imprecise, multiply constituted, historically contested, and in need of constant critical reconstruction. They are not artificial. They are permanently under revision.
