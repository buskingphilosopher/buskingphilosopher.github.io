A Debate on the History of England
Chesterton's Revisionist History vs The Medieval Record
G. K. Chesterton · A Short History of England (Chatto & Windus, 1917)
Rodney Hilton · Bond Men Made Free (1973) & the tradition of empirical medieval history
The Revisionist
G. K. Chesterton
A Short History of England (1917)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Professor Hilton,

I begin, as I begin my book, with a confession and a challenge. The confession: I am not a trained historian. The challenge: I believe I know something that trained historians, almost without exception, have contrived not to know — namely, the actual history of the English people, as distinct from the history of the English governing class, which is what passes for English history in most popular accounts. I write history from the standpoint of a member of the public, which is to say, from the standpoint of the people about whom the history was actually made and who are, in the telling, almost universally left out of it.

The central argument of my book is this: the standard narrative of English history is not merely incomplete. It is inverted. It presents as progress what was actually a series of catastrophes for the common people. It presents as liberation what was actually a consolidation of oligarchic power. It presents the Middle Ages as a dark, ignorant, oppressive prologue to the enlightened modernity that replaced them — and in doing so, it conceals from the English working man the most important truth about his own past: that he has been systematically robbed.

I. The popular histories lie by omission: what they leave out of "Merry England"

Popular history suppresses medieval popular civilisation in favour of elitesConsider what the popular encyclopaedias and school histories taught the English carpenter or bricklayer about his own past. He was told about the Magna Carta — one charter, given to barons, substantially in the interest of barons. He was not told about the charters given to carpenters, to coopers, to every trade and craft that constituted medieval civic life. The medieval city was a city of charters. Society was not a pyramid of power with the poor crushed at its base but a web of corporate bodies — guilds, parishes, fraternities — each with its own charter, its own self-governance, its own dignity. The carpenter who did not know this could not know what had been taken from him. The whole thrust of popular historiography has been to make the English poor forget that they were ever anything but poor — to present their current condition as natural and inevitable rather than as the consequence of deliberate acts of expropriation that can be dated, named, and condemned.

II. The guilds: self-government from below, not from above

Medieval guilds as genuine popular self-government — democracy before democracyThe medieval guild was one of the most remarkable political inventions in human history, and it has been almost completely written out of the popular understanding of the past. Men banded together in guilds and parishes long before Local Government Acts were dreamed of. The guilds were not invented by the state and handed down to the people. They were invented by the people and the state was eventually prevailed upon to recognise them. They were self-governing bodies that set wages, maintained quality, trained apprentices, cared for sick members and their widows, and gave the craftsman a dignity and a corporate identity that no modern trade union — for all its indispensable virtues — has been able to replicate. The modern trade union is a defensive organisation, a last-ditch bulwark against the power of capital. The medieval guild was an offensive organisation — it embodied the positive claim of the craftsman to own the conditions of his own work. That distinction is everything, and historians who treat the guild as a primitive precursor to the union have understood neither.

III. Magna Carta: a barons' document, but not only that

Magna Carta: important precisely because it was not democratic — that is Chesterton's pointI am often misread on Magna Carta, and I want to be precise about what I actually say. I do not deny that Magna Carta was primarily a barons' document, designed to limit royal power in the interests of those barons. I insist upon it. My argument is the opposite of the Whig one: I say that Magna Carta was not the beginning of English democracy but the beginning of the best method ever devised for indefinitely postponing English democracy — by substituting the power of the great landlords for the power of the king, and calling the substitution liberty. When Parliament was first summoned, it was summoned by kings to find out what ordinary people thought about taxation — a genuinely popular impulse. Parliament became a governing class, not a popular body, almost immediately. The lesson is that the institutions we are told celebrate popular liberty were, from the beginning, mechanisms by which one set of rich men displaced another, and the poor were used as leverage in the contest and then discarded when the contest was won.

IV. The Peasants' Revolt of 1381: the king was with the people — Parliament was not

The Peasants' Revolt: Richard II offered concessions; Parliament sabotaged them — the crucial inversionThe episode that most illuminates my thesis is the Peasants' Revolt of 1381. I want to describe what actually happened, because it is the opposite of what popular history has contrived to suggest. The peasants rose — driven by the poll tax, by the Statute of Labourers that tried to freeze their wages after the Black Death had given them, for a moment, the leverage of scarcity, and by a generalised fury at their condition. They were met by the young King Richard II, who promised them abolition of serfdom and fair terms. When the revolt was suppressed and its leaders killed or executed, Richard urged a humane settlement upon Parliament. Parliament furiously refused it. Parliament — that body we are told represents the liberties of England — was at that moment the instrument of the landlord class, determined to drive the peasants back into servitude. The king, the monarch we are told was the oppressor, was the one body in the English state with the popular instinct for a just settlement. This is not a paradox for its own sake. It is the most important historical fact about the relationship between formal political power and the interests of the poor in English history.

V. The Reformation as "the Rebellion of the Rich"

The Dissolution of the Monasteries as deliberate expropriation of the medieval commonsThe chapter I title "The Rebellion of the Rich" concerns what we are taught to call the Reformation. I want to say something precise and verifiable about what actually happened to the monasteries. The monasteries were not merely religious houses. They were the social safety net of medieval England. They were hospitals, schools, inns, poor relief, and repositories of accumulated common wealth that had been donated by generations of ordinary English people who believed they were giving to God and their community. When Henry VIII dissolved the monasteries, the wealth did not go to the poor. It went — with the most extraordinary speed and the most naked greed — to a new class of men: lawyers, speculators, courtiers, and money-lenders, whose names became the great aristocratic families of subsequent English history. Lawyers and lackeys and money-lenders, the meanest of lucky men, looted the art and economics of the Middle Ages like thieves robbing a church. This was not reform. This was robbery — the single largest act of expropriation in English history before the enclosures, carried out in the name of religion and described ever since as spiritual progress.

VI. The enclosures: Parliament as instrument of dispossession

Parliamentary enclosures: Parliament as the vehicle for stripping common land from the poorAll through the eighteenth century, through the great Whig speeches about liberty, through the Tory speeches about patriotism, through Trafalgar and Waterloo, Parliament was passing bill after bill for the enclosure of common lands — the last remnants of the medieval communal system that had sustained the English peasantry for centuries. This happened not despite Parliament but through it. Parliament was the landlord class legislating for its own enrichment. The common lands that were enclosed had not belonged to the great landlords. They had been used by the poor since time before memory. The enclosures destroyed an entire way of life — the smallholder, the commoner, the man with a pig and a strip and a right of pasture — and replaced it with the landless wage labourer, entirely dependent on the factory or the farm for his survival and with no property, no independence, and no recourse. The modern working class was not the natural condition of the English poor. It was manufactured, by an act of deliberate legislative violence, in the interest of those who controlled the legislature.

VII. The franchise extensions: homeopathic remedies against revolution

Reform Bills and franchise extensions as deliberate release valves, not genuine democracyWe are told that the story of English politics from 1832 onward is the story of the progressive extension of democracy — the Great Reform Bill, the Reform Act of 1867, the Representation of the People Act of 1884, and so on down to the present. I want to offer a different account. Each of these extensions of the franchise was granted not because the governing class had decided that democracy was a good idea but because popular pressure had become dangerous enough that some small release of that pressure was necessary to preserve the governing structure as a whole. Disraeli extended the franchise to the artisans in 1867 not because he believed in democracy but because he believed the working class was now weak enough to be safely included. The politicians said the working class was now strong enough to be given the vote. It would be truer to say it was now weak enough. Each Reform Bill was not a step forward for the people. It was a skilfully calibrated step backward from revolution — a homeopathic dose of democracy administered precisely to prevent the real thing. Parliament was never the friend of the common man. It became, when it was convenient to do so, his jailer with a slightly larger cage.

VIII. The Norman Conquest: the imposition of a foreign aristocracy the English never accepted

The Norman Conquest as alien aristocratic imposition — the English people never became NormanThe Norman Conquest was an act of raw military violence that imposed a French-speaking aristocracy on an English-speaking people who had their own traditions, laws, and institutions — and who never, in any meaningful cultural sense, became Norman. The great achievement of the English people in the centuries after 1066 was not in learning to be Norman but in Anglicising their conquerors — in dragging the language, the customs, and eventually the institutions of England back toward the English common stock, against the constant pressure of the aristocracy that had been placed over them. Even the Tapestry of Bayeux, woven by Norman hands to celebrate a Norman victory, stops at the battlefield and claims nothing more — because the Norman conquerors themselves knew, at some level, that what they had won was a kingdom they would never truly possess. The great kings of medieval England who are celebrated in popular history — Alfred, Edward the Confessor — are celebrated precisely because they were with the people against the encroaching power of the great lords. The story of English history is not the story of an aristocracy benevolently extending its privileges downward. It is the story of a people repeatedly and unsuccessfully trying to reclaim what was taken from them in 1066.

IX. The real Dark Ages: not the Middle Ages but the centuries after the Reformation

The real "Dark Ages" are the post-Reformation centuries of dispossession — not the Middle AgesI close with the inversion that is the heart of my book. We are taught to call the Middle Ages the Dark Ages — a period of ignorance, superstition, and misery from which the Reformation and the Renaissance delivered us. I submit that the evidence points in the opposite direction. In the Middle Ages, the English peasant, for all his hardships, owned his strips of land, had rights of common pasture, was protected by the guild if he was a craftsman, was sustained by the monastery if he was sick or destitute, and could appeal to a tradition of custom and law that, however imperfectly, acknowledged his human dignity. After the Reformation — after the rebellion of the rich — he had nothing. He had been expelled from his land by enclosures, stripped of his guild by the destruction of corporate life, robbed of his hospital by the dissolution of the monasteries, and reduced to the condition of a landless wage labourer selling his time to whoever would buy it and protected by nothing except the uncertain mercy of his employer. If that is progress, it is progress of a very peculiar kind. The true dark age in English history is not the age of faith. It is the age that replaced it.

Thank you.

The Historian
Rodney Hilton
Bond Men Made Free (1973) & the empirical medieval record

Logical Arguments

Mr Chesterton,

I want to begin with a genuine acknowledgement. Your book is one of the most stimulating pieces of historical writing produced in England in the twentieth century. It is wrong in important ways — wrong in ways that matter, and ways that I intend to demonstrate with evidence rather than contempt. But it is wrong in the direction of truth. It is wrong because it has seen something that the professional consensus of your era almost entirely missed: that the history of England is, at its centre, a history of class conflict, of the dispossession of the poor, and of the systematic use of formal political institutions to protect the interests of the rich against those of the many. You reached the right destination by the wrong road. Let me show you why the road matters.

I. The medieval peasant was not a free yeoman — he was a serf

Villeinage: the actual legal and material condition of the medieval majority was serfdom, not freedomYour account of medieval life implies that the English peasant enjoyed a dignified independence — land, guild, parish, custom — that was subsequently taken from him. This picture requires a fundamental correction. The majority of English peasants in the high medieval period were not free yeomen. They were villeins — serfs, legally bound to the land of their lord, unable to leave without his permission, unable to bring actions in the royal courts against him, subject to arbitrary tallage at his discretion, compelled to perform labour services on his demesne several days a week for nothing, and liable to have their goods seized on the pretext that everything they owned belonged by right to their master. Paul Freedman's research on the imagery of medieval peasantry and my own work in the manor court rolls of Worcestershire and other counties makes this brutally clear: between fifty and seventy percent of the English rural population before the Black Death were in this condition. This is not a dark interpretation imposed by later Marxist analysis. It is what the documents say, in the dry language of legal obligation that is far more reliable evidence than romantic inference.

II. The guilds were real — and also exclusionary monopolies

Medieval guilds: genuine institutions, but also restrictive monopolies that excluded women, outsiders, and the poorYou are right that medieval guilds were remarkable institutions, and right that their destruction was a genuine loss. I will not argue with your account of what the guild represented at its best. But the full historical picture is considerably more complicated than your celebration allows. Medieval guilds operated as monopolies. They controlled entry into trades through lengthy, costly apprenticeships that effectively excluded the poor who could not afford them. They excluded women almost entirely from the upper reaches of craft governance, and where women participated, their participation was increasingly restricted from the fourteenth century onward. They excluded non-members from trading within their jurisdiction by force and legal penalty. The guild was a mechanism of popular self-governance for its members — but its members were already the relatively privileged artisan class, not the poorest. The journeyman who could never afford to become a master, the rural labourer entirely outside the guild structure, the woman barred from the trade — these were also people, and the guild was not their institution. Sheilagh Ogilvie's extensive research in the guild records of northern Europe demonstrates that guilds consistently used their monopoly power to restrict entry, suppress wages for those below master level, and transfer income from consumers and non-members to established members. This was not the romance of craft democracy. It was a restrictive trade association.

III. Magna Carta: you are right on the substance but wrong about what follows

Magna Carta: Chesterton is correct that it was a barons' document, but wrong that it had no popular consequencesOn Magna Carta you are, in the narrow technical sense, correct: it was an aristocratic document, made by barons against a king, and its immediate beneficiaries were those barons. But you draw the wrong conclusions from this correct observation. The history of Magna Carta is also the history of how peasants, townsfolk, and labourers gradually appropriated its language to their own purposes. We know from court records that peasants in Bocking, Essex, were invoking Magna Carta against their lord's exactions as early as 1300. We know that the language of the charter — that no free man shall be imprisoned, dispossessed, or harmed except by the lawful judgment of his peers — was stretched, slowly and painfully, over centuries, to cover more and more of the population who were technically excluded from it. The charter was made into a document of liberty by the people who needed liberty, not by those who drafted it. And as for Parliament — yes, it quickly became a governing class. But the same Parliament that Chesterton condemns as the instrument of the landlord class was also the body that abolished the poll tax after 1381, that passed the first Factory Acts in the nineteenth century, and that ultimately extended the franchise to every adult. The institution and the class that dominated it are not the same thing across time.

IV. The Peasants' Revolt: Richard II did not offer liberation — he offered tactical deception

Richard II's promises to the rebels were a tactical deception, not sincere sympathy — the documents prove itYour account of Richard II and the Peasants' Revolt is one of the most tendentious passages in a book full of tendentious passages, and I want to be specific about why. You present Richard as genuinely sympathetic to the peasants, as a young king whose humane impulses were thwarted by a reactionary Parliament. The evidence does not support this. Richard rode out to meet the rebels at Smithfield after Wat Tyler was killed precisely because he was surrounded and needed to disperse a dangerous crowd. He promised the rebels their demands would be met. He then led them to St John's Fields under the pretext that Tyler would meet them there — and while they waited, a militia of seven thousand was organised, and the revolt was crushed. The charters of manumission he had granted were revoked within weeks. His famous response — "Villeins ye are, and villeins ye shall remain" — is documented in multiple contemporary sources. This is not the language of a king thwarted by Parliament. It is the language of a king who used deception as a tactical weapon against his own people and then dropped the mask as soon as the danger had passed. The Peasants' Revolt shows the depth and coherence of popular political consciousness in fourteenth-century England — my book documents this in detail — but it was defeated by the entire ruling class, the king emphatically included.

V. The Dissolution of the Monasteries: real expropriation, but the monasteries were also landlords

Monastery dissolution: genuine expropriation, but monasteries were themselves feudal landlords exploiting their tenantsYour account of the Dissolution of the Monasteries is one of the most emotionally powerful in the book, and in its bare outlines it is historically sound: the wealth of the monasteries was redistributed to a new class of men whose descendants became the Whig aristocracy, and the social services the monasteries provided — however imperfect — were not replaced. I do not dispute this. What I dispute is your implication that the monasteries were benevolent popular institutions whose tenants grieved for them. The monasteries were feudal landlords. Their tenants held land in villeinage and owed labour services to the abbot just as other villeins owed them to secular lords. The peasants of Bury St Edmunds and St Albans, who were among the most active participants in the Peasants' Revolt of 1381, were not revolting against secular landlords. They were revolting specifically against the abbots of those houses — men of the Church, servants of God — who were extracting maximum rents and services from their tenants with all the rapacity of any secular lord. The evidence from the revolt itself — the burning of abbey records, the forced extraction of charters from abbots — tells us directly what the peasants thought of monastic landlordism. They did not weep for it when it went.

VI. The enclosures: your analysis is right, but earlier enclosures pre-date Parliament's dominance

Enclosures: Parliamentary enclosures are real, but enclosure began under feudal lords long before Parliament dominated itYour account of the Parliamentary enclosures of the eighteenth century is broadly accurate and important. The passage of hundreds of Enclosure Acts between 1750 and 1850, which transferred something between six and seven million acres of common land into private ownership, was one of the great acts of legalized robbery in English history — and E. P. Thompson's work in The Making of the English Working Class documents the human cost with devastating precision. But your narrative implies that the commons were intact until Parliament's eighteenth-century depredations began. They were not. Enclosure had been proceeding since the fourteenth century, carried out by individual lords — including monastic lords — converting arable strips and commons to sheep pasture in pursuit of the wool trade. The Statute of Merton in 1235 had already given lords the legal right to enclose waste land provided they left "sufficient" pasture for their free tenants. Medieval lords were enclosing land centuries before Parliament became the dominant instrument of dispossession. The process you correctly identify as robbery began under the very feudal system you are defending.

VII. The Reform Acts: correct diagnosis, wrong interpretation of mechanism

Reform Acts: Chesterton's cynicism about franchise extension is too sweeping — working class agency was realYour analysis of the Reform Acts as homeopathic anti-revolution medicine is genuinely insightful, and there is real historical substance to it. The Great Reform Act of 1832 was, as you say, as much about managing popular pressure as about extending democracy, and the continuing Chartist agitation after 1832 — demanding what the Act had not delivered — proves your point about the gap between the rhetoric of reform and its substance. But you systematically underestimate the role of working-class agency in these changes. The Reform agitation was not simply a pressure that the governing class managed and absorbed. It was a sustained political movement — with its own newspapers, its own organisations, its own theories of society — that genuinely changed the terms of English politics. The same is true of the Chartist movement, of the trade union movement, and of the suffrage campaigns that followed. These movements extracted real concessions. The Factory Acts, the legalisation of trade unions in 1871, the Eight Hours movement, the introduction of council housing — these were not tactical retreats by an undefeated ruling class. They were defeats for the ruling class, imposed by organised popular pressure. The English working class was not simply the passive object of a governing-class strategy. It was a historical agent in its own right, and your narrative, for all its sympathy with the poor, consistently denies them that agency.

VIII. The Norman Conquest: your cultural point is real — the class-structure point is complicated

The Norman Conquest: real cultural disruption, but Anglo-Saxon England was not a peasant democracy — it had its own thegns and ceorlsYou are right that the Norman Conquest was an act of military violence that imposed a foreign aristocracy on the English people, and right that its cultural legacy was contested and eventually reversed in important ways. But your implication that pre-Conquest England was a more popular and democratic society than what the Normans imposed misreads the evidence. Anglo-Saxon England had its own class structure — thegns, ceorls, and geburs (the Anglo-Saxon equivalent of the later villein) — and its own forms of aristocratic exploitation. A significant proportion of the rural population was already in conditions of servitude before 1066. The Domesday Book, completed twenty years after the Conquest, does not show us a population suddenly enslaved by Norman conquerors. It shows us a population that had been stratified for centuries, with the Conquest adding a new French-speaking layer at the top but not transforming the fundamental relationship between lords and peasants. The peasants who revolted in 1381 were not trying to restore an Edwardian golden age of popular liberty. As my research shows, they were articulating a new vision of social relations based on the specific conditions of the post-Black Death world — rising wages, labour scarcity, and the felt injustice of a Statute of Labourers designed to hold them down.

IX. Where Chesterton is right — and what the evidence actually shows

Where Chesterton is vindicated: the dispossession of the English poor is real — but it was already happening in the Middle Ages he idealisesI want to close not with refutation but with something more important: where Chesterton is right, and why the evidence vindicates him more profoundly than he knows — because the dispossession he identifies was not something that happened to a free medieval people after the Reformation. It was a process that had been running throughout the Middle Ages themselves, within the feudal system, before the dissolution of the monasteries and before the enclosures accelerated it. The recurring conflict between landlords and peasants over the appropriation of the peasant surplus — the central theme of my own life's work — is not a Reformation phenomenon. It is the defining feature of medieval English society. Serfdom was not a late imposition. It was the foundation. The Peasants' Revolt of 1381 was not the uprising of a previously free people against new oppressors. It was the uprising of people who had been in bondage for generations against the perpetuation of that bondage at the moment when — because the Black Death had reduced their numbers and increased their bargaining power — they finally had leverage. The England Chesterton mourns never existed in quite the form he imagines it. But the England he describes — a society in which the poor are repeatedly stripped of what little they have by those with the power to legislate — that is true, and was true from the beginning. His error is to date its beginning too late.

Thank you.

2.The charter's language — against arbitrary imprisonment and dispossession — was stretched over centuries to cover groups technically excluded from it, through the pressure of those who needed its protection.
3.Institutions mean what their users make them mean over time; the history of Magna Carta is a history of popular appropriation, not only of elite manipulation.
∴Chesterton is correct that Magna Carta was not originally democratic; he is wrong to conclude it had no popular significance — the people made it mean something its framers never intended.
Argument IV — Richard II's promises were tactical deception, documented in multiple sourcesclick for image
1.Contemporary sources — the Anonimalle Chronicle, Walsingham's Historia Anglicana, Froissart's Chronicles — record that Richard issued charters of manumission to the rebels and then revoked them within weeks.
2.Richard's documented statement — "Villeins ye are, and villeins ye shall remain" — is inconsistent with Chesterton's portrait of a king with genuine popular sympathies thwarted by Parliament.
3.Richard led the rebels to St John's Fields under false pretences while a militia was organised against them; this is the behaviour of a tactician, not a sympathiser. (Hilton, Bond Men Made Free, Chs. 8–9)
∴The documentary record refutes Chesterton's account of Richard II as a popular king thwarted by Parliament; Richard was a member of the ruling class who used deception to defeat the revolt, not its closet ally.
Argument V — Monasteries were feudal landlords whose tenants revolted against them in 1381click for image
1.The most intense and documented episodes of the Peasants' Revolt occurred at Bury St Edmunds and St Albans — both monastic towns whose peasants were specifically attacking their abbots as landlords. (Hilton, Bond Men Made Free)
2.Rebels forced abbots to hand over deeds confirming their freedoms and burned manor court rolls that recorded their servile obligations — showing they knew exactly what they were revolting against.
3.The monasteries' tenants held land in villeinage under the abbots on the same terms as secular villeins; the abbot was not a benevolent father but a feudal lord with full coercive power over his people.
∴The Peasants' Revolt refutes Chesterton's benevolent picture of monastic landlordism from the inside; the people whose welfare he says the monasteries protected were in open revolt against them in 1381.
Argument VI — Enclosure began under medieval feudal lords, not Parliamentclick for image
1.The Statute of Merton (1235) gave lords the legal right to enclose waste land — enclosure was legally sanctioned in England before Parliament became a dominant institution.
2.Individual landlords — including monastic landlords — had been converting arable strips and commons to sheep pasture since the fourteenth century, driven by the wool trade, long before the Parliamentary enclosures Chesterton focuses on.
3.The processes of dispossession that Chesterton attributes to the post-Reformation ruling class were operating within the medieval feudal system he romanticises, under the authority of the lords he credits with benevolent governance.
∴Enclosure and dispossession did not begin with the Reformation; they were features of the medieval system itself, undermining Chesterton's temporal boundary between a good medieval past and a bad modern present.
Argument VII — Working-class agency won real concessions, not only managed defeatsclick for image
1.The trade union movement, the Chartists, the Factory Acts agitation, and the suffrage campaigns were not simply absorbed by a ruling class strategy; they extracted genuine legislative changes against the resistance of that class.
2.The legalisation of trade unions in 1871, the introduction of the eight-hour working day, the Factory Acts, the extension of the franchise to women, and the creation of the National Health Service were defeats for the ruling class imposed by organised popular pressure.
3.Chesterton's narrative of permanent and complete ruling-class management of popular pressure denies the English working class historical agency and contradicts the evidence of what organised workers actually achieved. (E. P. Thompson, The Making of the English Working Class, 1963)
∴The English working class was not only the victim of ruling-class strategy; it was a historical agent whose organised pressure produced real gains — gains Chesterton's framework cannot account for.
Argument VIII — Anglo-Saxon England was not a peasant democracyclick for image
1.The Anglo-Saxon gebur was bound to his lord's land, owed labour services, and lived in conditions very similar to the later villein — servitude did not arrive with the Normans.
2.The Domesday Book records a population already stratified into lords, free men, sokemen, bordars, cottars, and serfs — a hierarchy that pre-dates the Conquest and to which the Normans added a new stratum at the top rather than creating the stratification from scratch.
3.The 1381 rebels did not demand a return to pre-Conquest conditions; they articulated specific demands relevant to post-Black Death labour conditions — abolition of serfdom, fair wages, free access to the market — that were forward-looking, not nostalgic.
∴Chesterton's contrast between a free Anglo-Saxon past and a Norman-imposed servitude misreads the evidence; servile conditions pre-date the Conquest, and the 1381 rebels were not looking backward to restore them.
Argument IX — Dispossession was continuous, not punctuated by the Reformationclick for image
1.The conflict between landlords and peasants over the appropriation of the peasant surplus — through rent, labour services, arbitrary tallage, and enclosure — was the central feature of medieval English society, not a post-Reformation innovation. (Hilton, Bond Men Made Free, Introduction)
2.Chesterton's historical inversion — good medieval past, bad modern present — requires a sharp temporal boundary at the Reformation that the evidence does not sustain; the dispossession was already happening.
3.The England Chesterton mourns — of free peasants, democratic guilds, and benevolent monasteries — is a construction from the more favourable elements of medieval evidence, not a description of the modal experience of the English poor.
∴Chesterton's revisionism replaces one myth (progress) with another (golden age); the historical truth is that class conflict and the dispossession of the poor are continuous features of English history, medieval and modern alike.
