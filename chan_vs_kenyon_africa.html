A Debate on African Leadership and Its Legacy
"Africa's Leaders: Visionary Philosophers or Men Who Stole a Continent?"
Stephen Chan OBE · African Political Thought: An Intellectual History of the Quest for Freedom (Hurst, 2021) & Grasping Africa (2007)
Paul Kenyon · Dictatorland: The Men Who Stole Africa (Head of Zeus, 2018)
The Philosopher-Kings
Stephen Chan OBE
African Political Thought (Hurst, 2021) · Grasping Africa (2007)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr Kenyon,

I want to begin with the sentence that opens the intellectual case I make in African Political Thought — a sentence whose implications, I think, your entire book fails to reckon with. Even Jean-Paul Sartre, writing his preface to Frantz Fanon's The Wretched of the Earth — a man of considerable sympathy to the anti-colonial cause — described the African as a figure with "coiled muscles about to explode into rebellion." Not a thinker. Not a philosopher. Not a theorist of governance or justice or sovereignty. A body tensed for violence. The African, even in Sartre's sympathetic gaze, is denied the condition of philosophy.

Your book, Mr Kenyon, written with genuine skill and obvious moral seriousness, repeats this denial. It is a book about what African leaders did with power. It is not a book about what they thought about power — about where it comes from, what it is for, what obligations it generates, what alternative worlds it might build. You are a brilliant chronicler of failure. What you are not is a chronicler of the ideas that preceded the failures, that shaped them, and that sometimes survived them. My argument is that we cannot understand African political leadership — its achievements, its betrayals, and the conditions under which it became what it became — without first taking seriously the extraordinary body of thought that its protagonists produced. Africa has given the world a philosophy of liberation that has been systematically denied the respect accorded to far inferior European equivalents. I intend to name it, describe it, and defend its significance against the reduction of its authors to the sum of their worst acts.

I. Africa was denied the condition of philosophy — and this itself needs explaining

The foundational injustice: Africa's intellectual tradition has been systematically excluded from the world's philosophical canonMy starting point is not a defence of any particular leader but a diagnosis of the epistemic injustice that makes the debate between us possible in the form it takes. African political thought — stretching from the Pan-African congresses in London and Manchester in the early twentieth century, through Nkrumah's Consciencism and Nyerere's Ujamaa essays, through Fanon's phenomenology of colonial violence, through Cabral's theory of culture as a site of liberation, through Biko's articulation of Black Consciousness as a philosophical position rather than a political slogan — constitutes a serious, substantial, and original contribution to political philosophy. It engages with the deepest questions of political life: the nature of sovereignty, the conditions of genuine self-determination, the relationship between culture and political identity, the structural violence embedded in economic arrangements that appear voluntary, the philosophical content of "liberation" rather than merely its political mechanics. This tradition has been almost entirely absent from the syllabuses of Western philosophy departments, from the canon of political theory as taught in British and American universities, from the indices of the great handbooks and companions to political thought. Not because it does not exist. Because its existence has not been convenient to acknowledge. Your book's silence about this tradition — its treatment of Africa's leaders purely as actors rather than as thinkers — is part of a very long silence. I am asking that the silence end. (African Political Thought, Introduction)

II. Kwame Nkrumah: consciencism and the philosophy of decolonisation

Nkrumah's Consciencism: a genuine philosophical synthesis addressing the triple heritage of Africa, Islam, and European ChristianityKwame Nkrumah was deposed by a military coup in 1966, while abroad, with the active connivance of the CIA. Your book notes this. What it does not adequately address is what Nkrumah was thinking — what intellectual project his leadership was in service of. Consciencism, published in 1964, is the key text. Nkrumah's philosophical problem is one of the most genuinely difficult in modern political thought: how does a society that has been subjected to colonialism reconstruct itself politically and intellectually when colonialism has not simply imposed external rule but has transformed the interior of consciousness — has produced, in the educated African elite, a being who has been formed by European philosophical categories that were themselves partly constituted by the logic of colonisation? His answer — philosophical consciencism — is a method for digesting the triple heritage of traditional African communalism, Islamic influence, and Euro-Christian education, and synthesising from these elements a distinctly African philosophical standpoint. This is not nativism or simple rejection of European modernity. It is a sophisticated philosophical enterprise that anticipates by decades what Western philosophy has only recently arrived at in post-colonial theory. Nkrumah was overthrown before it could be institutionalised. This is not his intellectual failure. It is the political failure of those who overthrew him — and of those who backed them. (African Political Thought, Ch. 3)

III. Julius Nyerere: Ujamaa and the philosophy of African socialism

Nyerere's Ujamaa: an original theory of socialist development rooted in African communal values, not imported MarxismJulius Nyerere was one of the most intellectually serious heads of state of the twentieth century — in any country, on any continent. He translated Shakespeare into Swahili not as a curiosity but as a demonstration of the intellectual range of his language and his culture. His political philosophy — Ujamaa, "familyhood" — was not a copy of Soviet socialism or Fabian welfare policy. It was an argument that the traditional African village community, with its ethic of mutual obligation, collective labour, and shared resource, provided the philosophical basis for a modern socialist state that was genuinely rooted in African values rather than imported from European ideology. The Arusha Declaration of 1967 — one of the most remarkable documents in postcolonial political thought — committed Tanzania to self-reliance, socialist development, and leadership by those who served the people rather than enriched themselves at the people's expense. Nyerere himself retired from the presidency in 1985, voluntarily, having established that the transfer of power was something Africa could do. He died relatively poor. His critics are right that the forced villagisation programme of the 1970s was coercive and economically damaging. But the critics who reduce Ujamaa to its worst administrative episode misread a substantial political philosophy as a failed development policy, when the relationship between the two is far more complicated than that. (African Political Thought, Ch. 4)

IV. Amílcar Cabral: culture as a weapon and the return to the source

Cabral's theory of culture and liberation: the most sophisticated anti-colonial political philosophy of the twentieth centuryIf I had to nominate the most philosophically original of the thinkers I examine, it would be Amílcar Cabral of Guinea-Bissau. He was murdered in 1973, shortly before he could see his country's independence. His body of work — the Havana speeches, the Tricontinental essays, above all the concept of "return to the source" — represents the most sophisticated theory of the relationship between culture and political liberation produced in the twentieth century. Cabral understood that colonialism was not merely an economic and political arrangement but a cultural one — that it worked by convincing the colonised to internalise the coloniser's understanding of the world, including the understanding that the colonised were historically and culturally inferior. Liberation, on this account, was not only the removal of colonial administration. It was the reconstruction of cultural self-respect — the return of the native intellectual, who had been formed by European education, to the cultural sources of her own people. This is not romanticism or nostalgia. It is a precise philosophical argument about the relationship between consciousness and political possibility, about why merely transferring formal political power without cultural transformation would produce the neocolonial outcomes that Nkrumah had already diagnosed. That Cabral's analysis has been substantially confirmed by the subsequent history of African politics — including, most painfully, the history of Guinea-Bissau after his death — is a tribute to its penetration, not evidence against it. (African Political Thought, Ch. 6)

V. Patrice Lumumba: the vision that had to be murdered

Lumumba: his assassination by Western powers proves the political threat of his vision, not its irrelevancePatrice Lumumba governed the Congo for less than three months before being deposed with American and Belgian assistance and murdered. In your book, Mr Kenyon, the story of the Congo is primarily the story of Mobutu — the man who replaced Lumumba and who, for thirty years, made the Congo a byword for kleptocracy. This is an entirely legitimate story to tell. But it cannot be told honestly without asking why Lumumba was removed. He was removed — with extraordinary speed, in the first weeks of Congolese independence — because he was perceived, by the CIA and by Belgian mining interests, as a threat to the enormous mineral wealth of Katanga province. He proposed to use that wealth for the Congolese people rather than for Western corporations. His famous independence speech — delivered in the presence of the Belgian king who had overseen the colonisation — was not anti-Western rhetoric produced by ideological animus. It was a statement of what political independence was supposed to mean: that the resources of a country belong to its people. He was murdered for saying this. Mobutu — the kleptocrat who replaced him — was armed, funded, and protected by the United States for three decades. The lesson of the Congo is not that Africa cannot govern itself. It is that, in the 1960s, Africa was not permitted to. (Grasping Africa, Ch. 2)

VI. Thomas Sankara: the most coherent anti-neocolonial programme ever attempted

Sankara's Burkina Faso: a genuine programme of self-reliant development and anti-corruption that was murdered by external interestsThomas Sankara governed Burkina Faso from 1983 until his assassination in 1987. In those four years, he achieved something that no subsequent African government has come close to replicating: a coherent, self-funded programme of development that reduced child mortality dramatically, achieved near-universal vaccination, dramatically expanded girls' education, planted millions of trees to halt the advance of the Sahara, and — most remarkably — did all of this without borrowing from the IMF or accepting the structural adjustment conditions that were simultaneously destroying public services across the continent. He refused personal enrichment to a degree that was conspicuous even among those who claim to be incorruptible: his declared personal assets were a car, two guitars, a refrigerator, and a broken-down freezer. His speeches on the illegitimacy of African debt — arguing that debt contracted by colonial governments to fund colonial infrastructure was not legitimately owed by independent states — were not demagogy. They were a sophisticated argument in international law and political philosophy that continues to be debated by legal scholars today. He said "we must dare to invent the future." He was murdered, almost certainly with the connivance of Côte d'Ivoire's Houphouët-Boigny and France, for doing precisely that. (African Political Thought, Ch. 8)

VII. Steve Biko: Black Consciousness as a philosophy of self-liberation

Biko's Black Consciousness: a philosophical argument about the conditions of genuine liberation that transcends the South African contextSteve Biko, murdered in a South African prison in 1977 at the age of thirty, left behind a body of philosophical writing — collected in I Write What I Like — that is one of the most important contributions to political philosophy produced in the twentieth century. His central argument — that Black Consciousness is not primarily about hating white people but about the psychological liberation of black people from an internalised sense of inferiority — is a philosophical argument in the tradition of Frantz Fanon, but more concrete, more practically oriented, and more immediately connected to the lived experience of apartheid South Africa. What Biko saw — with a clarity that European liberal anti-apartheid opinion consistently missed — was that genuine liberation required a prior act of self-liberation: that a people convinced by centuries of racist ideology that they were inherently inferior, inherently dependent, inherently incapable of governing themselves, could not simply be given political rights by a magnanimous white government and become free. Freedom had first to be claimed from the inside. This is not separatism. It is a precise diagnosis of the psychological infrastructure of oppression that anticipates much of what has subsequently become, under different names, a major strand of international political philosophy. Biko was murdered before any of it could be tested against the realities of power. (African Political Thought, Ch. 9)

VIII. The West's role: a context without which no account is honest

Western complicity: the CIA, French Françafrique, and Cold War interventionism systematically destroyed the most promising African governmentsI want to close by naming something that your book acknowledges but does not, I think, adequately weight. The Western role in the post-independence governance of Africa was not passive or merely enabling. It was actively destructive of precisely those governments that were attempting the kind of coherent, self-directed development that you reproach Africa for not achieving. Lumumba was murdered with CIA involvement. Nkrumah was overthrown with CIA backing. Sankara was murdered with French connivance. Cabral was assassinated by Portuguese colonial agents. The pattern is not coincidental. The pattern is policy. It was the policy of France's Françafrique — the network of political, financial, and military relationships through which France maintained neocolonial control of its former territories by supporting compliant kleptocrats and removing leaders who threatened to act in the interests of their own populations. It was the policy of Cold War America, which backed Mobutu in the Congo, Siad Barre in Somalia, and Houphouët-Boigny in Côte d'Ivoire — men whom your book correctly identifies as disaster-producing rulers — precisely because they were reliably anti-Communist. The thinkers and leaders I have defended in this speech were, overwhelmingly, removed by external forces from positions from which they might otherwise have been judged on their actual governance records. Your book judges them by what they became after being removed — or by what those who replaced them became. This is not a fair trial. (African Political Thought, Epilogue)

IX. Mugabe: the tragedy of a genuine thinker destroyed by power

Mugabe: a serious intellectual whose trajectory is a tragedy, not simply a story of wickedness — and whose worst years were preceded by genuine achievementI will end with the figure who most tests my thesis: Robert Mugabe, to whom I have devoted serious attention across two books — one of which, Robert Mugabe: A Life of Power and Violence, makes no attempt to excuse his crimes. Mugabe was a man of formidable intellectual ability: five university degrees, a genuine grasp of Marxist theory and anticolonial philosophy, a personal bravery demonstrated over eleven years of guerrilla war and imprisonment. The land reform he pursued from 2000 onward — the seizure of white-owned farms — was presented internationally as pure thuggery. In fact it addressed a genuine injustice: the preservation, since the Lancaster House Agreement of 1979, of a land distribution in which the white minority retained the best agricultural land of Zimbabwe while the black majority farmed marginal land. The political economy of this arrangement was indefensible and had been criticised by responsible economists since independence. Mugabe's method of addressing it — violent, chaotic, and catastrophically managed — produced disaster. But the underlying claim — that the land settlement of 1980 was unjust and that independence was therefore incomplete — was correct. The tragedy of Mugabe, as I describe it, is not the tragedy of a man who was always wicked but of a genuine liberator whose relationship with power underwent a catastrophic transformation that destroyed both his country and his legacy. That transformation deserves analysis, not simply condemnation. (Grasping Africa, Ch. 6; African Political Thought, Ch. 11)

Thank you.

The Stolen Continent
Paul Kenyon
Dictatorland: The Men Who Stole Africa (Head of Zeus, 2018)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Professor Chan,

I want to begin by accepting something from your argument, because I think honesty demands it and because it makes the genuine disagreement between us clearer. You are right that Western interference — the CIA, Françafrique, Cold War cynicism — bears a very large part of the responsibility for the trajectory of post-independence Africa. I say this explicitly in my book. It is not a revelation I require your instruction to accept. The oil companies that bankrolled Obiang in Equatorial Guinea, the Western governments that maintained Mobutu as a Cold War asset, the French political establishment that sustained its neocolonial network while publicly celebrating African independence — these are part of the story I tell, and I tell it without flinching.

But here is where I part company with you, and I want to be precise about the point of departure. The existence of Western complicity does not diminish — cannot diminish, by any logic I can follow — the agency of the men who chose to use the power they held in the ways they used it. And it is those choices, made by those men, with those consequences for those millions of people, that I want us to look at directly. I am a journalist. I have been to these places. I have spoken to the survivors. I am not going to allow a very sophisticated philosophical argument to remove from view the people whose lives were destroyed by the leaders under discussion, by recasting the perpetrators as visionaries who were misunderstood.

I. The philosophical tradition does not excuse the governance record

Philosophy and governance are not the same: the quality of an ideology must be judged by what it produces when appliedProfessor Chan argues that Africa has been denied the condition of philosophy — that its leaders' intellectual achievements have been ignored in favour of a focus on their political failures. I want to make a simple but, I think, decisive point in response. A philosophy that is consistently incapable of translating itself into just governance — that produces, in application after application, the same pattern of concentrated power, suppression of opposition, and misappropriation of national wealth — must be assessed not only as philosophy but as political programme. And as political programmes, the philosophies Professor Chan describes — Nkrumah's Consciencism, Nyerere's Ujamaa, Mobutu's Authenticité — produced, in the societies they governed, results that their ostensible commitments to liberation and human dignity should have precluded. Philosophy is not exempt from the test of its consequences. The question is not whether Nkrumah was a clever man. The question is what happened to the people of Ghana while he was in charge — and what happened was the suppression of political opposition, the detention without trial of critics, the personality cult, and the economic adventurism that left the country in debt and turmoil. These are not small things, and they are not explained away by the CIA. (Dictatorland, Introduction)

II. Mobutu Sese Seko: Authenticité as the philosophy of theft

Mobutu's Authenticité: a philosophical programme that functioned as a personal enrichment machine dressed in cultural nationalismLet us talk about a leader who also had a philosophy — Mobutu Sese Seko of Zaire, who renamed his country, renamed its cities, renamed himself, and invented a doctrine called Authenticité to give philosophical dressing to what was, in practice, a personal enrichment operation of extraordinary scale and duration. Mobutu took a country with some of the world's largest deposits of copper, cobalt, diamonds, and uranium and systematically stripped it of everything that could be moved to Swiss bank accounts, Belgian properties, and a palace complex at Gbadolite — in the deep jungle, connected to the outside world only by an airstrip long enough to land Concorde, which Mobutu chartered to fly in European delicacies for his personal consumption while his people starved. Authenticité was the philosophical claim that Africa must reassert its own cultural values against the legacy of colonialism. It is, in principle, a claim that Professor Chan might endorse. But in Mobutu's hands it was the vocabulary through which he made himself into the Father of the Nation — the living embodiment of the Zairian spirit — while redirecting the nation's wealth into his own pockets. Philosophy, in this case, was not the content of a vision for the people's liberation. It was the costume in which power dressed itself to avoid being called what it was. (Dictatorland, Ch. 2)

III. Robert Mugabe: a genuine liberator who became a genuine catastrophe

Mugabe's Zimbabwe: the intellectual sophistication of the man makes the scale of the catastrophe worse, not less culpableOn Mugabe, Professor Chan and I agree that the man had genuine intellectual ability and genuine liberation credentials. We disagree about what follows from this. I think the intellectual sophistication makes the catastrophe worse, not less culpable. A less intelligent man might claim the excuse of not understanding what his policies were producing. Mugabe could not claim this. He was a man with five university degrees who had read economics and who understood, with complete clarity, what hyperinflation does to a population — what the destruction of the agricultural sector means for food security, what the collapse of the health system means for life expectancy, what the intimidation and murder of political opponents means for democratic governance. Zimbabwe's life expectancy, which had been among the highest in sub-Saharan Africa, fell from sixty-one years in 1990 to thirty-seven years by 2006. This did not happen because the British refused to fund land reform. It happened because Mugabe systematically destroyed the institutions of governance, the rule of law, and the economic system in order to maintain political power. The land reform argument — that it addressed a genuine injustice — does not explain why the farms seized from white farmers were distributed not to poor black smallholders but to Mugabe's generals and political allies, who had no agricultural knowledge and no intention of farming them. That is not land reform. That is patronage politics using the rhetoric of liberation as cover. (Dictatorland, Ch. 3)

IV. Muammar Gaddafi: the philosopher with his own Green Book and 200 billion dollars

Gaddafi: the most literally philosopher-king of all — with the crimes to matchOf all the leaders under discussion, Gaddafi comes closest to Professor Chan's paradigm of the philosopher-king who has been misunderstood. He wrote his own political philosophy — the Green Book — proposing a "third way" between capitalism and communism that he called "direct democracy," funded universities and hospitals, and distributed Libya's oil wealth more broadly than any of his regional peers. He also ordered the Lockerbie bombing, which killed 270 people. He ordered the bombing of a Berlin discotheque. He spent decades funding the IRA, Carlos the Jackal, and Palestinian rejectionist factions. He imprisoned and tortured political opponents in prisons whose conditions were described by Amnesty International as among the worst in the world. He built a $200 billion personal fortune from the nation's oil revenues — money that belonged, by every principle in his own Green Book, to the Libyan people. The philosophical sophistication of the Green Book did not translate into governance consistent with its stated principles. It translated into the longest-ruling dictatorship in African history, maintained through terror, assassination, and bribery. The existence of a philosophical text does not validate the governance it accompanied. (Dictatorland, Ch. 5)

V. Sani Abacha: Nigeria's oil wealth and a simple, undisguised theft

Abacha: no philosophical pretensions — just the raw theft of a nation's wealth and the murder of those who objectedI want to introduce a figure who tests Professor Chan's philosophical framework from the other direction: Sani Abacha of Nigeria, who governed from 1993 until his death in 1998 and whose rule can serve as a kind of control case. Abacha made no philosophical claims. He had no liberation credentials. He wrote no political philosophy. He simply took Nigeria — Africa's most populous country, sitting on vast oil reserves — and stole it. His family and associates transferred between three and five billion dollars from Nigerian state accounts to personal bank accounts in Switzerland, Liechtenstein, Luxembourg, and Jersey in less than five years. He hanged Ken Saro-Wiwa, the writer and environmental activist who had campaigned against the Shell-facilitated destruction of the Niger Delta, together with eight other Ogoni activists, in 1995 — on fabricated charges, after a trial that even his own legal establishment condemned as a travesty. No philosophical framework, no vision of African liberation, no theory of cultural authenticity — just power, money, and murder. The point is not to excuse the philosopher-kings by comparison with Abacha. It is to notice that the outcomes — the stolen wealth, the suppressed opposition, the murdered critics — are disturbingly similar whether or not there is a philosophy attached. (Dictatorland, Ch. 6)

VI. Teodoro Obiang: the oil state and absolute dispossession of the people

Obiang in Equatorial Guinea: oil wealth discovered and almost entirely redirected to the ruling family while 77% of the population lives in povertyEquatorial Guinea was one of the poorest countries on earth until oil was discovered in 1995. By 2004, it had become sub-Saharan Africa's third largest oil producer. Teodoro Obiang Nguema — who came to power by executing his own uncle in 1979 and who still governs today — used this discovery to make his country the most extreme case study in what happens when a natural resource of extraordinary value is entirely captured by a single family and their associates. Equatorial Guinea's per capita GDP, on paper, is higher than many European countries. Approximately seventy-seven percent of its population lives in poverty. Its infant mortality rate is among the worst in the world. Obiang's son, Teodoro "Teodorin" Obiang, spending money at a rate impossible to reconcile with any legal income, bought a $35 million Malibu mansion, a private jet costing $38.5 million, a yacht valued at $380 million, and a Michael Jackson memorabilia collection worth $275,000. In 2011, he was appointed Deputy President of Equatorial Guinea. There is no philosophical framework here — no Consciencism, no Ujamaa, no Green Book. There is simply the question of who controls a commodity that Western companies need, and what they are prepared to ignore in order to access it. The people of Equatorial Guinea, surrounded by oil wealth that belongs to them by every natural right, received nothing. (Dictatorland, Ch. 8)

VII. The structural pattern: liberation philosophy as transition technology

The pattern across the continent: liberation ideology as a technology for achieving and legitimising power, subsequently abandoned when power was securedWhat I observe, looking across the leaders Professor Chan defends and those I document, is a structural pattern that his philosophical framing tends to obscure. The philosophy of liberation — Consciencism, Ujamaa, Authenticité, the Green Book — functions, in almost every case I can find, as what might be called a transition technology. It is the ideology of the struggle for independence: it mobilises populations, gives coherence to movements, provides an intellectually serious critique of the colonial order, and legitimises the claim of the liberation movement to govern. Once power is achieved, the philosophy typically undergoes one of two fates: it is either quietly abandoned, as governance realities assert themselves against ideological commitments, or it is formalised into a state ideology that, under single-party conditions, becomes a tool of political control rather than of liberation. I am not saying that the philosophies were always insincere at the moment of their articulation. I am saying that there is an almost universal pattern, which demands explanation, whereby the philosophy of liberation becomes the philosophy of authoritarian consolidation. That pattern is worth interrogating — and the interrogation requires looking at what actually happened to actual people. (Dictatorland, conclusion)

VIII. The survivors and the dead: philosophy must answer to the people it governed

The testimonial obligation: the sophisticated philosophical argument must be answerable to the people whose lives the philosophy governedI have spent years in the countries I describe in this book. I have spoken to survivors. I have spoken to people who remember what it was like to live under Mobutu — to need permission from the state to travel between cities, to watch your savings become worthless through orchestrated hyperinflation, to bury children from preventable diseases because the health system had been stripped to fund the palace at Gbadolite. I have spoken to Zimbabweans who remember what their country was in the 1980s — one of the most promising economies in sub-Saharan Africa — and what it became by 2008, when the currency was officially denominated in denominations of one hundred trillion dollars and a loaf of bread required a wheelbarrow of notes. Philosophy must, at some point, answer to the people it governs. The test of a political philosophy is not its intellectual sophistication but the quality of the lives it produces for the people in its care. By that test — the only test that finally matters — the philosophies Professor Chan defends failed, in application, with a consistency and a severity that cannot be explained entirely by Western interference, and that the people who suffered under them are not inclined to excuse on the grounds that their leaders were interesting thinkers.

IX. What actually happened to Felix Houphouët-Boigny's cocoa wealth

Houphouët-Boigny's Côte d'Ivoire: the man who built a cathedral in the jungle with his nation's cocoa money while the world called him moderateI want to close with a figure who is rarely discussed in the philosophical tradition Professor Chan describes, but who embodies the problem most clearly: Felix Houphouët-Boigny of Côte d'Ivoire, who governed from independence in 1960 until his death in 1993 and who was consistently described by Western governments and media as a "moderate," a "pragmatist," a "pro-Western statesman" — in other words, as everything that Nkrumah, Lumumba, and Sankara were not. What did this moderation consist of? He redirected an estimated eleven billion dollars of cocoa revenues from the Ivorian state into private accounts and personal projects. He built, in the jungle of his home village of Yamoussoukro, the Basilica of Our Lady of Peace — larger than St Peter's in Rome, at a cost approaching three hundred million dollars — as a personal monument, in a country where most citizens lacked access to clean water. He was the man who, with French connivance, orchestrated the assassination of Thomas Sankara — the man Professor Chan describes, correctly, as the most honest leader Africa has produced in the postcolonial era. The West called Houphouët-Boigny a moderate because he made no philosophical claims and asked no difficult questions about who African resources belonged to. He simply took the money and kept France informed. The comparison with Sankara — who asked the questions, made the philosophical claims, died poor, and was murdered — tells us everything about what the word "moderate" actually means in the context of African governance. (Dictatorland, Ch. 9)

Argument I — Philosophy must be judged by its governance consequencesclick for image
1.A philosophy that consistently produces, in application, the same pattern of concentrated power, suppression of opposition, and misappropriation of wealth must be assessed not only as idea but as political programme.
2.Nkrumah's Consciencism, whatever its intellectual merits, was applied in a Ghana characterised by detention without trial, personality cult, and economic ruin — outcomes his stated principles of liberation and human dignity should have precluded.
3.Philosophy is not exempt from the test of consequences; the quality of the ideas must eventually be judged against the quality of the lives they produced for the people in the philosopher-king's care. (Dictatorland, Introduction)
∴Taking African political thought seriously as philosophy requires, not precludes, holding it to account for its governance record; the claim of philosophical seriousness does not itself constitute a defence against the evidence of what the philosophy produced.
Argument II — Mobutu's Authenticité shows philosophy functioning as theft's costumeclick for image
1.Mobutu's doctrine of Authenticité — the reassertion of African cultural values against colonialism — is structurally similar to the philosophies Chan defends, yet its primary function was to make Mobutu the incarnation of the national spirit while he stripped the country's resources.
2.Mobutu transferred the Congo's mineral wealth to Swiss accounts and a jungle palace with Concorde-length runways while his population starved — a record that the philosophical framework of Authenticité did nothing to prevent and much to enable. (Dictatorland, Ch. 2)
3.The existence of a cultural-philosophical framework does not validate the governance it accompanies; it can equally function as the vocabulary in which power legitimises itself to avoid accountability.
∴Mobutu demonstrates that liberation philosophy can be as effective a tool of self-enrichment as crude force; the philosophical form does not guarantee the philosophical content.
Argument III — Mugabe's intelligence makes the Zimbabwe catastrophe more culpable, not lessclick for image
1.Zimbabwe's life expectancy fell from 61 in 1990 to 37 by 2006; this collapse of the health system, agricultural sector, and rule of law occurred under the direction of a man who understood economic and governance mechanisms thoroughly.
2.The seized farms were distributed primarily to Mugabe's military and political allies rather than to poor black smallholders — demonstrating that the land reform was patronage politics using liberation rhetoric as cover, not genuine redistribution. (Dictatorland, Ch. 3)
3.A man of Mugabe's intellectual capacity cannot claim the excuse of not understanding what his policies would produce; his philosophical sophistication is an aggravating circumstance, not a mitigating one.
∴The liberation credentials and intellectual sophistication that Chan emphasises in Mugabe do not provide mitigation for the Zimbabwe catastrophe; they provide the opposite.
Argument IV — Gaddafi's Green Book did not prevent his $200 billion personal fortuneclick for image
1.Gaddafi wrote a political philosophy — the Green Book — proposing direct democracy and the distribution of oil wealth to the Libyan people; he simultaneously accumulated a personal fortune estimated at $200 billion. (Dictatorland, Ch. 5)
2.His government ordered the Lockerbie bombing (270 dead), funded the IRA and rejectionist Palestinian factions, and maintained prisons condemned by Amnesty International — all while its stated philosophy argued for justice and self-determination.
3.The gap between the stated philosophy and the governance record is, in Gaddafi's case, as wide as it can be; the philosophical text is evidence of the distance between rhetoric and practice, not of its author's visionary character.
∴Gaddafi is the limiting case of the philosopher-king who does not govern by his philosophy; the Green Book is evidence that philosophically sophisticated articulation and just governance are entirely separable.
Argument V — Abacha shows the outcomes are similar whether or not there is a philosophyclick for image
1.Sani Abacha made no philosophical claims, had no liberation credentials, and wrote no political philosophy — yet produced governance outcomes (stolen billions, murdered critics, suppressed opposition) structurally identical to the philosopher-kings.
2.He hanged Ken Saro-Wiwa and eight Ogoni activists on fabricated charges after a trial condemned by his own legal establishment — on the same day Shell published its annual report praising Abacha's government. (Dictatorland, Ch. 6)
3.If the outcomes are similar with and without a liberation philosophy, then the philosophy is not doing the causal work Chan attributes to it in either enabling or preventing the governance pathology.
∴Abacha serves as a control case demonstrating that governance pathology in Africa is not primarily a function of philosophy but of structural conditions — including the availability of extractable wealth and the absence of institutional constraints on power.
Argument VI — Equatorial Guinea: 77% poverty alongside the world's most oil-rich eliteclick for image
1.Equatorial Guinea's oil discovery transformed its GDP per capita figures to European levels while 77% of its population lives in poverty, demonstrating that resource wealth without accountable governance produces the maximum possible inequality. (Dictatorland, Ch. 8)
2.Teodorin Obiang's documented personal expenditure — $35m Malibu mansion, $38.5m private jet, $380m yacht — while serving as Deputy President is not explained by philosophy, ideology, or Western intervention but by the simple absence of any mechanism of accountability.
3.The Western oil companies that enabled this — ExxonMobil, Marathon, Hess — bear complicity, but so does Obiang, whose choices about how to distribute oil revenues were his own.
∴Equatorial Guinea is the clearest demonstration that the material conditions for catastrophic governance are structural — resource wealth plus absent accountability — and do not require an ideology to produce them.
Argument VII — Liberation philosophy functions as transition technology, then as control ideologyclick for image
1.The philosophy of liberation is the ideology of the struggle: it mobilises, legitimises, and critiques colonialism effectively — then, once power is achieved, it either is abandoned or becomes a state ideology used as a tool of political control.
2.This pattern — liberation philosophy as transition technology — recurs across Consciencism, Ujamaa, Authenticité, and the Green Book, suggesting a structural dynamic rather than individual failure. (Dictatorland, conclusion)
3.Explaining this pattern as external interference alone cannot account for the choices made at the moment when power was secured and the philosophy could have been enacted but was instead instrumentalised.
∴The structural pattern demands explanation beyond individual character or Western conspiracy; the relationship between liberation philosophy and authoritarian consolidation is the central problem that Chan's account raises but does not resolve.
Argument VIII — The philosophical argument must answer to the testimonial recordclick for image
1.Zimbabwe's currency reached one hundred trillion dollar denominations in 2008; life expectancy fell to 37; the agricultural sector that had fed southern Africa collapsed — under a government with a serious political philosophy and serious liberation credentials.
2.The survivors of Mobutu's Congo, Gaddafi's Libya, Abacha's Nigeria, and Mugabe's Zimbabwe are not inclined to excuse the governance that destroyed their lives on the grounds that their rulers were interesting thinkers.
3.A political philosophy that cannot answer to the people it governed — that takes its validation from academic reception rather than from the quality of the lives it produced — has failed the most basic test of what political philosophy is for.
∴The testimonial record — the lived experience of the governed — is the final court of appeal for any political philosophy; philosophy that cannot answer this court has not yet been adequately defended.
Argument IX — Houphouët-Boigny exposes what "moderation" meant in practiceclick for image
1.Houphouët-Boigny redirected an estimated $11 billion of Ivorian cocoa revenues to personal and political ends and built a cathedral larger than St Peter's in his home village while most Ivorians lacked clean water. (Dictatorland, Ch. 9)
2.He was consistently described by Western governments as a "moderate" — meaning he made no philosophical claims, asked no difficult questions about ownership of resources, and kept France informed — while almost certainly organising the assassination of Thomas Sankara.
3.The comparison between Sankara (who asked the questions, died poor, was murdered) and Houphouët-Boigny (who didn't, died rich, was celebrated) reveals that Western approval of "moderate" African leaders was approval of theft that didn't threaten Western interests.
∴The "moderate/radical" distinction applied by the West to African leaders tracked not the justice of their governance but the convenience of their relationship to Western economic interests; Houphouët-Boigny makes this visible.
