Mr President,

The motion before this House poses the central question of rational and political philosophy in its most austere form: when we cannot assign probabilities to outcomes, how should we choose? I shall argue that the maximin principle — choosing the option whose worst possible outcome is best — is the only rational answer, and further that this answer is not merely theoretically satisfying but is the foundation of a theory of justice.

On what distinguishes deep uncertainty from risk

Deep uncertainty vs riskWe must begin by marking a distinction that my opponents will try to obscure. Risk is uncertainty with known probabilities: I know there is a one-in-six chance of rolling a six. Deep uncertainty — what Knight called genuine uncertainty and what I call the situation of the original position — is uncertainty without even a probability distribution over outcomes. When we are behind the veil of ignorance, we do not merely lack knowledge of which social position we will occupy. We lack any principled basis for assigning probabilities to those positions. The counsel that we should simply assume each position is equally likely — the Principle of Insufficient Reason — is not a theorem but a question-begging stipulation, and Harsanyi's reliance on it is precisely what undermines his argument.

On the three conditions that justify maximin

Three conditions for maximinThe maximin principle is not a universal rule for rational choice. It is the rational rule under three jointly sufficient conditions. First, one has no reliable basis for estimating probabilities. Second, the chooser is indifferent to gains above a satisfactory minimum — gains above the threshold do not compensate for the risk of catastrophic loss. Third, the worst outcomes of the alternatives are intolerably bad — what I call the strains of commitment. When all three conditions hold simultaneously, maximising expected utility is not rational boldness; it is recklessness masquerading as rationality. And in the original position, all three conditions hold.

On the strains of commitment

The strains of commitment argumentThe deepest argument for maximin concerns what I call the strains of commitment. A principle of justice must be one that persons can actually live by — one that does not demand that those in the worst social positions affirm the justice of a system that has treated them catastrophically for the benefit of the better-off. If persons behind the veil of ignorance chose principles that might place them in positions of severe deprivation, and if they then found themselves in those positions, they could not sincerely affirm the justice of those principles. The maximin principle, by guaranteeing the best possible worst outcome, is the only principle that survives this test.

On the objection from the Principle of Insufficient Reason

Against the Principle of Insufficient ReasonProfessor Harsanyi and those who follow him assume that ignorance of probabilities licenses assigning equal probabilities to all outcomes — the Principle of Insufficient Reason. But this principle generates contradictions. If I am equally ignorant about whether I will be born into the top half or the bottom half of the income distribution, and equally ignorant about whether I will be in the top or bottom quarter of each half, I generate different probability distributions depending on how I partition the space. There is no principled partition; the Principle of Insufficient Reason is thus not a solution to the problem of ignorance but a concealment of it. Maximin requires no such spurious probabilities.

On minimax regret and the Hurwicz criterion as half-measures

Against minimax regret and HurwiczMy opponents may reach for minimax regret — minimising the maximum loss relative to what one could have achieved — or for Hurwicz's weighted combination of best and worst outcomes. Both are improvements on pure expected utility maximisation under ignorance; both acknowledge that worst-case outcomes demand special attention. But both introduce elements — the regret calculation, the Hurwicz coefficient α — that are themselves underdetermined in conditions of deep uncertainty. What value of α expresses rational caution? Minimax regret requires comparing outcomes across states of nature in ways that presuppose a common metric not available under genuine ignorance. Maximin alone requires only an ordering of outcomes — the minimal information available under deep uncertainty.

Gentlemen, the veil of ignorance is not a thought experiment. It is the situation of moral reasoning about institutions. And from behind that veil, only maximin is rational.

Mr President,

Professor Rawls has made the classical case for maximin. I want to make a more refined case — one that retains the central insight of maximin while addressing its most serious internal difficulties. The view I defend is what I call threshold maximin or leximin with a welfare floor: below a critical threshold of decent human functioning, the interests of the worst-off have absolute priority; above that threshold, standard aggregative reasoning is appropriate.

On the limits of pure maximin

The limits of pure maximinPure maximin, as Professor Rawls defends it, faces a genuine internal problem. It implies that we should always prefer a policy that raises the welfare of the single worst-off person by one unit over a policy that raises the welfare of a billion people by a million units each — provided the worst-off person remains worst off. This implication is extreme, and I think it stems not from a deep commitment to justice but from an excessive formalism. The correct insight behind maximin is not that the worst-off always trump in all circumstances, but that we owe a special, non-negotiable duty of concern to those below a threshold of decent life.

On the welfare threshold

The welfare threshold argumentWhat I call the welfare threshold is the level of welfare below which a person's life lacks the conditions for basic human functioning and dignity — the level below which she cannot reasonably be asked to accept the ordinary risks and trade-offs of social cooperation. Below this threshold, the absolutism of maximin is correct: no aggregative calculation justifies leaving persons below it for the benefit of the better-off. Above the threshold, the urgency disappears and standard aggregative or prioritarian reasoning becomes appropriate. This is not an ad hoc qualification. It follows from the same reasoning that supports maximin — the strains of commitment argument applies with full force only below the threshold.

On separateness of persons and interpersonal aggregation

Separateness of persons supports threshold maximinThe deepest objection to both pure utilitarianism and Harsanyi's expected utility approach is that they aggregate across persons in ways that treat the separateness of persons as morally irrelevant. A large benefit to one person compensates for a smaller loss to another — but this ignores that these are distinct lives, not a single life in which gains in one period compensate for losses in another. Threshold maximin respects the separateness of persons where it matters most — below the threshold — while permitting sensible aggregation where the individual stakes are not catastrophic. It is the most defensible position in the moral space between absolute priority for the worst-off and indifference to distribution.

On Bradley's decision-theoretic objections

Response to decision-theoretic objectionsProfessor Bradley will argue from decision theory that any departure from expected utility maximisation violates coherence conditions on rational choice. But this argument assumes that the coherence constraints of decision theory are the appropriate norms for ethical choice under deep uncertainty about welfare distributions. I deny this assumption. Decision theory characterises rationality for a single agent choosing among gambles for her own benefit. The choice of principles of justice is not this: it is a choice of rules that will govern the claims of distinct persons against one another, under genuine uncertainty about who those persons will be and what claims they will have. The coherence norms of expected utility theory do not transfer without argument to this domain.

On Binmore's evolutionary objection

Response to Binmore's evolutionary critiqueKen Binmore has argued that the original position, properly understood using evolutionary game theory, selects for average utilitarianism rather than maximin — because agents who maximise expected utility outcompete those who maximise minimum outcomes. But this argument shows at most that evolved social norms tend toward average utilitarianism. It says nothing about whether such norms are morally correct. Rawls's project is normative, not descriptive or evolutionary. The fact that natural selection might produce Harsanyian preferences in social bargaining is entirely compatible with the moral conclusion that those preferences are not what justice requires. Evolutionary stability is not moral justification.

Mr President,

Professor Rawls has presented maximin as the voice of rational caution under deep uncertainty. I want to show that maximin is not rational caution but irrational phobia — the systematisation of extreme risk aversion into a principle of justice — and that expected utility maximisation is the correct standard of rational choice even behind the veil of ignorance.

On the Principle of Insufficient Reason and equiprobability

Defence of the Principle of Insufficient ReasonRawls claims that there is no basis for assigning probabilities to social positions behind the veil. But this is simply incorrect. The Principle of Insufficient Reason — assigning equal probabilities to events when one has no evidence favouring any over the others — is not a question-begging stipulation but the unique probability assignment consistent with total symmetry of information. If you know nothing that distinguishes position A from position B, assigning equal probability is not an assumption; it is the only assignment that does not arbitrarily privilege one position over another. The veil of ignorance creates precisely this symmetry. Behind it, equal probabilities are mandatory.

On maximin as infinite risk aversion

Maximin implies infinite risk aversionMaximin, as a decision rule, is equivalent to infinite risk aversion — a preference so extreme that it would refuse any chance of gain, however large, to avoid any chance of worsening the worst outcome, however small. Consider: maximin would prefer a world where the worst-off person has welfare level 10 and everyone else has welfare level 11, over a world where the worst-off has welfare 9 and everyone else has welfare 10,000. This is not rational caution. This is a pathological prioritisation of the worst case that no coherent account of rational preferences could sustain. A tiny reduction in the worst outcome matters infinitely more than a vast improvement for all others — this is not justice but a kind of obsessive focus that is antithetical to rational choice.

On the aggregation theorem and rational social choice

The aggregation theoremMy 1955 aggregation theorem establishes that if individual preferences are rational in the von Neumann-Morgenstern sense, and if social preferences respect the Pareto principle, then the social welfare function must be a weighted sum of individual utility functions. With equal weighting — which the veil of ignorance demands — we obtain average utilitarianism. This is not a philosophical argument that can be resisted by appeal to intuitions about the worst-off. It is a mathematical theorem. To reject expected utility in the original position, one must reject either individual rationality or the Pareto principle — both of which are more firmly established than maximin.

On the strains of commitment — a misleading argument

Against the strains of commitmentRawls argues that persons in the worst position could not sincerely affirm principles that sanctioned their deprivation. But this argument proves too much. Persons in the worst position under average utilitarian arrangements might also find those arrangements hard to affirm from their position — this is simply a feature of any system that does not guarantee the maximum for the worst-off. More fundamentally, the strains of commitment argument is a psychological observation about what people can tolerate, not a logical argument about what is just. Justice is not determined by what the least fortunate can psychologically affirm. If it were, justice would be held hostage to the most extreme psychological fragility.

On what rational persons would actually choose

Rational persons choose expected utilityWhat would a rational person actually choose behind the veil of ignorance? Not the obsessive focus on the worst case that maximin recommends. A rational person — one who does not suffer from infinite risk aversion — would weigh all possible positions equally and choose the arrangement that maximises her expected utility across them. This is precisely what average utilitarianism recommends. The claim that rational persons would adopt maximin depends on attributing to them an extreme and incoherent risk preference that is not required by rationality and is, if anything, a symptom of irrationality. Maximin is not wisdom. It is a phobia dressed in philosophical language.

Mr President,

The debate so far has been framed as a choice between maximin and expected utility maximisation. I want to challenge this framing. Even granting Professor Rawls's premise that deep uncertainty precludes probability assignments, maximin does not follow as the uniquely rational response. The decision-theoretic literature offers better alternatives — specifically minimax regret and the Hurwicz criterion — that are more sensitive to the full structure of decision problems under ignorance.

On minimax regret

Minimax regret as an alternative to maximinThe minimax regret criterion — developed by Leonard Savage and Jürgen Niehans — advises choosing the option that minimises the maximum regret one could experience. Regret is measured as the difference between what one actually obtains and what one could have obtained by choosing differently, given how the world turned out. Crucially, minimax regret does not require probability assignments — it operates on the same ordinal information as maximin. But unlike maximin, it is sensitive to the opportunity costs of caution: the cost of the maximin choice, measured in what one sacrifices by avoiding the worst case, is part of the minimax regret calculation. Maximin ignores these opportunity costs entirely.

On the Hurwicz criterion

The Hurwicz criterion as a generalisationThe Hurwicz criterion, due to Leonid Hurwicz, proposes a weighted average of the best and worst outcomes of each option:

H(α, option) = α · (worst outcome) + (1−α) · (best outcome)

The parameter α ∈ [0,1] represents the agent's degree of pessimism. When α = 1, Hurwicz reduces to maximin. When α = 0, it reduces to maximax — choosing the option with the best possible outcome. Rational choice under deep uncertainty, I submit, involves selecting some intermediate value of α appropriate to the stakes. The motion's claim that α = 1 is rationally mandated — that we should be maximally pessimistic — is precisely what requires justification, and Rawls does not provide it. He stipulates maximin rather than deriving it.

On the objection that α is underdetermined

Defending the Hurwicz criterion against underdeterminationRawls objects that the Hurwicz criterion is underdetermined because the choice of α is not constrained by the criterion itself. But this objection, if pressed, defeats maximin too — why should the degree of pessimism be maximal rather than merely high? The appropriate response to underdetermination is not to collapse to an extreme case and call it uniquely rational, but to acknowledge that deep uncertainty creates genuine indeterminacy in the choice of decision rule. My position is that the decision theory of deep uncertainty is itself uncertain — a point that favours neither maximin nor expected utility, but does favour a framework flexible enough to model the choice of risk attitude.

On dominance reasoning and when maximin is appropriate

When maximin is and is not appropriateThere is a genuinely good decision-theoretic argument for something like maximin: the argument from dominance. If option A produces a better outcome than option B in every possible state of the world, A dominates B and should be chosen. Extended to the case where one option has a better worst case and is not worse in any other case, something like maximin reasoning is warranted. But this reasoning applies only in cases of lexical dominance — where one option is genuinely better in the worst case without sacrificing better outcomes elsewhere. When there are genuine trade-offs between worst-case protection and better expected or best-case outcomes, dominance reasoning does not select maximin, and Rawls's argument does not show otherwise.

On the decision-theoretic status of Rawls's three conditions

The three conditions do not uniquely select maximinRawls specifies three conditions under which maximin is appropriate. I accept that conditions of deep uncertainty with catastrophic worst cases are special. But the three conditions, even jointly, do not uniquely select maximin over minimax regret or an appropriately calibrated Hurwicz criterion. Both of these alternatives are also responsive to the badness of worst outcomes, neither requires probability assignments, and both take into account information about the full outcome matrix that maximin ignores. The motion claims maximin is uniquely rational under these conditions. This claim is false. Multiple decision criteria satisfy the conditions, and maximin is merely one of them — not the most defensible one.

Mr President,

Both previous speakers have treated the original position as a thought experiment about individual rational choice in isolation. I want to argue that this is the fundamental error underlying the entire debate. The original position is a bargaining problem — a problem in which the principles of justice emerge from a strategic interaction among rational agents — and evolutionary game theory, not individual decision theory, is the correct tool for analysing it. When properly analysed, the original position selects average utilitarianism, not maximin.

On the original position as a bargaining problem

The original position is a bargaining problemRawls treats the original position as if it were the decision problem of a single isolated agent choosing behind a veil of ignorance. But the original position is populated by many persons, all choosing simultaneously. This is not an individual decision problem; it is a bargaining problem. The appropriate framework is Nash bargaining theory, or its evolutionary extension — not individual decision theory under uncertainty. When the original position is analysed as a bargaining problem, the equilibrium concept selects average utilitarianism, because average utilitarianism corresponds to the Nash bargaining solution with equal bargaining weights.

On evolutionary stability and the empirical original position

Evolutionary stability argumentThe evolutionary approach asks not what a single ideally rational agent would choose from behind a veil, but what norms of justice would emerge and be stable in a population of agents who repeatedly interact under conditions of impartiality. The answer, established by evolutionary game theory, is that norms approximating average utilitarianism are evolutionarily stable — they resist invasion by alternative norms — while maximin norms are not. This is not merely a descriptive point. If we ask what principles of justice could be sustained as stable social arrangements — could actually function as principles that persons live by — average utilitarian norms pass the test and maximin norms fail it.

On Rawls's use of the original position

Rawls misuses the original position deviceRawls introduces the veil of ignorance to achieve impartiality — to model the idea that principles of justice should not be biased toward any particular person's interests. This is a sound methodological aim. But Rawls then makes two unjustified moves. First, he treats the original position as an individual decision problem, ignoring its strategic character. Second, he imports maximin as the decision rule for this individual problem without deriving it from the conditions of the original position. The veil of ignorance secures impartiality; it does not, by itself, mandate maximin. The additional stipulation of maximin is a substantive and unjustified addition to the original position framework.

On the Nash bargaining solution and equal division

Nash bargaining selects average utilitarianismThe Nash bargaining solution with equal bargaining weights divides the cooperative surplus equally among agents in expected utility terms. Behind the veil of ignorance, where all persons have equal bargaining weight by assumption, the Nash solution selects the arrangement that maximises average expected utility — average utilitarianism. This is not a coincidence. The Nash solution is the unique solution satisfying the axioms of efficiency, symmetry, scale invariance, and independence of irrelevant alternatives — axioms that are themselves conditions of impartiality and rationality. Average utilitarianism follows from conditions Rawls himself should accept.

On the role of maximin in Rawls's actual argument

Maximin as a rhetorical deviceI want to make a diagnostic observation about the structure of Rawls's argument. Maximin does the real work in Rawls's theory not because it is rationally mandated by the original position but because Rawls has constructed the original position to select what he already knows he wants — the difference principle. The features of the original position — the choice of maximin as the decision rule, the fineness of the outcome partition, the treatment of the worst case — are all calibrated to produce the difference principle. This is the original position as reverse-engineering, not as derivation. A properly constructed original position, with the right decision theory, selects average utilitarianism.
