Mr President,

Let me state the paradox precisely and then explain why I believe the near-universal reaction to it — that it refutes expected utility theory — gets the diagnosis exactly wrong. The Allais paradox does not demonstrate a deficiency in the theory. It demonstrates a systematic tendency of human beings to make reasoning errors when comparing gambles that involve certainty — errors that, when pointed out, the agents themselves typically wish to correct.

"When Allais presented his pairs of choices to me at a 1952 Paris conference, I made the common preference pattern. I chose A over B, and D over C. When the violation of the independence axiom was pointed out to me, I recognised that I had made a mistake. The paradox is a demonstration that intelligent people reason poorly about compound lotteries under the influence of a certainty effect. It is not a demonstration that the independence axiom is wrong."
i. the paradox stated

Allais choices — common consequence effect — independence axiom violatedThe paradox involves two pairs of gambles. In the first pair, most people choose A over B:

A: $1 million with certainty
B: 89% chance of $1m · 10% chance of $5m · 1% chance of $0
In the second pair, most people choose D over C:
C: 11% chance of $1m · 89% chance of $0
D: 10% chance of $5m · 90% chance of $0
The pattern A≻B and D≻C violates expected utility theory's independence axiom. To see why: A and B differ only in what happens in the 11-in-100 cases where B offers a lottery between $5m and $0 instead of $1m certain. The 89-in-100 common consequence ($1m) should be irrelevant to this comparison. If you prefer the certainty of $1m to the lottery in those 11 cases — choosing A — you should prefer C to D, because C just is A with the common consequence replaced by $0, and D just is B with the common consequence replaced by $0. To prefer A over B but D over C is to weigh the same underlying 11-in-100 lottery differently depending on what the other 89 cases deliver. That is the independence axiom's violation, and it is a mistake.

ii. the sure-thing principle — why independence is rationally required

Sure-thing principle — Savage's argument; common consequence irrelevance; partition invarianceIn The Foundations of Statistics, I formulated the sure-thing principle: if you prefer one act over another in every possible state of the world, you should prefer it overall. The independence axiom is a probabilistic expression of this principle: if two gambles agree in their outcomes across 89% of scenarios and differ only in the remaining 11%, then the preference between them should be determined solely by the 11% where they differ. The 89% common consequence is irrelevant. It is literally a sure thing that drops out of the comparison. To allow a common consequence to influence the preference between two gambles that agree on it is to let something that cannot matter causally influence something it is supposed to be irrelevant to. This is not a preference pattern that rational agents should wish to have on reflection.

iii. the certainty effect — a cognitive bias, not a rational consideration

Certainty effect — psychological salience of certainty; isolation effect; framing-dependence reveals errorThe typical explanation for the Allais preferences is the certainty effect: agents overweight outcomes that are certain relative to merely probable ones. In the first pair, the certainty of $1m in option A is psychologically salient in a way that creates a disproportionate attraction — a fear of the 1% chance of $0 in option B that distorts the comparison. This effect is a cognitive bias, not a rational consideration. Evidence for its bias-status comes from framing experiments: the same underlying choice can be presented in multiple ways, and whether people make the Allais-pattern choice or the EU-consistent choice depends heavily on how the gambles are described. When the common-consequence structure is made transparent — when the gambles are presented as sequential decisions with the 89% common outcome paid out before the 11% lottery is run — the majority preference reverses. This framing-dependence is the mark of a cognitive error, not a stable rational preference.

iv. the Dutch book and money pump arguments

Money pump — Allais preferences are money-pumpable; Dutch book argument; dynamic consistencyAllais preferences generate dynamic inconsistency and vulnerability to money pumps. An agent with Allais preferences who faces a sequential version of the paradox — making decisions at different nodes as the game tree unfolds — will make choices that, taken together, guarantee a worse outcome than a simpler strategy would have achieved. The money pump argument is decisive: a clever bookmaker can exploit the agent's intransitive or independence-violating preferences to extract money from them through a series of trades, each of which the agent accepts, but which collectively leave the agent with less than they started. An agent who can be systematically exploited in this way has preferences that fail at the most basic level of practical rationality. The Allais preferences are not merely suboptimal in some sense — they are exploitable, and exploitability is the practical counterpart of the normative failure the independence axiom identifies.

v. the response to behavioural evidence

Normative vs. descriptive — EU is normative; widespread violations do not refute it; mathematics is also widely violatedThe opposition will argue that the pervasiveness of Allais preferences — the fact that a large majority of subjects, including highly educated economists and philosophers, exhibit them — is evidence that the preferences are rational. I reject this inference. The pervasiveness of a cognitive error does not make it rational. A majority of people make systematic errors in probability estimation — the base rate neglect, the conjunction fallacy, the gambler's fallacy — and none of these become normatively acceptable because they are widespread. Expected utility theory is a theory of ideal rationality, not a description of how human beings actually reason. The fact that human beings systematically fail to reason in accordance with it is evidence of the difficulty of reasoning correctly under uncertainty, not evidence that the theory is wrong.

Mr President,

I want to occupy a middle position between Savage's full defence of expected utility and the opposition's claim that EU theory must be abandoned. My argument, developed with Robert Sugden in regret theory, is that the Allais preferences can be explained by a psychologically realistic and normatively defensible extension of the standard EU framework — one that takes seriously the emotional consequences of decisions, specifically the feelings of regret and rejoicing that arise when agents learn what would have happened under alternatives not chosen. This is not merely a descriptive story. The emotions of regret and disappointment are morally and practically relevant features of decision situations that a comprehensive normative theory must incorporate.

"The standard expected utility framework treats outcomes as if they occur in isolation — as though what matters about a lottery is only what it delivers, not what it would have delivered had you made a different choice. But this is psychologically and normatively false. When you choose the lottery over the certain sum and receive nothing, you do not merely experience the disutility of having nothing. You experience regret — the painful awareness of what you could have had. A rational agent who foresees this regret has a reason to choose the certain sum that is not captured by EU theory."
i. regret theory — the basic framework

Regret theory — modified utility; choiceworthiness function; comparative evaluation; Loomes-Sugden 1982In our 1982 paper, Robert Sugden and I proposed that agents evaluate outcomes not only in terms of their intrinsic utility but also in terms of their comparison with outcomes that would have obtained under alternatives not chosen. When you choose act A over act B and learn the state of the world, you receive not only the outcome utility Q(x_A) but also a regret-rejoicing component R(Q(x_A) − Q(x_B)) — where R is an increasing, convex function capturing the asymmetry between regret (worse than the alternative) and rejoicing (better than the alternative). Regret is, characteristically, more intense than the corresponding rejoicing — an asymmetry with robust psychological support. The choiceworthiness of an act is the expected value of its modified utility, integrating over all states the combination of intrinsic utility and regret-rejoicing. This framework is not merely descriptive — it incorporates a normatively defensible motivation: the rational anticipation of an emotion that is genuinely relevant to the agent's wellbeing.

ii. explaining the Allais pattern

Allais pattern explained — regret in B's 1% case enormous; anticipatory regret justifies A preferenceThe regret framework explains the Allais pattern with considerable precision. In the first pair, option B carries a 1% chance of receiving nothing while option A delivers $1m with certainty. If you choose B and land in the 1% case, you experience profound regret: you could have had $1m for certain, and you received nothing. The anticipated intensity of this regret — very large, because the counterfactual outcome is very good and the foregone outcome is very bad — justifies assigning extra weight to avoiding the 1% worst case. In the second pair, both C and D carry substantial probabilities of receiving nothing (89% and 90% respectively), so the regret from the worst-case scenario in D is not intensified by the contrast with a certain alternative. The asymmetry between the first and second pairs is precisely the asymmetry in regret intensity between the two 1% worst cases — which depends on what the common consequence is. The common consequence is therefore not irrelevant: it determines the regret intensity of the worst-case outcome.

iii. is regret a normatively legitimate consideration?

Normative legitimacy of regret — regret affects wellbeing; rational anticipation of regret is rational; consequentialist theory must include itSavage's response will be: regret is a psychological contingency, not a normative consideration. A rational agent should evaluate outcomes in terms of their intrinsic utility, not in terms of how they compare to counterfactuals. I want to resist this strongly. Regret is a genuine component of wellbeing: when an agent chooses B and receives nothing, they are not merely in the state "possessing zero dollars" — they are in the state "possessing zero dollars while knowing they could have had $1m." These are different states of wellbeing. A rational agent who foresees this difference in wellbeing has a normatively legitimate reason to prefer the certain outcome — not because certainty is intrinsically valued, but because the transition from a certain $1m to a lottery that risks zero involves genuine wellbeing consequences that the lottery's expected monetary value does not capture. Consequentialist decision theory must include all consequences of an act — including the emotional consequences of learning what would have happened.

iv. against Savage's money pump

Against money pump — regret theory is dynamically consistent in specific tree structures; the pump requires ignoring regret throughoutSavage argues that Allais preferences generate money pumps. The regret-theory response is that this objection applies to preferences that violate transitivity — which regret theory does imply in some cases — but that the money pump is only devastating if the agent is naïve about their own preferences. A sophisticated agent who knows they have regret-sensitive preferences can commit in advance to a strategy that avoids exploitation. Furthermore, the money pump argument assumes that the agent's preferences in sequential choice should be identical to their preferences in one-shot choice. But if regret is a genuine component of wellbeing, sequential choices involve different regret calculations than one-shot choices, and the apparent inconsistency may be a rational response to genuinely different decision situations.

v. a compromise position — the spirit of EU preserved

Compromise — regret theory preserves consequentialism and utility maximisation; extends not abandons EU; Allais pattern is rational under regretI want to be clear about where I stand relative to the motion. I do not believe EU theory is normatively adequate as currently formulated — because it does not account for regret and disappointment. But I do not believe it must be abandoned. Regret theory preserves the spirit of EU: it is consequentialist, it evaluates acts by their expected value, and it derives choices from a utility representation. What it adds is a richer specification of what consequences include — specifically, the relational, comparative emotional consequences of learning counterfactuals. The Allais preferences are not a cognitive error on this account. They are a rational response to the complete outcome space of the decision, including its emotional topology. The motion is right that EU theory as standardly stated is normatively inadequate. It is wrong if it implies that no extension within the EU spirit can adequately account for the pattern.

Mr President,

The proposition has framed the debate as one between expected utility theory and various attempts to extend or correct it. I want to challenge the entire framework of that framing. The right question is not "can we preserve EU theory by restricting or extending it?" but "what is the empirical structure of human decision-making under risk, and how can we build a theory that correctly describes it?" Prospect theory, which Amos Tversky and I developed in 1979 and extended in cumulative prospect theory in 1992, is our answer — and its central claim is that the phenomena the Allais paradox reveals are not isolated violations of a correct theory but symptoms of a systematic psychology that EU theory is structurally unable to accommodate.

"Expected utility theory treats risk as a single dimension: the probability-weighted sum of outcome utilities. Prospect theory recognises that people evaluate gambles differently depending on whether outcomes are framed as gains or losses relative to a reference point, that they weight probabilities non-linearly with special overweighting of small probabilities and underweighting of moderate ones, and that they are more sensitive to losses than to equivalent gains. These are not biases to be corrected. They are the structure of human value and probability perception."
i. prospect theory — the four core components

Prospect theory — reference dependence; loss aversion; probability weighting; fourfold patternProspect theory rests on four empirical findings that are each robustly replicated and structurally inconsistent with expected utility theory. First, reference dependence: people evaluate outcomes as gains and losses relative to a reference point, not in terms of final wealth states. The same absolute amount is experienced differently as a gain or loss. Second, loss aversion: losses loom approximately twice as large as equivalent gains — a finding with strong evolutionary plausibility and consistent experimental support. Third, probability weighting: people do not treat probabilities linearly. Small probabilities are systematically overweighted; moderate and large probabilities are underweighted. The probability weighting function is inverse-S shaped: concave for low probabilities, convex for high ones, with a crossing near certainty. Fourth, diminishing sensitivity in both the value function and probability domain, producing the fourfold pattern of risk attitudes.

ii. the certainty effect in prospect theory

Certainty effect — probability weighting at the boundary between risk and certainty; overweighting of 100%The Allais paradox's common consequence effect is a direct expression of the probability weighting function's behaviour near certainty. A 100% probability of $1m is weighted as 1.0 by the weighting function w(1.0) = 1.0 — certainty has its full weight. An 89% probability of $1m has weight w(0.89) considerably below 0.89, because the probability weighting function underweights moderate-to-high probabilities. The difference between w(1.0) and w(0.89) is disproportionately large — much larger than the difference between w(0.11) and w(0.10). This is why the 89% common consequence matters so much to the comparison between A and B: changing it from $1m to $0 removes a guaranteed component and replaces it with a probability, and the subjective weight of certainty is disproportionately large. The independence axiom fails because it ignores the qualitative difference between certainty and probability — a difference that is real in human psychology.

iii. the fourfold pattern — EU cannot accommodate it

Fourfold pattern — risk aversion for gains/high prob; risk seeking for losses/low prob; crosses EU's risk aversion as a single parameterEU theory captures risk aversion through the concavity of the utility function: a concave utility function generates risk aversion across all probability-outcome pairs. But human risk attitudes follow a fourfold pattern that a single utility function shape cannot accommodate: risk aversion for moderate-to-high probability gains (preferring a certain $50 to a 50% chance of $100); risk seeking for low-probability gains (preferring a 1% chance of $10,000 to certain $90 — explaining lottery ticket purchases); risk aversion for low-probability losses (insurance purchase for small probability high-consequence events); risk seeking for high-probability losses (preferring a gamble over a certain loss — explaining why people gamble to avoid bankruptcy). No concave utility function generates all four patterns simultaneously. Prospect theory's combination of loss aversion, reference dependence, and probability weighting generates all four — which is the decisive evidence that EU theory's structural assumptions are wrong, not merely its parameter values.

iv. against Savage — the normative/descriptive distinction is less clean than he claims

Normative/descriptive collapse — if theory systematically misrepresents rational agents it must be revised; pervasive violations by sophisticated agents challenge the pure normative statusSavage argues that the pervasiveness of Allais preferences does not refute EU's normative status. But this argument has limits. When a normative theory of rationality is violated not only by naive subjects but by economists, philosophers, mathematicians, and decision theorists — including Savage himself, as he acknowledged — the argument that the violations reflect cognitive failures rather than a deep feature of the decision structure becomes implausible. A normative theory that virtually no agent, including the theory's most sophisticated practitioners, can implement in practice is normatively suspect: it describes an ideal that is not merely difficult to achieve but systematically inaccessible to human cognition. At some point, a normative theory that is systematically divorced from human possibility must be revised — not because the human is the measure of all things, but because normative theory must be action-guiding to be action-relevant.

v. cumulative prospect theory — the normatively serious successor

Cumulative prospect theory — rank-dependent; stochastic dominance respected; avoids original PT's problems; full replacement not patchThe original 1979 prospect theory had technical deficiencies — it violated stochastic dominance in some cases, a particularly serious normative failing. Cumulative prospect theory (Tversky and Kahneman, 1992) corrected this by applying the probability weighting function to cumulative distributions rather than individual outcomes — the rank-dependent approach developed independently by John Quiggin. The cumulative formulation respects stochastic dominance while retaining all of prospect theory's empirical virtues. It is not a patch on EU theory but a complete replacement: a theory with different structural assumptions about value and probability perception that correctly predicts the fourfold pattern, the Allais effect, the Ellsberg paradox, and a wide range of other anomalies that EU theory cannot accommodate without increasingly ad hoc modifications.

Mr President,

I want to make the most ambitious claim in this debate: not merely that EU theory is descriptively inadequate, but that the Allais preferences are normatively permissible — that an agent who prefers A over B and D over C in the standard Allais setup is not making a mistake, not failing to reason properly about compound lotteries, not the victim of a cognitive bias, but expressing a coherent and legitimate attitude toward risk that EU theory is structurally unable to accommodate. My book Risk and Rationality defends this claim through a theory — risk-weighted expected utility (REU) — that shows how risk-attitudes function as a third independent component of rational agency alongside beliefs and desires.

"Savage tells us that an agent who chooses the certain million is making an error — failing to apply the independence axiom correctly. I say: the agent who prefers a policy of cautious, sure-thing-securing choices over a policy of expected-value-maximising choices is expressing a coherent global preference for how their life goes. They are not irrationally fixated on certainty. They are making a legitimate judgment that, when things matter enough, they prefer to guarantee good outcomes over gambling for better ones. This is a risk-attitude, and it is rational."
i. REU theory — the formal framework

REU — risk function r; weights outcomes by rank and probability; EU is the special case r(x)=x; Allais preferences recoveredRisk-weighted expected utility theory introduces a third parameter alongside the utility function u and the subjective probability function p: a risk function r, which maps cumulative probabilities to decision weights. Where EU computes the expectation E[u] = Σ p_i u(x_i), REU computes a weighted sum where the weight on outcome x_i depends not just on its probability p_i but on its rank in the outcome distribution — specifically, on the cumulative probability of doing at least as well as x_i. A risk-averse risk function r is convex: it gives extra weight to worse outcomes relative to their probability, reflecting caution about the worst cases. The Allais preferences are recovered by a risk-averse risk function: the agent gives extra weight to the worst outcome in option B (the 1% zero case) because they evaluate the gamble globally — attending to what happens in the worst scenarios with more than proportional weight. EU is the special case where r(x) = x — linear risk function, risk-neutral in the REU sense.

ii. the global sensitivity argument

Global sensitivity — REU agents care about how good/bad things are across the whole gamble; not just local pairwise comparisons; rationality of prudenceThe core philosophical argument for REU is what I call global sensitivity. EU theory evaluates gambles by summing the probability-weighted utilities of outcomes independently: each outcome contributes its utility times its probability, and the contributions are simply added. This means the agent's assessment of any one outcome is independent of the rest of the gamble's structure. REU agents, by contrast, are sensitive to how each outcome compares to the full range of possible outcomes in the gamble: the weight given to an outcome depends on where it falls in the rank ordering of the whole distribution. An agent who gives extra weight to worst-case outcomes across the whole distribution — who prefers, as a global policy, to secure good outcomes rather than gamble for better ones — is expressing a coherent attitude toward the shape of their gamble's outcome distribution. This is prudence, and prudence is a rational virtue, not a cognitive error.

iii. the representation theorem — beliefs, desires, and risk-attitudes are separately derivable

Representation theorem — REU represents a unique triple (u, p, r); three independent components of rational agency; not reducible to each otherA critical technical achievement of REU theory is its representation theorem: from an agent's preferences over gambles, one can derive a unique utility function u, a unique probability function p, and a unique risk function r — three distinct and non-reducible components of rational agency. This is philosophically important because it shows that the risk function captures something that neither the utility function nor the probability function captures. An agent with Allais preferences cannot be captured by any EU agent — there is no utility function that generates the Allais pattern. The attempt to explain the Allais preferences by invoking a steeper marginal utility decline from $1m to $5m relative to $0 to $1m fails: the same mathematical pattern cannot be reproduced by any utility function alone without generating absurd implications elsewhere. The risk function is therefore a genuinely new component of rational agency — not a disguised utility function.

iv. against Savage's money pump
