Mr President,

Let me set out the problem with precision, because the opposition will inevitably mischaracterise what we are actually being asked to decide.

Box A: transparent · contains £1,000 · always
Box B: opaque · contains £1,000,000 if Predictor forecast one-box · empty if forecast two-box
Predictor accuracy: stipulated at 99% (or near-perfect)
Your choice: take both boxes, or take only Box B

The opposition will tell you that by the time you make your choice, the boxes are already filled or empty — that your decision cannot reach back in time and change what the Predictor put inside. This is true. And it misses the point entirely.

On what decision theory is actually for

Argument from EDT dominance in expected valueDecision theory is a guide to action. It asks: given my situation, my beliefs, and my values, what should I do? The answer has to be evaluated by what actions are associated with in the world as we actually find it — not in some idealised counterfactual where the Predictor does not exist or is unreliable. When I am considering one-boxing versus two-boxing, the conditional expected value of one-boxing is approximately £1,000,000 and the conditional expected value of two-boxing is approximately £1,000. If you accept that a near-perfect predictor has examined you and made its forecast, then being a one-boxer is almost certainly associated with finding £1,000,000 in Box B. This is not a coincidence to be explained away. It is the central datum.

On the dominance argument — why it fails here

Against the dominance argumentThe opposition will invoke dominance: whatever is in the boxes, you are always £1,000 better off taking both. This is true in a vacuum. But dominance reasoning is only valid when the states of the world it invokes are independent of your decision. Here they are not. The contents of Box B are not independent of your decision — they are correlated with it through the Predictor. When dominance reasoning is applied to cases where the states are decision-relevant evidence, it systematically leads to the wrong answer. This is not a special pleading for this case. It is a recognised theoretical result.

On agents who would two-box

Argument from the type of agent you areHere is a question worth sitting with. Suppose you know the Predictor has already examined you. Two-boxers, by hypothesis, almost certainly find Box B empty. One-boxers almost certainly find it full. If you are the kind of agent who would two-box — if dominance reasoning is so compelling to you that you cannot resist it — then you are almost certainly standing in front of an empty Box B. Your very disposition to two-box is, in this problem, a near-certain indicator that you have lost. The Predictor does not merely predict what you will do in isolation. It predicts what kind of agent you are. And the kind of agent who is swayed by dominance arguments in Newcomb cases is precisely the kind who ends up with £1,000.

On the pre-commitment and agency objection

Argument from rational pre-commitmentSome two-boxers concede that you might wish you had been the kind of person who one-boxes. They say that while it is irrational to one-box at the moment of choice, it would have been rational to pre-commit to one-boxing. But this concession is devastating. If the one-boxing disposition systematically produces better outcomes — and it does, by stipulation — then a decision theory that condemns the very disposition that produces those outcomes is a defective decision theory. An account of rationality that recommends against the strategy that wins is not a rigorous theory. It is a stipulative definition of rationality that has been disconnected from the thing rationality is supposed to serve.

On near-certainty of prediction

Argument from near-certaintyThe standard two-boxer response is to raise the spectre of determinism — that if the Predictor is perfect, your choice was already determined and the problem is incoherent. We need not stipulate a perfect predictor. A 99% accurate predictor is enough. Under evidential decision theory, the expected value of one-boxing is £990,000 + £1,000 = £991,000. The expected value of two-boxing is £10,000 + £990 = £10,990. The one-boxer wins by an enormous margin. The two-boxer's dominance intuition is worth sacrificing far less than that. Rational agents should care about expected value, and one-boxing maximises it.

Take the one box. Walk away with a million. Leave the opposition their thousand pounds and their principles.

Thank you.

Mr President,

My colleague Professor MacAskill has made the case from expected value. I want to make a more philosophically precise case — one that addresses the causal decision theorist on their own ground and shows why the causal approach fails even by its own standards.

On what causal decision theory actually requires

Argument from EDT dominance in expected valueThe causal decision theorist tells us that rational action is determined by the causal consequences of your choice — the difference you make to the world by intervening. Causal decision theory says: evaluate actions by what would happen if you were to do them, not by what your doing them is evidence for. This sounds plausible. But notice what it requires in Newcomb's problem. It requires that you model your choice as causally inert with respect to the contents of Box B, because the Predictor has already acted. You are treating your choice as if it were merely a matter of picking up objects that already exist — as if you were Sherlock Holmes discovering a crime scene, not an agent whose character has predictive power.

The tickle defence

Against the dominance argumentConsider a parallel case: smoking and cancer. The evidential decision theorist might seem to say: don't smoke, because smoking is evidence for lung cancer. But the causal decision theorist objects that smoking doesn't cause cancer via rational deliberation — the correlation is explained by a common genetic cause. The tickle defence says the rational agent's disposition to smoke produces a tickle — a pre-decisional signal — that explains why the agent smokes and independently predicts the cancer. But in Newcomb's problem, this defence is not available. The Predictor reads your deliberative character — your decision-theoretic dispositions — and those dispositions are precisely the subject of rational assessment. There is no tickle that explains the correlation independent of the rationality of the choice. The Predictor predicts you because of how you reason. A theory of rational reasoning that is self-defeating when reasoned through is defective.

On the lesson from Prisoner's Dilemma

Argument from the type of agent you areIn a Prisoner's Dilemma between two committed causal decision theorists, both defect. In a Prisoner's Dilemma between two committed evidential decision theorists who recognise their shared rationality as evidence, both cooperate. The causal decision theorists each get worse outcomes. This is not accidental. Causal decision theory, by refusing to treat the agent's own reasoning disposition as evidence about outcomes, systematically recommends against strategies that would produce better results when deployed among similarly-reasoning agents. Newcomb's problem is the pure case of this general pathology.

On Lewis's objection — the deliberational autonomy argument

Argument from rational pre-commitmentDavid Lewis argues that the one-boxer has confused being the kind of person who one-boxes with the act of one-boxing — that a sophisticated agent should recognise the Predictor has already acted and choose on that basis. But this conflation is precisely what a sophisticated evidential decision theorist endorses, not disavows. The agent's choice is evidence about her type; her type is what the Predictor read. Separating the type from the choice, as Lewis demands, severs the evidential connection that makes one-boxing rational. It is Lewis who has made the error — treating a deliberating agent as if she were already a physical system whose outputs are determined, while simultaneously asking her to deliberate.

On why one-boxing is the correct rational recommendation

Argument from near-certaintyThe right test for a decision theory is: does it recommend actions that systematically produce good outcomes for agents who follow it? One-boxing does. Agents who are committed one-boxers in Newcomb-like situations are predicted to have Box B full. Agents who are committed two-boxers have Box B empty. A decision theory that recommends two-boxing has failed its own basic test. It has produced a procedure for decision that, when generalised and followed, leads to worse outcomes. This is not just an ad hoc complaint about Newcomb. It is a structural critique of causal decision theory's treatment of cases where the agent's deliberative character is part of the causal-evidential structure of the problem.

The Predictor is watching. It has already read your rational character. Reason well.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to begin with an observation that I hope will unsettle even those who came here convinced by the proposition. The problem is extraordinarily difficult. I know of no other problem in philosophy that has led so many intelligent people to defend positions they themselves acknowledge to be strange. That should make us suspicious of any confident prescription — including the proposition's.

I want to defend two-boxing. Not because I am certain it is right — I have famously said I am not — but because the arguments for it are, on reflection, the more powerful ones, and because the case for one-boxing rests on a fundamental confusion about what rational action is.

On the distinction between making and finding

Argument from causal dominanceWhen you stand before the boxes, the Predictor has already acted. Box B either contains the million or it does not. Your choice cannot reach backward in time and change that. You are not making the contents of Box B. You are finding them. The only question before you is: given that Box B contains some fixed amount — a million or nothing — would you rather also have a thousand pounds or not? The answer is obvious. You would rather have the thousand. This is not insensitivity to what the Predictor has done. It is clarity about the structure of your actual choice: you are choosing between one act and another, given a fixed state of the world.

On the evidential decision theorist's mistake

Against the evidential argumentThe proposition tells you that one-boxing is associated with finding a million pounds. This is true. They then say you should act to maximise what your action is associated with. But this conflates two entirely different things: acting to cause good outcomes and acting to be the kind of agent who is associated with good outcomes. A patient with a genetic marker for cancer who learns that chemotherapy is negatively correlated with the marker — perhaps because only sick people take it — does not thereby have reason to refuse chemotherapy. The correlation between refusing chemotherapy and being healthy is evidence about her prognosis, not evidence that refusing chemotherapy will improve it. The one-boxer makes precisely this error: she confuses what her action is evidence for with what her action causes.

On the dominance argument reconsidered

Argument from dominanceThe proposition claims that dominance reasoning fails here because the states are not independent of the choice. But consider: is independence the right criterion? The point of the dominance argument is that whatever state obtains — million in Box B or not — you are better off taking both boxes. This is not a claim about causal independence. It is a claim about the relationship between states and payoffs. The states — million present, million absent — are fully realised before you choose. Your choice cannot alter them. In that sense, the relevant independence holds: the states are fixed, your choice selects between payoffs given those fixed states. Dominance applies.

On the confusion between disposition and act

Argument from the separability of disposition and actThe proposition has argued that being a one-boxer is better than being a two-boxer — and this is true. If the Predictor predicts based on type, then one-boxing dispositions are associated with greater riches. But notice what follows. We should all try to cultivate the one-boxing disposition. We should perhaps even pre-commit. But at the moment of choice, you cannot change your disposition — the Predictor has already read it. The disposition is settled. The question is only what to do, given the disposition you have. And given that your disposition is settled — whatever it is — you should take both boxes. This is not a defect of decision theory. It is the recognition that disposition and act are separable, and that two-boxing is always the better act given any fixed disposition.

On the asymmetry of regret

Argument from asymmetry of regretHere is a test. Suppose you one-box and find Box B empty — the Predictor made an error, or you are in the 1% of cases. You walk away with nothing, when you could have had £1,000. Suppose instead you two-box and find Box B full — again, a predictor error. You walk away with £1,001,000 when you could have had £1,000,000. The asymmetry: the two-boxer's worst case is finding an extra thousand. The one-boxer's worst case is finding nothing at all. The causal decision theorist can survive any outcome; she has done the causally right thing. The one-boxer who is unlucky has been led by her decision theory into a strategy of ignoring a thousand pounds on the table. No amount of post-hoc expected value argument softens that reality.

Take both boxes. If the Predictor got it right, you were always going to. If it got it wrong, you have a bonus million pounds and a clear philosophical conscience.

Thank you.

Mr President,

I want to approach this problem rather differently from Professor Nozick — not from the outside in, through the lens of agency theory, but from the inside out, through a rigorous examination of what decision theory should actually be trying to do.

I am, as is well known, an evidentialist about many things. My decision theory is itself cast in evidential terms. But in Newcomb's problem, the correct evidential analysis leads to two-boxing, not one-boxing. The proposition has mistaken which evidence the evidential decision theorist should be tracking.

On the news value of actions

Argument from causal dominanceIn my framework, the value of an action is its news value — what the action, if performed, is evidence for about the state of the world. The proposition says: one-boxing is evidence you are a one-boxer, being a one-boxer is evidence Box B is full, so one-boxing is good news. But this analysis is too quick. At the moment you deliberate, you already know you are deliberating. You know something the Predictor was trying to predict. The deliberating agent who knows she is deliberating receives no new news about her type from her eventual choice — she already knows her type, or should. The action cannot update your beliefs about Box B's contents if you already have all the relevant information about what kind of decision-maker you are.

On deliberation crowding out prediction

Argument from deliberational autonomyHere is the key point. The very act of rational deliberation — genuinely weighing your options — is evidence that you have not yet committed to either choice. An agent who has genuinely not yet decided is not the kind of agent the Predictor has modelled as a pure one-boxer or pure two-boxer. She is an undecided agent. Insofar as the deliberating agent takes her deliberation seriously, she cannot simultaneously regard herself as the kind of determined-one-boxer that the Predictor relied upon when filling Box B. Genuine rational deliberation and the Predictor's model are in tension. The attempt to deliberate has already, in a sense, undermined the evidential connection that the proposition relies upon.

On why the causal interpretation is correct

Argument from dominanceThe correct resolution is to recognise that rational action must be indexed to what the agent can control — the causal upshot of her choice. You control whether to lift Box A. You do not control what was placed in Box B. A decision theory that asks you to optimise over what you can control is not betraying rationality — it is respecting the real structure of agency. The proposition treats the agent as if she were optimising over a class of possible worlds that includes worlds where she chose differently and the Predictor predicted accordingly. But those worlds are not available to her by choice. Her choice is a local act in a world where Box B is already filled or empty. She controls only the act, not the state.

On the symmetry of the problem for a genuine deliberator

Argument from the separability of disposition and actThe proposition assumes the deliberating agent can simply decide to become a one-boxer. But if you are genuinely uncertain about what to do — if you are here, in this debate, weighing the arguments — then you are not the pure one-boxing type the Predictor relies upon for its prediction. The problem as stated presupposes that the Predictor has already correctly identified you as one or the other. If you are genuinely deliberating, the problem's setup already assigns you to the borderline case where prediction is least reliable. The very fact of your genuine deliberation is evidence that the Predictor's forecast is in doubt — and under doubt, you should take both boxes.

On the deeper lesson

Argument from asymmetry of regretNewcomb's problem is extraordinarily illuminating precisely because it reveals a tension internal to the idea of rational agency. The tension is not, as the proposition suggests, a refutation of causal decision theory. It is a demonstration that problems can be constructed in which the agent's own nature is implicated in the outcome structure in ways that no decision theory can fully resolve. Both theories handle the problem imperfectly. CDT handles it better because it does not ask the agent to perform the impossible act of treating herself as an external observer of her own deliberations. EDT, applied naively, requires the agent to regard her choice as news about herself — which she cannot receive, having been on the inside all along.

The boxes are already set. The deliberation is already happening. Take both.

Thank you.
