Mr President,

A member of my family is a cleaner in Lancashire. She pays a higher tax rate on her salary than her landlord pays on the growing value of the home she lives in. She slogs her guts out, he puts in far less effort — and the state rewards him more than her. That single image contains, I think, most of what is wrong with the British tax system, and most of what I want to say today.

On what capital gains actually are — and what they are not

Most capital gains are not entrepreneurial — they are passive, socially generated, and structurally undertaxedBefore we debate the rate, we need to be clear about what we are actually taxing. The opposition will present capital gains as the hard-won reward of the entrepreneur who risked everything and built something new. Some gains are exactly that. But the great bulk of capital gains in Britain — especially among the wealthiest households — arise not from Schumpeterian innovation but from passive asset inflation. A house in London that has trebled in value did not do so because the owner worked harder. It did so because of planning restrictions engineered by existing homeowners, public infrastructure investment funded by taxpayers, population growth that nobody chose, and a monetary policy that suppressed interest rates for a decade and a half. When a landlord pockets a gain of £400,000 on a property held for ten years, most of that gain was generated collectively — by the society that built the tube line, the school, the hospital that made the area desirable. The current CGT regime allows that collectively generated value to be captured privately and taxed at half the rate of a nurse's overtime. That is not a reward for risk-taking. It is a subsidy for owning.

On neutrality and the distortion of incentives

Tax neutrality: taxing identical economic gains differently distorts behaviour away from productivity and into tax engineeringOne of the central principles of good tax design is neutrality — the state should not distort economic decisions between activities that are economically equivalent. Yet Britain's current system is the opposite of neutral. Two people each receive £100,000 in economic gain in the same year — one through wages, one through a rise in asset value. We tax them at rates that can differ by fifteen to twenty percentage points. That difference creates a powerful incentive not to produce more value, but to reclassify how existing value is taken. Lawyers, consultants, fund managers, private equity partners, and owner-managers of small companies have all developed sophisticated mechanisms for converting what is economically a salary into what is legally a capital gain. This does not create a pound more of wealth. It simply moves income from one column of a tax return to another, at enormous cost in legal fees, and at enormous cost to the Exchequer. The proposal before this House is not a radical idea. It is the application to capital of the most basic principle of sound tax design: if two gains are economically the same, they should be taxed the same.

On the cleaner and the landlord — the labour market argument

Labour market legitimacy: when work is taxed more heavily than passive ownership, it corrodes the social contract and the incentive to workCritics of the proposal often discuss it in the abstract language of economics — marginal rates, elasticities, Chamley-Judd theorems. I want to bring it back to the concrete. We are living through a period of profound popular disillusionment with market economics. People — particularly younger people — have watched a system in which wages have stagnated, housing has become unaffordable, and the gap between those who own assets and those who do not has widened dramatically. They are not wrong to be angry. A tax system that institutionalises the principle that passive ownership should be more lightly taxed than active labour is not merely inefficient — it is a statement about whose contributions the state values. It says: work less, own more. It says: the nurse who works nights is less deserving of reward than the landlord who does nothing. Every time a young person looks at the ratio of what they earn to what they would need to save to buy a house — and gives up — part of the explanation is a tax system that has systematically subsidised asset ownership relative to earned income for forty years.

On the revenue — fiscal sustainability and what £12 billion can do

Revenue: £12bn/year is fiscally significant; further increases in labour taxation would be more economically damagingThe opposition will make much of the behavioural response — the claim that investors will flee, shares will be dumped, capital will evaporate. I will address these claims directly. But first I want to register what is at stake fiscally. An alignment of CGT with income tax rates is estimated to raise in the region of twelve billion pounds per year. Britain faces acute pressures from an ageing population, NHS demand, defence obligations, and weak underlying growth. The question is not whether revenue needs to be raised — it is where. The alternatives are further increases in National Insurance (which falls on labour, reinforcing the very distortion we are trying to correct), increases in VAT (which is regressive), or reductions in public services that disproportionately benefit those without private wealth. Against that menu of options, taxing passive gains at the same rate as earned income is not merely the fairest option. It is almost certainly the least economically damaging one, precisely because it falls on returns that are less sensitive to taxation than labour supply.

On the behavioural response — will investors flee?

Behavioural response overstated: investment decisions are driven by fundamentals, not CGT rates; pensions are largely unaffected; markets clear at new equilibriumThe opposition will predict economic catastrophe. Let me meet those predictions with evidence. First: pension funds. The claim that equalising CGT will devastate pensions is economically illiterate. Pension funds — whether defined contribution or defined benefit — are not subject to capital gains tax on their internal transactions. The CGT change affects taxable investors outside pension wrappers. Pension values are driven by long-run real returns on equities and bonds, not by the CGT rates faced by retail investors. Second: the capital flight argument. The UK stock market is overwhelmingly owned by institutional and international investors — sovereign wealth funds, US pension funds, ETFs — none of whom are primarily driven by UK CGT. For every domestic seller, there is an international buyer indifferent to British personal tax rates. Third: the "shares will be dumped" scenario contains an internal contradiction. Lower prices raise expected returns and attract buyers. Markets clear. The adjustment is a repricing, not a destruction of underlying value. And fourth: most comparable economies tax capital gains at lower rates than income — but at rates still well above zero, and the claim that UK rates are currently uniquely restrictive does not survive comparison with France's flat tax regime or Germany's 25% rate on financial gains.

On the Adam Smith argument — taxing rent, not productive profit

Classical economics: Smith and Ricardo distinguished productive profit from economic rent — CGT on passive gains taxes rent, which is economically efficientThe classical liberal tradition in economics — from Adam Smith through Ricardo to Henry George — drew a sharp distinction between two types of return on assets. Productive profit is the return generated by genuine enterprise — invention, risk-taking, organising productive resources. Economic rent is the return generated by the mere ownership of scarce assets — land, spectrum, monopoly positions — that appreciate regardless of any activity by the owner. Economists since Smith have argued that taxes on rent are the most economically efficient taxes because they do not deter the activity that generates the value in the first place. Land does not move abroad because you tax it. A London house does not stop being a house because capital gains on it are taxed more heavily. The value generated by planning scarcity and population growth does not disappear. What changes is who captures it: the private owner, or the public that collectively generated it. Aligning CGT with income tax does not tax productive entrepreneurs equally with passive renters — it taxes them at the same headline rate, but the economic burden falls most heavily on those whose gains are most rent-like, precisely where economic theory says taxation is most efficient.

On why this is pro-growth, pro-entrepreneur, and pro-work

Pro-growth reframing: CGT alignment redirects capital from passive speculation toward productive investment — net positive for the economyLet me anticipate and rebut the most common framing error in this debate. The opposition will say: "You are taxing enterprise. You are punishing risk-taking. You are driving entrepreneurs away." But this framing conflates the headline rate with the economic impact. The proposal before this House is a rate alignment, not the abolition of reliefs for genuine entrepreneurial activity. Business asset disposal relief, investor reliefs, rollover provisions — these can be preserved and targeted at precisely the kind of risk-bearing productive investment that genuinely deserves support. What is being removed is the blanket preferential treatment that currently applies equally to a founder who built a business from nothing and a landlord who bought a flat and watched its value rise with the tide. More fundamentally: a tax system that channels capital into property speculation and away from productive business investment is not pro-growth. It is pro-asset inflation. Britain has spent forty years building an economy dependent on rising house prices as a substitute for productivity growth. The young cannot afford homes, business investment lags our peers, and real wages have stagnated. Removing the structural tax incentive to speculate in assets rather than invest in businesses is a rebalancing that economic theory and empirical evidence both recommend.

Mr President,

I want to provide the empirical and theoretical underpinning for what my colleague Streeting has argued. My work with Gabriel Zucman over many years has attempted to do something that the policy debate on capital taxation often does not: to measure what is actually happening, rather than to model what theory says should happen. When you look at the data carefully, the case for preferential capital gains taxation collapses — not merely as a matter of fairness but as a matter of economic analysis.

On what the data actually show — the effective tax rates of the wealthy

Empirical finding: the effective tax rate of billionaires is lower than that of middle-class workers once unrealised gains are includedThe most important empirical finding from our work is this: when you compute the effective tax rate of the very wealthy — not their statutory marginal rate, but the actual share of their total economic income paid in tax — it is substantially lower than the effective rate paid by middle-income workers. This is not because they pay lower rates on wages. It is because most of their income does not take the form of wages at all. It takes the form of unrealised capital appreciation — gains that accumulate year after year without ever being taxed until realisation. A hedge fund manager, a property developer, a tech founder whose stake appreciates by £50 million in a year pays no tax on that appreciation in the year it occurs, while the nurse working double shifts pays income tax and National Insurance on every pound. The preferential CGT rate compounds this: when the wealthy eventually do realise gains, they face rates fifteen to twenty points below those faced on labour income. The result is a tax system that is, across the full income distribution, barely progressive at all — and may actually be regressive at the very top.

On the Chamley-Judd theorem — why it does not settle the argument

Chamley-Judd theorem: the theoretical case for zero capital income tax rests on assumptions that do not hold in the real worldThe opposition will invoke the Chamley-Judd theorem — the celebrated result in optimal tax theory which holds that in the long run the optimal tax rate on capital income is zero. This is, in my view, the most commonly misused result in public finance economics. The Chamley-Judd theorem holds only under a very specific set of assumptions: a representative agent model with infinitely lived households, perfect capital markets, and a fixed labour supply. None of these assumptions holds in the real world. In reality, households are heterogeneous — some are hand-to-mouth workers, others are wealthy capitalists — and the distributional consequences of capital taxation matter. The theorem also assumes that capital income taxation and labour income taxation are the only policy instruments available, which ignores the role of public investment, education, and redistribution in shaping the long-run capital stock. When you relax these assumptions and work with realistic models of heterogeneous agents, the optimal tax rate on capital income is not zero — it is positive, often substantial, and depends heavily on the degree of wealth concentration. The Chamley-Judd theorem is a mathematical result of great elegance; it is not a description of the British tax system's policy options.

On income shifting — the tax arbitrage that CGT differentials create

Income shifting: large CGT differentials drive reclassification of labour income as capital income — reducing the revenue and fairness of both taxesOne of the most important — and most underappreciated — distortions created by large CGT differentials is income shifting. In the United States, Saez, Slemrod, and Giertz have documented extensively how high-income individuals respond to tax rate differentials not primarily by working less or investing less, but by reclassifying income. In Britain, the structure of small company taxation creates a powerful incentive for high-income owner-managers to pay themselves through dividends and capital distributions rather than salaries, shifting income from the income tax base to the CGT base. The private equity industry has taken this to an extreme through carried interest arrangements, whereby fund managers take what is economically a performance fee — a payment for services rendered — but structure it as a capital gain, taxable at CGT rates rather than income tax rates. This is not innovation. It is rent extraction from the tax code. The revenue cost of this arbitrage is substantial; the distributional cost is that it overwhelmingly benefits the highest earners; and the economic cost is the misallocation of sophisticated professional talent into tax engineering rather than productive activity.

On the international evidence — what actually happens when CGT rates change

International evidence: CGT rate changes have modest effects on investment; the capital flight narrative is not supported empiricallyThe opposition's behavioural argument — that higher CGT rates will devastate investment — is an empirical claim, and it should be evaluated as such. The empirical literature on the elasticity of capital gains realisations with respect to tax rates is extensive and, on balance, does not support the catastrophist narrative. Several robust findings stand out. First: investment decisions are overwhelmingly driven by expected pre-tax returns, interest rates, and macroeconomic conditions — not marginal CGT rates. The correlation between CGT rates and business investment across developed economies is weak. Second: the United States raised its capital gains tax rate significantly in 1986 as part of the Tax Reform Act — aligning it with ordinary income — and business investment did not collapse; indeed, the late 1980s and 1990s saw a sustained investment and productivity boom. Third: the short-run realisation elasticity of capital gains — how much people delay or accelerate sales when rates change — is larger than the long-run elasticity, meaning that the revenue impact of rate changes is more muted over time than initial behavioural responses suggest. Fourth: Denmark, which taxes capital gains at rates comparable to income tax, maintains robust capital markets, strong business investment, and a vibrant startup ecosystem. The empirical record does not validate the opposition's predictions.

On the lock-in effect — the genuine concern and how to address it

Lock-in effect is real but manageable: it argues for careful design, not against alignment — indexation, rollover relief, and graduated implementation address itI want to acknowledge the one objection from the opposition that has genuine empirical substance: the lock-in effect. When capital gains tax rates are high, investors have a strong incentive to defer realisation — to hold assets rather than sell, even when selling would be economically rational — because crystallising gains means triggering a large tax liability. This reduces capital mobility and can allocate capital inefficiently. I take this seriously. But the correct policy response to lock-in is not to maintain a large structural differential between CGT and income tax rates — it is to design the CGT regime carefully. Specifically: indexing the cost basis for inflation eliminates the taxation of nominal rather than real gains, which is the largest source of genuinely unfair lock-in. Rollover relief for reinvestment into qualifying business assets preserves the incentive for capital reallocation without triggering the tax. Graduated rates for longer holding periods can be combined with alignment at the top. These design features can eliminate the most economically harmful lock-in effects while maintaining the principle that a pound of gain should not be taxed materially less than a pound of wages.

On unrealised gains — the accruals question and its implications

Accruals taxation: the real reform required goes beyond rate alignment — unrealised gains are the largest source of permanent tax deferral for the wealthyMy colleague Streeting has argued for aligning rates at realisation. I want to raise a more fundamental question that the proposition's position implies even if it does not fully articulate it: why do we tax capital gains only on realisation? The answer is largely administrative — measuring unrealised gains continuously is difficult and liquidity-constraining. But the consequence is substantial: the very wealthy can defer tax indefinitely by simply not selling, allowing gains to compound on a pre-tax basis for decades. In extreme cases — assets passed on at death where the cost basis is stepped up — the gain is never taxed at all. The effective tax rate on capital gains for the wealthiest is therefore not the headline CGT rate but something much lower, because of the time value of indefinite deferral. Rate alignment at realisation is a necessary first step. But a complete reform of capital income taxation would also need to address deferral through annual mark-to-market taxation on liquid assets, a modified accruals approach, or at minimum the abolition of the CGT-free step-up at death. These are the tools that would make the effective tax rate on capital genuinely comparable to the effective tax rate on labour.

On what the revenue would do — the growth argument runs both ways

Revenue use matters: £12bn invested in education, infrastructure, and R&D raises long-run growth more than CGT preferences that finance property speculationLet me end with the argument that the opposition most consistently ignores: what happens to the revenue. The debate has been framed as "CGT alignment versus growth." But that framing assumes that the alternative to CGT alignment is no taxation at all — that the £12 billion annual yield simply vanishes from the economy. Of course it does not. If the revenue from CGT alignment is used to fund education, productive infrastructure, R&D, or deficit reduction, the long-run growth impact can be positive even on the opposition's own theoretical terms. The new growth theory — Romer, Aghion, Jones — emphasises that growth is driven by investment in human capital, ideas, and productive infrastructure. A tax system that extracts revenue from passive asset appreciation and redirects it toward these inputs raises the long-run growth rate relative to a system that leaves those gains untaxed and channels capital into property speculation. The choice is not between taxing capital and growing the economy. It is between taxing productive labour and passive rents — and sound economics, backed by the empirical record, says the latter is both fairer and better for long-run growth.

Mr President,

The proposition has made an argument that sounds like common sense: a pound is a pound, and it should be taxed at the same rate wherever it comes from. I want to argue that this is precisely the kind of intuition that sounds most compelling when it is most wrong. The reason we tax capital gains differently from labour income is not a historical accident or a gift to the wealthy — it is a deliberate response to a genuine and deep problem in the design of an income tax system. When you understand what that problem is, the proposition's case dissolves.

On the double taxation of capital income

Double taxation: capital gains are the fruit of already-taxed income; taxing them again at full income tax rates is a compounding tax on savingHere is the fundamental problem. Capital gains arise from assets that were purchased with income that has already been taxed. When I earn a salary, I pay income tax on it. What remains is after-tax income. If I choose to save that after-tax income rather than spend it, and if those savings generate a return — through interest, dividends, or capital appreciation — that return will be taxed again, under the proposition's scheme, at full income tax rates. The man who earns a pound and spends it pays income tax once. The man who earns a pound and saves it, watches it grow, and then spends it pays income tax twice — once on the original earning and once on the return to saving. This is not neutrality between consumption and saving; it is a systematic penalty on the decision to defer consumption. And since saving generates the capital stock that funds investment, wages, and productivity growth, taxing it at full rates is not merely unfair to savers — it is a tax on the mechanism by which economies grow. The compound effect of double taxation over a lifetime of saving is not a minor distortion. At a forty percent rate applied to gains from already-taxed income, the lifetime tax rate on saved income substantially exceeds the lifetime rate on income consumed immediately.

On inter-temporal neutrality and the savings decision

Inter-temporal neutrality: a neutral tax system should not distort the choice between present and future consumption — income tax on capital returns violates thisThe principle of inter-temporal neutrality — a cornerstone of modern public finance theory — says that a well-designed tax system should not distort the choice between consuming income now and saving it to consume later. If I earn £100 after tax and choose to spend it immediately, I get £100 of consumption. If I choose to save it for ten years at a five percent return, I should — under a neutral tax system — get roughly the future-value equivalent of £100 of consumption in real terms. But if the returns to saving are taxed at full income tax rates, the future value of my saved pound is eroded relative to the immediately spent pound. This creates a systematic bias toward present consumption — an implicit penalty on patience, thrift, and long-term investment. From a purely economic efficiency perspective, this is a significant distortion. A consumption tax — or equivalently, an income tax with full exemption for the normal return to capital — would eliminate this distortion. The preferential CGT rate is a partial, imperfect, but genuine attempt to reduce the over-taxation of saving that a full income tax on capital returns implies.

On the Chamley-Judd theorem and the theory of optimal capital taxation

Chamley-Judd: in a growing economy, the long-run optimal tax on capital income is zero — taxing capital compounds the tax on labour into the infinite futureMy colleague Saez dismisses the Chamley-Judd theorem as inapplicable to the real world. But he dismisses it too quickly. The core intuition of Chamley and Judd is not merely a mathematical curiosity. It is this: because capital accumulates over time, a tax on the return to capital is not simply a tax on today's capital income — it is a tax on every future period of income that today's capital would have generated. In that sense, a positive tax on capital income in period one implies an ever-increasing effective tax on labour income saved in period one, compounding into the future. As the horizon extends, this compound tax converges to a tax rate that approaches infinity on the original saved labour income. In a growing economy with patient agents, that is a profound distortion. The theorem says: in the long run, you should not tax the return to saving at all, because doing so taxes labour income saved for the future at much higher effective rates than labour income consumed immediately. The result does not depend only on representative-agent assumptions — it extends to many heterogeneous models. My colleague's dismissal of it misrepresents the technical literature.

On dynamic scoring — the long-run growth cost the proposition ignores

Dynamic scoring: static revenue estimates ignore the long-run reduction in the capital stock and wage growth that higher capital taxation impliesThe proposition cites a revenue estimate of twelve billion pounds per year. This is a static estimate — it assumes that the tax change does not alter the behaviour of investors, savers, and entrepreneurs in ways that affect the tax base. But in the long run, a higher tax on capital returns will reduce the after-tax return to saving and investment. This reduces the supply of saving and therefore the capital stock. A smaller capital stock means less productive investment, lower labour productivity, and lower wages — for the very workers whose interests the proposition claims to champion. The Congressional Budget Office in the United States, the Office for Budget Responsibility in the UK, and the empirical macroeconomics literature all document that dynamic effects substantially erode — and sometimes reverse — the static revenue gains from capital income tax increases. The proposition tells us that aligning CGT with income tax is good for workers. Dynamic scoring suggests the opposite: in the long run, workers' wages are lower in an economy with a smaller capital stock, whatever the headline tax rate on capital gains says.

On the cost of capital for small businesses and entrepreneurs

Cost of capital: higher CGT raises required returns for angel investors and early-stage funders, disproportionately affecting SMEs and startups that lack access to institutional capitalThe proposition asserts that genuine entrepreneurial activity can be protected through targeted reliefs, and that only passive gains will be affected by alignment. I want to question this confidence. The venture and angel investment ecosystem — the primary source of early-stage risk capital for startups and small businesses — depends on investors expecting substantial capital gains from a small number of successful bets. Angel investors typically receive no salary; their return is entirely in the form of capital appreciation on the equity stakes they acquire. If that appreciation is taxed at forty or forty-five percent rather than twenty or twenty-four, the required pre-tax return goes up — and more potential investments become unattractive. The proposition says: preserve the reliefs for true entrepreneurs. But which reliefs, exactly? Business Asset Disposal Relief is capped at a lifetime limit of one million pounds of gains — meaningless for a growing technology company. Rollover relief applies only to qualifying reinvestment. The residual exposure for angel investors and seed-stage venture funders — the people who provide risk capital before institutional investors arrive — is substantial. These are precisely the investors who cannot be replaced by pension funds and sovereign wealth funds, because those investors require audited accounts, proven track records, and institutional-grade governance that startups cannot provide.

On the asset repricing transmission mechanism — pensions, stability, and the financial system

Repricing transmission: even if pensions are CGT-exempt, mark-to-market pension valuations fall when underlying asset prices reprice — the wealth effect is realThe proposition makes much of the fact that pension funds are not directly subject to CGT. This is technically true in its narrow form but misses the transmission mechanism. Consider a defined contribution pension fund whose portfolio is marked to market. If higher CGT causes domestic equity prices to reprice downward — as the required pre-tax return for taxable investors increases — the value of that pension fund's equity holdings falls on its balance sheet, even though the fund itself does not pay CGT. The pensioner who checks their pot has less money. This is not a nominal or illusory effect; it is a genuine wealth effect on retirement savings. The proposition dismisses this as "catastrophising." But the financial crisis showed precisely how interconnected asset values and household balance sheets are — even through institutional intermediaries. I am not predicting a crisis. I am pointing out that the transmission mechanism the proposition denies is not hypothetical — it is the mechanism by which monetary policy works. Central banks raise asset prices to make people wealthier. The proposition says that reducing asset prices through tax changes has no wealth effect. You cannot have it both ways.

On what the data actually show about wealth taxation and growth

Wealth taxation and growth: the cross-country evidence on high capital tax rates and long-run growth is more negative than the proposition suggestsMy colleague Saez cites Denmark as a model. Denmark taxes capital gains comparably to income — and has strong capital markets and a startup ecosystem. This is true. But Denmark also has an exceptionally high rate of household saving, strong institutional capital, deep labour market flexibility, and a social contract that has produced near-universal trust in public institutions. Comparing Britain's tax system to Denmark's without comparing the institutional contexts is cherry-picking. More relevant comparisons might ask: what happened to French investment after the 2012 introduction of higher taxes on financial capital under Hollande? What happened to Swedish capital markets after the introduction of high wealth taxes in the 1970s and 1980s, which ultimately had to be abolished because of capital flight and investment decline? The international evidence is not uniformly in the proposition's favour. The cases where high capital taxation has worked are cases where the surrounding institutional environment — quality of public spending, labour market institutions, trust in government — made taxation and investment compatible. Britain's current institutional environment does not necessarily replicate those conditions.

I — Double taxation: capital gains are the fruit of already-taxed income; a second full tax on them is a compound tax on saving

1.Inter-temporal neutrality — a core principle of optimal tax design — requires that the tax system not distort the choice between consuming income now and saving it to consume later.
2.Income tax on the return to capital systematically biases the choice toward present consumption — the future value of saved income is eroded relative to immediately spent income.

2.The negative examples — France post-2012 financial capital tax, Sweden's wealth tax experiment (ultimately abolished) — suggest that high capital taxation can cause investment decline and capital flight where institutional conditions are unfavourable.
3.Britain's current institutional environment — low household saving, weak productivity growth, structural reliance on foreign capital inflows, and declining trust in public institutions — more closely resembles the unfavourable conditions than the favourable ones.

Mr President,

My colleague Mankiw has made the theoretical case against CGT alignment with characteristic rigour. I want to make the practical case — the case from the ground up, from the perspective of how risk capital actually works, how startups actually get funded, and what actually happens to capital markets when the tax treatment of gains changes. The proposition's argument treats capital as a homogeneous thing: a pound is a pound, a gain is a gain. But capital is not homogeneous. Risk is not homogeneous. And a tax system that fails to distinguish patient, risk-bearing capital from passive asset-holding does real damage to the parts of the economy that most need to grow.

On risk, return, and the economics of capital gains

Risk premium: capital gains reflect compensation for risk-bearing; taxing them at income tax rates on gross gains understates the true burden on net risk-adjusted returnsCapital gains are not simply income in a different form. They are, in most cases, the realised outcome of investments that carried substantial risk of total loss. When an angel investor puts £100,000 into a startup, they face a real probability — historically around seventy percent for early-stage investments — that the money is simply lost. The gains that accrue from the thirty percent of investments that succeed are not pure profit; they are compensation for the losses on the seventy percent that failed. From an economic perspective, the relevant comparison is not the gross gain on the successful investment but the risk-adjusted net return across the entire portfolio. A higher tax rate on gains — without any corresponding relief on losses that cannot be offset — taxes the compensation for risk-bearing at full income tax rates, while the losses are only partially deductible. The asymmetric tax treatment of gains and losses means that aligning CGT with income tax increases the effective tax burden on risk-bearing by more than the rate alignment alone implies. This is the systematic underpinning for a lower rate on gains: it is a rough-and-ready correction for the asymmetric tax treatment of risk.

On the venture capital and startup ecosystem

Venture ecosystem: the UK's startup economy depends on a pipeline of private capital that is CGT-sensitive; alignment would raise the cost of early-stage risk capitalThe United Kingdom has, over the past two decades, developed one of the most vibrant startup ecosystems in the world — arguably the best in Europe. London is home to more unicorn companies than Paris, Berlin, and Amsterdam combined. This did not happen by accident. It happened because of a combination of factors including talent, language, legal infrastructure, and — critically — a tax environment that made it attractive for private individuals to take risk on early-stage companies. The Enterprise Investment Scheme, the Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme, and Business Asset Disposal Relief collectively provide CGT advantages that make the expected after-tax return from angel and venture investing materially higher in the UK than in comparable European jurisdictions. If CGT is aligned with income tax, the expected after-tax returns from startup investment fall. Some investors will continue to invest; many will divert capital to tax-advantaged pension assets, real estate, or overseas vehicles. The proposition tells us this effect will be small. I want to ask: on what basis? The entire logic of tax-advantaged savings schemes — ISAs, pensions, EIS — is that tax treatment materially affects where people put money. You cannot simultaneously argue that preferential tax treatment is essential for ISAs and pension savings, and then argue that preferential treatment for startup investment has no effect on the supply of startup capital.

On the asset repricing mechanism — what actually happens to prices

Asset repricing: higher CGT increases the required pre-tax return for domestic taxable investors, causing a one-off downward repricing with genuine wealth effectsThe proposition says that if assets are repriced downward, buyers step in and the market clears. This is true — in equilibrium. But the transition from one equilibrium to another involves real people experiencing real losses on assets they own today at current prices. A homeowner who planned to sell their rental property to fund retirement faces a materially different calculation under CGT alignment: the after-tax proceeds are substantially lower. They have three choices: sell before the rate change and crystallise gains at the old rate; hold the property and defer the gain indefinitely; or sell at a lower gross price to achieve the same net return. All three of these responses have economic consequences. Mass realisation before a rate change creates a brief surge in asset supply and a price crash. Indefinite deferral creates the very lock-in effect that even the proposition acknowledges is harmful. And the third response — selling at a lower gross price — is precisely the asset repricing that reduces the real wealth of sellers. This is not catastrophe. It is a material and real economic adjustment that falls on people who made investment decisions under the assumption of one tax regime and will exit under another. The transition is not cost-free, and the proposition should not pretend that it is.

On the cost of capital for UK plc — new issuance and the structural discount argument

Cost of capital for UK firms: higher CGT raises required returns for some investor classes, affecting the pricing of new equity issuance in the medium termThe proposition argues that the cost of capital for UK firms is not materially affected by CGT, because the marginal investor is an international institution indifferent to UK retail CGT rates. I want to accept this argument in part while questioning its completeness. For large-cap UK equities listed on the FTSE 100 — owned overwhelmingly by global institutional investors — this is largely correct. For mid-cap and small-cap equities — owned in substantial part by domestic wealth managers, high-net-worth individuals, and family offices — it is less clearly correct. For private equity and venture-backed companies — whose investors are primarily domestic taxable individuals — it is quite wrong. The UK's innovation economy is not primarily driven by FTSE 100 companies. It is driven by the mid-market, the growth companies, the spinouts from universities, and the startups that will be the FTSE 100 of 2050. These are precisely the companies for whom domestic private capital — CGT-sensitive capital — is the relevant funding source. Raising CGT to income tax levels does not leave the cost of capital for these companies unchanged. It raises it, potentially significantly, at exactly the stage in their development where access to risk capital is most critical.

On the proposition's conflation of passive and productive gains

Conflation of passive and productive gains: the policy instrument of headline CGT rate is too blunt to achieve the proposition's stated aim of taxing only passive rentThe proposition says it wants to tax passive rent — the landlord's unearned gain — while protecting the entrepreneur's earned gain through targeted reliefs. I want to press on the practical feasibility of this distinction. In the real world, the economic boundary between rent and productive return is extremely blurry. A property developer who takes genuine development risk — identifying sites, managing planning, funding construction, managing contractors — generates gains that are partly rent (from underlying land appreciation) and partly productive return (from their enterprise and risk-taking). A founder-owner whose company has appreciated partly because of their innovation and partly because of the bull market in tech valuations faces the same ambiguity. The current system uses a blunt instrument — a lower rate on all capital gains — to provide a partial correction for these mixed cases. The proposition's alternative — a high rate with targeted exemptions — requires the tax authority to make the very rent-versus-enterprise distinction that even economists find difficult to draw. In practice, well-advised wealthy investors will qualify for exemptions; the less sophisticated will not. That is likely to be more distortionary and less fair, not less.

On the patient capital problem and Britain's investment gap

Patient capital: Britain's investment gap is not caused by preferential CGT — it is caused by pension fund short-termism, institutional risk aversion, and capital markets biased toward income over growthThe proposition argues that Britain's chronic underinvestment in productive business is exacerbated by the CGT differential. I want to challenge this causal story. Britain's investment gap — its chronic underinvestment in R&D, manufacturing, and business capital relative to peers — has multiple causes, none of which is primarily the CGT rate. They include: the structure of British pension fund investment, which has historically underweighted private equity and domestic growth capital; the dominance of short-term institutional investors who prioritise dividends and buybacks over retained earnings and R&D; planning restrictions that constrain productive development; and the structure of bank lending, which overwhelmingly favours property over business investment. The CGT differential is not the cause of this investment gap, and removing it will not close it. The proposition has identified a genuine problem — underinvestment in productive business — and proposed a solution — CGT alignment — that does not address the actual structural causes. That is at best an ineffective policy and at worst a policy that makes the venture and angel capital ecosystem worse while leaving the root causes of underinvestment intact.

On the carried interest argument — conceding the specific while defending the general

Carried interest: the specific case for reform of carried interest is strong; the general case for full CGT-income alignment is not — these are distinct policy questionsI want to end by making a distinction that the proposition has carefully avoided making. The carried interest loophole — the arrangement by which private equity managers take what is economically a performance fee as a capital gain — is genuinely indefensible. It is not a return to entrepreneurial risk-taking; it is a payment for services rendered, structured as a capital gain purely for tax advantage. The case for treating carried interest as income is strong, and I would support it. Similarly, the use of owner-manager small company structures to convert salary into capital distributions deserves targeted reform. These are genuine abuses of the CGT differential that cause real distortions and real unfairness. But conceding these specific cases is not the same as conceding the general principle that all capital gains should be taxed at income tax rates. The right response to the specific abuses is targeted reform — aligning carried interest with income tax, closing the owner-manager arbitrage — not a blunt instrument that applies income tax rates to the full scope of capital gains, including genuine risk-bearing investment, patient capital in growth companies, and the savings of ordinary investors. Good tax policy is precise. The proposition's instrument is not.
