The Great Risk ShiftJacob Hacker — The Great Risk Shift (2006)
Over the past half-century, the major economic risks of life — retirement income, healthcare costs, job security, income volatility — have been systematically transferred from broad risk-pooling institutions (defined-benefit pensions, employer-based and state guarantees) onto individuals, who now bear these risks alone, exposed directly to market fluctuation. The ISA is not a neutral savings product incidentally caught up in this shift; it is one of its purest expressions — a vehicle whose entire design presupposes that the individual, not any collective institution, will absorb the market risk of their own old age.

Privatised KeynesianismColin Crouch — "Privatised Keynesianism: An Unacknowledged Policy Regime" (2009)
When governments became unwilling or unable to sustain demand and living standards through redistribution and wage policy, they turned instead to encouraging private debt and private asset accumulation as a substitute — what I have called privatised Keynesianism. The promotion of tax-advantaged saving vehicles like the ISA, expanded and re-branded repeatedly since the 1980s, sits squarely within this regime: a multi-billion-pound annual tax expenditure, representing a real and substantial redistributive choice, presented instead as a neutral incentive for prudent individuals — precisely while real wage growth for the median household has stagnated.

Manufacturing the Investor SubjectPaul Langley — The Everyday Life of Global Finance (2008)
The significance of everyday financial products like the ISA is not exhausted by their distributive effects. The Asset-Based Welfare CritiqueKaren Rowlingson — Wealth and the Wealthy (with Stuart McKay, 2011)
Wealth is distributed far more unequally than income, and policies that pursue welfare goals through the medium of asset accumulation — "asset-based welfare" — therefore start from, and tend to reproduce, this much steeper inequality. A flat, universal allowance applied to a domain (asset ownership) that is itself radically unequal does not produce equal outcomes; it produces outcomes that scale with existing assets. The ISA is the paradigm case: formally available to everyone on identical terms, substantively usable in proportion to wealth one already has.

Chair,

I want to situate the ISA within a much longer arc than this debate usually allows, because I think its true significance only becomes visible at that scale. The question is not "is this savings product well-designed?" The question is: what happened to the institutions that used to bear the risks this product now asks individuals to bear alone, and why?

Two generations ago, a worker's retirement income was, for a great many people, the responsibility of an employer-sponsored defined-benefit scheme or the state — institutions that pooled risk across thousands or millions of people and across time. Today, the worker is handed an account, a tax wrapper, and a set of investment choices, and told the outcome is theirs to manage. Something has been transferred. The question is what, and to whom.
i. from pooled risk to individual exposure

Risk pooling — defined-benefit pensions and comparable collective institutions pool longevity risk, investment risk, and inflation risk across a large membership and across time, smoothing the impact of any individual's bad luck (a market downturn in the year before retirement, an unexpectedly long life); defined-contribution and individual-account vehicles like the ISA pool none of this — each individual's outcome depends entirely on their own contribution history and the market's performance over their own specific timelineA defined-benefit pension pools several distinct risks across its membership: investment risk (the scheme, not the individual, bears the consequence of a bad decade in markets), longevity risk (members who live a long time are subsidised by the scheme's overall mortality experience, not bankrupted by their own longevity), and to some extent inflation risk. None of this pooling exists for an individual ISA holder. If markets fall 30% in the two years before someone planned to draw on their ISA for retirement, that loss is theirs alone — there is no scheme-wide smoothing, no cross-subsidy from members who happened to retire in better years. If someone lives to ninety-five, their ISA does not know or care; it simply runs out, on whatever schedule the individual's own drawdown decisions produced. The shift from DB pensions and equivalent collective vehicles to individual accounts is not a shift in where money is administratively held; it is a shift in who absorbs the consequences of bad luck — market luck, longevity luck — that pooling used to absorb collectively.

ii. the rhetoric of choice and flexibility as risk-transfer language

Choice rhetoric — the ISA is marketed and discussed primarily in the language of flexibility, choice, and access — no restrictions on withdrawal, freedom to choose investments, "your money, your way"; this language describes real features but also performs ideological work, framing what is fundamentally a transfer of risk and responsibility as an enhancement of individual freedomIt is genuinely true that the ISA offers flexibility that a DB pension does not: no penalty for withdrawal, no requirement to annuitise, freedom to choose underlying investments. I do not dispute any of this as a description of the product's features. What I want to draw attention to is the function this language performs in public discourse. "Flexibility" and "choice" describe, accurately, the absence of the very constraints that, in a DB scheme, were the mechanism by which risk was pooled and managed on the member's behalf. A DB scheme's restrictions on early withdrawal and its conversion of a pot into a guaranteed income stream (effectively, compulsory annuitisation) are not arbitrary bureaucratic impositions; they are the technical mechanisms of risk pooling. To describe their absence as "flexibility" is accurate at the level of the individual transaction and deeply obscuring at the level of the system: it frames the removal of a risk-management mechanism as a gift of freedom, without any corresponding acknowledgement that the risks the mechanism managed have not disappeared — they have simply been relocated, onto the individual now enjoying their new "flexibility."

iii. why "more saving" doesn't answer the risk-shift critique

More saving isn't the point — even granting that ISAs increase individual saving rates (the question debated extensively in public-finance terms), this does not address the risk-shift critique, because the critique is not about the quantity of resources individuals end up with but about who bears the variance — the uncertainty — around that quantity; an individual with a larger but more volatile pot has not had their risk addressed merely by virtue of the pot being largerDefenders of the ISA often respond to risk-shift critiques by pointing to evidence (contested, as the previous debate on this floor explored) that ISAs increase individual saving. I want to be clear that this response, even if fully granted, does not engage with the critique I am making. The risk-shift thesis is not a claim about the expected value of what individuals end up with; it is a claim about who bears the variance around that expected value — the uncertainty, the exposure to bad outcomes. An individual who saves diligently into an ISA for forty years and retires into a market downturn has, by hypothesis, "saved more" than they would have without the ISA — and they are still exposed to a risk that a pooled, collective institution would have substantially mitigated. "More saving, same risk-bearer" is not a rebuttal to "the risk-bearer has changed." It is, if anything, a confirmation of how completely the individual has been positioned as the entity whose careful behaviour is now solely responsible for managing risks that used to be managed institutionally.

I want to place the ISA within a specific historical-political pattern that I have called privatised Keynesianism, because I think this pattern explains something the previous speaker's framework does not fully capture: not just that risk has been individualised, but why now, and what political function this individualisation serves for governments facing a particular set of constraints.

i. the regime of privatised keynesianism

Privatised Keynesianism — the term describes a policy regime in which governments, unable or unwilling to sustain aggregate demand and rising living standards through fiscal redistribution and wage policy (the classic Keynesian/social-democratic toolkit), instead encourage private debt and private asset accumulation to perform a similar function — individuals borrow and invest their way to the living standards that redistribution used to underwrite collectivelyClassic post-war Keynesianism sustained demand and rising living standards through a combination of fiscal policy, wage bargaining institutions that ensured productivity gains were shared with workers, and an expanding welfare state. From the 1980s onward, in Britain as elsewhere, several of these mechanisms were dismantled or weakened — collective bargaining coverage fell sharply, and the political consensus shifted against using fiscal redistribution as the primary tool for managing living standards. But the underlying problem — how do living standards continue to rise, or at least not visibly collapse, for the median household — did not go away. What I call privatised Keynesianism is the regime that emerged to manage this problem without redistribution: encourage private debt (mortgage credit expansion, equity withdrawal from housing) and private asset accumulation (tax-advantaged saving and investment) to do the work that wage growth and redistribution used to do. The household's balance sheet, not its pay packet or the state's transfer system, becomes the primary site where living standards are managed.

ii. the isa as fiscal choice, not neutral incentive

Tax expenditure as redistribution — the revenue the Exchequer forgoes through ISA tax relief is not a cost-free background fact; it is a real fiscal resource that could be deployed differently (wage subsidies, public services, direct transfers); choosing to forgo it in favour of subsidising private asset accumulation is itself a redistributive choice, even though it is not presented or debated as oneA multi-billion-pound annual tax expenditure is, in fiscal terms, indistinguishable from multi-billion-pound annual spending: both represent resources that the state has chosen to direct toward a particular purpose rather than another. When a government spends several billion pounds a year on, say, a wage subsidy programme, this is debated as redistributive policy — who benefits, how progressive is it, what are the alternatives. When the same sum is forgone through ISA tax relief, it is typically discussed, if at all, as a "cost of the policy" in technical terms, not as the redistributive choice it actually is. The choice to forgo this revenue specifically in order to enhance the tax-free returns on assets — which, as Professor Rowlingson will detail, are held overwhelmingly by those who already have them — while not extending equivalent resources to those without assets to shelter, is a distributive choice with a clear direction. That this choice is encoded in the tax base rather than the spending side of the budget does not make it less of a choice; it makes it a choice that escapes the scrutiny that equivalent spending decisions receive.

iii. the timing tells the story

Timing — the expansion and promotion of ISA-type tax-advantaged saving has occurred during, and has been politically emphasised during, periods of stagnant real wage growth for median and lower-income households; the policy substitutes an offer ("save what you have, tax-free") for an absent one ("your wages will grow")Consider the political-economic backdrop against which ISA allowances have been repeatedly expanded and the product repeatedly re-promoted: a period, especially since the 2008 financial crisis, of historically weak real wage growth for median and lower-income households in Britain. During this same period, the policy emphasis on "helping people save and invest" — expanded allowances, new variants (Help to Buy ISA, Lifetime ISA), prominent "ISA season" campaigns — has, if anything, intensified. I do not think this is coincidental, and I do not think it requires any conspiratorial account to explain. When the lever that used to raise living standards — rising real wages — is not available, a government under pressure to show it is "doing something" for household finances has an obvious alternative available: offer a tax break on whatever households can manage to save from wages that are not rising. The ISA, in this reading, is not simply a savings product that happens to exist; it is one of the available substitutes for a wage-growth policy that the prevailing economic settlement has made very difficult to deliver. It tells people: we cannot make your wages rise, but we can make sure that whatever you manage to put aside from them is not taxed.

I yield the floor.

2.The underlying problem (managing median household living standards) persisted; privatised Keynesianism is the regime that emerged to manage it via private debt and asset accumulation instead.

Both previous speakers have, in different ways, examined what the ISA does — how it allocates risk, what it costs the Exchequer and to whose benefit. I want to ask a different kind of question: what does the ISA make? Specifically, what kind of person does engagement with this product, and the cultural apparatus that surrounds it, produce?

i. everyday finance and the investor subject

Investor subject — the "investor subject" is a way of understanding oneself and one's future primarily through the vocabulary and logics of financial markets: thinking in terms of portfolios, allocation, risk and return, "my money working for me"; this is not a natural or inevitable way for people to relate to their own economic lives, but one that is actively cultivated through engagement with everyday financial products and their surrounding discourseThe argument I develop is that engagement with everyday financial products — mortgages, pensions, and savings and investment vehicles like the ISA — does not merely allocate resources or risk. It also constitutes the person engaging with these products as a particular kind of subject: an "investor subject," who understands their own financial future, and increasingly their own security and identity, through the vocabulary and logics of financial markets. This is not how people have always related to money, nor is it the only possible way to relate to it. It is a historically specific mode of subjectivity, actively produced through repeated, mundane engagement with products that frame the individual's relationship to their own future in terms of portfolios, allocation, risk, return — "your money working for you." The ISA, as one of the most widely-held such products in Britain, is a major site where this subjectivity is produced and reproduced, at scale, as an unremarkable feature of ordinary financial life.

ii. the cultural apparatus of "isa season"

ISA season — the annual cycle of media coverage, provider marketing, and informal social discussion around the ISA deadline (the "use it or lose it" framing discussed in the previous debate) does more than prompt transactions; it rehearses, every year, for a very large population, a set of categories — "allowance," "wrapper," "tax year," "maximise" — that constitute a shared vocabulary for thinking about one's finances in investment terms, regardless of whether the individual's actual financial position makes "investment thinking" materially relevant to themConsider the cultural apparatus that surrounds the ISA's annual deadline — what is widely referred to, in the financial press and provider marketing, as "ISA season." Every spring, a very large population is exposed to a recurring set of messages: have you used your allowance, are you maximising your wrapper, is your money working hard enough for you. The previous debate on this floor discussed this deadline as a behavioural device that prompts saving — and it may well do that. But I want to draw attention to a different effect, operating regardless of whether anyone actually acts on the prompt: the annual rehearsal, for millions of people, of a vocabulary — allowance, wrapper, tax year, maximise — that frames personal finance in the categories of investment management. This vocabulary becomes available, and indeed expected, as the way one thinks about one's own money, even for people whose actual financial position — renting, modest or no savings, little prospect of ever approaching the allowance — means that "investment thinking" bears little relationship to their material circumstances. The categories are rehearsed regardless.

iii. depoliticisation through individual portfolio management

Depoliticisation — when economic insecurity is framed, through products like the ISA and their surrounding discourse, primarily as a matter of individual financial planning — have you saved enough, have you invested wisely — collective and political dimensions of that insecurity (wage levels, housing costs, the absence of collective pension provision) become harder to see as political questions at all, appearing instead as personal failures of planning or disciplineThe constitutive effect I am describing has a political dimension that I think is its most significant consequence. When the dominant framework for thinking about one's economic security is "have I saved and invested enough, have I made good use of the available allowances and wrappers" — a framework the ISA and its surrounding discourse actively cultivate — then economic insecurity itself comes to be understood, by the person experiencing it, primarily in these individual, planning-oriented terms. The question "why do I not have enough put aside for my retirement" is answered, within this framework, by reference to one's own savings discipline, investment choices, and use of available products — not by reference to wage levels, housing costs that absorb an ever-larger share of income, or the absence of the collective pension provision that previous generations could rely on. This is not to say that no one ever makes these connections. It is to say that the everyday, repeated, normalised framework for thinking about one's own finances — a framework the ISA is one of the principal vehicles for — makes the individual-planning framing the default, the one that does not require any particular political awareness to arrive at, while the structural framing requires swimming against this current. Economic insecurity, refracted through the investor subject, looks like a personal planning problem first, and a political question only with effort.

I want to bring this debate back to a basic empirical fact that I think gets lost in discussions of allowances, wrappers, and behavioural nudges: wealth — the stock of assets a household owns — is distributed far more unequally than income. Any policy that pursues its goals through the medium of asset accumulation inherits this underlying inequality, unless it is very deliberately designed not to. The ISA was not so designed.

i. wealth inequality as the starting distribution

Wealth vs income inequality — the distribution of wealth (assets minus debts) in Britain is substantially more unequal than the distribution of income; a policy mechanism that operates on the wealth side of household balance sheets therefore starts from, and absent specific countervailing design, will reproduce, a steeper inequality than an equivalent policy operating on incomeIt is well established in the wealth distribution literature that the share of total wealth held by the top decile of households substantially exceeds the equivalent share of total income held by the top decile — wealth inequality in Britain is consistently found to be considerably more concentrated than income inequality. This matters directly for how we should think about any policy mechanism — like the ISA — that operates by allowing households to accumulate and hold assets more advantageously. A policy operating on the wealth side of the household balance sheet starts from a baseline that is already much more unequal than the income side. A flat allowance — the same £20,000 ceiling, formally available to a household with no spare assets and to a household with an investment portfolio already in the hundreds of thousands — applied against this starting distribution does not produce an equal outcome. It produces an outcome that scales with the existing, highly unequal distribution of assets, because only households with assets (or income well in excess of consumption needs) can make substantive use of an asset-accumulation-based allowance.

ii. asset-based welfare as a category, and its track record

Asset-based welfare — a policy framework, prominent particularly from the late 1990s, that addresses welfare goals (financial security, intergenerational mobility, retirement adequacy) through policies that encourage or facilitate asset ownership — housing, savings, investments — rather than, or alongside, income-based transfers and services; the ISA, alongside policies like Help to Buy, sits within this frameworkThe ISA should be understood as one instrument within a broader policy category: asset-based welfare. This is the framework, prominent in policy debates from the late 1990s onward, that addresses welfare goals — financial resilience, intergenerational mobility, adequate retirement provision — primarily through policies that facilitate asset ownership, rather than through income-based transfers or universal services. Housing policy (Help to Buy, shared ownership schemes), the ISA itself, and pension auto-enrolment into individual accounts all sit within this framework. The appeal of the framework, to its proponents, is precisely that asset ownership is supposed to deliver effects — security, a stake in the system, a buffer against shocks — that income transfers alone do not. I do not dispute that asset ownership can have such effects for those who achieve it. What the track record of asset-based welfare policies shows, however, is that achieving meaningfully redistributive asset accumulation among those who start with little or nothing requires very substantial, deliberately progressive design — large state contributions specifically targeted at those without existing assets. The ISA contains no such design. Its allowance is flat, untargeted, and usable in proportion to existing resources.

iii. the matthew effect, named precisely

Matthew effect — "to those who have, more is given": the ISA's flat allowance interacts with the underlying inequality of wealth and spare income such that the policy's substantive benefit — both the amount sheltered and the cumulative tax-free growth over time — scales with how much a household already has, producing a pattern where existing advantage is the primary determinant of who benefits, and by how much, from a formally universal policyThe pattern that results from a formally universal mechanism applied to a substantively unequal starting distribution has a name in the sociological literature: the Matthew effect, after the biblical verse "to him who has, more shall be given." A household with substantial existing assets and high income relative to consumption needs can direct a large fraction of the £20,000 annual allowance toward sheltering returns on wealth it already has — precisely the "Bed and ISA" practice discussed in the previous debate. A household with little or no spare income or assets can direct, at most, a small fraction of the allowance, if any. Over time, as Mr Sturrock argued in the earlier debate, this differential compounds: the household that could use the allowance substantially in year one can do so again in year two, and the tax-free growth on what it sheltered in year one adds to what it can shelter and grow in year two, while the household that could not use the allowance in year one has nothing additional to compound in year two. The ISA does not merely fail to redistribute; its structure actively channels its substantive benefit toward those who already have the most to channel.

Professor Hacker's account of the great risk shift is, as a description of an institutional transformation, one I do not contest. Where I want to press back is on its implicit normative framing — the suggestion that this transformation is straightforwardly a loss, that the institutions being displaced represented a settled good that individualisation has simply taken away. I want to offer a more two-sided account.

i. detraditionalisation cuts both ways

Detraditionalisation — the process by which individuals' life trajectories become less determined by fixed social structures (class position, gender roles, institutional membership that was assigned rather than chosen) and more a matter of ongoing, reflexive choice and self-construction; this process is the same one that underlies the "risk shift" Hacker describes, but it is not reducible to risk shift — it also describes the expansion of the sphere in which individuals can, and must, actively shape their own biographiesThe institutions that the great risk shift describes as having been displaced — the defined-benefit pension tied to a single employer over a career, the welfare state's assumption of a particular life trajectory — were not merely risk-pooling mechanisms. They were also, for the individuals enrolled in them, mechanisms of fixed biographical determination: a life trajectory substantially set in advance by one's position within an institution one had not chosen and could not meaningfully renegotiate. The process of detraditionalisation that has accompanied the rise of individual accounts is the same process, more broadly, that has loosened the grip of fixed class trajectories, of the assumption that one's economic life would simply replicate one's parents', of institutional memberships assigned by birth or early circumstance rather than chosen. This is the same process Professor Hacker describes as risk shift, examined from a different angle: not only "who now bears the risk" but "who now has the latitude, and the obligation, to actively construct their own economic biography." Both descriptions are true of the same underlying shift. The risk-shift framing captures one face of it; it is not the whole of it.

ii. the displaced institutions were not neutral

Not neutral — the collective institutions that individualisation displaced (DB pensions tied to lifetime employment with a single employer, the "family wage" model underpinning much welfare provision) encoded their own significant exclusions — most prominently, they worked far better for people with stable, continuous, full-time employment histories with a single employer than for people with interrupted, part-time, or multiple-employer careers, a pattern with well-documented gender dimensionsIt matters, in assessing what has been lost, to be precise about who the displaced institutions actually served well. A defined-benefit pension built on lifetime continuous employment with a single employer delivered real security — to people whose careers fit that pattern. It delivered much less, often very little, to people whose employment histories were interrupted, part-time, or spread across multiple employers — a pattern that, as feminist critiques of welfare state design have extensively documented, correlates strongly with gender, and with caring responsibilities that fall disproportionately on women. The "family wage" model that underpinned much mid-century collective provision — a single (male) earner's wage and pension entitlement covering a household — was not a neutral arrangement temporarily interrupted by individualisation; it was an arrangement that worked by assigning a particular, often dependent, position to those — mostly women — outside it. An individual account, for all the risks it transfers onto its holder, is at least an account that belongs to the individual who holds it, regardless of their employment pattern or marital status. This is not nothing, and a framework that treats the displaced institutions as simply "collective provision, now lost" elides what, for some people, was provided and to whom.

iii. life politics and reflexive engagement

Life politics — a form of political engagement concerned not with the traditional distributive questions of who gets what (though these remain important) but with how individuals, given the loosening of traditional structures, should live — questions of identity, lifestyle, and the active shaping of one's biography; engagement with financial planning, including the ISA, can be a site of this kind of reflexive, identity-constituting activity, not merely a site of risk-management or ideological enrolmentProfessor Langley's account of the "investor subject" treats engagement with products like the ISA as primarily a matter of subjects being constituted by, or enrolled into, market logics — a largely passive process happening to people through their exposure to financial discourse. I want to suggest a complementary reading: that active, reflexive engagement with one's financial situation — thinking about one's own future, making deliberate choices about saving and investment, developing one's own understanding of how these systems work — is itself a form of what I have called life politics: the kind of active self-construction that characterises late modernity more broadly, encompassing not just finance but choices about work, relationships, health, and identity. This is not to deny that this engagement happens within structures (market logics, product design, marketing) that the individual did not choose and that may not serve their interests. It is to say that the relationship between the individual and these structures need not be purely one of subjection; it can also be, and for many people is, a relationship of active, critical, even strategic engagement — which is closer to where my colleague Monsieur Feher will take this discussion.

I want to bring this debate back to the ground of comparative institutional analysis, because I think both Professor Crouch's "tax expenditure as redistribution" argument and Professor Rowlingson's "asset-based welfare" critique, while each individually well-founded, lead to a misdirected conclusion when applied specifically to the ISA — because they are not being applied comparatively, against the actual alternatives.

i. the comparison that matters: isa vs pension tax relief

Pension tax relief comparison — pension contributions receive tax relief at the saver's marginal income tax rate (20%, 40%, or 45%), meaning a higher-rate taxpayer receives more than double the relief, per pound contributed, that a basic-rate taxpayer receives; this "relief at marginal rate" structure is substantially more regressive than the ISA's structure, which gives no relief on contributions at all — the entire benefit is on growth and withdrawal, applied at a flat rate regardless of the saver's income tax bandThe single largest tax-advantaged savings vehicle in Britain, by a very wide margin, is not the ISA but the pension — and pension tax relief has a structural feature that the ISA entirely lacks: relief on contributions is given at the saver's marginal rate of income tax. A basic-rate taxpayer contributing £100 to a pension receives £20 of relief; a higher-rate taxpayer contributing the same £100 receives £40; an additional-rate taxpayer receives £45. The relief is, by design, worth more than twice as much to a higher-rate taxpayer as to a basic-rate taxpayer, for an identical contribution. The ISA has no equivalent feature at all — there is no relief on contributions whatsoever; the entire tax advantage is on growth and withdrawal, and it is the same flat advantage (full exemption) regardless of whether the saver is a basic-rate or additional-rate taxpayer. Measured purely on this dimension — the structure of relief by income tax band — the ISA is flat where pension relief is steeply regressive. If the proposition's critique of "tax-advantaged saving as redistribution-in-disguise" is sound — and I think it largely is — it identifies pension tax relief, which costs the Exchequer several times what ISA relief does, as by far the larger target.

ii. opportunity cost of attention, not just revenue

Opportunity cost of reform attention — political and analytical attention devoted to critiquing and reforming the ISA is attention not devoted to pension tax relief, which is both larger in fiscal scale and more regressive in structure; a debate that concludes "the ISA is the problem" risks directing scarce reform energy toward the comparatively smaller and less regressive of the two major UK tax-advantaged savings instrumentsProfessor Crouch is right that tax expenditures are fiscal choices deserving the same scrutiny as spending, and that this scrutiny is too often absent. I want to extend this point to the allocation of scrutiny itself. Political and analytical attention is a scarce resource. A debate that concludes "the ISA is the central instrument of privatised, individualised, regressive savings policy" directs whatever reform energy it generates toward an instrument that, while imperfect, is smaller in fiscal scale and flatter in structure than the alternative sitting right alongside it. Pension tax relief is rarely subjected to anything like the scrutiny the ISA receives in debates of exactly this kind — in part, I suspect, precisely because pensions feel less like "investing" and more like a default, background feature of employment, and so attract less of the cultural fascination that Professor Langley's "investor subject" framework would lead us to expect to be directed at the more visibly "investment-like" product. If the goal is to reduce the regressivity of UK savings taxation, the evidence points toward pension relief reform as the priority, by a substantial margin.

iii. "least bad" is not nothing

Least bad as a real property — within a set of policy instruments that all involve some departure from a hypothetical ideal, the instrument that departs least is not merely "less bad" in a dismissible sense — it represents the best available approximation, given real constraints, to the properties the ideal would have; identifying the ISA as comparatively the least regressive available savings instrument is therefore a substantive finding about its design, not merely a rhetorical move to deflect criticismI want to close by addressing directly the implicit objection that "the ISA is less regressive than pension relief" is a low bar, a deflection rather than a defence. I think this objection misunderstands what comparative institutional analysis is for. We do not get to choose policy from a menu that includes a perfectly progressive, perfectly redistributive savings instrument alongside the ISA and pension relief; we choose, in practice, among the instruments that are politically and administratively available, each of which embodies some departure from an ideal. Within that real choice set, the instrument that departs least from flat, non-regressive treatment is not merely "the least of several evils" in a sense that licenses ignoring it — it is the instrument whose design features come closest to the properties that a genuinely progressive reform would want to build on. The ISA's flat structure — no relief skewed to higher rate-payers, no feature analogous to pension relief's marginal-rate design — is not an accident, and it is not nothing. It is a design feature that, as Professor Sherraden will argue, makes the ISA a more promising foundation for progressive reform than the alternative would be.

I want to take up directly the question Professor Langley's analysis leaves open: given that engagement with products like the ISA does constitute people as "investor subjects" in the way he describes, what follows politically? My answer is that the appropriate response is neither denial of the condition nor abstention from it, but the politicisation of its terms — and I want to explain why abstention, in particular, is not the alternative it might appear to be.

i. the investee condition

Investee — under contemporary financialised capitalism, individuals relate to economic life not only as workers selling labour but as "investees": entities whose creditworthiness, employability, "human capital," and investment performance are continuously assessed and rated by institutions (credit reference agencies, employers, investment platforms, the state's own assessments of who counts as a "good saver"); this is a condition one is in, not a choice one makes, structured by institutions external to the individualThe condition I describe as being an "investee" is one in which a person's economic standing depends substantially on how they are rated, assessed, and positioned by institutions external to themselves — their credit score, their employability profile, the performance of whatever investment or pension assets they hold, their categorisation by the state as a "saver" deserving of particular tax treatment. This is not, in the first instance, a subjectivity one chooses to adopt or can simply decline; it is a structural condition of how contemporary economic life is organised, which Professor Langley's "investor subject" describes from the angle of how individuals come to understand themselves within it. I do not dispute this description. What I want to add is: this condition is the terrain on which contemporary economic politics has to operate, because it is the terrain that contemporary economic life actually occupies. There is no available alternative terrain to retreat to.

ii. why abstention is not a real alternative

Abstention's cost — given how compounding and lifetime financial structures work, declining to participate in tax-advantaged saving vehicles like the ISA — as a form of political refusal of "investor subjectivity" — has real, substantial, and irreversible costs to the person who abstains, accruing over decades; it is not a costless gesture, and the costs fall on the individual abstainer, not on the structures being refusedSuppose an individual, persuaded by Professor Langley's analysis, decides that the appropriate response to the investor subjectivity the ISA cultivates is to decline to participate — no ISA, no engagement with "allowance" and "wrapper" vocabulary, a refusal of the categories on offer. What does this refusal accomplish? The structures Professor Langley describes — the tax treatment of savings, the cultural apparatus of "ISA season," the broader financialisation of retirement provision — are entirely unaffected by one individual's non-participation; these are large-scale institutional and cultural patterns that do not register, let alone respond to, individual abstention. What the individual abstainer does experience, given how tax-free compounding works over decades, is a real and irreversible loss of the tax-free growth they would otherwise have accumulated — a loss that, compounded over a working lifetime, can be very substantial. The refusal is therefore not a costless political gesture aimed at the structure; it is a costly individual sacrifice that the structure does not notice, borne entirely by the person making it. This is precisely the situation that, I argue, characterises the investee condition generally: individual exit is available in principle and is costly and ineffective in practice.

iii. politicising the terms of participation

Politicising terms — if individual exit from investor subjectivity is costly and ineffective, the alternative form of politics available is collective contestation of the terms on which participation occurs: what the defaults are, who is automatically enrolled, what state contributions accompany participation for those with little to contribute, how the products are designed and regulated; this is "investee politics" — politics conducted on the terrain of financialised participation rather than against it from outsideIf individual exit is costly and structurally ineffective, the alternative form of politics — what I call investee politics — operates not by rejecting the terrain of financialised participation but by contesting its terms collectively: campaigning for better defaults (automatic enrolment with sensible default investment choices, as has occurred with pension auto-enrolment), for state contributions that accompany participation for those with little spare income (precisely the kind of progressive redesign Professor Sherraden will discuss), for product design and regulation that serves savers' interests rather than providers' (addressing the "loyalty penalty" dynamics discussed in the previous debate on this floor). This is politics conducted on the terrain Professor Langley describes, not from some position outside it — because, as I have argued, no such outside position is practically available to most people. The "ambivalence" that Professor Langley himself notes — people who participate in these products while remaining critical of the system that requires them to — is not a contradiction to be resolved by choosing one side. It is the actual, lived form that economic politics takes for most people under current conditions, and it is a coherent form, not a confused one.

I want to take Professor Rowlingson's critique with the seriousness it deserves, because I think it is correct as a description of the ISA's current design — and I want to argue that this is precisely why the right response is to redesign the instrument along lines that have already, in Britain, been tried and shown to work, rather than to conclude that asset-based welfare as such is the problem.

i. asset effects: why assets are not just stored income

Asset effects — a body of research finds that holding assets, even modest ones, is associated with effects on attitudes and behaviour — orientation toward the future, sense of security and efficacy, educational aspirations parents hold for children, civic and community participation — that are distinct from, and not simply explained by, the income-equivalent value of those assets; an asset functions differently, psychologically and behaviourally, than an equivalent amount of income or consumptionMy own work, going back over three decades, has focused on a body of evidence suggesting that holding assets — even quite modest amounts, even amounts well below the level at which they could fund significant consumption — is associated with effects on how people orient themselves toward the future, their sense of security and capacity to plan, the educational aspirations parents report for their children, and rates of civic and community participation. These "asset effects" are found to hold even after accounting for the income-equivalent value of the assets in question — that is, holding a modest asset appears to do something that an equivalent amount of additional income, received and spent, does not straightforwardly do. If this body of evidence is broadly right, then a welfare framework that operates only through income — however generous — may be leaving on the table a set of effects that asset-holding specifically produces. This is the foundation of my argument for asset-based welfare: not that it should replace income-based welfare, but that it addresses something income-based welfare, on its own, does not.

ii. the child trust fund: progressive asset-based welfare, implemented

Child Trust Fund — introduced in Britain in 2002 and running until its abolition in 2010-11, the Child Trust Fund gave every child born in the UK a savings/investment account with an initial government contribution, with larger contributions for children from lower-income families, and allowed (but did not require) family top-ups within an annual limit; this was a universal, progressively-structured asset-based welfare policy operating on the same TEE tax logic as the ISA but with state contributions specifically designed to be larger for those with the leastBritain has already implemented, for nearly a decade, exactly the kind of progressively-structured asset-based welfare policy that Professor Rowlingson's critique implies is needed: the Child Trust Fund. Every child born in the UK during the scheme's operation received a government-funded savings and investment account, with the government's initial contribution — and in some versions, additional contributions at later ages — larger for children from lower-income families. Families could add to the account, within annual limits, and the account's growth was tax-free, on the same TEE logic as the ISA. This was not a flat, untargeted allowance available in proportion to existing resources; it was a universal entitlement with progressive weighting built in, specifically designed to ensure that children from families with no spare income to contribute would nonetheless begin adult life with an asset — smaller than a wealthier family's contributions might produce, but an asset nonetheless, accumulated tax-free from birth. This existed. It worked as designed. Professor Rowlingson's critique of the ISA's flat structure is not a critique of something asset-based welfare is incapable of avoiding; it is a critique of a design choice that Britain had, at one point, chosen not to make.

iii. abolition as political fragility, not theoretical refutation

Political fragility — the Child Trust Fund was abolished in 2010-11 as part of post-financial-crisis fiscal consolidation; its abolition is best explained by the specific political vulnerability of universal programmes whose direct beneficiaries (children) are not voters and whose benefits are not immediately visible, making such programmes comparatively easy targets for near-term fiscal savings — not by any demonstrated failure of the programme's underlying design or its asset-effects rationaleThe Child Trust Fund was abolished as part of the fiscal consolidation following the 2008 financial crisis — a period in which a great many spending programmes were cut, and in which a universal programme whose direct beneficiaries were children (who do not vote, and for whom the benefit — an account maturing at eighteen — was not immediately visible to anyone) was, politically, a comparatively easy target for near-term savings, whatever its long-run merits. I do not think this abolition tells us anything about whether progressive, universal asset-based welfare works as a matter of design — it tells us about the political economy of fiscal consolidation and the particular vulnerability of programmes for non-voting, future-oriented beneficiaries. The lesson I draw is not "abandon the approach" but "the approach requires either durable cross-party commitment or integration into an instrument, like the ISA, that has proven much harder to abolish because its beneficiaries are current adult voters who notice immediately if it is removed." Combining the ISA's political durability with the Child Trust Fund's progressive design — auto-enrolment, state top-ups weighted toward those with least, beginning in childhood — is precisely the "universalise it properly" response that Professor Rowlingson's critique, taken seriously, points toward.
