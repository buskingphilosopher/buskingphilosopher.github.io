Mr Speaker,

I want to begin by setting out exactly what the proposition is claiming and why it is hard to dispute on reflection. We are not claiming that death is always the worst thing that can happen to a person. We are claiming something more modest and more defensible: that death is, in general, bad for the person who dies, and that the source of this badness is deprivation — the foreclosure of goods the person would otherwise have had.

This claim is not merely intuitive. It has a structure, and the structure can be made precise. The opposition will press us on the puzzle of subjecthood — how can there be a harm when there is no subject to undergo it? I want to show why this puzzle, though genuine, does not defeat the deprivation account.

The structure of the deprivation account.

The core claim is comparative: a person is harmed by an event if she would have been better off had that event not occurred. Death makes things worse for the person who dies because, had she not died, she would have enjoyed further goods — pleasures, achievements, relationships, experiences. The harm is constituted by the gap between how well things go for her given that she dies and how well they would have gone had she not.

The puzzle: the subject of the harm does not exist at the time the harm is supposed to obtain. But this does not show that the harm is unreal. It shows only that the harm has an unusual temporal structure — a structure we should accept rather than reject.
i. the subject-and-timing problem

Subject & timing — harm need not be temporally located; the person is the subject even posthumouslyThe opposition will argue that harm requires a subject and that the dead have no welfare. I accept the first claim and reject the second. The harm of death has the person who dies as its subject — not as a dead person who undergoes something, but as the person who is deprived of goods they would otherwise have had. We can locate the harm in the person, even if we cannot locate it at a time when the person exists. Nagel’s suggestion: the harm is located atemporally in the person rather than at any particular time. The absence of a temporal location for the harm is unusual but not paradoxical. We routinely speak of events as harmful to people who are no longer in a position to experience their effects.

ii. against the Epicurean premise

Epicurus — experiential requirement on harm is too strong; deprivation is a harm even without a suffering subjectThe Epicurean argument runs: where death is, we are not; therefore death is nothing to us. The key premise is that harm requires the possibility of experiencing it. But this premise is too strong. We do not generally require that harms be experienced to count as harms. Consider betrayal: if my closest friend deceives me throughout our friendship and I die never knowing, I have been harmed even though I experienced nothing amiss. The Epicurean must say I am not harmed. This is counterintuitive and should be rejected. Harm is constituted by the obtaining of conditions that are bad for the person, not by the experience of those conditions.

iii. the asymmetry with prenatal nonexistence

Asymmetry — prenatal nonexistence is not bad because the person could not have come into existence earlier; this is not true of deathLucretius argues that since we do not regret our prenatal nonexistence, we should not regret our posthumous nonexistence either. But there is a crucial asymmetry. My posthumous nonexistence deprives a particular existing person — me — of goods I would otherwise have had. My prenatal nonexistence does not deprive me of anything, because there was no person to be deprived: I could not, as a matter of contingent fact grounded in my identity, have come into existence substantially earlier than I did. This asymmetry — developed further by Fischer and Brueckner — is why we rationally feel differently about the two nonexistences.

iv. the deprivation account and the commonsense verdict

Common sense — deprivation account vindicates ordinary grief and premature-death intuitions without circularityThe deprivation account has one decisive advantage over all rivals: it vindicates the commonsense verdicts that death is worse when it occurs prematurely, that the death of a young person is a greater loss than the death of one who has lived a full life, and that the grief of survivors tracks something real about the harm done to the person who died. These verdicts are not merely sentimental; they are grounded in the deprivation structure. The person who dies at thirty is deprived of more goods than the person who dies at eighty, and this is why the former death is worse. No other theory explains this pattern as cleanly.

2.That we cannot locate the harm at a time when the person exists is unusual but not paradoxical. We routinely attribute harms to persons in abstraction from the time at which those harms are experienced.
3.The harm can be located atemporally in the person: it consists in the gap between how well things go given death and how well they would have gone otherwise.

∴The deprivation account is the only theory that vindicates commonsense verdicts about premature death systematically and without requiring additional conditions that the most vulnerable subjects may not satisfy.

My task is to show that the deprivation account Nagel has outlined can be given a precise and systematic articulation that survives the most demanding technical objections. I want to do this by bringing hedonism about welfare to bear on the question of what makes death bad.

The hedonist framework is the most natural home for the deprivation account. If welfare is constituted by pleasant experience, then death is bad exactly to the extent that it forecloses pleasurable experiences that the person would otherwise have had. The badness is measured by the gap between the actual hedonic value of the person’s life and the counterfactual hedonic value it would have had, had death not occurred.

“If we can make it plausible that hedonism is true and that the comparative account of the evil of death is true, then we can explain the badness of death in a theoretically unified way.” — Feldman, 1992
i. intrinsic vs. extrinsic value and the subject problem

Intrinsic/extrinsic — death bad extrinsically; subject problem dissolved; harm need not be intrinsically experiencedThe most pressing objection to the deprivation account is the subject problem: harm requires a subject, and death destroys the subject. My response distinguishes intrinsic from extrinsic value. Death is not intrinsically bad for the person who dies — it is not an experience of suffering. It is extrinsically bad: it prevents the occurrence of intrinsically good states (pleasant experiences) that would otherwise have obtained. The harm is constituted by this prevention. We need not posit a subject who undergoes the harm at a time when they no longer exist; we need only note that the obtaining of the harm consists in the failure of goods to occur that would have occurred for this person.

ii. the comparative account and counterfactual structure

Comparative account — harm is a comparative matter; counterfactual structure handles timing objections naturallyThe comparative account of harm holds that an event harms a person if it makes her worse off than she would otherwise have been. On this account, death harms a person when the world in which she dies is worse for her than the nearest world in which she does not. The counterfactual structure handles the timing problem naturally: we do not need to say the harm obtains at any particular time, only that the actual world is worse for her than the counterfactual world. The comparison is between entire histories of the person’s welfare across possible worlds. This is philosophically unproblematic and technically precise.

iii. against the Epicurean experiential requirement

Against Epicurus — experience-requirement collapses harm into suffering; extrinsic harms are real even when unfeltThe Epicurean argument requires that harm consist in experienced suffering. But this conflates intrinsic and extrinsic harm. A surgeon who botches an operation and leaves a patient permanently paralysed has harmed them even if the patient is under anaesthesia throughout and experiences no pain. The harm consists in the bad state the patient is now in, not in any experience of that state. Similarly, death harms the person who dies by placing them in a bad comparative position — the position of having fewer goods in their life than they would otherwise have had — and this harm is real even though it is not experienced. Epicurus mistakes the absence of the experience of harm for the absence of harm itself.

iv. responding to the symmetry argument

Symmetry — prenatal nonexistence doesn’t deprive because there was no person; post-mortem nonexistence deprives an existing personLucretius asks why we do not grieve prenatal nonexistence as we grieve posthumous nonexistence. The comparative account gives a clean answer: prenatal nonexistence does not make anyone worse off than they would otherwise have been, because prior to my existence there is no person for whom a comparison can be made. My posthumous nonexistence makes me worse off than I would have been: there is a person — me — for whom the comparison holds, and that person would have had more goods had she survived. The symmetry argument fails because it ignores the asymmetry in the existence of the subject of comparison.

Argument I — Intrinsic/extrinsic distinction dissolves the subject problem
1.Death is not intrinsically bad for the person who dies: it is not an experience of suffering undergone by an existing subject.
2.Death is extrinsically bad: it prevents the occurrence of intrinsically good states (pleasant experiences) that would otherwise have obtained for the person.
3.Extrinsic harm does not require a subject who undergoes the harm at the time it obtains; it requires only that goods fail to occur that would have occurred for this person. The subject problem is dissolved.
∴The subject problem does not defeat the deprivation account when harm is understood as extrinsic: the harm consists in the prevention of goods, not in any positive suffering undergone by an existing subject.
Argument II — The comparative counterfactual account handles the timing problem without positing posthumous subjects
1.An event harms a person if it makes her worse off than she would otherwise have been. Death harms when the world in which she dies contains fewer welfare goods for her than the nearest world in which she does not die.
2.The comparison is between entire histories of welfare across possible worlds. No temporal location for the harm within the person’s life is required.
3.This is philosophically unproblematic and technically precise: the counterfactual structure handles timing objections naturally by moving the comparison to the level of whole-life welfare histories.
∴The comparative counterfactual account is the correct framework for the deprivation account: it handles the timing problem without positing posthumous subjects and is technically precise.
Argument III — Epicurus conflates the absence of experienced harm with the absence of harm itself
1.The surgical paralysis case: a patient harmed under anaesthesia experiences no suffering but is clearly harmed. The harm consists in the bad state induced, not in any experience of it.
2.Similarly, death places the person in a bad comparative position — having fewer welfare goods than they would otherwise have had — and this position is bad for them even though it is not experienced.
3.Epicurus’s experiential requirement conflates two distinct things: the experience of harm and the existence of harm. The absence of the former does not entail the absence of the latter.
∴Epicurus conflates experienced harm with harm itself; extrinsic harms are real even when unfelt; the deprivation account is not defeated by the absence of posthumous experience.
Argument IV — The symmetry argument fails: the asymmetry of subject-existence dissolves the Lucretian problem
1.Prenatal nonexistence does not make anyone worse off because prior to my existence there is no person for whom a welfare comparison can be made.
2.Posthumous nonexistence makes me worse off: there is a person — me — for whom the comparison holds, and that person would have had more welfare goods had she survived.
3.The symmetry argument ignores this asymmetry in the existence of the subject of comparison; once the asymmetry is noted, the Lucretian inference collapses.
∴The symmetry argument fails because prenatal and posthumous nonexistence are asymmetric with respect to the existence of a subject for whom the welfare comparison holds; the Lucretian inference from parity of attitude is therefore unjustified.

In Well-Being and Death I defend pure hedonism as the correct account of welfare and argue that it supports a powerful version of the deprivation account. I want to add two specific claims to what Nagel and Feldman have said: that death is bad for its victim at times after death, and that it is worse the earlier it occurs.

“Death is bad for its victim at all those times at which the victim would have been living well. It is worse the earlier it occurs. It is bad for animals and foetuses in just the same way it is bad for adult humans.” — Bradley, 2009
i. subsequentism: death is bad at times after death

Subsequentism — death is bad at times after it occurs; when victim would have been living well; non-paradoxicalI defend what I call subsequentism: the view that death is bad for its victim at times after the death occurs — specifically at those times at which the victim would have been living well. This sounds paradoxical: how can something be bad for you at a time when you do not exist? But the paradox dissolves when we distinguish between being bad for someone at a time and that person experiencing something bad at that time. Death is bad for you at t if, had you survived to t, you would have been living well at t. The harm is constituted by the counterfactual gap at each such time. This is an unusual feature of the harm of death, but it is not incoherent.

ii. earlier death is worse

Earlier death worse — more times at which victim would have been living well; clean quantitative accountOn this framework, earlier death is worse than later death because there are more times at which the victim would have been living well, had she survived. The person who dies at thirty is harmed at more post-death times than the person who dies at seventy. This is not merely intuitive; it follows from the structure of the subsequentist account and gives it a quantitative precision that other versions of the deprivation account lack. We can in principle compare the badness of different deaths by comparing the counterfactual hedonic histories they foreclose.

iii. death is bad for animals and foetuses

Animals and foetuses — hedonism grounds same account of death’s badness for all sentient creatures; no special conditions requiredOne advantage of the hedonist version of the deprivation account is its generality. McMahan’s psychological continuity account makes the badness of death depend on psychological connections between present and future self. This makes it difficult to account for the badness of the death of infants, animals, and foetuses, who have weak psychological connections to their future selves. The hedonist account has no such difficulty: death is bad for any being capable of having pleasant and unpleasant experiences, because it forecloses future pleasant experiences. The death of a healthy animal is bad for that animal in exactly the same way and for exactly the same reason as the death of a human adult.

iv. the opposition cannot explain the scalar badness of death

Scalar badness — only deprivation account explains why some deaths are worse than others in a systematic wayHere is a decisive challenge to the opposition. If death is not bad — if the Epicurean is right — then we cannot explain why some deaths are worse than others. Why do we judge the death of a twenty-year-old worse than the death of a ninety-year-old? Why do we judge an unexpected death worse than one following a long illness? The deprivation account explains both: the twenty-year-old is deprived of more goods; the unexpected death forecloses goods the person expected to have. Epicureanism and the categorical desire account both struggle with this scalar structure. The deprivation account handles it systematically and naturally.

I want to develop a version of the deprivation account that is more fine-grained than what my proposition colleagues have offered, and that bridges the metaphysics of harm directly to the ethics of killing.

My central claim is that the badness of death is not simply proportional to the quantity of goods it forecloses, but is modulated by the strength of the psychological connections between the person now and the future self who would have enjoyed those goods. This person-relative account is more accurate and more practically useful than the undifferentiated deprivation views.

The time-relative interests account.

The badness of death for a person at a time is a function of: (i) the amount of good the person’s life would have contained had she not died; and (ii) the degree to which the person now is psychologically connected to the future self who would have enjoyed those goods. The weaker the psychological connections, the less bad the death is — because the person now has a weaker interest in the welfare of their future self.

This explains why the death of an infant, though genuinely bad, is typically less bad than the death of a young adult: the infant is weakly psychologically connected to the adult it would have become.
i. psychological continuity and the strength of the interest in survival

Psychological continuity — interest in survival proportional to strength of psychological connections; not binary but scalarParfit taught us that personal identity over time is constituted by psychological continuity and connectedness — overlapping chains of memory, intention, belief, desire, and character. I argue that the strength of one’s interest in one’s own future welfare is proportional to the strength of these connections. A person who is strongly psychologically connected to their future self has a strong interest in that future self’s welfare; one who is weakly connected has a weaker interest. Death defeats this interest. The stronger the interest, the greater the harm of death. This gives us a scalar account of the badness of death that is more fine-grained than the undifferentiated deprivation account.

ii. bridging metaphysics to the ethics of killing

Ethics of killing — time-relative interests ground why killing is wrong; strength of interest explains comparative wrongnessThe time-relative interests account bridges naturally to the ethics of killing. Killing is wrong because it defeats the victim’s interest in their own continued existence. The strength of this interest — determined by the strength of psychological connections — explains the comparative wrongness of different killings. Killing a psychologically rich adult who is strongly connected to a flourishing future is worse than killing an infant who is weakly connected to any future self. This is not to say the infant’s death is harmless — it is still bad — but the degree of wrongness is graduated in a way that matches our considered moral judgements.

iii. against the Epicurean: the interest in survival is real and present

Against Epicurus — interest in future welfare is present now; death defeats a present interest, not a future oneThe Epicurean argues that once we are dead, we have no interest in continuing to live, and that a future interest we do not yet have cannot be harmed. My account responds: the interest in future welfare is present now, in the living person. It is the interest I now have in my future self’s welfare, grounded in my psychological connections to that future self. Death defeats this present interest by preventing its realisation. There is no need to posit a posthumous subject whose interests are frustrated; the subject whose interests are frustrated is the living person at the time of death.

iv. the practical implications: treating the dying and the very young

Practical implications — time-relative interests determine how we should treat the dying, infants, and animals; ethics follows metaphysicsThe time-relative interests account has direct practical implications that the undifferentiated deprivation account lacks. How bad is the death of a patient in a persistent vegetative state? How bad is late-term abortion? How should we weigh the death of an animal against the death of a human? The time-relative interests account gives principled answers: the badness of each death is a function of the psychological richness of the being and the strength of its connections to a future self. This makes the ethics of killing tractable in a way that the blunt deprivation account cannot achieve on its own.

My contribution, developed with Anthony Brueckner, is to answer the Lucretian symmetry argument directly and to show why it is rational to have asymmetric attitudes toward prenatal and posthumous nonexistence. This is the most technically demanding challenge to the proposition, and I want to meet it head on.

“There is a rational basis for distinguishing our attitudes toward prenatal and posthumous nonexistence: we have a future-directed bias toward goods, and it is rational to care more about future pleasures than past ones.” — Fischer & Brueckner, 1993
i. the Lucretian argument stated

Lucretian argument — symmetric nonexistences; no rational asymmetry of attitude if deprivation account is rightLucretius invites us to consider the time before our birth as a mirror of the time after our death. We are not distressed by our prenatal nonexistence; therefore we should not be distressed by our posthumous nonexistence. This is an argument against the deprivation account: if posthumous nonexistence is bad, prenatal nonexistence is equally bad, and since prenatal nonexistence is clearly not bad, neither is posthumous. The argument has considerable force and must be answered on its own terms. I want to show that the asymmetry in our attitudes is not merely a psychological fact about us but is rationally grounded in the structure of our temporal perspective.

ii. future-bias and its rational grounding

Future-bias — rational asymmetry; caring more about future than past pleasures is normatively appropriate, not merely psychologically contingentMost people prefer a past pain to a future one of the same intensity and duration. Parfit called this the bias toward the future: we care more about future goods and evils than past ones of equal magnitude. Is this bias rational? I argue that it is, or at least that it is rationally permissible. The future is the dimension in which action is possible; caring about the future is tied to the very possibility of agency and planning. Past pleasures and pains are settled; future ones are open to influence. A rational agent who cares about action-guiding reasons has reason to be future-directed in a way that is not merely psychological contingency but normatively appropriate.

iii. applying future-bias to the symmetry argument

Asymmetry answer — future pleasures rationally matter more; posthumous deprivation of future goods rationally worse than prenatal non-occurrenceThe future-bias rationale applies directly to the Lucretian argument. Prenatal nonexistence deprives us of past goods — goods that, from our present perspective, are in the past. I want to address a specific gap in the proposition’s case: the question of posthumous harm. The opposition — particularly Rosenbaum and Smuts — will argue that not only is death not bad, but that nothing that occurs after death can be bad for the person who has died. I disagree on both counts, and I want to explain why the distinction between the mortem and post-mortem theses matters.

Two theses, both defensible.

The mortem thesis: death may harm those who die. Virtually all non-Epicureans accept this.

The post-mortem thesis: events occurring while a person is dead and gone may also harm them. This is more contested. Epicurus denies both; most critics of Epicurus accept both.

The intermediate view: death harms (mortem thesis is true), but posthumous events do not — because death leaves its victims so severely harmed that they are immune from further posthumous harm. I reject even this intermediate view and defend both theses.
i. the mortem thesis

Mortem thesis — death harms: deprives of future goods; experiential requirement too strong; extrinsic harm is realDeath harms the person who dies by depriving them of the goods their life would have contained had they not died. This is the core deprivation claim. The Epicurean’s experiential requirement — that harm must be felt to be real — is too strong. We do not generally require that harms be experienced; we require only that they make the person’s situation worse than it would otherwise have been. Death clearly does this: the world in which you die earlier is worse for you than the world in which you die later or not at all. This is the mortem thesis, and it is well established.

ii. the post-mortem thesis

Post-mortem thesis — posthumous events can harm via frustrated ante-mortem desires and interests; harm is ante-mortem even if event is post-mortemPosthumous events can harm a person by frustrating interests and desires they had while alive. The mechanism is clear: a person forms desires and projects while alive that extend beyond their death. If those desires and projects are frustrated by posthumous events, the person is harmed — not as a dead person who experiences something, but as the living person who formed those ante-mortem desires. The harm is located in the ante-mortem desire-formation, not in any posthumous experience. This is the post-mortem thesis, and it is equally defensible.

iii. against the intermediate view

Intermediate view — unsatisfying; death does not produce immunity from posthumous harm; ante-mortem interests persist as a normative basisSome philosophers accept the mortem thesis but reject the post-mortem thesis, holding that death leaves its victims immune from further harm. This intermediate view is unsatisfying. It rests on the claim that death is so severe a harm that nothing further can count as a harm to the person. But this confuses the severity of the harm with a logical immunity from further harm. There is no principled reason why a person who has been severely harmed cannot be further harmed by posthumous events that frustrate their ante-mortem desires. The ante-mortem interests of the person persist as a normative basis for posthumous harm claims even after the person no longer exists.

iv. the practical stakes of posthumous harm

i. the desire satisfaction principle

Desire satisfaction principle — desires held while alive can be frustrated by posthumous events; harm accrues to the ante-mortem desire-holderThe desire satisfaction principle holds: a person B is harmed by act A if (i) B wants P to be true, and (ii) A makes P false. This principle generates posthumous harm naturally: a person who desires that her ashes be scattered at a particular place is harmed if that desire is frustrated, even if the frustration occurs after her death. The harm accrues to B as the person who held the desire while alive. This is not a posthumous harm in the sense of a harm undergone by a posthumous entity: it is an ante-mortem harm whose triggering condition occurs post-mortem. The desire satisfaction principle is the correct account of what makes posthumous frustrations into harms.

No-Subject Objection — fails; subject is the ante-mortem person who held the desire; harm need not be contemporaneous with subject’s existenceThe No-Subject Objection holds that posthumous harm is impossible because there is no subject to be harmed: the person who had the interests no longer exists. We can make it now the case that something is true of the past without backwards causation: I can now make it the case that yesterday was my penultimate day at my job by quitting today. Innocuousism distinguished from pure Epicureanism.

Pure Epicureanism (Rosenbaum): being dead cannot harm; death is nothing to us; fear of death is irrational.

Innocuousism (Smuts): death is not bad for the person who dies; dying may sometimes be less good than living; death can be regrettable without being bad; fear of death is not vindicated by the claim that death harms, though other attitudes toward death may be appropriate.

The distinction matters: innocuousism is a weaker claim that does not depend on the sentience requirement alone.
i. the Dead End Argument

Dead End Argument — death leads to no states; extrinsic badness requires causal chain to intrinsic badness; therefore death is not extrinsically badThe Dead End Argument runs as follows. An event is extrinsically bad if and only if it leads to intrinsically bad states of affairs. Death leads to no states of affairs at all: it is a dead end. Therefore death is not extrinsically bad. The argument does not depend on the sentience requirement: it depends on a claim about the causal structure of badness. Even if we allow that harm does not require the capacity for experience, it still requires that the harmful event lead to something bad. Death leads to nothing. It cannot therefore be extrinsically bad.

ii. less good but not bad: the key distinction

Less good not bad — dying may be less good than continued life; but “less good” does not entail “bad”; failing to obtain a good is not the same as incurring a badHere is the crucial distinction that the proposition misses. Death may prevent a person from obtaining goods they would otherwise have had. In that sense, dying may be less good than continued life: the life with more goods is better than the life with fewer. But “less good than the alternative” does not entail “bad.” Failing to obtain a benefit is not the same as incurring a harm. If I decline to offer you a gift you would have enjoyed, I have failed to benefit you, but I have not harmed you: I have left you no worse off than you were before. Death is analogous: it fails to provide goods the person would have had, but it does not impose any bad states on them. It is less good than the alternative, but it is not bad.

iii. against the deprivation account’s causal hypothesis

Deprivation account causal hypothesis — requires harm without any causal chain to bad states; but this is incoherent; foreclosure of goods is not the same as incurring badnessThe deprivation account holds that death is bad because it forecloses goods. But “foreclosing goods” is not the same as “incurring badness.” The deprivation account treats the absence of goods as a harm, but this conflates two distinct evaluative relations. Not having something good is not the same as having something bad. If it were, then every person who failed to achieve every possible good they might have had would be harmed by each such failure — but this is absurd. We cannot be harmed by every possible good we fail to obtain. The deprivation account needs a principled distinction between foreclosed goods that harm and foreclosed goods that merely fail to benefit, and it has not provided one.

iv. innocuousism and appropriate responses to death

Appropriate responses — death regrettable without being bad; grief appropriate as response to loss; innocuousism not practically eliminativistInnocuousism does not imply that we should be indifferent to death or that grief at death is inappropriate. Death forecloses goods that we had reason to value and pursue; we can regret their absence without claiming that their absence is a harm. We can grieve the death of a loved one — recognising that their life would have contained more goods, and that those goods are genuinely valuable — without holding that they have been harmed by not obtaining them. Innocuousism preserves the significance of death as an event without requiring that it be a harm: death matters because goods that existed and were valued now no longer occur, not because the dead person is in a bad state.
