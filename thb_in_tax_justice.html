Logical Arguments

Mr President,

The global tax system is broken in a specific, diagnosable way. It is not merely that some people pay less than they should. It is that the architecture of the system — the interaction of mobile capital, national tax sovereignty, and absent international coordination — generates a structural race to the bottom that no individual state can arrest alone. My book Catching Capital is an attempt to name this problem with philosophical precision and to propose institutional remedies adequate to its scale.

"Tax competition is not an unfortunate side effect of globalisation. It is a systemic failure — a prisoner's dilemma in which each state's individually rational fiscal strategy produces a collectively irrational outcome: eroded revenues, undermined redistribution, and the progressive de-democratisation of fiscal policy."
i. fiscal autonomy as the core value at stake

Fiscal autonomy — democratic self-determinationThe value I place at the centre of my analysis is fiscal autonomy: the capacity of a democratic polity to determine, through legitimate political processes, the size of its public budget relative to GDP and the degree of redistribution it wishes to achieve. This is not a technocratic preference. It is a condition of democratic self-government. When capital is mobile and states compete to attract it — by cutting corporate tax rates, offering preferential regimes, or tolerating financial secrecy — the effective range of fiscal policy available to any individual state narrows. Citizens of a high-redistribution polity may democratically endorse a social contract requiring substantial public revenues. Tax competition makes that contract practically unenforceable. The democratic will of the majority is vetoed by the exit option of mobile capital.

ii. the two principles: membership and fiscal constraint

Membership principle — pay where you belongI propose two regulatory principles. The first is the membership principle: individuals and corporations should pay tax in the jurisdictions of which they are members, where membership is determined by genuine economic and social ties — residence, employment, sales, production — rather than the paper registration of a brass-plate subsidiary in the Caymans. Google generates revenue in Britain, employs engineers in London, and serves British consumers. Its membership in Britain is not contingent on whether it has chosen to route profits through Dublin and Bermuda. The second principle is a fiscal policy constraint: any fiscal policy of a state is unjust and should be prohibited if it is both strategically motivated — designed to attract capital from elsewhere — and has a negative aggregate impact on the fiscal self-determination of other states. Luxembourg is not offering low rates because it has made a principled social choice about the size of the state; it is poaching the tax bases of France and Germany.

iii. the International Tax Organisation

ITO — mandate, legitimacy, enforcementEnforcement requires institutions. The OECD is a club of rich countries that systematically excludes the Global South from the design of rules it is then expected to follow — its legitimacy deficit is decisive. The United Nations has universal membership but lacks the enforcement capacity and technical mandate to function as a tax regulator. I propose an International Tax Organisation with a genuinely inclusive membership, a mandate covering both the membership principle and the fiscal policy constraint, and the authority to impose sanctions on non-compliant jurisdictions. This is not without precedent: the WTO enforces trade commitments with precisely this architecture, and its founding generated the same sovereignty objections now levelled at an ITO. Those objections were wrong then; they are wrong now.

iv. sovereignty is not the objection — it is the argument

Sovereignty paradox — cooperation enables, not constrainsThe standard objection to international tax regulation is that it infringes national sovereignty. I want to turn this argument on its head. What does sovereignty mean for a state that cannot, in practice, enforce the fiscal social contract its citizens have endorsed? A state whose corporate tax rate is perpetually undercut by neighbours offering preferential deals does not enjoy meaningful fiscal sovereignty — it has the form of sovereignty without its substance. Pooling some sovereignty through an ITO, in exactly the way states pool trade sovereignty through the WTO, does not diminish sovereignty. It restores it. The choice is not between sovereignty and cooperation. It is between nominal sovereignty in a race to the bottom, and real sovereignty in a cooperative regime.

v. poaching and luring — both must be addressed

Poaching vs. luring — OECD's incomplete reformThe current reform agenda — OECD BEPS, the Pillar Two global minimum rate — addresses poaching: the sheltering of profits in jurisdictions where no genuine activity occurs. It does not address luring: the competitive reduction of tax rates to attract real economic activity. Any crackdown on poaching will intensify luring as its substitute. A multinational that can no longer shift profits to Bermuda on paper will have a greater incentive to genuinely relocate to Ireland's 12.5% regime. Poaching and luring must be regulated simultaneously or the reform is merely redirecting the same underlying pressure into a different channel. This is what distinguishes my proposal from the OECD framework: completeness.

Thank you.

Argument I — Fiscal autonomy: democracy's condition of possibility
1.Democratic self-government requires that majorities can effectively implement their fiscal social contract — determining public budget size and redistribution levels.
2.Tax competition systematically narrows the range of fiscal policy available to individual states, subjecting democratic choices to a capital-exit veto.
3.No individual state can arrest this dynamic unilaterally; coordination is logically required.
∴Tax competition is a structural threat to democratic self-government; binding international regulation is a requirement of democracy, not a constraint upon it.
Argument II — The membership principle: tax where you truly belong
1.The legitimacy of taxation rests on genuine membership — economic, social, and civic ties between taxpayer and jurisdiction.
2.Current international tax rules allow artificial severing of those ties through paper subsidiaries and profit-shifting, enabling entities to enjoy membership benefits while evading membership obligations.
3.The membership principle restores the normative connection between economic presence and tax obligation that existing rules have allowed to be engineered away.
∴The membership principle corrects the fundamental mismatch between where value is created and where tax is paid; it is a requirement of basic fairness in the allocation of taxing rights.
Argument III — The ITO: legitimacy and enforcement both required
1.The OECD lacks legitimacy — it excludes the Global South from rule-design while expecting compliance. The UN lacks enforcement capacity and technical mandate.
2.An International Tax Organisation with inclusive membership and sanctions authority fills this institutional gap — modelled on the WTO's architecture for trade.
3.The WTO precedent demonstrates that pooling regulatory authority in an international body is both feasible and compatible with state interests properly understood.
∴An ITO is the necessary and feasible institutional vehicle for enforcing global tax justice; existing institutions are structurally inadequate to the task.
Argument IV — The sovereignty paradox: cooperation restores what competition destroys
1.Under unregulated tax competition, states nominally retain sovereign fiscal authority but are practically constrained to a narrow band of policies that will not trigger capital flight.
2.Pooling sovereign authority through an ITO — as states pool trade authority through the WTO — does not reduce real fiscal capacity; it expands it by eliminating the race-to-the-bottom constraint.
3.The choice is not between sovereignty and constraint; it is between a sovereignty that is real and a sovereignty that is merely formal.
∴The sovereignty objection inverts the truth: binding tax regulation restores effective sovereignty by ending the race to the bottom that renders formal sovereignty hollow.
Argument V — Poaching and luring must be addressed together
1.Current OECD reforms address profit-shifting to no-activity jurisdictions (poaching) but leave competitive rate-cutting to attract real activity (luring) entirely unregulated.
2.Successful crackdown on poaching will intensify luring — multinationals will substitute real relocation for paper relocation.
3.An incomplete reform that eliminates one channel of tax competition while leaving another open is not a reform of the system — it is a rearrangement of its pressure points.
∴Only a framework addressing both poaching and luring can stabilise the international tax system; the OECD's Pillar Two is a necessary but deeply insufficient beginning.

Mr President,

My colleague Professor Dietsch has made the structural and institutional case for binding regulation. I want to approach the same conclusion from a different direction: the analysis of the OECD's 2021 Pillar Two agreement — hailed by its architects as a historic achievement — reveals, on careful examination, the insufficiency of what passes for reform in the current international order, and the deeper fairness principles that any adequate framework must satisfy.

"The OECD deal was designed by rich countries, for rich countries. Its global minimum tax primarily benefits high-income jurisdictions that can introduce top-up taxes on undertaxed profits; it leaves developing nations without comparable mechanisms and without a seat at the table where the rules were made. This is not a fair deal. It is a carve-up dressed in the language of global governance."
i. three complementary fairness principles

Three fairness principles — free riding, autonomy, inequalityIn our 2022 analysis, Tove Ryding and I identify three complementary principles against which any international tax framework must be evaluated. First: the prevention of free riding by multinational corporations — entities that use the infrastructure, educated workforce, rule of law, and consumer markets of multiple jurisdictions while paying tax in none of them. Second: respect for and promotion of fiscal autonomy — the capacity of states, especially developing states, to fund the public services their citizens need without being constrained by the threat of capital flight. Third: the limitation of distributive and relational inequalities — ensuring that the tax system does not systematically concentrate resources in wealthy jurisdictions at the expense of poorer ones, and that the architecture of international tax governance does not entrench a relationship of domination between high-income rule-makers and low-income rule-takers.

ii. why the OECD deal fails all three

OECD deal fails — free riding persists, South excludedThe 2021 agreement establishes a 15% global minimum effective tax rate for large multinationals. This sounds significant. It is not. Fifteen percent remains far below historical corporate tax rates in most developed countries. The carve-outs — the substance-based income exclusion for payroll and tangible assets — create large exceptions that sophisticated tax planning readily exploits. The income inclusion rule primarily benefits the parent-country jurisdiction; source countries where value is actually created receive little additional revenue. And the 137-country "Inclusive Framework" is inclusive in name only: the key design decisions were made by the G7 and OECD members, with developing country input limited to endorsing a framework whose parameters were already fixed. The UN Tax Committee — which more genuinely represents developing country interests — was deliberately marginalised in favour of an OECD process that serves the interests of capital-exporting nations.

iii. relational inequality — the governance dimension

Relational inequality — OECD as club of rich nationsThe third fairness principle — relational equality — is the most philosophically demanding and the most systematically violated. International tax governance is not merely unfair in its distributional outcomes; it is unfair in its process. The relationship between the OECD's rich-country members and the developing countries expected to implement OECD-designed rules is not a relationship between equal participants in a common enterprise. It is, to speak plainly, a relationship of domination: the strong design rules that benefit themselves, legitimise those rules through the language of technical expertise and global consensus, and then present the result to weaker nations as international law. A framework that perpetuates this relationship — even if it marginally improves distributional outcomes — reproduces the structural injustice it purports to address.

iv. what a genuinely fair reform would require

Genuine reform — inclusive UN process, higher minimum, unitary taxationA genuinely fair international tax reform would require, at minimum: a substantially higher global minimum effective tax rate — the academic consensus clusters around 21-25%, not 15%; a shift of taxing rights toward source countries, not just residence countries; a move toward unitary taxation of multinationals as single global entities with profits apportioned by genuine economic factors; and governance through an inclusive body with democratic legitimacy — the United Nations Tax Committee, reformed and empowered, rather than the OECD. The motion before this house asks whether international tax competition should be regulated. We have argued that it should. We add: it must be regulated by an institution that represents all the world's nations, on terms that do not simply recapitulate the existing hierarchy of power.

Thank you.

Argument I — Three fairness principles: the evaluative framework
1.Preventing free riding by multinationals — entities benefiting from public infrastructure without contributing to it — is a basic requirement of reciprocity and fairness.
2.Fiscal autonomy — the capacity of all states, including developing ones, to fund public services — is a requirement of democratic self-governance and international justice.
3.Limiting distributive and relational inequalities — not only in outcomes but in the governance process itself — is required by any adequate theory of global justice.
∴Any adequate international tax framework must satisfy all three principles; a deal failing any of them is not a fair reform even if presented as one.
Argument II — The OECD deal fails all three principles
1.At 15% with large substance exclusions, the minimum rate allows sophisticated multinationals to continue significant profit-shifting — free riding is not prevented.
2.The income inclusion rule concentrates new revenues in parent-country (rich) jurisdictions, not source countries where value is created — developing fiscal autonomy is not promoted.
3.The Inclusive Framework is nominally universal but was designed by G7/OECD members with developing countries limited to endorsing pre-fixed terms — relational inequality is reproduced.
∴The OECD 2021 deal fails each of the three fairness principles; it is a carve-up by rich countries that perpetuates structural injustice beneath the language of global consensus.
Argument III — Relational inequality: governance as domination
1.The relationship between OECD rule-designers and developing-country rule-implementers is not one of equal participation — it is structural domination dressed in technical language.
2.A framework reproducing this relationship — even if marginally improving distributional outcomes — perpetuates the structural injustice it claims to address.
3.The deliberate marginalisation of the UN Tax Committee in favour of the OECD process is direct evidence of this power asymmetry operating in real institutional choices.
∴Legitimate global tax governance requires equal participation, not the subordination of developing nations to rich-country-designed rules; the OECD process fails the legitimacy test at its foundation.
Argument IV — What genuine reform requires
1.A genuinely fair minimum rate — academic consensus: 21–25% — would prevent the residual profit-shifting that 15% tolerates.
2.Unitary taxation with formulary apportionment based on economic presence (sales, payroll, assets) would shift taxing rights toward source countries where value is created.
3.Governance through a reformed, empowered UN Tax Committee — not the OECD — would provide the inclusive legitimacy that any binding global regime requires.
∴Genuine reform is achievable but requires higher rates, source-country taxing rights, and inclusive governance — none of which the current OECD framework delivers.

Mr President,

The proposition before this house is, at bottom, a proposal for an international tax cartel. The language is dressed in the respectable robes of justice and democratic autonomy. But let us be precise about what is actually being proposed: governments would agree, through a supranational body, to limit their competition for capital — in exactly the way that OPEC limits competition for oil revenues. I oppose cartels in the private sector. I oppose them with equal vigour in the public one.

"Tax competition is not a problem to be solved. It is a discipline to be welcomed. It constrains the ability of governments to exploit their subjects. It forces politicians to account for what they spend before demanding more. The proposal before this house would remove that discipline — and governments freed from competitive constraint have never, in the whole of history, chosen to do less."
i. tax competition as a restraint on leviathan

Competitive restraint on government overreachThe proposition speaks of governments being unable to implement their citizens' fiscal preferences because of tax competition. I would ask: which citizens? And which preferences, as revealed through behaviour rather than survey responses? When capital flees a high-tax jurisdiction, it is not subverting democracy — it is expressing a preference about the relationship between what government provides and what it extracts. If a government cannot retain the capital base it wishes to tax, this is information — information about whether the taxes being levied are proportionate to the services being delivered. Competition between jurisdictions, like competition between firms, disciplines poor performance and rewards good governance. The proposition would eliminate this discipline entirely and calls the elimination justice.

ii. the corporate tax — a confused instrument

Corporate tax incidence — workers bear it, not capitalProfessor Dietsch's framework rests on the assumption that corporate taxation achieves redistribution from capital to labour and the public. The economic evidence does not support this assumption. The corporate income tax is not borne by corporations — corporations are legal fictions. It is borne by some combination of shareholders through reduced returns, consumers through higher prices, and — crucially — workers through lower wages and reduced employment, as capital-labour substitution responds to the increased cost of capital. A tax instrument whose incidence falls substantially on workers and consumers is a poor vehicle for the redistributive goals the proposition claims to advance. The proposition is fighting to protect an instrument that often harms the people it purports to protect.

iii. the sovereignty objection — real, not formal

Sovereignty — international cartel vs. self-determinationProfessor Dietsch inverts the sovereignty argument elegantly. Let me invert it back. When Ireland sets a 12.5% corporate tax rate, it exercises sovereign fiscal choice — the choice of a small open economy to compete on what it can offer rather than on the scale it cannot achieve. When an International Tax Organisation prohibits that choice by classifying it as impermissibly "strategic," it does not restore sovereignty — it substitutes the preferences of large, wealthy nations for the preferences of small ones. The ITO, as Professor Dietsch envisions it, would effectively empower France and Germany to veto the fiscal strategies of Luxembourg, Ireland, and — with the same logic — Barbados, Mauritius, and Singapore. This is not the restoration of sovereignty. It is the exercise of power by the large against the small, dressed in the language of collective action.

iv. flat taxation — a simpler remedy

Flat tax — eliminates avoidance, reduces distortionI have long argued for a simple, flat-rate tax on income from all sources, with no deductions, no preferences, and no international gymnastics. The complexity that enables profit-shifting, transfer pricing abuse, and the entire infrastructure of tax planning is not a feature of taxation per se — it is a feature of the labyrinthine complexity of tax codes designed by politicians for political rather than economic purposes. A flat tax, applied uniformly to all income of all types, would dramatically reduce the scope for avoidance. The proposition's remedy is an international bureaucracy to police a broken system. My remedy is to fix the system so that it requires no policing.

Thank you.

Argument I — Tax competition disciplines governments, benefiting citizens
1.Competition between jurisdictions, like competition between firms, disciplines poor performance and rewards efficient governance — capital exit reveals whether tax levels are proportionate to services.
2.Removing competitive pressure through a tax cartel eliminates this discipline entirely, freeing governments to raise revenues without accountability to the implied value they deliver.
3.Throughout history, governments freed from external constraint on revenue extraction have consistently chosen to extract more, not to govern better.
∴Tax competition is a constraint on government overreach that benefits citizens; eliminating it through a binding global regime removes the most effective check on fiscal excess.
Argument II — Corporate taxes fall on workers, not capital
1.Corporations are legal fictions; corporate tax is borne by some combination of shareholders, consumers, and — evidence suggests substantially — workers through wage suppression and reduced employment.
2.A tax instrument whose incidence falls primarily on workers is a poor vehicle for the redistributive goals the proposition claims to advance through protecting corporate tax revenues.
3.The proposition's justice argument depends on corporate taxation redistributing from capital to labour; if incidence runs the other way, the justice argument runs the other way too.
∴The justice case for protecting corporate tax revenues depends on an assumed incidence that the evidence does not sustain; the proposition may be protecting an instrument that harms those it claims to help.
Argument III — The ITO empowers the large against the small
1.Small, open economies — Ireland, Singapore, Barbados — rationally choose low-tax competitive strategies; this is genuine exercise of fiscal sovereignty by nations that cannot compete on scale.
2.An ITO with authority to prohibit "strategically motivated" fiscal policy would effectively empower large, wealthy nations to veto small nations' fiscal strategies.
3.This is not restoring sovereignty — it is the exercise of power by the strong against the weak, reframed as collective action.
∴The ITO proposal would systematically disadvantage small nations in favour of large ones; its sovereignty argument is the sovereignty of the powerful, not the many.
Argument IV — A flat tax eliminates avoidance without international bureaucracy
1.The complexity enabling transfer pricing, profit-shifting, and tax planning is a product of complex, politically-distorted tax codes — not an inherent feature of taxation.
2.A flat tax applied uniformly to all income from all sources, with no deductions or preferential regimes, would remove most of the architecture of avoidance without requiring international enforcement.
3.The proposition proposes a vast international bureaucracy to police a broken system; the simpler remedy is to fix the system so it does not require policing.
∴Tax simplification is a superior remedy to tax cartelisation; the proposition's institutional solution is proportionate to a problem it has misdiagnosed.

Mr President,

I find myself in an unusual position: I agree with the proposition that the international tax system is a scandal, and I agree that it requires radical reform. I do not agree that the motion before us captures the depth of the problem — and I believe that any reform programme that fails to understand the architecture of the offshore system will fail to dismantle it, because it will be dismantling a facade while leaving the structure intact.

"The offshore system is not a collection of exotic anomalies on the periphery of respectable international finance. It is a spider's web, with the City of London at its centre, spun deliberately and over decades, from the remnants of a formal empire that was never truly relinquished — merely rebranded."
i. the spider's web — the British imperial network

British spider's web — BOTs, Crown Dependencies, City of LondonMy research, documented in Treasure Islands, identifies a layered network that is the most important single structure in global offshore finance. At its hub is the City of London — not the United Kingdom as a sovereign nation, but the City in its peculiar constitutional role as a jurisdiction unto itself, with its own governance, its own representative in Parliament, and its own direct relationships with the financial secrecy jurisdictions that orbit it. The inner rings are Britain's Crown Dependencies — Jersey, Guernsey, the Isle of Man — and its Overseas Territories: the Cayman Islands, British Virgin Islands, Bermuda, Gibraltar, Turks and Caicos. These jurisdictions combine futuristic offshore finance with medieval political arrangements: their legislatures are, in effect, captured by financial services interests, with meaningful democratic opposition to offshore business eliminated. Around this core clusters an outer ring of jurisdictions — Hong Kong, Singapore, Ireland, Dubai — with strong historical and commercial ties to the British financial network. Together, these account for roughly half the world's secrecy jurisdictions.

ii. empire rebranded as "competitive advantage"

Post-imperial finance — empire in new formThis network did not emerge spontaneously from market forces. It was constructed. In the late 1950s, as Britain's formal empire was contracting, the Bank of England and the City of London deliberately facilitated the growth of the Eurodollar market — a stateless, offshore financial zone operating in London but technically outside British regulatory jurisdiction. This was the original offshore escape hatch: a mechanism by which capital could access the liquidity and professionalism of London's financial markets while evading the regulatory constraints that applied to domestic finance. From this foundation, the City extended its network through the Crown Dependencies and Overseas Territories — jurisdictions that Britain formally administers but which adopt secrecy laws, zero-tax regimes, and trust arrangements designed to attract mobile capital from the onshore world. The formal empire ended. The financial empire continued. It was simply redesignated "competitive advantage."

iii. plausible deniability — the structure of complicity

Plausible deniability — City adjacent, BOTs complicitWhat makes this network particularly difficult to confront politically is the architecture of plausible deniability it has constructed. The City of London does not, formally, offer the most aggressive offshore services — it is one step removed. The British Virgin Islands offers nominee directors and bearer structures. Jersey offers trust arrangements that obscure beneficial ownership. The Cayman Islands offers investment fund structures beyond effective regulatory reach. The City of London processes the resulting flows, provides the legal, accounting, and financial services, and profits enormously — while maintaining that it is simply a well-regulated financial centre operating within UK law. Officially, London is not a tax haven. Officially. The spider's web allows the City to act as an offshore hub while retaining the reputational cover of an onshore financial centre.

iv. why standard reform proposals leave the web intact

Reform limits — OECD protects the web's architectsThe OECD reform process — Pillar One, Pillar Two, BEPS — is designed by precisely the countries that benefit most from the current system. The United Kingdom, which administers the BOT network, is a leading architect of OECD tax reform. The United States, which runs its own offshore network through Delaware, Wyoming, and its Pacific territories, is another. These countries are not neutrally adjudicating global tax justice. They are managing a controlled demolition of the most egregious practices while protecting the structural architecture that generates their competitive advantage. Any binding global regime that does not include the mandatory unwinding of the Crown Dependencies' secrecy regimes, the extension of full beneficial ownership transparency to the BOTs, and the dismantling of the City's role as an offshore clearing house, is not addressing the system. It is addressing its symptoms.

v. what genuine reform looks like from this vantage

Genuine reform — transparency, BOT accountability, public registersI am not, let me be clear, a libertarian on this question. The offshore system is, as I have documented at length, enormously damaging — to developing countries drained of tax revenues by illicit capital flight, to ordinary citizens in rich countries who bear a higher tax burden because corporations and wealthy individuals do not, and to democracy itself as the financial sector accrues political influence proportionate to its ability to fund the alternatives to tax compliance. What I insist is that genuine reform must name the system honestly. It must require the United Kingdom to extend public beneficial ownership registers to all its Overseas Territories — something it has committed to and repeatedly delayed. It must treat the Crown Dependencies as the regulatory extensions of the British state they functionally are. And it must acknowledge that the global tax reform agenda is currently being designed by the system's primary beneficiaries — which is not a recipe for genuine change.

Thank you.
