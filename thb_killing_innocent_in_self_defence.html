“Resolved: That innocent threats are on a moral par with innocent bystanders and may not permissibly be killed in self-defence”

The FoundationMichael Otsuka, 1994
The Moral Equivalence Thesis — the property that explains why we may not kill a bystander is her lack of responsible lethal agency. Because an innocent threat shares this same lack, killing her is on a moral par with the worst kind of bystander killing. The innocent threat may not permissibly be vaporised.

Logical Arguments

I will state the problem as sharply as I can, then give my answer.

A Villain throws a large person — call him Falling Man — off the roof of a building. Falling Man is descending toward you. If he lands on you, you will be crushed and killed; Falling Man, well-padded, will survive. You have a ray gun. May you vaporise Falling Man to save yourself?

The intuitive answer, I submit, is no. And the reason why not is precisely the same reason you may not redirect a javelin hurtling toward you by pulling a Bystander into its path. Falling Man is, morally speaking, a Bystander. He is not responsible for the threat his body poses to you. The property that makes it impermissible to kill Bystander is lack of responsible lethal agency — the fact that Bystander exercises no agency or control over whatever endangers your life. Falling Man shares this property exactly. He is not exercising agency. He is not even acting. He has been thrown. He is, in the morally relevant sense, identical to Bystander — and what may not be done to Bystander may not be done to him.

The Core Taxonomy.

Villainous Aggressor. Culpably and intentionally threatens your life. Clearly may be killed. Has full moral agency over the threat.

Innocent Aggressor. Intentionally threatens your life but her action is beyond her control (e.g. hypnotised to kill you). Has the form of intentional agency but lacks moral responsibility.

Innocent Threat. Poses a threat with no agency whatsoever (e.g. Falling Man thrown by Villain). No intention, no control, no moral responsibility.

Innocent Bystander. Neither threatens nor is responsible for whatever threatens. Clearly may not be killed.

My thesis: Innocent Threat is on a moral par with Innocent Bystander. Both lack responsible lethal agency. What explains impermissibility in Bystander’s case is not absence of causal involvement in the threat sequence — it is absence of responsible agency. Since Innocent Threat equally lacks this, she equally may not be killed.
i. the moral responsibility view: what grounds the bystander’s immunity

MRV — lack of responsible lethal agency grounds immunity, not causal non-involvementThomson argues that the bystander’s immunity derives from the fact that the bystander is not in the threat sequence — not causally implicated in what threatens you. On that view, Falling Man lacks the bystander’s immunity because his body is in the threat sequence: it is what will kill you. My argument is that Thomson has identified the wrong property. What grounds the bystander’s immunity is not her absence from the causal chain but her lack of responsible lethal agency over whatever endangers you. A bystander is defined as one who does not herself endanger your life and is not responsible for whatever does. The morally operative word is responsible. Falling Man satisfies the same condition: though his body is the instrument of harm, he exercises no agency, control, or responsibility over that fact. He was thrown. He cannot stop himself. He does not intend your death. The moral property that matters — responsible lethal agency — is absent in Falling Man exactly as it is absent in Bystander.

ii. the trolley cases: pushing the intuition

Trolley cases — killing tied-up person on track is monstrous; killing Falling Man is equally monstrousConsider a runaway trolley bearing down on you as you lie strapped to the track. An innocent person is also strapped to the side of the trolley — tied there by the Villain who set the whole scene in motion. You have a device that will vaporise this person, detaching her body from the trolley and halting it, saving your life. May you use it? I submit not. The person tied to the trolley is not exercising any agency over the threat. She is being used, like a biological component, to deliver lethal force to you. She is not responsible for the threat her presence poses. Killing her would be among the worst kinds of bystander killing: initiating a lethal sequence that exploits her body to save oneself. This case is structurally identical to Falling Man. In each case, you propose to kill a non-responsible person whose body is the instrument of a threat over which she has no control. The moral abhorrence is equally intense.

iii. against thomson: the rights-violation view fails the transitivity test

Against Thomson — rights-violation view generates absurd transitivity: Innocent Aggressor = VillainThomson argues that what unifies the cases in which defensive killing is permissible is that the threatening person is about to violate your right not to be killed — and that a person forfeits her right not to be killed the moment she is about to do so, regardless of fault. This view has a deeply counterintuitive implication. If the hypnotised Innocent Aggressor forfeits her right simply by being about to kill you — without fault, without control, without genuine agency — then the basis for liability has been severed from everything that makes liability morally intelligible. We do not normally say that a person forfeits a fundamental right by doing something entirely outside her control. The sleepwalker who accidentally places her hands around your throat does not thereby forfeit her right not to be killed. If she did, liability would be indistinguishable from mere causal implication — and the distinction between persons and natural threats, between moral agents and falling rocks, would collapse entirely.

iv. the cost-bearing asymmetry

Cost-bearing asymmetry — you must bear the cost; Falling Man must not; Otsuka’s conclusionI acknowledge that my conclusion is demanding. You must allow Falling Man to crush you to death rather than vaporise him. This seems a terrible imposition. But consider the alternative: we permit the killing of a person who has done absolutely nothing wrong, who is entirely without agency or control in the matter, and who is in every morally relevant respect indistinguishable from a bystander dragged involuntarily into danger. The question is: whose right to life takes priority when both parties are entirely innocent? My answer is that neither party’s right automatically overrides the other’s. But since we are proposing to kill Falling Man — to extinguish his life — the burden of justification for that killing is not met. He has given us nothing — no culpability, no responsible agency, no choice — that could ground such a justification.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Moral Responsibility View: what grounds bystander immunity is responsible lethal agency, not causal non-involvement
1.A bystander is someone who does not endanger your life and is not responsible for whatever does. Her immunity from defensive killing rests on her lack of responsible lethal agency over the threat to you.
2.Falling Man’s body is in the causal sequence (it will kill you), but Falling Man exercises no agency, control, or responsibility over this fact: he was thrown, cannot stop himself, and has no intention to harm.
3.The morally operative property — responsible lethal agency — is absent in Falling Man precisely as it is absent in Bystander. The causal difference (presence vs. absence in the threat sequence) is morally inert.
∴Innocent Threat is on a moral par with Innocent Bystander: both lack responsible lethal agency, and this is the property that generates the immunity that makes defensive killing impermissible.
Argument II — Trolley cases: initiating a lethal sequence against a non-responsible person is impermissible regardless of the causal structure
1.A person tied to a trolley bearing down on you exercises no agency over the threat her presence poses; vaporising her to halt the trolley would be among the worst kinds of bystander killing — initiating a lethal sequence that exploits her body.
2.Falling Man is structurally identical: you would initiate a lethal sequence (vaporising him) that exploits a non-responsible person’s body to save yourself.
3.The moral abhorrence of killing a non-responsible person in this way generalises across both cases and does not depend on whether the person is “in” the threat sequence or merely constitutes it.
∴The Trolley cases confirm that killing non-responsible persons — whether bystanders or threats — by initiating lethal sequences is impermissible; the causal structure of the person’s involvement does not override the moral property of non-responsibility.
Argument III — Against Thomson: the Rights-Violation View collapses the distinction between persons and natural hazards
1.Thomson: a person forfeits her right not to be killed the moment she is about to violate your right not to be killed, regardless of fault or control.
2.But if the hypnotised, non-agentive Innocent Aggressor forfeits her right simply by being about to cause your death, then liability is grounded in bare causal implication, not in any property of moral agency that could make it intelligible.
3.On this view, the sleepwalker who accidentally threatens you forfeits her right as fully as the culpable villain — erasing the distinction between persons and natural hazards that is fundamental to our understanding of liability.
∴Thomson’s Rights-Violation View generates an absurd equivalence between culpable villains, non-agentive innocents, and natural threats; liability severed from moral responsibility is not morally intelligible liability.
Argument IV — Cost-bearing: when both parties are innocent, the burden of justification for killing is not met
1.When you propose to kill Falling Man, you bear the burden of justifying the extinguishing of his life. He has given you nothing — no culpability, no responsible agency, no choice — that could ground that justification.
2.Both parties are entirely innocent. Neither’s right to life automatically overrides the other’s. The tiebreaker must rest on who possesses the property (responsible lethal agency) that grounds a reduction in immunity. Falling Man lacks it.
3.Therefore the burden of justification for killing Falling Man is not met: you must bear the cost of his fall rather than impose death on someone who is morally innocent in every relevant sense.
∴The cost of Falling Man’s descent falls on you, not on him: the burden of justification for killing a non-responsible innocent is insuperable, and the Moral Equivalence Thesis is vindicated.

I want to explain both how I arrived at this position and why, in 2005, I explicitly reaffirmed it against the critics who had emerged in the intervening decade.

In my 1994 paper I independently developed a view closely related to Otsuka’s, though my framing was somewhat different. I argued that the innocent attacker — by which I mean a person who threatens your life through no fault of her own — cannot be permissibly killed if we are to preserve any morally coherent notion of what grounds the right of self-defence. In 2005, writing specifically on non-responsible killers, I examined the objections the intervening decade had produced and concluded that Otsuka’s narrower formulation was superior to my original one on two grounds: he restricted his argument to the genuinely non-responsible, and he drew the comparison with bystanders in a more precise and compelling way.

“The argument that Otsuka presented is superior to the one I advanced in two respects. First, he restricts his discussion to nonresponsible threateners. Second, Otsuka compared killing a nonresponsible threatener both with killing an innocent bystander used as a means and with killing a bystander as a side-effect — and showed that in both cases, killing the nonresponsible threatener is equally impermissible.” — McMahan, 2005
i. the 1994 argument: absence of agency defeats liability

1994 — absence of responsible agency defeats liability; non-responsibility different from mere non-culpabilityIn 1994 I argued that there is a morally crucial difference between absence of culpability and absence of moral responsibility altogether. A person who acts in reasonable but mistaken self-defence may be non-culpable but is nonetheless morally responsible: she has exercised agency, deliberated, and chosen to act. She is liable to defensive force, I argued, because she exercises a form of responsible agency over the threat she poses, even if that agency is not blameworthy. The truly non-responsible threatener — someone whose bodily movements pose a threat with no agentive input whatsoever, like Falling Man — is categorically different. No deliberation, no choice, no agency. The moral foundation of liability — the idea that a person has done something, chosen something, or taken a risk — is completely absent. Liability without any grounding in responsible agency is not morally intelligible: it would make a person liable for the weather she brings with her.

ii. the 2005 defence: no liability grounded in non-responsibility means no justified killing

2005 defence — two routes to permissible killing; neither available against non-responsible threatBy 2005, the literature had proposed various alternatives to the Moral Responsibility View. In my 2005 paper on non-responsible killers, I argued that there are precisely two routes by which killing a person in self-defence might be morally justified: the liability route and the lesser-evil route. The liability route holds that the person is liable to be killed because she is morally responsible for posing an unjust threat. The lesser-evil route holds that even though the person is not liable, the lesser-evil calculus justifies the killing because it averts a greater harm. Against a non-responsible threatener, the liability route is completely closed: she is not morally responsible, so she cannot be liable. The lesser-evil route is also typically unavailable: it generates no permission to kill an innocent person merely to save oneself, since the lives of the two parties are not to be aggregated on a simple greater-good calculus. In the typical Falling Man scenario, therefore, there is neither liability nor a lesser-evil justification. The killing is impermissible.

iii. why my 2009 revision does not undermine this framework

2009 pivot explained — calibrated model is refinement, not abandonment; non-responsible still hardest caseI should be honest about the evolution of my views. By 2009 I had come to believe that the non-responsible threatener case, as Otsuka frames it, sets the constraint at its strongest but that there are other cases — involving minimally responsible threats — where the constraint is weakened. A soldier ordered to fight an unjust war under threat of execution is minimally but genuinely morally responsible for the threat he poses; his liability, I argued in 2009, is correspondingly diminished but not zero. Killing him may be permissible as a less-than-fully-proportionate harm. But this revision does not undermine the Otsukan framework: it refines it by distinguishing genuinely non-responsible threats from minimally responsible ones. The truly non-responsible threatener — Falling Man, who has made no choices and taken no risks — remains at the far end of the spectrum. My 2009 position acknowledges, not retracts, this.

iv. against frowe: “material responsibility” is not enough

Against Frowe — material responsibility is not moral responsibility; cannot ground liability to be killedFrowe argues that innocent threats bear a unique “material responsibility” for the danger their bodies pose, and that this distinguishes them from bystanders. I find this unconvincing. Material responsibility — being the causal source of a threat — is precisely what we have been debating. The question is not whether Falling Man’s body is the instrument of threat (obviously it is) but whether this causal fact, absent any exercise of moral agency, can ground a reduction in his immunity to being killed. My answer, and Otsuka’s, is no. Rights can be forfeited, and immunity diminished, by what one does in a morally responsible sense. They cannot be forfeited by what one’s body does while one exercises no agency whatsoever. A person thrown through a window does not thereby forfeit her right not to be shot by the homeowner.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Absence of responsible agency (not merely culpability) is what defeats liability
1.A non-culpable but morally responsible agent — one who has deliberated and chosen to act under a reasonable but false belief — remains liable to defensive force because she has exercised responsible agency over the threat she poses.
2.The genuinely non-responsible threatener (Falling Man) exercises no agency, deliberation, or choice whatsoever. No agentive input of any kind connects her to the threat her body poses.
3.Liability requires some grounding in responsible agency; where that grounding is entirely absent, the moral foundation of liability is missing and the person cannot be liable.
∴Non-responsibility (absence of moral agency) defeats liability to defensive killing in a way that mere non-culpability does not; the genuinely non-responsible threatener is not liable and may not be killed on a liability justification.
Argument II — Two routes to justified killing; neither available against a non-responsible threat
1.Killing in self-defence can be justified by (a) liability: the person is morally responsible for an unjust threat and therefore liable to defensive force; or (b) lesser evil: killing the non-liable person is a lesser evil that averts a greater harm.
2.Route (a) is closed: the non-responsible threatener is not morally responsible and therefore cannot be liable. Route (b) is also unavailable in the standard Falling Man case: merely saving your own life at the cost of an innocent person’s death does not satisfy a lesser-evil justification, since neither life is worth more than the other.
3.If neither the liability nor the lesser-evil justification is available, the burden of justification for killing is not met.
∴Killing a non-responsible innocent threat is impermissible: the liability route is closed by non-responsibility, and the lesser-evil route does not generate a permission to kill an innocent person merely to save oneself at equal cost.
Argument III — McMahan’s 2009 revision is a refinement, not an abandonment, of the non-responsibility constraint
1.McMahan 2009: minimally responsible threateners (e.g. coerced unjust soldiers) retain a diminished liability; killing them may be permissible under specific proportionality conditions. This differs from Otsuka’s strict 1994 formulation.
2.But the 2009 view preserves the core insight: liability comes in degrees proportional to moral responsibility; the genuinely non-responsible threat (Falling Man) remains at zero liability — the hardest case on the spectrum.
3.The 2009 revision does not entail that Falling Man may be killed; it entails only that between non-responsible and fully responsible threats lies a calibrated range of diminished liability. Falling Man is not in that range.
∴McMahan’s evolution of view is a refinement of the non-responsibility constraint, not its abandonment; the genuinely non-responsible threatener remains at zero liability and the Moral Equivalence Thesis stands for this case.
Argument IV — Against Frowe: material responsibility cannot ground liability to be killed
1.Frowe: innocent threats bear “material responsibility” for the danger their bodies pose, distinguishing them from bystanders and grounding a permission to kill them.
2.Material responsibility is causal implication, not moral responsibility. The question is whether causal implication alone — in the complete absence of agentive input — can reduce a person’s immunity to being killed. It cannot: a person thrown through a window does not forfeit her right not to be shot by the homeowner whose wall she has breached.
3.Rights and immunity are forfeited by what one does in a morally responsible sense; they cannot be forfeited by what one’s body does while one exercises no agency whatsoever.
∴Material responsibility without moral agency cannot ground liability to defensive killing; Frowe’s distinction between threats and bystanders rests on a morally inert causal difference rather than the agent-involving property that makes liability intelligible.

I want to show why the Moral Equivalence Thesis matters far beyond the individual cases in which philosophers have debated it.

In War and Self-Defense I argue that the received theory of national self-defence is built on an unstable foundation, and that Otsuka’s thesis helps to reveal why. The standard justification for wars of national defence asserts that a state’s soldiers may kill enemy soldiers because enemy soldiers threaten the defending state’s people — just as an individual may kill an attacker who threatens her life. But this analogy fails, and the failure is illuminated by the innocent threat problem.

“Otsuka’s position — that one cannot distinguish a falling innocent threat from a bystander whom one kills to save one's own life — is the primary philosophical challenge to the standard view that Victim may kill innocent threats. If this position is correct, it has radical implications not only for individual self-defence but for the entire structure of the morality of war.” — Rodin, 2002
i. fault is necessary for the right of self-defence

Fault necessity — self-defence requires fault in the attacker; innocent threat has noneI argue for what I call the “resilience of responsibility”: the right to kill in self-defence is grounded in the attacker’s fault. A culpable attacker disturbs the normative relationship between herself and the victim by culpably violating her duty not to threaten the victim’s life. This disturbance forfeits her right not to be killed. Nothing analogous occurs in the case of the innocent threat: Falling Man has committed no wrong, violated no duty, disturbed no normative relationship. He has simply fallen. The traditional intuition that self-defence is justified because the attacker has done something wrong — something that makes her liable — is, I argue, correct. Stripping away fault does not leave the right of self-defence intact. It dissolves it.

ii. the implication for national self-defence

National self-defence — unjust soldiers may be innocent threats; if not killable, war of national defence is illicitThe implications are radical. Most soldiers who fight unjust wars are, from the perspective of their own psychology, innocent: they have been conscripted or deceived, fight under legitimate-seeming orders, and genuinely believe they are defending their country. If we apply Otsuka’s thesis, such soldiers are innocent aggressors or innocent threats at best. If innocent threats may not permissibly be killed, then the soldiers of a defending nation may not permissibly kill the soldiers of an invading nation if those soldiers are genuinely innocent. The right of national self-defence, as standardly conceived, would collapse for precisely the cases where innocent soldiers on the unjust side form the bulk of the enemy. This is not a reductio of the Moral Equivalence Thesis. It is what follows from taking it seriously.

iii. against the causal-involvement response

Causal involvement — Thomson-style response fails; being causally implicated does not restore faultThomson and others who permit killing innocent threats typically do so on the ground that the threat is causally implicated in the sequence that will kill the victim. I deny that causal implication is sufficient. The question before us is whether Victim has the right to kill in self-defence — not merely the permission. Rights are not grounded in causal chains; they are grounded in normative relationships between persons. If Falling Man has done nothing to disturb his normative relationship with Victim — if he has exercised no agency, taken no risk, made no choice — then no right-to-kill arises on Victim’s side. Victim may have an excuse for killing Falling Man; she does not have a right to do so. This distinction matters enormously for the ethics of war, where we are interested in whether soldiers fight with right, not merely whether they can be excused.

iv. self-defence and the limits of individual morality

Limits of self-defence — self-defence is not an open-ended permission; justified only against those who bear responsibilityI want to resist the suggestion that self-defence is a primitive, pre-moral right of any organism to preserve itself. The moral right of self-defence is a specific right that arises in specific circumstances. Those circumstances include, at minimum, the fact that the person threatening you bears some form of responsibility for that threat. Where that condition is not met, self-defence does not apply. We may have other moral resources — lesser-evil reasoning, for instance — but these are weaker and more constrained. They do not generate the robust permission to kill that “self-defence” implies. Otsuka is right that the innocent threat may not be killed in self-defence. Whether she may be killed in anything else is a further question that calls for more careful argument than the simple assertion that self-preservation justifies itself.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Fault is necessary for the right of self-defence: the resilience of responsibility
1.The right to kill in self-defence is grounded in the attacker’s fault: by culpably violating her duty not to threaten the victim, the attacker disturbs their normative relationship and forfeits her right not to be killed.
2.Falling Man has committed no wrong, violated no duty, and disturbed no normative relationship. He has simply fallen. The condition that grounds the right of self-defence is absent.
3.Stripping away fault does not leave the right of self-defence intact but diminished; it dissolves it entirely. There is no attenuated right of self-defence against the entirely innocent.
∴Fault is a necessary condition for the right of self-defence: where the threatening person has no fault or responsibility for the threat, no right to kill arises on the victim’s side, even if a lesser-evil permission might exist.
Argument II — The Moral Equivalence Thesis has radical implications for national self-defence
1.Many soldiers fighting unjust wars are genuinely innocent: conscripted, deceived, or acting under seemingly legitimate orders in reasonable belief that they defend their country.
2.If innocent threats cannot permissibly be killed, these soldiers cannot permissibly be killed by defending forces — which means the standard right of national self-defence is unavailable against the cases that typically constitute an invading army.
3.This is not a reductio of the Moral Equivalence Thesis but its implication: taking seriously the requirement that killing be grounded in responsibility has radical and largely unacknowledged consequences for the received ethics of war.
∴The Moral Equivalence Thesis, applied consistently, undermines the standard justification for wars of national defence; this reveals that the received ethics of war is built on a morally unsustainable foundation.
Argument III — Causal involvement without fault does not ground a right to kill
1.Thomson: Falling Man’s causal involvement in the threat sequence distinguishes him from a bystander and generates a permission to kill him. Rights are forfeited by being about to violate another’s rights, regardless of fault.
2.But rights are grounded in normative relationships between persons, not in causal chains. Falling Man has done nothing to disturb his normative relationship with Victim; causal implication without agentive input does not restore what fault-based responsibility provides.
3.Victim may have an excuse for killing Falling Man if she does so; she does not have a right to do so. Excuses do not transfer to others; rights do. This distinction matters enormously for the ethics of war.
∴Thomson’s causal-involvement response conflates having an excuse for killing with having a right to kill; causal implication without fault generates at most an excuse, not the impersonal right that the standard ethics of self-defence and war requires.
Argument IV — The right of self-defence is not a primitive permission to preserve oneself at any cost
1.If self-defence were simply a pre-moral permission for any organism to preserve itself, it would apply equally against genuinely non-responsible threats; but this would make the category of “right of self-defence” morally vacuous.
2.The moral right of self-defence arises in specific circumstances that include, at minimum, some form of responsibility on the threatening person’s part. Where that condition fails, we must rely on weaker and more constrained moral resources — lesser-evil, necessity — which do not generate a robust permission to kill.
3.Self-preservation does not justify itself: the question is always whether the conditions that ground a moral permission to kill are satisfied. Against a non-responsible threat, those conditions are not satisfied.
∴The right of self-defence is not a primitive self-preservation permission but a structured moral right with necessary conditions; those conditions include some form of responsibility in the threatening person, which the innocent threat lacks entirely.

My position is nuanced, and I want to be precise about where I agree with Otsuka and where I depart from him — and what the departure amounts to.

I agree with Otsuka’s central conclusion: the physical movement of a body through space, in the absence of any agentive input by the person whose body it is, cannot constitute the kind of thing that forfeits a person’s right to life. Falling Man’s body is falling; Falling Man is not doing anything. The distinction between what a person does and what happens to her body is morally fundamental. If we lose it, we lose the distinction between persons and natural hazards — between being killed in self-defence by a murderer and being struck by lightning.

“It is sometimes permissible to harm people who have a duty to bear certain costs in order to protect others, when they are unable or unwilling to fulfil that duty. This applies to some non-responsible threats who have a duty to sacrifice themselves. But the duty must exist. And a duty requires more than bare causal implication.” — Tadros, 2011
i. bodily movements are not actions: the agency distinction

Agency distinction — bodily movement ≠ action; Falling Man is not doing anythingA physical movement of a body through space is not, without further qualification, an action. An action is an event controlled and initiated by an agent. When Falling Man falls, his body moves, but Falling Man does not act. The distinction is not merely semantic: it is the distinction between what a person does and what happens to her. Liability, in deontological ethics, attaches to what a person does — to the exercises of agency, the choices, the risks undertaken. It does not attach to events that merely involve a person’s body. Therefore, Falling Man’s physical descent toward you, over which he has no control and in which he exercises no agency, cannot be the kind of event that reduces his immunity to being killed. His body threatens you. He does not. The two are morally distinct, and it is the latter that is relevant to liability.

ii. the duty route: a refinement on otsuka’s position

Duty route — innocent threat may have duty to sacrifice; if so, enforceable; if not, may not be killedHere I depart from Otsuka slightly, while arriving at a similar destination. I argue that it is sometimes permissible to harm an innocent person who has an enforceable duty to bear certain costs in order to protect others. This is different from the liability view: the person is not liable because she has done something to forfeit her rights; she has a duty to bear costs because she is the source of a threat and, in some cases, her bearing those costs is what others are owed as a matter of moral protection. Now: does Falling Man have such a duty? Does he have a duty to sacrifice himself — to allow himself to be killed — in order to save you? In the paradigm case, I think the answer is no. Falling Man has been thrown. He has done nothing. No causal role he has voluntarily assumed, no risk he has taken, gives him a duty that could be enforced by your ray gun. Therefore, even on my more permissive duty-route, killing Falling Man remains impermissible.

iii. where otsuka’s account might be too strict

Where Otsuka may be too strict — voluntary risk-takers may bear duty even without full liabilityMy account makes space for cases where Otsuka’s strict view might be too demanding. Consider a person who, through voluntary activity in the vicinity of a cliff, places herself at risk of falling and becoming an innocent threat to those below. She has not acted wrongly; she has not chosen to threaten anyone. But she has voluntarily assumed a risk of becoming an instrument of harm. I am inclined to think that in such a case, the person may have a weak duty to bear the cost of her own death rather than allow that risk to materialise as a threat to others — because she voluntarily placed herself in a position where that risk was foreseeable. This is not full liability; it is a weaker moral relationship grounded in voluntary assumption of risk. It permits some weighting in the victim’s favour that Otsuka’s strict Moral Equivalence Thesis might not allow.

iv. against quong: the means/ends structure does not settle the case against otsuka

Against Quong — eliminative vs opportunistic distinction doesn’t exhaust the moral structure; duty matters independentlyQuong will argue that killing an innocent threat is eliminative rather than opportunistic: you kill her to eliminate the threat she constitutes, not to exploit her body as a strategic resource. Quong thinks this distinction morally significant and sufficient to permit killing innocent threats in self-defence. I contest this. Even if the killing is purely eliminative — even if Falling Man’s death is not being used instrumentally but is simply the inevitable result of removing the threat he constitutes — this does not settle whether the killing is permissible. Permissibility cannot be read off the means/ends structure of the killing alone; it requires asking whether there is a sufficient justification for the killing. And that justification must come from somewhere: liability, duty, or lesser-evil. In the paradigm case of Falling Man, none of these suffices. The eliminative character of the killing reduces its moral weight somewhat, but does not remove the fundamental question of justification.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The agency distinction: bodily movement is not action; Falling Man is not doing anything
1.An action is an event controlled and initiated by an agent. A physical movement of a body without agentive input is not an action but an event that merely involves a person’s body.

I want to put Otsuka’s position under pressure from an angle he has not adequately addressed: the structure of rights themselves.

Otsuka builds his case on the idea that because the innocent threat lacks responsible lethal agency, her right not to be killed remains fully intact — and that killing her would be on a par with the worst kind of bystander killing. I want to challenge the premise that a right not to be killed is the kind of right that can remain fully intact while the person who holds it is in the process of killing you.

Thomson’s Yes Cases and No Cases.

Yes (may kill): Villainous Aggressor (culpable, intentional threat); Innocent Aggressor (unintentional but agentive threat); Innocent Threat (no agency at all — e.g. Falling Man). Thomson argues all three are permissible to kill in self-defence.

No (may not kill): Bystander (not in the threat sequence at all). Thomson argues that a bystander who has no causal connection to the threat retains her right not to be killed fully.

The key difference: In all Yes cases, the person will kill you if you do nothing. In all No cases, the person will not kill you if you do nothing. Thomson’s view: whether the person is about to kill you — not whether she is at fault for this — is what determines whether she forfeits her right.
i. rights and their forfeiture: the structure of the claim

Rights forfeiture — forfeiture triggered by threatening to violate another’s rights, not by faultA right, on my account, is a moral constraint on others’ behaviour. Every person has a right against every other that they not be killed. This right can be forfeited. I claim it is forfeited precisely when the person holding it is about to violate a corresponding right in someone else. Falling Man is about to violate your right not to be killed. His doing so will be entirely without his agency or fault; the Villain bears responsibility for this. But the moral constraint that the right generates on Victim’s behaviour is a constraint that runs in the other direction: Falling Man’s right against Victim that she not kill him. That constraint is weakened — the right forfeited — not because Falling Man has done anything culpable but because the situation places him in the role of one who will kill you if you do nothing. Otsuka objects that forfeiture must be grounded in responsible agency. But this presupposes a particular theory of rights-forfeiture that I contest.

ii. fault makes no moral difference: the overrating argument

Fault is irrelevant — we overrate fault’s role in self-defence; what matters is whether you will be killedConsider the series: Villainous Aggressor (full culpability), Innocent Aggressor (no culpability but some agentive control), Innocent Threat (no culpability, no agency). I claim there is no moral difference between these cases as far as the permissibility of defensive killing is concerned. In each case, if you do nothing, you die. In each case, the other person is the immediate instrument of your death. The differences between the cases — the presence or absence of fault and agency — are morally significant in many other ways: they determine who is punishable, who owes compensation, who bears moral responsibility for the overall situation. But they do not determine whether Victim may defend herself. As I wrote in 1991: I fancy we overrate the role of fault in many areas of moral theory. Self-defence is one of them.

iii. the bystander distinction is the key asymmetry

Bystander distinction — bystanders not in threat sequence; threats are; this causal difference is morally decisiveOtsuka argues that the morally relevant property is responsible lethal agency, which is absent in both Bystander and Falling Man. I argue that the morally relevant property is causal involvement in the threat sequence — which is present in all three Yes cases (Villainous Aggressor, Innocent Aggressor, Innocent Threat) and absent in the No case (Bystander). Bystander will not kill you if you do nothing. Falling Man will kill you if you do nothing. This difference is decisive. It is not merely a causal fact; it is a normative fact: Falling Man’s body is in the process of violating your right not to be killed, whether or not Falling Man bears responsibility for this. Bystander’s body is not. That is the morally operative distinction, and Otsuka has misidentified it.

Martyrdom — Otsuka requires you to die; this is a devastating cost that his theory cannot absorbOtsuka’s conclusion requires you to lie back and allow Falling Man to crush you to death rather than vaporise him. This is a demand of martyrdom — a demand that you sacrifice your life for the life of someone who (through no fault of either of you) is about to take it. I find this demand morally insupportable. The purpose of the right of self-defence is precisely to protect persons from this kind of situation: from being required to die when they could prevent their own death. Otsuka’s account, by extending full bystander immunity to Falling Man, effectively gives Falling Man’s right to life priority over Victim’s — with no justification drawn from anything Falling Man has done. That cannot be right. The symmetry of the situation — two wholly innocent persons, one of whom will die — argues for a permission to defend, not a requirement to submit.

I want to press the most precise and, I believe, decisive challenge to Otsuka’s argument.

Otsuka claims that killing an innocent threat is on a moral par with the worst kind of bystander killing. I want to show that this comparison is flawed: that killing an innocent threat and killing a bystander are structurally different kinds of action, and that the difference has direct moral significance. In making this argument I will draw on Warren Quinn’s distinction between eliminative and opportunistic harmful agency.

“When you kill a bystander to save yourself from a threat, you use that person — her body, her presence — as a strategic resource. When you kill an innocent threat, you do no such thing. You merely remove the threat she constitutes. The difference between using a person and removing a threat is not morally trivial.” — Quong, 2009
i. eliminative vs opportunistic harmful agency

Eliminative vs opportunistic — threat’s body constitutes the threat; bystander’s body is merely exploitedWarren Quinn distinguished two modes of harmful agency: eliminative and opportunistic. In opportunistic harmful agency, the victim’s body or presence is being used as a strategic tool — the harm to her is instrumentally connected to the benefit you seek. In eliminative harmful agency, the victim constitutes the very source of the threat you face, and killing her removes the threat without exploiting anything about her beyond the fact that she is the threat. The standard bystander killing is opportunistic: you pull Bystander into the path of the javelin because her body will stop it — you are exploiting her presence as a shield. Killing Falling Man is eliminative: his body just is the threat that will kill you; eliminating him removes the threat. This is a morally significant structural difference. Even the Doctrine of Double Effect, which is sensitive to how an agent relates to the harms she causes, recognises that instrumental use of a person’s body is more objectionable than causing death as a side effect of removing a threat.

ii. otsuka’s argument conflates killing as a means with treating a person as a means

Conflation — killing as means vs treating person as means; Otsuka runs them togetherOtsuka argues that killing Falling Man is comparable to the worst kind of bystander killing because in both cases you are initiating a lethal sequence for your own benefit. But Otsuka runs together two importantly different notions: killing as a means, and treating the person herself as a means. For a killing to be a use of the person as a means, there must be something instrumental about the person’s death — her death must be what produces the benefit, by virtue of some feature of her beyond the mere fact that she is the threat. When you kill Bystander to stop the javelin, you are using her body as a shield; her physical presence is the instrument of your survival. When you vaporise Falling Man, his death is not instrumental in this way: what saves you is not that he has died but that the threat he constituted has been removed. You are not exploiting him; you are removing the threat he constitutes. Otsuka has conflated these.

iii. the agent-relative permission: self-preference in symmetric cases

Agent-relative permission — Victim may prefer her own life; symmetric innocence doesn’t require submissionEven if we grant that both Victim and Falling Man are wholly innocent, this symmetry does not entail that Victim must accept death. I argue that persons have an agent-relative permission to give some priority to their own life over that of others. This permission is not unlimited: it does not permit opportunistic killing of bystanders or the exploitation of innocents as strategic resources. But it does permit Victim to kill Falling Man when he constitutes the threat to her life and his death would remove that threat — in other words, when the killing is eliminative rather than opportunistic. The symmetric innocence of the parties is not a tiebreaker that falls in favour of the person who will kill you; it is a context in which the agent-relative permission to defend oneself operates without special constraint.

iv. the bystander case is genuinely different: why killing bystanders remains impermissible

Bystander case preserved — bystanders are opportunistically used; threats are eliminatively removed; no slippery slopeOtsuka worries that permitting the killing of innocent threats gives us no principled basis for prohibiting the killing of innocent bystanders. I deny this. The prohibition on killing bystanders rests on the prohibition against using persons as means — the Kantian principle that one may not treat a person merely as an instrument for one’s own ends. When you pull Bystander into the path of the javelin, you are treating her body as a tool. Her death is the instrument of your survival. You are using her. This use is precisely what is prohibited. Killing Falling Man involves no such use. You are not exploiting his body; you are removing the threat it constitutes. The distinction is principled, not arbitrary, and it does not open the door to bystander killing.

I want to expose a structural error in Otsuka’s argument and then propose a positive account of why innocent threats may permissibly be killed.

Otsuka claims that the moral property which explains why we may not kill bystanders is lack of responsible lethal agency — a property shared by both bystanders and innocent threats. He concludes from this shared property that innocent threats and bystanders are on a moral par. But this argument moves too quickly. It assumes that the only morally relevant difference between bystanders and threats is the causal one: that threats are in the sequence and bystanders are not. If we remove that difference as morally inert (as Otsuka does), nothing distinguishes them. But Otsuka’s diagnosis of why the causal difference is morally inert is wrong. There is a morally significant property that distinguishes innocent threats from bystanders that Otsuka’s account cannot capture: material responsibility for the threat.

“The idea of a responsible bystander is not only unattractive, but conceptually incoherent. Bystanders cannot be morally responsible for the threat, because they pose no threat at all. The threat is what distinguishes a threat from a bystander — and that distinction carries moral weight even in the absence of culpability.” — Frowe, 2014
i. otsuka’s sleight of hand: killing as means vs killing a person as means

Sleight of hand — Otsuka shifts between killing as means and treating person as means mid-argumentOtsuka discusses two types of bystander killing: killing the bystander as a side-effect of saving oneself, and killing the bystander in order to use her body or presence as a tool. He claims that killing an innocent threat is analogous to the second and worst type. But there is a sleight of hand here. For a killing to be the “useful destruction” of a person — for it to be a case of using someone as a means — the person must be instrumental in the threat to Victim’s life in some strategic way. A permission to kill a person as a means will be a permission to kill persons who are instrumental in the threat. Falling Man is the threat. He is not instrumental to some other threat. He is not being exploited. He just is what must be removed. Otsuka’s comparison to the worst kind of bystander killing requires that we treat eliminating a threat as equivalent to exploiting a person as a tool. These are not equivalent.

ii. material responsibility: threats are not bystanders

Material responsibility — threats pose threat; bystanders pose no threat; distinction carries moral weightThe concept of a responsible bystander is, I argue, not merely unattractive but conceptually incoherent. A bystander is by definition someone who poses no threat to Victim. A bystander cannot be morally responsible for the threat because there is no threat of theirs to be responsible for. An innocent threat is not a bystander in this sense: she is the source of the lethal threat, even if she exercises no moral agency over this fact. I call this material responsibility: the threat bears a unique causal and material relationship to Victim’s peril that the bystander does not. This material relationship is not the same as moral responsibility, and it does not ground liability in the full sense. But it does distinguish the threat from the bystander in a way that is morally relevant: it means that in killing the threat, Victim is removing a source of peril; in killing the bystander, Victim is imposing a peril on someone who has nothing to do with it.

iii. what material responsibility grounds

What material responsibility grounds — diminished immunity, not full liability; weaker permission to killMaterial responsibility does not ground the same degree of liability as full moral responsibility. I accept this. A person who is materially but not morally responsible for a threat is not as liable to be killed as a culpable attacker. Her immunity to defensive killing is diminished rather than removed. This means, practically, that killing her requires a higher necessity condition and that the cost the victim must absorb before killing becomes permissible is higher. Victim must bear more cost to avoid killing a merely materially responsible threat than to avoid killing a culpable one. But when that cost reaches a level that makes the killing necessary and proportionate, the killing is permissible. In the Falling Man case, where Victim faces certain death, the necessity and proportionality conditions are met.

That she did not choose to be in this position is a fact about her culpability, not about her material relationship to the threat. She materially constitutes the danger in a way that a bystander standing beside the track does not. My account predicts that the costs to Victim before killing becomes permissible are higher in her case than in the case of a culpable threat — but not infinitely higher. If killing her is the only way to save Victim’s life, it may well be permissible.

I owe the symposium an explanation of why I changed my mind, and what my 2009 position actually commits me to.

In 1994 I argued, alongside Otsuka, that the truly non-responsible threatener cannot be permissibly killed in self-defence. In my 2005 paper I explicitly endorsed Otsuka’s formulation as superior to my original one. By 2009, in Killing in War, my view had shifted. I had come to believe that Otsuka’s line is drawn too strictly, and that a calibrated model of liability — one in which liability tracks moral responsibility in degrees rather than in a binary way — better captures our considered judgements about the full range of cases in self-defence and war. Let me explain the shift.

The Calibrated Liability Model (2009). Liability comes in degrees proportional to moral responsibility for the unjust threat. The more morally responsible a person is for posing an unjust threat to another, the more liable she is to defensive force and the broader the range of responses that are narrowly proportionate.

A fully culpable aggressor is fully liable: Victim may use any proportionate force.
A minimally responsible threat (e.g. a coerced unjust soldier) is minimally liable: defensive force is permissible but only narrowly proportionate — a lesser harm may be inflicted.
A genuinely non-responsible threat has zero liability: no defensive force can be justified on the liability route. But the lesser-evil route remains available, and in multi-person cases where the numbers are significant, it may generate a permission to kill.
i. why otsuka’s line is drawn too strictly

Too strict — non-responsible vs minimally responsible conflated; soldier cases show the gradient mattersOtsuka restricts his 1994 argument to genuinely non-responsible threateners — those who pose threats with literally no agentive input. This restriction makes his argument more precise than my original formulation. ii. the non-responsible threat: my 2009 account

2009 on non-responsible threats — lesser-evil route available; numbers matter; not absolute immunityI want to be precise about what my 2009 view says about the genuinely non-responsible threatener. I accept that on the liability route, she cannot be killed: she is not morally responsible and therefore not liable. But I no longer accept that this closes all routes to permissible killing. The lesser-evil route remains open. In the paradigmatic one-on-one Falling Man case, the lesser-evil route fails because there is no good aggregate reason to prefer Victim’s life over Falling Man’s: they are both wholly innocent, and neither’s death is a lesser evil than the other’s simply because one party would otherwise survive. But where the numbers are significantly different — where one person’s death would avert the deaths of several others, or where collateral damage to a non-responsible threat is an unavoidable consequence of averting a large-scale catastrophe — the lesser-evil route may well generate a permission to kill even the non-responsible. Otsuka’s framework has difficulty accommodating this.

iii. the problem of the innocent soldier

Innocent soldier problem — if non-responsible soldiers are immune, just war is impossible; better to calibrateThe most powerful argument against Otsuka’s strict position comes from the ethics of war. If genuinely non-responsible threats may never be permissibly killed on the liability route, and if the lesser-evil route requires substantially favourable numbers to justify killing, then wars of national self-defence conducted against armies of largely innocent soldiers become close to impermissible. This seems wrong. My 2009 calibrated model offers a better account: minimally responsible soldiers bear diminished liability; this liability, combined with the lesser-evil considerations that massive threats to civilian populations generate, makes their defensive killing permissible — but only under conditions of narrow proportionality that are more stringent than those governing the killing of culpable aggressors. This is a more nuanced and more accurate picture than Otsuka’s binary immunity framework allows.

iv. what i retain from my 1994 position

Retained from 1994 — non-responsibility remains the hardest case; liability is not zero for all unjust threatsI want to be clear about what I retain from my earlier position. I still believe that moral responsibility is the primary ground of liability to defensive killing. I still believe that fully non-responsible threats generate zero liability and may not be killed on that route alone. I still believe that Otsuka’s formulation of the 1994 argument was superior to mine. What I have changed is my view about (a) the prevalence of genuinely non-responsible threats in practice — most are minimally responsible — and (b) the availability and conditions of the lesser-evil route, which Otsuka’s framework underestimates. In the pure one-on-one Falling Man case, Otsuka and I remain close: I would not permit the killing on the liability route, and I would permit it on the lesser-evil route only under conditions that the paradigm case does not satisfy. But as the cases become more complex — as numbers mount, as degrees of responsibility become more important — my account diverges increasingly from his.

Argument I — Otsuka’s binary immunity framework is too strict: liability tracks responsibility in degrees
1.Otsuka: only the genuinely non-responsible threat has full immunity; moral responsibility is a binary condition of liability. Persons are either responsible (and potentially liable) or non-responsible (and fully immune).
2.McMahan 2009: liability tracks moral responsibility in degrees. A minimally responsible person is minimally liable; a fully culpable person is fully liable. Between zero and full responsibility, liability is diminished but not zero.
3.Many practically important cases — coerced unjust soldiers, persons acting under false information — involve minimal rather than zero moral responsibility. Treating them as fully immune (as Otsuka’s strict view requires) generates an implausible result: they cannot be permissibly killed even when necessary to prevent massive harm.
∴The calibrated liability model better captures the full range of cases: liability comes in degrees proportional to moral responsibility, and even minimal responsibility generates some (diminished) liability to proportionate defensive force.
