Mr Speaker,

The central question of this debate is whether inequality is non-instrumentally bad — bad as such, not merely because of what it does to welfare levels. I believe it is, and I want to defend that belief against the prioritarian challenge that Parfit has mounted with characteristic force.

Parfit argues that what matters is not whether people are worse off than others but how badly off they are in absolute terms; we should give extra moral weight to benefits conferred on those who are badly off, not because inequality is bad but because those people matter more. I think this mislocates the source of our concern. When I contemplate a world in which half the population lives in splendour while the other half scrapes by — not through any choice of their own, not through any differential effort or desert, but simply through the lottery of birth — I find something objectionable in the distribution itself, not merely in the absolute position of the bottom half.

“I believe that inequality is bad — that a world in which some people are worse off than others through no fault of their own is, as such and for that reason, a worse world than one in which they are equally well off. This is not merely a claim about welfare levels; it is a claim about how people stand in relation to one another.”
i. the non-instrumental badness of inequality

Non-instrumental badness — inequality bad as relational fact, not only as welfare deficitConsider two worlds: in the first, everyone is at welfare level 50. In the second, half the population is at 80 and the other half at 20 — same average, higher total if we are strict totalists. The prioritarian says the second world might be better or worse depending on how we weight the gains and losses at different levels; the egalitarian says there is something about the relational structure itself that makes it worse. I believe the egalitarian is right. The people at 20 are not merely badly off; they are worse off than their fellow citizens through no fault of their own, and that comparative fact is itself a moral defect in the world. Parfit's prioritarian view cannot represent this relational dimension of justice because it evaluates each person's position in isolation, assigning weights to welfare levels without reference to anyone else's level.

ii. the levelling down objection and its limits

Levelling down — reveals genuine evaluative tension but does not refute non-instrumental badness of inequalityThe strongest objection to my view is the Levelling Down Objection: if inequality is bad in itself, then levelling down — making the well-off worse off without improving anyone else's position — should improve the world in at least one respect. But surely making everyone worse off improves nothing. This objection has genuine force, and I accept it reveals a tension in our evaluative commitments. But I do not think it refutes the non-instrumental badness of inequality. What it shows is that inequality is one bad-making feature among several; levelling down eliminates it at the cost of creating other bad-making features. A world with equality at level 20 is better in one respect than one with inequality at 80/20, even if it is worse all-things-considered. Our evaluative practices are pluralistic; recognising that a state has one good-making and several bad-making features is not incoherent.

iii. why prioritarianism cannot substitute for egalitarianism

Against prioritarianism — personal welfare weights abstract away from relational structure that grounds egalitarian complaintPrioritarianism assigns greater moral weight to welfare increments that accrue to those who are absolutely worse off. It is sensitive to absolute position but not to comparative position. This means it cannot generate a complaint against a society in which all members are at welfare level 80 but deeply unequal in resources, social standing, and political power — because all are well-off in absolute terms. And it cannot distinguish, morally, between a society where the bottom quintile is at 20 and those above them are at 21, from a society where they are at 20 and those above them are at 10,000. In both cases the bottom quintile is equally badly-off in absolute terms; the prioritarian assigns them equal moral priority. But the relational structures are profoundly different, and justice is sensitive to that difference. Egalitarianism captures it; prioritarianism does not.

iv. the transitivity of our egalitarian commitments

Transitivity problem — egalitarian intuitions may be intransitive; this reveals complexity in value, not error in egalitarianismI have argued elsewhere that our egalitarian intuitions may not be transitive — that the all-affected principle and the best-off person principle generate inconsistent rankings across some triplets of populations. I maintain this position and find it revealing rather than embarrassing. What it reveals is that the concept of inequality is genuinely complex: it admits of multiple interpretations, each of which captures something real, and these interpretations can conflict. The appropriate response is not to abandon the non-instrumental badness of inequality but to develop a more nuanced theory of what inequality consists in and how its different aspects trade off against one another. The paradoxes are a research programme, not a refutation.

2.The comparative fact — that some are worse off than others through no fault of their own — is itself a moral defect in the world, not reducible to the absolute positions of individuals.
3.Prioritarianism evaluates each person's position in isolation and cannot represent this relational dimension; it therefore misses something that a complete theory of distributive justice must capture.
∴Inequality is non-instrumentally bad: the relational structure of a distribution is itself a moral datum, not merely a symptom of individual welfare deficits, and egalitarianism is required to represent it.

My contribution to this debate is to provide contractualism as the grounding for egalitarian obligations — and to explain why Rawls's Difference Principle, despite its contractualist structure, does not go far enough.

The fundamental question is: what makes a principle of distribution morally binding? My answer is that principles are binding when no one can reasonably reject them. This contractualist criterion has direct implications for distributional justice that diverge from both Rawlsian maximin and Parfitian prioritarianism. It generates egalitarian constraints that neither of those views can fully explain.

“An act is wrong if its performance under the circumstances would be disallowed by any set of principles that no one could reasonably reject insofar as he or she was moved to find principles for general agreement. What makes a principle of distribution just is not that it maximises a weighting function but that it could be justified to each person it governs.”
i. the contractualist grounding of distributive obligation

Contractualism — principles must be justifiable to each individual; aggregation fails this testPrioritarianism sums weighted welfare across persons. Whatever weight it assigns to the worse-off, it remains in principle willing to impose serious losses on individuals for sufficiently large aggregate weighted gains. This cannot be justified to the people who bear those losses: they can reasonably object to being sacrificed for the aggregate, no matter how much the aggregate is weighted. The contractualist structure explains why: the unit of moral justification is the person, not the aggregate. Any principle that overrides a strong individual complaint in favour of aggregate welfare fails the contractualist test, regardless of the weights assigned. Rawls understood this better than Parfit, which is why his original position generates lexical priority rules rather than weighted sums. But Rawls applies contractualism only to the structure of basic institutions, not to the full range of distributive choices. I want to press the contractualist logic further.

ii. why the difference principle is insufficient

Against Rawls — Difference Principle permits inequalities that no reasonable egalitarian can acceptThe Difference Principle permits inequalities in primary goods provided they benefit the least-advantaged group. This is a significant departure from strict equality, justified by Rawls on the grounds that the worst-off representative individual would choose to permit inequalities that raise their absolute position. But this reasoning is too aggregative at the level of the representative person: it trades the relational complaint of equality — that some people are subordinated to others — for a promise of absolute improvement. The egalitarian's complaint is not merely that the worst-off have too few primary goods; it is that the structure of society places them in a dependent, subordinated position relative to those above them. Contractualism, properly applied, generates a stronger demand for equality than the Difference Principle delivers.

iii. the interpersonal justification of equality

Interpersonal justification — equality uniquely satisfies the demand to be justifiable to each; inequality cannot beWhy is equality specially connected to the contractualist demand for justifiability? Because equality is the unique distribution that can be justified to each person from their own standpoint without reference to aggregate benefits. If I receive as much as you, there is nothing I can complain about from my own perspective; the comparison is the justification. But any departure from equality requires justification to those who receive less: we must explain to them why they should accept less than others. Prioritarianism proposes to justify this by pointing to the absolute goodness of their position; contractualism says this misses the point. What must be justified is not merely how well off they are but why they are less well off than others. This is a fundamentally relational demand that only egalitarian principles can satisfy.

iv. aggregation across persons and the separateness of persons

Separateness of persons — contractualism blocks aggregation that prioritarianism permits; genuine egalitarian constraintPrioritarianism aggregates weighted welfare. This means it can in principle recommend trade-offs in which many small benefits to the moderately worse-off outweigh severe losses to a few individuals, provided the weighting function makes the arithmetic come out right. Contractualism blocks this: each individual retains the right to reject principles that treat their interests as merely one input into an aggregate calculation. This is not an absolute veto — reasonable rejection requires that the objection be stronger than countervailing claims — but it does generate constraints that no purely aggregative view can replicate. These constraints are egalitarian in structure: they protect individuals from being sacrificed for the aggregate in a way that prioritarian weighting cannot guarantee.

1.A principle of distribution is morally binding iff no one can reasonably reject it. The unit of justification is the individual, not the aggregate.
2.Prioritarianism in principle permits overriding serious individual losses for aggregate weighted gains; individuals bearing those losses can reasonably reject any such principle.
3.Any principle that fails individual justifiability fails the contractualist test regardless of how it performs on aggregate welfare metrics.
∴Contractualism generates egalitarian constraints that prioritarianism cannot deliver: the standard of individual justifiability blocks sacrifices for aggregate benefit that prioritarianism permits.
Argument II — The Difference Principle is insufficient: it permits subordinating inequalities
1.The Difference Principle allows inequalities in primary goods that benefit the least-advantaged group in absolute terms, even if they establish social hierarchies of dependence and subordination.
2.The egalitarian complaint is not merely about absolute position but about relational subordination; the Difference Principle trades relational equality for absolute improvement, which no reasonable egalitarian must accept.
3.Contractualism, properly applied to the full range of distributive choices (not just institutional structure), generates stronger egalitarian demands than the Difference Principle.
∴The Difference Principle is too weak: it fails to satisfy the contractualist demand for justifiability at the relational level; egalitarianism requires more than maximin primary goods.
Argument III — Equality uniquely satisfies the interpersonal demand for justification
1.Equal distribution can be justified to each person from their own standpoint: receiving as much as others requires no further justification, since the comparison itself is the justification.
2.Any departure from equality requires justification to those who receive less — not merely pointing to their absolute welfare level but explaining why they should accept a lesser share.
3.Prioritarianism cannot provide this justification: it points only to absolute position, not to the comparative relation that grounds the complaint.

I want to sharpen the debate around responsibility — to show that responsibility matters to egalitarianism in a way that Arneson's prioritarian reformulation cannot fully capture, and that taking responsibility seriously leads us toward, not away from, genuine egalitarianism.

Arneson argues that responsibility-catering prioritarianism — giving extra weight to the worse-off except where they are responsible for their position — is superior to responsibility-sensitive egalitarianism because it avoids the harshness of leaving the imprudent to suffer. I want to challenge both parts of this argument: the claim that luck egalitarianism is excessively harsh, and the claim that prioritarianism with a responsibility caveat is a coherent alternative.

The Luck-Choice Distinction.

Brute luck: outcomes that befall us through no voluntary risk-taking — genetic conditions, family of birth, natural disasters, social structures not of our making.

Option luck: outcomes of deliberate gambles where the agent knowingly takes a risk. If the risk goes badly, the outcome is traceable to an exercise of genuine choice.

The egalitarian aim: eliminate disadvantages arising from brute luck; hold agents responsible for disadvantages arising from option luck. The difficulty is determining which disadvantages fall into which category — an empirical and philosophical task that neither luck egalitarianism nor prioritarianism can avoid.
i. the luck-choice distinction is irreducible to welfare levels

Luck-choice distinction — morally irreducible; cannot be captured by absolute welfare weights aloneArneson's responsibility-catering prioritarianism assigns extra weight to those who are worse off through no fault of their own while reducing or eliminating the claim of those who are worse off through imprudence or reckless choice. This sounds like luck egalitarianism. But there is a crucial structural difference: Arneson builds responsibility into the weighting function, so that the fundamental currency of justice remains absolute welfare position, modified by a responsibility discount. The luck egalitarian says something different: the fundamental demand of justice is that no one should suffer disadvantage through brute luck, and this demand is about comparative relations — about how people stand relative to one another — not merely about how the weighting function is calibrated. The difference is not merely technical; it reflects a genuinely different conception of what justice requires.

ii. the harshness objection and its limits

Against harshness objection — luck egalitarianism can be non-harsh; responsibility attribution is the hard problem, not the basic principleThe standard objection to luck egalitarianism is that it is excessively harsh: it abandons those whose poverty is traceable to their own choices, however constrained those choices may have been. I accept that badly formulated luck egalitarianism can produce harsh verdicts. But this is an objection to particular formulations, not to the underlying principle. The hard problem is determining which disadvantages genuinely result from free choice as opposed to structurally constrained pseudo-choices. A person who grows up in poverty with limited education and few genuine options cannot be said to have freely chosen their economic position even if they nominally made choices along the way. A sophisticated luck egalitarianism is sensitive to these constraints and does not produce the harsh results that critics attribute to it. The responsibility-catering prioritarian faces exactly the same determination problem — they too must decide which disadvantages are choice-traceable — but without the clarity that the egalitarian's comparative framework provides.

iii. against the socialist convergence: why not just prioritise?

Against socialist convergence — egalitarianism captures something prioritarianism misses even when policies overlapIt might seem that luck egalitarianism and responsibility-catering prioritarianism recommend the same policies in most real-world cases: both will direct resources toward those suffering through no fault of their own. If so, why does the theoretical distinction matter? It matters because the underlying justification determines how we reason at the margins and in non-standard cases. When a person suffers disadvantage through a mix of luck and choice, the egalitarian and prioritarian reason differently: the egalitarian asks how much of the disadvantage is luck-traceable; the prioritarian asks how badly off the person is and how responsible they are. These frameworks can diverge on cases involving wealthy risk-takers who lose everything and poor people who decline modest risk. A theory of justice needs to handle such cases, and the frameworks give different answers.

iv. on rawlsian avoidance of responsibility

Against Rawls — Difference Principle ignores luck-choice distinction; accepts contingent inequalities luck egalitarianism must rejectRawls deliberately sets aside the luck-choice distinction at the level of the basic structure, arguing that the original position abstracts from all morally arbitrary facts. This produces a theory that is, in one sense, maximally sensitive to luck — since all natural endowments are treated as morally arbitrary — but not appropriately sensitive to choice, since the Difference Principle benefits all improvements to the worst-off position regardless of whether those improvements are traceable to choices made by the better-off. A society in which talented people develop their talents and become wealthy, contributing thereby to an improved position of the worst-off, is just under the Difference Principle even if the original inequality of talent was a form of brute luck that the talented did nothing to deserve. Luck egalitarianism tracks this distinction more precisely.

2.Luck egalitarianism demands that no one suffer comparative disadvantage through brute luck — a relational demand about how people stand to one another, not merely a modification of welfare weights.
3.This is a genuinely different conception of what justice requires: comparative relations matter independently of absolute welfare positions and their weights.
∴The luck-choice distinction is morally irreducible: it structures the demands of egalitarian justice in a way that cannot be replicated by modifying a prioritarian weighting function.

The debate between egalitarians and sufficientarians turns, in part, on the question of currency: what are we supposed to be equalising, or bringing up to a threshold? My answer is resources, understood as anything that can be transferred from one person to another and that enables them to form and pursue a conception of the good life.

Crisp's sufficientarianism holds that justice demands that we bring everyone up to a threshold of sufficiency — a level at which they have enough — and that above this threshold equality is not required. I want to argue that this view misidentifies what equal concern for persons requires, and that the envy-free test for resource equality provides a better framework.

“Equality of welfare is the wrong goal because it is ambiguous and self-defeating: it rewards expensive tastes and punishes prudent ones. Equality of resources is the right goal because resources are the proper currency of interpersonal comparison: they measure what people are given, not the varying use they make of what they are given.”
i. why welfare equality fails and resources succeed

Currency — welfare equality rewards expensive tastes; resources are the correct interpersonal currencyEquality of welfare would require compensating those with expensive tastes for the fact that their equal resource share leaves them less satisfied than those with modest tastes. This is wrong: a person who cultivates a taste for rare claret cannot claim a greater resource share on grounds of welfare equality simply because their satisfaction requires more resources. The correct currency is resources, not welfare: we give people equal resources and hold them responsible for developing tastes that their resource share can satisfy. This is what it means to treat people as equals: to give them the same platform for constructing a good life, not to guarantee them the same level of satisfaction regardless of how they construct it. Sufficientarian views that focus on welfare levels miss this point.

ii. the envy test and hypothetical insurance

Envy test — envy-free distribution as a test of resource equality; hypothetical insurance markets handle handicapsResource equality is measured by the envy test: a distribution is envy-free if no person prefers another's bundle of resources to their own. This test operationalises equal concern because it ensures that no person can claim, from a position of equal standing, that they have been given worse than their fair share. The hardest cases are handicaps — congenital impairments and disabilities that cannot be transferred or equalised in resource terms. Here I invoke hypothetical insurance markets: behind a veil of ignorance, rational agents would purchase insurance against handicap; the redistribution that actual insurance markets would produce in this hypothetical situation defines the egalitarian duty of compensation. This is not a mere device; it reflects what equal concern actually requires when resources cannot be perfectly equalized.

iii. against sufficientarianism: why the threshold is unjustified

Against Crisp — sufficientarian threshold is arbitrary; above-threshold inequality receives no egalitarian justificationCrisp's view holds that once everyone is above the sufficiency threshold, inequalities above it require no egalitarian justification. But where is the threshold set, and why? Any particular threshold will be either arbitrary — a line drawn without principled reason — or will turn out on examination to be the level at which resource equality would also be satisfied. If the threshold is set at the level where everyone has an envy-free resource share, sufficientarianism and resource egalitarianism converge. If it is set elsewhere, the sufficientarian owes us a principled account of why justice demands bringing everyone up to that level and no further. Crisp's compassion-based account — we are moved by sympathy for those who fall below the threshold — is not a theory of justice but a description of a moral sentiment. Justice requires more than that.

iv. resource equality and the separateness of persons

Separateness — resource equality respects persons as distinct agents; sufficientarianism conflates themResource equality treats each person as a distinct agent with their own life to lead, giving them equal standing in the distribution of society's resources and then holding them responsible for the choices they make. Sufficientarianism treats persons primarily as welfare-receivers, asking only whether their welfare level exceeds the threshold. This is a less respectful conception: it treats people as vessels to be filled to a sufficient level rather than as agents to be given equal platforms. Equal concern for persons, I maintain, requires treating them as agents, not as welfare-recipients; and this means the currency of justice must be resources, not welfare levels or their sufficiency.

My argument concerns the formal foundations of distributive justice — specifically, whether the badness of unequal distributions can be captured by axiomatic aggregation in the way that Broome proposes.

Broome's approach is elegant: he starts from axioms governing the structure of rational preferences over distributions and derives what kinds of aggregation are rationally required. On his account, distributive concerns show up as concavity in the welfare transformation function: we count welfare gains to the worse-off as more valuable than equal gains to the better-off. This is a form of prioritarianism embedded in the formal structure. I want to argue that this approach, however elegant, misses the relational dimension of distributive justice that the egalitarian tradition has always insisted upon.

i. pairwise comparative badness as a relational concept

Relational badness — pairwise comparison between persons captures what positional welfare weights cannotConsider two distributions: in the first, A has 80 and B has 20. In the second, A has 50 and B has 50. Broome's axiomatic approach will rank these by summing transformed welfare: if the transformation function is concave, the second may rank higher. But the reason for preferring the second is not merely that the transformed sum is greater; it is that in the first distribution, B is worse off than A in a relation that is itself bad — a relation of comparative disadvantage among persons who share a society and face common institutions. This relational fact — that B stands below A through no fault of their own — is not captured by any function of individual welfare levels, however that function is transformed. The badness is in the pair (A, B) under the distribution, not in any function of B's level alone.

ii. broome’s axiomatic approach misses the relational structure

Against Broome — axiomatic aggregation is person-separable; pairwise relational badness is not person-separableBroome's axioms are person-separable: the social welfare function is a function of individual welfare levels, and the contribution of each person's welfare to the social value is determined independently of the others' welfare. This is precisely what allows him to derive clean additively separable social welfare functions. But pairwise comparative badness is not person-separable: the badness of B being worse off than A depends essentially on A's level as well as B's. You cannot read off the relational badness from B's level alone. Any axiomatic approach that is person-separable will therefore systematically miss the relational dimension of distributive justice. Broome's framework captures the absolute-level concern for the worse-off but not the comparative concern, and these are different moral considerations.

iii. the separateness of persons argument from the egalitarian side

Separateness from egalitarian side — egalitarian complaint is irreducibly interpersonal; cannot be read from individual utility functionsRawls argued from the separateness of persons against utilitarian aggregation: it ignores the boundaries between individuals, treating the aggregate as if it were a single welfare system. The egalitarian argument makes a different but related point: the injustice of inequality is constituted by how persons stand in relation to one another, and this relational fact is irreducibly interpersonal. The separateness of persons means, for the egalitarian, that each person's comparative position matters not merely as an input into a welfare function but as a relational fact about their standing in the distributive scheme. Broome's approach treats the separateness of persons as a structural constraint on the social welfare function; the egalitarian treats it as a source of relational facts that the function must represent.

iv. on the prospects for a formally adequate egalitarian framework

Formal prospects — egalitarianism requires non-separable aggregation; technically harder but not impossibleI do not deny that formal precision matters in distributive theory. What I deny is that Broome's person-separable axiomatic approach can capture the relational dimension of egalitarian justice. A formally adequate egalitarian framework would need to represent pairwise comparative badness as a function of pairs of welfare levels, not individual levels. This is technically more complex — the resulting social welfare function is not separable — but it is not formally impossible. Temkin's various inequality measures can be understood as attempts to formally represent this relational dimension. The fact that they generate intransitive rankings is a challenge for the formalism, not a reason to abandon the relational insight. Broome has the formal machinery; he has not yet aimed it at the right target.

I work at the intersection of normative theory and welfare economics, and I want to show that the egalitarian programme is not merely philosophically defensible but is formally tractable in a way that allows it to compete with Adler's systematic prioritarianism on its own methodological ground.

Adler's programme — developed in his work on prioritarianism with risk — is genuinely impressive in its formal precision. He constructs social welfare functions from axioms, integrates priority weighting with expected utility theory, and produces actionable frameworks for policy analysis. My argument is that this impressive formal apparatus is aimed at the wrong target: it captures priority but not responsibility-sensitive equality, and the omission is morally significant.

i. equivalent income as the correct metric for egalitarian welfare economics

Equivalent income — responsibility-sensitive metric; tracks real freedom and genuine welfare, not bare utilityStandard welfare economics uses utility as its metric, and prioritarianism applies a concave transformation to utility. The problem is that utility reflects both objective circumstances and subjective adaptation: a person who adapts to poverty and reports high utility is not well-off in the relevant sense. Equivalent income — the income at reference prices and a reference utility function that would leave the person as well off as they actually are — provides a responsibility-sensitive metric that tracks real freedom and genuine welfare rather than adaptive preferences. A person at low equivalent income is genuinely badly off even if they report high utility; a person at high equivalent income who reports low utility has the resources for a good life but has chosen not to deploy them fully. This distinction is invisible to Adler's utility-based prioritarianism but is morally crucial for any responsibility-sensitive theory.

ii. responsibility-sensitive social welfare functions

Responsibility-sensitive SWFs — formally tractable egalitarian aggregation; tracks luck vs. choice in welfare spaceStandard prioritarian social welfare functions apply a concave transformation to individual utilities and sum the results. Responsibility-sensitive egalitarian social welfare functions do something more complex: they partition individual welfare into luck-traceable and choice-traceable components, applying egalitarian transfers to the luck-traceable component and allowing differential outcomes in the choice-traceable component. This is formally tractable using equivalent income metrics and conditional welfare distributions. The resulting SWF is not additive across persons in the simple prioritarian sense, but it is still a well-defined aggregation rule that can guide policy. Adler's formal precision is admirable; the target of that precision is the question.

iii. why abstracting from responsibility trades policy relevance for axiomatic elegance

Against Adler — abstracting from responsibility is not neutral; it builds in a normative choice that forecloses egalitarian conclusionsAdler justifies the abstraction from responsibility partly on grounds of measurement difficulty: it is hard to determine empirically which welfare differences are choice-traceable and which are luck-traceable. I accept that this is difficult. But the difficulty is a practical challenge for application, not a reason to omit responsibility from the theoretical framework. When we embed a prioritarian SWF in policy analysis, we are implicitly treating all welfare differences as morally equivalent regardless of their causal source. This is not a neutral theoretical move; it forecloses the egalitarian conclusion that choice-traceable differences warrant less redistributive attention than luck-traceable ones. Adler's axiomatic elegance comes at the cost of a substantive normative commitment to treating all welfare differences as morally equivalent in causal origin — a commitment that most egalitarians must reject.

iv. the policy stakes: real freedom and genuine opportunity

Policy stakes — equivalent income and real freedom produce different policy rankings from priority-weighted utilityThe practical difference between egalitarian welfare economics and systematic prioritarianism shows up sharply in policy evaluation. Consider two policies: one that raises the utility of a large group of low-equivalent-income individuals through transfers that expand their real freedom; one that provides the same utility increment to a slightly smaller group of lower-utility but higher-equivalent-income individuals. Prioritarianism, tracking utility levels, may recommend the second; egalitarian welfare economics, tracking equivalent income and real freedom, recommends the first. These are different verdicts with real policy consequences for healthcare, education, and social insurance design. The formal tools matter because they determine which trade-offs we can see and which we are blind to. An approach blind to responsibility-sensitive freedom differences is not merely incomplete; it is systematically biased against the worst-off in a sense that goes deeper than their utility level.

I close the proposition's case by addressing what I take to be the deepest challenge to any egalitarian view: the problem of aggregation under uncertainty, and specifically whether the competing-claims framework that I have developed provides a superior alternative to Porter's intrapersonal-focused prioritarianism.

Porter argues that prioritarianism should be understood as tracking what is rationally required of individuals considering their own life prospects — a form of intrapersonal rationality extended to the interpersonal case. I want to challenge this move: the extension from intrapersonal to interpersonal aggregation is precisely where the egalitarian critique of prioritarianism bites hardest.

The Competing Claims Framework.

When resources are scarce and we must choose who to benefit, we should identify the claims that each affected person has on those resources. A claim is stronger when: (i) the benefit is greater for that person; (ii) the person is worse off in absolute terms; (iii) the person has a stronger moral entitlement based on fairness considerations.

Aggregation across claims is permitted when claims are of comparable strength; it is not permitted — or is severely restricted — when it would override a strong claim of one person with many weak claims of others.

This structure captures the separateness of persons in a way that both simple prioritarianism and simple egalitarianism fail to do.
i. why interpersonal aggregation under risk cannot follow the intrapersonal model

Against interpersonal aggregation — intrapersonal rationality cannot be extended to interpersonal case without violating separateness of personsPorter suggests that prioritarianism is grounded in what rational individuals would choose when considering their life prospects from a unified temporal perspective: giving extra weight to benefits at worse-off life stages because these are periods of greatest need. This intrapersonal model is genuinely illuminating within a single life. But the extension to separate persons is illegitimate: it treats the aggregate welfare of many separate persons as if it were the welfare of a single individual over time, which it is not. Each person's life is distinct; there is no super-person for whom aggregate welfare improvements constitute improvements of a unified life-course. When we sacrifice person A for aggregate gains to persons B, C, and D, we are not making a trade-off within a single life; we are treating A as a means to others' ends, and that requires separate justification.

ii. the competing claims framework and irreversible harms

Competing claims — protects individuals from aggregative sacrifice; strong claims cannot be overridden by many weak onesThe competing-claims framework protects individuals from being sacrificed for aggregate benefits when their claims are strong. Consider a medical resource allocation case: one person will suffer permanent paralysis without treatment; many people will suffer minor inconveniences without the same resource. Prioritarianism, giving extra weight to the paralysis sufferer's benefit because she is absolutely worse off, will in many cases recommend the treatment — but the justification is aggregate welfare, not the irreducible strength of her individual claim. The competing-claims framework says: her claim is strong because the harm is severe and irreversible; many weak claims cannot override it in aggregation. This is a more adequate representation of why we owe her the resource: not because aggregate priority-weighted welfare is maximised by treating her, but because her individual claim is the strongest.

iii. why egalitarian considerations enter the competing claims framework

Egalitarian entry — comparative position strengthens claims; inequality is a morally relevant feature of the claims landscapeThe competing-claims framework is not purely prioritarian: comparative position matters. A person's claim to a benefit is strengthened not only by being absolutely worse off but also by being worse off relative to others in a way that is morally arbitrary. This is the egalitarian element: the person who is worst off through brute luck has a stronger claim than the equally absolutely-positioned person who is worst off through choice, and a stronger claim than the prioritarian framework (sensitive only to absolute position) would recognise. The egalitarian insight — that comparative position matters and that luck-driven inequality strengthens claims — is preserved in the competing-claims framework without the need for a global egalitarian distributive principle that generates paradoxes at the aggregate level.

iv. the final synthesis: why the egalitarian tradition is correct

Synthesis — competing claims framework vindicates egalitarian core; prioritarianism is adequate only in special casesThe proposition has defended a family of views: non-instrumental badness of inequality, contractualist justifiability, luck-choice sensitivity, resource-based currency, relational standing, formal responsibility-sensitivity, comparative egalitarianism in population ethics, political anti-domination, and competing claims under risk. These are different theories, and we have disagreed with each other on points of formulation. But we share a commitment to the claim that justice is irreducibly about how persons stand in relation to one another, not merely about where they stand in absolute terms. The prioritarian tradition — Parfit, Rawls, Arneson, Crisp, Broome, Adler, Holtug, Porter — captures something important: absolute position matters, welfare matters, and we must take seriously how badly off individuals are. But it cannot capture the relational dimension. On that dimension, the egalitarian tradition is correct, and the proposition should win this debate.

I want to establish the central distinction of this debate: the distinction between Telic Egalitarianism and Prioritarianism. Once this distinction is properly understood, I believe Prioritarianism is clearly superior.

Temkin claims that inequality is non-instrumentally bad — that a world in which some are worse off than others, through no fault of their own, is worse for that reason alone. This is the Telic Egalitarian view. I deny it. What I affirm is Prioritarianism: benefits to worse-off people matter more, not because of any comparison with how others are doing, but simply because those people are worse off. The moral weight of a benefit is a function of the absolute position of the recipient, not of their position relative to others.

“It is often bad that people are worse off than others. But this is not, I believe, because of the inequality itself. What is bad is not that these people are worse off than others. What is bad is that these people are worse off than they might be. We should not say: ‘It is bad that these people are worse off than others.’ We should say: ‘What is bad is that these people are so badly off.’” — Parfit, 1997
i. the levelling down objection

Levelling Down — egalitarianism implies levelling down improves the world in some respect; this is incoherentThe Levelling Down Objection is, I believe, decisive against Telic Egalitarianism. If inequality is non-instrumentally bad, then eliminating it — even by making the better-off worse off without helping anyone — must improve the world in at least one respect. Temkin accepts this implication and treats it as merely one bad-making feature offset by others. But this is to accept that there is a respect in which levelling down improves the world — a respect that consists purely in equalising welfare levels, regardless of what happens to any person. I find this incoherent. An improvement in the world must be good for someone or something. Levelling down benefits no one and harms some; how can it constitute an improvement in any respect? The egalitarian's answer commits them to a form of value that is not grounded in anyone's wellbeing, which I regard as deeply implausible.

ii. prioritarianism avoids levelling down

Prioritarianism avoids LDO — extra weight to worse-off grounded in their absolute position; levelling down helps nobody, so is not recommendedPrioritarianism gives extra weight to benefits that accrue to those who are worse off, but this extra weight is grounded in their absolute position, not in comparison with others. Levelling down does not improve anyone's absolute position; it only harms the better-off. Prioritarianism therefore straightforwardly implies that levelling down is wrong: it helps nobody (in absolute terms) and harms some. There is nothing in the prioritarian calculus that recommends it. This is exactly the right verdict, and it shows that the moral force of our concern for the worse-off is captured by reference to their absolute position, not by reference to their relative position. Temkin's relational account of inequality is unnecessary and generates the problematic implication that levelling down improves the world in some respect.

iii. what prioritarianism captures that egalitarianism cannot

Prioritarian advantage — captures compassion for badly-off without generating paradoxes of relational egalitarianismPrioritarianism captures what is morally compelling about the egalitarian tradition without generating the paradoxes that telic egalitarianism faces. We do give extra weight to benefits for the badly-off, and this weight is properly calibrated to their absolute position: the more badly off they are, the more their welfare gains matter morally. This is not an ad hoc weighting function; it reflects the deeply intuitive thought that it matters more whether a very badly-off person receives a benefit than whether a very well-off person does. Egalitarianism tries to explain this intuition by appeal to comparative relations among persons; prioritarianism explains it more parsimoniously by appeal to absolute position. The simpler explanation is better.

iv. against the relational view: why comparative position does not matter in itself

Against relational view — comparative position has no independent moral weight; all moral weight derives from absolute positionTemkin argues that there is something morally significant in the comparative fact that B is worse off than A, independently of how badly off B is in absolute terms. I deny this. Consider two worlds: in the first, everyone is at level 100. In the second, everyone is at level 50. The first world is clearly better — not because it is more equal (it is equally equal) but because people are better off in absolute terms. Now consider a third world where half are at 100 and half at 80. Is this world worse than the first in some respect that consists purely in its inequality? I do not believe so. What is worse about it, if anything, is that the people at 80 are worse off than they might be — an absolute, not comparative, fact. Comparative position adds nothing to the moral evaluation once absolute position is accounted for.

I want to defend the Difference Principle against Scanlon's contractualist critique, and to show that the original position provides a superior foundation for principles of distribution to the contractualist framework he offers.

Scanlon argues that the contractualist standard — principles must be justifiable to each individual — generates stronger egalitarian demands than the Difference Principle. I want to argue that the Difference Principle is the correct output of a properly specified contractualist procedure, and that Scanlon's version of contractualism generates demands that are either too strong or indeterminate.

i. the original position and the veil of ignorance

Original position — veil of ignorance produces principles that rational persons would accept regardless of their social positionThe original position is a device of representation: it models the conditions under which principles of justice must be chosen, namely conditions of ignorance about one's own place in the social order, one's class position, natural assets, and conception of the good. Behind this veil, rational persons would not gamble on occupying a favoured position; they would choose principles that guarantee the best available outcome for the worst-off, since they may turn out to be the worst-off themselves. This produces the Difference Principle: inequalities are just only if they maximise the position of the least-advantaged representative person. This is not arbitrary; it is the rational choice of persons who know they may be the worst-off and who are appropriately risk-averse about their most fundamental interests.

ii. against scanlon’s extension of contractualism

Against Scanlon — reasonable rejection criterion is indeterminate without the structural features of the original positionScanlon's reasonable rejection criterion asks whether any individual can reasonably reject a principle. This sounds more demanding than the original position because it focuses on individual claims rather than representative position. But it is less determinate: what counts as reasonable rejection depends on a prior account of which objections are reasonable, which requires a prior account of what persons are owed — which is precisely what the theory is supposed to deliver. The original position avoids this circularity by using a specific and well-defined choice situation: persons choose principles for their mutual advantage under ignorance of their specific position. This produces determinate principles. Scanlon's criterion, without this structural specification, tends to generate either indeterminate verdicts or verdicts that track the theorist's prior egalitarian intuitions.

iii. the difference principle is not too weak

Difference Principle defensible — not too weak; inequalities benefiting the worst-off are just; egalitarian demand for strict equality is too strongScanlon argues that the Difference Principle permits inequalities that generate subordination. But inequalities that maximally benefit the least-advantaged are not subordinating — they are the structural conditions under which the least-advantaged are as well-off as they can possibly be. A strict equality that rejected these inequalities would leave the least-advantaged worse off in order to prevent the better-off from being better off. This is not justice; it is envy elevated to a principle. The Difference Principle correctly identifies the goal of distributive justice: to maximise the position of those who are worst-off, not to equalise positions at the cost of reducing everyone's wellbeing.

iv. on the basic structure and the limits of distributive justice

Basic structure — justice applies to institutions, not individual transactions; this limits but does not weaken the theoryI deliberately apply the principles of justice to the basic structure of society — to the major social institutions that distribute rights, opportunities, and the advantages of social cooperation — rather than to every individual transaction or choice. Scanlon and Cohen both press for application of egalitarian or contractualist principles beyond the basic structure. I resist this extension: principles of justice for the basic structure may not be appropriate principles for individual choices, and extending them to every level of social life risks generating a totalising demand that eliminates the space for personal pursuits and private commitments. The Difference Principle applied to institutions is powerful and demanding; applied to every individual decision, it would be both impracticable and intrusive.

My argument is that responsibility-catering prioritarianism is a superior framework to Cohen's strict luck egalitarianism — both more coherent and less harsh — and that the difference between the frameworks is practically significant despite surface-level similarities.

Cohen argues that the fundamental demand of justice is to eliminate inequalities traceable to brute luck. I agree that responsibility matters. But I believe Cohen's framework makes responsibility do more work than it can bear and generates verdicts that no plausible moral theory should endorse.

i. the harsh verdicts problem

Harsh verdicts — strict luck egalitarianism abandons the imprudent in ways that no adequate moral theory can endorseCohen's luck egalitarianism holds that justice does not require compensating those whose disadvantage is traceable to their own choices. Applied strictly, this means that a person who voluntarily takes up motorcycle riding and is seriously injured in an accident has no claim to health care as a matter of justice — they chose the risk. Similarly, someone who makes imprudent financial decisions has no claim to relief from the resulting poverty, even if those decisions were made under conditions of limited information and constrained options. These verdicts are harsh. Cohen argues that a sophisticated luck egalitarianism would be sensitive to structural constraints on choice. But once we allow these qualifications, the line between luck-traceable and choice-traceable disadvantage becomes so difficult to draw that the framework risks either abandoning the distinction or becoming systematically indeterminate.

ii. responsibility-catering prioritarianism handles the harsh verdicts

RCP handles harshness — priority gives weight to absolute position; responsibility modifies but does not eliminate the obligationResponsibility-catering prioritarianism avoids these harsh verdicts by making priority to the worse-off the fundamental principle, with responsibility as a modifier that reduces (but does not necessarily eliminate) the obligation to help. Even those who are worse off through their own choices retain some claim to assistance when they are badly enough off, because being badly off matters regardless of why one is badly off. The degree of the obligation is modulated by responsibility, but it is not extinguished. This is both more humane and more consistent with our actual moral judgments: we do not think that imprudent motorcyclists deserve no medical treatment at all; we think they have somewhat weaker claims than those who are injured through no fault of their own, but still claims.

iii. the luck-choice determination problem affects both frameworks equally

Determination problem — both frameworks face it; RCP is more forgiving when determination failsCohen argues that responsibility-catering prioritarianism faces the same difficulty in determining which disadvantages are choice-traceable as luck egalitarianism does, without the comparative clarity of the egalitarian framework. I accept that both frameworks face this determination problem. But they handle the problem differently when determination fails. When we cannot determine whether a disadvantage is luck-traceable or choice-traceable — a very common situation — luck egalitarianism has no principled answer about whether justice requires compensation. Responsibility-catering prioritarianism gives a principled answer: we give the person priority proportional to their absolute position, discounting for responsibility to the extent that we can determine it, but not excluding them from moral consideration when determination fails. This is a more robust response to the determination problem.

iv. why priority captures what the egalitarian tradition genuinely cares about

Genuine egalitarian concern — captured by priority to worse-off; relational structure is not the deep moral concernThe egalitarian tradition is motivated by concern for those who are badly off and for those who are worse off than others. I accept both concerns. But I believe the former is more fundamental: what matters, at bottom, is how badly off people are, not how they compare to others. The relational framing — inequality as such is bad — generates paradoxes (Levelling Down, intransitivity of inequality measures) that the priority framing avoids. Responsibility-catering prioritarianism captures the genuine egalitarian concern for the badly-off while maintaining a framework that is both more formally tractable and more immune to the moral paradoxes that telic egalitarianism generates. The proposition's attachment to the relational framing is a philosophical mistake; the opposition's priority framing is what does the real moral work.

I want to defend sufficientarianism against Dworkin's resource egalitarianism by showing that the sufficiency threshold, grounded in a principle of compassion, captures our deepest moral responses to deprivation more adequately than the envy-free resource test.

“We are moved by compassion for those who are badly off. i. the compassion principle and its grounding

Compassion principle — absolute deprivation triggers moral response; comparative structure is not what moves usOur most basic moral responses to poverty, illness, and deprivation are responses to absolute levels of need, not to comparative structures of distribution. When we see a person suffering from severe hunger, our moral response is not: how does this person's welfare compare to others? It is: this person is suffering badly and needs help. The comparative question — whether others are equally hungry or more fortunately placed — affects our causal analysis of what can be done but does not change the fundamental moral response to the person's absolute situation. Sufficientarianism grounds this observation in a principle: below a threshold of welfare that constitutes having enough, individuals have strong moral claims to assistance, grounded in the principle of compassion; above the threshold, the claims of equality lose their distinctive force.

Against Dworkin — envy test and hypothetical insurance are idealised; sufficiency threshold is more action-guidingDworkin's envy test requires constructing a hypothetical auction in which persons bid for resource bundles with equal amounts of imaginary currency. How do we operationalise the envy-free criterion in a world of complex, non-transferable goods and information asymmetries? Natural resources — unowned initially; egalitarian distribution derived from moral arbitrariness of initial resource claimsNo person created the natural resources of the earth. I want to defend the axiomatic approach to distributive justice against Rabinowicz's charge that person-separable aggregation misses the relational dimension of egalitarian concern.

Rabinowicz argues that pairwise comparative badness — the fact that B is worse off than A — generates moral demands that no person-separable social welfare function can represent. I want to deny both parts of this claim: I deny that pairwise comparative badness is a genuine and irreducible moral datum, and I affirm that person-separable aggregation captures everything morally important about distributive concern.

i. the axioms of fair aggregation

Axioms of fairness — separability and monotonicity generate concave SWFs; these capture distributive concern formallyMy approach begins from axioms that any plausible social evaluation function must satisfy: separability (the welfare of individuals not affected by a choice is irrelevant to it), monotonicity (more welfare is better), and anonymity (permuting who has which welfare level leaves social value unchanged). From these axioms, plus a weak preference for equality under uncertainty, the social welfare function must be a strictly concave transformation of individual utilities summed across persons. Strict concavity is precisely what represents distributive concern: it means that a transfer from a better-off to a worse-off person (holding total welfare constant) increases social value. This is the prioritarian insight, derived axiomatically rather than asserted.

ii. why pairwise relational badness is not a genuine moral datum

Against relational badness — comparative facts reduce to absolute facts once all morally relevant information is includedRabinowicz claims that the fact of B being worse off than A is a morally irreducible relational fact, not derivable from B's and A's individual welfare levels. I deny this. Once we know the complete distribution of welfare — everyone's absolute level — we know all the morally relevant facts. The comparative fact that B is worse off than A is fully determined by the absolute facts: B is at level x, A is at level y, x < y. There is nothing further in the relational description that is not already in the absolute description. The additional moral weight that Rabinowicz wants to assign to the relational fact must show up in the social welfare function — and it does, via the concavity that gives extra weight to welfare improvements for those at lower absolute levels. Consider a choice between two options that affect only A and B but not C: a non-separable SWF may rate these options differently depending on C's welfare level, even though C is not affected. Introducing non-separable aggregation to represent a moral datum that is already fully represented by separable aggregation does not add moral content; it adds formal complexity without substantive gain. Metaphysical suspicion — state-evaluative egalitarianism generates demands not grounded in any person's claim; suspiciousPersson's state-evaluative egalitarianism holds that a world containing inequality is bad independently of whether any person has a complaint against it — the world simply has a bad structural feature that calls for correction. I am suspicious of this metaphysical structure. Moral demands, I believe, must ultimately trace back to facts about persons and their interests. A demand to reduce inequality that is not grounded in any particular person's claim to a better distribution is not a moral demand at all; it is an impersonal preference for a certain world-structure. Such preferences may be aesthetically compelling but they do not generate moral obligations in the way that person-grounded claims do. The history of population ethics is full of counterintuitive results generated by taking such impersonal world-evaluations too seriously.

iv. the practical superiority of person-affecting prioritarianism for population policy

Practical superiority — person-affecting prioritarianism generates clearer policy guidance; avoids repugnant conclusions of impersonal viewsPersson claims that state-evaluative egalitarianism has an advantage for population-affecting policies: it generates constraints on which populations are produced independently of whether particular persons have complaints. But this apparent advantage generates serious problems. Person-affecting prioritarianism, by grounding distributional demands in particular persons' claims, avoids these repugnant implications. The practical superiority of the person-affecting approach lies precisely in its resistance to the counterintuitive conclusions that impersonal state-evaluation generates.

I want to explain why both egalitarianism and prioritarianism, whatever their internal differences, commit a fundamental error: they treat persons as means to the achievement of a distributive pattern rather than as ends in themselves.

Anderson charges me with sanctifying unjust historical structures. Let me engage this seriously. I acknowledge that the historical baseline of distribution is not just by my own principles: Locke’s proviso — that appropriation is just only if it does not worsen the position of others — has been massively violated in history. But the remedy for historical injustice is not the installation of a patterned distributive scheme maintained by continuous redistribution; it is the correction of specific historical violations through a principle of rectification.

“Taxation of earnings from labour is on a par with forced labour... Seizing the results of someone’s labour is equivalent to seizing hours from him and directing him to carry on various activities. If people force you to do certain work, or unrewarded work, for a certain period of time, they decide what you are to do and what purposes your work is to serve apart from your decisions. This process whereby they take this decision from you makes them a part-owner of you.” — Nozick
i. the fundamental error of patterned distribution

Patterned distribution — requires continuous interference with voluntary exchange; treats persons as means to pattern maintenanceEvery patterned distributive principle — whether it demands equality, priority to the worse-off, or sufficiency — requires continuous interference with the outcomes of voluntary exchanges. The reason is simple: liberty upsets patterns. People will freely exchange their holdings in ways that move distributions away from any pattern. To maintain the pattern, the state must continuously intervene: taxing, transferring, and regulating. This continuous intervention treats persons as resources to be redistributed in pursuit of a social goal that they have not individually chosen. It is a form of treating persons as means to the realisation of the distributive ideal rather than as ends whose voluntary choices are themselves morally authoritative. I find this incompatible with the basic commitment to the equal moral status of persons that both egalitarians and prioritarians profess.

ii. historical entitlement and the wilt chamberlain argument

Wilt Chamberlain — voluntary transactions from just baseline produce just outcomes; liberty upsets any patternConsider Wilt Chamberlain: people freely pay to watch him play basketball, each giving a small amount, until he is very wealthy. Is this outcome unjust? Each transaction was voluntary; no one was coerced. If the initial distribution was just — satisfying my entitlement principles — then the outcome of voluntary transactions from it must also be just, regardless of what pattern it generates. Anderson replies that the initial distribution is not just. This is correct as a historical matter, and I accept that my principle of rectification applies to this. But the response to historical injustice is not continuous redistribution in pursuit of an ideal pattern; it is rectification of the specific violations. Once violations are rectified, the resulting distribution — whatever its pattern — is just, and further redistribution is not required.

iii. against anderson’s structural critique

Against Anderson — structural domination is real but remedy is rights-protection and anti-monopoly, not redistributionAnderson argues that structural injustice — the systematic domination of some groups by others through legal and economic institutions — requires egalitarian remedies going beyond simple rights protection. I take this objection seriously. Structural domination is real; the use of the state to protect monopoly power, to enforce discriminatory contracts, and to deny political participation to some groups are all violations of my principles. But the remedy is not patterned redistribution; it is the removal of state-enforced advantages and the protection of equal rights. Free markets without state-backed monopoly, political institutions without formal discrimination, and equal legal standing for all persons address the structural domination that Anderson identifies without requiring the state to impose a distributive pattern on the voluntary outcomes of free exchange.

iv. why self-ownership is non-negotiable

Self-ownership — non-negotiable; egalitarianism and prioritarianism both violate it in requiring redistribution of self-owned labourThe deepest objection to egalitarianism and prioritarianism alike is that they require redistribution of the fruits of self-owned labour. When the state taxes earnings to fund redistribution — whether to achieve equality, to improve the position of the worst-off, or to bring everyone up to a sufficiency threshold — it is claiming a portion of a person's labour output that the person did not voluntarily give. This is equivalent to claiming a portion of the person's time and directing how it shall be spent. People are not merely associated with their labour; they are their labour in the morally relevant sense. To tax it in the name of distributive justice is to treat persons as partially owned by others — as partly a means to others' ends. This is wrong regardless of the distributive goal sought.

I close the opposition's case by responding directly to Voorhoeve's competing-claims framework and by articulating what I take to be the most defensible grounding for prioritarianism — one that does not rely on Broome's formal axiomatics or Parfit's relatively simple welfare-level account.

My thesis is that prioritarianism is best understood through an analogy with rational intrapersonal trade-offs across time, and that this grounding explains the priority principle without invoking the metaphysically suspicious relational claims of egalitarianism or the implausible separateness-of-persons objections to interpersonal aggregation.

i. the intrapersonal analogy and its justification

Intrapersonal analogy — rational persons give extra weight to benefits at worse-off life stages; this grounds interpersonal priorityConsider a rational person planning their life over time. They know that some periods will be better than others — they will be healthier or sicker, more or less prosperous, more or less satisfied. A rational person with this knowledge gives extra weight to benefits that accrue at worse-off life stages: they save more when prosperous against the contingency of hard times; they insure against illness when healthy; they sacrifice present satisfactions to secure better outcomes in anticipated periods of deprivation. This is not irrational; it reflects appropriate concern for the whole of one's life, weighted appropriately toward its potentially worse periods. Prioritarianism generalises this: across persons as across life stages, we should give extra weight to benefits for those who are worse off. The analogy illuminates rather than obstructs the interpersonal case.

ii. why the intrapersonal analogy does not violate the separateness of persons

Separateness — intrapersonal analogy respects separateness; generalises rational concern, does not aggregate across distinct persons illegitimatelyVoorhoeve argues that extending intrapersonal rationality to the interpersonal case violates the separateness of persons: there is no super-person for whom aggregate welfare constitutes a unified life-course. This is correct as a claim about utilitarian aggregation: treating the aggregate welfare of many persons as if it were the welfare of one involves a fiction. But the intrapersonal analogy for prioritarianism does not make this move. It does not treat the aggregate as a single welfare system; it uses the structure of rational concern within a single welfare system to illuminate what we should value across separate welfare systems. The analogy is a heuristic for understanding why welfare-level matters morally, not a claim that separate persons are really one person.

iii. against voorhoeve’s competing claims framework

Against competing claims — framework is indeterminate on aggregation; prioritarianism provides clearer guidanceVoorhoeve's competing-claims framework holds that claims of comparable strength can be aggregated while strong claims cannot be overridden by many weak ones. This is intuitively appealing but practically indeterminate: when are claims comparable? When is a claim strong enough to resist aggregation? The framework provides no principled criterion for answering these questions without invoking independent assumptions that amount to a substantive distributive theory. Prioritarianism, by contrast, provides a determinate verdict for any comparison: we give extra weight to benefits accruing at lower welfare levels. The priority principle, calibrated by an appropriate weighting function, generates rankings without invoking the indeterminate notion of claim comparability. In policy contexts, prioritarianism's determinacy is a decisive advantage.

iv. the final case for the opposition: why absolute position, not relational structure, is what justice requires

Final synthesis — prioritarianism captures all morally important distributional concerns; egalitarian relational add-on generates paradoxes without additional contentThe opposition has made a unified case across ten pairings: that absolute position matters more than relational structure; that the priority to give extra weight to the worse-off can be grounded in what persons are owed without invoking the non-instrumental badness of inequality; that formal adequacy, empirical tractability, and respect for self-ownership all point toward prioritarian and sufficientarian frameworks rather than toward egalitarianism. The proposition has defended a family of views sharing the commitment to the relational dimension of justice. But as Parfit showed at the opening, the relational dimension generates the Levelling Down paradox; as Arrhenius might note in the population case, it generates further impossibility results; as Broome showed, it is formally redundant once absolute priority is axiomatised; as Adler showed, it is empirically inaccessible at the policy level. The opposition's case is cumulative: prioritarianism captures the morally important content of the egalitarian tradition without its paradoxes, and the motion should be rejected.
