Logical Arguments

Mr President,

Individuals have rights, and there are things no person or group may do to them without violating these rights. So strong and far-reaching are these rights that they raise the question of what, if anything, the state and its officials may do. I want to establish why the self-ownership thesis is the correct starting point for political philosophy, why it entails powerful constraints on redistribution, and why the objections mounted against it ultimately fail.

On the self-ownership thesis

Self-ownership: each person owns herself fully, as a matter of rightThe self-ownership thesis holds that each person is the full owner of her own person — her body, her talents, her capacities, and the labour they produce. This is not a metaphor and it is not a mere rhetorical device. It is a claim about the structure of moral rights. To own something is to have a complex bundle of rights over it — the right to use it, to benefit from it, to control others' access to it, to transfer it. The self-ownership thesis says that each person has exactly this bundle of rights over herself. She is not partly owned by the collective, or by the state, or by any other person. She is the full and exclusive owner of herself. This is what the opposition denies — at least implicitly — whenever they endorse compulsory redistribution of the fruits of her talents.

On taxation as forced labour

Taxation is forced labour: taking the product of labour is taking the labourer's timeConsider what redistributive taxation actually amounts to. When the state takes a portion of what you have earned through the exercise of your capacities, it is not merely taking money — it is taking the product of your time and effort and skill. But the product of your time and effort and skill just is the materialisation of your time and effort and skill. To take the product is to take the labour; to take the labour is to take a portion of your working hours; and to take a portion of your working hours without your consent is to put you to work for others — which is to treat you as partially owned by those others. Seizing the results of someone's labour is equivalent to seizing hours from her life and directing that she work for another's purposes. This is the indignity — and the wrong — of compulsory redistribution. It does not respect the self-owner.

On the Wilt Chamberlain argument

Wilt Chamberlain: liberty upsets patterns; any patterned distribution is unstableSuppose we begin with whatever distribution of holdings the opposition considers just — any distribution they nominate. Now Wilt Chamberlain is greatly in demand as a basketball player. People freely pay to see him play; he signs a contract giving him twenty-five cents from each ticket. One million people attend his games across the season; Chamberlain ends with two hundred and fifty thousand dollars — far more than the average. Has he done anything wrong? Have the people who paid done anything wrong? Each transaction was voluntary. Each person gave what she was entitled to give. And yet the pattern has been disrupted. The opposition must either forbid these free exchanges — continuously interfering with people's lives — or permit them and allow their favoured distribution to unravel. There is no stable patterned principle of justice that does not require constant, intrusive interference with voluntary transactions. Liberty upsets patterns.

On the entitlement theory

Entitlement theory: justice in holdings depends on just acquisition and just transferMy alternative is the entitlement theory. A distribution of holdings is just if it arises from a just situation by just steps. The principles governing this are: justice in acquisition (original appropriation of unowned things), justice in transfer (voluntary exchange, gift, bequest), and rectification of injustice (correcting past violations of the first two principles). There is no pattern — no end-state — to which just holdings must conform. What matters is the history of how holdings came about, not the shape of their current distribution. If you acquired what you hold justly, and transferred and received justly, then whatever distribution results is a just distribution — however unequal it may look from the outside. The opposition's objection to inequality as such betrays a failure to recognise that justice is historical, not structural.

On the separateness of persons

Separateness of persons: the deepest objection to utilitarian redistributionUtilitarians and egalitarians are willing to sacrifice the interests of some for greater aggregate benefit to others — or for a more equal distribution. But this fails to take seriously the separateness of persons. There is no social entity that undergoes a sacrifice so that others may gain. There are only individual people, each with their own life to live. Using one person merely as a means to benefit others — however many those others may be, however great their need — does not respect that the person sacrificed is a separate individual with a life of her own, not a resource to be deployed for collective ends. The self-ownership thesis is the political expression of this deepest Kantian insight: persons are ends, not means. They are not receptacles for welfare to be distributed by whoever controls the levers of the state.

Mr President,

Cohen's critique of Nozick is sophisticated and deserves engagement. But I want to argue that it does not succeed. The self-ownership thesis survives both the internal critique — the charge that self-ownership without world-ownership is empty — and the external critique that it arbitrarily privileges brute luck over option luck in a way that undermines the very concept of personal responsibility it is meant to vindicate.

On the internal critique — self-ownership without world-ownership

Cohen's internal critique: self-ownership without world-ownership leaves the self-owner effectively unfreeCohen's most powerful internal objection is this: if you own yourself but have no right to the external world — no right to stand anywhere, use any resources, breathe any air — then your self-ownership is a kind of empty formalism. You own your talents and capacities but can exercise them only on sufferance of those who own the world. Cohen argues this means that self-ownership without a corresponding right to the world is compatible with a radical form of practical unfreedom. I want to concede the force of this point while arguing it does not defeat self-ownership but rather shows we need a complementary account of original appropriation — exactly what Nozick's Lockean proviso provides.

On the Lockean proviso and world-ownership

The Lockean proviso: appropriation is just if it leaves enough and as good for othersNozick's Lockean proviso holds that original appropriation of unowned resources is just if it leaves others no worse off than they would have been in a state of nature — the "enough and as good" condition. Cohen objects that under capitalism this condition is not met for the propertyless proletarian. But this objection targets the historical application of the proviso, not the self-ownership thesis itself. A world in which the Lockean proviso were genuinely satisfied — including by robust compensation for those who cannot appropriate — would preserve self-ownership while addressing the practical unfreedom Cohen identifies. Cohen's internal critique generates a demand for better property rules, not an argument against self-ownership as the foundational principle.

On exploitation and the separateness of contributions

Exploitation: taking the surplus of another's labour without consent is wrong precisely because of self-ownershipCohen's account of exploitation — the extraction of surplus value from workers who have no alternative but to submit — actually presupposes self-ownership. The reason exploitation is wrong, if it is wrong, is precisely that workers own themselves and their labour, and that their surplus is taken without their genuine free consent. An egalitarian who dismisses self-ownership cannot coherently invoke exploitation as a wrong — for exploitation is wrong only if the exploited party has a prior entitlement to the product of her labour. Cohen's exploitation critique is itself an implicit vindication of self-ownership at the foundational level, even as he attacks it at the distributive level.

On luck egalitarianism and its limits

Luck egalitarianism: correcting brute luck but not option luck; the problem of abandoning personal responsibilityCohen and his allies in the luck-egalitarian tradition argue that inequalities arising from brute luck — from unchosen circumstances — are unjust, while inequalities arising from option luck — from genuine choices — may be acceptable. But this distinction faces serious problems. If all the talents, capacities, and even preferences with which a person chooses are themselves products of unchosen circumstances — genetics, upbringing, social situation — then the category of genuine choice that generates acceptable inequality shrinks to nothing. The luck-egalitarian critique of self-ownership, pressed to its logical conclusion, eliminates responsibility entirely. Self-ownership is the only framework that can preserve a meaningful notion of personal responsibility — and responsibility, I submit, is something even Cohen cannot do without.

On why self-ownership is not merely libertarian

Self-ownership is not merely libertarian: it grounds bodily integrity, reproductive autonomy, and resistance to servitudeIt is tempting to think of self-ownership as an ideology of the right. But consider what self-ownership grounds. It grounds the wrongness of rape and assault — because the violated person's body is her own. It grounds reproductive autonomy — because decisions about one's own body belong to oneself, not to the state or to majorities. It grounds the wrongness of slavery and conscription — because one's person may not be appropriated by others without consent. These are the very rights that the left has historically championed. Cohen's critique, if successful, does not merely undermine libertarian political economy — it undermines the foundational rights to bodily integrity and personal sovereignty that all sides of the debate affirm. The self-ownership thesis is not the property of libertarians; it is the bedrock of any serious account of personal rights.

Mr President,

I want to attack Nozick's self-ownership thesis from the inside — not by denying it outright, but by showing that it cannot do the work libertarians require of it. Even granting self-ownership, the thesis fails to establish the libertarian conclusions about redistribution, taxation, and property that Nozick draws from it. The argument from self-ownership to laissez-faire capitalism is not the straightforward inference Nozick supposes it to be.

On the internal critique — self-ownership is compatible with socialism

Internal critique: self-ownership is compatible with socialism; it does not entail capitalist property rightsMy central argument is this: the self-ownership thesis, even if true, does not entail private ownership of external resources. Self-ownership concerns your rights over your own person; it says nothing, by itself, about who owns the external world — the land, the natural resources, the means of production. Nozick simply assumes that individuals may appropriate external resources through the kind of Lockean process he describes. But this assumption is not derivable from self-ownership. A world of self-owners who jointly own the external world, with collective decisions about how resources are used and their fruits distributed, is fully compatible with self-ownership. Socialism without slavery is coherent. This is the internal critique: you can grant self-ownership and still be a socialist about external resources.

On the proletarian's freedom

Proletarian freedom: the worker is formally free but structurally compelled to sell her labourThe proposition celebrates the freedom of the self-owner in a market society. But consider the situation of the propertyless worker — the proletarian who owns her labour power but has no access to means of production or subsistence except by selling that labour power to a capitalist. She is formally free: no specific person is forcing her to work for any particular employer. But she is not free in any substantive sense from the necessity of selling her labour. She must sell her labour to survive; the only question is to whom. This is the structural compulsion of capitalism — it is a compulsion exerted not by any individual but by the social system as a whole. Formal self-ownership, in this context, coexists with real unfreedom. The self-ownership thesis, applied to a world of unequal access to external resources, delivers not freedom but the ideology of freedom.

On the Wilt Chamberlain argument

Against Wilt Chamberlain: the argument presupposes just initial holdings, which it cannot establishNozick's Wilt Chamberlain argument is a beautiful piece of rhetoric, but it begs the central question. The argument assumes that the initial distribution from which Chamberlain's voluntary transactions proceed is just — that each person is entitled to what she holds at the start of the story. But this assumption is precisely what is in dispute. If the initial distribution includes holdings that arose from unjust appropriation — from the expropriation of common lands, from slavery, from colonial extraction — then transactions built on those foundations are not rendered just merely by being voluntary. Voluntariness does not launder injustice. The entitlement theory cannot get started until we have an account of just initial acquisition, and that account is where the self-ownership thesis fails to do the work Nozick requires.

On the talents objection and its implications

Talents objection: if I own my talents, why may I not own others' talents in the same way?Here is a further difficulty. Nozick says I own my talents. But my talents are in large part the product of the natural lottery — genetics, fortunate upbringing, a society that nurtured and educated me. If I own the product of my talents, then the society that produced those talents has some claim on their product too. Nozick cannot consistently insist that I own my talents while denying that the collective conditions that shaped those talents ground any social claim on their exercise. Moreover, if talent-ownership is the model, then a Rawlsian might argue — consistently with something like self-ownership — that talents are a common asset, since no one deserves the natural capacities they were born with. The self-ownership thesis, taken seriously, does not obviously support the libertarian conclusion; it opens the door to a redistributive response.

On what the thesis cannot explain — the site of genuine exploitation

Exploitation cannot be explained within Nozick's framework: voluntary exchange on unjust background conditions is still exploitativeMoller has argued that my account of exploitation presupposes self-ownership. I want to respond directly. What makes capitalist exploitation wrong is not merely that workers do not consent — formal consent exists. It is that the background conditions under which consent is given are unjust. Workers consent to wages that do not reflect the full product of their labour because they have no access to the means of production and no realistic alternative. This kind of wrongful extraction does not require self-ownership as its ground — it requires a theory of justice in background conditions, which is exactly what egalitarians provide and libertarians cannot. The wrong of exploitation is better explained by the injustice of the background structure than by appeal to any ownership claim over oneself. This is precisely where Nozick's framework fails and the egalitarian framework succeeds.

Mr President,

My position is distinctive within this debate. I do not reject the self-ownership thesis. I accept it — indeed, I accept it more consistently than Nozick does. My argument is that a coherent and rigorous application of self-ownership, combined with a properly egalitarian principle of world-ownership, generates left-libertarian conclusions that are deeply at odds with Nozick's laissez-faire capitalism. The proposition has captured self-ownership for the right; I am here to reclaim it.

On left-libertarianism

Left-libertarianism: self-ownership plus egalitarian world-ownership generates strong redistributive conclusionsLeft-libertarianism combines the self-ownership thesis — accepted without qualification — with an egalitarian principle of world-ownership. The central claim is that unowned natural resources belong, in some morally significant sense, to everyone — or at least to no one in particular. When individuals appropriate natural resources from this common stock, they owe compensation to those who thereby lose access to an equal share. This is not a concession to egalitarianism that compromises self-ownership; it is a rigorous application of the insight that the self-ownership thesis concerns persons, not the world, and that a separate principle is needed to govern the world. Nozick's Lockean proviso is an inadequate substitute for a proper egalitarian principle of world-ownership.

On the joint ownership proviso

Joint ownership proviso: original resources are jointly owned; appropriation requires the consent of allThe position I favour holds that natural resources are initially jointly owned — not by the state, not by abstract humanity, but by actual persons who have an equal claim on the world's resources. Appropriation of jointly owned resources requires — as a matter of principle — the agreement of all joint owners, or failing that, full compensation to those whose equal share is diminished. Nozick's proviso, by contrast, requires only that others be left no worse off in a narrowly counterfactual sense. This is too weak. The proletarian who receives wages barely above subsistence has not been left "no worse off" in any meaningful sense — she has been denied access to the common stock that was rightfully hers. A genuine application of self-ownership together with the joint ownership proviso generates powerful redistributive obligations that Nozick's framework suppresses.

On why Nozick's appropriation theory is internally unjustifiable

Nozick's appropriation theory is internally unjustifiable: self-ownership does not generate the right to appropriate from the commonNozick's move from self-ownership to private appropriation of the external world is not merely unsupported by the thesis — it is in tension with it. Self-ownership establishes that each person owns herself. But from the fact that I own myself, it does not follow that I may mix my labour with unowned resources and thereby come to own them. I might just as easily lose my labour in the mixing — as Cohen has noted, this is no argument. More importantly: if natural resources are initially unowned or jointly owned, then my act of appropriation affects the holdings of all other joint owners. Self-ownership gives me no right to worsen their position without their consent. Nozick's appropriation theory requires an additional premise that self-ownership cannot provide.

On the difference between self-ownership and self-determination

Self-ownership vs self-determination: Nozick conflates two distinct conceptsA crucial ambiguity runs through the proposition's case. There is a difference between self-ownership — the claim that I have property rights in my person in the full Hohfeldian sense — and self-determination — the claim that I have the right to direct my own life and not be subject to the arbitrary will of others. These two concepts can come apart. One can defend robust self-determination — the right to author one's own life — without accepting the full property-rights model of self-ownership that Nozick deploys. The property-rights model carries with it implications about alienability, about markets in labour, about the permissibility of voluntary self-enslavement, that many find deeply problematic. A left-libertarian can accept self-determination without the full weight of the Nozickian ownership model — and this is sufficient to resist Moller's claim that critics of self-ownership undermine personal rights.

On voluntary self-enslavement as a reductio of Nozick's self-ownership

Voluntary self-enslavement: Nozick's full self-ownership implies the permissibility of selling oneself into slaveryThe most powerful internal objection to Nozick's full self-ownership thesis is the problem of voluntary self-enslavement. If I own myself fully — in the way that I own a bicycle — then I should be permitted to sell myself into slavery. Full ownership includes the right of alienation: the right to transfer one's property to another. A self-owner, on Nozick's account, should be able to contract away all future control over herself, permanently and irrevocably. Nozick acknowledges this implication and accepts it as permissible. But this is deeply counterintuitive — and more importantly, it is at odds with the very value of self-determination that makes self-ownership appealing in the first place. A person who has sold herself into slavery is no longer self-determining. The full self-ownership thesis, on Nozick's own account, can be used to destroy the very freedom it is supposed to protect.
