Mr President,

The question before this House is deceptively technical. It is posed in the language of rates and parameters. But underneath the mathematics lies one of the simplest and most consequential moral questions one can ask: ought we to care less about the welfare of people who happen to live in the future than about the welfare of people who happen to live now? I shall argue that the answer is no — and that the contrary view is not merely wrong but represents a failure of reason itself.

On the meaning of pure time preference

Pure time preference: discounting welfare by mere temporal distance, not by any morally relevant differenceLet us be precise about what pure time preference means. It is the practice of discounting future utility — future welfare, future pleasure and pain, future flourishing — solely on account of its temporal distance from the present. It says that a unit of welfare experienced in fifty years is worth less than a unit experienced today, not because the future person is less real, not because the future welfare is less certain, not because the world will be richer and a marginal unit of welfare thus less valuable — but simply because fifty years have passed. The bare lapse of time, on this view, diminishes moral weight. I stated in my 1928 paper that this is "ethically indefensible." I do not retract a syllable of that claim. The mere passage of time is not a morally relevant difference between persons. To discount on account of it alone is to treat future persons as less important than present persons, for no reason that survives scrutiny.

On the defect of imagination

Telescopic defect: pure time preference reflects a psychological failing, not a genuine moral principleWhy do people discount the future? Not, I think, because they have a considered view that future welfare matters less. They discount because they have what Pigou called a defective telescopic faculty — they simply cannot see the future as vividly as the present. A toothache tomorrow is feared less than a toothache now, not because tomorrow's pain matters less, but because the imagination does not project it with equal force. This is a psychological fact about human cognition, not a moral insight. We do not, in our considered moments, endorse the view that the pain of another person matters less because that person is further away in space — we recognise that spatial distance is morally irrelevant. Temporal distance stands in exactly the same position. A future person's suffering is not less real because it has not yet occurred. To build a defect of imagination into our social welfare functions is not to discover a deep truth about value; it is to launder a cognitive bias into an ethical principle.

On the mathematics of the optimal savings rate

Ramsey formula: the discount rate should reflect growth and elasticity — not include a pure time preference termMy 1928 paper derived what is now called the Ramsey rule: in an optimally saving economy, the social rate of return on investment equals the rate of growth of consumption multiplied by the elasticity of marginal utility of consumption. This gives us a principled basis for the social discount rate that makes no reference to pure time preference whatsoever. The discount rate that governs intertemporal choices need not include a "pure" impatience term. The relevant considerations are: how fast will consumption grow (affecting the marginal utility of a future unit of wealth), and how strongly does marginal utility decline with rising income. Both of these are empirically tractable and morally relevant — they measure real differences in the value of a unit of welfare, not mere temporal distance. The opposition's preference for a positive pure time preference rate adds an unjustified term to the formula — one whose only function is to make future welfare count for less.

On the separateness of persons across time

Intertemporal separateness: future persons are as real and as separate as present persons; temporal location is morally arbitraryThe opposition sometimes argues that pure time preference is justified by an analogy with prudential discounting — the fact that individuals rationally weight their own future welfare at a discount because they may not survive to enjoy it. But this analogy fails when we move from individual to social decision-making. When I discount my own future welfare, I am at least discounting welfare that belongs to the same person across time. When the state discounts future welfare, it is discounting the welfare of future persons — persons as real and as separate as present persons, who happen to occupy a different temporal position. The social discount rate is not a prudential calculation; it is an ethical weighting of different persons' welfare. And persons do not become less morally significant merely because they have not yet been born. Temporal location is as morally arbitrary as spatial location or nationality.

On the catastrophic implications of high discount rates

Catastrophic discounting: high discount rates render any harm occurring beyond fifty years practically weightlessA practical observation that deserves the force of a philosophical argument: at conventional discount rates of five or six percent per year, the welfare of people living a century hence is worth, in present value terms, less than one percent of the same welfare today. At such rates, a catastrophe that kills a billion people in two hundred years has negligible present value. The present value of all human welfare for the next millennium — all the people who will ever live — is dominated by the welfare of people alive today and in the next few decades. This is not a technical quirk. It is a moral monstrosity. It means that our decisions about long-run investment, about climate change, about the preservation of conditions for human life, are made on the implicit assumption that the billions of people who will live after us barely exist. This is what pure time preference, in practice, recommends. I do not see how anyone who has followed the argument to this point can regard it as ethically defensible.

On growth and the ethics of diminishing marginal utility

Growth discount: richer future generations need less help per unit; this justifies some discounting but not pure time preferenceI want to be careful about one thing. There is a form of discounting that is entirely defensible — indeed, required. If we expect future generations to be richer than present generations, then a marginal unit of wealth will be worth less to them — their marginal utility of consumption will be lower. This is the growth-based component of the discount rate, and it has a firm ethical foundation in the declining marginal utility of income. I do not dispute it. But this growth discount is entirely separate from pure time preference. It says: future welfare counts equally, but future people may need less of certain resources because they are better off. It does not say: future welfare counts for less. The two components must not be conflated. When they are, the morally arbitrary component — pure time preference — smuggles itself into the formula under cover of the legitimate growth component.

On the extinction of civilisation as the limit case

Extinction: the destruction of all future life has near-zero present value under high discount rates — a decisive reductioLet me close with the limit case. Suppose an action today would, with certainty, prevent all human civilisation after the year 2200. Trillions of people who might otherwise have lived would not exist; all future culture, science, love, and achievement would be extinguished. Under a discount rate of five percent, this catastrophe has a present value of roughly zero. The entire future of humanity is worth less, in present-discounted terms, than a modest GDP increase in the near term. If this does not constitute a reductio ad absurdum of high discount rates — if the destruction of all future humanity is a price worth paying for present convenience — then I do not know what an absurdity looks like. Pure time preference, pursued to its logical conclusion, makes the long-run future of civilisation invisible to our moral calculus. This is not a technical problem with the discount rate. It is a moral catastrophe embedded in our decision procedures.

I — Pure time preference: mere temporal distance is not a morally relevant difference
1.Pure time preference discounts future welfare solely on account of its temporal distance — not because of uncertainty, growth, or any other morally relevant factor.
2.Temporal distance, like spatial distance, is not itself a morally relevant difference between persons — it does not affect the reality or significance of their welfare.
3.A principle that assigns different moral weight to welfare solely on the basis of morally irrelevant differences is arbitrary and indefensible.
∴Pure time preference is ethically indefensible — it discriminates against future persons on the basis of a morally arbitrary feature, temporal location, exactly as spatial discrimination would.
II — Telescopic defect: discounting reflects cognitive bias, not moral principle
1.Empirical psychology shows that agents discount the future partly because they cannot vividly imagine distant future welfare — Pigou's "defective telescopic faculty."
2.Cognitive biases — failures of imagination — do not generate moral principles; describing how people do reason is not prescribing how they should reason.
3.A prescriptive social welfare function that encodes cognitive biases launders psychological failings into ethical principles.
∴The observed preference for present over future welfare reflects a failure of moral imagination — building pure time preference into social welfare functions converts a bias into a norm, which is methodologically and ethically impermissible.
III — The Ramsey formula: a principled discount rate with no pure time preference term
1.The Ramsey rule states that the socially optimal discount rate equals the elasticity of marginal utility times the growth rate of consumption.
2.Both components — elasticity of marginal utility and consumption growth — are morally and empirically grounded: they measure real differences in the value of a unit of welfare.
3.A pure time preference term adds nothing to this formula except a discount for temporal distance — which we have established is not morally relevant.
∴The Ramsey formula provides a fully principled social discount rate without pure time preference — the opposition's insistence on a positive impatience term adds an unjustified, morally arbitrary component.
IV — Intertemporal separateness: social discounting cannot borrow from individual prudence
1.Individual prudential discounting may have some justification as a response to mortality and the uncertainty of personal continuity.
2.Social discount rates weight the welfare of different persons — future people who are as real and morally significant as present people, just temporally displaced.
3.Justifications for individual impatience — mortality, personal identity uncertainty — do not transfer to the social case, where we are comparing welfare across distinct persons.
∴The prudential analogy fails — social discounting is an inter-personal, not intra-personal, comparison; pure time preference in the social case amounts to valuing some persons' welfare less than others' without moral justification.
V — Catastrophic discounting: high discount rates render distant harm invisible
1.At a five percent annual discount rate, welfare one hundred years hence has a present value of less than one percent of the same welfare today.
2.This renders catastrophes affecting people beyond a few generations practically weightless in cost-benefit analysis — the destruction of civilisation in two centuries is of negligible present value.
3.Any decision procedure that assigns near-zero weight to the destruction of all future humanity is morally catastrophic, whatever its technical properties.
∴High discount rates incorporating pure time preference lead to a moral reductio — they make the long-run future of civilisation invisible to our decision procedures; this is not a technical inconvenience but a fundamental ethical failure.
VI — Growth discount vs pure time preference: the two must not be conflated
1.Growth-based discounting is justified: if future generations are richer, the marginal utility of a unit of wealth to them is lower, so we should invest less in their welfare per unit now.
2.This growth discount says future welfare counts equally, but that future people may need less; it does not say future welfare counts for less per se.
3.Pure time preference adds an additional, morally unjustified discount on top: it says future welfare counts for less just because it is future — which no growth consideration can justify.
∴Conflating the two components allows pure time preference to smuggle itself into the discount rate under cover of the defensible growth component — they must be disaggregated, and only the growth component is morally justified.
VII — Extinction reductio: pure time preference at scale implies all future humanity is worthless
1.An action permanently destroying all human civilisation after 2200 would, under a five percent discount rate, have present cost approaching zero — dwarfed by any modest near-term benefit.
2.The conclusion that it would be rational to trade all future humanity for near-term convenience is a decisive moral reductio of any decision procedure that generates it.
3.The only way to avoid this reductio is to set the pure time preference component to zero or near zero — as Ramsey originally proposed.
∴The extinction reductio is a decisive argument against any significant pure time preference in social welfare functions — the near-zero discount rate is not a contestable political choice but a requirement of coherent moral reasoning.

Mr President,

My colleague Ramsey has established the philosophical foundations. I want to press those foundations into the field where they do their most urgent work — the economics of climate change — and to meet head-on the arguments of those who insist that standard market discount rates should govern our response to a warming planet.

On the Stern Review and the discount rate controversy

Stern Review: near-zero pure time preference implies urgent, costly climate action; Nordhaus's higher rate implies delayThe central controversy about the 2006 Stern Review was not, in the end, primarily about climate science. It was about discount rates. I used a pure time preference rate of just 0.1 percent per year — close to Ramsey's zero — combined with a standard estimate of the elasticity of marginal utility. Nordhaus, using a pure time preference rate of around 1.5 percent and a lower elasticity, arrived at a social discount rate of roughly five percent. The difference in recommended policy was dramatic: my analysis suggested that the costs of unmitigated climate change could amount to the equivalent of losing between five and twenty percent of global GDP, permanently, and that aggressive early action was warranted. Nordhaus's analysis suggested a slower, more modest response. The same physical science; the same economic models; radically different policy conclusions — all downstream of a single ethical parameter: how much do we care about people in the future?

On the asymmetry of catastrophic risk

Catastrophic risk asymmetry: the risk of severe, irreversible climate damage is not well-handled by standard discountingThe case for near-zero pure time preference becomes even stronger when we attend to the specific character of climate risks. Climate change does not merely threaten a steady deterioration in welfare that can be smoothly discounted back to the present. It threatens tail risks — low-probability, high-consequence events that could be catastrophic and irreversible. Weitzman has shown that the standard expected-value framework for cost-benefit analysis breaks down when probability distributions have fat tails: the expected present discounted cost of low-probability catastrophes can be infinite, or at least dominated by scenarios that standard models marginalise. The appropriate response to this uncertainty is not to discount more aggressively — that would make the catastrophe even less visible — but to treat the possibility of irreversible catastrophic harm as a dominant consideration, which requires discounting future welfare much less than market rates imply.

On why market discount rates are the wrong guide for social decisions

Market rates are descriptive, not prescriptive: they reflect observed behaviour, including the cognitive biases Ramsey identifiedThe opposition will argue that we should use market discount rates — observed rates of return on capital — rather than philosophically derived prescriptive rates. This argument confuses two questions: the question of how people do behave, and the question of how policy should be made on behalf of present and future generations. Market rates reflect the revealed preferences of present consumers and investors. But those revealed preferences embody exactly the cognitive biases and failures of imagination that Ramsey identified — they reflect present impatience, not considered moral judgement about the equal standing of future persons. Market rates also fail to account for intergenerational externalities: transactions between present people cannot price in the welfare of future people who are not party to those transactions. The proposition is not that people should be forced to save at the socially optimal rate in their private lives; it is that social decisions — about public investment, about climate policy, about infrastructure — should be governed by a rate that takes future welfare seriously.

On the ethics of growth assumptions

Growth assumptions: if climate change damages growth, discounting on growth grounds becomes circular and self-defeatingNordhaus's defence of higher discount rates depends in part on the assumption that future generations will be substantially richer than present ones — making a marginal unit of welfare worth less to them. But this assumption is not exogenous to the decision at hand. Climate change, at the severe end of the probability distribution, threatens to substantially damage economic growth. If we use growth-based discounting to justify doing less about climate change, and less action about climate change damages the very growth we were counting on, we have embedded a circularity into our decision procedure. The growth discount is defensible when growth is not threatened by the decision being made. When the decision is precisely about whether to protect the conditions for growth, the discount rate cannot presuppose that growth will occur.

On equity weighting across generations

Equity weighting: transferring resources from poorer present people to richer future people requires justification that pure time preference cannot provideThere is a further dimension that the opposition's approach handles badly. Climate change mitigation transfers real resources from present generations — including the world's poor — to future generations. Whether this transfer is justified depends on the relative positions of those generations. If future generations are substantially richer, there is an equity argument against large present sacrifices on their behalf — not from pure time preference, but from a straightforward declining-marginal-utility argument. I take this equity consideration seriously. But it cuts both ways: in a world where climate change threatens to leave some future generations substantially worse off than present ones, the equity argument runs in the opposite direction — toward greater present investment in mitigation. Neither consideration supports pure time preference; both support the careful, empirically grounded Ramsey formula.

On the precautionary principle and irreversibility

Irreversibility: some climate damages are permanent; the precautionary principle demands lower discounting of irreversible harmsClimate change presents a distinctive feature that should lower the effective discount rate independently of any philosophical argument about pure time preference: irreversibility. Extinct species cannot be restored. Destabilised ice sheets cannot be re-frozen on human timescales. Societies displaced by rising seas cannot simply move back. Standard cost-benefit analysis, even with a Ramsey discount rate, tends to underweight irreversible harms because it treats all costs symmetrically. But the option value of avoiding irreversible catastrophe — keeping open the possibility of reversing course — is substantial and systematically undervalued by conventional discounting. This provides an additional, non-philosophical argument for treating future harms with near full weight: the asymmetry between reversible and irreversible outcomes is a reason to be especially risk-averse about long-run catastrophic risks.

On the political economy of discount rate choice

Political economy: high discount rates systematically benefit present wealth-holders at the expense of the unborn; this is not neutralI want to close with a political economy observation. The choice of discount rate is not a technical parameter settled by experts in the back rooms of economics departments. It is a choice with massive distributive consequences — it determines how much present wealth-holders must sacrifice for the benefit of future generations. A high discount rate, by rendering future welfare nearly invisible, systematically favours the interests of those alive today — and in particular the wealthiest, who are the primary beneficiaries of the economic activities whose externalities fall on the future. A low discount rate redistributes moral weight toward those who cannot vote, cannot lobby, cannot be present in our political deliberations at all: future persons. The choice between these rates is not merely technical; it is political and ethical. And the ethics, as Ramsey saw almost a century ago, are clear.

Mr President,

The proposition has offered us a beautiful piece of Cambridge moral philosophy. But beautiful philosophy, imposed on an economy of billions of present people who are poor, insecure, and have their own urgent claims on resources, can be a form of tyranny dressed up as ethics. I want to defend the use of market-based discount rates — not because I am indifferent to future generations, but because I believe the alternative produces worse outcomes for everyone, present and future alike.

On the descriptive approach to discounting

Descriptive discounting: market rates reflect actual preferences of actual people and should guide social decisionsThe proposition insists on a prescriptive approach — deriving the discount rate from ethical first principles and imposing it on economic decisions regardless of what people actually want. I prefer the descriptive approach: using market interest rates that reflect the actual choices of actual persons about how to trade present for future consumption. Why? Because these rates are the ones that govern real investment decisions in a market economy. If the social discount rate diverges dramatically from market rates, we face a practical problem: the policy recommendations derived from the social rate are not implementable within real economic institutions. More fundamentally: the proposition assumes that we, the philosopher-economists, know better than actual people how much they should sacrifice for the future. This is a form of paternalism — and a particularly sweeping one, since it involves overriding the preferences of the entire present generation in favour of generations who do not yet exist.

On the savings rate paradox

Savings paradox: near-zero pure time preference implies current generations should save far more than they do or canHere is the most devastating practical objection to Ramsey's and Stern's position. If pure time preference should be zero, and if there are investment opportunities available now that yield positive returns in the future, then present generations are morally obligated to save and invest at very high rates — to sacrifice present consumption for future gain. The Ramsey formula with δ=0 and positive expected growth implies an optimal savings rate that is dramatically higher than what any existing economy achieves or that any political system has ever successfully imposed. Applied consistently, the logic of near-zero discounting would require us to drastically reduce present consumption — including the consumption of the global poor — to fund investments whose benefits will accrue to people who will, on reasonable growth assumptions, be far richer than we are. A moral theory that implies we are all currently acting catastrophically wrongly by not transferring enormous resources from the present poor to the future rich has gone somewhere wrong.

On the governance of real capital allocation

Capital allocation: a prescriptive rate far below market rates produces economic incoherence in a mixed economyIn a mixed market economy, public investment decisions are embedded in a context of private investment governed by market rates. If the public sector uses a social discount rate of one percent while the private sector uses rates of five to eight percent, the result is not an optimal allocation across generations — it is a massive misallocation of capital within the present. Projects with low but certain long-run returns are funded instead of projects with higher present returns; the dynamic efficiency of the economy is damaged; and the tax base that funds future public services is eroded. The proposition imagines a world in which we can simply declare a near-zero social discount rate and implement it. In the real world of mixed economies with private capital markets, a prescriptive rate that diverges sharply from market rates produces incoherence, not justice.

On uncertainty as justification for positive discounting

Uncertainty justification: pure time preference may proxy for real uncertainty about whether future benefits will be receivedI want to offer a partial defence of positive discount rates that does not rely on the ethically indefensible argument that future welfare matters less per se. Future benefits are genuinely uncertain: the people who will receive them may not exist as predicted; the projects generating them may fail; economic, political, or technological shocks may render them worthless. A positive discount rate may function as an implicit correction for this genuine uncertainty — not as a statement that future welfare is less important, but as a recognition that future receipts are less certain than present ones. This is not the morally objectionable form of pure time preference that Ramsey attacked. It is a rational response to genuine epistemic uncertainty about the future.

On the DICE model and gradualism

DICE model: optimal climate policy under market rates involves gradual, increasing carbon taxes — not radical near-term sacrificeMy Dynamic Integrated model of Climate and the Economy — the DICE model — uses market-consistent discount rates to evaluate climate policy. The result is not that we should do nothing about climate change. It is that we should implement an optimal carbon tax that starts modestly, rises steadily as the damages of climate change become more severe, and is complemented by investment in clean technology. This gradualist approach outperforms both the do-nothing position and the radical mitigation Stern recommends — because it respects the opportunity costs of present resources, maintains the incentives for investment and growth that will make future generations better placed to adapt, and avoids the enormous transfers from present to future that Stern's approach requires. The ethical case for gradualism is not indifference to the future; it is a considered judgement that sustainable, growth-compatible climate policy does more good — over all generations — than heroic present sacrifice.

On the intergenerational equity objection to Stern

Future generations will be richer: transferring from poorer present to richer future is regressive, not progressiveThe proposition affects the language of progressive redistribution on behalf of the future poor. But the empirical facts do not support this framing. On reasonable growth projections, people living in 2150 will be vastly richer than people living today — including the world's present poor. Stern's policy prescriptions involve enormous present sacrifices — particularly by developing countries that are currently trying to industrialise — to protect the welfare of people who will, in expectation, enjoy standards of living unimaginable today. This is not progressive redistribution. It is a transfer from the present poor to the future rich, justified by a philosophical argument that overrides the preferences of those making the sacrifice. A genuinely egalitarian approach would be more concerned with present global poverty than with the welfare of people who will, in all probability, be far better off than we are.

On why Ramsey's argument is not decisive

Ramsey's argument is not decisive: legitimate components of the discount rate justify substantially positive rates even with δ=0Let me be clear about what I accept and what I reject. I accept that bare temporal distance is not, by itself, a morally relevant difference between persons. I accept that pure time preference — in Ramsey's strict sense — is difficult to defend in the abstract. What I deny is that this concession delivers the near-zero discount rate Ramsey and Stern recommend. The social discount rate includes multiple components: pure time preference, uncertainty corrections, the opportunity cost of capital, and the growth-based component. Even if the pure time preference component is zero, the resulting discount rate — properly accounting for uncertainty and opportunity cost — will be substantially positive. Stern's choice of near-zero overall discount rate is not mandated by the ethical argument against pure time preference; it represents a choice of parameters that conveniently supports the policy conclusions he arrived at by other means.

1.The DICE model, using market-consistent rates, recommends a rising carbon tax and technology investment — not inaction but also not the radical present sacrifice Stern implies.
2.Gradualist climate policy respects opportunity costs of present resources and maintains growth incentives that will make future generations better equipped to adapt.
3.A policy optimised over all generations using realistic discount rates achieves better total outcomes than heroic present sacrifice that damages present welfare and democratic sustainability.
∴Optimal gradualism under market-consistent rates is not indifference to the future — it is a multi-generational optimisation that delivers better total welfare than either extreme; the choice is not between caring and not caring but between coherent and incoherent policies.
VI — Intergenerational equity: transfers from present poor to future rich are regressive
1.On reasonable growth projections, people in 2150 will be far wealthier than people alive today — including the world's present poor.
2.Stern's prescriptions require large present sacrifices — particularly by developing countries — to protect the welfare of people who will in expectation be enormously better off.
3.Transferring resources from poorer present to richer future generations is distributively regressive — the opposite of progressive redistribution, whatever philosophical language accompanies it.
∴Near-zero discounting is not the progressive policy it presents itself as — it requires regressive transfers from present poor to future rich; a genuinely egalitarian approach would prioritise present global poverty reduction over future welfare enhancement.
VII — Ramsey's argument is not decisive: uncertainty and opportunity cost justify substantial positive rates
1.Accepting that pure time preference is ethically questionable does not imply the total social discount rate should be near zero.
2.Uncertainty corrections, opportunity costs of capital, and the growth component can together generate substantially positive social discount rates even with δ=0.
3.Stern's near-zero overall rate requires setting all these other components implausibly low — a choice of parameters not mandated by the ethical argument against pure time preference.
∴Conceding the argument against pure time preference does not concede Stern's overall rate — the legitimate components of the discount rate, properly estimated, generate substantially positive rates; Stern's near-zero rate goes beyond what ethics requires.

Mr President,

I want to approach this question from a different direction from my colleague Nordhaus. I share Ramsey's conviction that pure time preference, as he defined it, is difficult to defend. But I want to argue that the philosophical foundations of our obligations to future generations are far more complex — and far stranger — than Ramsey's elegant formula suggests. The problem of discounting future welfare is entangled with some of the deepest and most unresolved puzzles in population ethics; and the proposition, in my view, has papered over those puzzles with a deceptively clean analysis.

On the non-identity problem

Non-identity problem: our long-run policy choices affect which people will exist; we cannot harm someone by causing them to existHere is a problem that Ramsey's framework does not address. Our policy choices today — including our choices about climate change — will affect not just the welfare of future people, but which future people exist. Different energy policies, different patterns of economic development, different global political arrangements, will lead to different people meeting, marrying, and having children. Over the span of centuries, virtually no one who will actually exist under one policy regime would have existed under a different one. This is the non-identity problem. If our policies lead to a world where people exist who would not otherwise have existed, and those people live lives worth living, we have not harmed them — even if their lives are somewhat worse than the lives of the people who would have existed under a more protective policy. They were not harmed; they were created. And one cannot harm someone by bringing them into a life that is worth living. This creates a profound puzzle for the proposition's framework: it cannot be that we wrong future people by our choices if the very people who will judge themselves wronged owe their existence to those choices.

On the repugnant conclusion

Repugnant conclusion: total welfare maximisation implies a vast population of barely-worth-living lives is better than a small flourishing oneRamsey's optimal savings framework, and Stern's welfare economics, work within an additive, aggregative framework: the goal is to maximise the sum of welfare over all persons across all time. But this framework — total utilitarianism — leads to what I call the Repugnant Conclusion. A world containing ten billion very happy people is judged, by total utilitarian calculus, to be worse than a world containing one hundred billion people whose lives are barely worth living — because the summed total of the larger, barely-satisfied population exceeds the summed total of the smaller, flourishing one. If we take seriously the goal of maximising the present-discounted value of all future welfare, we are implicitly endorsing this framework. But the conclusion is repugnant — as I believe most people immediately recognise. Any ethical framework that leads to the Repugnant Conclusion must be either false or in need of substantial revision. Ramsey's analysis, and Stern's, import a utilitarian aggregation structure that has not been shown to avoid this and related conclusions.

On the asymmetry between creating and harming

Asymmetry: we have strong duties not to harm existing people, but our duties toward non-existent potential people are different and weakerThere is a widespread and, I believe, correct intuition about our obligations with respect to future people. We have a strong duty not to harm people who will exist — that is, people whose existence is already determined and whose welfare will be affected by our choices. But we do not have the same strong duty to create additional people so as to maximise welfare. The asymmetry is this: causing misery to people who will exist is a moral wrong; but failing to create happy people who would otherwise not exist is not in the same moral category. Now, this asymmetry complicates the proposition's framework in a specific way. If climate change reduces the welfare of people who will exist in any case — because they will be born and will suffer from its effects — then we do wrong them, and Ramsey's framework is appropriate. But if our choices affect which people exist — as they will over long periods — then the obligations are different and less clear-cut. The proposition has not distinguished these cases; it has treated all future welfare as equivalent, when the ethics are asymmetric.

On personal identity and the continuity of persons across time

Personal identity: the self is not a persistent, unified entity; this complicates intertemporal welfare comparisonsRamsey's framework treats each person across time as a unified welfare subject — my welfare in twenty years counts the same as my welfare today, and the welfare of a person born in a century counts the same as mine now. But the metaphysics of personal identity across time is more complex than this. There is serious philosophical debate about whether the person I will be in twenty years is really "me" in any deep sense — whether personal identity is a matter of psychological continuity, physical continuity, or some more complex relation. I argued in Reasons and Persons that the self is not a deep, persistent entity; personal identity is a conventional matter of degree. If this is right, then the intra-personal analogy that Ramsey uses to ground inter-personal discounting is more complicated than it appears — and the same complications apply to our ethical relations with future generations, who may bear only the most attenuated continuity relations with present people.

On the independence of moral status from welfare level

Moral status is not proportional to welfare level: this conflicts with additive welfare frameworksHere is a further puzzle for the proposition's welfare-aggregative framework. In additive welfare economics, a person with a very low welfare level contributes less to the total than a person with a high welfare level. But this conflicts with our intuition that moral status — the weight a person's welfare and interests should receive in ethical deliberation — does not vary with welfare level. A barely-surviving person is not a lesser moral patient than a flourishing one; their suffering deserves equal consideration even if their welfare score is lower. This suggests that the additive framework underpinning Ramsey's and Stern's analysis — which discounts welfare levels that are lower, and aggregates them with those that are higher, to produce a total to be maximised — may not correctly represent what actually matters morally. The relationship between welfare as measured by economists and moral weight as required by ethics is not the simple identity that Stern's framework assumes.

On uncertainty about future populations

Population uncertainty: we do not know how many people will exist in the future; this radically complicates welfare maximisationA practical consequence of the non-identity problem and related puzzles is this: the population that will exist in the future depends on present choices in ways we cannot predict with confidence. Different policies lead not just to different welfare levels for a fixed future population, but to different populations altogether — different numbers of people, with entirely different lives. This makes the task of welfare maximisation across generations radically indeterminate. When we try to add up the present value of future welfare, we do not know what that welfare is attached to — how many people, which people, living what kinds of lives. Stern's framework implicitly treats the future population as fixed, or as a risk over a distribution of fixed populations. This is a simplification that systematically understates the genuine complexity of our obligations to the future.

On what follows — humility, not high discounting

Conclusion: population ethics complexity demands humility about prescriptive frameworks, not complacency about climateI want to be clear about the implications of my arguments. I am not defending Nordhaus's market-rate discounting. I am not arguing that future welfare does not matter, or that we may treat it as nearly weightless. The non-identity problem and the other puzzles I have raised are not arguments for complacency about climate change — they are arguments for intellectual humility about our ability to derive clean prescriptive frameworks from welfare economics. The proposition has offered us Ramsey's elegant formula as if it settled the question. I have argued that the question is harder than Ramsey's formula acknowledges. Our obligations to future people are genuine, but they are also strange, complex, and not fully captured by the additive welfare-maximising framework that the proposition deploys. We need a richer ethics of the future — not just a lower discount rate.
