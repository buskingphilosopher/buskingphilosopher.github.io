Sonnet 2

Structure & Context
Form: Shakespearean sonnet — ABAB CDCD EFEF GG; the couplet delivers the argument's resolution Sequence: Continues directly from Sonnet 1 — the same young man, the same accusation, but now projected forty years forward Rhetorical shift: Sonnet 1 accused; Sonnet 2 imagines — the young man is placed inside his own future and made to feel it before it arrives Dominant metaphors: War (besiege, trenches), finance (treasure, use, sum, count), law (excuse), and clothing (livery, weed) — all operating simultaneously Volta: Line 9 — 'How much more praise' pivots from accusation to the positive case: what reproduction would actually give him Intertextuality: Several words echo Sonnet 1 deliberately — 'lies,' 'all-eating,' 'treasure' — building a cumulative argument across poems

When forty winters shall besiege thy brow, And dig deep trenches in thy beauty's field, Thy youth's proud livery, so gazed on now, Will be a tattered weed of small worth held: Then being asked where all thy beauty lies, Where all the treasure of thy lusty days, To say within thine own deep sunken eyes Were an all-eating shame and thriftless praise. How much more praise deserved thy beauty's use, If thou couldst answer This fair child of mine
Shall sum my count, and make my old excuse, Making beauty still through beauty's act new: This were to be new made when thou art old, And see thy blood warm when thou feel'st it cold.
