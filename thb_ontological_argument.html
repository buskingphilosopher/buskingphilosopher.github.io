The Original ArgumentAnselm of Canterbury — Proslogion, ch. 2–3 (1078)
God is, by definition, that than which nothing greater can be conceived (id quo maius cogitari nequit). Even the fool who denies God has this concept in his understanding. But if it existed only in the understanding and not in reality, we could conceive of something greater — the same being existing in reality as well — which contradicts the definition. Therefore that-than-which-nothing-greater-can-be-conceived exists in reality. The argument depends on greatness admitting of a unique, coherent maximum — a feature, I shall argue against Gaunilo, that distinguishes perfection-in-general from any merely quantitative excellence like an island's fertility.

The Modal Ontological ArgumentAlvin Plantinga — The Nature of Necessity (1974), ch. 10
Define maximal excellence as the possession of omniscience, omnipotence, and moral perfection in a given world, and maximal greatness as the possession of maximal excellence in every possible world. The key premise is: possibly, maximal greatness is exemplified. Given the S5 axiom — if possibly necessarily P, then necessarily P — it follows that maximal greatness is exemplified in every possible world, including the actual one. The argument is valid. Whether it is a proof, in the sense of compelling assent, depends on whether the possibility premise is more reasonable to accept than its denial — and I will argue it is at least not less reasonable.

The Formalization via Positive PropertiesKurt Gödel — ontological proof, notes c. 1941–1970, published 1987
Take "positive property" as a primitive, governed by axioms: positive properties are closed under entailment and conjunction (including infinite conjunction); for any property, exactly one of it and its negation is positive; the property of God-likeness — having every positive property — is itself positive; every positive property is necessarily positive; and necessary existence is positive. From these axioms, in second-order S5 modal logic, it is a theorem that God-likeness is necessarily exemplified. The argument's validity has been machine-verified. The substantive dispute concerns the axioms themselves — and I will address the modal collapse result directly.

The Modal Reconstruction: Proslogion 3Norman Malcolm — "Anselm's Ontological Arguments" (1960); Charles Hartshorne, Anselm's Discovery (1965)
Anselm in fact gave two arguments. Proslogion 2 treats existence as a perfection and is genuinely vulnerable to Kant's objection — I concede this. But Proslogion 3 argues that necessary existence — existence such that non-existence is impossible — is a perfection, and a being whose non-existence is possible is thereby less great than one whose non-existence is impossible. Given the concept of God, God's existence is either impossible, contingent, or necessary; contingent existence is ruled out by the very concept of a being dependent on nothing; so God's existence is impossible or necessary. The entire argument therefore reduces to: is the concept of God possibly instantiated? If it is not impossible, it is necessary — and hence actual.

Chair,

I want to set out the argument exactly as I gave it, because nine centuries of commentary have produced many paraphrases that subtly alter its structure, and then I want to address directly the objection that was put to me in my own lifetime — the one the monk Gaunilo raised — because I think the response I gave then remains the correct response now, including to its modern descendants.

Even the fool, when he hears the words "that than which nothing greater can be conceived," understands what he hears, and what he understands exists in his understanding — even if he does not understand that it exists in reality.
i. the argument of proslogion 2

The reductio — the argument proceeds by reductio: assume the being-than-which-none-greater-can-be-conceived exists only in the understanding; derive that something greater than it can be conceived (namely, the same being existing in reality too); but nothing can be greater than that-than-which-nothing-greater-can-be-conceived, by definition — contradiction; therefore the assumption is falseThe argument has the structure of a reductio. Suppose, for contradiction, that that-than-which-nothing-greater-can-be-conceived (call it G) exists only in the understanding and not in reality. Now, it is greater to exist in reality and in the understanding than to exist in the understanding alone — this is, I take it, simply true of greatness as it applies to whatever we are considering. So if G existed only in the understanding, we could conceive of something greater than G: namely, a being just like G but which also exists in reality. But this contradicts the definition of G as that-than-which-nothing-greater-can-be-conceived — nothing can be greater than G, by hypothesis, yet we have just conceived of something greater. The assumption that G exists only in the understanding is therefore false. G exists in reality.

ii. gaunilo's island and the response i gave

The island disanalogy — Gaunilo's parody substitutes "island" for "being" and derives the absurd existence of a perfect island; my response is that this substitution is illegitimate because island-making properties (fertility, number of harbours, beauty) do not admit of an intrinsic maximum — for any island with such properties, a greater one is conceivable by simply adding more of the same; but greatness-as-such, the ordering with respect to which G is defined, is not a quantity of this kind, and a maximum for it is intrinsic to the concept of G itselfThe monk Gaunilo, in his reply on behalf of the fool, offered what he took to be a parallel argument: conceive of the island than which no greater island can be conceived; if it existed only in the mind, a greater one (existing in reality too) could be conceived; therefore it exists. Since this is absurd — no one supposes a perfect island exists merely because we can form the concept — the reasoning, applied uniformly, must be flawed. My response, which I maintain, is that the substitution of "island" for G is not a parallel case, because of a disanalogy in how "greater" functions in each case. For islands, "greater" tracks quantities — more palm trees, more gold, more inhabitants, greater fertility — each of which can always be increased further; there is no island such that a greater one, by these measures, cannot be conceived, because the measures themselves have no intrinsic ceiling. But G is defined as that-than-which-nothing-greater-can-be-conceived in an absolute sense — greatness simpliciter, not greatness along some open-ended quantitative dimension. The concept of "the greatest possible island" is, on inspection, an incoherent concept — not because islands cannot be great, but because "greatest possible" applied to island-making properties does not pick out a determinate maximum, in the way that "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" does pick out a determinate maximum when greatness is considered as such, unrestricted to any particular dimension.

iii. why greatness-as-such has a maximum and island-greatness does not

Intrinsic maximum — the disanalogy can be put more sharply: properties like knowledge, power, and goodness admit of an intrinsic maximum (omniscience, omnipotence, perfect goodness) — there is a coherent notion of "as much as it is possible to have," because these properties have a natural endpoint defined by the logical limits of the property itself; quantities like "number of palm trees" or "amount of gold" have no such endpoint — for any number, a greater number is conceivable, with no logical limit reachedConsider the properties in terms of which G's greatness is to be understood: knowledge, power, goodness. For each of these, there is a coherent notion of an intrinsic maximum — to know everything that can be known (omniscience), to be able to do everything that it is possible to do (omnipotence), to be good without any possible deficiency (perfect goodness). These maxima are defined by the logical limits of the properties themselves, not by comparison to some external scale that could always be extended. Now consider the properties in terms of which an island's "greatness" would have to be understood: number of palm trees, quantity of gold, number of inhabitants, variety of fruits. For each of these, there is no intrinsic maximum — for any number of palm trees, one more is conceivable; for any quantity of gold, more is conceivable; the property itself sets no logical limit. This is the disanalogy. G's greatness is constituted by properties that have intrinsic maxima; the island's greatness, as Gaunilo's parody constructs it, is constituted by properties that do not. The parody therefore does not apply the same reasoning to a parallel case; it applies superficially similar words to a case where the crucial premise — that "greatest possible X" picks out a determinate concept — fails to hold.

I yield the floor.

I want to set out the modal version of the argument with full formal precision, because much of the historical debate has turned on ambiguities that modal logic, properly applied, resolves. I also want to be candid from the outset about what I think the argument does and does not establish — because I think both overclaiming and underclaiming have obscured its actual interest.

i. maximal excellence and maximal greatness

Definitions — maximal excellence in a world W is the property of having omniscience, omnipotence, and moral perfection in W; maximal greatness is the property of having maximal excellence in every possible world — i.e., having those properties not merely as actual facts but as essential, world-invariant properties of the being in questionDefine maximal excellence as follows: a being has maximal excellence in a world W just in case it has omniscience, omnipotence, and moral perfection in W. Now define maximal greatness: a being has maximal greatness just in case it has maximal excellence in every possible world. Note the difference. A being could have maximal excellence in the actual world — be, as things actually stand, omniscient, omnipotent, and morally perfect — without having maximal greatness, if there is some other possible world in which that very being lacks one of these properties, or fails to exist at all. Maximal greatness requires having maximal excellence essentially and necessarily — in every world, including worlds quite unlike the actual one.

ii. the s5 derivation

S5 derivation — Premise: possibly, maximal greatness is exemplified, i.e. ◇∃x(MG(x)). If maximal greatness is exemplified in some world W, then by definition the being in question has maximal excellence in every world accessible from W — but since maximal greatness is itself a modal property (concerning all worlds), if it is exemplified at all, it is exemplified necessarily: □∃x(MG(x)) follows from ∃x(MG(x)) at W together with the content of MG. So ◇□∃x(MG(x)). By the S5 axiom (◇□P → □P), □∃x(MG(x)). And □P → P, so ∃x(MG(x)) — maximal greatness is exemplified in the actual world, and that being is, in the actual world, omniscient, omnipotent, and morally perfect — i.e., God existsThe argument runs as follows. Premise: possibly, maximal greatness is exemplified — formally, ◇∃x MG(x). Now, if maximal greatness is exemplified in some possible world W, then the being that exemplifies it has, by the very definition of maximal greatness, maximal excellence in every world accessible from W — including, if accessibility is an equivalence relation (as in S5), every possible world whatsoever. So if maximal greatness is exemplified anywhere, it is exemplified necessarily: from ∃x MG(x) at W, we get □∃x MG(x) holding at W — that is, ◇□∃x MG(x) is true. Now apply the characteristic S5 axiom: ◇□P → □P. This gives us □∃x MG(x) — maximal greatness is exemplified in every possible world. Since the actual world is a possible world, □P → P gives us ∃x MG(x) actually — and the being that exemplifies maximal greatness is, in the actual world, omniscient, omnipotent, and morally perfect. That is what we mean, in this context, by "God." The argument is valid; every step follows from the S5 axioms and the definitions given.

iii. rational acceptability, not proof

Rational acceptability — I do not claim this argument is a "proof" in the sense of a demonstration from premises that any rational person must accept on pain of irrationality; I claim only that the central premise — possibly, maximal greatness is exemplified — is not unreasonable to accept, and indeed may be more reasonable to accept than to deny, for someone without an independent reason to think theism false; this establishes that theistic belief can be rational, which is the limited but real claim the argument supportsI want to be precise about the dialectical status of this argument, because I think both its proponents and its critics have sometimes overstated what a valid argument of this kind can do. I do not claim that the argument is a "proof" in the sense that would compel assent from any rational agent who understood it — that no one could rationally deny the possibility premise. What I claim is more modest and, I think, more defensible: the premise that maximal greatness is possibly exemplified is not an unreasonable premise to accept. It is, on the face of it, no less plausible than its denial — the claim that maximal greatness is impossible, that there is some hidden incoherence in the very idea of a being with omniscience, omnipotence, and moral perfection essentially and necessarily. Absent some independent argument establishing such an incoherence, a person who already finds theism attractive on other grounds is not being irrational in accepting the possibility premise — and given the S5 derivation, accepting that premise commits one to the conclusion. The argument's value, then, is not that it manufactures belief from neutral ground, but that it shows the rational acceptability of theism is at least as well-grounded, from the possibility premise alone, as the rational acceptability of its denial.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Maximal greatness (maximal excellence in every world) is distinct from, and stronger than, maximal excellence in the actual world alone
1.Maximal excellence in W: omniscience, omnipotence, and moral perfection in W.
2.Maximal greatness: maximal excellence in every possible world — these properties held essentially and necessarily, not merely actually.
3.A being could have maximal excellence actually without maximal greatness, if some other world finds it lacking; maximal greatness is the modally robust property.
∴Maximal greatness is a coherent, well-defined modal property distinct from mere actual maximal excellence, and it is this property the argument's possibility premise concerns.
Argument II — The S5 derivation: from "possibly maximal greatness is exemplified" to "maximal greatness is exemplified" via ◇□P → □P
1.Premise: ◇∃x MG(x) — possibly, maximal greatness is exemplified.
2.If MG is exemplified at any world W, then by its own definition (maximal excellence in every world), its exemplification at W entails □∃x MG(x) holds at W — i.e. ◇□∃x MG(x).

1.A "proof" in the strong sense would compel assent from any rational agent; this argument does not claim that status.

∴The argument's contribution is to establish the rational acceptability, not the compulsory truth, of theism — a real but limited result that does not depend on the premise being uncontroversial, only on its not being less reasonable than its denial.

I want to present a formalization that replaces the somewhat informal notion of "greatness" with a precisely axiomatized primitive — "positive property" — and to show that, given a small number of axioms governing this primitive, the existence of a God-like being is a theorem of second-order modal logic. I will then address directly the result that has generated the most discussion: the modal collapse.

i. positive properties and god-likeness

Axioms — A1: if P is positive and P entails Q, then Q is positive (closure under entailment). A2: the conjunction of any set of positive properties is positive (closure under conjunction, including infinite conjunction). A3: for any property P, either P or its negation is positive, but not both (exhaustiveness/exclusivity). Define God-like: x is God-like iff x has every positive property. A4: being God-like is itself a positive property. A5: positive properties are necessarily positive (if P is positive, then necessarily P is positive)Take "positive property" as a primitive notion, governed by the following axioms. A1: if P is a positive property and P entails Q (necessarily, everything with P has Q), then Q is positive — positivity is closed under logical entailment. A2: for any set of positive properties, their conjunction is positive — this includes infinite conjunctions, which will matter shortly. A3: for any property P, exactly one of P and its negation ¬P is positive — positivity is exhaustive and exclusive. Now define: x is God-like iff x has every positive property — that is, God-likeness is the conjunction of all positive properties, which by A2 is itself a property (and, I will assert as A4, a positive one). A5: every positive property is necessarily positive — if P is positive, then □(P is positive).

ii. essence, necessary existence, and the theorem

Essence and necessary existence — define: P is an essence of x iff x has P and P entails every property x has necessarily. Theorem (from A1-A5): if x is God-like, then God-likeness is an essence of x — every property x has necessarily follows from God-likeness. Define necessary existence: x has necessary existence iff, for every essence P of x, necessarily something has P. Axiom A6: necessary existence is a positive property. Since God-likeness entails necessary existence (it is itself a candidate essence, and is positive), and God-likeness is positive (A4), by A1 necessary existence is among the properties any God-like being has — and since necessary existence is positive, and positive properties are possibly co-exemplified with God-likeness (by a compossibility lemma from A1-A3), it follows that if God-likeness is possibly exemplified, it is necessarily exemplified, hence actually exemplifiedDefine: P is an essence of x just in case x has P, and P entails every property that x has necessarily. It follows from A1–A5 that if x is God-like, then God-likeness is an essence of x — because God-likeness, being the conjunction of all positive properties, entails (via A1) every property x has necessarily, given that necessary properties of a God-like thing must themselves be positive or entailed by positive properties. Now define: x has necessary existence iff, for every essence P of x, it is necessary that something exemplifies P. Add the final axiom, A6: necessary existence is a positive property. Since God-likeness is positive (A4) and entails necessary existence (via the essence relation just established, together with A6), and since A1–A3 jointly entail that any set of positive properties is possibly co-exemplified (no two positive properties can be incompatible, since if P and Q are positive but ¬(P&Q), then by A1 ¬Q would be entailed by P and hence positive, contradicting A3's exclusivity for Q), it follows that God-likeness is possibly exemplified — and since God-likeness entails necessary existence, if it is possibly exemplified, it is necessarily exemplified, and hence actually exemplified. This is the theorem. It has been verified by automated theorem provers operating directly on the axioms stated.

iii. the modal collapse and how to read it

Modal collapse — the formalization, as stated, has been shown to entail □p → p for any true p whatsoever (everything actually true is necessarily true) — the so-called "modal collapse." This is often presented as a reductio of the axioms. My response is that the collapse follows specifically from A3's exclusivity clause combined with the assumption that all true propositions correspond to positive properties of some object; weakening A3 to avoid this consequence, while retaining A1, A2, A4-A6, is a live option that several formalizations have pursued, preserving the theorem while avoiding collapse — the substantive debate is therefore about which axioms to retain, not about whether the argument as I originally intended it is soundThe result that has attracted the most technical attention is this: the axioms as formalized entail not merely that a God-like being necessarily exists, but the much stronger claim that □p → p for every true proposition p — that is, everything that is actually true is necessarily true, collapsing the distinction between the actual and the necessary entirely. This is widely presented as a reductio of the axiom set. I want to address this directly rather than minimise it. The collapse result, as the computer-verified derivations show, traces specifically to the combination of A3's exclusivity clause — exactly one of P and ¬P is positive — with the closure principles A1 and A2 applied to arbitrary propositions, not merely to the properties relevant to God-likeness. Several philosophers working on this formalization have shown that A3 can be weakened — restricting exclusivity, or restricting which properties the axioms quantify over — in ways that block the collapse while preserving the derivation of necessary existence for a God-like being. The honest statement of where matters stand is this: the original axiom set, taken exactly as I stated it, proves more than was intended, and this is a genuine problem with that exact axiom set. But the core strategy — positive properties, God-likeness as their conjunction, necessary existence as positive — survives in weakened axiomatizations that avoid collapse. The debate has moved to which weakening is independently motivated, not abandoned the strategy.

1.A1 (closure under entailment), A2 (closure under conjunction, including infinite), A3 (exactly one of P, ¬P is positive) govern the primitive "positive property."
2.God-likeness =df having every positive property — well-defined as a property by A2, the conjunction of all positive properties.
3.A4 asserts God-likeness is itself positive; A5 asserts positive properties are necessarily positive.

Argument III — The modal collapse traces to A3's unrestricted exclusivity clause; weakened axiomatizations preserve the theorem while blocking collapse, relocating rather than resolving the debate
1.The original axioms entail □p → p for all true p (modal collapse), traced specifically to A3's exclusivity combined with A1/A2 applied to arbitrary propositions.
2.Weakened versions of A3 (restricting exclusivity or the domain of properties quantified over) block the collapse while preserving the derivation of necessary God-like existence.
3.The original axiom set, exactly as stated, is therefore too strong; but the core strategy survives in motivated weakenings.
∴The modal collapse is a genuine problem for the original axiom set but not for the strategy of formalizing "greatness" via positive properties; the live debate concerns which axiom-weakening is independently motivated, not whether some such formalization can succeed.

I want to make a concession that I think clarifies, rather than weakens, the case for the ontological argument — and then build the strongest surviving version on the foundation that concession leaves intact. The concession concerns Kant, and it is this: Kant is right about Proslogion 2.

i. conceding proslogion 2 to kant

Conceding Proslogion 2 — Proslogion 2 treats "existing in reality" as an additional perfection that a being can have or lack, over and above its other properties — exactly the structure Kant's "existence is not a real predicate" objection targets; if existence-simpliciter is not a predicate that adds to a concept's content, then Proslogion 2's reductio (a being existing-in-reality-too is "greater" than the same concept existing-in-the-mind-alone) does not get off the ground, because "existing in reality" does not name a property the being could have more or less of, or have-or-lack as an addition to its conceptProslogion 2, on its most natural reading, treats "existing in reality" as a further perfection — an additional respect in which a being can be greater or lesser, over and above whatever properties are built into its concept. The reductio depends on this: a being that is F, G, H, ... and also exists in reality is greater than a being that is F, G, H, ... and exists only in the understanding. But this is exactly the structure that Kant's objection — which we will hear shortly — targets. If "exists" does not function as a predicate that adds to the content of a concept, if it is not a property a thing can have more or less of or have as an addition to its other properties, then there is no sense in which "the same being, but also existing in reality" describes something with an additional perfection that the merely-conceived being lacks. I think this objection is correct against Proslogion 2 as I have just described it. The question is whether Anselm's argument is exhausted by Proslogion 2.

ii. proslogion 3 and necessary existence

Proslogion 3 — Anselm's third chapter argues differently: a being whose non-existence is impossible is greater than a being whose non-existence is possible, even if both in fact exist. This is not a claim that "existing" is a perfection added to a concept; it is a claim about the modal status of a being's existence — whether its existence is necessary or contingent — which is a different kind of property, concerning how the being exists across possibilities, not whether a concept has an additional feature called "existence"Anselm's third chapter makes a different argument, one that Kant's objection does not touch in the same way. The claim is: a being whose non-existence is impossible — whose existence is necessary — is greater than a being whose non-existence is possible, that is, whose existence is merely contingent, even supposing both beings in fact exist. Notice what kind of claim this is. It is not the claim that "exists" is a predicate that one being has and another (otherwise identical) being lacks, with the existing one thereby being greater — that was Proslogion 2, and Kant is right about it. It is a claim about the modal profile of a being's existence: does this being exist necessarily, such that there is no possible world in which it fails to exist, or does it exist merely contingently, such that some possible worlds lack it? This is not a question of whether "exists" is added to a concept; both beings, in the comparison, exist. The question is whether their existence has a different modal status — and modal status is not the kind of thing Kant's objection, which concerns the logical role of "exists" within a concept's content, addresses.

iii. the trilemma and where the argument now rests

The trilemma — given the concept of God as a being dependent on nothing else for its existence, God's existence is either (a) impossible, (b) contingent, or (c) necessary. (b) is ruled out by the concept itself: a being dependent on nothing else cannot be the kind of thing whose existence depends on contingent circumstances (which is what contingent existence would amount to). So God's existence is either impossible or necessary. The entire ontological argument, on this reconstruction, reduces to the question: is the concept of God possibly instantiated — i.e., is (a) false? If (a) is false, (c) is the only remaining option, and necessary existence entails actual existenceGiven the concept of God — a being whose existence depends on nothing else, the ultimate ground of everything else's existence — consider the three modal statuses God's existence could have: impossible, contingent, or necessary. Contingent existence can be set aside immediately: for a being to exist contingently is for its existence to depend on, or be compatible with the absence of, certain other circumstances — but this is precisely what is excluded by the concept of a being dependent on nothing else. A being whose existence is contingent in this sense is not the being the concept of God describes; if "God" exists contingently, then "God" is not God, by the concept's own lights. So God's existence is either impossible or necessary. Now the entire weight of the argument rests on a single question, the question Plantinga's possibility premise and Gödel's axioms are, in their different formal idioms, also addressing: is the concept of God possibly instantiated — is there any incoherence in it? If there is no such incoherence — if God's existence is not impossible — then by the trilemma, God's existence is necessary, and necessary existence, whatever else it involves, entails actual existence. The ontological argument, in this form, is not a trick about definitions; it is a demonstration that the question of God's existence reduces entirely to the question of the coherence of the concept of God — a single, focused question on which the entire debate should concentrate.

i. the common pattern: dialectical inertia

Dialectical inertia — every ontological argument we have examined is valid, and every one rests on a premise (a possibility, coherence, or "exists in the understanding" claim) that a rational person who does not already accept the conclusion can reject with at least as much warrant as the theist has for accepting it. The arguments are therefore "valid but question-begging" in a sense distinct from the narrow logical sense (premises literally containing the conclusion): rational acceptance of the crucial premise tracks prior acceptance of the conclusion, so no one is moved from disbelief to belief by encountering the argument, however carefully they follow its logicConsider the pattern across the four versions we have heard. In every case: the argument is valid; and in every case, there is a premise that a rational non-theist can reject without thereby exhibiting any logical or rational failure that the theist does not exhibit in accepting it. I call this dialectical inertia. It is a stronger charge than "begging the question" in the narrow sense — none of these premises literally states the conclusion — but its dialectical effect is the same: no one is moved.

ii. leibniz's attempted rescue

Leibniz's proof — Leibniz attempted what no other defender of the ontological argument has seriously attempted: an independent argument FOR the possibility premise, rather than simply asserting it. His argument: perfections are simple, positive qualities (this is the ancestor of Gödel's "positive properties"); two simple positive qualities cannot be incompatible, because incompatibility can only arise from the analysis of complex concepts into simpler components that conflict — but simple qualities have no such internal complexity to generate conflict; therefore all perfections are compossible; therefore a being with all perfections (God) is possibleThe one figure in this history who attempted something different from simply asserting the possibility premise was Leibniz. His argument runs: perfections — this is the direct ancestor of Professor Gödel's "positive properties" — are simple, positive qualities. Incompatibility between properties, Leibniz argued, can only arise through the analysis of complex properties into components that turn out to conflict; but a simple quality, having no internal complexity, offers no components that could conflict with the components of another simple quality. Therefore, no two perfections (simple positive qualities) can be incompatible; all perfections are jointly compossible; therefore a being possessing all of them — God — is possible. This is precisely the structure of Gödel's A1–A3: the claim that positive properties cannot conflict, because conflict would require one to entail the negation of another, and by the closure and exclusivity axioms this cannot happen among properties that are all positive.

iii. why the rescue fails, and what the modal collapse confirms

Why it fails — the claim that "simple" properties cannot conflict assumes we have an independent grip on which properties are "simple" and "positive" — but we do not have such a grip prior to, and independent of, the very theory the argument is meant to support; "simplicity" and "positivity" threaten to be defined, circularly, as "whatever is needed for compossibility to hold." Gödel's modal collapse result is the technical symptom of exactly this: an axiom set strong enough to guarantee compossibility (A1-A3) turns out to be strong enough to collapse modality altogether, suggesting the axioms were not identifying an independently-motivated notion of "positive" but reverse-engineering whatever closure conditions would deliver the desired compossibility result — and reverse-engineering of this kind cannot, by its nature, supply the INDEPENDENT warrant for the possibility premise that the dialectical inertia problem requiresThe difficulty with Leibniz's argument — and with Gödel's formalization of essentially the same idea — is that it requires an independent grip on which properties count as "simple" and "positive," prior to and independent of the question the argument is meant to settle. Without such an independent grip, "simple positive quality" threatens to mean nothing more than "the kind of property that, by axioms we are about to stipulate, turns out to be compossible with every other property of its kind" — which would make the conclusion (compossibility, hence possibility, hence existence) a consequence of how the primitive was set up, not an independently established fact about the world. Professor Gödel's modal collapse result is, I suggest, the technical symptom of exactly this circularity. An axiom set strong enough to guarantee that all "positive" properties are compossible (which is what A1–A3 jointly deliver, as Professor Gödel himself showed in deriving the theorem) turns out to be strong enough to collapse the distinction between actual and necessary truth entirely — because the same closure conditions that force compossibility among the special class of "positive" properties, applied without restriction, force every truth into the necessary category. The axioms were not found by independently identifying which properties are positive and discovering, as a further fact, that they happen to be compossible; the compossibility was built in by the closure axioms themselves, and the collapse is what that much closure, applied generally, produces. Leibniz's rescue, and Gödel's formalization of it, do not supply the independent warrant for the possibility premise that would break the dialectical inertia; they relocate the assertion of compossibility into the axioms, where its cost — modal collapse — becomes visible for the first time, but its lack of independent grounding remains exactly what it always was.
