Fellow inquirers,

I am told I am the first to have asked the question before this house — what is the single stuff of which all things are made? — and I freely confess that the asking of it was itself a kind of wonder. For before me, men answered such questions with stories of gods and titans, of Okeanos encircling the world, of Gaia rising from chaos. I do not say those stories were nothing. But I say they were not yet thinking. Thinking begins when we ask: what is actually there? What persists beneath the coming and going of things? And my answer, which I put before you now not as dogma but as the best account I can give of what I have observed, is this: it is water. Everything that is, is water in some form or transformation.

On why the archē must be a determinate substance

Against indeterminacy: an archē that is no particular thing cannot explain how particular things arise; explanation requires a definite substrate with definite properties from which definite things can be derivedMy colleague Anaximander, who was my student and whom I love, will argue that the origin of all things must be the apeiron — the boundless, the indefinite, the without-limit. He will say that water cannot be the origin of all things because water is itself a determinate thing, and if it were the source of everything, it would devour its opposites: the hot would be drowned out by the wet, fire would be impossible. I have heard this argument and I do not accept it. The apeiron is no explanation at all. If I ask how this fire before me came to be, and you answer “from the boundless,” you have told me nothing. Explanation requires that the source have determinate properties from which the explained thing can be seen to follow. Water has such properties: it can be heated into vapour, cooled into ice and earth, can nourish and sustain living things. The boundless, having no properties, explains nothing.

On water as the source of life and the evidence of the senses

Water and life: all living things require moisture; seeds are moist; life begins in wet environments; the earth floats on water; nourishment is moist; these observations converge on water as the principle of life and therefore of all thingsLook at what lives and how it lives. Every seed is moist. Every animal requires moisture to survive; deprive it of water and it dies before it dies of hunger. The nourishment of all things — of plants, animals, and the very earth — is moist. The warmth of living creatures is a moist warmth, not a dry one: when moisture leaves the body, warmth leaves with it. And consider the earth itself: the ancient poets knew that Okeanos was the source of all things, and they were not wrong, though they expressed it mythically. The earth rests upon water; earthquakes are the water beneath it shifting. When the sun draws up moisture and rain returns it to the earth, we see the cycle by which water moves through all its forms. These are not myths: they are observations. And the convergence of these observations on water as the principle of life, and through life of all things, is not a coincidence but an insight.

On divinity and the intelligence pervading matter

Hylozoism: Thales held that all things are full of gods; the magnet has soul because it moves iron; soul is what initiates motion; if the archē is alive and intelligent, it needs no external moverI am reported to have said that all things are full of gods, and I stand by it — though not in the way the poets mean. The magnet moves iron without being touched; the amber rubbed with fur draws light things to itself. These are not the actions of dead matter. Soul — the capacity to initiate motion — is present in things we might call inanimate. If water is the archē, it is not dead water, passive and inert. It is water alive with an intelligent principle that drives it through its transformations. The earth does not sit upon dead water; it sits upon something that is, in its own way, divine — not a person, not a god with a face, but the living, intelligent substrate of all change. My colleague Heraclitus will speak of the Logos; my colleague Anaximenes of a breath that is the soul of the world. These are not different insights from mine: they are restatements, in different idioms, of the same recognition that the archē is not merely stuff but is the principle of life and motion itself.

On the transformation of water into all things

Transformations of water: water becomes vapour (fire and air) when heated, earth and stone when consolidated; the cycle of evaporation and precipitation demonstrates the transformability of water into all apparent substancesHow does water become fire, earth, air? The sceptic will press me on this, and rightly. I point to what we can see. Heat water and it rises as vapour — it becomes what we call air and cloud. Continue to heat it and the lightest parts become what we call fire. Cool water and it thickens; cool it further and it becomes ice, then solid earth. The salt of the sea, left by evaporation, becomes a mineral crust. The sediment carried by rivers becomes the land at their mouths. These are not speculations but observations: the Nile delta grows before our eyes as the river deposits what it carries. The transformation of water into all the forms of matter is not a claim I invented; it is what the world shows us if we attend to it carefully. The other substances — air, fire, earth — are water in different conditions, subject to different degrees of heat and cold, density and rarefaction.

On why water rather than any other single substance

Water’s unique fitness: water is the only observable substance that naturally presents itself in all three states — solid, liquid, vapour — demonstrating that a single substance can become many; no other candidate exhibits this transformative range naturallyMy colleagues on the opposition will ask: why water and not air, fire, or earth? The answer lies in what water uniquely demonstrates about itself without philosophical argument. Water is the only substance we can observe, in the ordinary course of nature, moving through all of its forms: it is the sea, it is the cloud, it is the rain, it is the ice of winter, it is the mist on the hillside at dawn. No other candidate exhibits this range of transformation so plainly to the senses. Fire cannot become earth without ceasing to be fire. Earth does not become water before our eyes without violent intervention. But water cycles through all its states continuously, without violence, as a matter of its nature. This alone makes it the most plausible candidate for the thing that underlies all apparent transformations: it is the substance that has already shown us, by its own behaviour, that one thing can be many.

On Democritus and the atoms

Against atomism: atoms are invisible and in principle imperceptible; a philosophy built on the imperceptible abandons the grounding in observation that makes inquiry honest; the archē must be something we can point toMy younger colleague Democritus will offer us atoms — invisible, imperceptible, indivisible particles moving through a void that is likewise imperceptible. I confess I find this extraordinary. We began this inquiry by asking what is actually there — what we can see and touch and reason about. Democritus answers by postulating entities that are in principle never seen or touched, inferred only by argument. Now I do not say argument is worthless. But when argument leads us away from everything that can be observed into a realm of invisible particles and empty nothing, I begin to wonder whether we have explained the world or merely invented a new mythology, one populated by atoms instead of gods. The archē I propose — water — can be seen, touched, tasted, observed in its transformations. It is honest about what it is.

On the beginning of philosophy

The achievement of the question: whatever the correct answer, the act of asking what the archē is was itself the birth of philosophy; the tradition honours the question as much as any particular answerI want to say, finally, that whatever the house decides about which substance is the archē, we should all recognise what the question itself has achieved. Before Miletus, the world was explained by stories. After Miletus, the world was to be explained by reasons. This is the birth of philosophy: not my specific answer, but the form of the question. What is actually there? What persists? What is the principle of change? These questions, once asked, cannot be unasked. And I believe water is the best answer we have yet found — it is visible, transformable, life-giving, and universal. But even if I am wrong about the substance, I was not wrong to ask the question. And it is the question that matters most.

I — Determinacy required: an archē must have definite properties to explain definite things; the apeiron explains nothingclick for image
1.For something to count as an explanation of how a particular thing came to be, the explanans must have determinate properties from which the explanandum can be derived.
2.The apeiron (Anaximander’s boundless) has no determinate properties by definition; it therefore cannot explain how any determinate thing arises from it.
3.Water has determinate properties (moisture, coolness, weight, transformability) from which all other apparent substances can be seen to arise by heating, cooling, condensation, and evaporation.
∴Water is a more genuine explanation of all things than the apeiron: the archē must be something, not nothing-in-particular, and water’s determinate nature makes it causally adequate where the boundless is not.
II — Water and life: all living things require moisture; life is the best evidence for what the archē isclick for image
1.All seeds are moist; all animals require water; all nourishment is moist; moisture is necessary and sufficient (in the right conditions) for the origin and sustenance of life.
2.Life is the most striking fact about the world: that inert matter organises itself into living, self-sustaining, reproducing beings. Whatever makes life possible is the fundamental principle.
3.Water makes life possible in a way no other proposed substance does: fire destroys, earth is inert, air alone cannot nourish; only water is universally correlated with the presence of life.
∴The universal correlation between moisture and life is the strongest empirical argument for water as the archē: it is the substance without which no living thing exists, which makes it the best candidate for the substance from which all things arise.
III — Hylozoism: all things are full of gods; soul is in the magnet; the archē is living and intelligent, not dead matterclick for image
1.The magnet moves iron without being in contact with it; the amber rubbed with fur attracts light objects; these phenomena show that what appears inert matter has the capacity to initiate motion — a property of soul.
2.If soul is present in what we call inanimate things, then the material world is not dead but alive with an intelligent principle that drives change — “all things are full of gods” (Aristotle, De Anima 411a7).
3.Water as the archē is not merely wet stuff: it is the living, intelligent, self-moving substrate of all change, divine in the sense of being the principle of life and motion throughout the cosmos.
∴Thales’ water is hylozoic: the archē is not passive matter to be acted on by a separate mover, but matter alive with intelligence and the capacity for self-initiated change, making a separate cosmological principle unnecessary.
IV — Observed transformations: water becomes vapour, cloud, ice, earth; the cycle of transformation is visible to allclick for image
1.Water heated becomes vapour and rises; the lightest vapour becomes fire and air. Water cooled becomes ice; further consolidated it becomes the mineral and earthy substance of rock and soil.
2.The Nile delta grows as the river deposits sediment: earth is visibly produced by water carrying matter to rest. Evaporation from the sea produces clouds and rain, completing the cycle.
3.These are not inferences but observations available to any attentive person; the transformation of water into all apparent substances is not a philosophical postulate but a record of what the world shows us.
∴The transformations of water into all other apparent substances are empirically attested: the archē = water thesis is not a mere speculation but the best unifying account of observable transitions between states of matter.
V — Water’s unique transformative range: the only substance observable in solid, liquid, and vaporous states in the natural course of thingsclick for image
1.The archē must be a substance that can become all other apparent substances without ceasing to be itself; it must have a natural transformative range spanning all states of matter.
2.Water is the only candidate that exhibits, in ordinary nature and without violent intervention, all three states: liquid (ocean, river, rain), solid (ice, mineral deposits), vaporous (mist, cloud, steam).
3.No other proposed archē — fire, earth, air, the boundless — demonstrates this natural transformative range without philosophical argument; water demonstrates it by its own behaviour.
∴Water uniquely demonstrates by its own nature that one substance can be many: it is the archē’s ideal candidate because it shows in ordinary experience what the archē thesis claims in theory.
VI — Against atomism: the archē must be observable; atoms are in principle imperceptible and constitute a new mythologyclick for image
1.Philosophy begins by asking what is actually there, grounded in what can be observed and reasoned about from observation; the archē must therefore be something accessible to sense and reason together.
2.Atoms (Democritus) are in principle imperceptible: no human has seen or touched one; they are inferred by argument from the assumption that matter must be ultimately indivisible.
3.Positing an imperceptible archē is trading one mythology (gods) for another (invisible particles); it abandons the empirical grounding that makes natural philosophy an advance over story-telling.
∴Atomism, for all its ingenuity, exchanges the gods of myth for the atoms of abstraction; the archē should be sought in what we can observe and reason about directly, which points to water rather than invisible particles.
VII — The question itself as the founding achievement: philosophy begins with the asking, not only with the answerclick for image
1.Before Thales, the origin of things was explained by genealogies of gods and cosmogonic myths; these were stories, not explanations in the sense of derivations from a natural principle.
2.The question “what is the archē?” demands a natural, non-mythological answer expressed in terms of principles accessible to reason; this is the founding act of philosophy as a distinct mode of inquiry.
3.Even if water is not the final answer, the form of Thales’ inquiry — seek one principle, express it in natural terms, support it by observation — is the form all subsequent natural philosophy must follow.
∴The archē question is the birth of philosophy: Thales’ greatest contribution is not necessarily his specific answer (water) but the form of the question, which inaugurated the tradition of reasoned natural inquiry that all present speakers continue.

Fellow inquirers,

My master Thales was right in two things: that the archē is a single, determinate, material substance, and that it is the kind of substance that is also the principle of life. He was wrong only about which substance. Water has merits — I have learned from him and I do not dismiss them. But water cannot explain itself. Why does water flow downhill? Why does it evaporate in heat? What is the cause of its own transformations? Water moves and changes because of something that acts upon it and within it — and that something is breath, is air, is the invisible but ever-present stuff that surrounds the world and fills every living thing: aēr. Air is the archē, and I will defend this before you now.

On breath as the principle of life and the world

Breath as archē: just as our soul, which is breath, holds us together and gives us life, so breath and air hold together the whole cosmos; the macrocosm mirrors the microcosmThere is a fragment of my thought reported by Aetius that I want to make the centre of this speech: “Just as our soul, being air, holds us together and controls us, so do breath and air encompass the whole world.” This is not a metaphor. It is an observation about the structure of living things writ large onto the structure of the cosmos. What is it that holds a living creature together — that makes it one organism rather than a pile of flesh and bone? It is the breath: the invisible, warm current that animates and organises. Remove the breath and the organism collapses into its parts. The cosmos is an organism of this kind: it is held together and ordered by the surrounding air, which is its breath and its soul. The analogy between the individual living thing and the cosmos is not an ornament; it is the key to understanding why air must be the archē. Air is the principle of life and unity in both, and what explains both must be one and the same.

On condensation and rarefaction as the mechanism of transformation

Condensation and rarefaction: all apparent substances are air in different degrees of density; rarefied air is fire; condensed air is wind, cloud, water, earth, stone in order of increasing density; the mechanism is observableMy greatest improvement on Thales is this: I have given a mechanism. Thales tells us all things are water, but he does not tell us precisely how water becomes fire or earth. I say: air becomes all things by two processes — rarefaction and condensation. When air is rarefied — when it is spread thin and heated — it becomes fire. Fire is simply the least dense form of air. When air condenses — when it is compressed and cooled — it becomes wind, then cloud, then water, then earth, then stone, in order of increasing density. The mechanism is observable: we see air condense into cloud and cloud into rain; we see moisture pressed into the earth by weight and time become the solidity of rock. The transition from one state to another is a matter of degree, not of kind: there is no mysterious leap from air to water, only a continuous variation in density driven by heat and cold. This is the first time in the history of our inquiry that a mechanism for transformation has been offered, and it is, I submit, decisive.

On air’s priority over water

Air over water: water is itself condensed air; air is therefore more fundamental than water; we can explain water as a state of air but cannot explain air as a state of waterMy master argues for water; I argue for air. But notice that air explains water, while water cannot explain air. On my account, water is simply condensed air — air that has been cooled and compressed into its liquid state. Water is a stage in the spectrum of air’s transformations, not the origin of them. Thales cannot run the explanation the other way: he cannot tell us how water becomes the invisible, penetrating, boundless air that surrounds and fills all things, because water is too heavy, too particular, too tied to its liquid form, to account for the lightness and omnipresence of air. Air is more fundamental than water because it occupies a more extreme position in the spectrum of density: it is what we approach as we rarefy matter toward its most dispersed state. Fire, the most rarefied of all, is beyond air in this direction; earth and stone, the most condensed, are at the opposite extreme. Water is a middle state. Air is the principle because it is the median from which all extremes can be derived.

On the evidence for air as the encompassing substance

Air as encompasser: air surrounds the entire cosmos and encompasses it; it is the substance that is most everywhere; the archē should be ubiquitous, and air satisfies this better than any other candidateConsider what fills all space. Not water: there are dry places. Not fire: there are cold places. Not earth: there is the open sky. But air — air is everywhere. The entire cosmos is enveloped in air; the winds carry it to every corner of the world; it fills every cave and every lung and every space between things. If the archē is to be the single substance from which all things arise, it must be ubiquitous enough to be the source of everything. Water is too confined to rivers and seas, too particular in its distribution. Air encompasses all things in the way only an originating principle can: it is already present at every point of origination. And it is precisely because air is invisible — present but not seen — that it can be everywhere without displacing anything. It is the perfect candidate for the stuff from which all visible things arise: it is behind and beneath all visible things without itself being visible.

On cold and heat as the drivers of change

Heat and cold as agents: the transformation of air proceeds by the action of heat (rarefaction, toward fire) and cold (condensation, toward earth); these two agents drive all change in the cosmosI want to say something about what drives the transformations of air, since without an account of the motor of change my mechanism is incomplete. The agents are heat and cold. Heat rarefies air, spreading it and thinning it toward fire. Cold condenses air, thickening it toward water and earth. These are not mysterious forces: we experience heat and cold every day; we see what they do to water (evaporation, freezing) and to air (the warm wind rising, the cold settling). The cosmos is a continuous field of air being acted upon by heat and cold in varying proportions, and the result of these actions at every point is the apparent diversity of substances we observe. Fire is where heat has worked most intensely on air; rock and earth are where cold has worked most intensely. The cosmos is a single medium in a state of continuous thermal variation, and the things within it are local concentrations and rarefactions of that single medium. This is a picture of extraordinary simplicity and power, and I believe it is true.

On Heraclitus and the problem of fire

Against fire as archē: fire is the product of the most extreme rarefaction of air; it is therefore less fundamental than air, being a derived rather than a primary state; the process of rarefaction explains fire, not vice versaHeraclitus, my contemporary and in some ways my heir, argues that fire is the archē. I honour him as a profound thinker, but I think he has confused the most vivid manifestation of the principle with the principle itself. Fire is what you get when you push rarefaction to its limit — when air is so spread out, so hot, so energetic that it glows. Fire is the extreme state of air, not the state from which air derives. On Heraclitus’s own account, fire turns into water, water into earth, and earth back into fire — all things are in flux, all things are exchange of fire. But he cannot explain what sustains this exchange, what the medium is through which fire becomes water. I can: it is air, thinning and thickening under the action of heat and cold. Fire presupposes rarefaction; rarefaction presupposes a medium. That medium is air. Heraclitus has given us the most dramatic image of the principle, but not the principle itself.

On the earth and the cosmos sustained by air

Earth floats on air: Anaximenes held that the flat earth floats on air like a leaf; the heavenly bodies likewise ride on air; air is the cosmic substrate in which all things are embedded and supportedThales said the earth floats on water. I say it floats on air — like a broad, flat leaf riding on the breath of the world. And the sun, moon, and stars likewise ride on air: they are fiery bodies that are carried on the surrounding air as leaves are carried on a river. This is not merely an image: it is an account of the physical structure of the cosmos. The air does not merely fill the spaces between things; it supports them, sustains them, carries them in their courses. The cosmos is not a machine with wheels and axles; it is an organism breathing, a system of pressures and currents in the great body of air that is the world-soul. And when we understand this, we understand why air must be the archē: because it is not merely the origin of things, it is their continuous support and the medium in which they move.

3.The more fundamental substance is the one that can explain the other; since air explains water but water cannot explain air, air is more fundamental.

∴The ubiquity criterion uniquely singles out air as the archē: it is the only candidate substance that is already present everywhere that things originate, which is exactly what the archē thesis requires.

3.The cosmos is therefore a continuous thermal field of air in varying states of rarefaction and condensation; the apparent diversity of substances is the local expression of continuous variation in a single medium.
∴Heat and cold acting on air constitute a complete and empirically grounded cosmological mechanism: all apparent diversity of substances is continuous thermal variation in a single medium, explaining the cosmos’s variety without positing multiple archēi.

2.A derived state cannot be more fundamental than the medium from which it is derived: if fire arises from the rarefaction of air, then air is the archē and fire is its product.
3.Heraclitus’s account cannot explain what the medium is through which fire becomes water and water becomes earth; Anaximenes’ air provides that medium and thereby completes the account.
∴Fire is air at its most rarefied extreme and therefore presupposes air: Heraclitus has identified the most vivid expression of the archē’s transformative energy, but not the archē itself, which is the medium — air.

1.The earth is flat and floats on air like a broad leaf; the heavenly bodies are fiery discs carried on the surrounding air; air supports all cosmic bodies in their positions and movements.
2.This is not merely the origin story of things (where they came from) but the ongoing sustaining story (what keeps them in existence and motion now); the archē must sustain as well as originate.
3.Air satisfies both conditions: it is the origin of all things (by condensation and rarefaction) and the ongoing support of all things (the medium in which they move and are embedded).
∴Air is the archē in the fullest sense: it is not only the originating substance from which all things arose but the continuing substrate in which all things float and move — cosmological origin and ongoing support unified in one substance.

Let those with ears hear,

Most men do not understand the Logos even when they hear it; they are asleep even while awake, wandering through the world as if it were their private dream. I will say what is true, though few will grasp it: the archē is fire — but to say this requires understanding what fire is, and most people do not. Fire is not merely the flame in the hearth. Fire is the principle of perpetual transformation: the exchange that underlies all becoming, the ratio by which all things change into all other things and back again. Fire is the Logos made visible. And the Logos is the single rational principle of ordered change that governs all things, including you, though you live as if there were no such principle.

On the Logos as the principle of all things

The Logos: the rational principle governing all change; it is common to all things and to all minds; those who live as if each had private reason are asleep; fire is the Logos made materialI begin with the Logos, because without understanding it, none of what follows will be understood. The Logos is this: the account, the ratio, the rational principle according to which all things happen. It is not my principle or yours; it is common to all things. When I say fire is the archē, I do not mean that everything is literally burning. I mean that reality has the structure of fire: it is process, not substance; it is the ratio of exchange, not a static stuff. The river does not step aside for you: it is always different water, always the same river. The river is its flowing. The cosmos is its changing. What is real is the process of transformation, and fire — always changing, always consuming and giving back, always in the act of becoming something else — is the most perfect visible image of the process that is everything.

On the unity of opposites

Unity of opposites: day and night are one; hot and cold are one; living and dead are one; the bow and the lyre demonstrate that tension between opposites is what holds things together and drives all changeThe fundamental truth that most people miss is this: opposites are one. Day and night are not two things but two aspects of the single alternation of light. Hot and cold are not enemies but partners in the generation of warmth. Living and dead are not opposite states but stages in the single process of transformation: we live the death of fire, and fire lives our death. The bow and the lyre are my favourite illustrations: the bow is held in tension between two forces pulling against each other, and it is precisely this tension that gives it its power. The lyre string must be stretched between two pegs, and it is the tension that makes music. Reality is like the bow and the lyre: it is tension between opposites held in a ratio — the Logos — and the tension is not a flaw but the source of all power and all music. Fire is the archē because it is the most vivid instance of this unity: it is simultaneously consuming and creating, hot and becoming cold as it turns to ash and smoke.

On the river and the impossibility of stepping into it twice

The river: you cannot step into the same river twice; the river is the same river only because it is always changing; identity through change is the fundamental structure of reality, and fire is its best modelYou cannot step into the same river twice — for it is not the same river, and it is not the same you. The river is always different water, yet it is always the same river: its identity consists not in the persistence of any particular water but in the continuity of the flowing. This is not a paradox to be resolved; it is the truth of reality to be understood. All things have identity of this kind: they persist through change, they are the same only by virtue of being always different. The cosmos is the river. The archē is not a static stuff that lies beneath change and persists unchanged while change happens to it; the archē is change itself, is process itself. And fire is the most honest model of this: fire is never the same fire from one moment to the next, yet it is continuously fire. The flame does not persist; the burning does.

Fire is the measure of transformation in the cosmos: everything can be expressed in terms of fire — in terms of the ratio of heat and energy it embodies — and fire can become anything. The seas are measured out in equal portions of earth-and-water, the cosmos in equal portions of fire and not-fire. The total is conserved; only the distribution changes. This is why fire is the archē: not because fire is the most common or most visible thing, but because it is the universal measure of exchange, the ratio that remains constant through all transformations, the Logos of the cosmos made material.

It steers all things with the thunderbolt. This is the divine fire — not a god in the human sense, not a person with desires, but the intelligent principle of transformation that governs all change according to the Logos. The thunderbolt is the most concentrated expression of fire: sudden, total, all-illuminating. When the thunderbolt falls, all opposition is resolved in a single flash. This is the action of the archē on the world: not a slow seeping of water through rock, not a steady breath of air, but the violent intelligence of fire that transforms everything it touches in an instant and reveals, in the flash, the unity that the darkness had concealed. Fire does not merely sustain; it governs, it judges, it steers.

Fellow seekers,

I come last among the proposition’s speakers, and I have listened carefully to my predecessors. Thales points to water; Anaximenes to air; Heraclitus to fire. Each has grasped something true, and I do not dismiss any of them. But all three make a common error: they identify the archē with one of the things we can see and touch, and the things we can see and touch are not the ultimate constituents of the world — they are what those constituents look like to creatures of our size and perceptual limitations. The true archēi are the atoms: indivisible, indestructible, imperceptible particles, infinite in number and variety of shape, moving through the void. Everything else — water, air, fire, earth, colour, taste, heat, cold — is a convention of the senses. By convention sweet, by convention bitter; by convention hot, by convention cold; by convention colour — in reality atoms and void.

On the problem that requires atomism

The problem of divisibility: if matter is infinitely divisible, we reach either nothing (and nothing cannot compose something) or infinitely small somethings (which face the same problem); atomism resolves this by positing a limit to divisionThe problem my predecessors have not solved is the problem of divisibility. Take a stone. Divide it. Divide it again. Keep dividing. Where does this process end? My colleague Anaxagoras will say it never ends — that matter is infinitely divisible, that there is always a smaller part. But consider what follows. If you divide without end and find no limit, one of two things must be true. Either the last things you reach are nothing at all — true extensionless points with no size — and then you face the question how nothing can compose something: how an infinity of nothings can add up to a real stone. Or the process never truly reaches an end, and the stone is composed of infinitely many layers of things that are themselves composed of further things, turtles all the way down. Neither option is satisfying. My answer: there is a limit to division. The smallest things are not nothing but something — real, sized, shaped, solid. They are indivisible because they are solid all the way through, without gaps. These are the atoms, and they are the true archēi of all things.

On the void and the necessity of empty space

The void: motion requires empty space; if there were no void, matter would be packed solid and nothing could move; the existence of motion proves the existence of void; Parmenides was wrong that not-being does not existMy predecessor Parmenides, whom Melissus and Zeno have followed, argued that not-being cannot exist, that the void is nothing, and therefore the void does not exist — and since motion requires empty space, motion is impossible and change is an illusion. I admire the logic of this argument. It is iron. But the conclusion — that motion does not exist — contradicts everything we perceive, and I am not prepared to say the world is an illusion simply to save an argument. So I accept what the argument denies: the void does exist. Not-being is — in the sense that empty space is a real feature of the world, that there are places where atoms are not. It is as real as the atoms themselves, though of a different character: atoms are full; the void is empty. These two — the full and the empty, being and not-being — are the two principles of all things. And it is precisely because atoms move through the void that all apparent change and motion are possible.

On the atoms and their properties

Properties of atoms: atoms differ in shape, size, and arrangement but not in quality; they have no colour, taste, or heat; these apparent qualities arise from the arrangements of qualityless atoms; the atoms themselves are eternal and indestructibleThe atoms are real: they exist, they are solid, they have extension, shape, and size. But they do not have colour, taste, smell, heat, or cold. These qualities that we perceive are not in the atoms themselves but arise from the arrangements, groupings, and motions of atoms in relation to one another and in relation to our bodies. When atoms of a certain shape and arrangement interact with the organs of taste, we perceive sweetness; when atoms of a different arrangement interact with the skin, we perceive heat. The perception is real — something is happening — but the quality as we perceive it is not in the things themselves: it is in the interaction between the thing’s atoms and our body’s atoms. This is the distinction between primary and secondary qualities: shape, size, and arrangement are primary (in the atoms themselves); colour, taste, heat, and smell are secondary (in the perceiving subject). Thales, Anaximenes, and Heraclitus have all mistaken secondary qualities — the wetness of water, the warmth of fire — for the archē itself. They have confused the perception with the reality.

On the vortex and the formation of worlds

The densest, heaviest atoms congregate at the centre of a vortex to form the earth; the lightest, roundest atoms fly to the periphery to form fire and the stars. In between arise water, air, and all the compound substances. This is how our world was formed, and it is how countless other worlds in the infinite void have formed and will dissolve. The cosmos is not unique: it is one of an infinite number of worlds formed by the chance motions of atoms in the void, each lasting until its vortex dissipates. No divine intelligence designed it. No Logos governs it. The apparent order is the necessary consequence of atoms moving according to their shapes and sizes.

On necessity and the rejection of teleology

When a round atom hits a hooked atom, it moves in the direction determined by the collision, not by any intention. The apparent order of the cosmos — the regularity of seasons, the stability of the earth, the courses of the stars — is not the product of intelligent design but of the necessary consequences of atomic motion over vast amounts of time. Order can arise from necessity without purpose. The cosmos does not need a steersman. It needs only atoms, void, and sufficient time for the vortex to do its work. This is more satisfying than Heraclitus’s divine fire, because it does not invoke an invisible, unprovable intelligence; it relies only on what atoms do, and what atoms do follows necessarily from what they are.

On the soul and the nature of mind

The soul is fire-atoms: the soul consists of spherical, smooth, fast-moving fire-like atoms; thought arises from the motion of soul-atoms; the mind is not immaterial but the finest kind of matterMy colleagues attribute soul to the archē itself: Thales says all things are full of gods; Anaximenes says air is soul; Heraclitus says the driest soul is closest to the divine fire. I say soul is atoms — the finest, roundest, smoothest, fastest-moving atoms, of the kind we call fire-like. These soul-atoms are distributed throughout the body, one between each pair of body-atoms, maintaining the animal’s life. Breathing draws in fresh soul-atoms from the surrounding air (which contains them in abundance) and prevents the soul-atoms already in the body from being squeezed out by the weight of the surrounding air. Thought is the motion of soul-atoms; sensation is the impact of external atoms on the soul-atoms of the sense organs. The mind is not immaterial, not divine, not a special principle of a different kind from matter: it is the finest matter, moving most freely. Fellow inquirers,

I learned from Thales, and I honour him. But I departed from him on the question that matters most: what is the archē? He said water; I say the apeiron — the Boundless, the Indefinite, the Without-Limit. I know this sounds like a retreat from explanation into vagueness. I will show you that it is the opposite: it is the only truly honest answer to the question, and those who name a particular substance as the archē have not thought through what they are claiming. The archē cannot be any determinate substance, because every determinate substance has an opposite, and if one substance were dominant it would have long since consumed all the rest. The archē must be beyond all determinate substances — the inexhaustible source from which all opposites arise by separation, and to which they return as penalty for their excess.

On why no determinate substance can be the archē

The problem of opposites: every determinate substance has an opposite; if water (wet, cold) were the archē, it would overwhelm fire (hot, dry); the archē must be prior to and neutral between all oppositesHere is my central argument. Every determinate substance has a contrary: water is wet and cold, its contrary is fire, which is hot and dry. Earth is cold and dry, its contrary is air or fire. If any one of these were the fundamental substance, it would be unlimited in quantity — the whole cosmos would be nothing but that substance — and its contrary would be overwhelmed. If water were the archē, there would be no fire: the wet and cold would extinguish everything hot and dry. But we observe that fire, heat, and dryness exist in the world alongside moisture and cold. Therefore water cannot be the archē. The same argument applies to fire: if fire were primary, everything would be burned up. If air: the cosmos would be nothing but vapour. None of the determinate substances can be the archē because none can coexist with its contrary without devouring it — and yet we observe all the contraries coexisting. The archē must be neutral between all contraries: it must itself be hot nor cold, wet nor dry, dense nor rare. It must be the Boundless.

On the apeiron and what it is

The apeiron: neither hot nor cold, wet nor dry, dense nor rare; it is the source of all opposites because it is prior to and neutral between them; it is divine, immortal, and steers all things; it is infinite in extent and inexhaustibleThe apeiron is not nothing. It is not the void. It is a real substance — the most real substance, the most truly existing thing, more real than water or fire or any determinate stuff. But it lacks the qualities that would make it any one thing rather than another: it is not hot, not cold, not wet, not dry. It is the stuff that underlies all these qualities, the matrix from which they are separated out by the eternal motion that governs the world. It is infinite in extent: it surrounds and encompasses all the worlds that arise from it, which are finite, like bubbles in an infinite sea. It is divine: it never came into being (for what could have produced it?) and it will never perish. It steers all things — not by the intelligence of a mind, but by the eternal motion that ensures things arise from it and return to it. And it is inexhaustible: no matter how many worlds arise from it, the apeiron is never depleted, because it is without limit. That is what the Boundless means: not unlimited merely in extent, but unlimited as a source, an origin that can never be used up.

On the separation of opposites and the origin of the world

Separation of opposites: from the apeiron, the hot and cold are separated out first; then from the hot, fire; from the cold, earth and water; the cosmos arises by differentiation from the undifferentiatedHow does the determinate world arise from the indeterminate? By the separation of opposites. The eternal motion of the apeiron causes hot and cold to separate out first: the hot forms a fiery sphere around the cold and moist core. The cold in turn separates into water and earth. The fiery sphere, as it expands, breaks into rings of fire enclosed in tubes of mist, and these rings of fire, seen through gaps in their mist-envelopes, appear to us as the sun, moon, and stars. The earth, cylindrical in shape, floats unsupported at the centre of the cosmos, held in place not by any material support but by the symmetry of its position: it is equidistant from all sides and has no reason to move in any direction. This is a remarkable thought: the earth is held in place by pure geometric reasoning, not by water beneath it or air around it. The cosmos arises not from any one of its components but from the differentiation of the undifferentiated, the drawing of distinctions out of what had none.

On retribution and the moral order of nature

Retribution: things pay penalty to one another for their injustice; when fire grows too hot, cold reasserts itself; the cosmos maintains balance through mutual retribution; the apeiron is the judge and source of this orderI preserved a fragment of my own prose, and I want to use it now — the earliest surviving prose sentence in Greek philosophy. I said that things pay penalty and retribution to one another for their injustice, according to the ordinance of time. This is not mere poetry. It is a cosmological observation: the determinate things that arise from the apeiron — the hot, the cold, the wet, the dry — each tends to encroach upon its contrary. Summer encroaches upon winter, dryness upon moisture. But no encroachment is permanent: in time, the encroached-upon reasserts itself; winter returns; the rain comes. Nature maintains a kind of justice — a balance of opposites — by mutual retribution. And when things have run their course and perish, they return to the apeiron from which they came. This return is itself a kind of justice: the determinate things pay back to the Boundless what they borrowed from it. The apeiron is not merely the physical source of all things: it is the seat of the moral order that governs nature.

On why Thales’ water fails

Against Thales: water, being wet and cold, cannot explain the origin of its opposite (fire, hot and dry) without suppressing it; Thales’ answer does not survive the test of the opposites argumentI love my master Thales. He was right that there is a single archē and right to seek it in nature rather than in myth. He was wrong about what it is. Water is wet and cold. If water were the archē — unlimited in quantity and the source of all things — it would suppress everything hot and dry. The sun would be extinguished by an infinity of wet, cold water. The seasons would give way to permanent winter. Fire, heat, and dryness would be impossible. But we observe all of these. We observe that fire burns, that summer comes, that desert sands are hotter and drier than the sea. These observations refute water as the archē directly. Thales might respond that water can become fire by rarefaction. But the rarefied water is no longer water in the sense of being wet and cold: the quality by which he identified water as primary has been abandoned in the transformation. He has not explained fire; he has renamed it. The apeiron avoids this problem because it is prior to both hot and cold, wet and dry, and their separation from it is genuine, not a mere renaming.

On infinity and the inexhaustibility of the source

Inexhaustibility: a finite archē would eventually be used up in generating all things; the archē must be infinite to be inexhaustible; the apeiron is infinite and therefore never depleted no matter how many worlds arise from itThere is a further argument for the apeiron that my colleagues on the proposition have not addressed. If the archē is a finite quantity of water, air, or fire, then the generation of the world uses up some of it. And if countless worlds arise and perish — as Democritus himself admits, and as I also hold — then the supply of the archē would eventually be exhausted. An archē that can run out is no archē: the source of all things must itself be without limit, inexhaustible, capable of producing all things without being diminished. This is another reason why the archē must be the apeiron: it is infinite in extent and inexhaustible as a source. A finite, determinate substance — however much water or air or fire there is — is finite. Only the Boundless is truly adequate to the infinity of what arises from it.

On the divine nature of the archē

The divine archē: the archē is divine because it is immortal, inexhaustible, self-governing, and encompasses all things; the divine is not a person but the characterisation of the truly fundamental principleI said the apeiron is divine, and I mean it. Not divine in the sense of having a human face, human desires, or a personal name — the gods of the poets are not what I have in mind. The apeiron is divine in the philosopher’s sense: it is immortal, for it never came into being and will never perish; it is omnipresent, for it encompasses all worlds; it is self-governing, for it steers all things by the eternal motion it contains; it is inexhaustible, for it is unlimited. These are the characteristics of the divine as reason understands it, stripped of the personal mythology of the poets. And it is a consequence of this divine character that the archē must be the apeiron: only the truly unlimited, the truly immortal, the truly self-sufficient is adequate to play the role of the ultimate principle of all things. Water, air, and fire are too particular, too finite, too subject to change and opposition to be truly divine. Only the Boundless is truly at the beginning.

Hear me, friends — for I will tell you of the roots of all things,

I speak in verse because truth has a beauty that demands beauty in its expression. But the content is serious, and I want you to attend to it carefully. My colleagues on the proposition have each championed one substance as the archē. Their error is not in championing a substance but in championing only one. Water, air, fire, and earth are all real, all eternal, all indestructible. None of them arose from anything else. None of them will perish into anything else. They are the four roots of all things — and the motion that combines and separates them is driven by two forces that are as eternal as the roots themselves: Love (Philia) and Strife (Neikos). All that we see is a mixture of these four in varying proportions, brought together by Love and separated by Strife. There is no single archē. There are four roots and two powers, and from these six, the whole of the apparent world arises.

On the eternity of the four roots

The four roots are eternal: earth, water, air, and fire are each indestructible; they are not transformed into one another but mixed and separated; there is no becoming in the strict sense, only rearrangementI begin from a truth that I share with Parmenides: genuine becoming — the coming-into-being of something from nothing — is impossible. Nothing comes from nothing. If water came from fire — truly came from it, as a genuinely new thing — fire would be destroyed in the process. But fire is not destroyed when water is formed. We can still produce fire from the conditions that produce water; the fire has not been used up. What has happened is not transformation but mixing and separation. The four roots — earth, water, air, fire — are each eternal, indestructible, unchanging in their own nature. They do not transform into one another. What we call “water becoming fire” or “air becoming earth” is a confusion: what is actually happening is the separation of fire-parts from a compound and the combination of earth-parts into a new mixture. The roots themselves are permanent and unchanging; only their proportions in mixtures vary. This is why no single substance can be the archē: each of the four is real and none is derived from any other.

On Love and Strife as the forces of change

Love and Strife: Love combines unlike things into wholes; Strife separates mixtures into their components; the cosmos cycles between the Sphere (all-Love) and maximum Strife; the present world is in a phase of increasing StrifeThe four roots do not move themselves. They are moved by two forces: Love (Philia) and Strife (Neikos). Love is the force that brings unlike things together into harmonious wholes; Strife is the force that separates mixtures into their component parts. When Love dominates entirely, the four roots are blended into a perfect, featureless Sphere — a divine, blissful ball in which all things are mixed together without distinction. When Strife dominates, the roots separate entirely and exist apart from one another. The present world is neither of these extremes: we are in a phase in which Love and Strife are both active, mixing and separating the roots in varying proportions at different points, producing the apparent variety of things. The cosmos cycles through these phases: from Sphere (all-Love) through increasing Strife to maximum separation, and then back through increasing Love to Sphere again. Our world is in the Strife-increasing phase. This cycle is eternal; the cosmos does not originate or perish but cycles perpetually between these states.

On the mixtures and what we call substances

All things are mixtures: bone is earth, water, fire, and air in specific proportions (2:4:2:4 by parts); blood is an equal mixture of all four; what we call a substance is a temporarily stable ratio of the four rootsWhat we call water is mostly the root Water, but not pure Water alone: it contains traces of all four. What we call fire is mostly the root Fire, but it is mixed with the others. What we call bone — I have worked out the ratios — contains two parts earth, four parts water, two parts fire, and four parts air in each eight-part unit. Blood is a mixture in which all four roots are present in equal proportions, and it is this equal mixing that makes blood the seat of thought — for thought requires contact with all things in equal measure. The apparent substances of the world are not pure instances of one root but temporary, locally stable mixtures of all four. And this explains what the monists cannot: the tremendous diversity of the world. A single archē — water or air — can produce variety only by quantitative change (more or less dense, more or less hot). But the four roots, combined in varying proportions, produce qualitative variety — the smell of wine is different in kind from the texture of bone, not merely different in degree of one thing.

On biology and the generation of living things

Biology from the roots: first came heads without necks, arms without shoulders, eyes without faces; Love combined compatible parts into living things; the surviving combinations were those that could sustain themselves — an early version of selectionI want to tell you something wonderful about how living things first arose, because it reveals the power of my account. In the beginning of the world’s present phase, when Love was increasing and combining the separated roots, the first things that formed were limbs and organs in isolation: heads without necks wandered the earth; arms without shoulders moved without direction; eyes searched without faces. Love, combining these randomly, produced the first composite things — many of which were monstrous and incompatible: human-headed cattle, cattle-headed humans, creatures of mixed sex that could not reproduce. Those that were incompatible perished; those that were mutually suitable — whose parts worked together — survived and reproduced. This is not divine planning: it is the result of Love combining parts at random and the compatible surviving. The living world as we know it is the residue of countless incompatible combinations that have already failed. This process could not arise from a single archē: it requires four distinct types of material part that can be combined in every possible configuration.

On perception and how we know the four roots

Like knows like: we perceive earth by the earth in us, water by the water in us, fire by fire, air by air; perception is the contact of like elements; this explains both the reliability of perception and its limitsI have a theory of perception that confirms my account of the four roots: we perceive like by like. The earth in us perceives earth in the world; the fire in us perceives fire; the water in us, water. This is not a mere analogy: it is a physical claim about how perception works. When I see fire, it is because the fire-parts of my eye are in contact with, or moved by, the fire-parts in the world; the likeness of composition is the condition of perceptual contact. This explains why perception is reliable: we genuinely contact the things in the world because we share their composition. It also explains why we cannot perceive what we have no part of: if I lacked all water in my body, I could not perceive water. And it explains why thought is possible: thought requires equal mixture of all four roots, which is the composition of blood. The equal mixture allows contact with all things equally, which is what thinking — thinking about everything — requires. All of this presupposes four real, distinct roots; it cannot be stated in terms of a single archē.

On the cosmic cycle and the unity of the Sphere

The Sphere: when Love is total, all four roots are blended into the divine Sphere; the Sphere is the true unity of things, but it is a temporary phase, not the permanent archē; the archē is not the Sphere but the four roots and two forcesSomeone may object: if in the phase of total Love all things are blended into the Sphere — a single, undifferentiated, divine whole — does this not mean that unity is the true nature of things, and the archē is the Sphere? I deny it. The Sphere is not the permanent state of the cosmos; it is a phase of the eternal cycle. The roots are no less real in their separated state than in the blended Sphere; Strife is no less real and eternal than Love. The archēi — the permanent constituents of all things — are the four roots and the two forces, not the Sphere that is only one phase of their combination. The Sphere is beautiful and divine, but it is no more ultimate than the phase of maximal Strife: both are products of the interaction of the same six eternal principles. The unity of the Sphere is temporary; the plurality of the roots is permanent. No phase is more real than another; all phases are equally real expressions of the same six eternal principles.

On why the proposition’s speakers are each partially right

Anaximenes saw that air is likewise a genuine constituent, present everywhere and essential to life — and he was right. Heraclitus saw that fire is the most vivid image of the process of transformation, the most energetic and transformative of the four — and he was not wrong. Each of them identified one of the four roots. Each then made the mistake of claiming that the one root they identified was the archē — the source of all the others. This cannot be: if water is a root and fire is a root, neither can be derived from the other, for each is eternal and indestructible. They are co-equal partners in the composition of all things. The proposition’s speakers are each right about a root; they are wrong about the exclusivity of their choice. The archē is not one of the four: it is all four together, combined and separated by Love and Strife in the great eternal cycle.

Inquirers,

I have listened to all the speakers — those who argue for one substance, those who argue for four. Each has grasped something. But each has also failed to confront the deepest problem: the problem of mixture and separation, the problem of how one thing can become genuinely many, the problem of the presence of everything in everything. I will offer you an account that I believe solves these problems and all the others — an account that begins not with a particular substance or a pair of forces, but with two fundamental truths: that everything contains a portion of everything else, and that Nous — Mind — is the only thing that is itself alone, pure, unmixed, and infinite, and it was Nous that initiated the rotation from which all things arose.

On the problem that requires the everything-in-everything principle

Everything in everything: if something genuinely distinct from X could come from pure X, it would arise from nothing; to avoid this, each thing must contain a portion of everything; what dominates in a mixture determines what the thing appears to beConsider this: I eat bread and from it my body produces bone, hair, blood, and flesh. If bread were nothing but bread — pure, homogeneous wheat-stuff — then bone, hair, and blood would arise from nothing, which is impossible. Therefore bread must already contain portions of bone, hair, and blood within it — very small portions, invisible to the senses, but really present. When I eat bread, the portion of bone in the bread is separated out and added to my bones; the portion of blood goes to my blood. This generalises: in everything there is a portion of everything. In the hair, a portion of fire; in the stone, a portion of life. What makes a thing appear to be what it is — what makes this appear to be bread rather than bone — is that bread contains a much larger portion of the wheat-like stuff than of any other; the dominant portion determines the apparent character. But the other portions are there, genuinely present, however small. Everything is in everything.

On the primordial mixture and the beginning of rotation

The beginning: all things were together in the beginning; the mixture was so complete that nothing was discernible; Nous initiated a rotation; the rotation caused separation of the denser, colder, darker, and moister from the rarer, hotter, and drierIn the beginning, all things were together. The mixture was perfect and complete: everything was in everything, and the proportions were so equal that nothing was discernible from anything else. The mixture was dark (because light had not yet separated from dark), moist, dense, cold. Nothing rotated; nothing moved. Then Nous initiated the rotation — beginning from a small region and expanding outward. The rotation was the fastest thing in the world, and it caused separation: the denser, colder, darker, and moister parts moved toward the centre of the rotation, becoming earth; the rarer, hotter, lighter, and drier parts moved to the periphery, becoming fire and the aether. Everything we see — every star, every stone, every living thing — is a product of this rotation, a locally stable pattern of different proportional mixtures that the great separating motion of the cosmos has produced from the primordial all-mixture. The rotation continues; the separation continues; the cosmos is still in the process of differentiating from the original unity.

On Nous and its unique character

Nous is pure and unmixed: the only thing that is itself alone; it is infinite, autonomous, has the finest texture of all things; it knows all things, controls all things, and initiated the rotation; without Nous, nothing would have movedHere is what separates my account from all others: Nous. All other things have a portion of everything in them. Nous alone is unmixed: it is itself alone, not mingled with anything else. If it were mixed with anything — if it had a portion of earth or fire or moisture in it — it could not control all things, because the mixture would prevent it from acting on everything equally. To know and control all things, it must be itself alone, unconditioned by mixture with any particular thing. Nous is the finest of all things and the purest, and it has all knowledge about everything and the greatest power; and it controls all things that have soul — the larger and the smaller. The rotation it initiated was not random: it was the act of an intelligence that knows all things and ordered them accordingly. This is where Democritus goes most wrong: he denies Nous and gives necessity in its place. But necessity without intelligence cannot produce the order we observe; the cosmos is too orderly, too articulated, too finely calibrated to be the product of blind collision. Nous is real, and it is the most important thing.

On the infinite divisibility of matter and why Democritus is wrong

I argue the opposite: the regress is not to be feared but embraced. There is no smallest part. In everything, however small, there is always a smaller portion with everything in it. This is what the everything-in-everything principle entails: if matter had a smallest part, that smallest part would contain nothing — it would be a mere extensionless point, and a collection of extensionless points cannot produce an extended object. The truly philosophical response to the divisibility problem is not to stop the regress artificially with an atom but to accept that the regress is real — that matter is infinitely divisible and that at every scale, however small, the same principle holds: everything is in everything. The atom is a philosophical fiction, a place where Democritus stopped because he was uncomfortable with the infinite. I am not uncomfortable: the infinite structure of matter is a truth about nature, not a paradox to be resolved by inventing an imaginary limit.

On how hair comes from non-hair

Hair from non-hair is impossible; therefore everything has a portion of hair; this is the mereological principle underlying everything-in-everything: like comes from like, applied to every portion of matterAristotle will record my argument in this form: how can hair come from non-hair? How can flesh come from non-flesh? If we accept that like comes from like — a principle I share with Empedocles — then hair can only arise from a substance that already contains hair. Since hair arises from bread and meat (by way of the body), bread and meat must contain hair. Since anything can arise from anything else under the right conditions, everything must contain everything. This is not mere word-play: it is a rigorous application of the principle that nothing comes from nothing, extended to every specific kind of substance. You cannot get hair-substance from a substrate that contains no hair-substance whatsoever. Therefore: in the bread, there is hair; in the stone, there is the potential of living tissue. What we call the nature of a thing is determined by what it contains in greatest proportion — but it contains all natures in some proportion, however small.

On gold and the gold-in-everything

Gold in the gold-bearing river: the river contains gold in sufficient proportion to be called gold-bearing; but all rivers contain some gold; the everything-in-everything principle is confirmed by the continuous extraction of minerals from waterHere is a beautiful confirmation of my principle, drawn from what miners and metallurgists know. Gold-bearing rivers produce gold not because gold arises from water by transformation, but because the river contains gold already, in a proportion large enough to be extracted. But every river contains some gold — too small to extract in most, large enough to extract in gold-bearing rivers. The gold is in all rivers; it is only separated out where the proportion is high enough. This is the everything-in-everything principle at work: the gold is not created by the river; it is present in the river in a proportion determined by the conditions of that stretch of water. And the same is true of every mineral, every element, every substance: it is present in every other in some proportion, however small. The cosmos is an infinite mixture of everything with everything, and what we call a substance is only a locally dominant proportion, not a pure, exclusive presence of one thing alone.

On why Nous is not a substance among substances

Nous as archē: Nous is not a material substance like the others; it is of different kind entirely; it is the principle that knows and moves all things; the archē in the highest sense is Nous, not any mixture or any rootI want to end by saying something that my colleagues on both sides have perhaps not fully grasped: the true archē, in the deepest sense, is not a material substance at all. It is Nous. The primordial mixture — everything-in-everything — would have remained forever motionless and undifferentiated without the act of Nous that initiated the rotation. The rotation is what produced the world. And the rotation was initiated by an intelligence that is itself alone, unmixed, infinite, and all-knowing. In this sense, Nous is the archē in the most important sense: it is the principle that explains why anything happened, why the primordial mixture began to rotate rather than remaining forever at rest. Matter — however constituted — does not explain its own motion. The motion of matter requires a mover that is not itself material, not itself mixed, not itself subject to the conditions it imposes on everything else. That mover is Nous. And this is the most important answer to the question before this house: the archē is not water, air, fire, or any combination of material substances. It is the intelligence that set the world in motion — pure, unmixed, infinite, and alone.

In everything there is a portion of everything. Nous alone is itself alone. I have spoken.

I — Everything in everything: genuine coming-to-be from a purely different substance is impossible; each thing must contain a portion of everything; dominant portions determine apparent characterclick for image
1.Bread produces bone, hair, blood in the body; if bread contained none of these, they would arise from nothing — which is impossible; therefore bread contains portions of bone, hair, and blood.
2.By generalisation: in everything there is a portion of everything; the apparent character of a thing is determined by what it contains in greatest proportion, not by its exclusive possession of one substance.
3.This principle preserves the Parmenidean truth (nothing from nothing) without restricting genuine change to a finite set of eternal substances; it allows any substance to arise from any other without violating conservation.
∴Everything-in-everything is the only consistent application of ‘nothing from nothing’: it preserves the impossibility of genuine creation while explaining the apparent emergence of any substance from any other by the separation of pre-existing portions.
II — The primordial mixture and the rotation: all things were together; Nous initiated rotation; separation produced the cosmos we inhabitclick for image
1.In the beginning: all things were together in a perfect mixture, nothing discernible from anything else; the mixture was dark, moist, dense — everything present in equal proportions.
2.Nous initiated rotation from a small region; rotation expanded; denser/colder/moister moved to centre (earth); rarer/hotter/drier to periphery (fire, aether); separation continues to this day.
3.Everything we observe is a product of the separating rotation: locally dominant proportions of different stuffs, produced by the cosmic rotation from the primordial everything-mixture.
∴The rotation-from-mixture model explains cosmogenesis without positing creation from nothing: all cosmic structures are separations from a primordial all-mixture, initiated by Nous and driven by the mechanics of the rotation.
III — Nous: the only thing that is itself alone, unmixed, infinite, all-knowing, all-controlling; the condition of possibility of all cosmic orderclick for image
1.Nous is the only thing that is itself alone, unmixed with anything else; if it were mixed, it could not act on all things equally — mixture with any particular thing would prevent control of its contrary.
2.Nous is infinite, has the finest texture of all things (so it can penetrate everywhere), and has knowledge of all things and the greatest power; it controls all things that have soul.
3.Without Nous, the primordial mixture would have remained at rest forever; Nous is the necessary first mover of the cosmos, the explanation of why anything began to move rather than nothing.
∴Nous is the archē in the deepest sense: it is the mover without which all matter remains inert, the intelligence that knows all things and set the cosmic rotation in motion; it is irreducibly different from any material substance.
IV — Against atomism: matter is infinitely divisible; there is no smallest part; the atom is an artificial limit on the real infinite structure of matterclick for image
1.If matter had a smallest part (the atom), that part would be extensionless (for any extended part can be further divided in principle); and extensionless points cannot compose extended objects.
2.Matter is genuinely infinitely divisible: in every portion, however small, there is a smaller portion with everything in it; the everything-in-everything principle holds at every scale.
3.The atomist’s limit (the atom) is a philosophical fiction: an arbitrary stopping point in the real infinite divisibility of matter, motivated by discomfort with the infinite rather than by any physical necessity.
∴Matter is infinitely divisible and the everything-in-everything principle holds at every scale: the atom is a fiction; there is no smallest part, and the infinite structure of matter is not a problem to be solved by artificial limits but a truth to be embraced.
V — Hair from non-hair is impossible: like from like, applied universally, entails everything-in-everythingclick for image
1.Hair can only arise from a substance that already contains hair-stuff; flesh only from a substance containing flesh-stuff; this is the strict application of like-from-like to every specific kind of substance.
2.Since hair arises from bread (via the body), bread contains hair-stuff; since flesh arises from meat, meat contains flesh-stuff; this is observable rather than speculative.
3.Generalised: anything can arise from anything under the right conditions; therefore everything must contain a portion of everything — which is the everything-in-everything principle, derived directly from like-from-like.
∴The impossibility of hair from non-hair is a rigorous argument for everything-in-everything: like-from-like applied to every specific substance entails that every substance contains all others in some proportion.
VI — Gold in the gold-bearing river: the everything-in-everything principle confirmed by the continuous presence of minerals in water in varying proportionsclick for image
1.Gold-bearing rivers produce gold not by transforming water into gold but because the river contains gold already in extractable proportions; all rivers contain some gold in smaller proportions.
2.This empirical observation confirms the everything-in-everything principle: mineral substances are present in water in varying proportions, not absent from most waters and present only in special waters.
3.What we call a substance is only a locally dominant proportion in an infinite mixture; the gold-bearing river is not uniquely different in kind from other rivers, only in the proportion of gold it contains.
∴The gold-in-rivers observation confirms everything-in-everything: apparent differences between substances are differences in proportion, not in the exclusive presence or absence of ingredients — everything is in everything, always.
VII — Nous as archē in the highest sense: the mover of all things, not a material substance, the only thing that truly is itself aloneclick for image
1.Matter alone — however constituted — does not explain its own motion; the primordial mixture would have remained at rest without an external initiation of the rotation.
2.Nous is the first mover: pure, unmixed, infinite, all-knowing; it initiated the rotation and thereby produced the cosmos; it explains why anything happened rather than the mixture remaining forever at rest.
3.In this sense, Nous is the archē in the highest sense — the principle without which no material archē could have given rise to a world; the highest explanation is not of what things are made of but of why they began to move.
∴Nous is the archē in the deepest sense: it is not a material constituent but the unmoved mover that set all material constituents in motion; the deepest answer to ‘what is the principle of all things’ is not a stuff but the intelligence that first moved stuff.
