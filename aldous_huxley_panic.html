Panic

Aldous Huxley — The Burning Wheel (1916)
Hover or click highlighted passages to read annotations
Structure & Context
Title: 'Panic' derives from Pan — the Greek god of wild nature whose sudden appearance caused irrational terror; the etymology is active throughout the poem Form: Two stanzas of unequal length with irregular line lengths; the second stanza is more tightly rhymed than the first Two-part structure: Stanza 1 is interior — portraits, rational self-reassurance; Stanza 2 is exterior — gardens, peacocks, an unanswered question Rhyme scheme: Stanza 2 rhymes lake/wake/break/wake (returning to its own opening word) with forlorn/horn — the word 'wake' closes on itself Movement: The poem moves from attempted rational control (stanza 1) to an event that overwhelms it (stanza 2) without resolving either Context: Huxley was 22 when The Burning Wheel was published; the poem anticipates the psychological preoccupations of his later fiction

The eyes of the portraits on the wall Look at me, follow me, Stare incessantly: I take it their glance means nothing at all? —Clearly, oh clearly! Nothing at all... Out in the gardens by the lake The sleeping peacocks suddenly wake; Out in the gardens, moonlit and forlorn, Each of them sounds his mournful horn: Shrill peals that waver and crack and break. What can have made the peacocks wake?
