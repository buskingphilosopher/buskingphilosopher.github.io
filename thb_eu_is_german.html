Mr President,

The motion invites us to examine a structural fact that is almost embarrassingly well-documented, yet rarely stated plainly: the institutional architecture of European integration has, decade by decade, been remade in the image of German economic preferences — export discipline, low inflation, fiscal austerity, wage restraint — and imposed upon a continent of states that neither share those preferences nor possess the adjustment mechanisms that would make them compatible with their own economic conditions. This is not an accusation of malice. It is an analysis of structural outcomes.

I. THE ORDOLIBERAL IMPRINT ON EUROPEAN INSTITUTIONS

Instrument vs objective independence — mandate accountabilityLet us begin with monetary architecture. The Maastricht Treaty of 1992 created a European Central Bank modelled explicitly on the Bundesbank — not merely inspired by it, but, as the Christian Science Monitor reported at the time, "a near replica" in structure, mandate, and administration. The ECB's sole primary mandate is price stability; it has no employment mandate, unlike the Federal Reserve. This was a direct German constitutional preference, rooted in the ordoliberal tradition of the Freiburg School, and it was exported wholesale to the entire eurozone. The ECB in 2011 — in the depths of a sovereign debt crisis — raised interest rates because of inflation concerns, a decision that exacerbated the crisis in peripheral economies and that a Fed with a dual mandate would almost certainly not have taken. That decision had German preferences written through it. But the monetary architecture is only part of the picture. The Stability and Growth Pact, the Fiscal Compact, the Macroeconomic Imbalances Procedure — each of these governance mechanisms encodes the ordoliberal principle that fiscal rectitude is the primary obligation of member states. Critically, the Macroeconomic Imbalances Procedure, which is supposed to discipline both surplus and deficit countries, sets the alert threshold for current account deficits at 4% of GDP — but for surpluses at 6%. Germany's current account surplus has exceeded 6% of GDP consistently since 2012, reaching 8.5% in 2015 — one of the largest sustained surpluses in the history of modern economics. The IMF and US Treasury both issued formal warnings. The European Commission opened a procedure. Germany demurred. The asymmetry between the institutional treatment of deficits (enforced with fines and conditionality) and surpluses (tolerated despite formal thresholds) is not accidental. It reflects whose preferences designed the system.

II. THE CURRENT ACCOUNT SURPLUS AS STRUCTURAL DOMINANCE

Time-consistency problem — government faces systematic inflation biasThis brings me to what Yanis Varoufakis has called "monetary mercantilism" — and what the proposition considers the most important structural argument. Germany's export-led growth model generates persistent current account surpluses. Before the euro, a persistent surplus would have caused the Deutschmark to appreciate, correcting the imbalance automatically. Within the eurozone, this exchange-rate mechanism is abolished. Germany therefore benefits from an implicit real exchange rate subsidy: the euro is weaker than a standalone Deutschmark would be, because it aggregates the weaker economies of the south, keeping German exports artificially competitive on world markets. The counterpart of Germany's surplus is the periphery's deficit. Capital flows generated by German surplus countries rushed into peripheral economies before 2008, fuelling unsustainable credit booms and real estate bubbles. When the financial crisis reversed those flows, the burden of adjustment fell entirely on deficit countries — through austerity, unemployment, and wage suppression — while Germany's surplus continued to accumulate. The IMF estimated in 2016 that Germany's real exchange rate was 15–20% undervalued relative to its economic fundamentals. In a currency union, that undervaluation cannot self-correct. The periphery pays the price; Germany absorbs the benefit.

III. SELECTIVE APPLICATION OF ORDOLIBERAL PRINCIPLES

Democratic accountability through mandate transparencyHere is the argument that the opposition will find most difficult to answer. If Germany's insistence on fiscal austerity, strict liability, and sound money were genuine ideological commitments rather than strategic interests, we would expect them to be applied consistently — including when their application conflicts with German national interests. They are not. When the ECB's bond-purchasing programmes under Mario Draghi threatened to mutualise eurozone risk, Germany's constitutional court objected strenuously — and yet Germany ultimately acquiesced, because the collapse of the euro would have been catastrophic for German export markets. When Volkswagen faced a €14.7 billion emissions fraud settlement, the German government lobbied intensively against the application of EU competition rules that it aggressively enforces in other contexts. Ordoliberalism, the academic Gabriel Goodliffe argues in his published analysis, was pursued as a strategic idea "when it was consistent with core German economic and political interests" — and deferred when it was not. This is not ideology. It is interest dressed in ideological language.

IV. THE PHILOSOPHICAL POINT: LEGITIMACY WITHOUT ACCOUNTABILITY

Time-consistency problem — government faces systematic inflation biasThe deepest problem with German hegemony through European institutions is not its material consequences — serious as those are — but its democratic structure. When Germany imposes preferences on Greece, Italy, or Spain through the conditionality of troika programmes, it does so without any direct accountability relationship to Greek, Italian, or Spanish voters. The German finance minister is elected by Germans, accountable to Germans, and answerable to the German constitutional court. Yet his decisions govern the healthcare systems, pension ages, and labour markets of populations who did not vote for him and cannot remove him. This is not a European demos. It is a German bureaucracy with a European franchise. The motion captures this dynamic with precision: the EU has been German expansionism in disguise — not because Germans want an empire, but because that is what a currency union without political union, governed by the largest economy's preferences, structurally produces.

Mr President,

My colleague King has made the theoretical case. I want to make the empirical case. In the social sciences, theory must be checked against evidence. And the evidence on central bank independence is the most compelling body of empirical work in political economy.

On the Alesina-Summers findings

Empirical evidence — CBI delivers lower inflation at no output costIn a 1993 paper with Lawrence Summers, we established the following: across OECD countries, there is a strong negative correlation between central bank independence and average inflation. Countries with more independent central banks have significantly lower average inflation. The correlation is robust — it survives controls for fiscal policy, trade openness, exchange rate regime, and a variety of institutional variables. More striking than the inflation finding is the output finding: there is no significant correlation between central bank independence and real economic outcomes — growth, unemployment, output variability. Countries with independent central banks do not pay for their lower inflation with worse real economic performance. The alleged trade-off between inflation and output, which underlies the political case for keeping monetary policy under electoral control, does not show up in the cross-country data.

On the credibility premium

Time-consistency problem — government faces systematic inflation biasThe credibility premium is the mechanism through which independence delivers better outcomes. When a central bank is independent and has established a track record of hitting its inflation target, private agents incorporate this expectation into their behaviour. Workers do not demand high nominal wage increases because they expect low inflation. Firms do not raise prices pre-emptively. Bond markets do not demand a high inflation risk premium. The result is that price stability can be maintained at a lower real cost — less output sacrifice when inflation needs to be reduced, because expectations are already anchored. A non-independent central bank, by contrast, must continually demonstrate its credibility through costly disinflation. Because private agents expect political accommodation, they build higher inflation into expectations. The central bank is trapped in a cycle of credibility destruction and costly reconstruction that the independent central bank avoids by never destroying credibility in the first place.

On the European evidence

Credibility capital and European convergenceThe European experience provides a natural experiment. In the 1980s, countries of what would become the eurozone had very different inflation rates. Germany, with its constitutionally entrenched Bundesbank, had inflation below 3%. Italy and France, with less independent central banks, had inflation of 10% or more. When these countries joined the European Monetary System and began to coordinate monetary policy with Germany, they progressively acquired German credibility — not merely by matching German interest rates but by building credible low-inflation institutions. Italian and French inflation converged toward German levels. Interest rate differentials narrowed. The cost of disinflation was real, but the long-run gain was a credible low-inflation environment those countries had not been able to achieve domestically. Credibility is institutional capital. It takes time to build, it is costly to establish, and it delivers permanent benefits once established.

Mr President,

My colleagues have made the theoretical and empirical cases. I want to make the practical case — the case from crisis. I was Chair of the Federal Reserve during the 2008 financial crisis. What that experience taught me about the value of central bank independence is the most direct and compelling argument in this debate.

On what 2008 required

2008 crisis — independence enabled decisive action political paralysis preventedIn autumn 2008, the global financial system was within days — perhaps hours — of complete collapse. The failure of Lehman Brothers triggered a run on the shadow banking system. Money market funds broke the buck. Commercial paper markets froze. Interbank lending stopped. The Federal Reserve extended emergency lending facilities to institutions that had never before had access to the Fed's balance sheet. It provided dollar swap lines to foreign central banks to prevent a global dollar shortage from triggering cascading failures. It intervened in the commercial paper market, the money market, and the mortgage-backed securities market. It ultimately expanded its balance sheet from approximately $900 billion to over $2 trillion in the space of a few months. These actions were enormously controversial, attacked from left and right simultaneously. Congress was in paralysis — it took three attempts to pass TARP legislation, and the first failure nearly destroyed the recovery it was designed to begin.

On what independence made possible

Instrument vs objective independence — mandate accountabilityWhat made the Federal Reserve's response possible was its independence. Because the Fed did not require Congressional authorisation for its emergency lending operations — conducted under Section 13(3) of the Federal Reserve Act — it could act in hours rather than weeks. Because it did not require Treasury approval for each individual intervention, it could respond to the specific and rapidly changing needs of a crisis evolving faster than any deliberative political process could track. An elected government facing the same situation would have been paralysed. The political economy of crisis intervention is brutal: every institution that receives emergency support becomes a political target. A Treasury Secretary who proposed the measures the Fed took would have faced immediate Congressional opposition, legal challenges, and political attacks that would have prevented timely action.

On the distributional objection

QE distributional consequences — democratic legitimacy of asset purchasesThe objection that the Fed's crisis actions illustrate the problem with independence — that an independent bank inevitably serves financial sector interests — conflates two things. The Fed's crisis actions were not undertaken to benefit the financial sector. They were undertaken to prevent the collapse of the financial sector from destroying the real economy — from producing a depression that would have impoverished millions of ordinary households through unemployment, falling asset prices, and the destruction of savings. The evidence from the Great Depression, which the Fed's crisis management was explicitly designed to prevent repeating, suggests that the consequences of allowing financial collapse to run its course fall hardest on those with the least financial resilience. The 2008 response was imperfect — the distributional consequences of QE were not fully anticipated. But the alternative — policy paralysed by political process, and a full financial collapse — would have been worse.

Mr President,

The proposition has made a sophisticated case. The time-consistency argument is theoretically elegant. The Alesina-Summers findings are genuinely important. And the 2008 story is compelling in its urgency. I want to argue that, taken together, these arguments prove something quite different from what the proposition intends — that the case for central bank independence is a case for a particular distribution of economic power masquerading as a technical institutional arrangement.

On whose preferences central banks actually serve

Democratic accountability through mandate transparencyThe most important question about central bank independence is rarely asked: whose preferences do independent central banks actually serve? The answer is not "the preferences of no one" or "the preferences of the economy in the abstract." Central banks are staffed by people with backgrounds, training, and social networks. The overwhelming majority of senior central bank officials in the advanced economies are trained economists who have spent significant portions of their careers in financial institutions or in academic departments with close ties to them. Their professional formation, their understanding of good monetary policy, and their intuitions about acceptable trade-offs all reflect this formation. The result is not corruption — these are generally honest people. The result is a systematic bias: independent central banks consistently prioritise low inflation over low unemployment, consistently treat creditor interests as the baseline of policy, and consistently interpret their mandates in ways more comfortable for the financial sector than for workers and households.

On the time-consistency problem reframed

Time-consistency problem — government faces systematic inflation biasThe time-consistency argument illustrates this bias perfectly. The problem it identifies — political pressure to inflate — is framed entirely from the perspective of creditors. From the perspective of debtors and workers, the problem with unrestrained central bank independence looks very different: it is the systematic bias toward deflation, toward high real interest rates, toward tight credit, that distributes income from debtors to creditors and from workers to capital owners. The framing of the time-consistency problem as the central problem of monetary policy is itself a political choice — one that reflects whose interests the framers were most worried about protecting.

On inflation targeting as a class project

Empirical evidence — CBI delivers lower inflation at no output costThe shift to inflation targeting in the 1990s was not a politically neutral technical improvement. It was a response to the distributional conflicts of the 1970s, in which labour had the bargaining power to translate nominal wage gains into real wage gains through wage-price dynamics. Inflation targeting, combined with central bank independence, was the institutional mechanism through which the creditor class reasserted control over that dynamic. The countries that adopted independent central banks with inflation targets in the 1990s — the UK, Sweden, New Zealand, Canada — did so in the context of deliberate political projects to shift the balance of bargaining power away from labour and toward capital. The 1970s inflation cited by my opponent King was not the product of irresponsible politicians printing money. It was the product of genuine distributional conflict — workers and capital competing for shares of national income in a context of oil price shocks. The resolution of that conflict through CBI was its resolution in favour of capital.

Mr President,

My colleague Palley has made the political economy case against central bank independence. I want to make a different and complementary argument — one grounded in what has happened since 2008. My argument is that the post-2008 world has rendered the intellectual foundations of central bank independence obsolete, and that the case for independence rests on assumptions about what central banks do that are no longer true.

On the Fisher separation theorem and why it no longer holds

Instrument vs objective independence — mandate accountabilityThe case for CBI rests on a version of the Fisher separation theorem — the idea that monetary policy and fiscal policy can be cleanly separated: monetary policy is a purely technical instrument affecting the level of nominal demand without distributional consequences, while fiscal policy is the politically contested domain of redistribution and public expenditure choices. On this view, the division of labour is clear and defensible. Governments make the politically contested choices about taxing and spending. Central banks make the technically demanding choices about the level of nominal demand. The two domains are separable, and central banks can operate independently precisely because their domain is technical rather than political. But the events of 2008 and their aftermath have made this separation untenable.

On QE as fiscal policy by another name

QE distributional consequences — democratic legitimacy of asset purchasesWhen the Federal Reserve, the Bank of England, and the ECB engaged in quantitative easing — purchasing trillions of assets — they were not making purely technical decisions about nominal demand. They were making decisions about which assets to support, which institutions to assist, and which sectors of the economy to favour. They were making distributional choices of enormous consequence. The Bank of England's Asset Purchase Facility involved the purchase of £895 billion of assets, primarily gilts. This suppressed long-term interest rates, boosting asset prices — equities, property, bonds — disproportionately held by the wealthy. The Bank of England's own research estimated that the first £200 billion of QE raised asset values by approximately £600 billion, with the top 5% holding 40% of those assets. This was a distributional intervention that, had it been proposed as a fiscal measure, would have required Parliamentary approval and would have been bitterly contested.

On the coordination problem

2008 crisis — independence enabled decisive action political paralysis preventedThere is a final underappreciated argument against CBI in the post-2008 world: the problem of macroeconomic coordination. The UK's experience between 2010 and 2015 illustrates this: the Treasury was pursuing aggressive fiscal consolidation through spending cuts and tax increases, while the Bank of England was attempting to support nominal demand through low interest rates and asset purchases. Each policy, taken individually, was internally coherent. Taken together, they produced a slow recovery that required unprecedented monetary stimulus to prevent outright recession. The institutional separation of fiscal and monetary policy created a coordination failure that neither institution could individually resolve. An independent central bank is not a coordination mechanism. It is an independent institution. And independent institutions, by definition, do not coordinate — they pursue their mandates. In a world where the interaction between fiscal and monetary policy determines macroeconomic outcomes, institutional independence is a recipe for coordination failure.

Mr President,

I want to close the opposition's case with an argument that takes the proposition's strongest points seriously — more seriously, perhaps, than the proposition expects — while demonstrating that the conclusion it draws does not follow. I am not a critic of central banking. I have been a central banker. What I want to argue is that the form of independence that has developed in the advanced economies has generated systemic risks that the proposition's account ignores.

On the political economy of easy money

Time-consistency problem — government faces systematic inflation biasThe time-consistency argument holds that governments face a systematic bias toward inflation because of electoral incentives — and that CBI removes this bias. But the post-2008 experience reveals the opposite problem: a systematic bias toward monetary easing among independent central banks that has nothing to do with electoral pressure. The Federal Reserve, the Bank of England, the ECB, and the Bank of Japan all maintained interest rates at or near zero for periods far longer than any analysis of economic fundamentals would justify. They did this not because of electoral pressure — they face no elections — but because of institutional incentives the time-consistency literature entirely ignores. Independent central banks are judged by financial markets. Their officials move between central banks and financial institutions. The financial sector strongly prefers low interest rates — which support asset prices, reduce debt service costs, and make balance sheets easier to manage. The systematic bias of independent central banks is not toward inflation, as the proposition argues. It is toward financial sector accommodation.

On what CBI made it easier to ignore before 2008

2008 crisis — independence enabled decisive action political paralysis preventedIn the years before 2008, the Federal Reserve under Greenspan and the Bank of England under King failed to take action against the build-up of financial sector risk that produced the crisis. The political insulation that independence provided made it easier to ignore the accumulating risks. A Treasury that was also responsible for monetary policy might have been more alert to the financial stability risks — because a financial crisis produces fiscal costs that elected politicians must account for. An independent central bank, insulated from the fiscal consequences of financial sector failure, faces weaker incentives to police financial stability. The 2008 crisis was, in part, the product of a form of central bank independence that separated monetary policy from financial stability and from the fiscal consequences of getting financial stability wrong. Independence enabled the crisis response. It also enabled the policy failures that made the crisis necessary.

On what actually anchors expectations

Credibility capital and European convergenceMy final argument is about what actually anchors inflation expectations — and whether CBI is necessary for this. Germany before the formally independent Bundesbank — before the constitutional independence the proposition celebrates — had very low inflation. Japan's central bank, before it was formally made independent in 1998, had some of the lowest inflation in the world during the 1980s. The Federal Reserve was not formally independent in the legal sense through much of the post-war period, yet delivered decades of relatively stable prices. What anchors inflation expectations is not formal independence. It is a political consensus — a societal commitment to price stability sufficiently broad and robust that monetary policy reliably reflects it. When that consensus exists, formal independence and informal independence deliver similar results. When it does not exist — when there is genuine societal disagreement about the desirable inflation rate — formal independence does not resolve the disagreement. It merely suppresses it, concentrating the costs on those with the least economic power.
