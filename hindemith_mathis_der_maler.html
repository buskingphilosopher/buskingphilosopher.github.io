Paul Hindemith 'Mathis der Maler' Symphony — Analysis

Published May 16, 2026

First Movement: Engelkonzert

Form

The movement divides into three broad sections, each defined by tempo and metre. The opening slow section (Ruhig bewegt, dotted crotchet ≈ 66) is in 6/4 and runs from bar 1 to fig. 3. A fast central section (Ziemlich lebhafte Halbe, minim ≈ 108–112) in 2/2 begins at fig. 3 and runs through to the area of fig. 16, where the music broadens and returns to the opening character. The movement's climax arrives in a Breit section in 3/2 shortly before fig. 18, after which a long coda withdraws to extreme quiet before the final bars.

Introduction (bars 1 to fig. 1)

The movement opens with strings alone, marked geteilte sul ponticello, pp. Bar 1¹ presents a sustained chord in all five string parts: the composite sonority is G–B–D–F♯, a major seventh on G. The sul ponticello instruction removes warmth from the tone, producing a glassy, unanchored sound that prevents the listener from settling into the implied key. The chord is not attacked; the scoring instruction get. (divided) with fermata-like tied values across bars 1–3 means the listener hears it as a held colour rather than a harmonic event. A short geteilte rhythmic figure on beat 4 of bar 1 disturbs the stasis and is the first incursion of duple subdivision into the triple 6/4 grid — a tension between two and three that runs through the entire movement.

By bar 3 the strings have shifted to a new sonority, still sul ponticello, with the inner parts moving by step. The absence of any wind instrument in these first nine bars strips the sound to a single orchestral colour, so that the entry of horns and trombones at fig. 1 registers as a structural change of the first order.

The Chorale (fig. 1 to fig. 3)

At fig. 1 Hindemith marks the score „Es sungen drei Engel" — identifying the folk-chorale melody he is using as a cantus firmus. Horns 1, 2, 3 and 4 enter together on beat 1 of fig. 1¹ in unison, piano, marked sanft hervortreten (emerging gently). Trombones 1, 2 and 3 join simultaneously, also zus. (together). The melody moves in half-notes and dotted half-notes, the rhythmic values deliberately longer than the surrounding string material, reinforcing the temporal separation between the chorale layer and the accompanying figuration.

The chorale pitch centre is F. It opens with a rising minor third (F to A♭) at fig. 1¹ in the horns, immediately establishing a Dorian-flavoured F — the A♭ denies any F major tonic, placing the melody in a modal space where major and minor coexist without resolution. Each phrase of the chorale ends by descending stepwise to rest on F, and the regularity of this return acts as a rhythmic anchor against the freer string parts below.

From fig. 1 onwards, violins, violas and cellos enter in a chromatic counterpoint, pp, zus., built from semiquaver turns around chromatic neighbours. The string texture is independent of the chorale's phrase rhythm: the strings move continuously while the chorale sustains, then the chorale moves while the strings hold momentarily. This two-layer independence — cantus firmus above long values, counterpoint below short values — is the governing texture of the entire movement.

At fig. 2¹ the glockenspiel enters for the first time, doubling the chorale's upper notes at pitch, pp. The metallic colour extends the chorale's timbre without thickening its texture. The full woodwind join at fig. 2 in an ascending figuration marked f cresc., the first loud moment in the movement: the effect of sudden dynamic expansion after eighteen bars of pp creates an abrupt jolt to the listener's sense of scale, before the music subsides immediately.

Fast Section (fig. 3 to fig. 16)

The tempo change at fig. 3 is the largest structural boundary in the movement. The Ziemlich lebhafte Halbe marking shifts the pulse unit from the dotted crotchet to the minim, and the metre moves from 6/4 to 2/2. The effect on the listener is of a doubling of perceived pulse speed: where bar 1 moved at six slow beats, each bar of the new section moves at two brisk ones.

The first violins lead at fig. 3¹ with a rising figure that outlines a perfect fourth (B♭ to E♭), followed by a falling third — a two-note motto that will recur throughout this section. The second violins enter on beat 2 of the same bar with a countermelody at the sixth below. Violas have a separate harmonic filler pattern. Cellos sustain a pedal. This four-layer string texture with differentiated rhythmic roles is very different from the unison sul ponticello of the opening. The register is middle-high, the sound bright and lean.

At fig. 3 the bass instruments (cellos, double bass) hold open fifths on C and G, establishing a pitch centre that contrasts directly with the F-centred chorale. The choice of C as a bass pedal at the start of the fast section means that the interval of a fifth separates the two main tonal areas of the movement, a relationship Hindemith uses consistently.

One bar before fig. 4¹, the texture suddenly compresses into the full orchestra at f. At fig. 4¹ horns 1 and 2 enter fp, the first entry of brass in the fast section. The fp accent on beat 1 followed by an immediate fall to p by beat 2 is a fingerprint of this section: strong downbeat accents that collapse immediately into softness, preventing any sustained sense of brass weight.

Between figs. 5 and 6 the score marks einleiten — Ruhig, a written instruction to the conductor anticipating the return of slow material. This kind of notated transition instruction is unusual in orchestral scores and signals that Hindemith considered the moment of gear-change structurally critical. The texture thins to woodwind only in the two bars before fig. 6, with the strings dropping out entirely at fig. 5⁴, so the re-entry of strings at fig. 7 in sul pont. divisi, pp, recalls the opening texture precisely and identifies the formal return.

At fig. 7 the strings are back in their divided sul ponticello disposition from bar 1, but the held sonority has shifted: the bass note is now A rather than G, a tone higher than the opening, producing a mild tonal displacement that gives the recurrence an unsettled rather than a stable feeling. The clarinet enters alone at fig. 7⁺² with a solo line marked mp, unaccompanied for two bars. Pizzicato cellos and double bass enter underneath at fig. 7⁺³, also pp. The sudden reduction to three timbres — clarinet, pizzicato cello, pizzicato bass — after the fuller textures of the fast section produces the sparsest moment in the movement to this point.

Development and Return (fig. 8 to fig. 16)

From fig. 8 onwards the movement begins a second large buildup that mirrors and extends the one from bar 1 to fig. 2. Strings re-enter in 8va figuration at fig. 10, sustaining high-register semiquaver runs pp over several pages. At fig. 11 the texture thins again rather than continues to grow: violins and violas remain in the high register, but dynamics fall to ppand then diminuendo to nothing. A solo triangle entry at the bar before fig. 12 marks the nadir of this withdrawal — the single soft metallic stroke against near-silence is the movement's most exposed moment.

At fig. 12¹ two bassoons alone play a falling figure, mf, unaccompanied. The effect of reduced scoring — two bassoons after a long passage for strings and woodwind — resets the timbral frame entirely. Violas enter at fig. 12⁺³ with a stepwise line marked mf, and the bassoons respond. This is the only passage in the movement structured as a dialogue between two specific instrumental choirs (low woodwind and violas), and it creates a texturally intimate space quite unlike the layered polyphony elsewhere.

Between figs. 13 and 14 the texture builds again, this time faster than before. The score marks einleiten — Ruhig again at fig. 14⁺², and the strings re-enter in sul ponticello get. chords at pp immediately after. This second written einleitennotation confirms that the movement's architecture is symmetrical at this level: two buildups separated by two retreats, both announced in advance.

Climax and Coda (fig. 15 to end)

The movement's main climax begins at fig. 15 with all woodwind and strings at f, building through fig. 16 (where the full brass and percussion enter for the first time in several pages) to fig. 17, marked verbreitern (broadening). At fig. 17¹ the metre shifts from 2/2 to 3/2 and the dynamic reaches fff, with long sustained notes in all parts. This is the movement's most harmonically dense passage: virtually every instrument is sounding simultaneously, the harmonic rhythm slows to one change per bar, and the chorale melody — now in its full Breit form — returns in all voices fortissimo.

The pitch centre at fig. 17's climax is Bb, a whole step below the F of the original chorale — a characteristic Hindemith relationship. The return of the chorale at the wrong pitch (Bb rather than F) is corrected only gradually over the following pages, as the music broadens and slows toward the coda.

At fig. 18 the marking Ruhiger signals the coda. The metre is now 2/2, dynamics have fallen to mp and below, and the orchestration thins to woodwind only for several bars. At fig. 19 (Noch ruhiger) strings re-enter sul ponticello, pp, returning to the opening colour. At fig. 20 (Hauptzeitmass) the strings return to their exact opening disposition — divided, sul ponticello, pp — but the bass now pizzicato rather than arco. A solo flute enters at fig. 20⁺¹, p, with a stepwise falling figure over the pp string chords.

The movement does not end on the home pitch F. The final bars, around fig. 22 and 23, reduce to held harmonics in the upper strings and fragments of woodwind melody against a sustained bass. The last chord is not a recognisable tonic chord in any conventional sense but a sustained open sonority that fades into silence, the dynamic marked ppp and then nothing.

Metre and Rhythm

The 6/4 of the opening is tonally ambiguous in its subdivision: it can be heard as 3+3 or 2+2+2. Hindemith exploits both readings simultaneously. The string counterpoint from fig. 1 onwards subdivides in duple quaver groups, while the chorale phrases favour the triple 3+3 reading. This metrical counterpoint — duple versus triple within a nominally shared bar — creates a persistent rhythmic instability that persists even when the listener has located the main pulse.

In the 2/2 fast section, accents are systematically displaced from beat 1. The violin figure at fig. 3¹ begins on beat 1 but the answering figure in the second violins enters on beat 2, creating a hemiola relationship within the 2/2 bar. At fig. 4⁺² the horns enter on beat 2 rather than beat 1, displacing the downbeat. These displacements accumulate: no section of the fast material settles into a regular 1–2 accent pattern for more than four consecutive bars.

Second Movement: Grablegung

Form

The movement is in a slow arch shape. A quiet opening in muted strings expands to a single climax at the bar before fig. 4 and then contracts to near-silence. There is no recapitulation of the opening material as a structural event; the return is one of texture and dynamic rather than of specific theme.

Opening (bars 1 to fig. 1)

All strings enter at bar 1¹, marked mit Dpfr. (with mutes) and pp. The first violins begin the melody on beat 1: the first two notes are a rising minor third (C to E♭), followed by a stepwise ascent to F on beat 3. The opening interval of a minor third from C gives the movement an immediate sense of modal ambiguity — the E♭ precludes C major, the absence of any G-minor preparation precludes C minor in classical terms, and the melody moves through a scale that is neither definitively major nor minor.

The remaining strings (violins 2, violas, cellos) enter within bar 1, also mit Dpfr., adding a slow-moving harmonic counterpoint below the melody. Double bass is silent for the first four bars. The five-voice string texture in bar 1 moves in rhythmic unison on beat 1, then diverges into independent lines by beat 3, so that the texture shifts from homophony to polyphony within a single bar. This rhythmic divergence increases from bar to bar: by bar 4 each string line has its own rhythmic pattern, and the texture is fully polyphonic.

At fig. 1¹ a solo flute enters, p, taking over the melody while the strings continue their inner parts. The flute's tone contrasts with the muted strings by being completely unmuted: the result is that the melody sounds as if it has moved slightly outside the main texture into a separate acoustic space. Pizzicato strings enter in the second bar after fig. 1, replacing the arco inner parts: the change from arco to pizzicato shifts the accompaniment from sustained to punctuated and reduces the overall resonance.

Build and Climax (fig. 2 to bar before fig. 4)

At fig. 2 flute and oboe enter in a brief canon at the distance of one beat, the oboe following the flute's figure a minor third lower. This is the first contrapuntal imitation in the movement: its effect is of the melody expanding into dialogue rather than being carried by a single voice. Strings are pizzicato throughout this passage, keeping the accompaniment light.

From fig. 3 onwards the movement builds to its climax. The metre, which has been 4/4 throughout, develops hemiola patterns in the strings as the tempo marking (Sehr langsam, crotchet ≈ 54) is held. At fig. 3¹ all strings are marked arcoand the dynamic moves from mp to cresc. Six bars before fig. 4 the full orchestra is involved, with brass entering for the first time in the movement: horns 1 and 2 enter at mf cresc., followed by trombones and trumpets. The brass writing here is in sustained whole notes and half notes, adding weight to the harmonic mass without countermelody.

The climax itself is the bar immediately before fig. 4: ff in all parts, with a fermata on the entire orchestra. Every part holds its note. The fermata on the ff chord creates a moment of complete suspension — there is no rhythmic activity anywhere in the score, and the dynamic is at its maximum. The effect on the listener is an absolute punctuation, a full stop rather than a comma.

Withdrawal (fig. 4 to end)

At fig. 4¹ the dynamic falls immediately to mf dim. and within two bars to pp. This is Hindemith's most abrupt dynamic reversal in the symphony: from ff fermata to pp in one bar. The brass are silent from fig. 4 onwards. The string texture that returns here mirrors the opening — muted, divided, slow-moving — but the violas and cellos carry a new figuration of alternating pizzicato and arco notes, get., creating a broken rather than sustained line.

The movement closes with long sustained held notes in all remaining parts, strings and woodwind, the dynamic falling from pp to nothing. No cadential formula is present: the music simply ceases to move.

Melody

The opening violin melody is constructed from adjacent intervals: the minor third (C to E♭), followed by a rising second (E♭ to F), followed by a further rising second (F to G). This stepwise continuation after the initial leap is a distinguishing feature: the melody never moves by a large interval after bar 1. The largest leap in the entire main melody is a perfect fourth, and it appears only once, in bar 6. The melodic contour overall is arch-shaped, rising through bars 1–4 and descending through bars 5–8, before the flute takes over.

The melody has no regular phrase structure. The first phrase is five bars long, the second three bars, the third seven bars. These irregular lengths resist the listener's attempt to anticipate phrase endings, keeping attention on the melodic line itself.

Tonality and Harmony

The movement's pitch centre is C, but C is never confirmed by a perfect cadence. The harmony is built from the convergence of the independent contrapuntal lines rather than from functional chord progressions. At bar 1 the combined string sonority is C–E♭–G–B♭–D: a C minor eleventh chord in Hindemith's harmonic language, a sonority that contains both a minor third and a major ninth, resisting resolution in either a minor or a major direction. At the climax bar before fig. 4 the combined sonority is more dissonant, containing minor seconds (D♭ against C, A against B♭) stacked in the brass. The resolution that follows — the fall to pp at fig. 4 — is one of texture and dynamic, not of harmonic tension.

Third Movement: Versuchung des heiligen Antonius

The score places a Latin epigraph before bar 1: „Ubi eras, bone Jesu ubi eras, quare non affuisti ut sanares vulnera mea?"

Form

The movement is the largest of the three and the most complex structurally. It falls into five identifiable sections, which are not conventional sonata sections but rather alternations of fast and slow tempo with accumulating intensity:

Section A: Slow introduction (Sehr langsam, bars 1 to fig. 1, and resuming after fig. 1)
Section B: Fast main body (Sehr lebhaft, from after fig. 1 to approximately fig. 13, with internal variations of tempo and texture)
Section C: Lyrical interlude (approx. fig. 6 to fig. 10, slower passages within the fast section)
Section D: The Lauda Sion Salvatorem cantus firmus section (from fig. 31 to fig. 35)
Section E: Final Alleluia chorale (fig. 35 to end)

Introduction (bars 1 to fig. 1)

The movement opens Sehr langsam, frei im Zeitmaß (very slow, free in tempo) with all strings marked ohne Dpfr.(without mutes), pp. This is the only movement to begin without mutes. Bar 1¹ presents all string sections simultaneously — violins 1 and 2, violas, cellos and basses — with a descending chromatic figure in the first violins marked pp rising to mf by beat 2. The score marks rubato two bars after fig. 1, and the tempo flexibility is written into the rhythm: bar lines in this section do not align with perceived metric accents. Groups of 6 notes against a 4/4 framework, triplets against duplets, and the rubato instruction combine to make the opening bar rhythmically unplaceable until several bars have passed.

The dynamic trajectory of bar 1–3 is pp–mf–cresc.–f, a foreshortened version of the movement's overall arc. The fpmarking at bar 1⁴ (a loud beat immediately followed by quiet) establishes a pattern of interrupted dynamics that recurs at the climaxes throughout the movement.

At fig. 1¹ the full orchestra enters at ff, woodwind and brass together in accented homophony, marked rubato. This is the movement's first violent outburst, lasting one bar, followed immediately by a fermata on beat 4. The single-bar eruption and immediate silence produces a jolt to the listener calibrated to the epigraph's cry („where were you, good Jesus?") without the analysis requiring reference to that text.

After fig. 1 the tempo is marked mf, 3 cresc. and continues building to another climax.

Fast Section (Sehr lebhaft, ♩≈ 176)

The main fast section begins after the slow introduction with the marking Sehr lebhaft and crotchet ≈ 176, in 6/8. The strings enter at the beginning of this section in a rapid semiquaver ostinato, mf, all four string choirs playing the same figuration. Simultaneously, oboes and clarinets begin a separate ascending figure in 6/8 quavers. The two layers — string semiquavers and woodwind quavers — are in rhythmic counterpoint: three string notes to every two woodwind notes within each beat. This proportional relationship (3:2) is maintained for several pages and creates a persistent sense of metric instability without any change of time signature.

The woodwind figure at the start of the Sehr lebhaft section is the movement's principal subject. It opens with a rising perfect fourth (from the lowest note to beat 2) followed by a falling minor third, a profile that mirrors the opening interval of the chorale in movement I in inversion. This intervallic relationship — rising fourth, falling third — becomes a connecting thread between the movements.

At fig. 2¹ the full woodwind choir joins at f, the dynamic having built from mf at the start of the fast section in ten bars. The brass remain tacet through this passage; their absence from the fast woodwind texture is structural, reserving their entry for later climaxes.

At fig. 4¹ the full orchestra plays the semiquaver figure fff: the first moment where all sections play simultaneously in the fast tempo. Horns 1–4 marked zus. with the string texture, while trumpets and trombones play a separate sustained melody above. The superimposition of rapid-movement and sustained-movement layers at the loudest dynamic creates the maximum textural density of the entire movement.

Between figs. 5 and 6 the score shows a sustained trill (tr...) in all woodwind parts that extends for two full bars, followed by a sudden pp. The trill-to-silence effect creates the impression of a mechanical rundown: sustained pitch energy collapsing without arrival on a specific chord.

The Interlude (figs. 6–7)

At fig. 6 the full orchestra stops in a ff chord, followed immediately (bar 2 after fig. 6¹) by a solo oboe line p, marked cresc. over a sustained note. Strings are pizzicato under the oboe, pp. The reduction from ff full orchestra to a single woodwind voice is the most dramatic textural change in the movement. The oboe's line at fig. 6⁺² opens with a falling minor second (B♭ to A), then a falling major third, and moves in a free stepwise idiom without regular metre. The cello line that enters beneath it at fig. 6⁺³ is a countermelody in pizzicato, beginning on G and moving stepwise upward: the two voices create a two-part texture, oboe and pizzicato cello, in the centre of the movement. The listener, after sustained high-intensity music, encounters this two-voice passage as an abrupt change of scale.

At fig. 7 the clarinet takes over from the oboe in the solo role, with strings continuing pizzicato. The melody moves by step and minor third, with no interval larger than a perfect fourth. This intervallic compression — avoiding the leaps that characterised the fast material — distinguishes the interlude's melodic character from the surrounding fast sections.

The Lauda Sion Salvatorem Section (fig. 31 to fig. 35)

At fig. 31¹ Hindemith places a label in the score: „Lauda Sion Salvatorem". The Gregorian chant melody begins at this point in the woodwind, in long note values (dotted half-notes and half-notes), mf. Flutes and oboes carry the cantus firmus, while strings continue their rapid-movement figuration below, now f and building. The two layers — cantus firmus in long values and string figuration in short values — recall the texture of the chorale and counterpoint in movement I, but with all dynamics louder and the counterpoint more aggressive.

The cantus firmus continues through figs. 32 and 33 with the same pitch profile: stepwise motion, predominantly rising, in equal note values. At fig. 34 the woodwind and then brass join the cantus firmus at f cresc., swelling the chorale texture. One bar before fig. 35 the score marks verbreitern (broadening) and the dynamics reach fff.

The Alleluia Chorale (fig. 35 to end)

At fig. 35 the score marks „Alleluia" and Breite Halbe (broad half-notes), with the metre shifting to 6/2. All brass — 4 horns, 2 trumpets, 3 trombones, tuba — play mit aller Kraft (with all force). The strings are entirely silent for the first time in the movement. The chorale is stated in unison by the brass choir alone, in long equal note values, the melody rising by step from B♭ to B♭ an octave above over sixteen bars. The sustained removal of strings removes the rapid-movement layer that has been present since the opening of the fast section, leaving only the chorale, sustained, in its maximum brass scoring.

The final bars (the last page of the score) reduce from the brass tutti to a held chord under a fermata at ff, then a final decay to pp. The sustained chord fades, with the strings re-entering in very long values pp beneath the held wind chord, and the movement closes in near-silence, the last note a single sustained Bb in the bassoons under a fermata.

Rhythm and Metre

The third movement uses the widest range of metres of the three: bars of 4/4, 3/4, 6/8, 2/4, 3/2, 2/2, and 6/2 all appear, and changes of metre from bar to bar are frequent from fig. 10 onwards. At fig. 22¹ a single bar of 2/4 interrupts three consecutive bars of 3/4, displacing the expected beat pattern by one quaver and producing a momentary stumble in the metric frame. At fig. 28¹ (Sehr lebhaft, crotchet ≈ 80, 3/4) the three-beat frame is set against an internal rhythmic pattern in the strings that consistently accents beat 2, creating a persistent syncopation. The listener cannot locate beat 1 by accent for the first eight bars of this section.

Instrumentation

The movement withholds the full brass for extended stretches, deploying them only at structurally critical moments: fig. 1 (first violent outburst), fig. 4 (first full-orchestra climax in the fast section), and figs. 31–35 (the Lauda Sion and Alleluiasections). Between these entries the brass are frequently tacet — sometimes for twenty or more bars at a time. The effect is that each brass entry registers as a new event rather than as a continuation, and the final brass chorale at fig. 35, with strings completely silent, carries a weight accumulated from all prior withdrawals.

The percussion instrumentation is graduated across the movement. The small drum (Kl. Trommel) enters for the first time at approximately fig. 3, pp, maintaining a continuous roll that functions as a rhythmic pressure beneath the texture rather than a punctuation. The bass drum (Gr. Trommel) enters later, nearer fig. 5. The cymbal (Becken) appears only at the largest climaxes. The triangle (Triangel) — used in the quietest passages of movement I — reappears here at the quiet passage around fig. 21, creating a cross-reference to the earlier movement's texture. Each new percussion instrument enters at a dynamic level and at a structural point where its specific timbre can be heard in isolation before it is absorbed into the full texture.
