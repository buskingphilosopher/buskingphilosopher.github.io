"This House Holds the Clintons in Contempt" — The Indictment and Its Rebuttal

Sidney Blumenthal · The Clinton Wars (Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 2003) — Senior Adviser to President Clinton 1997–2001
The Prosecution
Christopher Hitchens
No One Left to Lie To (Verso, 1999)
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr Blumenthal,

I am going to be brief about the personal dimension between us, because the personal dimension, while it happened to be historically illuminating — your role in spreading the description of Monica Lewinsky as a "stalker" through the Washington press corps, and your subsequent denial of having done so under oath — is not the main argument, and I do not wish the obvious acrimony between us to obscure the question at hand, which is whether the Clinton presidency, evaluated in its actual effects on actual people, deserves the reputation for progressive achievement that its defenders have laboured so assiduously to construct for it.

My book was called No One Left to Lie To. I want to explain the title. It is not a reference to Clinton's personal dishonesty in the banal sense of a politician who is occasionally economical with the truth. It is a reference to a systematic practice — a political method — of telling each constituency exactly what it wanted to hear, and then delivering to the other side while the first was still applauding the speech. This triangulation, as Dick Morris named it, was not a technique for governing effectively. It was a technique for remaining in power without governing at all. It corroded public discourse, it betrayed the Democratic Party's actual constituents, and it prepared the ground — by hollowing out the meaning of political language — for everything that came after. I want to make this case specifically and in detail.

I. Triangulation as the organised deception of the electorate

Triangulation: a deliberate method of telling the Left what it wanted to hear while delivering to the Right what it wanted to haveThe concept of triangulation — borrowed from Dick Morris, which itself tells you something — means, in practice, the following. You campaign for the Democratic nomination on promises to the liberal base: universal healthcare, welfare defence, economic fairness. You win the nomination and the general election on those promises. You then govern by appropriating the Republican agenda — welfare reform more punitive than anything Reagan managed to pass, a crime bill that sent incarceration rates to historically unprecedented levels, NAFTA pushed through over trade union opposition, a Defence of Marriage Act that threw gay Americans to the homophobic right as a pre-election sop — while maintaining the rhetorical posture of a progressive. The liberal base, having nowhere else to go, swallows it. The Republican right, getting what it wanted while losing the credit, is infuriated. And Clinton calls this "the vital centre." What it actually is, as I wrote, is "the cynical fusion of corporate money and evangelical Christian enthusiasm," deployed in the service of keeping one specific couple in power. The triangulation does not produce better policy. It produces no policy — or rather, Republican policy with a Democrat in the White House to absorb the blame when it fails. (No One Left to Lie To, Ch. 1)

II. The welfare bill: "more hasty, callous, and ill-considered than anything the Republicans could have hoped to carry"

The 1996 welfare reform: a savage dismantling of the New Deal safety net, praised as "ending welfare as we know it" while creating deeper and longer-term povertyI want to linger on the welfare reform of 1996, because I think it is the single act that most completely exposes the fraud of Clintonian triangulation. The Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Act — the welfare bill — was passed with bipartisan support after Clinton had vetoed earlier versions twice. He signed the final version in an election year, against the explicit recommendations of three members of his own Department of Health and Human Services, who resigned in protest. My description of the bill was that it was "more hasty, callous, short-term, and ill-considered than anything the Republicans could have hoped to carry on their own." The bill slashed the federal guarantee of support to poor families with children, imposed work requirements without ensuring jobs existed to satisfy them, and cut food stamps and support for legal immigrants. It did nothing to address the structural causes of poverty — the disappearance of industrial employment, the growth of low-wage service work, the destruction of union representation — and everything to penalise the poor for being poor. When Clinton boasted of having "ended welfare as we know it," he was boasting of having inflicted on the most vulnerable American citizens a punishment more severe than any Republican Congress had dared to enact. This is not the centre. This is the right, achieved by a man running as the left, to applause from an electorate that had not yet understood what had been done to them. (No One Left to Lie To, Ch. 2)

III. The bombing of the Al-Shifa pharmaceutical factory: a war crime on the day of testimony

The Sudan bombing: missiles launched against a pharmaceutical plant supplying 60% of Sudan's medicines, on the day of Clinton's grand jury testimony, with fabricated intelligenceOn 20 August 1998, the day after Clinton's grand jury testimony about Monica Lewinsky was concluded, the United States launched cruise missile strikes against a pharmaceutical factory in Khartoum, Sudan — the Al-Shifa plant — on the grounds that it was producing chemical weapons precursors for Osama bin Laden. I wrote at the time, and I maintain, that this was a war crime, and that the intelligence on which it was ostensibly based was fabricated, or at best so thin as to constitute recklessness rather than genuine belief. The Al-Shifa factory produced roughly sixty percent of Sudan's supply of medicines for tuberculosis, malaria, and veterinary infections. Its destruction killed its guard, injured others, deprived Sudan of medicines it had no means to replace in the short term, and resulted in deaths among people who could not access the treatments the factory had been producing. The Sudanese government requested international inspection of the site. The Clinton administration refused. Independent scientists who examined soil samples after the strike found no evidence of the precursors cited as justification. The man who owned the factory, Salah Idris, a Saudi businessman, sued the United States government. The US quietly unfroze his assets, tacitly acknowledging the strike had been unjustified, without admitting the error publicly. The timing was not a coincidence. I have never once been given a satisfactory explanation of why it was not. (No One Left to Lie To, Ch. 5)

IV. The bombing of Iraq on the eve of the House impeachment vote

Operation Desert Fox timed to delay the House impeachment vote: foreign policy as personal legal defenceOn 15 December 1998, after Clinton's last-ditch non-apology had failed to deflect wavering House members toward acquittal, and after every headline was reporting a decisive move toward impeachment, Clinton announced that Saddam Hussein was not complying with UN weapons inspectors. Two days later, Operation Desert Fox — four days of cruise missile and air strikes against Iraq — began. Within hours of the announcement, the House Republicans, described by the New York Times as "furious and fractured," postponed the impeachment debate that had been scheduled to begin the following morning. I want to be precise about what I am and am not claiming. I am not claiming that there was no Iraqi non-compliance with UNSCOM — there was, and it had been documented for months. I am claiming that the decision to act militarily on that non-compliance at that specific moment — after months of non-response, on the eve of the impeachment vote, in a pattern that had already been established by the Sudan strike five months earlier — is too convenient to be coincidental, and that a President who was demonstrably willing to use military force to manage his domestic political calendar had forfeited the presumption of good faith that would ordinarily attach to the decision to go to war. And that the representatives who postponed the impeachment vote to defer to a commander-in-chief who was fighting a war were being manipulated by precisely the man they were supposed to be judging. (No One Left to Lie To, Ch. 5)

V. The treatment of women: a pattern, not a series of isolated incidents

Clinton's conduct toward women: a sustained pattern of predation, intimidation, and smear, not a private indiscretionI want to be clear, because I have been systematically mischaracterised on this point, about what my argument concerning Clinton's conduct toward women is and is not. It is not about adultery. I do not think adultery is a serious political crime or that private consensual affairs are the public's business. My argument is about the systematic use of state and legal resources to intimidate, discredit, and destroy the women who made credible allegations against Clinton that went beyond consensual affairs. Paula Jones was a state employee who alleged that the sitting governor of Arkansas had summoned her to his hotel room and exposed himself. Kathleen Willey alleged that the President had groped her against her will in the Oval Office. Juanita Broaddrick alleged, in a taped interview whose credibility I found persuasive, that Clinton had raped her. The response of the Clinton White House to each of these women was not merely to deny the allegations. It was to organise the deployment of private investigators, the feeding of damaging information about each woman to the press, the use of friends and associates to pressure potential corroborating witnesses, and, in the Lewinsky case, the characterisation of an intern as a "stalker" — a characterisation spread through the press corps by a man sitting across this table from me today. The private conduct and the public conduct were inseparable. Clinton used the apparatus of power against the women he had wronged, and called this, through his spokesmen, "fighting right-wing smears." (No One Left to Lie To, Ch. 3)

VI. The Chinese fundraising scandal and the corruption of foreign policy by campaign finance

The China fundraising scandal: the deliberate trade of military and intelligence secrets for campaign contributions from People's Liberation Army front companiesI want to address the Chinese fundraising scandal, because I think it is the most serious instance of the corruption that I identify as structurally inseparable from the Clintonian method. The Thompson Committee investigation, and subsequent reporting, established that the Democratic National Committee had knowingly accepted campaign contributions channelled through front companies connected to the People's Liberation Army of China. The contributions were connected to a period during which the Clinton administration reversed years of policy by granting the export of satellite technology to China that had previously been denied on national security grounds, and which subsequently enabled improvements in Chinese ballistic missile accuracy. This was not merely a procedural campaign finance violation. It was, if the connection between the contributions and the policy reversal is as direct as the evidence suggests, the trading of a national security asset for campaign money from a foreign military. I am not a partisan Republican manufacturing this allegation. I am a man of the left who finds it remarkable that the same people who spent the Clinton years defending every other betrayal of progressive principle on the grounds of lesser-evilism could not bring themselves to take seriously the question of whether the President was selling military technology to China's army in exchange for re-election funding. (No One Left to Lie To, Ch. 4)

VII. The execution of Rickey Ray Rector: cruelty as political strategy

The execution of Rickey Ray Rector: a severely brain-damaged man executed in the middle of the New Hampshire primary to demonstrate that Clinton was tough on crimeI want to describe a specific event, because I think it illustrates more clearly than any other single act the nature of the man whose defender sits across from me. During the New Hampshire primary of 1992, while Clinton was under pressure from the Gennifer Flowers story, he returned to Arkansas specifically to be present for the execution of Rickey Ray Rector — a man who had shot himself in the head after committing murder, and who had thereby destroyed the frontal lobes of his own brain, leaving him so cognitively impaired that he did not understand what execution was. At his last meal, Rector set aside the dessert from his final supper "for later," not understanding that there would be no later. Clinton presided over this execution — which required multiple needle insertions because the execution team could not find a vein in the arms of a man who had suffered catastrophic brain damage — not because justice required it, not because there was any deterrent value, but because being seen to execute a black man with brain damage would signal to Southern white voters that he was not soft on crime. This is the man whose legacy I am asked to defend as progressive. The private vileness meshed exactly with the public style. The execution of Rickey Ray Rector was a campaign act, conducted at the expense of a man who did not know he was dying. (No One Left to Lie To, Ch. 2)

VIII. The impeachment: perjury about sex is still perjury, and using power to cover it is obstruction

The impeachment: the charge was not adultery but perjury and obstruction of justice — the deliberate use of presidential power to defeat a legal proceedingLet me address the impeachment directly, since Mr Blumenthal was himself a central figure in those events. The charge against Clinton was not that he had an affair with Monica Lewinsky. The charge was that he committed perjury in a civil proceeding under oath, and that he used the resources of the White House — the deployment of surrogates to describe Lewinsky as a deranged stalker, the coaching of witnesses, the use of presidential friends to offer Lewinsky a job in exchange for a helpful affidavit — to obstruct the legal process investigating that perjury. Both of these things happened. Clinton acknowledged the perjury, in effect, when he accepted disbarment in Arkansas and agreed to pay a fine rather than contest charges that would have led to a full hearing. The obstruction — the use of allies to spread false information about a witness, the exertion of social and professional pressure on those who might have corroborated the case — is documented in the record. The Clinton defence was "the Republicans did it for political reasons." This may be true. The Republicans did pursue impeachment for political reasons. But a crime remains a crime regardless of the motives of those who prosecute it, and a man who used the power of the White House to defeat a legal proceeding that was investigating his own conduct under oath has committed an offence against the rule of law that his political sympathisers' outrage at his persecutors does not excuse. (No One Left to Lie To, Ch. 4)

IX. Hillary Clinton and the Senate campaign: ambition without ideology

Hillary Clinton's New York Senate campaign: a calculated carpetbagger operation demonstrating that the couple had no political home, only political ambitionI want to close with the figure who has been, in a sense, the junior partner of the enterprise under examination, but who became in later years its primary face: Hillary Clinton. The New York Senate campaign of 2000 was described by its admirers as a triumph of political skill. I described it as evidence that the Clintons had no political home and no political convictions of their own — only an appetite for power that would seek whatever vessel offered itself. Hillary Clinton had no connection to New York. She had never lived there. She had never worked there. She had never expressed a political position specific to New York's concerns, interests, or communities. She sought the New York Senate seat because it was available, because it offered a platform for her own eventual national ambitions, and because the Senate was the only major political office available to a First Lady who had exhausted the possibilities of her husband's position. The campaign itself was notable chiefly for its studied vagueness — a characteristic the Clintons share, perfected across decades of triangulation — and for the calculation that New York's liberal electorate could be relied upon to prefer any Democrat over the Republican alternative, regardless of the Democrat's actual convictions. They were right. And the victory of that calculation is itself the indictment: of a political culture so hollowed out by Clintonian method that a carpetbagger with no platform could win a Senate seat from one of America's great cities simply by being acceptable and available. (No One Left to Lie To, expanded 2000 ed., Ch. 6)

Thank you.

The Defence
Sidney Blumenthal
The Clinton Wars (Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 2003) — Senior Adviser to President Clinton 1997–2001
Speech
Logical Arguments

Mr Hitchens,

I am going to address the personal dimension between us once, directly, and then not again, because it has been used — by you, among others — to discredit the substantive argument. You submitted an affidavit to the Republican impeachment managers stating that I had described Monica Lewinsky as a stalker, which I did not do, and which contradicted my sworn testimony. When the time came to testify against me under oath, you withdrew. Your relationship with the truth on this matter is not, therefore, as straightforwardly prosecutorial as your posture in this debate implies. What concerns me more, and what I want to address at length, is the picture of the Clinton presidency that your book presents — a picture so systematically stripped of context, so indifferent to the political conditions under which the decisions you condemn were made, so determined to reach its pre-ordained verdict, that it is, as I have written, a work of advocacy rather than of analysis. I take each charge seriously. I want to show why, taken in full context, each of them either misrepresents the facts or reaches a conclusion the facts cannot support.

I. Triangulation as the only available route through a hostile political environment

Triangulation was not betrayal but navigation: governing from the centre was the only available strategy after 1994 and produced the most prosperous peacetime period in American historyHitchens describes triangulation as a systematic deception of the electorate. I want to describe it as what it actually was: the political strategy of a Democratic president governing after the 1994 midterms, in which the Democrats lost control of both houses of Congress for the first time in forty years, against a Republican Congress that was determined to destroy his presidency at any cost and that had the legislative power to block anything it opposed. The choice was not, as Hitchens implies, between progressive purity and triangulation. The choice was between triangulation — finding areas of agreement that could produce actual legislation while preserving Democratic priorities where the executive branch had discretion — and complete legislative paralysis. The result of this navigation, by 1999, was the lowest unemployment rate in thirty years, the first sustained budget surpluses since the 1960s, twenty-two million new jobs, the largest expansion of the Earned Income Tax Credit in history, the Family and Medical Leave Act, the State Children's Health Insurance Programme, and the lowest poverty rates in twenty years. These are not rhetorical achievements. They are the actual lives of actual Americans who were better off in 2001 than they were in 1993. No analysis of Clinton's presidency that mentions welfare reform without also mentioning the EITC, SCHIP, and the sustained economic expansion that made welfare reform survivable for millions of families is an honest analysis. (The Clinton Wars, Introduction)

II. The welfare bill: a flawed compromise that preserved more than it conceded

Welfare reform: the bill was a compromise under extreme legislative pressure that preserved federal support for the working poor and was accompanied by the largest expansion of the Earned Income Tax Credit in historyOn welfare reform, Hitchens quotes the bill's harshest critics while omitting the context in which it was passed. By 1996, the Republican Congress had already passed two more punitive welfare reform bills, both of which Clinton had vetoed. The bill he eventually signed was the least bad version available — it preserved the Earned Income Tax Credit, maintained Medicaid eligibility, retained food stamp eligibility for most families, and was passed alongside a minimum wage increase. Clinton simultaneously pushed for and signed the largest single expansion of the EITC in history, directing more than ten billion dollars of additional support to working families over five years. The net result — taken as a whole, not in the single worst-case provision Hitchens cites — was an overall package that, combined with the economic expansion of the period, lifted millions of people out of poverty. Child poverty fell to its lowest rate in twenty years by 1999. The three HHS officials who resigned in protest did so over specific provisions that were subsequently modified. The claim that this was "worse than anything the Republicans could have hoped to pass" is false: the Republican bills Clinton vetoed were considerably more punitive. The choice was between this bill and the one that followed it. (The Clinton Wars, Ch. 6)

III. The Al-Shifa strike: the intelligence failure was real, but the decision was made in good faith on the available evidence

Al-Shifa: the intelligence on which the strike was based was flawed but genuinely believed; the timing coincidence is contested by every senior official involvedThe Al-Shifa bombing remains a genuinely troubling episode, and I will not pretend otherwise. The intelligence assessment that the factory was producing VX nerve agent precursors was wrong, and the consequences — destruction of civilian pharmaceutical infrastructure — were severe. These are facts and I acknowledge them. What I contest is Hitchens's claim that the strike was a deliberate act of military violence deployed to cover Clinton's legal exposure, and his implied claim that this is the only rational explanation for its timing. Every senior official involved — the Secretary of State, the National Security Advisor, the Secretary of Defense, the Director of Central Intelligence — has stated that the decision was made on intelligence that was genuinely believed at the time, in the context of very recent al-Qaeda bombings of two American embassies in Kenya and Tanzania that had killed hundreds of people. A president who had received specific intelligence about bin Laden and chosen not to act on it would have been rightly condemned if another attack followed. The timing was unfortunate. The intelligence was wrong. Neither of these things establishes, as a matter of fact rather than rhetoric, that the strike was ordered to manage domestic politics. Hitchens's "the timing was not a coincidence" is assertion. It has never been established as fact. (The Clinton Wars, Ch. 15)

IV. Operation Desert Fox: enforcing UN resolutions is not obstruction of justice in reverse

Desert Fox: the Iraqi non-compliance that triggered the strike was genuine and had been building for months; the impeachment timing was not proof of causationThe claim that Operation Desert Fox was timed to delay the House impeachment vote requires accepting that the President of the United States ordered four days of military strikes against Iraq, at the cost of American military resources and international diplomatic capital, primarily to delay a congressional vote by a few days. I want to press on the implausibility of this as a causal claim. The Iraqi non-compliance with UNSCOM that preceded Desert Fox was genuine, extensively documented, and had been escalating through the autumn of 1998 as Saddam Hussein systematically refused access to weapons inspectors and eventually expelled them entirely. Richard Butler, the UNSCOM chairman, reported to the Security Council on 15 December that Iraq was actively obstructing inspections. The decision to respond militarily was made at that point, when the legal and diplomatic basis for it was strongest — not manufactured. The fact that this coincided with the impeachment timeline is, I accept, a terrible misfortune for Clinton politically. It is not proof that the military decision was made for political reasons, any more than any other military decision that occurred during a politically inconvenient period would be proof of that. (The Clinton Wars, Ch. 17)

V. The allegations against Clinton and the organised right-wing smear machine

The women's allegations were processed through a right-wing network funded by the Scaife Foundation that manufactured and amplified false stories — the media's failure to interrogate this was the real scandalI want to describe something that Hitchens's book studiously omits: the infrastructure through which the allegations he treats as straightforwardly credible were generated, funded, and distributed. The Arkansas Project — funded by Richard Mellon Scaife, who spent approximately seventeen million dollars on a systematic programme of opposition research, fabrication, and press placement against Clinton — was the single most important source of the allegations that drove the Starr investigation. Paula Jones's case was originally brought with support from conservative legal groups with explicit political agendas. The "Arkansas troopers" who provided accounts of Clinton's sexual conduct were paid for those accounts by Scaife-connected journalists. This is not a conspiracy theory. It is a documented network, exposed over time, that produced stories that the mainstream media then amplified without adequate scepticism. I am not saying that every allegation against Clinton was manufactured. I am saying that the credibility Hitchens accords to allegations whose provenance runs through a billionaire's personally funded opposition research programme, without examining that provenance, is not the behaviour of a disinterested journalist. It is the behaviour of someone who wanted the story to be true. (The Clinton Wars, Ch. 3)

VI. The Chinese fundraising allegations were investigated and largely unsubstantiated

The Thompson Committee inquiry produced no evidence that policy was traded for contributions; the investigation was conducted by a Republican-controlled committee with its own political agendaThe Thompson Committee investigation — conducted by a Republican-controlled Senate committee whose chairman, Fred Thompson, had announced before it opened that it would demonstrate systematic Chinese infiltration of the Clinton campaign — ultimately produced no evidence that any specific policy had been traded for any specific contribution. What it found was that the DNC had accepted contributions from donors who turned out to be impermissible foreign nationals, which was a genuine compliance failure and resulted in the return of the money and reforms to fundraising procedures. The connection between the satellite technology export decisions and the Chinese contributions — the specific claim Hitchens makes about a quid pro quo — was not established by the Thompson investigation or any subsequent proceeding. The satellite export decision was, in fact, driven by the American aerospace industry's lobbying for the commercial value of Chinese launch contracts, lobbying that had begun under the Reagan administration and that crossed administrations and parties. To take the most sinister available interpretation of genuinely ambiguous facts, and to present it as established, is exactly the method of investigation Hitchens criticises when it is applied to Clinton by the right. (The Clinton Wars, Ch. 11)

VII. Rickey Ray Rector: the context of capital punishment politics that Hitchens omits

Rector's execution: Clinton's capital punishment record reflected the political reality that Democrats had lost the presidency for a generation partly on the perception of being soft on crime; this does not excuse it but contextualises itThe execution of Rickey Ray Rector was genuinely and deeply troubling, and it reflects a view of capital punishment that I personally find repugnant and that Clinton himself later expressed misgivings about. I will not pretend otherwise. What I want to contest is Hitchens's framing of this single act as definitive proof of Clinton's comprehensive cynicism, as opposed to evidence of a genuine, if morally serious, tension that defined Democratic politics throughout the 1980s and 1990s. The Democratic Party had lost five of the previous six presidential elections partly on the perception of being insufficiently serious about crime and punishment — a perception that Michael Dukakis's response to the Willie Horton episode had catastrophically reinforced. The question of whether a Democratic candidate in 1992 could win a general election without demonstrating distance from this perception is not a trivial one. It is not an excuse for an unconscionable act. It is the context within which the act occurred — context that Hitchens omits because his argument requires Clinton to be simply wicked rather than the product of a genuine political trap that the Democratic Party had set for itself over a decade of electoral losses.

VIII. The impeachment was a coup attempt by a criminal conspiracy masquerading as a constitutional proceeding

The impeachment: a Republican constitutional coup, manufactured by a prosecutor who exceeded his mandate and a media establishment that abandoned its obligations to accuracy and scepticismHitchens frames the impeachment as straightforwardly about perjury and obstruction, and treats the Republican partisan motivation as a separate question that does not affect the validity of the charges. I want to resist this severance, because the way the charges were manufactured is directly relevant to the question of whether they constitute the category of high crimes and misdemeanours the Constitution requires for impeachment.
