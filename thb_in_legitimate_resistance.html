Just War Theory Extended to Non-State ResistanceChristopher Finlay — Terrorism and the Right to Resist (2015)
Classical just war theory was developed to govern conflicts between states, but its underlying moral logic — that defensive force may be justified when ordinary political and legal remedies are unavailable — applies equally to non-state actors facing oppression. When a population is subject to systematic rights-violations by a state that monopolises legitimate violence and forecloses all non-violent redress, those people may acquire a justified right to resist through force. This is not a blanket endorsement of terrorism but the application of existing jus ad bellum criteria to asymmetric contexts.

The Principle of Humanity & Palestinian TerrorTed Honderich — Humanity, Terrorism, Terrorist War (2006)
The Principle of Humanity holds that we are obligated to take the rational, effective steps available to us to save people from bad lives. Where a people is systematically deprived of a homeland and of the freedoms that constitute a minimally decent life, and where all non-violent routes to remedy have been exhausted or foreclosed, the Principle of Humanity supports their use of violence to secure those freedoms. The Palestinians have had a moral right to their terrorism in the occupied territories. This is a consequentialist judgement grounded in the magnitude of the wrong suffered.

Liability, Moral Responsibility & Defensive KillingJonathan Parry — “Legitimate Authority and the Ethics of War” (2017) & related work
The question of who may be targeted in defensive violence turns not on their status as combatant or civilian, soldier or rebel, but on their moral responsibility for an unjust threat. Those who are morally responsible for rights-violating policies — whether state agents or civilians who causally sustain an unjust order — may be liable to defensive force. This liability-based account extends the permission to use defensive violence to non-state actors in ways that state-centric just war theory cannot accommodate, and does so without conceding that anything goes.

Chair,

I want to begin by clarifying what the right to resist is and is not, because the opposition will be tempted to treat any defence of non-state political violence as a blanket endorsement of terrorism. It is not. My argument is precise: under conditions that satisfy the criteria of jus ad bellum — just cause, right intention, proportionality, discrimination, and last resort — non-state actors may acquire a moral right to use political violence in resistance against oppressive states. This is not a radical departure from just war theory. It is just war theory applied consistently.

“The question is not whether terrorism is ever justified — most people accept that extreme cases exist. The question is whether the framework that justifies state violence in war can, with appropriate modifications, also justify non-state resistance. I argue that it can.” — Finlay, Terrorism and the Right to Resist
i. the moral equality of combatants reconsidered

Revisionist just war — McMahan's revisionism: combatant moral equality is false; what matters is whether the combatant fights for a just cause; this opens space for non-state actors fighting justly to have full combatant permissionsThe orthodox view of just war theory — what Michael Walzer defends and what Coady relies upon — holds that combatants on both sides of a conflict are morally equal: soldiers fighting for an unjust war are not murderers, provided they fight within the rules of engagement. This doctrine of combatant moral equality insulates the jus in bello from the jus ad bellum. I reject this doctrine, following the revisionist tradition associated with Jeff McMahan. Combatants fighting for an unjust cause are not morally equivalent to combatants fighting for a just cause. The moral permission to kill in war is grounded in the justice of the cause and the moral responsibility of those targeted, not in the holding of a state-issued uniform. Once we abandon combatant moral equality, the state’s monopoly on legitimate violence in war dissolves. A non-state actor fighting for a just cause has the same moral permissions as a state combatant fighting for a just cause — because those permissions derive from the same source: the justice of the cause and the liability of those threatened.

ii. the conditions for justified non-state resistance

Jus ad bellum applied to resistance — just cause (systematic rights violation), last resort (non-violent remedies foreclosed), proportionality (violence calibrated to wrong), discrimination (targeting the responsible): all apply to non-state actorsThe jus ad bellum criteria apply to non-state resistance in the following way. Just cause: a population must be subject to systematic, serious rights-violations by a state that has forfeited its claim to political authority over them. This is not mere policy disagreement; it requires something approaching a Lockean dissolution of the social contract. Last resort: non-violent remedies — legal challenge, political organisation, civil disobedience, international appeal — must have been exhausted or be genuinely unavailable. This is the criterion that most frequently fails: most putative cases of justified resistance in fact have non-violent routes available. Proportionality: the violence used must be proportionate to the wrong resisted and must not cause greater harm than it prevents. Discrimination: force must be directed at those who are morally responsible for the unjust situation, not at the general population. These are demanding criteria. They are designed to be demanding. But they are not criteria that non-state actors are structurally incapable of satisfying.

iii. the asymmetry objection and why it fails

Asymmetry objection — the objection that non-state actors cannot satisfy discrimination because they lack military capacity to fight symmetrically misunderstands discrimination: the criterion governs targeting, not tactics; guerrilla action can be discriminateCoady and others press what I call the asymmetry objection: non-state actors, lacking the military capacity of states, are structurally unable to satisfy discrimination requirements because they can only conduct violence through tactics — assassination, bombing, ambush — that inevitably kill non-combatants. I deny this. The criterion of discrimination governs who is targeted, not what tactics are used. A guerrilla campaign that directs force at the political and military officials responsible for an unjust occupation, while taking care to minimise harm to civilians, can satisfy discrimination. The fact that such campaigns are often conducted indiscriminately does not show that discrimination is structurally impossible; it shows that most actual resistance movements fail to meet the standard. That is a critique of particular movements, not of the framework. The Israeli state, for its part, has repeatedly failed discrimination criteria in ways that generate no comparable philosophical objection to the state’s right to use force at all.

iv. political violence vs. terrorism: the importance of the distinction

PV vs. terrorism — political violence that targets the responsible is not terrorism; terrorism deliberately targets civilians to spread fear; the distinction matters morally; the right to resist extends to the former, not the latterI want to insist on a distinction that is absolutely central to my position and that the opposition tends to collapse: the distinction between political violence that is directed at those morally responsible for an unjust situation, and terrorism, which deliberately targets civilians to spread fear and coerce political change. My argument defends a right to the former; it does not defend terrorism as typically understood. I agree with Coady that deliberately targeting non-combatant civilians to spread terror is not justified by the justice of the political cause. What I deny is that this conclusion rules out all non-state political violence. A resistance movement that targets the military and political officials of an occupying power, proportionately and discriminately, is not engaged in terrorism in the morally relevant sense. Calling all non-state violence “terrorism” is a political move, not a philosophical one.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — Revisionist just war: combatant moral equality is false; moral permission to fight derives from justice of cause, not state authorisation
1.Orthodox just war theory (Walzer) holds that combatants on both sides are morally equal: the permission to kill derives from combatant status, not from the justice of the cause. This insulates jus in bello from jus ad bellum.
2.Revisionist just war theory (McMahan, Finlay) holds that combatant moral equality is false: the moral permission to kill in war derives from just cause and the moral responsibility (liability) of those targeted, not from state-issued status.
3.If the moral permission to fight derives from just cause and liability, then a non-state actor fighting for a just cause has the same moral permission as a state combatant fighting for a just cause. State authorisation is morally irrelevant.
∴The revisionist account of just war grounds combatant permissions in just cause and liability; state authorisation is morally irrelevant; non-state actors fighting justly have the same moral permissions as state combatants fighting justly.
Argument II — Jus ad bellum criteria apply to non-state resistance and can in principle be satisfied
1.Just cause, last resort, proportionality, and discrimination are the standard jus ad bellum criteria. They were developed for state-versus-state conflicts but their moral logic is independent of that context.
2.Non-state actors can satisfy these criteria: just cause (systematic rights-violation by the state), last resort (non-violent remedies exhausted), proportionality (violence calibrated to the wrong), discrimination (targeting the morally responsible).
3.The criteria are demanding but not structurally inaccessible to non-state actors. That many actual resistance movements fail them is a critique of those movements, not of the framework.
∴Jus ad bellum criteria apply to non-state resistance; they can in principle be satisfied; their demandingness does not make them structurally inaccessible to non-state actors.
Argument III — The asymmetry objection fails: discrimination governs targeting, not tactics; guerrilla action can be discriminate
1.The asymmetry objection holds that non-state actors, lacking conventional military capacity, cannot satisfy discrimination because their available tactics inevitably kill civilians.
2.But discrimination governs who is targeted, not what tactics are used. A guerrilla campaign can target only those morally responsible for an unjust order while minimising civilian harm — satisfying discrimination despite using unconventional tactics.
3.States also frequently fail discrimination in practice. That non-state actors often fail it does not show structural incapacity; it shows that specific movements have not met the standard.
∴The asymmetry objection conflates tactics with targeting; discrimination governs targeting; non-state actors can satisfy it with discriminate guerrilla action directed at the morally responsible.
Argument IV — Political violence targeting the responsible is not terrorism; the right to resist extends to the former only
1.Terrorism deliberately targets civilians to spread fear and coerce political change. Political violence directed at those morally responsible for an unjust situation is categorically distinct.
2.The right to resist defended here extends to discriminate political violence targeting the responsible, not to terrorism. Collapsing this distinction is a political move, not a philosophical one.
3.A resistance movement targeting military and political officials of an occupying power, proportionately, is not engaged in terrorism in the morally relevant sense even if the state calls it terrorism.
∴The right to resist extends to discriminate political violence targeting the morally responsible; it does not extend to terrorism deliberately targeting civilians; the distinction is morally fundamental and must be preserved.

Chair,

I want to approach this question from a different philosophical tradition from my colleague Finlay. I am not primarily a just war theorist. I am a consequentialist philosopher working in the tradition of the Principle of Humanity, and my argument is that the Palestinian people — and peoples in comparable situations of deprivation and dispossession — have had a moral right to their terrorism in the occupied territories. I use the word deliberately, knowing that it will draw objection. Let me explain why I use it and what I mean.

“Was it wrong for the Palestinians to resort to violence? I was led to conclude, by the Principle of Humanity, that it was not wrong, that in fact they had a moral right to their terrorism in the Occupied Territories.” — Honderich, Humanity, Terrorism, Terrorist War
i. the principle of humanity

Principle of Humanity — we are obligated to take the rational effective steps available to us to end or prevent bad lives, defined by deprivation of the great goods: length of life, quality of life, freedom, respect, relationships, goods of a communityThe Principle of Humanity states that we are obligated to take the rational, effective steps actually open to us to end or prevent people from having bad lives. A bad life, on this account, is defined by deprivation of the six great human goods: a decent length of life, bodily quality of life, freedom and power, respect and self-respect, goods of relationship with others, and the goods of a community. When a people is systematically deprived of these goods by an unjust political order — as the Palestinians in the occupied territories are deprived of a homeland, of freedom of movement, of security, of political agency — the Principle of Humanity is engaged. It asks: what rational, effective steps are available to end this deprivation? And it answers: whatever steps are rational and effective, given the magnitude of the wrong and the unavailability of alternatives.

ii. why the particular case of palestine satisfies the principle

Palestine — the Palestinian case satisfies the Principle of Humanity: systematic dispossession since 1948, occupation of the West Bank and Gaza, foreclosure of non-violent political remedies by Israeli state power and US veto at the UNThe Palestinian case satisfies the Principle of Humanity in an unusually clear way. Since 1948, Palestinian Arabs have been subject to a systematic dispossession that has deprived millions of their homes, their land, their political community, and their freedom. The occupation of the West Bank and Gaza since 1967 has subjected a further several million to military rule, movement restrictions, settlement expansion, and the destruction of their economic and social life. Non-violent remedies have been systematically foreclosed: the United Nations has been blocked from effective action by the American veto; international legal judgements have been ignored; internal political organisation has been suppressed. When Meisels argues that terrorism is never justified, she must confront this specific case. What are the rational, effective steps available to the Palestinian people to end their deprivation? If non-violence is the answer, it must be a non-violence with a realistic prospect of success. Fifty years of non-violent advocacy have not ended the occupation.

iii. responding to the civilian targeting objection

Civilian targeting — Honderich distinguishes between terrorism that is justifiable under the Principle of Humanity and terrorism that is not; the criterion is whether civilians targeted are part of the unjust political order being resistedMeisels will argue that any violence targeting civilians is unjustifiable regardless of the political cause. I want to make a distinction that she tends to collapse. Some of those whom Israeli and Western commentators classify as civilian non-combatants are, in the context of a settler-colonial occupation, significant participants in the political and material structures of that occupation. Settlers on occupied land are not equivalent to random uninvolved civilians. I am not arguing that the murder of anyone is desirable or admirable. I am arguing that the moral calculus of a people deprived of all effective political remedy is different from the calculus of people who have full access to legal and political means of redress. The question “is this terrorism justified?” cannot be answered in the abstract; it must be answered in the context of the specific deprivation, the specific foreclosure of alternatives, and the specific political order being resisted.

I yield the floor.

Argument I — The Principle of Humanity grounds an obligation to take rational effective steps to end bad lives; violence can be such a step
1.The Principle of Humanity: we are obligated to take rational, effective steps available to us to end or prevent people from having bad lives defined by deprivation of the six great goods.
2.When non-violent steps are unavailable or ineffective, and violence is the only rational effective step available, the Principle of Humanity supports using violence. The criterion is rational effectiveness, not the mode of action.
3.This is a consequentialist framework that does not rely on just war criteria or state authorisation; it derives the permission to resist from the magnitude of the wrong and the availability of effective remedies.
∴The Principle of Humanity grounds a permission to use political violence when it is the rational effective step available to end systematic deprivation; this permission is consequentialist and independent of just war frameworks.
Argument II — The Palestinian case satisfies the Principle: systematic deprivation, foreclosure of non-violent remedies, magnitude of the wrong
1.Palestinians have been subject to systematic dispossession since 1948 and military occupation since 1967, depriving them of the great goods: homeland, freedom, political community, security, economic life.
2.Non-violent remedies have been systematically foreclosed: UN resolutions blocked by US veto, legal judgements ignored, internal political organisation suppressed. Fifty years of non-violent advocacy have not ended the occupation.
3.The magnitude of the wrong (millions of people deprived of homeland and freedom for generations) and the foreclosure of alternatives satisfy the Principle of Humanity’s conditions for justified violence.
∴The Palestinian case satisfies the Principle of Humanity: the magnitude of deprivation, its duration, and the foreclosure of non-violent remedies justify the use of political violence under the Principle.
Argument III — The civilian targeting objection fails to account for the political complicity of settlers and the differential calculus of the occupied
1.The civilian/combatant distinction as applied in settler-colonial occupation does not map cleanly: settlers on occupied land are material participants in the political structure of the occupation, not equivalent to uninvolved bystanders.
2.The moral calculus of a people with no effective political remedy differs from that of people with full access to legal and political means of redress. The same action can have different moral valence in different contexts of political agency.
3.Context-blind prohibitions on targeting civilians fail to engage with the specific political structures that make some putative “civilians” participants in an unjust order against which no other effective remedy exists.
∴The civilian targeting objection fails to account for political complicity and differential access to remedy; context-blind prohibitions cannot settle the moral question of Palestinian resistance.

Chair,

I want to build on Finlay’s revisionist framework by pressing the liability account of defensive killing, which I think provides the strongest philosophical foundation for the right to resist. The crucial question is not who counts as a combatant under international humanitarian law, but who is morally responsible for an unjust threat and therefore liable to defensive force. These are different questions with importantly different answers.

i. liability to defensive killing: the moral responsibility account

Liability — a person is liable to defensive killing if they are morally responsible for an unjust threat to others; liability is grounded in moral responsibility, not in combatant status; this applies to state agents enforcing unjust policiesOn the moral responsibility account of liability, a person becomes liable to defensive killing when they are morally responsible for posing or sustaining an unjust threat to others. This account generates a very different picture of who may be targeted than the combatant/civilian distinction of orthodox just war theory. A soldier following orders in an unjust occupation is morally responsible for the threat he poses to the occupied population, even if he is personally sympathetic to their cause and acting under institutional pressure. A politician who votes for and enforces unjust settlement policy is morally responsible for the threat that policy poses, even if she never holds a weapon. A civilian who votes for, materially supports, and benefits from an unjust occupation sustains the threat that occupation poses, even if her causal contribution is diffuse. Each of these agents has, in proportion to their moral responsibility, some degree of liability to defensive force by those against whom they are threatening.

ii. the authority problem and its solution

Authority — Rodin argues non-state actors lack legitimate authority to wage war; the liability account dissolves this objection: individual defensive permissions do not require institutional authorisationRodin argues, and I want to engage with this seriously, that non-state actors lack the legitimate authority required to wage war. The authority condition in just war theory holds that only legitimate political authorities may declare war and authorise their members to fight. Non-state resistance movements, on this view, lack this authorisation and therefore their members fight without the institutional warrant that converts killing into something other than murder. But the liability account dissolves this objection. Individual rights of self-defence and defence of others do not require institutional authorisation: a person may kill in self-defence without a state licence to do so. The authority condition, on the revisionist account, is at most a condition for the collective organisation of defensive force — a condition governing when a group may act as a group — and not a condition on the individual moral permission to defend oneself and others against unjust threats. Members of a non-state resistance movement who kill those who are liable to be killed do not commit murder, regardless of whether their organisation has been recognised as a legitimate authority.

iii. proportionality, risk imposition and the ethics of asymmetric warfare

Asymmetric proportionality — in asymmetric conflicts, non-state actors face choices about risk distribution that are structurally different from those of state armies; they may impose greater risk on opponents when the cause is just and alternatives are unavailableIn asymmetric conflicts, resistance fighters face a distinctive problem of risk distribution. Conventional military ethics requires combatants to accept some risk to themselves in order to reduce risk to non-combatants. State armies can accept this requirement because they have military capacity that allows them to engage opposing forces while protecting their own personnel through training, equipment, and support. Resistance fighters often have none of these advantages. Requiring them to accept the same absolute constraints as well-equipped state armies is to require them to accept conditions under which effective resistance is impossible. This is not an endorsement of anything-goes warfare. It is the recognition that proportionality requirements must be calibrated to the actual capacities of the actors involved. A resistance movement that cannot engage occupying forces in conventional battle may be justified in using tactics that impose greater risk on those who are morally responsible for the unjust occupation, provided those tactics are directed at the responsible and are proportionate to the wrong being resisted.

Just War Scepticism & the Limits of Non-State ViolenceC.A.J. (Tony) Coady — Morality and Political Violence (2008)
Just war theory provides a framework for evaluating political violence, but its criteria are demanding and are systematically misapplied by those seeking to justify terrorism. Self-Defence, State Authority & the Incoherence of War’s JustificationDavid Rodin — War and Self-Defense (2002)
My scepticism about the justification of political violence cuts in a different direction from Coady and Meisels. I am not primarily defending the state’s monopoly on legitimate violence. I am questioning whether political violence on the scale of war — whether by states or non-state actors — can ever be adequately justified by appeal to individual rights of self-defence and defence of others. The analogy between individual self-defence and collective political violence is far weaker than its proponents assume, and the revisionist framework that Finlay and Parry rely on inherits these weaknesses.

Chair,

I want to engage directly with Finlay’s revisionist framework, because I think it is the most sophisticated version of the proposition’s case and the one that most needs careful critical attention. Finlay presents himself as applying just war criteria to non-state actors rather than exempting them from those criteria. I want to show that the revisionist framework he relies on actually dissolves the criteria in ways that make them unavailable to ground the discriminations he claims to draw.

“Terrorism involves the intentional targeting of non-combatants for political purposes. This is wrong not merely because of the harm it causes but because of what it is: the deliberate infliction of violence on those who have done nothing to forfeit their immunity from it.” — Coady, Morality and Political Violence
i. the revisionist account dissolves discrimination

Dissolution of discrimination — McMahan's revisionism, extended by Finlay, makes virtually any civilian a potential legitimate target by grounding liability in diffuse moral responsibility; this destroys the practical function of the combatant/civilian distinctionFinlay and Parry ground the permission to target in the moral responsibility of the target for an unjust threat. Parry goes further and explicitly extends liability to civilians who vote for, support, and benefit from unjust policies. I want to press the implications of this extension. In a democratic state pursuing an unjust occupation, virtually every citizen participates to some degree in the political structures that sustain the occupation: they vote, they pay taxes, they benefit from the economic exploitation of occupied resources. On Parry’s account, each of these citizens bears some moral responsibility for the unjust threat the occupation poses. Each is, to some degree, liable to defensive force. The combatant/civilian distinction has, on this account, been replaced by a spectrum of varying moral responsibility. But a spectrum is not a distinction. In practice, resistance fighters operating under this framework have no principled basis on which to identify who may not be targeted. The practical function of the combatant/civilian distinction — to provide a clear, operationalisable rule that limits violence to those who pose direct threats — has been dissolved.

ii. last resort is almost never satisfied

Last resort — the last resort criterion is genuinely demanding; virtually all actual cases of non-state resistance occur before non-violent remedies have been fully exhausted; advocates of resistance systematically understate what non-violence has achievedFinlay acknowledges that last resort is a demanding criterion. He acknowledges that most actual resistance movements fail it. But he does not press the implications of this acknowledgement. In virtually every actual case where non-state resistance is advocated as justified — Palestine, Northern Ireland, the Basque Country, Chechnya, Kashmir — non-violent alternatives have not been exhausted in the relevant sense. Non-violent alternatives have not produced the desired political outcome within the desired timeframe. This is not the same thing as being exhausted or unavailable. The history of successful non-violent resistance — Gandhi, the Civil Rights Movement, the anti-apartheid movement, Solidarity in Poland — shows that organised non-violent resistance can succeed against enormous state power. The advocates of armed resistance consistently underestimate what patient, sustained non-violence can achieve, and they consistently overestimate how foreclosed non-violent routes are in their specific case. The result is that last resort is invoked as satisfied when it is not.

iii. the definitional move and what it conceals

Definitional move — Finlay distinguishes political violence from terrorism and applies his defence only to the former; but this distinction does the work only if we can clearly identify what counts as political violence vs terrorism; in practice this distinction is contested and manipulableFinlay distinguishes “political violence” from “terrorism” and defends only the former. This distinction does the philosophical work in his argument: it allows him to claim that he is not defending terrorism while defending a practice that looks very like it. But the distinction is operationally unstable. Is a bombing that kills two soldiers and injures six civilian bystanders “political violence” or “terrorism”? Is an assassination of a political official “targeted political violence” or “terrorism against elected representatives”? Is Honderich’s own example of Palestinian violence “political violence against participants in an unjust order” or “terrorism against civilians”? Each of these characterisations depends on contested judgements about who counts as a combatant, what counts as participation in an unjust order, and which harms are proportionate. The very judgements that a theory of justified resistance is supposed to guide are the ones on which the distinction between political violence and terrorism depends. The distinction therefore cannot serve as the foundation of the theory without circularity.

iv. the moral corruption of resistance movements

Moral corruption — even initially discriminate resistance movements tend toward indiscriminate violence through a well-documented political dynamic; philosophical justifications for resistance ignore this empirical pattern at our perilThere is a further argument against the proposition that is empirical rather than purely philosophical, and it is one that philosophers in this area too readily ignore. The history of resistance movements that began with philosophical justifications for targeted political violence against combatants and state agents is, almost without exception, a history of progressive moral corruption: of the extension of targeting to increasingly distant civilian populations, of the use of increasingly indiscriminate methods, of the subordination of ethical constraints to political expediency. The IRA began with a broadly republican political programme and ended bombing city centres and murdering civilians. Hamas’s founding documents called for the destruction of Israel and the killing of Jews. The FARC, ETA, the Red Brigades — all began with philosophically articulable grievances and all descended into indiscriminate violence. The philosophical framework that justifies the beginning does not constrain the middle and the end. A just war theory that provides the initial justification without providing the institutional and political constraints that prevent escalation is not a theory that serves justice; it is a theory that serves violence.

2.A spectrum of varying moral responsibility provides no operationalisable rule for identifying who may not be targeted. The combatant/civilian distinction’s practical function — a clear, applicable limit on targeting — has been dissolved.
3.Without an operationalisable distinction, resistance fighters have no principled basis for limiting their violence. The theoretical framework enables rather than constrains targeting of virtually all members of an opposing society.
∴The revisionist moral responsibility account dissolves discrimination: it replaces a clear operationalisable distinction with an unoperationalisable spectrum that provides no principled limit on targeting.

Argument IV — Resistance movements tend toward moral corruption; philosophical justifications for initial targeted violence do not constrain subsequent escalation

My scepticism in this debate differs importantly from that of my colleagues Coady and Meisels. I am not primarily defending the state’s monopoly on legitimate violence, nor am I committed to the absolute inviolability of non-combatant immunity as a deontological constraint. My argument is more fundamental: the analogy between individual self-defence and collective political violence is far weaker than either the proposition or the orthodox just war tradition assumes, and this weakness infects both the state’s justifications for war and the non-state actor’s justifications for resistance.

“The right of national defence cannot be derived from the individual right of self-defence. The attempt to do so results in a fundamental confusion between the moral situation of the individual and the moral situation of states and peoples.” — Rodin, War and Self-Defense
i. the self-defence analogy and its failures

Self-defence analogy — individual self-defence is plausibly justified by the immediacy of the threat, the identity of the attacker, and the absence of alternatives; collective political violence shares none of these features; the analogy failsIndividual self-defence is morally justified, if it is justified, by three features of the situation: the immediacy and directness of the threat (there is no time for recourse to law or authority); the clear identity of the attacker (the threat is posed by a specific agent whose agency is responsible for it); and the absence of alternatives (retreat is unavailable or insufficiently protective). These features are jointly sufficient to justify killing in self-defence. Now consider collective political violence — whether by states in war or by non-state resistance movements. The threat addressed is typically not immediate: political oppression operates over years and decades, not in the moment of attack. The identity of the responsible agents is diffuse: it is spread across governments, institutions, populations, and historical processes, not concentrated in a single attacking agent. And alternatives are typically available: legal remedy, international pressure, political organisation, civil disobedience. The conditions that justify individual self-defence are systematically absent from the situations that the proposition claims justify collective political violence.

ii. the liability account cannot be extended to collective violence

Collective liability — Parry's liability account works for individual defensive killing; it cannot be extended to collective political violence without generating the same failures as the self-defence analogy; collective violence involves killing people who are not the specific liable agentsParry’s liability account is most plausible as an account of individual defensive killing: a person who poses an unjust threat may be killed by their specific victim in self-defence. The account becomes far less plausible when extended to collective political violence. In a resistance campaign, fighters kill individual soldiers, officials, and in Honderich’s version, settlers and civilians. But each individual killed may or may not be the specific agent whose moral responsibility grounds their liability. A soldier following orders in an unjust occupation may or may not be morally responsible for the occupation in a degree that grounds liability. A settler who is a recent economic migrant may or may not be morally responsible for the political structures of the occupation. Parry’s account requires assessing the moral responsibility of each individual target. In practice, collective violence kills people based on their group membership — their being soldiers, settlers, Israelis, or members of whatever class is defined as the enemy. This is precisely the kind of collective attribution of liability that the individual-rights framework cannot support.

iii. political violence does not solve political problems

Political problems need political solutions — political violence does not resolve the underlying political problems that generate it; it typically entrenches them; the proposition's framework provides the permission to fight without addressing what fighting achievesMy final argument is one that the philosophical literature on political violence tends to ignore because it concerns the efficacy rather than the permissibility of violence. Even if we grant, for argument’s sake, that a resistance movement has a moral permission to use political violence, the question remains: will doing so actually improve the political situation of those in whose name the violence is conducted? The evidence is overwhelmingly that political violence does not solve political problems. It generates retaliatory cycles, it hardens the positions of the opposing party, it discredits moderate voices on both sides, it provides justification for state repression, and it typically leaves the underlying political dispute exactly where it was while adding layers of trauma, grievance, and radicalization. The proposition has provided a framework for justifying violence. It has not provided a framework for asking whether the violence it justifies will actually help those it purports to defend. That omission is not a minor oversight; it is the most important practical question at stake.
