Prime Minister — The Wrongful Intentions ParadoxGregory Kavka — Moral Paradoxes of Nuclear Deterrence, 1987
The standard deontological objection to nuclear deterrence — that intending to kill innocents is impermissible regardless of consequences — confronts a paradox: forming the conditional intention to retaliate may be the only act that prevents the very catastrophe that would make the intention wrong to execute. Deterrence creates a moral asymmetry in which the wrongfulness of the intention is offset by the enormity of the harm its absence would cause. The right-making features of intending to deter can, in this uniquely tragic case, outweigh the wrong-making features of the content of that intention.

Mr Speaker,

The nuclear deterrence debate presents moral philosophy with one of its hardest problems: can an intention that would be wrong to execute ever be right to form? I believe it can, and that nuclear deterrence is the paradigm case that demonstrates this possibility. I call this the Wrongful Intentions Paradox, and understanding it is essential to understanding why the Opposition’s clean deontological refusal of deterrence is not only politically naive but philosophically mistaken.

The standard deontological argument against deterrence runs as follows: it is impermissible to intend to kill innocent people; nuclear retaliation would kill innocent people; therefore it is impermissible to intend to retaliate with nuclear weapons. This argument is valid. But it reaches its conclusion by ignoring the strategic context that gives the intention its moral significance — namely, that forming this conditional intention may be the only act capable of preventing a nuclear attack in the first place.

The Wrongful Intentions Paradox, stated formally.

Standard principle: It is wrong to intend to do what it would be wrong to do.
Application: It would be wrong to execute a retaliatory nuclear strike (it kills innocents); therefore it is wrong to intend to execute one.

The paradox: forming this conditional intention — publicly and credibly — may be the only available means of preventing the aggressor’s first strike. The absence of the intention makes the catastrophe it would prevent more probable. So we face a genuine moral dilemma: forming a wrongful intention may be the only available means of preventing an even greater wrong. The Principle of Moral Obligation — that one is sometimes obligated to choose the lesser evil — applies here, and it licenses forming the wrongful conditional intention because the alternative is a greater harm.
i. the paradox of deterrence and the lesser evil

Lesser evil — forming wrongful intention prevents greater harm; standard lesser-evil reasoning applies to intention formation, not just actionThe lesser-evil principle is applied routinely in ethics: a surgeon who amputates a limb to save a life performs an act that would otherwise be gravely wrong. The paradox of deterrence is that the lesser-evil reasoning must apply not merely to actions but to the formation of intentions. Forming a conditional intention to retaliate is an action — a public, political, strategic act — that has causal consequences independent of whether the intention is ever executed. That action, the formation and communication of the deterrent intention, may prevent a catastrophe that the absence of the intention would make likely. Measuring the moral weight of the intention-forming act requires including its causal consequences, including the catastrophe it prevents. The deontological objection focuses only on the wrongfulness of the intention’s content; it ignores the wrongfulness of the catastrophe that the absence of the intention would enable.

ii. the special feature of the conditional intention

Conditional intention — its moral weight depends on context; an intention one sincerely hopes never to execute is different from an intention one plans to act on; deterrence exploits thisA conditional intention — “I will retaliate if and only if you strike first” — has a distinctive moral structure. The agent who forms it sincerely hopes never to be in the position of executing it. The whole point of the intention is to make its execution unnecessary. This differs fundamentally from the intention to murder: the murderer hopes to execute their intention; the deterrer hopes never to. This asymmetry does not eliminate the wrong-making feature of the intention’s content, but it is a morally significant difference that affects the overall moral assessment. The conditional deterrent intention is formed under compulsion by the strategic situation; it is not an expression of the agent’s values but a strategic response to a world the agent did not choose.

iii. the intention-action gap and moral luck

Intention-action gap — having an intention does not commit one to acting on it if circumstances change; the deterrer retains moral agency at the moment of executionHaving a conditional intention does not commit the agent to acting on it if the context of execution reveals morally decisive reasons not to act. The deterrer who finds themselves at the moment of retaliation — if that moment ever comes — retains full moral agency. They may, at that point, decide not to retaliate, accepting whatever consequences follow. The intention is not an irrevocable commitment that removes agency from the moment of execution. This means that the moral cost of forming the deterrent intention is less than the deontologist assumes: the agent is not thereby committing to perform a future wrong but expressing a conditional policy whose execution remains subject to moral review. The wrongfulness of the content is moderated by the agent’s retained moral agency at the execution point.

iv. the practical necessity argument

Practical necessity — given nuclear proliferation, deterrence may be the least-bad strategy; moral philosophy must engage with the world as it isThe moral assessment of nuclear deterrence cannot be made in abstraction from the strategic world in which nuclear weapons already exist and are possessed by states whose intentions are not reliably benign. Given this world, the practical question is not “should nuclear weapons exist?” — they do — but “what is the least-bad strategy for managing the risks they create?” For states that are targets of nuclear threats, unilateral disarmament offers a worse risk profile than deterrence in the strategic circumstances that actually obtain. The wrongfulness of the deterrent intention must be weighed against the practical necessity of the strategy in a world that nuclear knowledge has permanently transformed.

I approach this debate from the perspective of longtermism — the view that what matters most, morally, is the long-run trajectory of human civilisation and the protection of humanity’s potential future. From this perspective, the most important question in the nuclear deterrence debate is not whether deterrence is morally clean by deontological standards but whether it reduces existential risk — the risk of events that would permanently and drastically curtail humanity’s potential.

In The Precipice, I estimated the probability of existential catastrophe from nuclear weapons at around one percent per century. That number, while contested, represents a civilisational risk so large that it demands the most serious possible policy response. The question before us is whether nuclear deterrence, compared to its most realistic alternatives, increases or decreases that risk.

“If we are serious about protecting humanity’s long-run potential, we must be serious about all the ways in which it could be catastrophically curtailed — including the risk that a world without deterrence is a world more hospitable to nuclear first strikes by revisionist powers.” — Ord, paraphrased from The Precipice, 2020
i. the astronomical value of humanity’s future potential

Astronomical value — long-run human potential; small reductions in existential risk justify large present costs; the longtermist calculus favours risk reductionThe number of potential future humans, across a civilisation that might endure for millions of years, is so large that even small reductions in existential risk have enormous expected value. If deterrence reduces the probability of large-scale nuclear war by even one percent of one percent per century, the expected benefit in terms of future lives saved and futures preserved is enormous — far larger than any present cost that deterrence imposes. The longtermist calculus does not automatically vindicate deterrence; it demands a rigorous comparison of the risk profiles of deterrence and its alternatives. But it sets the stakes correctly: small differences in existential risk probability matter enormously.

ii. deterrence and the empirical record of great-power war

Empirical record — long peace since 1945; nuclear deterrence plausibly contributes; counterfactual without deterrence is worseThe empirical record of great-power conflict since 1945 is striking: there has been no direct military confrontation between the major nuclear powers, despite the existence of significant political and military tensions throughout the Cold War and after. Before 1945, great-power wars occurred with regularity and escalating destructiveness. The most plausible explanation for this extended peace — the “Long Peace” as John Gaddis termed it — is that nuclear deterrence has made the costs of great-power conflict credibly catastrophic for both sides. This is not proof that deterrence works; it is evidence that the counterfactual world without deterrence would plausibly have been more dangerous.

iii. the risk profile of unilateral disarmament

Disarmament risk — unilateral disarmament invites coercion or attack; multilateral disarmament requires verification conditions not currently met; the realistic alternative to deterrence is worseThe relevant comparison is not between deterrence and a disarmed world in which all nuclear powers have verifiably eliminated their arsenals; that world is not currently available. The relevant comparison is between deterrence and unilateral disarmament. In a world where some states maintain nuclear arsenals, unilateral disarmament by others invites coercion, blackmail, and potentially nuclear aggression. The existential risk posed by this scenario — the subjugation or annihilation of disarming states by nuclear-armed revisionist powers — plausibly exceeds the existential risk of maintaining deterrence. The longtermist analysis favours deterrence until verifiable multilateral disarmament becomes achievable.

iv. the distinction between deterrence and first-use

Deterrence vs. first-use — retaliatory deterrence has a different risk profile from first-strike capability; longtermism favours the former as existential risk reductionLongtermism does not vindicate all nuclear strategies equally. The existential risk reduction argument applies most clearly to retaliatory deterrence — the maintenance of a second-strike capability credible enough to deter first use by others. First-strike capability, by contrast, may increase existential risk by making pre-emptive nuclear attack more tempting for adversaries who fear being disarmed. The longtermist case is for retaliatory deterrence specifically: maintaining the credible threat of catastrophic retaliation to reduce the expected probability of anyone initiating nuclear exchange. This is a morally and strategically more defensible position than blanket nuclear maximalism.

My argument is fundamentally about decision theory under conditions of deep uncertainty. The Opposition has presented the nuclear deterrence debate as if it were a choice between two clean options: a world with deterrence and a world without it. But this framing ignores the decision-theoretic complexity of the choice. The relevant comparison is between the risk profiles of deterrence and its available alternatives, assessed under genuine uncertainty about aggressor intentions, capabilities, and rationality.

Under such conditions, standard expected-value maximisation is an unreliable guide, because the probability assignments it requires are themselves deeply uncertain. The appropriate decision rule for choices involving catastrophic, potentially irreversible outcomes under deep uncertainty is a maximin-type rule: choose the strategy that minimises the worst credible outcome. And the worst credible outcome of unilateral disarmament is worse than the worst credible outcome of deterrence.

The decision-theoretic structure of the deterrence choice.

Strategy A (Maintain deterrence): Best case — deterrence succeeds, no war; Worst case — deterrence fails, nuclear exchange.
Strategy B (Unilateral disarmament): Best case — adversary responds with peaceful engagement; Worst case — adversary exploits vulnerability, coercion or nuclear blackmail, possible subjugation or attack.

Under deep uncertainty about which scenario will obtain, the maximin rule selects A: even though A’s worst case is catastrophic, it is constrained by the adversary’s own vulnerability to retaliation. B’s worst case involves no such constraint and may include annihilation without the possibility of meaningful resistance. The maximin analysis favours deterrence.
i. the inadequacy of expected-value maximisation under deep uncertainty

Expected value fails under deep uncertainty — probability assignments are themselves uncertain; maximin is the appropriate rule for catastrophic risksExpected-value maximisation requires assigning probabilities to outcomes. But in the context of nuclear deterrence, the probabilities are themselves deeply uncertain: we do not know with any precision how likely a nuclear attack is under deterrence, or how likely coercion or attack is under unilateral disarmament. When probability assignments are themselves uncertain, expected-value maximisation is unreliable: the choice of strategy depends sensitively on the probabilities assumed, and those probabilities are not objectively available. The appropriate response to this situation is not to pretend that we have reliable probabilities but to adopt a decision rule that is robust to variation in the probability assumptions. Maximin — or more precisely, a leximin approach that prioritises avoiding the worst credible outcomes — is the appropriate rule.

ii. the worst credible outcome of unilateral disarmament

Worst case of disarmament — subjugation or annihilation without capacity for resistance; worse than worst case of deterrence which is constrained by mutual vulnerabilityThe worst credible outcome of unilateral disarmament is subjugation or annihilation by a nuclear-armed adversary in the absence of any retaliatory deterrent. This outcome involves the elimination of political freedom, cultural self-determination, and potentially the physical destruction of millions of people, without any corresponding constraint on the adversary. The worst credible outcome of maintaining deterrence is a nuclear exchange in which both sides suffer catastrophically. The deterrence worst case is not better in absolute terms; both are catastrophic. But the deterrence worst case is at least constrained by the adversary’s vulnerability to retaliation, which gives them an incentive to avoid it. The disarmament worst case has no such constraint; the adversary faces no cost for exploitation.

iii. irreversibility and the asymmetry of catastrophes

Irreversibility — subjugation may be permanent; deterrence failure leaves both sides; asymmetric irreversibility favours maximin rule applied to deterrenceThe worst outcomes of the two strategies differ in their reversibility. A nuclear exchange under failed deterrence, while catastrophic, leaves both parties in a position from which recovery — however slow and painful — may be possible. A successful coercive takeover following unilateral disarmament may be genuinely irreversible: the political and cultural identity of the subjugated community may be permanently eliminated. From the perspective of a maximin-type rule sensitive to irreversibility, this asymmetry favours deterrence: the worst case of deterrence failure is reversible in a sense that the worst case of disarmament exploitation is not.

iv. deep uncertainty about adversary rationality

Adversary rationality uncertain — deterrence works if adversary is rational; disarmament is bad even if adversary is irrational; deterrence dominates under uncertainty about adversary typeDeterrence requires that adversaries be sufficiently rational to respond to the threatened costs of retaliation. The Opposition may object that irrational adversaries will not be deterred. But this objection, while partially valid, actually supports the maximin case for deterrence: deterrence does relatively well if the adversary is rational and no worse than disarmament if the adversary is irrational (since an irrational adversary who ignores deterrence would also exploit disarmament). Deterrence therefore weakly dominates unilateral disarmament under uncertainty about adversary rationality: it is better when the adversary is rational and no worse when they are not. This dominance argument does not require precise probability assignments and is therefore robust to the uncertainty about adversary rationality.

Mr Speaker,

I want to address the Opposition from within the just war tradition — the tradition to which most of the Opposition themselves appeal. I am not a utilitarian defender of deterrence. I take seriously the deontological constraints on the conduct of war, including the principle of non-combatant immunity. But I argue that even within the just war framework, there exists a supreme emergency exception that applies to nuclear deterrence, and that this exception provides the moral foundation for a conditional deterrent threat.

In Just and Unjust Wars, I argued that Winston Churchill’s decision to bomb German cities in 1940 — targeting civilian populations — was justified as a supreme emergency, because Britain faced an imminent, existential threat from Nazi Germany whose victory would have constituted a moral catastrophe without parallel. I hold the same analysis applies to nuclear deterrence against a totalitarian nuclear power.

The supreme emergency exception and nuclear deterrence.

Conditions for supreme emergency: (1) The threat is imminent and credible. (2) The threatened harm is of a morally catastrophic character: not merely military defeat but the destruction of the political community and the values it embodies. (3) No other means of defence is available or adequate.

Application to nuclear deterrence: A state facing a nuclear-armed totalitarian adversary that is both able and potentially willing to strike first meets condition (1); the destruction of a free political community by totalitarian subjugation or nuclear annihilation meets condition (2); in a world where unilateral disarmament leaves the state defenceless against nuclear coercion, nuclear deterrence may meet condition (3). The deterrent threat inherits the moral justification of the supreme emergency exception.
i. the supreme emergency exception within just war theory

Supreme emergency exception — just war framework permits targeting civilians in extremis; genuinely exceptional, not a general licence; McMahan misapplies the traditionThe principle of non-combatant immunity is not absolute in the just war tradition as I understand it. It admits of an exception when the survival of the political community is genuinely at stake and no other means of defence is adequate. This exception is genuinely exceptional: it does not apply to ordinary military conflict or to the pursuit of political advantage. It applies only when the alternative to targeting non-combatants is the destruction of the entire political community and the values it embodies. McMahan’s objection that deterrence violates non-combatant immunity is correct as a general matter; but it ignores the exception that the just war tradition itself provides for genuine supremacy of emergency.

ii. deterrence as the minimum application of the exception

Minimum application — deterrence uses the supreme emergency exception in its most minimal form; threat rather than act; better than Churchill’s actual bombingNuclear deterrence applies the supreme emergency exception in its most minimal form: as a threat rather than an act. Churchill actually bombed German cities; the deterrent state only threatens to do so, and the threat is conditional on a first strike that the deterrent is designed to prevent. The gap between the act and the conditional threat is morally significant. The moral cost of deterrence — maintaining a conditional threat that one hopes never to execute — is far smaller than the moral cost of the supreme emergency acts that the just war tradition has already recognised as justified. If the tradition endorses Churchill’s actual bombing in 1940, it should be even more willing to endorse the conditional threat of retaliation that constitutes deterrence.

iii. the morality of the political community and its survival

Community survival — political communities embody shared values and ways of life that have independent moral worth; their destruction is a special kind of harm that can justify extreme measuresI want to resist the Opposition’s tendency to treat the deterrence debate as purely a question about individual rights and individual welfare. Political communities — their institutions, their cultures, their ways of life — have independent moral worth that is not reducible to the welfare of their individual members. The destruction of a free political community is not merely a harm to the people who currently inhabit it; it is the permanent elimination of something that has value in itself. This communitarian dimension of the moral analysis gives the supreme emergency exception a deeper grounding than simple aggregation of individual interests: we are defending not merely lives but a form of life.

iv. the moral residue and the politics of limits

Moral residue — deterrence carries moral costs even when justified; acknowledgement of the costs is part of the justification; not a clean slateI do not claim that nuclear deterrence is morally clean. It is not. The supreme emergency exception, where it applies, justifies an act or threat that remains morally tainted: the agent who invokes it acts rightly, but they are not free of all moral responsibility for the harm their action threatens. This moral residue is a feature, not a bug, of the analysis. The acknowledgement that deterrence carries moral costs — that it is a terrible strategy made necessary by terrible circumstances — is itself part of the justification. A state that maintains deterrence with appropriate moral seriousness, that seeks actively to achieve the conditions under which it can be abandoned, is in a different moral position from a state that embraces deterrence as a permanent and unproblematic policy.

My argument concerns the rationality of deterrence strategies, specifically the claim that it is irrational to maintain a commitment to retaliate when, if deterrence fails, executing the retaliation can serve no further deterrent purpose. David Lewis has argued that the moment a nuclear first strike has occurred, the rational agent has no reason to retaliate: retaliation after the fact kills people without preventing the original attack. This argument, if correct, establishes that nuclear deterrence is a bluff, and therefore that it cannot deter a sophisticated adversary who understands rational choice theory.

I reject this argument. My theory of constrained maximisation, developed in Morals by Agreement, shows that rational agents should adopt dispositions — not merely act-by-act policies — that make them credible long-run cooperators and credible long-run retaliators. An agent who reasons dispositionally, not only act-by-act, has compelling reasons to adopt and maintain genuine retaliatory commitments, because the disposition to retaliate is more valuable than the disposition to bluff.

Constrained maximisation and deterrence.

Standard rational choice: evaluate each decision at the moment it is made, maximising expected value from that decision point. On this standard, retaliation after a first strike yields negative expected value (it kills people without reversing the strike), so the rational agent should not retaliate.

Constrained maximisation: evaluate dispositions rather than individual decisions. A state that genuinely adopts the disposition to retaliate — not merely the act-by-act policy — achieves better long-run outcomes than one that adopts the disposition to bluff. The disposition to retaliate is credible in a way that the disposition to bluff is not; and credibility is the operative variable for the deterrent effect. Constrained maximisation therefore vindicates the adoption of the genuine retaliatory commitment as the rational long-run strategy.
i. the dispositional argument for genuine commitment

Dispositional rationality — rational agents should reason about dispositions not individual decisions; genuine retaliatory commitment dominates bluffing in the long runA state that merely bluffs — that publicly commits to retaliation but privately intends not to retaliate if the deterrence fails — is in a strategically inferior position to one that genuinely adopts the retaliatory commitment. The bluffing state’s deterrent effect depends on the adversary’s inability to detect the bluff; a sophisticated adversary who understands rational choice theory can calculate that the bluffing state will not retaliate, and will therefore not be deterred. The genuinely committed state’s deterrent effect does not depend on concealing an internal disposition; it depends on the credible commitment itself. Dispositional rationality therefore favours genuine commitment over bluffing.

I want to make a mathematical argument that I believe is decisive. Nuclear deterrence, as a long-run strategy, is not a policy for preventing nuclear war; it is a policy for delaying nuclear war while making its eventual occurrence mathematically near-certain. Any system whose survival depends on the non-occurrence of a low-probability event will, given sufficient time, produce that event. The only question is when.

I have spent many years arguing for international control of nuclear weapons and for the abolition of war as a mechanism of dispute resolution. I remain convinced that the only rational long-run policy with respect to nuclear weapons is their abolition through enforceable international agreement. But in the absence of such agreement, the question before us is whether deterrence is a stable long-run equilibrium or an unstable interim strategy that guarantees its own eventual failure. The mathematics are clear: deterrence guarantees failure.

The mathematical argument for inevitable deterrence failure.

Let p = annual probability of nuclear exchange under deterrence (conservative estimates: 0.5%–2%, based on historical near-miss analysis and strategic accident studies).

P(at least one exchange in N years) = 1 − (1 − p)N

For p = 0.5% (optimistic): P(exchange in 50 years) = 22%; P(exchange in 100 years) = 39%; P(exchange in 200 years) = 63%.
For p = 1%: P(exchange in 50 years) = 39%; P(exchange in 100 years) = 63%; P(exchange in 200 years) = 86%.
For p = 2%: P(exchange in 50 years) = 64%; P(exchange in 100 years) = 87%; P(exchange in 200 years) = 98%.

These are not catastrophic probabilities in any single year; they are mathematically near-certain outcomes over the civilisational timespan across which a deterrence strategy must perform.
i. the compound probability argument

Compound probability — small annual failure probability compounds to near-certainty over time; deterrence guarantees eventual failureThe Proposition presents deterrence as a risk management strategy. But risk management strategies are evaluated over their full operating lifetime, not only over any single year. The annual probability of deterrence failure is small; the probability of failure over a hundred years is not. A strategy that faces a 1% annual failure probability faces a 63% probability of failure over a century. If the costs of failure are civilisation-ending — as Glover has shown — then a 63% probability of civilisation-ending catastrophe over a century is not a risk management success; it is a delayed catastrophe. The deterrence strategy does not eliminate nuclear risk; it temporally redistributes it while compounding it over time.

ii. the instability of the deterrence equilibrium

Unstable equilibrium — deterrence requires indefinite maintenance under changing conditions; new entrants, technology, leadership changes all increase failure probability over timeThe deterrence equilibrium is not stable over long run. It requires the indefinite maintenance of several conditions simultaneously: that all nuclear-armed states have rational leaderships; that command and control systems remain secure against accident and cyber-attack; that no new nuclear-armed states acquire the capability and use it; and that no technological development changes the strategic calculus in a destabilising direction. As time passes, the probability that all these conditions are simultaneously maintained decreases. New nuclear-armed states enter the game; leadership quality deteriorates; technology changes; near-misses occur and are sometimes not avoided. The stability assumption that the Proposition’s risk calculations require is not maintained over civilisational timescales.

iii. the only rational response is multilateral disarmament

Disarmament as rational policy — deterrence is not a stable equilibrium; multilateral verifiable disarmament is the only policy with a positive long-run expected value; institutional frameworks are the solutionThe Proposition has argued that multilateral disarmament is not currently available. I accept this as a short-run fact. But it is a policy goal, not a permanent impossibility. The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, the International Atomic Energy Agency, and the Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons represent the institutional framework within which verifiable multilateral disarmament can be pursued. The question is whether the effort to achieve multilateral disarmament is a better use of diplomatic and political capital than the maintenance of deterrence. Given that deterrence guarantees eventual failure, and that failure means civilisational catastrophe, the rational response is to invest maximum effort in the institutional mechanisms for multilateral disarmament rather than to accept deterrence as a permanent equilibrium.

iv. gauthier’s precommitment argument and the time horizon problem

Time horizon problem — Gauthier’s precommitment argument works for finite strategic games; deterrence operates over an infinite time horizon; precommitment rationality does not transferGauthier argues that precommitment to a retaliatory strategy is rational because it achieves better long-run outcomes than bluffing. This argument assumes a finite strategic game with a well-defined endpoint. But nuclear deterrence operates over an indefinite time horizon: there is no planned termination point. In an infinite time horizon game, the analysis changes: the cumulative probability of failure grows without bound, and the precommitment to retaliation does not improve the long-run expected outcome because the long-run expected outcome of the strategy is eventual catastrophe. Gauthier’s argument is valid for finite strategic games; it fails for the infinite-horizon game of nuclear deterrence.

I close the Opposition’s case with what I take to be the decisive decision-theoretic argument: nuclear deterrence, as a strategy, is either rational and morally impermissible, or morally impermissible and irrational. There is no combination of rational and permissible available to the Proposition, and I shall prove this through an analysis of the decision facing the retaliating state at the moment a first strike has been executed.

My argument is this: at the moment a nuclear first strike has occurred, executing a retaliatory second strike has zero rational utility. It cannot reverse or prevent the first strike that has already occurred. It kills millions of people who bear no responsibility for the original attack. It compounds the catastrophe without reducing it. A rational agent at the execution point has no reason to retaliate. But this means that nuclear deterrence, if its threat of retaliation is a bluff, provides no deterrent effect against a rational adversary who can calculate that the threat is empty. And if the threat is genuine — if the retaliating state really would execute the second strike — then the strategy is irrational and morally monstrous.

The decision-theoretic structure at the execution point.

Scenario: A nuclear first strike has occurred. The retaliating state must decide whether to execute its retaliatory second strike.

Option A (Execute retaliation): Costs: millions of non-combatant deaths in the target country; escalation risk; possibly triggering further exchanges. Benefits: demonstrating commitment credibility for future deterrence (Gauthier’s argument); revenge (not a rational benefit).

Option B (Do not retaliate): Costs: loss of credibility for future deterrence. Benefits: millions of lives saved; no escalation; possible negotiated settlement.

At the execution point, Option B dominates in direct welfare terms. The only argument for Option A is Gauthier’s credibility/reputation argument. I shall show that this argument fails at the execution point for the reasons Russell has identified regarding infinite-horizon games.
i. execution-point rationality: second strikes have zero direct utility

Zero direct utility — second strike cannot prevent the first; kills innocents without benefit; the only rational choice at execution point is not to retaliateAt the execution point, after a first strike has been absorbed, the retaliating state faces a simple question: what do we gain by killing several million innocent civilians in the adversary country? The answer is: nothing. The first strike has already occurred. The people who will die in the second strike played no role in ordering it; they are mostly children, the elderly, workers — precisely the non-combatants that both sides of this debate agree have done nothing to deserve death. The second strike does not undo the first; it does not reduce the casualties of the first; it does not prevent further attacks (which have already been launched or which can be launched regardless). The second strike compounds the catastrophe. At the execution point, the rational choice is not to retaliate.

ii. the bluff or genuine dilemma

Bluff or genuine — if deterrence is a bluff, a rational adversary is not deterred; if genuine, the retaliating state commits in advance to an irrational and immoral act; no combination worksThe Proposition must choose between two positions, both fatal. If the deterrent threat is a bluff — if the retaliating state does not genuinely intend to execute the second strike — then a rational adversary who understands decision theory can calculate this. The deterrent effect depends on the adversary’s inability to detect the bluff, which cannot be guaranteed and which becomes less reliable as strategic sophistication increases. A bluff that a rational adversary can detect provides no deterrent effect. Alternatively, if the deterrent threat is genuine — if the state really would execute the second strike — then it has committed in advance to performing an irrational and morally monstrous act. The Proposition cannot have it both ways: either the threat is empty and deterrence fails, or the threat is genuine and morally monstrous.

iii. against gauthier: reputation does not rescue execution-point irrationality

Against Gauthier — at execution point after civilisational catastrophe, reputation for future deterrence has zero value; the game is over; reputational argument failsGauthier argues that executing the second strike has value in maintaining the reputation for retaliatory commitment in future deterrent relationships. But this argument assumes that future deterrent relationships will obtain after the first exchange. If the first exchange is of the civilisation-ending character that Glover and Russell have described, there may be no future deterrent relationships to maintain a reputation for. And if there are surviving parties, the question of whether they respect the retaliating state’s future deterrent commitments is of negligible importance compared to the question of whether the civilisation has survived the first exchange. The reputational value of the second strike is zero in the cases that matter most — precisely the cases where the first strike has been catastrophic enough to trigger the second.

iv. the rational conclusion: deterrence as theatre, not strategy

Theatre not strategy — deterrence is rational only as a bluff never called; a bluff a rational adversary can detect is not a deterrent; the strategy only “works” while the adversary remains irrationally credulousThe logic of my argument leads to a conclusion that the Proposition cannot comfortably accept: nuclear deterrence is rational as a strategy only if the adversary is not fully rational — only if they cannot calculate that the retaliating state would not in fact retaliate at the execution point. Deterrence is theatre: it is a performance designed to create a credibility impression in an adversary who is not sophisticated enough to see through it. Against a fully rational adversary, it provides no deterrent effect. And the security of a strategy that depends on adversary irrationality is a very fragile foundation for a policy of planned mass murder. This is not a marginal defect; it is the central defect of nuclear deterrence as a rational strategy.
