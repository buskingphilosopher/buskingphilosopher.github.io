“This House Believes That Fleurbaey's Conditional Equality, Built on the No-Envy/Egalitarian-Equivalence Framework, Successfully Resolves the Compensation-Responsibility Dilemma”
Proposition (Conditional Equality & Its Foundations) • Fleurbaey · Pazner-Schmeidler · Roemer · Van Parijs  —  Opposition • Dworkin · Cohen · Arneson · Sen
Conditional EqualityMarc Fleurbaey — Fairness, Responsibility, and Welfare (2008); "Equal Opportunity or Equal Social Outcome?" (1995)
The compensation-responsibility dilemma is this: a theory of justice should compensate people for disadvantages arising from circumstances beyond their control, while holding them responsible for outcomes flowing from their own choices and preferences — but any allocation rule based on people's actual preferences (including no-envy itself) inevitably lets responsibility-characteristics contaminate the compensation, and any allocation rule that ignores preferences entirely cannot respect responsibility either. Conditional equality resolves this by computing each person's entitlement using their actual circumstances but a single, fixed reference preference profile for the responsibility-characteristics — decoupling compensation from the very preferences for which people are held responsible.

No-Envy & Egalitarian Equivalence: The Formal FoundationsElisha Pazner & David Schmeidler — "Egalitarian Equivalent Allocations" (1978); building on Foley (1967) and Varian (1974)
The no-envy criterion — no agent prefers another's bundle to their own — is the formal core of resource egalitarianism, but in production economies with heterogeneous skills, no-envy allocations frequently fail to exist, or exist only via the "slavery of the talented." Egalitarian-equivalent allocations solve the existence problem: an allocation is egalitarian-equivalent if there is some reference bundle such that every agent is indifferent, by their own preferences, between their actual bundle and that reference bundle. This concept exists far more generally than no-envy, is Pareto-compatible, and is precisely the formal object that Fleurbaey's conditional equality refines for the responsibility-sensitive case.

Types, Tranches, and ImplementabilityJohn Roemer — Equality of Opportunity (1998)
Conditional equality and my own responsibility-sensitive egalitarianism are complementary formalizations of the same underlying ethical idea, addressing it from different ends. I partition the population into "types" sharing the same circumstances, and within each type, individuals are ranked by "effort tranches" — their percentile in the effort distribution of their own type. Equalizing outcomes across types at each tranche compensates for circumstance while respecting that effort is measured relative to what was available to people like you, not in absolute terms. This is empirically implementable with real data, and shares with conditional equality the crucial move of evaluating people's effort/preference position relative to a reference distribution rather than in raw, preference-contaminated absolute terms.

From Conditional Equality to Real FreedomPhilippe Van Parijs — Real Freedom for All (1995)
The conditional-equality/egalitarian-equivalence framework is not merely a technical curiosity for theorists of fair division; it generalizes directly into policy. Leximin over endowments of "real freedom" — the capacity to pursue whatever life one might want, evaluated independently of which life one actually chooses to pursue — is structurally a reference-bundle approach: the policy-relevant comparison is what each person could do with their resources under a neutral reference specification of preferences, not what they actually choose to do. An unconditional basic income, set at the sustainable maximum, is the practical instrument that a conditional-equality-style framework recommends once translated into feasible policy instruments.

Chair,

I want to begin by stating the dilemma in its sharpest form, because I think the solution I am going to propose is best understood as a direct response to a problem that every resourcist theory of justice runs into — including, I will argue, Professor Dworkin's own theory, in a form he did not fully resolve.

i. the dilemma stated formally

The dilemma — a theory of distributive justice must (a) compensate for disadvantageous circumstances (unchosen) and (b) respect responsibility for preferences/effort (chosen). Any allocation rule whose output depends on agents' actual preferences — including the no-envy criterion itself — makes the resources a person receives depend partly on their preferences over leisure, consumption, risk, etc. — exactly the responsibility-characteristics that (b) says should not generate entitlements to extra compensation. But a rule that ignores preferences entirely cannot evaluate whether circumstances have actually disadvantaged someone, since "disadvantage" is itself preference-relative (a circumstance is disadvantageous only relative to what one would want to do)Take any allocation rule that satisfies the most basic test of resource egalitarianism — no agent envies another's bundle, evaluated by the agent's own preferences. This rule's output, for any given agent, depends on that agent's preferences: whether agent i envies agent j's bundle depends on how i ranks j's bundle against i's own, which depends on i's preferences over the goods in question — consumption, leisure, risk, and so on. But preferences over leisure and consumption are exactly the kind of thing a responsibility-sensitive theory says should not, by themselves, generate claims to extra compensation: if I simply prefer more leisure and less income than you, that is my choice, not a disadvantage requiring redress. Yet the no-envy criterion, applied directly, lets precisely these preferences shape what counts as an envy-free — hence "fair" — allocation for me. Conversely, if we strip preferences out of the evaluation entirely and use some preference-independent metric of resources, we lose the ability to say whether a given circumstance — a disability, say — is actually disadvantageous, since disadvantage is only meaningful relative to what the person would want to do with the resources in question. This is the dilemma: preference-dependence contaminates compensation with responsibility-characteristics; preference-independence cannot identify disadvantage at all.

ii. the conditional equality construction

Conditional equality — partition each person's characteristics into circumstances C (unchosen: talent, health, social background) and responsibility characteristics R (preferences over leisure/consumption, ambition, etc.). For a fixed reference profile R* (the same for everyone — e.g., a specified reference indifference map over leisure and consumption), compute, for each person using their ACTUAL C_i but the REFERENCE R*, what egalitarian-equivalent allocation they would receive. Give each person the allocation determined this way. Because R* is the same for everyone, no one's allocation depends on their own actual preferences — eliminating preference-contamination — while C_i still varies person to person, so circumstantial differences are still compensatedThe construction is as follows. Partition each person's relevant characteristics into circumstances C — talent, health, social and family background, anything we judge the person not responsible for — and responsibility characteristics R — preferences over leisure and consumption, ambition, the choices the person makes given their circumstances. Now fix a single reference profile R* for the responsibility characteristics — the same R* for every person in the population, chosen as a normatively defensible reference point (I will return to how it is chosen). For each individual i, compute the egalitarian-equivalent allocation they would be entitled to if their responsibility characteristics were R* — using their actual circumstances C_i, but the reference R* in place of their actual R_i. Give person i that allocation. Notice what has happened: because R* is identical for everyone, no person's allocation depends on their own actual preferences at all — the preference-contamination problem is eliminated by construction. But C_i still varies from person to person, so two people with identical R* but different circumstances receive different, circumstance-compensating allocations. The dilemma's two horns are addressed by two different variables in the construction, rather than being forced into a single preference-dependent or preference-independent evaluation.

iii. dissolving the slavery of the talented

Slavery of the talented dissolved — the "slavery of the talented" objection arises when an allocation rule's demands on a talented person depend on the ACTUAL preferences of the untalented (e.g., an untalented person's strong preference for income over leisure generates a no-envy constraint that the talented person's bundle must not be enviable given THAT preference, forcing the talented person toward higher output/less leisure). Under conditional equality, the allocation any person receives — talented or not — is computed using the SAME reference preferences R* for everyone. The talented person's required transfer depends on their circumstance (talent, C_i) evaluated against R*, not on the untalented person's actual revealed preference for income over leisure. No one's entitlement is hostage to anyone else's actual choices about how hard to workThe "slavery of the talented" objection — pressed against Dworkin's hypothetical-insurance construction and against pure no-envy alike — arises because, in a production economy, what counts as an envy-free or equal allocation for the talented depends on the actual preferences of the untalented: if the untalented strongly prefer income to leisure, the no-envy constraint may require the talented person's (income, leisure) bundle to be one the untalented would not envy given that strong preference for income — which can mean the talented person must produce a great deal, at the cost of their own leisure, regardless of their own preferences. Under conditional equality, this cannot happen. Every person's allocation — talented or untalented — is computed using the same reference preference profile R*, applied to that person's own circumstances. The talented person's required contribution depends on their circumstance (their talent, as an element of C_i) evaluated against R*, full stop. It does not depend on what the untalented person actually, in fact, prefers regarding their own income and leisure. No one's entitlement is held hostage to anyone else's revealed preferences. The slavery-of-the-talented problem, which is fundamentally a problem of cross-contamination between one person's allocation and another person's preferences, is dissolved because the construction removes everyone's actual preferences from the determination of anyone's allocation.

I yield the floor.

1.No-envy and similar criteria evaluate an agent's bundle against others' using the agent's own preferences — preferences over leisure, consumption, etc., which are responsibility-characteristics.
2.This lets a person's preferences (e.g., for leisure over income) shape what allocation counts as "fair" for them — contaminating compensation (for circumstance) with responsibility (preferences).
3.A preference-independent metric avoids this contamination but cannot identify whether a circumstance (e.g., disability) is disadvantageous, since disadvantage is meaningful only relative to what the person would want to do with resources.
∴Resource-egalitarian rules face a genuine dilemma: preference-dependence contaminates compensation with responsibility-characteristics, while preference-independence cannot evaluate disadvantage; a solution must decouple these without falling into either horn.

1.Partition characteristics into circumstances C (compensable) and responsibility characteristics R (not compensable). Fix a single reference profile R* identical for all individuals.
2.For each person i, compute the egalitarian-equivalent allocation using actual C_i but reference R* (not actual R_i).

3.No person's entitlement is therefore hostage to another person's actual choices about work and leisure.
∴Conditional equality dissolves the slavery-of-the-talented problem at its root — the cross-contamination between individuals' allocations and others' actual preferences — by removing actual preferences from the construction entirely.

I want to take this debate back to the formal foundations, because I think the strength of Professor Fleurbaey's construction is best appreciated against the background of what the no-envy literature had already established — and what it had not.

i. no-envy and its existence problem

No-envy existence problem — the no-envy criterion (Foley 1967): allocation x is envy-free iff for all i, j, agent i does not strictly prefer x_j to x_i (by i's own preferences). In pure exchange economies with suitable conditions, envy-free and Pareto-efficient allocations exist (Varian 1974). But in PRODUCTION economies where agents have heterogeneous skills entering as inputs to production, envy-free allocations can fail to exist altogether — there may be NO allocation, efficient or not, satisfying no-envy, because a high-skill agent's (consumption, labor) bundle may be such that, for ANY feasible bundle a low-skill agent could be given, either the low-skill agent envies the high-skill agent's bundle or vice versa, with no feasible adjustment eliminating bothThe no-envy criterion, due to Foley, requires that for any two agents i and j, agent i does not strictly prefer agent j's bundle to their own, evaluated by i's own preferences. In pure exchange economies — where the total endowment is fixed and the only question is how to divide it — Varian showed that envy-free and Pareto-efficient allocations exist under standard conditions. This was an important early result. But once we move to production economies — where some agents' skills are themselves inputs to what gets produced — the picture changes substantially. With heterogeneous skills, it is a standard result that envy-free allocations can fail to exist at all: there may be no feasible allocation, efficient or otherwise, such that no agent envies any other. A high-skill agent's bundle of (consumption, labor supplied) may be such that any bundle a low-skill agent could feasibly be given leaves either the low-skill agent envying the high-skill agent, or the reverse, with no adjustment eliminating both directions of envy simultaneously. This is not a minor technical wrinkle; it means the most natural formalization of "fairness as no-envy" can simply have no solution in exactly the economies — with differential talents — that matter most for theories of justice.

ii. egalitarian equivalence as the generalization

Egalitarian equivalence — an allocation x is egalitarian-equivalent (Pazner-Schmeidler 1978) iff there exists a single reference bundle z such that, for every agent i, agent i is indifferent (by i's own preferences) between x_i and z. Crucially, z need not itself be a feasible bundle for everyone simultaneously (e.g., z could be "the per-capita endowment" or some other reference point) — only each agent's actual bundle needs to be EQUIVALENT, from that agent's own perspective, to the common reference z. Egalitarian-equivalent and Pareto-efficient allocations exist under much weaker conditions than envy-free + efficient allocations, including in many production economies where envy-free allocations fail to existEgalitarian equivalence, which David Schmeidler and I introduced, generalizes the underlying idea in a way that restores existence. An allocation x is egalitarian-equivalent if there is some single reference bundle z — not necessarily a bundle that could itself be given to everyone simultaneously, but a fixed point of reference — such that every agent i is indifferent, by their own preferences, between their actual bundle x_i and z. The key relaxation is this: no-envy compares agents' bundles pairwise to each other; egalitarian equivalence compares each agent's bundle to a common external reference point, with the comparison made entirely within each agent's own preferences. This relaxation is enough to restore existence under much weaker conditions, including in a wide range of production economies where envy-free allocations do not exist at all. Every agent ends up, from their own point of view, equally well off as they would be with the reference bundle z — which is a natural and defensible notion of equality — without requiring the pairwise comparisons that generate non-existence.

iii. why this is the right formal object for conditional equality

Why egalitarian equivalence fits conditional equality — Fleurbaey's conditional equality computes, for each person, "the egalitarian-equivalent allocation given C_i and R*" — this is precisely an egalitarian-equivalence construction where the reference bundle z is determined by applying R* to the reference scenario. Because egalitarian equivalence (unlike no-envy) does not require pairwise comparison of actual bundles across agents, and because Fleurbaey's construction already removes actual inter-agent preference comparison by fixing R*, the two ideas are not just compatible but are, in a precise sense, the natural completion of each other: egalitarian equivalence supplies the existence-guaranteeing formal apparatus that a responsibility-sensitive theory needs once it has, for independent ethical reasons (Fleurbaey's dilemma), already decided to evaluate people against a common reference rather than against each otherThis is why egalitarian equivalence is not merely compatible with conditional equality but is, in a precise sense, the formal object conditional equality was looking for. Conditional equality computes, for each person, the allocation they are entitled to given their actual circumstances C_i and the reference responsibility-characteristics R* — which is exactly an egalitarian-equivalence construction, where the reference bundle z is whatever bundle the reference profile R* would deem appropriate. Recall that egalitarian equivalence's key formal advantage over no-envy is that it does not require comparing agents' actual bundles to each other — only to a common reference. Recall, separately, that Fleurbaey's construction, for independent ethical reasons concerning responsibility, already needed to avoid evaluating any person's allocation by reference to another person's actual preferences. These are, formally, the same requirement, arrived at from two different directions — one technical (existence), one ethical (responsibility-sensitivity). Conditional equality is best understood not as an ad hoc addition to egalitarian equivalence, but as the responsibility-sensitive specification of what the reference bundle z should be: the bundle determined by applying R* to each person's actual circumstances.

We yield the floor.

Argument I — No-envy allocations can fail to exist in production economies with heterogeneous skills — precisely the economies that matter for theories of justice
1.No-envy requires, for all i,j, that i not strictly prefer j's bundle to their own, by i's preferences. In pure exchange economies, envy-free + efficient allocations exist under standard conditions (Varian).
2.In production economies with heterogeneous skills, no feasible allocation may satisfy no-envy at all — a high-skill agent's bundle may generate envy from or toward low-skill agents under any feasible redistribution.

∴Pure no-envy is formally inadequate as a foundation for a general theory of fair allocation, because it can fail to have any solution in the production economies that are the central case of interest.

3.Conditional equality is therefore not an ad hoc addition to the egalitarian-equivalence framework but its natural responsibility-sensitive completion.
∴Conditional equality and egalitarian equivalence are not merely compatible but mutually completing: the formal apparatus that solves the existence problem is exactly the apparatus the ethical dilemma independently requires.

I want to approach this from the direction of implementation, because I think one of the strongest arguments for the conditional-equality family of solutions is that a structurally similar idea has already been formalized in a way that can be, and has been, applied to real data — and the structural similarity, I will argue, is not superficial.

i. types and tranches

Types and tranches — partition the population into "types": groups of individuals sharing the same circumstances (family background, race, place of birth, etc. — the variables a policy-maker judges to be circumstances). Within each type, rank individuals by their percentile ("tranche") in the distribution of some outcome variable (e.g., effort, or an outcome partly reflecting effort) WITHIN their own type — not in absolute terms across the whole population. The responsibility-respecting comparison is then: how does this person's outcome compare to others at the SAME tranche of DIFFERENT types? Equalizing across types at each tranche compensates for circumstance (type) while holding the within-type relative effort position (tranche) fixed as the dimension of responsibilityMy approach partitions the population into types: groups of individuals who share the same circumstances — the variables a policymaker has judged, for the purposes of the policy in question, to be matters of unchosen circumstance rather than individual responsibility: family background, parental education, place of birth, and so on. Within each type, individuals are ranked by their percentile — their tranche — in the distribution of some effort-related variable, measured relative to others of their own type, not relative to the population as a whole. The responsibility-respecting comparison then asks: how does a person's outcome compare to the outcomes of people at the same tranche but in different types? If a person at the 70th percentile of effort within their type has a worse outcome than a person at the 70th percentile of effort within a more advantaged type, this is attributed to circumstance (type), not to a difference in effort — because both individuals occupy the same relative position within their respective effort distributions. Equalizing outcomes across types, tranche by tranche, compensates for the circumstance (type) while treating the within-type relative effort position (tranche) as the dimension for which individuals remain responsible.

ii. the structural parallel to conditional equality

Structural parallel — both approaches face the same underlying problem (preferences/effort are partly shaped by circumstance, so raw effort cannot be the basis for differential treatment, but SOME notion of relative effort/responsibility must be preserved) and solve it the same way: by evaluating each person's responsibility-characteristic RELATIVE TO A REFERENCE that holds circumstance fixed. Fleurbaey's R* is a reference preference profile held constant ACROSS circumstances; my tranche is a reference position WITHIN a circumstance-defined type. In both cases, the move is: don't compare raw/absolute preferences or efforts across people with different circumstances — compare relative position against an appropriately-specified reference, then compensate for circumstance differences in the outcome that relative position is allowed to produceThe structural parallel to conditional equality is, I think, exact, even though the two formalizations were developed somewhat independently. Both approaches confront the same underlying problem: a person's revealed preferences or effort levels are themselves partly shaped by their circumstances — someone from a disadvantaged background may have, through no fault of their own, a lower-effort disposition than someone from an advantaged background, simply because effort-dispositions are themselves influenced by upbringing and environment. Comparing raw effort levels across people with different circumstances therefore cannot be the basis for differential treatment without smuggling circumstance back in through the effort variable. Both approaches solve this by evaluating each person's responsibility-characteristic relative to an appropriately specified reference that holds circumstance fixed. Fleurbaey's R* is a reference preference profile held constant across all circumstances. My tranche is a reference position — a percentile — within the effort distribution of one's own circumstance-defined type. In both cases, the operative move is identical: do not compare raw preferences or efforts across people with different circumstances; compare relative position against a reference, and then let circumstance-based compensation operate on the outcome that relative position generates.

iii. empirical implementability as evidence of coherence

Implementability as evidence — the types/tranches approach has been applied to real datasets (e.g., income mobility, educational attainment data, partitioned by family background "types" and ranked by within-type percentile) to compute concrete policy-relevant measures of "how much of observed inequality is attributable to circumstance vs. responsibility." That a structurally parallel construction to conditional equality can be operationalized this way, producing determinate numerical answers from real data, is evidence that the underlying conceptual move (reference-relative evaluation of responsibility-characteristics) is not merely formally consistent but tractable — addressing a natural worry that conditional equality's R* might be a purely theoretical device with no operational contentOne natural worry about conditional equality is that the reference profile R* might be a purely theoretical device — mathematically well-defined but with no clear operational content, no way to actually compute what R* should be or what allocations it generates from real data. The types-and-tranches approach addresses this worry by demonstration rather than argument: it has been applied to real datasets — income, educational attainment, and mobility data, partitioned by family-background "types" and ranked by within-type percentile — to produce concrete, numerical measures of how much observed inequality is attributable to circumstance versus responsibility, under specified judgements about which variables count as which. That a structurally parallel construction can be operationalized this way, yielding determinate answers from real data rather than remaining a purely formal possibility result, is evidence that the underlying conceptual move — reference-relative evaluation of responsibility-characteristics — is tractable, not merely consistent. Whatever difficulties remain in specifying R* for conditional equality in full generality, they are difficulties of the same kind that the types-and-tranches approach has already shown can be addressed in practice.

I want to close the proposition's case by showing that the conditional-equality/egalitarian-equivalence framework is not confined to the abstract fair-division problems where it was developed — it generalizes directly into a concrete policy recommendation, and the generalization illuminates what the framework is really doing.

i. real freedom and the reference-bundle structure

Real freedom — "real freedom" is the capacity to pursue whatever conception of the good life one might have, as distinct from "formal freedom" (the mere absence of legal prohibition). Crucially, evaluating someone's real freedom does NOT require knowing or using their actual conception of the good life / actual preferences — it requires evaluating what their resource endowment would allow them to do under ANY of a range of possible life-plans, including ones they do not actually hold. This is structurally a reference-bundle evaluation: comparing what a person's endowment permits, evaluated against a neutral specification of possible uses, not against that person's actual revealed choicesReal freedom is the capacity to pursue whatever conception of the good life one might have — as opposed to formal freedom, which is merely the absence of legal prohibition on pursuing whichever conception one happens to actually hold. The crucial structural feature of real freedom, for our purposes, is this: evaluating how much real freedom someone has does not require knowing, or using, that person's actual conception of the good life. It requires evaluating what their resource endowment would permit across a range of possible life-plans — including life-plans the person does not actually hold and may never pursue. This is, I want to suggest, exactly a reference-bundle evaluation in Pazner and Schmeidler's sense: the comparison is between what a person's endowment permits, evaluated against a neutral specification of possible uses of that endowment, not against the particular use that person's actual preferences happen to select. Real freedom is egalitarian equivalence read as a policy concept rather than a fair-division-problem concept.

ii. leximin over endowments, not over achieved welfare

Leximin over endowments — the policy criterion is leximin (maximize the position of the worst-off, then the next-worst-off, etc.) applied to endowments of real freedom — i.e., to what people's resource bundles WOULD permit under the reference specification — not to people's actual achieved welfare or actual chosen outcomes. This mirrors conditional equality's structure precisely: the comparison across people is made in terms of (circumstance-determined endowment) x (reference specification of use), not in terms of (actual choice) x (actual preference) — the same separation of "what you have" from "what you actually do with it" that conditional equality builds into its R*/C_i splitThe policy criterion I defend is leximin — maximize the position of the worst-off endowment of real freedom, then the next-worst-off, and so on — applied to endowments evaluated under the reference specification, not to people's actual achieved welfare or the outcomes of their actual choices. Someone who has a generous endowment of real freedom but chooses, say, a contemplative life with low monetary income is not thereby worse-off in the sense leximin cares about; their endowment permitted other choices too. This mirrors the conditional-equality structure precisely: the interpersonal comparison is made at the level of (circumstance-determined endowment) evaluated against (a reference specification of possible uses), not at the level of (actual choice) evaluated against (actual preference). It is the same separation — what you have, versus what you actually do with it — that Fleurbaey's C_i/R* split builds in directly. Real-freedom leximin is conditional equality's separation, applied at the level of aggregate policy design rather than individual fair-division.

iii. unconditional basic income as the implementing instrument

UBI as implementation — an unconditional basic income, set at the highest sustainable level, directly implements the real-freedom-leximin criterion: it provides every person with an unconditional resource floor (addressing the worst-off endowment first, as leximin requires) that they may use according to their own actual preferences (the basic income itself does not require knowledge of, or conditionality on, actual preferences — it is allocated identically regardless of what each recipient will do with it, exactly as conditional equality's allocation does not depend on each recipient's actual R_i). The "unconditional" feature of UBI is the policy-level expression of "preference-independence of the allocation rule" that is the technical core of conditional equalityAn unconditional basic income, set at the highest level the economy can sustainably support, is the policy instrument that directly implements this criterion. It provides every person with a resource floor — addressing the worst-off endowment first, as leximin requires — that each person may then use according to their own actual preferences, for whatever conception of the good life they actually hold. Note what "unconditional" means here, formally: the basic income's amount does not depend on, and is not conditioned upon, each recipient's actual preferences, choices, or effort — it is the same allocation regardless of what each recipient will do with it. This is precisely the preference-independence-of-the-allocation-rule that is the technical core of conditional equality: just as no individual's conditional-equality allocation depends on their own actual R_i, no individual's basic income depends on their own actual choices about work, leisure, or lifestyle. "Unconditional," at the policy level, is the practical name for what "evaluated at R*, not at actual R_i" means at the level of fair-division theory. iii. adaptive preferences and the limits of any preference-based reference

Adaptive preferences — a further problem, distinct from the "still preference-space" point: people's actual preferences (and hence, on Cohen's worry, their classification under R) can adapt to severe deprivation, leading the deprived to report satisfaction with very little. Conditional equality's R*, by being FIXED and not adapting to each person's circumstances, might seem to escape this — but R* is chosen ONCE, by the theorist, and so embeds WHATEVER assumptions about "normal" preferences the theorist held, which may themselves reflect a society's adaptive norms about what different groups should expect or want. The capability approach avoids this entirely by evaluating opportunities to function directly, without ANY preference profile — actual, reference, or otherwise — mediating the evaluationThere is a further, related problem. It is well documented that people's actual preferences can adapt to conditions of severe and persistent deprivation — people who have never had access to certain goods, opportunities, or forms of participation can come to not want them, and report subjective satisfaction with very little. Conditional equality's fixed R*, applied uniformly, might seem to escape this — it does not adapt to each person's circumstances the way their actual R_i might. But R* is chosen once, by the theorist, at the outset — and in being chosen, it embeds whatever assumptions about "normal" or "reasonable" preferences the theorist held when specifying it. If those assumptions themselves reflect a society's adaptive norms about what different groups can reasonably want or expect — norms that are not innocent, and that often track exactly the inequalities a theory of justice should be scrutinizing — then R*, however fixed and uniform in its application, can encode the very distortion it was meant to neutralize, just at one remove. The capability approach avoids this problem at its root, by evaluating opportunities to function directly — without any preference profile, actual or reference, mediating the evaluation at all.
