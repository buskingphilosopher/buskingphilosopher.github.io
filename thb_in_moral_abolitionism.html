Chair,

I want to begin by stating the error theorist’s central claim as clearly as possible, because it is a claim that has been softened and qualified by too many of those who followed me. All positive, substantive moral judgements — that torture is wrong, that kindness is good, that we ought to keep our promises — are false. Not merely unverified, not merely culturally relative, not merely subjective. False. The world does not contain the properties these judgements purport to describe.

The structure of the error theorist’s claim.

Moral discourse is cognitivist in form: when we say “lying is wrong” we are making a genuine assertion, purporting to state a fact. This distinguishes error theory from non-cognitivism. The error theorist accepts the cognitivist analysis of moral language and then adds the metaphysical claim: there are no moral facts of the relevant kind. All such assertions fail, not because they are confused or meaningless, but because what they purport to describe does not exist.

The “relevant kind” of moral fact is crucial. Moral discourse does not merely purport to describe our preferences or social conventions. It purports to describe what Mackie called objective prescriptivity — facts about what we are required to do that have their requiring force independently of our desires, social agreements, or any other subjective state.
i. the argument from queerness

Argument from queerness — objective prescriptivity would be metaphysically and epistemologically unlike anything else we know; best explanation is that no such thing existsIf moral properties existed they would be deeply strange entities — quite unlike anything else in the natural world. Consider what objective prescriptivity would have to be: a property of actions or states of affairs that is both descriptive (it tells you something about the world) and intrinsically prescriptive (its mere recognition gives you a reason to act, independently of your desires). Nothing in our natural-scientific or everyday ontology resembles this. We recognise properties like redness, mass, charge — none of which is intrinsically action-guiding. The prescriptive force of ordinary facts is always mediated by our desires: the fact that the train leaves at six gives me a reason to hurry only because I want to catch it. Moral prescriptivity would have to be built into the world in a way that requires no such mediation. This is metaphysically queer.

ii. the epistemological queerness

Epistemological queerness — what faculty would detect objective moral properties? Moral intuition is culturally variable; no reliable mechanism for moral knowledge exists if properties were objectiveThe epistemological arm of the queerness argument is equally damaging. If objective moral properties existed, we would need some faculty for detecting them — a kind of moral perception or intuition that tracks objective prescriptivity. But our moral intuitions vary dramatically across cultures and historical periods. They are shaped by upbringing, social convention, and biological evolution. They do not display the reliability and convergence we would expect from a genuine truth-tracking faculty. The rationalist tradition — from Plato to Price — posited a faculty of moral reason that could perceive moral truths. But this faculty would be, as I said, utterly unlike our other cognitive faculties. It would have to detect entities of a radically different kind from those detected by perception, introspection, or scientific inference. The hypothesis that no such faculty exists and that our moral intuitions are instead explained by evolutionary, cultural, and psychological processes is far more economical.

iii. the argument from relativity

Argument from relativity — persistent, deep moral disagreement is better explained by cultural variation than by differential epistemic access to objective moral factsThe second major argument proceeds from the observable fact of moral disagreement. Moral views differ across cultures, historical periods, and individuals in ways that resist resolution. If objective moral facts existed, the best explanation of this disagreement would be that different cultures have unequal epistemic access to those facts. But this hypothesis is implausible: are we really to believe that one culture’s view of monogamy reflects access to objective moral facts while another culture’s divergent view reflects moral blindness? A far better explanation is that moral views “reflect adherence to and participation in different ways of life” and that there are no objective facts for anyone to have differential access to. The error theory is the best explanation of the pattern of persistent, intractable moral disagreement.

iv. why this grounds abolitionism

Grounds abolitionism — if all moral claims are false and the falsity is systematic and tied to the form of moral discourse, then carrying on with moral discourse is carrying on with systematic errorMy colleagues on the proposition will draw the abolitionist conclusion more forcefully than I originally did. I confess that in Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong I was too conciliatory — I suggested we could carry on with moral discourse while accepting that it is false, treating it as a kind of useful fiction. I now think this view is unstable. If the falsity of moral discourse is not incidental but systematic — tied to the very form of moral assertions, which inherently presuppose objective prescriptivity — then carrying on with moral discourse, even fictionally, means perpetuating the presupposition of something we know does not exist. The opposition will defend this perpetuation. I submit that intellectual honesty, and the practical harms that flow from moral authority claims, require us to go further.

Argument I — The metaphysical queerness argument: objective prescriptivity would be unlike anything in our natural-scientific ontology
1.Moral discourse purports to describe objective prescriptivity — properties that are intrinsically action-guiding independently of any subject’s desires or social agreements.
2.No such entity appears in our natural-scientific ontology. All ordinary properties are descriptive; their prescriptive force is mediated by the desires of subjects. Objective prescriptivity would be a radically novel kind of entity.
3.When a novel kind of entity is postulated but nothing in our best theories or experiences requires it, the most economical conclusion is that it does not exist. Objective moral properties are not required by any natural-scientific or ordinary experiential framework.
∴The postulation of objective prescriptive moral properties is metaphysically extravagant: such entities would be queer in a way that nothing else we know is, and no good reason exists to accept them; the error theory is the more parsimonious conclusion.
Argument II — The epistemological queerness argument: no reliable faculty for detecting objective moral properties exists or is credible
1.If objective moral properties existed, we would need a cognitive faculty for detecting them. This faculty would have to track objective prescriptivity — a property unlike those tracked by perception, introspection, or scientific inference.
2.Our actual moral intuitions vary dramatically across cultures, historical periods, and individuals, and are shaped by evolutionary, cultural, and psychological processes. They do not display the reliability and convergence expected of a genuine truth-tracking faculty.
3.The hypothesis that moral intuitions are explained by non-truth-tracking processes is better supported than the hypothesis that they involve a special faculty detecting queer moral properties. We should prefer the simpler explanation.
∴The epistemological arm of the queerness argument reinforces the metaphysical: no credible faculty for detecting objective moral properties exists; the cultural and evolutionary explanation of moral intuitions is far more plausible than any moral-perception hypothesis.
Argument III — The argument from relativity: persistent moral disagreement is best explained by the absence of objective moral facts
1.Moral views differ profoundly and persistently across cultures, historical periods, and individuals on fundamental questions. This disagreement is more intractable than disagreement in mathematics, natural science, or ordinary factual matters.
2.The best explanation of this pattern is that moral views “reflect adherence to and participation in different ways of life” — cultural and historical contingencies — rather than differential epistemic access to objective moral facts.
3.If objective moral facts existed and were accessible by a reliable faculty, we would expect the same convergence over time that we observe in mathematics and natural science. We do not observe it; the error theory explains why.
∴Persistent moral disagreement is better explained by the error theory (no objective moral facts, so no convergence to be expected) than by the hypothesis that one culture has access to facts that others lack; the argument from relativity supports error theory.
Argument IV — Systematic falsity grounds abolitionism: the error is built into the form of moral discourse, not merely its content
1.The error of moral discourse is not incidental but systematic: every positive moral assertion inherently presupposes objective prescriptivity, regardless of the specific content of the assertion. The error is in the form, not merely the content.
2.If the error is systematic and formal, then retaining moral discourse — even fictionally — means perpetuating the systematic presupposition of objective prescriptivity. The fiction carries the presupposition with it.
3.Intellectual honesty requires that we not perpetuate systematic presuppositions we know to be false, especially when the perpetuation has practical costs in the form of moral authority claims, dogmatism, and suppression of criticism.
∴Because the error of moral discourse is formal and systematic, retaining moral discourse even as fiction perpetuates the false presupposition; intellectual honesty and the practical costs of moral authority claims jointly support abolitionism over fictionalism.

Chair,

My position in the volume is direct: once you accept that all moral claims are false, there is one intellectually honest response, and it is abolitionism. Not fictionalism, not negotiationism, not conservationism with a philosophical disclaimer attached. Abolition. And the case for it is not merely philosophical but deeply practical.

“A moral abolitionist holds that the world would be a better place if people stopped thinking and talking in moral terms. This is not the view that we should stop caring about people or that anything goes. It is the view that the false pretence of objective moral authority should end.” — Garner, 2019
i. fictionalism promotes a culture of deception

Fictionalism as deception — the fictionalist says false things she knows to be false in order to influence others; this undermines epistemic norms and models dishonestyJoyce proposes that the error theorist can retain moral discourse by treating it as a convenient fiction — continuing to say “that is wrong” while knowing this is false, in order to preserve the practical benefits of moral motivation. But consider what this amounts to. The fictionalist asserts something she knows to be false in order to influence the behaviour of others. She uses the rhetorical force of moral authority claims — their pretended objectivity, their categorical demand — to motivate action, while privately believing there is no such authority. This is manipulation. It is the deliberate deployment of false beliefs in others to secure outcomes you prefer. Even if the outcomes are desirable, the method is corrosive: it models dishonesty, it undermines epistemic norms, and it exempts the fictionalist from the critical scrutiny that only honest assertion invites.

ii. moral authority claims cause social harm

Moral authority harms — categorical moral claims suppress compromise, entrench conflicts, and provide cover for coercion; abolishing moral authority removes these harmsThe harms of moral authority claims are not hypothetical. When people frame their preferences and political positions as moral requirements — as objectively binding on everyone — several things happen: compromise becomes treachery, disagreement becomes vice, and political opponents become not merely wrong but wicked. Moral framing hardens conflicts, forecloses negotiation, and provides ideological cover for coercion and persecution. Hinckfuss was right that the moral society is a damaging social arrangement. But the mechanism goes deeper than the hierarchies he identified: it is the categorical authority structure of moral discourse itself that generates these harms, regardless of its specific content. Abolishing morality does not mean anything goes; it means replacing categorical demands with negotiable preferences, which are subject to honest scrutiny and compromise in a way that moral claims are not.

iii. the challenge of reinvention

Reinvention objection — the worry that abolishing morality just reinvents it under another name; Garner's response: naming matters if the authority claim is stripped outThe opposition will press what I call the reinvention objection: if we abolish moral discourse but continue to express preferences, make demands, and recommend courses of action, are we not simply doing morality under another name? Bernard Williams pointed to something like this in his distinction between morality and the broader concept of “ethics.” My response: the naming matters because the authority claim matters. When I express a preference I am being transparent about its status. When I make a moral claim I am asserting an objective requirement that transcends my preferences and that applies to you regardless of your own preferences. Abolishing morality specifically abolishes the false authority claim. What remains — preferences, prudential reasoning, social negotiation — can do everything morality was supposed to do, without the deception.

iv. the abolitionist’s positive vision

Positive vision — the abolitionist is not a nihilist; she replaces moral claims with honest prudential and cooperative reasoning; this is more robust and less harmful than moralityAbolishing morality is not a counsel of despair. The abolitionist does not stop caring about people, does not stop reasoning about what to do, does not become indifferent to the suffering of others. She simply stops making those cares, reasons, and responses in the false currency of objective moral authority. She says: “I want the suffering to stop and here is why I want that; here is what I am willing to do about it and here is what I am asking of you.” This is more honest, more negotiable, and more resilient than moral discourse, which forecloses the very questioning of its own authority. The abolitionist world is not a world of chaos but a world of honest coordination — and honest coordination is more durable than coordination secured through the systematic assertion of falsehoods.

3.The abolitionist world does all the productive work that the moral world does, without the deception, the authority coercion, or the foreclosure of legitimate critique. It is the better world to aim for.
∴Abolitionism has a positive vision: honest coordination via transparent preferences and prudential reasoning; this is more honest, more resilient, and less harmful than the alternative of perpetuating moral authority claims that are known to be false.

Chair,

The arguments made by Mackie and Garner are philosophical and conceptual. My argument is different in kind: it is empirical and sociological. I do not need to convince you that the metaphysics of moral objectivism is wrong, though I believe it is. What I want to show is that even if we bracket the metaphysical question, the social effects of moral thinking are sufficiently damaging to justify abolition on purely practical grounds.

What “the moral society” means.

A moral society is one organised around categorical moral claims — claims that certain things are objectively required or forbidden, independently of any particular person’s preferences. In such a society, certain behaviours, identities, and lifestyles are classified as morally inferior or wrong, and this classification carries social authority. The moral society is characterised not merely by having norms but by treating those norms as categorically binding and morally superior — a feature that distinguishes it from a society organised around negotiated conventions and explicit agreements.
i. morality reinforces hierarchies and elitism

Hierarchy reinforcement — moral authority claims always operate in social contexts where some have more power to make and enforce those claims; morality systematically advantages the already-powerfulThe structure of moral authority is not neutral with respect to social power. Moral claims carry authority — the claim that something is not merely undesirable but objectively wrong — and this authority is exercised in social contexts where power is distributed unequally. Those with greater social, economic, or political power are better placed to make their moral claims heard, to enforce compliance with them, and to stigmatise those who deviate. The result is that moral discourse systematically amplifies existing hierarchies: the dominant group’s preferences become moral requirements; the subordinate group’s preferences become moral failures. This is not a contingent feature of particular moral systems but a structural consequence of moral authority claims operating in unequal social conditions.

ii. moral inflexibility impedes progress

Inflexibility — moral convictions resist revision in ways that contingent preferences do not; moral progress requires overcoming moral resistance, a self-undermining processMoral convictions are, by their nature, resistant to revision. If you believe that something is objectively wrong — not merely undesirable, not merely conventionally prohibited, but categorically impermissible — you will resist changing that belief in the face of evidence and argument far more tenaciously than you would resist changing a contingent preference. This resistance is not irrational given the structure of moral claims; it is a direct consequence of their categorical character. The result is that moral progress — the historical expansion of the moral circle, the revision of once-entrenched moral convictions about slavery, about the status of women, about sexuality — has required overcoming precisely the categorical moral resistance that morality itself generates. Moral progress is a process of fighting morality with morality, an inherently wasteful and brutal form of social change.

iii. morality in service of propaganda

Propaganda — moral framing is the instrument of choice for militarism, nationalism, and ideological extremism; it converts preference into duty and opponent into enemyThe historical record of the moral society’s products is not encouraging. Moral framing — the conversion of political, territorial, and ethnic preferences into categorical moral requirements — has been the instrument of choice for militarism, nationalism, religious persecution, and ideological extremism. The extraordinary violence of the twentieth century was not conducted by people who thought they were pursuing mere preferences; it was conducted by people who believed they had a moral obligation — indeed, a duty — to purify their nation, their race, or their class. The categorical force of moral conviction converts a preference-conflict into a duty-conflict, and duty-conflicts permit no compromise. A world organised around honest preferences rather than moral duties would be a world with more negotiation and less holy war.

iv. the alternative: cooperative prudential reasoning

Cooperative prudentialism — replacing morality with explicit cooperative reasoning produces better outcomes; game theory and social contract theory show this; morality adds nothing and subtracts muchThe alternative to the moral society is not chaos but cooperative prudential reasoning: the explicit negotiation of mutually beneficial conventions that serve the interests of all parties without the false veneer of moral authority. Game theory and social contract theory have developed sophisticated accounts of how stable cooperation can emerge from rational, self-interested agents without any appeal to objective moral requirements. These accounts show that morality is not needed for coordination — that the work morality claims to do can be done better by explicit, revisable, negotiated conventions. The moral society pretends to offer more than this — objective requirements, not merely conventions. It delivers less: inflexible hierarchies, propaganda, and conflict. We should replace it with something humbler and more honest.

Argument I — Moral authority amplifies existing social hierarchies: the structure of moral claims systematically advantages the already-powerful
1.Moral claims carry categorical authority; this authority is exercised in social contexts where power is distributed unequally. Those with greater social power are better placed to make their moral claims heard, enforced, and stigmatised when violated.

∴Moral authority claims systematically advantage the already-powerful by converting their preferences into categorical requirements; abolishing moral discourse removes this structural amplification of hierarchy.
Argument II — Moral inflexibility impedes progress: categorical convictions resist revision in ways that contingent preferences do not
1.The categorical character of moral claims generates resistance to revision: if you believe something is objectively wrong, you will resist revision far more tenaciously than if you believe it is merely undesirable or conventionally prohibited.
2.Historical moral progress — the expansion of the moral circle, the abolition of slavery, the extension of rights — has required overcoming precisely this categorical resistance. It is a self-undermining process: progress means fighting the inflexibility that morality itself generates.
3.A society organised around negotiated conventions would undergo social change via revision of explicit agreements, a process that is quicker, less violent, and less traumatic than the overcoming of moral conviction.
∴The categorical character of moral claims impedes social change by generating inflexibility; moral progress requires overcoming moral resistance, a self-undermining process that abolition would replace with explicit and revisable social negotiation.

3.A world organised around honest preferences rather than moral duties would have preference-conflicts with negotiable resolution points rather than duty-conflicts that demand total victory.
∴Moral framing is the instrument of propaganda and extremist violence: it converts negotiable preference-conflicts into non-negotiable duty-conflicts; abolishing moral authority claims would replace duty-conflicts with preference-conflicts that permit honest compromise.

3.The moral society delivers worse outcomes than a cooperative prudential alternative while claiming superior authority. The claim to superior authority is false; the worse outcomes are real.
∴Cooperative prudential reasoning outperforms moral discourse in every practical respect while making none of the false metaphysical claims; abolishing morality in favour of explicit cooperative conventions is both more honest and more socially beneficial.

I want to speak personally. I was a Kantian for thirty years. I taught Kantian ethics. I believed that morality was rationally binding — that categorical imperatives had genuine force and that our duty to act morally was not contingent on our desires or consequences. And then I changed my mind. The arguments Mackie gave were, I came to believe, correct. And once I accepted that, I found I could not in good conscience continue to moralise. Not out of nihilism but out of honesty.

“I had come to the conclusion that there are no moral facts and that, because of this, there are no moral obligations. I was not suddenly amoral. I still cared about things: about people, about suffering, about fairness. But I no longer thought any of these cares were moral obligations. They were just cares.” — Marks, adapted from the Amoral Manifesto
i. the honest amoralist and the dishonest fictionalist

Honest amoralism vs. dishonest fictionalism — the amoralist is transparent about the basis of her recommendations; the fictionalist deploys false moral authority she privately disbelievesJoyce and the fictionalists say: accept that morality is false, but continue to speak in moral terms because the practical benefits of moral motivation are too valuable to abandon. I find this recommendation philosophically and personally dishonest. The fictionalist speaks with the authority of objective moral requirements while privately believing there are none. She uses the rhetorical force of moral language — its implication that you are not merely advised but bound — to move people who trust her to be speaking sincerely. The honest amoralist says instead: here is what I care about and why; here is what I am requesting of you and the reasons I offer; take it or leave it. This is less coercive and more honest than the fictionalist alternative, even if it is also less rhetorically powerful.

ii. desire-talk as a replacement for moral talk

Desire-talk — replacing moral claims with explicit desire and preference claims is transparent, honest, and rhetorically adequate for most practical purposesWhat does the amoralist say in place of moral claims? She says: “I strongly want that not to happen and here is why; I am asking you to help me prevent it.” She says: “I find that behaviour deeply distressing and I would be grateful if you would stop.” She says: “The consequences of that policy would, in my considered view, cause enormous suffering to many people, and I will do what I can to oppose it.” None of these formulations presupposes objective moral requirements. All of them communicate genuine cares with genuine reasons. The amoralist loses the false authority of moral claims but gains the authenticity of transparent preference-expression. In most practical contexts, the authentic expression of strong cares is more persuasive, not less, than the assertion of contested moral authority.

iii. against the motivational gap argument

Motivational gap — the fictionalist argues that moral motivation is stronger than prudential motivation; Marks denies this; caring about things is sufficient motivation without moral authorityThe fictionalist’s main argument is motivational: moral beliefs are uniquely effective at bolstering self-control and overcoming weakness of will in ways that merely prudential reasons are not. Joyce makes this argument explicitly. I deny it. The evidence that moral motivation is uniquely effective is largely anecdotal. More importantly, I submit that deep caring — caring about people and their suffering, caring about fairness and reciprocity, caring about the kind of person one is — is motivationally sufficient without any appeal to moral authority. The abolitionist does not ask people to abandon their cares; she asks them to reconceive their cares as cares rather than obligations. In my own experience of living amorally for many years, the reconception did not weaken my motivations. If anything, it clarified and focused them.

iv. the personal liberation of amoralism

Personal liberation — amoralism removes moral guilt, moral anxiety, and the coercive demands of others' moral authority; it replaces these with honest self-examination of cares and reasonsThere is a personal dimension to amoralism that the philosophical debate often neglects. Living without moral convictions — replacing “I ought” with “I care,” replacing “you are wrong” with “I find that distressing” — has practical benefits for the individual as well as for society. Moral guilt is debilitating in a way that remorse about having acted contrary to one’s cares is not. The coercive authority of others’ moral demands is oppressive in a way that their honest expression of preferences is not. The amoralist is free to examine her own cares honestly, to revise them in light of experience, and to resist the demands of others on their merits rather than on the basis of their claimed moral authority. This is not a counsel of selfishness but a programme of intellectual and personal liberation.

The feminist dilemma about moral error theory.

Moral discourse has historically been deployed against women: women were morally required to be modest, to obey their husbands, to confine themselves to the domestic sphere. Morality has been a tool of women’s oppression.

But moral discourse has also been the primary vehicle through which feminist philosophers and activists have made claims against that oppression: women have moral rights; rape is wrong; the subordination of women is unjust. Feminism is an irreducibly moral project — it makes claims about what is right and wrong, about what women are owed.

If moral error theory is true, both sets of claims are false. The feminist cannot say that women have moral rights. But the patriarch cannot say that women are required to be modest either. The error theory is, in one sense, equally corrosive of both: it takes away the patriarchal cudgel and the feminist claim simultaneously. The question for the feminist error theorist is whether this is a bargain she can live with.

My position in this debate is deliberately limited in scope. I am not going to argue that moral discourse is well-founded, or that the error theory is false, or that the opposition’s practical recommendations are all correct. What I want to argue is the more modest claim that the specific harms Garner, Hinckfuss, and Blackford identify as grounds for abolitionism are addressable by means short of abolition, and that the costs of abolition outweigh the costs of reform.

The misuse of morality and its alternatives.

The proposition’s case for abolitionism relies heavily on the observation that morality is misused: moral authority claims are selectively applied, deployed in service of power, used to justify coercion, and exploited for propaganda. This is true. But the question is whether these harms are reducible by reform of moral practice or only by its abolition.

I argue that the misuse of morality is not an inherent feature of moral discourse but a function of specific structural conditions — conditions of unequal power, epistemic closure, and authoritarian enforcement — that can in principle be addressed without abandoning the practice entirely.
i. the misuse is not inherent

Misuse not inherent — the harms Garner identifies arise from specific social conditions that enable misuse; they are not necessary features of moral discourse as suchGarner argues that the categorical authority structure of moral discourse is itself the source of its harms: any moral claim, however liberal, carries this authority and thereby generates the harms of dogmatism, propaganda, and coercion. I deny that the harms follow from the authority structure as such. They follow from the authority structure in combination with specific conditions: unequal enforcement, epistemic closure, and the absence of mechanisms for contesting moral claims. These conditions are variables. A moral discourse embedded in practices of open critical scrutiny, democratic contestation, and equal enforcement would have the categorical structure of moral claims without the harms the proposition identifies. The harms are contingent on structural conditions; the authority structure is not sufficient to generate them.

ii. the fictionalist problem with stability

Fictionalist instability — fictionalism requires psychological acrobatics that are unavailable to most people; make-belief in moral claims while knowing them false is cognitively demanding; abolitionism and fictionalism both fail the stability testBoth the abolitionist and the fictionalist proposals face stability problems. The abolitionist proposal requires people to abandon deeply constitutive patterns of moral thought — Joyce is right that this is psychologically unrealistic for most people. The fictionalist proposal requires people to maintain a deliberate make-belief stance toward moral claims — to deploy moral language in the full knowledge that its content is false while preserving the psychological benefits of the false beliefs. This requires a form of cognitive compartmentalisation that is psychologically demanding and probably unstable over time. If the fictionalist’s make-belief tips into sincere moral belief — as it is likely to do, given the psychological embeddedness of moral thinking — the fictionalist has simply drifted back into conservationism. Neither radical proposal is stably achievable.

iii. the harm-reduction alternative

Harm-reduction — the goal after accepting error theory should be reducing the specific harms of moral misuse: asymmetric enforcement, dogmatic closure, propaganda; this is achievable without abolitionThe practical goal I advocate is harm reduction: identifying the specific structural conditions that enable the misuse of moral authority and working to dismantle those conditions without abandoning moral discourse. The goal is not to preserve moral discourse as currently practised but to reform the conditions under which it operates. The specific interventions are identifiable: democratising the authority to make moral claims (so that the powerful do not have disproportionate moral authority); creating robust mechanisms for contesting moral claims (so that moral dogmatism is subject to critical scrutiny); distinguishing legitimate from illegitimate moral authority (so that exploitative deployments of moral discourse are identified as such). These are reforms that take the error theory seriously — they are motivated by the recognition that moral claims are false and that their misuse therefore lacks the justification that objective moral authority would provide — without requiring the wholesale abandonment of a practice that continues to serve genuine social functions.

iv. the epistemic benefits of retaining moral discourse

The proposition argues that moral discourse is exploited by the powerful. This is true. But it is also true that the criticism of that exploitation is conducted in moral terms: we identify the exploitation as unjust, as a wrong, as a violation of rights. The vocabulary through which the misuse of moral authority is identified, criticised, and challenged is moral vocabulary. If we abolish moral discourse, we abolish not only the misuse but the vocabulary of its critique. The abolitionist who wants to say that the powerful misuse of moral authority is a bad thing — that it harms people, that it should be stopped — is already using something that functions very much like moral language to make that case. The harm-reduction approach retains the vocabulary of critique while working to dismantle the structural conditions that enable misuse.
