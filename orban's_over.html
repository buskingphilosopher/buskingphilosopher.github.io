ORBÁN'S OVER

Published April 12, 2026

The result has just been announced that Viktor Orbán has been voted out of power in today's elections, and the first thing worth understanding is that it should not have been possible. He had spent sixteen years making it as close to impossible as democratic procedure allows.

He redrew district boundaries in 2011 to favour Fidesz's rural base — and did so again in late 2024, reducing Budapest's districts from 18 to 16 while adding two in the countryside. He also eliminated the second-round runoff that had previously forced parties to form coalitions. The result was a system where the largest party — even without a majority — wins far more seats than its vote share would suggest, as long as the opposition is fragmented. In 2018, Orbán won a two-thirds supermajority with only 49% of the vote. In 2022, independent pollsters underestimated Fidesz by as much as 16-20 points — one of the largest polling errors in Europe in recent history. They won with 54% of the vote. The system was not broken. It was working exactly as designed.

And yet on Sunday, April 12th 2026, turnout reached 77.8 percent at 6:30pm — above the previous record of 70.5 percent for the 2002 elections. Exit polls published after voting closed showed the upstart centre-right Tisza party of Péter Magyar garnering 55 to 57 percent support, ahead of Orbán's nationalist Fidesz party. Most Hungarian media now reporting the results so far are pointing to a big win for the opposition Tisza party.

The structural advantages that had delivered Orbán four consecutive terms had collided with a unified opposition, a record turnout, and an electorate that had well and truly had enough.

------------

What caused Orbán's downfall?

The story of how Orbán fell begins not with a grand opposition movement but with a presidential pardon for a child abuser.

In February 2024, it was revealed that President Katalin Novák had granted a pardon in April 2023 to a criminal involved in a pedophilia case. The scandal resulted in Novák's resignation, and that of former justice minister Judit Varga, who had countersigned the pardon. This was not, on its face, an obvious catalyst for a political revolution. But what happened next was.

Varga's ex-husband Péter Magyar posted on Facebook that he would resign from all of his government-related positions, stating that the past few years had made him realise that the idea of a "national, sovereign, bourgeois Hungary" stated as the goal of Orbán's rule was in fact a "political product" serving to obscure massive corruption and transfers of wealth to those with the right connections.

Magyar was not a nobody. He was a trained lawyer, a former member of the Fidesz inner circle, a man who had grown up admiring Orbán — apparently he kept a poster of a young Orbán on his bedroom wall during Hungary's democratic transition. On 15 March 2024, Magyar, despite initially refusing to participate in politics, held a rally attended by tens of thousands in Budapest at which he announced the formation of a new political party. Within weeks, around 15 percent of voters claimed they were "certain or highly likely" to vote for Magyar if he ran for office. By the European Parliament elections in June 2024, Tisza came second with almost 30 percent of the vote and 7 seats — a performance characterised by the media as a challenge to the ruling Fidesz of Viktor Orbán.

What Magyar had done that no previous opposition leader managed was to escape the strategic trap Orbán had set for his opponents. For years, the opposition had been fragmented across multiple parties with incompatible ideologies, competing against each other for a fixed pool of anti-Fidesz voters while Fidesz dominated every rural constituency. Magyar simply refused to play that game. He consolidated the anti-Fidesz vote under a single banner, ran candidates in all 106 single-member constituencies, and denied Orbán the fragmentation that had made the electoral system work in his favour. The structural advantage doesn't disappear in a two-horse race — but it narrows dramatically.

What the polls said, what Fidesz said about the polls, and what the polls got right

The polling picture leading into Sunday was unusually complex, and understanding it requires applying the same analytical rigour we would bring to any contested data.

In the run-up to the election, pro-Fidesz figures accused pollsters of undermining the government by publishing fake numbers. In August 2025, Tamás Lánczi, head of the Sovereignty Protection Office, claimed that some pollsters that were showing leads for Tisza were "abusing" public opinion research and carrying out "foreign assignments." The government's own aligned pollsters consistently showed Fidesz ahead or level. Every independent pollster showed Tisza with a double-digit lead.

This is structurally identical to the YouGov-Farage dispute playing out in British polling simultaneously — an incumbent political force alleging that independent methodology systematically undercounts their support. The Hungarian version was more extreme: the government created a state body specifically to investigate and delegitimise independent pollsters.

Polling agency Medián is considered one of the most accurate on Hungarian elections. Ahead of the most recent parliamentary elections four years ago, it surprisingly predicted a two-thirds majority for Fidesz just days before the election — and it turned out to have only slightly overestimated support for the opposition by a few seats. In 2026, Medián produced a seat projection based on its five most recent surveys, predicting that Tisza could expect to secure between 138 and 143 out of 199 seats.

The key analytical question for any serious reader of Hungarian polls was not which house to trust — it was whether the structural factors that caused the massive polling error of 2022 would repeat. In 2022, independent pollsters underestimated Fidesz by as much as 16-20 points. Two structural factors made 2026 different. First, in 2022 the opposition was fractured; in 2026 it was unified. The 2022 error was partly a genuine measurement failure but partly the inability of polls to model how a fragmented opposition vote translates into seats. That problem did not apply in a two-party contest. Second, three-quarters of those under 30 intended to vote for Tisza, and 63 percent of those aged between 30 and 40. Fidesz's support in these two age groups sat at just 10 and 17 percent respectively. A poll of polls that showed Tisza ahead by 15-20 points in the younger half of the electorate was not measuring a protest sentiment — it was measuring a structural generational shift that had been building since Orbán's return to power in 2010.

The exit polls published after voting closed — Tisza on 55-57%, Fidesz on 37-38% — align closely with what Medián had been forecasting. A poll conducted by 21 Research Center for Hungarian news service Telex predicted Tisza to win 132 of the parliament's 199 seats, with 55 percent of voters expected to vote for Tisza, while 38 percent chose Orbán's Fidesz.

The one major independent variable that no model could confidently predict was turnout. Turnout reached 77.8 percent — above the previous record. Record turnout in a politically polarised election almost always benefits the challenger. In Hungary, it benefited Magyar decisively.

------------

What Orbán built, and why it made victory harder to hold than any opponent expected

The narrative of Orbán's defeat risks being written as a story of democracy triumphing over authoritarianism. That is broadly correct but analytically incomplete. What Orbán built was not a dictatorship — it was a system designed to survive democratic elections while hollowing out everything that makes elections consequential. Understanding the distinction matters enormously for what comes next.

Hungary continues to hold regular elections, and opposition parties campaign freely. But analysts say the playing field is uneven. Changes to electoral rules, increasing executive branch control over the judiciary, media dominance and the use of state resources give the ruling party structural advantages. That means challengers must win decisively to overcome them. Freedom House now classifies Hungary as only "partly free." The V-Dem Institute describes it as an electoral autocracy.

Critics say the government's influence reaches far beyond politics and law-making. Much of Hungary's media landscape is now aligned with the government, with the public broadcaster shut down, and private networks in the hands of business networks close to Fidesz. Higher education has also been a flashpoint. The Central European University, funded by Hungarian-American philanthropist George Soros, was effectively pushed out of Budapest.

The corruption question was not abstract. In the 16 years since he took office in 2010, Hungary descended to the rank of the most corrupt country in the European Union, according to the UK-based anti-corruption group Transparency International. Reports alleging voter intimidation surfaced in the final weeks: a documentary film alleged that Fidesz was engaging in a campaign of mass voter intimidation in poor rural or small-town communities, with local Fidesz mayors in such communities offering cash, work, firewood, transport to polling stations, access to medicine, and synthetic drugs in exchange for "correct" votes.

There was also direct foreign interference. Investigative outlet VSquare reported that the Russian military intelligence service had deployed a team of "political technologists" to the Russian embassy in Budapest to assist Orbán's re-election campaign, modelled on previous Russian interference campaigns in Moldova. On 10 April, two days before the election, research published by digital forensics experts and reported by Reuters identified a coordinated influence operation on Telegram, where dozens of channels previously used to spread Kremlin-aligned narratives simultaneously shifted focus to the Hungarian election, promoting pro-Fidesz narratives while accusing the opposition of being "warmongers."

None of it was enough.

The geopolitics: what this means for Europe, Ukraine, and the West

The stakes of Sunday's result extend far beyond Hungary's borders, and they were understood as such by every major power involved.

For Ukraine, the calculus was immediate and concrete. For months, Orbán used Hungary's membership of the EU to veto a €90 billion EU loan for Ukraine, citing a dispute with Kyiv over a damaged pipeline. A Tisza government has committed to joining the European Public Prosecutor's Office and removing Hungary's veto on Ukrainian assistance. The unblocking of that loan alone would represent a significant geopolitical shift.

For the European Union, Orbán's Hungary had been a structural irritant for a decade — using the veto as leverage, blocking sanctions, refusing to implement rule-of-law requirements as a condition of receiving EU funds. Magyar has stated that a top priority for a Tisza government would be to recover the €20 billion of EU funds that the European Commission withheld due to the Fidesz-led government breaching EU law on immigration and asylum seekers. That money — and the institutional relationship it represents — is now potentially unlocked.

For the United States, the picture is more complicated. US Vice President JD Vance spent two days in Budapest this week, pledging to help Orbán "as much as I possibly can." President Trump said on social media that his administration "stands ready to use the full economic might of the United States to strengthen Hungary's economy if Orbán and the Hungarian people ever need it." The explicit intervention of a sitting US Vice President in a European election — and its failure to move the needle — is itself a significant data point about the limits of MAGA diplomatic influence in a country where voters had more immediate domestic grievances than geopolitical solidarity.

Orbán's defeat will also stress-test President Trump's emerging network of like-minded political allies in Europe. The model that Orbán represented — illiberal democracy, media capture, institutionalised corruption dressed as national sovereignty — was being watched closely in Warsaw, Bratislava, Rome, and Paris as either a template or a warning. Tonight it looks more like a warning.

------------

Magyar's governing challenge: winning an election is the easy part

The harder analysis concerns not what Magyar has won but what he has inherited.

Even if Tisza wins a majority, the system Fidesz has engineered — by largely capturing the judiciary, the media, and other institutions — will make it hard to govern. "There are many judges and many systems around the government that can block the work of a future government," said Marton, a 29-year-old surgeon in Budapest.

Magyar's first-day agenda is ambitious: anti-corruption steps, an asset recovery office, an EPPO bid, a transfer-of-power process, a two-term cap for prime ministers, and suspending public media news broadcasts until balanced reporting is ensured. The constitutional amendments that would entrench these changes require the 133-seat two-thirds supermajority. If the exit polls hold, he has it. But implementing structural reform against an entrenched bureaucracy, judiciary, and media ecosystem built over 16 years — while also managing an economy that has stagnated for three consecutive years and a population with high immediate expectations — is a governing challenge without recent precedent in European politics.

Péter Magyar is not an outsider seeking to dismantle the system from the ground up, but a political insider who understands how it operates. His campaign has deliberately avoided presenting the election as a clash between two irreconcilable Hungarys. That positioning matters. It may allow him to pull some Fidesz voters and institutions toward cooperation rather than resistance. It also means he cannot govern as though he has a revolutionary mandate, even if the arithmetic suggests otherwise.

The most honest framing of tonight's result is this: Hungary voted for change, decisively, in record numbers, in a system designed to prevent exactly that. Whether that change can be institutionalised before Fidesz — which retains a motivated base of 37-38% and significant control over non-parliamentary institutions — begins the process of recapturing what it lost is the question that will define Hungarian politics for the next decade.

The era that ended tonight took 16 years to build. It will not be dismantled in one election.
