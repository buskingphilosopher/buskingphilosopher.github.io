Sonnet 1

William Shakespeare — Sonnets (1609)

Structure & Context
Form: Shakespearean sonnet — three quatrains plus a closing couplet; ABAB CDCD EFEF GG Metre: Iambic pentameter — ten syllables per line, the natural rhythm of English persuasion and argument Series: First of 126 sonnets addressed to a young man; this opens the procreation sequence (Sonnets 1–17) Argument: A beautiful young man refuses to reproduce — Shakespeare argues his self-love is a form of theft from the world Rhetorical strategy: The poem recruits the reader into its 'we' before turning on the 'thou' — the accusation is framed as universal common sense before the target is named Central paradox: The young man's hoarding of his beauty is indistinguishable in its effects from destroying it — niggarding and waste are the same act

From fairest creatures we desire increase, That thereby beauty's rose might never die, But as the riper should by time decrease, His tender heir might bear his memory: But thou, contracted to thine own bright eyes, Feed'st thy light's flame with self-substantial fuel, Making a famine where abundance lies, Thy self thy foe, to thy sweet self too cruel: Thou that art now the world's fresh ornament, And only herald to the gaudy spring, Within thine own bud buriest thy content, And, tender churl, mak'st waste in niggarding: Pity the world, or else this glutton be, To eat the world's due, by the grave and thee.
