The correct foundation for left-libertarianism is not Lockean natural resource rights but the maximisation of real freedom — the capacity to do whatever one might want to do. Logical Arguments

Let me begin where all left-libertarianism must begin: with the concept of a right. A right, properly understood, is a protected domain of action — a claim that others may not enter a particular physical or temporal space that is yours. The fundamental question of political philosophy is how to distribute those protected domains consistently and without conflict. My answer is compossibility: a set of rights is just if and only if each right in the set is logically compatible with every other right in the set — if, that is, the successful exercise of each right cannot be defeated by the successful exercise of any other.

Compossibility imposes a strong structural constraint on justice. It rules out any theory of rights under which the fulfilment of one person’s rights necessarily frustrates another’s. It requires that rights be distributed as non-overlapping spatio-temporal domains of exclusive control. And crucially: it requires that the boundaries of those domains be drawn in a way that leaves each person an equal share of what nobody made — natural resources in the broadest sense.

Steiner’s compossibility constraint and the derivation of natural resource rights
Compossibility: A set of rights R is compossible iff for every pair of rights r⊂i, r⊂j ∈ R, the exercise of r⊂i does not logically preclude the exercise of r⊂j.
For rights to be compossible, each person must have a determinate domain of exclusive control. Overlapping or indeterminate domains generate rights-conflicts that violate compossibility.
Self-ownership + Compossibility → Equal Natural Resource Rights
Self-ownership gives each person exclusive control over their own body. Compossibility requires that this not conflict with anyone else’s self-ownership. For this, each person needs an external domain to exercise their self-owned capacities. The only external resources that are not the product of anyone’s self-owned labour are natural resources. Equal compossible rights therefore require equal natural resource rights.
The metric: equality of market value. Not equality of welfare opportunity (Otsuka) or real freedom (Van Parijs). Market value is the right metric because it is the only one that is independent of the subjective preferences and talents of the rights-holder — it measures the objective value of the resource itself, not what any particular person can do with it.
i. compossibility as the foundational constraint

Compossibility — rights must be logically compatible; this requires non-overlapping spatio-temporal domains; rights-conflicts are not moral dilemmas but logical errors in the specification of rightsThe compossibility requirement is not merely a desideratum for a system of rights; it is a necessary condition for a system of rights to exist at all. A system of rights in which some rights can only be exercised by violating other rights is not a genuine system of rights but a system of competing claims dressed up as rights. For rights to be genuinely vested — genuinely held by their owners in a way that cannot be taken away by others exercising their own rights — the domains they protect must be non-overlapping. This is not a contingent preference; it follows from what it means to have a right at all. The political philosophy that results from taking compossibility seriously is one that divides the world into non-overlapping domains and assigns each person an equal share of the unproduced domains. Van Parijs and Otsuka both accept self-ownership; neither has fully reckoned with what compossibility requires for the distribution of external resources.

ii. genetic endowments as natural resources

Genetic endowments — the genetic information in gametes is not the product of parental labour; it is a natural resource; parents who pass favourable genes to children confer a natural advantage that must be compensated through a global genetic endowment fundThe most distinctive and controversial element of my position is the inclusion of genetic endowments in the category of natural resources subject to equal distribution. My argument is straightforward. Natural resources are, by definition, resources that are not the products of anyone’s labour — that exist independently of human productive activity. The genetic information encoded in gametes — the hereditary information passed from parents to children — is not the product of parental labour. Parents do not create their gametes’ genetic content through productive effort; they inherit it from their own parents. The genetic advantages conferred by favourable heredity are therefore natural advantages, and they fall within the category of natural resources that must be distributed equally. The practical implication is a Global Fund, to which all prospective parents contribute according to the market value of the genetic advantages they are transmitting, and from which all children receive equal compensation for natural genetic endowments. This is not taxation of talents — which would violate self-ownership — but compensation for the unequal distribution of a natural resource.

iii. why equal market value, not equal welfare opportunity

Market value metric — Otsuka’s welfare opportunity metric requires interpersonal comparisons of welfare that are impossible; market value is objective and person-independent; it is the only metric consistent with respecting self-ownershipOtsuka argues that the correct metric of equality for natural resource rights is equal opportunity for welfare: each person should be able to appropriate natural resources such that their welfare opportunities are equalised. I have two fundamental objections. First: welfare opportunity comparisons are inherently person-relative and subjective. Whether person A has greater welfare opportunity than person B depends on their respective preferences, tastes, and capacities for satisfaction — facts that are deeply contested and impossible to measure objectively. A theory of justice built on such comparisons inherits all the indeterminacy and contestability of welfare theory. Second: welfare equalisation violates self-ownership in a subtle way. If what justifies redistribution is the goal of equalising welfare opportunities, then redistribution tracks the welfare needs and capacities of recipients rather than the objective value of what is being redistributed. This makes the extent of redistribution depend on the internal characteristics of persons — their tastes, preferences, and capacities — rather than on the objective value of the external resources being distributed. Market value is the right metric because it measures the resources themselves, not what any particular person can do with them.

iv. against van parijs: jobs are not natural resources

Jobs not natural resources — Van Parijs treats job-rents as external assets to be redistributed; but jobs are artifacts produced by human activity; redistributing their rents violates self-ownership of the talent that makes them productiveVan Parijs argues that jobs are assets with rental value that belong, in justice, to all members of society. The argument is that employment positions are scarce and carry rents — surpluses over what the position would pay in a perfectly competitive market — and that these rents constitute external assets that should be redistributed through an unconditional basic income. I reject this argument. Jobs are not natural resources; they are artifacts. They are productive positions created by the economic activity of employers, workers, and investors. The rent value of a job reflects the productive value of the particular worker who holds it — their talent, training, and effort. Redistributing job-rents is not the redistribution of a natural resource; it is the redistribution of value that derives from the self-owned capacities of the worker. This is incompatible with robust self-ownership, which Van Parijs claims to accept. He has imported a form of talent-taxation through the back door of the jobs-as-assets argument, and this is not consistent with the left-libertarian framework.

Compossibility demands equal natural resource shares. The metric is market value. Genetic endowments are included. Jobs are not. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Compossibility is a necessary condition for any genuine system of rights; it requires non-overlapping spatio-temporal domains of exclusive control
1.A right is a protected domain: a claim that others may not exercise control within a specified domain. For rights to be genuine, their exercise cannot be defeated by others exercising their rights.
2.Compossibility: a set of rights is just iff the exercise of each right does not logically preclude the exercise of any other. This requires non-overlapping, determinate domains of exclusive control.
3.Any theory of rights that generates rights-conflicts is not a genuine rights-theory but a system of competing claims. Compossibility is a necessary, not merely desirable, feature of a coherent rights-system.
∴Compossibility is the foundational structural constraint on any just system of rights; it requires the world to be divided into non-overlapping domains assigned to individuals; equal natural resource rights follow from applying this constraint universally.
Argument II — Genetic endowments are natural resources; their unequal distribution grounds a global genetic endowment fund
1.Natural resources are defined by what they are not: the product of anyone’s labour. They exist independently of human productive activity.
2.The genetic information in gametes is not produced by parental labour: parents inherit and transmit their genetic material without creating it. Favourable hereditary endowments are natural advantages, not earned ones.
3.Equal natural resource rights require a global fund to which prospective parents contribute the market value of genetic advantages transmitted and from which children are compensated for natural genetic inequalities. This is not talent-taxation; it is natural resource equalisation.
∴Genetic endowments fall within the category of natural resources and must be equalised through a global genetic endowment fund; this is the most consistent application of the left-libertarian framework to the actual sources of natural inequality.
Argument III — Equal market value is the correct metric; welfare opportunity is subjective, interpersonally incomparable, and covertly violates self-ownership
1.Welfare opportunity comparisons are inherently person-relative: they depend on the subjective preferences and capacities of the persons being compared. These cannot be measured objectively.
2.A theory of resource rights calibrated to welfare opportunity makes redistribution track the internal characteristics of recipients (their tastes, capacities, preferences) rather than the objective value of external resources. This covertly violates self-ownership by making others bear the costs of individual welfare profiles.
3.Market value is objective, person-independent, and measures the resources themselves. It is the only metric consistent with respecting both self-ownership and equal natural resource rights simultaneously.
∴Equal market value is the correct metric for natural resource equality; Otsuka’s welfare opportunity metric is subjective, interpersonally incomparable, and covertly violates self-ownership by tracking recipient welfare profiles rather than resource values.
Argument IV — Van Parijs’s jobs-as-assets argument covertly taxes talents; it is incompatible with robust self-ownership
1.Van Parijs: jobs are scarce assets carrying rents that belong to all. Redistributing job-rents through a UBI is redistribution of external assets, not talent-taxation.
2.But the rent value of a job reflects the productive value of the particular worker who holds it — their talent, training, and effort. Redistributing job-rents redistributes value that derives from self-owned capacities.
3.Robust self-ownership requires that individuals retain the full value of their self-owned productive capacities. Van Parijs’s jobs-as-assets argument redistributes that value under another name — a covert talent-tax incompatible with the self-ownership Van Parijs claims to endorse.
∴The jobs-as-assets argument is incompatible with self-ownership: job-rents reflect the value of self-owned capacities; redistributing them is talent-taxation by another name; Van Parijs has not escaped the tension between self-ownership and radical equality that he claims to resolve.

I want to explain why the real-freedom framework is not merely a variation within the Lockean left-libertarian tradition but represents a genuine philosophical advance over it — one that takes the insights of Steiner, Otsuka, and Vallentyne and builds on them toward a more coherent and politically compelling position. The key departure is the concept of real freedom. Steiner, Otsuka, and Vallentyne all talk about freedom, but they mean formal freedom: the absence of coercive interference with one’s choices. I insist on real freedom: the actual capacity to do what one might want to do. A starving person is formally free to dine at the Ritz; she is not really free to do so. A just society is one that maximises the real freedom of its least-free members, subject to equal formal freedom and individual security.

The policy instrument that maximises real freedom for all is an unconditional basic income — a cash payment made to every adult member of society, regularly, regardless of their willingness to work, at the highest sustainable level. I want to show why this follows from the left-libertarian framework even more directly than Steiner’s Georgist land-value tax or Otsuka’s welfare-opportunity proviso.

Van Parijs’s real-libertarian derivation of UBI
Real freedom: the actual capacity to choose among the widest feasible range of options — not merely the formal absence of coercive interference.
A society maximises real freedom for all iff it leximin-maximises the real freedom of its least free members, subject to: (1) equal formal freedom (security of persons and contract), (2) self-ownership (no forced labour or bodily invasion), (3) highest sustainable basic income for all.
Jobs as external assets:
Employment positions are scarce external assets carrying rents above competitive returns. These rents accrue to job-holders through luck and social convention, not through their own productive merit. They are external assets that belong, in justice, to all members of society — to be redistributed via UBI.
The UBI level: set at the highest sustainable rate — the rate that maximises the floor without undermining the tax base. This is not a utopian proposal; it is derivable from real-libertarian justice applied to the actual structure of modern labour markets. Even surfers who choose not to work are entitled to the UBI — Malibu’s surfers, as I famously put it, should get their share.
i. formal freedom is not enough: the case for real freedom

Real freedom — formal freedom fails to capture what matters; a pauper is formally free but really unfree; maximising real freedom requires resources; this is what left-libertarianism should deliverSteiner, Vallentyne, and Otsuka all ground their egalitarian commitments in Lockean natural resource rights and the proviso. I accept these as part of the story. But they miss something crucial: what we care about in freedom is not merely the absence of legal prohibition but the actual capacity to live the life one chooses. Freedom, properly understood, is real freedom: the set of things one is actually able to do, not merely the set of things one is not legally prevented from doing. The homeless person who is formally free to buy a house and the millionaire who is formally free to buy a house have the same formal freedom. Their real freedoms are radically different. A just society must attend to this difference. The left-libertarian framework, grounded in equal resource rights, provides the justification for doing so: equal shares of the world’s resources translated into the monetary transfers that actually expand the real freedom of the least-free members of society.

ii. jobs as external assets: the argument from scarcity rents

Jobs as assets — employment positions are scarce; their scarcity generates rents above competitive returns; these rents are not earned by workers but conferred by the labour market structure; they are external assets to be redistributedSteiner objects that jobs are artifacts, not natural resources, and that redistributing job-rents amounts to taxing talents. This objection misunderstands my argument. I am not claiming that jobs are natural resources in Steiner’s sense. I am claiming that employment positions in modern labour markets are scarce external assets whose value exceeds the competitive return to the worker’s labour, and that this excess — the job-rent — is not earned by the worker through their self-owned capacities but is a product of the social organisation of the labour market. A surgeon earns more than a nurse not only because of their greater talent and training but because surgical positions are scarce in ways that are partly a product of licensing, medical education systems, and social organisation. The scarcity rent — the premium above competitive return — is an external asset generated by the social structure, not a return to self-owned talent. It belongs, in justice, to all members of society, and should be redistributed via UBI. I am not taxing the surgeon’s talent; I am taxing the scarcity value of the position the surgeon holds.

iii. undominated diversity and the compensation for internal inequalities

Undominated diversity — internal endowments (talents, disabilities) cannot be equalised through resource redistribution; Van Parijs uses undominated diversity as the criterion for adequate compensation; this is less demanding than Otsuka’s welfare opportunity provisoBoth Steiner and Otsuka press the question of what to do about inequalities in internal endowments — talents, disabilities, physical capacities that affect what people can do with external resources. Steiner’s answer is to include genetic endowments in the natural resource pool — an ingenious but problematic proposal, as I shall explain. Otsuka’s answer is to calibrate the resource proviso to equal opportunity for welfare — ambitious but, I argue, overcalibrated. My answer is the criterion of undominated diversity: a distribution of internal endowments is just, from the perspective of resource redistribution, as long as no one’s internal endowment package is universally dominated by anyone else’s — that is, as long as there is no one who is worse than some other person on every dimension of endowment simultaneously. This is a modest criterion, designed to capture genuine cases of comprehensive disadvantage without requiring full welfare equalisation. The residual inequalities that survive undominated diversity are to be addressed by the UBI itself, which provides the resources that expand real freedom at the bottom.

iv. why ubi is the correct policy instrument

UBI — unconditional cash payment is the most freedom-expanding instrument; conditionality reduces freedom; UBI is the real-libertarian policy; the surfer objection is not an objection but a featureThe policy instrument that follows from real-libertarian justice is an unconditional basic income: a cash payment made to all adults, regardless of willingness to work, at the highest sustainable level. Steiner’s land-value tax and Georgist compensation schemes address only land and natural resources — a fraction of the total external asset base in a modern economy. Otsuka’s welfare proviso is difficult to operationalise and leads to complex case-by-case determinations. UBI is simple, universal, and maximally freedom-preserving: it gives each person the monetary capacity to pursue whatever life they choose, without attaching conditions that would constrain that choice. The famous objection — why should Malibu’s surfers who choose not to work receive a basic income? — is not an objection but a feature. The surfer who freely chooses a life of surfing over paid employment has exercised their real freedom. A society that respects real freedom must respect that choice and provide the material means to pursue it. The alternative — a conditional income that requires work — undermines the freedom that the payment is meant to protect.

Real freedom is what justice demands. Jobs carry redistributable rents. UBI is the instrument. The surfer deserves their share. I yield the floor.

Argument I — Formal freedom is insufficient: real freedom — actual capacity to choose — is what justice must maximise
1.Formal freedom: absence of legal prohibition. Real freedom: actual capacity to do what one might want to do. A homeless person is formally free to buy a house; she is not really free to do so. The difference matters morally.
2.The left-libertarian commitment to self-ownership and equal resource rights is motivated by the goal of expanding meaningful freedom for all. This motivation is better served by real freedom than by formal freedom alone.
3.A just society leximin-maximises real freedom: it maximises the real freedom of the least-free members, subject to equal formal freedom and the security of self-ownership. This is the correct specification of what left-libertarianism aims at.
∴Real freedom is the correct metric for left-libertarian justice: the framework’s motivation is the meaningful expansion of freedom for all, which requires attending to actual capacity, not merely formal permission; leximin real freedom is the right distributive principle.
Argument II — Job-rents are external assets generated by social structure, not returns to talent; redistributing them is not talent-taxation

2.The job-rent — the premium above competitive return — is not earned by the worker through talent and effort but conferred by the social structure of the labour market. It is an external asset generated by social convention, not by self-owned productivity.
3.Redistributing job-rents is therefore not talent-taxation: it does not take the value of self-owned capacities but the value of the external social position. Steiner’s objection misidentifies the target of redistribution.
∴Job-rents are external assets generated by social structure and are legitimately redistributable; this is not talent-taxation; the distinction between the competitive return to talent (kept by the worker) and the scarcity rent on the position (redistributed via UBI) is principled and coherent.

1.UBI: cash payment to all adults, unconditionally, at the highest sustainable level. This gives every person the monetary capacity to pursue whatever life they choose. It is more freedom-preserving than conditioned transfers.
2.Conditional income (requiring work) constrains the freedom that the payment is meant to protect. A real-libertarian society must not attach conditions that foreclose the choices whose expansion is the point of redistribution.
3.The surfer objection: Malibu’s surfers who choose not to work should receive UBI. This is a feature, not a bug: the surfer has exercised real freedom in choosing a non-paid life. Respecting real freedom requires respecting that choice materially.
∴UBI is the correct real-libertarian policy instrument: universal, unconditional, freedom-maximising; conditionality is incompatible with the real freedom UBI is designed to expand; the surfer objection is not a reductio but a demonstration of the principle in action.

Centrist Lockean • Sovereign Natural Resource Rights • The TaxonomyPeter Vallentyne — Missouri • Left-Libertarianism: A Primer, 2000; Phil. & Public Affairs, 2005
Left-libertarianism is a family of views, not a single position, and the task of the philosopher is first to map that family clearly. All left-libertarians share two core commitments: full self-ownership and egalitarian world-ownership. But they disagree about what egalitarian world-ownership requires, about the correct metric of equality, and about what counts as a natural resource subject to equal distribution. The most defensible version — centrist Lockean left-libertarianism — holds that natural resources are collectively owned by humanity and may be appropriated only on payment of a competitive rental to all. The disagreements among Steiner, Otsuka, and Van Parijs are real and important, but they are disagreements within a family that shares deeper commitments than any of its members share with either right-libertarians or liberal egalitarians.

I want to defend the position I call left-libertarianism without inequality: a view that takes both self-ownership and egalitarian resource rights with full seriousness, without either softening self-ownership to make room for redistributive taxation or weakening the egalitarian proviso to make appropriation easy. The key to reconciling these commitments lies in the correct interpretation of the Lockean proviso — the condition that governs when private appropriation of natural resources is just.

Locke said that appropriation is just when one leaves “enough and as good” for others. The question is: enough and as good by what measure? Nozick interpreted this as a non-worsening condition: you may appropriate as long as you leave others no worse off than they would have been without any appropriation at all — roughly, no worse off than under hunter-gatherer conditions. This is notoriously weak: in a world of productive private property, almost any appropriation leaves others better off than the Hobbesian baseline. I argue for a much stronger interpretation: you may appropriate only if others are left with equal opportunity for welfare after your appropriation. This is the egalitarian Lockean proviso, and it is what genuine commitment to the principle of equal world-ownership requires.

The egalitarian Lockean proviso: Otsuka’s formulation
Nozick’s weak proviso: Appropriation is just iff leavers are no worse off than under a no-appropriation baseline.
Implication: almost any appropriation is just, since private property generates surplus over the hunter-gatherer baseline. The proviso is effectively toothless.
Otsuka’s egalitarian proviso: Appropriation is just iff all others retain equal opportunity for welfare after the appropriation.
Equal opportunity for welfare (following Arneson): two persons have equal opportunity for welfare iff they face equally good prospects for welfare — where prospects are measured by the expected welfare they can achieve through choices within their control, with unequal unchosen factors equalised.
Why welfare opportunity, not market value (contra Steiner)? Because the value of a resource to a person depends on what they can do with it, which depends on their capacities and needs. Equal market value of resource shares leaves a disabled person less able to convert her share into welfare than an able-bodied person. Genuine equality in the morally relevant sense requires equalising the opportunity to achieve welfare, not equalising the market value of assets.
i. why the egalitarian proviso follows from self-ownership

Egalitarian proviso from self-ownership — if persons own themselves, they cannot be reduced to less than equal opportunity for welfare by others’ appropriation of the world; any such reduction treats them as less than self-owning agentsThe egalitarian proviso is not an external constraint imposed on self-ownership from outside; it follows directly from the principle of self-ownership itself, consistently applied. If you fully own yourself, then others cannot appropriate the external world in a way that reduces you to a position where you cannot meaningfully exercise that self-ownership — where you lack the basic resources to exercise agency at all. A person with full self-ownership who has been crowded out of all natural resources by prior appropriators is self-owning in name only: she has the right to her own body but no means of sustaining it. Self-ownership that generates no opportunity for welfare is an empty formal title. The egalitarian proviso secures the material conditions without which self-ownership has no practical content. Far from being in tension with self-ownership, the egalitarian proviso is what self-ownership demands when applied to a world of finite natural resources.

ii. why welfare opportunity, not market value

Welfare opportunity vs market value — Steiner’s market value metric gives equal purchasing power; but purchasing power does not translate into equal welfare opportunity for persons with different capacities; the disabled get equal value but unequal welfare opportunitySteiner argues for equal market value of natural resource shares. I accept that this is an improvement on Nozick. But it does not go far enough. Consider two people who receive equal market-value shares of natural resources: one able-bodied, one severely disabled. Both have the same purchasing power. But the disabled person faces much higher costs for achieving the same level of welfare — she must spend her resource share on care, mobility aids, and medical support before she can begin to pursue the activities that generate welfare for the able-bodied person. Equal market value translates into radically unequal opportunity for welfare. If the point of equalising resource shares is to give each person an equal chance to live a flourishing life — which is what equal world-ownership should mean — then the metric must be calibrated to welfare opportunity, not market value. Steiner’s metric is a proxy for what we actually care about, and a bad one.

iii. against steiner’s genetic endowment fund

Genetic fund objection — Steiner includes genetic endowments as natural resources; but the genetic information in a person is part of that person; taxing it at birth treats persons as means; it violates self-ownership in the very act of enforcing itSteiner argues that genetic information in gametes is a natural resource and that parents should pay into a global fund for the genetic advantages they transmit to their children. I have deep reservations about this proposal. The genetic constitution of a person is not merely a resource they happen to possess; it is constitutive of who they are. To treat a person’s genetic endowment as a natural resource belonging to all humanity — and to require compensation for the “excess value” of their genes — is to treat persons as means to the welfare of others in precisely the way that self-ownership is meant to prevent. There is a difference between equalising external resources (which is what natural resource rights require) and taxing the genetic constitution of persons (which treats persons as natural resources). My egalitarian welfare proviso handles the practical consequences of genetic inequality — by ensuring that appropriations leave everyone with equal welfare opportunity, including those with genetic disadvantages — without the conceptually problematic step of treating persons’ own constitutions as collective property.

iv. the superiority of left-libertarianism to liberal egalitarianism

Better than Rawls — Otsuka’s left-libertarianism is more libertarian than Rawls (respects full self-ownership) and more egalitarian (welfare proviso is more demanding than difference principle); it combines the best of both traditionsI want to close by situating left-libertarianism relative to the liberal egalitarian tradition of Rawls and Dworkin, which both Van Parijs and Vallentyne see as a close neighbour. I argue that my version of left-libertarianism is superior to both. It is more libertarian than Rawls: Rawls permits income and wealth redistribution through the difference principle, which allows the state to appropriate the returns to talent and effort without full respect for self-ownership. Rawls’s taxation of labour income is incompatible with full self-ownership. My left-libertarianism respects full self-ownership — workers keep the full product of their labour — while grounding equality in the egalitarian resource proviso rather than in redistribution of labour income. It is simultaneously more egalitarian than liberal egalitarianism in the resource sphere: the egalitarian welfare proviso is more demanding than either Dworkinian resource equality or the Rawlsian difference principle, requiring genuine equality of welfare opportunity rather than mere equality of resources or maximin welfare. The combination — more libertarian and more egalitarian — is precisely what left-libertarianism promises and what my version delivers.

Self-ownership demands the egalitarian proviso. Welfare opportunity is the right metric. Genetic endowments are persons, not resources. Left-libertarianism exceeds both Rawls and Nozick. I yield the floor.

Argument I — The egalitarian proviso follows from self-ownership: appropriation that leaves others without welfare opportunity violates self-ownership
1.Full self-ownership: each person has full control over their own body and the right to the returns from using it. Self-ownership that cannot be practically exercised — because one lacks resources to sustain oneself — is an empty formal title.
2.If prior appropriators take all natural resources and leave others without equal welfare opportunity, those others are reduced to self-ownership in name only: they own themselves but have no means to exercise that ownership in a world that is entirely owned by others.
3.Self-ownership consistently applied requires that appropriation leave others with the material conditions for genuine self-ownership: equal opportunity for welfare. The egalitarian proviso is not an external constraint on self-ownership but its internal demand.
∴The egalitarian proviso follows from self-ownership: consistently applied, self-ownership requires leaving others with equal welfare opportunity; Nozick’s weak proviso is not a genuine interpretation of what self-ownership demands when natural resources are scarce.
Argument II — Equal welfare opportunity, not market value, is the correct metric: equal purchasing power translates into unequal welfare for persons with different capacities
1.Steiner: equal market value of resource shares gives each person equal purchasing power. But a disabled person faces higher costs to achieve the same welfare as an able-bodied person. Equal purchasing power translates into unequal welfare opportunity.
2.If the point of equal resource shares is to give each person an equal chance at a flourishing life, the metric must track what we care about: the opportunity to achieve welfare, not the market value of the means to pursue it.
3.Market value is a proxy for welfare opportunity and a bad one: it ignores the different capacities persons have to convert resources into welfare. Equal welfare opportunity is what genuine equal world-ownership requires.
∴Equal welfare opportunity is the correct metric: it tracks what equal world-ownership should mean (equal chance at a flourishing life); market value is an inadequate proxy that leaves those with higher needs systematically worse off in welfare terms.
Argument III — Steiner’s genetic fund treats persons as natural resources; this violates self-ownership in the act of enforcing it
1.Steiner includes genetic information as a natural resource belonging to all humanity. But a person’s genetic constitution is not an external resource they happen to possess; it is constitutive of who they are as a person.
2.To require compensation for the “excess value” of one’s genes treats persons as means to others’ welfare — exactly what self-ownership prohibits. The genetic fund violates self-ownership in the act of claiming to implement it.
3.The practical consequences of genetic inequality are better addressed through the egalitarian welfare proviso: ensuring appropriations leave everyone with equal welfare opportunity, including those with genetic disadvantages, without treating persons’ constitutions as collective property.
∴Steiner’s genetic endowment fund is conceptually incoherent: it treats persons’ own constitutions as natural resources belonging to all, violating the self-ownership principle it purports to implement; the welfare proviso handles genetic inequality without this error.
Argument IV — Left-libertarianism without inequality is more libertarian than Rawls and more egalitarian than liberal egalitarianism
1.More libertarian than Rawls: Rawls permits redistribution of labour income via the difference principle, which is incompatible with full self-ownership. Otsuka’s left-libertarianism respects full self-ownership — workers keep all returns to labour — and grounds equality in resource rights alone.
2.More egalitarian than liberal egalitarianism: the egalitarian welfare proviso is more demanding than Dworkinian resource equality or the Rawlsian difference principle. It requires genuine equalisation of welfare opportunity, not merely resource equality or maximin welfare.
3.The combination — respecting full self-ownership while demanding genuine welfare opportunity equality — is what left-libertarianism uniquely promises: neither right-wing (accepting unequal resource appropriation) nor conventionally left-wing (taxing labour income).
∴Left-libertarianism without inequality occupies a genuinely distinct position: more libertarian than Rawls in respecting self-ownership, more egalitarian than liberal egalitarianism in the resource sphere; it delivers what neither Rawls nor Nozick can — a principled reconciliation of freedom and equality.

I want to step back from the specific disagreements between Steiner, Otsuka, and Van Parijs and offer what I think is the most useful philosophical contribution I can make to this symposium: a clear taxonomy of the positions within left-libertarianism, an account of what all left-libertarians share and where they genuinely disagree, and a defence of what I call the centrist Lockean position as the most defensible of the available alternatives. I am not claiming that Steiner, Otsuka, and Van Parijs are all wrong; I am claiming that they each capture part of the truth and that a careful taxonomy helps us see what each position gets right and where it overreaches.

All genuine left-libertarians share two core commitments. First: full self-ownership — each person has the most extensive rights of control over their own person compatible with everyone else having the same. Second: egalitarian world-ownership — natural resources may be privately appropriated only on terms that respect the equal claims of all persons on those resources. The disagreements are about what exactly egalitarian world-ownership requires: the correct metric of equality (market value, welfare opportunity, or real freedom), the scope of what counts as a natural resource (land only, all unproduced resources, genetic endowments), and the institutional mechanisms for enforcing equal claims (Georgist land tax, genetic fund, UBI).

Vallentyne’s taxonomy: the spectrum of left-libertarian positions
All left-libertarian positions: Full self-ownership + Egalitarian world-ownership (natural resources held in common)
Georgism / Henry George tradition: Land (and land only) is held in common; all persons have equal rights to land value; the appropriate instrument is a land-value tax.
Steiner’s left-libertarianism: All natural resources (including genetic endowments) held in common; metric is equal market value; mechanism is a global fund with rental payments.
Otsuka’s left-libertarianism: All natural resources held in common; metric is equal opportunity for welfare; mechanism is an egalitarian Lockean proviso on appropriation.

i. what all left-libertarians share: the common ground

Common ground — all left-libertarians share self-ownership and egalitarian world-ownership; these distinguish them from right-libertarians (who reject egalitarian world-ownership) and liberal egalitarians (who reject full self-ownership); the shared commitments are deeper than the internal disagreementsThe internal disagreements among Steiner, Otsuka, and Van Parijs are genuine and philosophically important. But they should not obscure the deep shared commitments that distinguish left-libertarianism from its rivals. Against right-libertarians like Nozick: all left-libertarians insist that natural resources cannot be privately appropriated without restriction, because the world belongs equally to all. The world was not made by anyone, and no one has a pre-political right to more than their equal share of it. Against liberal egalitarians like Rawls: all left-libertarians insist on full self-ownership. Rawls permits the state to appropriate the returns to talent and effort through income taxation; all left-libertarians deny this. The state may not take what you have produced through your own self-owned capacities. This double commitment — against Nozick on world-ownership, against Rawls on self-ownership — is what makes left-libertarianism a genuinely distinct position in the political philosophy landscape.

ii. the metric question: what equality of world-ownership means

Metric question — Steiner says market value; Otsuka says welfare opportunity; Van Parijs says real freedom; Vallentyne argues the metric question is genuinely open and depends on further argument about what equal claims on the world consist inThe deepest disagreement within left-libertarianism is about the correct metric of equality in the distribution of natural resources. Steiner argues for equal market value: what matters is that each person receives an equally valuable share of natural resources, where value is determined by market prices. Otsuka argues for equal welfare opportunity: what matters is that each person is left with equally good prospects for welfare. Van Parijs argues for leximin real freedom: what matters is that the least-free person is made as free as possible. I do not think any of these positions is decisively correct, and I am genuinely agnostic between Steiner and Otsuka on the metric question. What I can say is that Van Parijs’s real freedom metric represents the most significant departure from the Lockean framework, because it extends the scope of redistribution well beyond natural resources to job-rents and other scarcity values generated by social structure. Whether this extension is justified depends on whether jobs are natural resources — and on this question I side with Steiner against Van Parijs.

iii. the centrist lockean position on natural resources

Centrist Lockean — natural resources are held as collective sovereign property; appropriators owe competitive rental to all; this is more demanding than Nozick, less demanding than including genetic endowments or job-rents; the most defensible middle groundMy own preferred position within the left-libertarian family is what I call centrist Lockean left-libertarianism. Natural resources — land, minerals, water, air, the electromagnetic spectrum — are collectively owned by humanity. Any person who appropriates more than their pro-rata share owes a competitive rental payment to all other persons. This rental payment is the mechanism by which equal world-ownership is enforced: it ensures that those who use more than their share compensate those who use less. This position is more demanding than Nozick (who allows appropriation as long as the weak proviso is met) and less demanding than Steiner (who includes genetic endowments) and less expansive than Van Parijs (who includes job-rents). It grounds a robust and practically tractable form of redistribution — a dividend on natural resource use — without requiring the conceptually problematic inclusion of persons’ own genetic constitutions or the economically contested claim that job-rents are external assets.

iv. responding to g.a. cohen: does self-ownership really commit us to world-ownership equality?

Cohen objection — Cohen argues self-ownership does not entail world-ownership rights of any particular kind; Vallentyne responds that equal world-ownership follows not from self-ownership but from the independent principle that unproduced resources belong equally to all; Cohen mistakes the structure of the argumentG.A. Cohen famously argued that self-ownership does not by itself entail any particular world-ownership rights: you can accept full self-ownership without accepting any principle about the distribution of external resources. Cohen used this to argue that left-libertarianism is unstable — that its egalitarian world-ownership claims are not really grounded in self-ownership at all but are imported from external egalitarian principles, at which point the self-ownership premise does less work than left-libertarians claim. I accept Cohen’s structural point: self-ownership alone does not generate world-ownership equality. But this does not destabilise left-libertarianism. The egalitarian world-ownership claim is grounded independently — in the principle that natural resources are not the product of any person’s effort and therefore belong equally to all. Self-ownership and egalitarian world-ownership are two independent premises, both defensible, that together generate the left-libertarian conclusion. Cohen has shown that one premise does not entail the other; he has not shown that either premise is false or that the combination is incoherent.

The family shares self-ownership and egalitarian world-ownership. The metric is genuinely contested. Centrist Lockeanism is the most defensible position. Cohen’s objection misidentifies the structure. I yield the floor.

Argument I — All left-libertarians share self-ownership and egalitarian world-ownership; these commitments distinguish the family from all its rivals
1.Against right-libertarianism (Nozick): natural resources cannot be privately appropriated without restriction. The world was not made by anyone; no one has a pre-political right to more than their equal share. This commitment is shared by all left-libertarians.
2.Against liberal egalitarianism (Rawls): the state may not appropriate the returns to self-owned productive capacities through income taxation. Full self-ownership means workers keep the full product of their labour. This commitment is shared by all left-libertarians.
3.The double commitment — egalitarian world-ownership and full self-ownership — defines a genuinely distinct position that is neither right-libertarian nor liberal egalitarian. The internal disagreements about metrics and mechanisms are disagreements within this shared framework.
∴Left-libertarianism is a genuine and distinct position: the shared commitments to self-ownership and egalitarian world-ownership place it in a unique location between Nozick and Rawls; internal disagreements about metrics and mechanisms are secondary to this shared foundation.
Argument II — The metric question is genuinely open; Van Parijs’s real freedom metric departs most significantly from the Lockean framework
1.Steiner: market value of natural resource shares. Otsuka: equal welfare opportunity. Van Parijs: leximin real freedom. None is decisively correct on the available arguments; Steiner and Otsuka are more closely aligned with the Lockean framework than Van Parijs.
2.Van Parijs’s real freedom metric extends redistribution beyond natural resources to job-rents and other scarcity values. Whether this extension is justified depends on whether jobs are natural resources — a contested claim on which Van Parijs faces the strongest challenge from Steiner.
3.The metric question cannot be settled without a prior account of what equal claims on the world consist in — a question that requires further argument and that the left-libertarian family has not yet resolved.
∴The metric question is genuinely open within left-libertarianism; Van Parijs departs most from the Lockean framework by extending redistribution to job-rents; the most defensible positions are those that stay closer to the natural resource framework that gives the theory its distinctive character.
Argument III — Centrist Lockean left-libertarianism: collective sovereign natural resource ownership with competitive rental; the most defensible middle ground
1.Natural resources — land, minerals, water, air, electromagnetic spectrum — are collectively owned. Appropriators owe competitive rental payments to all others. This enforces equal world-ownership through a resource dividend.
2.This is more demanding than Nozick (weak proviso), less conceptually problematic than Steiner (no genetic fund), and more firmly grounded than Van Parijs (no contested job-rents claim). It represents a stable and tractable middle ground.
3.The competitive rental mechanism is economically tractable and institutionally implementable: it is a generalised version of the Georgist land-value tax extended to all natural resources, grounded in a clear ownership claim.
∴Centrist Lockean left-libertarianism — collective sovereign natural resource ownership with competitive rental — is the most defensible position: more demanding than right-libertarianism, less conceptually adventurous than Steiner or Van Parijs, and practically tractable.
Argument IV — Cohen’s objection misidentifies the structure: self-ownership and egalitarian world-ownership are independent premises; neither entails the other and neither is undermined by this independence
1.Cohen: self-ownership does not entail any particular world-ownership principle. Therefore left-libertarianism’s egalitarian world-ownership is not grounded in self-ownership but imported from external egalitarian sources — making the framework unstable.
2.Response: correct that self-ownership alone does not entail egalitarian world-ownership. But this does not destabilise left-libertarianism. The framework has two independent premises: self-ownership and the principle that natural resources belong equally to all (because no one made them).
3.Cohen has shown that one premise does not entail the other. He has not shown that either premise is false or that the combination is incoherent. Left-libertarianism is a two-premise theory, and both premises are independently defensible.
∴Cohen’s objection misidentifies the structure of left-libertarianism: it is a two-premise theory whose premises are independent but each defensible; the failure of entailment between them does not undermine the position; the combination generates a coherent and distinctive theory of justice.
